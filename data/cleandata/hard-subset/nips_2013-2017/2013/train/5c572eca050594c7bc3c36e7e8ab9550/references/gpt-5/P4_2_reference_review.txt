Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies estimation of the density ratio r(x) = q(x)/p(x) from samples, a key quantity for importance weighting under covariate shift and related settings. The central idea is to recast ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and to solve it via Tikhonov regularization in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS).

Two formulations are presented:
- Type I: Use the identity Eq[k(x,·)] = Ep[r(x)k(x,·)] = Kq 1(·), where Kp denotes the integral operator with kernel k(x,y) and measure p. With samples from both p and q, one solves minf∈H ||Kp f − Kq 1||^2 + λ||f||_H^2.
- Type II: Using a δ-like kernel kt (e.g., Gaussian) so that Kt,p f ≈ f, one obtains Kt,p r ≈ q, leading to minf∈H ||Kt,p f − q||^2 + λ||f||_H^2 when q is known at points sampled from p (q need not be a density).

By representer theorems, both yield closed-form kernel algorithms (FIRE) with explicit linear system solutions. The paper further considers a variant using an RKHS loss, and a combined empirical norm based on both p and q samples.

Theoretical contributions include finite-sample error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, for ℝ^d and compact manifolds, accounting for kernel bandwidth t, regularization λ, and sample sizes n,m. The rates degrade with dimension in a way consistent with nonparametric kernel smoothing. An unsupervised model selection procedure, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), chooses t and λ by matching importance sampling identities on random test functions (linear and half-space indicators).

Related work includes Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), which enforces Ep[rΦ(x)] = Eq[Φ(x)] in an RKHS via a constrained quadratic program, and LSIF/KLIEP, which minimize a discrepancy (L2,p or KL) over a parametric function class with out-of-sample capability. The paper clarifies that KMM is equivalent to solving a version of the same integral equation in H, but without explicit regularization and without a direct out-of-sample extension; the proposed approach provides regularization, explicit solutions, and theoretical rates. The work also builds on viewing learning as inverse problems (e.g., De Vito et al.), spectral regularization, and kernel operators on manifolds.

Quality (technical soundness):
- The variational formulations are standard and well-posed as Tikhonov problems in RKHSs. The empirical operators Kz,p and Kz,q and empirical L2 norms are conventional, and the representer-based closed-form solutions are correct given the setups.
- Theorems present L2,p error bounds with explicit dependence on t, λ, n, m, and dimension; Type II additionally includes a kernel approximation bias term ||Kt,q 1 − q||, argued to be O(t) (or O(t^(1−η)) on manifolds). The assumptions (bounded densities and smoothness of r and q/p^2 in a Sobolev space) are strong but standard for obtaining such rates.
- The rates are plausible for Gaussian kernels, with dimension-dependent exponents reflecting smoothing bias-variance tradeoffs and operator estimation error. As typical, rates deteriorate in high d.
- The CD-CV selection criterion is sensible (moment matching of importance identities on held-out data), akin to LSIF/KLIEP objectives used for validation rather than fitting. A theoretical justification of its consistency or robustness to the choice of test function class would further strengthen the contribution.

Clarity:
- Overall the paper is readable, but notation is sometimes confusing. The ratio is sometimes typeset as “q p” (or “qp”), and “pq” appears in some places—this should be standardized (e.g., r(x)=q(x)/p(x)) throughout. Carefully distinguish k vs kH, and Kp vs Kt,p, and ensure matrix notations match operator definitions.
- Some minor typographical issues (e.g., sub/superscripts, missing spaces) and occasional ambiguous references (“Eq. 2” vs “Eq. 4”). A notation table would help.
- The empirical complexity of solving the linear systems and any numerical stabilization strategies (e.g., adding λI, scaling, or using low-rank approximations) should be stated.

Originality:
- Framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation and solving with RKHS Tikhonov is a natural but under-explored angle in this subfield. The close connection to KMM is clearly articulated. The explicit Type II formulation (when q is known at p-sampled points) and its analysis are valuable.
- Providing finite-sample bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels in both Euclidean and manifold settings appears novel relative to prior KMM/LSIF analyses and contributes theoretical depth.
- The CD-CV unsupervised model selection based on random test functions is a pragmatic addition, though conceptually related to moment matching approaches.

Significance:
- Density ratio estimation is widely used in covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, and MCMC diagnostics; robust regularized operators with out-of-sample solutions are practically relevant.
- The theory gives guidance on parameter trade-offs and supports use on manifold-structured data.
- Empirical results suggest competitive or superior performance to LSIF and TIKDE; the claim of improved stability vs KMM is plausible but would benefit from a fair out-of-sample comparison to KMM using standard extensions.

Strengths:
- Clear operator-theoretic formulation (Type I and II) with simple closed-form kernel algorithms.
- Regularization built in; natural out-of-sample extension.
- Nonasymptotic bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels in ℝ^d and on manifolds.
- Practical, fully unsupervised model selection (CD-CV); sensible test function choices.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Constraints: The estimator may output negative weights; discuss nonnegativity/normalization constraints or post-projection and its effect on theory/practice.
- Assumptions: Strong smoothness and boundedness; clarify absolute continuity (q ≪ p) and behavior when supports mismatch.
- Clarity: Unify notation (define r, k, kH, Kp, Kt,p), fix typos, and include a concise algorithm box.
- Empirics: Include ablations (Type I vs II, effect of t and λ, number/type of CD-CV test functions), wall-clock/runtime and scalability (n^3 solves), and fair KMM comparisons with an out-of-sample mapping. Demonstrate Type II in scenarios where q is known up to a constant (e.g., MCMC), as motivated.
- Boundaries: State assumptions and implications for domains with boundary (briefly mentioned).
- Numerical stability: Discuss conditioning of K^2KH + nλI and implementation details (e.g., Cholesky, preconditioning, Nyström/random features).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid and principled formulation connecting density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems, yielding simple algorithms with guaranteed out-of-sample predictions.
- Meaningful theoretical analysis with finite-sample rates, including manifold settings.
- Practical, unsupervised model selection method and competitive empirical performance.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Notational clarity and presentation issues; some overlap in core idea with prior RKHS operator formulations (e.g., KMM), with novelty partly in packaging and analysis.
- Limited empirical breadth and fairness in comparisons; scalability and constraints (nonnegativity) not fully addressed.
- Strong assumptions and dimension-dependent rates limit immediate applicability in high dimensions.

Overall recommendation: Borderline accept (strength in theory and principled formulation; would benefit from improved clarity, broader empirical validation, and discussion of constraints/scalability).
