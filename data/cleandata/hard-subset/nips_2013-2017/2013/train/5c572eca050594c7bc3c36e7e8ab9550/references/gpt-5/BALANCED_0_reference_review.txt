Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies density-ratio (importance) estimation, r(x) = q(x)/p(x), by recasting it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two settings are proposed:
- Type I: When samples from both p and q are available, use the identity Kp r = Kq 1, and estimate r by minimizing an empirical L2,p loss with RKHS regularization. Variants with mixed L2,p/L2,q and with RKHS loss are also given. Closed-form solutions follow from the representer theorem, yielding linear systems in kernel matrices.
- Type II: When q is known (not necessarily a density) but only samples from p are available, use an approximate identity Kt,p r ≈ q with a “local” kernel kt and regularize in RKHS; again obtaining a closed-form linear system.

They call the resulting estimators FIRE. The paper analyzes concentration and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and on smooth compact manifolds, and proposes an unsupervised model selection criterion, CD-CV, that chooses bandwidth and regularization by minimizing a discrepancy between matched moments over random test functions under p and q. Experiments compare FIRE to LSIF, KMM (partially), TIKDE, and importance sampling on several datasets.

The formulation connects to and reframes prior work:
- Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) can be seen as the RKHS-norm version of the Kp r = Kq 1 identity. The paper’s RKHS-loss variant is a regularized linear-solver analogue of KMM.
- LSIF/KLIEP minimize divergence between r and a parametric family; FIRE differs by solving an operator equation with an explicit RKHS solution.
- The operator-theoretic angle relates to inverse problems and spectral regularization in learning; the manifold case connects to kernel integral operators on manifolds.

Strengths
- Clear operator-theoretic formulation that unifies several density-ratio estimators and yields simple, closed-form algorithms with standard kernel machinery.
- The Type II setting (known q, sample from p) is practically relevant for some Monte Carlo/MCMC contexts, and the approximate-identity derivation is elegant.
- Theoretical analysis provides finite-sample error bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings. Including the manifold case is a nice addition rarely addressed in density-ratio work.
- CD-CV is a practical, unsupervised selection criterion that leverages moment-matching without relying on basis approximation; easy to implement.
- Implementation is straightforward; the algorithms are ridge-regression-like and thus numerically routine.

Weaknesses
Quality
- The estimators do not enforce non-negativity or normalization of r(x). This can yield negative or arbitrarily large weights, which is problematic in importance weighting. Some experimental mitigation (e.g., clipping) is discussed only for TIKDE, not systematically for FIRE.
- Statistical optimality is not fully clarified. The obtained rates appear weaker than known minimax rates for likelihood ratio estimation (e.g., Nguyen et al.), and recommended choices of t and λ for rate-optimality are not distilled.
- The Type II approximation error term O(t) is acknowledged, but practical guidance on choosing t to balance approximation vs. estimation error is limited, and the dependence on dimension enters via td/2, potentially making selection ill-conditioned.
- The CD-CV criterion, while intuitive, lacks theoretical guarantees (e.g., consistency of hyperparameter selection, sensitivity to choice of test function family U, variance analysis).
- Claims about KMM lacking an out-of-sample extension are overstated. While KMM solves for weights at p-sample locations, this is sufficient for covariate shift correction in many learning pipelines; functional extension is also possible via additional modeling.

Clarity
- Generally well-written, but some notation is dense and occasionally inconsistent (e.g., switching between K, Kp, Kt, KH). A concise algorithm box with the exact matrices and computational complexity would help.
- Some assumptions are buried (boundedness of p and q, Sobolev smoothness of q/p and q/p^2). It would help to discuss their practical plausibility.
- Several choices (e.g., mixing L2,p/L2,q) are introduced but not clearly motivated experimentally.

Originality
- The Fredholm-equation framing and resulting linear system are a neat and reasonably novel perspective, though closely related to KMM and inverse-problem views of kernel learning. The manifold extension and Type II derivation add novelty.

Significance
- Potentially useful unifying viewpoint and a practically simple estimator. However, the empirical evaluation is modest in scope and does not convincingly demonstrate superiority over strong baselines (e.g., uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF, modern kernel mean matching variants). The absence of diagnostics on weight quality (e.g., effective sample size, variance of importance weights) is a missed opportunity.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean operator-theoretic approach with simple, closed-form estimators.
- Nontrivial theory, including manifold case.
- Practical unsupervised model selection heuristic.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical evidence and missing key baselines; no clear SOTA improvements.
- Lack of positivity/normalization constraints and analysis of their impact.
- CD-CV lacks theoretical justification.
- Some rates and practical guidance on tuning are unsatisfying; dependence on t and dimension is delicate.

Suggestions for improvement
- Enforce r(x) ≥ 0 and optional normalization with minimal bias, e.g., parametrize r = g^2 or r = exp(g), or add convex constraints and study their impact on theory and computation.
- Provide principled guidance for selecting t and λ (e.g., derive near-optimal schedules that balance approximation and estimation errors) and clarify achievable rates relative to known minimax results.
- Expand experiments: include uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF, recent KMM analyses, and report weight diagnostics (ESS, max weight, variance), performance under varying shift magnitude and dimensionality, and large-scale settings (possibly via random features or Nyström to reduce O(n^3) complexity).
- Add theory for CD-CV (at least concentration of the discrepancy and conditions under which it selects near-optimal hyperparameters) and study sensitivity to the choice and number of test functions.
- Clarify claims about KMM’s out-of-sample behavior and position FIRE fairly within that landscape.
- Provide pseudo-code, complexity analysis, and numerical stability tips (e.g., centering, scaling, regularization strength).
- Discuss when Type II is applicable in practice (e.g., unnormalized target densities in AIS/MCMC) and include a dedicated experiment.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper offers a nice reframing with some solid theory and practical ideas, but empirical validation and treatment of critical practical issues (positivity, tuning, baselines) are not yet at NeurIPS standard. I believe with stronger experiments, constraints handling, and better tuning guidance, this work could be competitive.

Confidence: Moderate.
