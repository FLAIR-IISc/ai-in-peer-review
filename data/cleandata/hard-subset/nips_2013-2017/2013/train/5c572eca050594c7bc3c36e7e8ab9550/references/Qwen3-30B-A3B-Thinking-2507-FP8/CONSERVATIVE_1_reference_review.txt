

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes reformulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it using Tikhonov regularization in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The authors introduce two types of formulations (Type I and Type II) and develop a method called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) for density ratio estimation. They provide theoretical error bounds for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings and propose a novel unsupervised model selection technique called CD-CV.

The paper builds upon well-established techniques in kernel methods and regularization. The connection to Fredholm equations is mathematically interesting, and the authors clearly articulate how their formulation relates to existing work, particularly Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF). The theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels in specific settings is technically sound, though limited in scope.

**Strengths:**
1. The mathematical formulation is clear and well-motivated, with proper connections to classical operator theory.
2. The theoretical analysis provides error bounds and convergence rates for specific cases (Gaussian kernels in Euclidean space/manifolds).
3. The paper acknowledges and properly contextualizes related work, particularly the connection to KMM.
4. The CD-CV model selection technique is novel and addresses an important practical challenge in unsupervised learning.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Novelty is limited:** The core approach is an application of established regularization techniques (Tikhonov regularization with RKHS norms) to a well-studied problem. The authors acknowledge this by stating their work "provides a flexible and principled framework, with a variety of different norms and regularization techniques available," but this doesn't constitute significant novelty. The connection to Fredholm equations is mathematically interesting but doesn't lead to substantially new insights or algorithms.

2. **Insufficient empirical validation:** The experimental section is weak. They use only two datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm) for regression and USPS for classification, which is insufficient for a NIPS paper. The comparison with baselines is limited to TIKDE, LSIF, and KMM (though they omit KMM in some experiments due to "out-of-sample extension" issues). Crucially, they don't compare with more recent methods like KLIEP or the more sophisticated importance weighting approaches that have emerged since the cited papers (e.g., [22, 23] are from 2010-2011, while more recent work like [26] on KMM is acknowledged but not directly compared).

3. **Theoretical limitations:** The theoretical results are restricted to Gaussian kernels and specific domain structures (Euclidean space, manifolds). The error bounds contain constants that are not explicitly characterized, making it difficult to assess practical implications. The Type II formulation explicitly contains an approximation error term (O(t)) that isn't fully addressed in the theoretical analysis.

4. **Lack of compelling evidence:** The paper claims "strong performance and better computational efficiency compared to the alternatives," but the experimental results in Table 1 don't convincingly support this claim. The error differences between methods are marginal (e.g., 0.1-0.2 in most cases), and the paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing or detailed analysis of when their method outperforms alternatives.

5. **Misleading claims about novelty:** The paper states "We provide a formulation of estimating the density ratio (importance function) as a classical inverse problem, known as the Fredholm equation, establishing a connections to the methods of classical analysis." However, this connection has been made before in [3, 20] as cited in the paper, and the authors' contribution is primarily the application of regularization techniques to this formulation rather than the formulation itself.

**Overall assessment:**
While the paper is technically sound and makes a clear mathematical contribution, it fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty or empirical advantage over established methods. The problem of density ratio estimation has been extensively studied, and the paper doesn't convincingly show that their approach provides meaningful improvements. The theoretical analysis is limited in scope, and the empirical validation is insufficient for a NIPS submission.

The paper's main contribution is a reformulation of an existing problem using Fredholm equations and applying standard regularization techniques. This is a reasonable approach but doesn't rise to the level of a significant contribution for NIPS, where we expect more substantial advances. The authors should either substantially strengthen the empirical validation with more comprehensive experiments against modern baselines or provide stronger theoretical justification for why their approach is superior in meaningful ways.

I recommend rejection. While the paper is well-written and technically sound, it presents an incremental improvement rather than a novel contribution that advances the state of the art in density ratio estimation. The lack of compelling evidence for superiority over existing methods and the limited scope of theoretical results make it difficult to justify acceptance for NIPS.
