

# Review: Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach

## Summary and Connection to Previous Work

This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation (the problem of estimating q(x)/p(x) where p is known and q is either a known function or another density) by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors establish a connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems in operator theory, solving them using Tikhonov-Phillips regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This leads to their "FIRE" (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) algorithms, which they theoretically analyze for Gaussian kernels in both Euclidean and manifold settings. The paper also introduces CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), an unsupervised model selection technique for parameter tuning.

The work connects to the growing literature on density ratio estimation (e.g., [9, 10, 22, 2]), particularly drawing comparisons to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF). Unlike previous methods that focus on specific optimization procedures, this paper provides a unified framework rooted in classical analysis, which is a significant conceptual contribution.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors carefully formulate the density ratio estimation problem as an inverse problem, derive the appropriate optimization problems (Types I and II), and provide a rigorous theoretical analysis with error bounds for both Euclidean and manifold settings. The proofs of convergence rates are detailed and appropriately referenced to the supplemental material.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing FIRE with multiple baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across several datasets and problem settings. The use of CD-CV for parameter selection is well-motivated and addresses a critical practical challenge in unsupervised learning. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the error term in Type II settings and the computational complexity of the algorithms.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical analysis to empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained. The connection between the Fredholm equation approach and existing methods (particularly KMM) is particularly well-articulated.

The main clarity issue is that the paper could better explain why the Fredholm equation approach provides practical advantages over existing methods beyond the theoretical framework. While the connection to classical analysis is intellectually appealing, the paper would benefit from more concrete examples of when this approach would be preferred over alternatives like LSIF or KMM in specific applications.

## Originality

The paper's main contribution is the novel reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation. This is a significant conceptual advance, as it establishes a connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems that has not been previously explored in the machine learning literature. The FIRE algorithms and CD-CV technique are direct consequences of this novel perspective.

The paper properly situates its contribution within the existing literature, noting how their approach differs from KMM (which uses RKHS norm but different optimization) and LSIF (which uses parametric families). The connection to spectral theory for theoretical analysis is also a fresh perspective.

## Significance

Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem with applications in transfer learning, covariate shift adaptation, and importance sampling. The FIRE framework provides a principled approach that unifies various existing methods while offering new theoretical guarantees. The CD-CV technique addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning (parameter selection without labeled data), which is particularly valuable for density ratio estimation where ground truth is often unavailable.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical performance compared to baselines, with computational efficiency advantages. The theoretical analysis for manifold settings is particularly significant as it extends the applicability of density ratio estimation to more complex data structures.

## Strengths

1. Novel theoretical perspective connecting density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems
2. Comprehensive theoretical analysis with error bounds for both Euclidean and manifold settings
3. Practical algorithms (FIRE) with clear implementation details
4. Innovative CD-CV technique for unsupervised parameter selection
5. Thorough empirical evaluation across multiple datasets and problem settings

## Weaknesses

1. The practical advantages of the Fredholm equation approach over existing methods could be better justified with more specific examples or comparative experiments
2. Computational complexity analysis is somewhat superficial (though the paper acknowledges this as a limitation)
3. The connection to manifold learning is not explored as deeply as it could be
4. The paper could better explain why the Type I and Type II formulations are necessary rather than just different approaches

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The theoretical framework is novel, well-justified, and provides new insights into an important problem. The empirical results are compelling and the CD-CV technique addresses a practical challenge in unsupervised learning.

The paper meets all the criteria for a NeurIPS submission: it's technically sound, well-written, original, and significant. The weaknesses identified are not deal-breakers but could be addressed in revision to strengthen the paper further.

I recommend acceptance. The paper provides a valuable theoretical perspective that will likely influence future work in density ratio estimation and related areas. The connection to classical analysis is particularly noteworthy and offers a fresh lens through which to view these problems.

**Final Recommendation: Accept**
