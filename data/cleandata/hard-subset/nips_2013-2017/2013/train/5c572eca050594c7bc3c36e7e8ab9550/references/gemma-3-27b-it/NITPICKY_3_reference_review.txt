## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by recasting it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and leveraging tools from regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors present two formulations (Type I & II) and propose algorithms, termed FIRE, along with a parameter selection method (CD-CV). While the connection to classical operator theory is interesting and the exploration of various norms and loss functions is commendable, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of substantial novelty in the algorithmic contributions, leading to a recommendation for rejection.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The central idea – framing density ratio estimation as solving a Fredholm equation – is clever. The authors correctly highlight connections to importance sampling and existing density ratio estimation techniques like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF). They accurately position their work within the context of covariate shift and selection bias. The attempt to apply spectral analysis and concentration inequalities to the problem is a positive aspect. However, the core concept of using integral equations to represent density ratios isn’t entirely novel; KMM, as the authors acknowledge, operates on a closely related principle. The novelty lies more in the specific regularization approach and the systematic exploration of different norms within the RKHS framework.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Foundation:** The attempt to provide a theoretical analysis, including concentration and error bounds, is a strong point. Although the derivation appears complex and may require careful verification, the intent is appreciated.
* **Flexibility:** The proposed framework is flexible, allowing for different norms and loss functions.
* **Connection to Established Theory:** Linking density ratio estimation to Fredholm equations provides a conceptual bridge to a well-developed field of mathematics.
* **CD-CV:** The unsupervised parameter selection method is a practical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Substantial Novelty:** While the formulation is interesting, the algorithms (FIRE) are, in essence, regularized kernel methods. The optimization problems, after applying the representer theorem, reduce to solving linear systems – a standard procedure in kernel learning. The “novelty” doesn’t justify a top-tier NIPS publication.
* **Excessive Detail & Notation:** The paper is *extremely* dense with notation. Equations are often repeated with minor variations, obscuring the core ideas. This impacts readability significantly. For instance, the numerous variations of Eq. 4 and the subsequent definitions of different FIRE algorithms feel overly verbose.
* **Inconsistent Terminology/Definitions:** The definition of "Type I" and "Type II" settings lacks clarity. It is stated that in Type II, `q` doesn't have to be a density, but subsequent discussions and formulations still implicitly treat it as such. The use of `Kt,p` and `Kt,q` is also inconsistent and confusing.
* **Minor Errors & Imprecisions:** Throughout the paper, there are minor mathematical inaccuracies and imprecisions. For example, the claim that the RKHS norm in KMM is not in the RKHS is incorrect; the weight function β *can* be represented in the RKHS, though it isn’t explicitly enforced. The notation for the kernel matrices also lacks consistency.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation (Section 4) is insufficient. The comparisons with TIKDE, LSIF, and KMM are not thoroughly justified. The choice of datasets is limited, and the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail (e.g., how parameters for TIKDE and LSIF were chosen). The reported results are not overwhelmingly convincing. Furthermore, the section's organization is poor and difficult to follow.
* **Missing Justification for Choices:** The choice of the Gaussian kernel is not adequately justified. Why is it preferred over other kernels, particularly in the theoretical analysis?
* **Redundancy:** The section on related work is repetitive and overly long. It lists numerous papers without providing a concise summary of their relevance to the presented work.
* **Formatting Issues:** The paper contains numerous minor formatting issues, including inconsistent spacing and hyphenation.

**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Against Acceptance:**

* The algorithmic contribution is incremental and doesn't represent a significant advancement in the field.
* The paper is overly dense and difficult to read due to excessive notation and redundancy.
* Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies detract from the paper's credibility.
* The empirical evaluation is weak and doesn’t provide strong evidence of the proposed method's superiority.

**For Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis, if verified, could be a valuable contribution.
* The framework’s flexibility is a positive aspect.
* The connection to classical operator theory provides a novel perspective.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the interesting premise and the attempt at a rigorous theoretical analysis, the paper’s numerous weaknesses outweigh its strengths. The lack of substantial novelty, combined with the excessive density, inconsistencies, and insufficient empirical validation, make it unsuitable for publication at NIPS. The authors would significantly benefit from simplifying the presentation, addressing the minor errors, and providing a more compelling empirical evaluation. I recommend **rejection**.
