This paper proposes a novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm equation, and leverages tools from regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to develop algorithms (FIRE) for solving it. While the connection to classical operator theory is interesting, and the formulation is mathematically sound, I find the paper ultimately lacking in substantial novelty and empirical validation justifying its complexity compared to established methods.

The core idea of reformulating density ratio estimation as solving a Fredholm equation, while mathematically valid, doesn’t immediately offer a clear advantage over existing approaches. The authors correctly identify connections to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), but present FIRE as offering improvements in stability and theoretical analysis. However, the presented theoretical analysis, while detailed, largely confirms known results (e.g., convergence rates dependent on sample size and kernel smoothness) and doesn’t reveal fundamentally new insights. The assumptions required for the theoretical guarantees (smoothness conditions, Gaussian kernels) are also quite restrictive.

The empirical evaluation is a major weakness. The comparison with TIKDE, KMM, and LSIF feels insufficient. The experimental setup, particularly the resampling strategies, needs more justification. Simply stating PCA resampling is used doesn’t explain *why* this specific method is appropriate or how it impacts the comparison. Moreover, the use of different validation and error-measuring function families (Table 1) is confusing and obscures the relative performance of FIRE.  It’s not immediately clear which configurations are most relevant or provide a fair comparison. The claim of improved computational efficiency isn’t substantiated with timings or resource usage. The reported performance improvements, where observed, are not dramatic enough to warrant adopting a more complex approach than well-established methods like KMM or LSIF. The CD-CV method for parameter selection is a positive contribution, but its effectiveness relies heavily on the choice of test functions (U), which seems somewhat arbitrary. 

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, but the mathematical notation is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.  The extensive list of references demonstrates awareness of the existing literature.

**Strengths:**

*   Mathematically sound formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
*   Connection to classical operator theory provides a different perspective.
*   The unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV) is a worthwhile contribution.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty; the core idea doesn’t fundamentally advance the state-of-the-art.
*   Insufficient empirical validation; comparisons lack depth and clarity.
*   Theoretical analysis largely confirms known results without significant new insights.
*   Restrictive assumptions for theoretical guarantees.
*   Complexity of the approach doesn't seem justified by the observed performance gains.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a mathematically rigorous framework for density ratio estimation.
*   The CD-CV method for parameter selection is potentially useful.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks substantial empirical evidence to demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The theoretical contributions are incremental and build upon known results.
*   The complexity of the approach doesn’t translate into significant performance gains.
*   The experimental setup and presentation of results are unclear and confusing.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. 

While the paper demonstrates a strong mathematical understanding and presents a novel perspective on density ratio estimation, it falls short of making a significant contribution to the field. The lack of compelling empirical results and the limited novelty of the theoretical analysis do not justify its inclusion in NIPS. The paper feels more like a preliminary exploration of a mathematical framework than a fully developed and validated algorithm with demonstrable advantages. I would encourage the authors to focus on more extensive empirical validation, including comparisons with a wider range of methods and a more thorough analysis of computational efficiency, before resubmitting.
