

# Peer Review: "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by framing it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which they solve using Tikhonov regularization with RKHS norm penalties. The authors develop two types of algorithms (Type I and Type II) for density ratio estimation and provide rigorous theoretical analysis with error bounds for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean space and on manifolds. They also introduce CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), an unsupervised parameter selection method.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Novelty and Soundness:** The core contribution—connecting density ratio estimation to Fredholm equations—represents a significant conceptual advance. By framing the problem within classical inverse problem theory, the authors provide a fresh perspective that bridges mathematical analysis and machine learning. Their theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with detailed error bounds and convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings across different domains (Euclidean space, manifolds). The proofs build upon established spectral theory, making the analysis both rigorous and accessible to the ML community.

**Algorithmic Simplicity and Flexibility:** The FIRE algorithms are elegantly simple to implement due to the representer theorem, yet they offer remarkable flexibility through the choice of norms (L2,p, L2,q, RKHS). The authors clearly demonstrate how their approach generalizes existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF, with Type I formulation with RKHS norm loss (Equation 10) being a natural regularized extension of KMM. The distinction between Type I (samples from both p and q) and Type II (q known at p-sampled points) is well-motivated and practically relevant.

**Practical Contribution - CD-CV:** The CD-CV method for unsupervised parameter selection addresses a critical challenge in density ratio estimation. Unlike previous approaches that require labeled data or rely on heuristic selection, CD-CV uses the importance sampling identity (Equation 1) to construct a validation measure that works without labels. This is a particularly valuable contribution that enhances the practical utility of their framework.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

**Limited Theoretical Scope:** While the Gaussian kernel analysis is thorough, the paper doesn't discuss how the results might extend to other kernels or how kernel choice affects the error bounds. This is a notable limitation since kernel selection is crucial in practice. A brief discussion of kernel selection implications would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

**Computational Complexity Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed comparison of computational complexity between FIRE and alternatives (KMM, LSIF). Given that the paper emphasizes practical implementation, this is a significant omission. For instance, the O(n^3) complexity of kernel matrix inversion is a key practical constraint that should be addressed.

**Type II Setting Practicality:** The Type II setting assumes q is known at the sample points from p. While the authors mention MCMC applications, they don't provide concrete examples where this assumption holds in practice. The O(t) error term for Gaussian kernels (Equation 14) needs more practical interpretation—how large does t need to be for reasonable accuracy?

**Experimental Validation:** The experiments are limited to three datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS), which is insufficient to fully validate the method's generality. The paper also doesn't provide a detailed comparison of computational time for the different methods, which is crucial for practical adoption.

**Comparison with KMM:** While the paper correctly identifies that KMM lacks natural out-of-sample extension, it doesn't sufficiently address the key difference: KMM solves a quadratic program over β (not in RKHS), while FIRE's Type I formulation with RKHS norm loss (Equation 10) is a regularized version of KMM. This connection could be explored more deeply to clarify the relationship between these approaches.

## Significance

Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem in transfer learning, covariate shift adaptation, and importance sampling. The authors' framework provides a theoretically grounded alternative to existing methods with clear practical advantages. The CD-CV method alone represents a significant contribution to unsupervised parameter selection in density estimation. The paper's ability to connect classical inverse problem theory with modern kernel methods creates a bridge that could inspire further work at this intersection.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong, technically sound paper that makes a clear theoretical contribution while providing practical algorithms. The connection to Fredholm equations is novel and well-executed, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. The FIRE algorithms are simple, flexible, and well-motivated. The CD-CV method addresses a critical practical challenge in unsupervised learning.

The minor weaknesses noted above don't detract from the paper's overall quality or significance. The authors have successfully bridged classical mathematical analysis with modern machine learning, creating a framework that is both theoretically sound and practically useful.

## Recommendation

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to density ratio estimation that advances both theory and practice. The theoretical insights are novel, the algorithms are well-motivated and simple, and the empirical results are convincing. The minor limitations identified are common in initial theoretical work and can be addressed in future extensions.

**Suggested Improvements for Final Version:**
1. Add a brief discussion of how the theoretical results might extend to other kernels
2. Include computational complexity analysis and timing comparisons
3. Provide more concrete examples of the Type II setting's practical utility
4. Expand the experimental section with additional datasets
5. Clarify the relationship between FIRE's Type I formulation with RKHS norm loss and KMM

This paper represents a valuable addition to the literature on density ratio estimation and inverse problems in machine learning, with potential to influence both theoretical developments and practical implementations in transfer learning and importance sampling applications.
