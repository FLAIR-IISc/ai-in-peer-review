This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques from the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) literature. The core idea is compelling – leveraging operator theory to provide a theoretical foundation and practical algorithms (FIRE) for a problem prevalent in transfer learning and related areas. The authors provide two formulations (Type I and Type II) catering to different data availability scenarios, theoretical analysis including convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, and an unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV).

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the mathematical derivations. The connection to classical operator theory is highlighted effectively, distinguishing this work from existing approaches like KMM and LSIF. The introduction does a good job of situating the work within the broader context of density ratio estimation and its applications. The presentation of the algorithms (FIRE) and the empirical formulations is clear, facilitating understanding of their implementation. The theoretical results, while focused on Gaussian kernels, provide a rigorous foundation for the proposed methodology. The inclusion of CD-CV is a practical contribution addressing a common challenge in unsupervised learning. The paper references relevant prior work, demonstrating an awareness of the existing literature.

However, the paper suffers from some limitations. The theoretical analysis, while valuable, is largely restricted to the Gaussian kernel. The extent to which the theoretical guarantees generalize to other kernels remains unclear. The experimental section, while comprehensive in terms of comparisons with existing methods, lacks a deeper investigation into the practical performance of FIRE across various datasets and settings. The parameters used for the baselines (TIKDE, LSIF, KMM) are not always clearly specified or tuned, making a fair comparison difficult. The choice of test functions in the CD-CV procedure can significantly influence the results, and the sensitivity to this choice isn’t thoroughly investigated. The ablation studies for the Type I versus Type II formulations are missing, making it difficult to understand which formulation works best in which scenarios.  The claim of improved computational efficiency isn't strongly supported by the provided experimental results – the reported timings could be more detailed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem provides a fresh perspective and connects the problem to a rich body of mathematical theory.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis, despite being limited to Gaussian kernels, establishes convergence rates and provides a solid foundation for the proposed algorithms.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the mathematical derivations are presented clearly.
*   **Potential Significance:** The FIRE algorithms, along with the CD-CV parameter selection method, offer a promising approach to density ratio estimation and could be valuable in various applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Generalization of Theory:** The theoretical results are primarily focused on the Gaussian kernel, limiting their general applicability.
*   **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The experimental section lacks a comprehensive investigation of FIRE's performance across diverse datasets and settings, and lacks detailed parameter tuning for the baselines.
*   **Missing Ablation Studies:** The paper does not compare the Type I and Type II formulations and thus doesn't provide guidance on which to use in what situation.
*   **Computational Efficiency Claim Not Fully Justified:** The evidence supporting the claim of improved computational efficiency is weak.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to density ratio estimation. The formulation as a Fredholm inverse problem is a valuable contribution, and the proposed algorithms show promise. However, the limited generalization of the theoretical results and the insufficient empirical validation raise concerns. While the paper reaches the NIPS quality threshold, it would benefit from more extensive experiments and a broader theoretical analysis.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept**.  The novelty and theoretical contributions are strong enough to warrant acceptance, but the authors should address the concerns regarding empirical validation and generalization of the theory in future work.  I recommend that the authors include additional experiments with other kernel functions and a more detailed comparison to existing methods with careful parameter tuning in the camera-ready version.
