Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses density-ratio estimation r(x)=q(x)/p(x) by recasting it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two formulations are proposed:

- Type I (two-sample): Kp r = Kq 1, where both Kp and Kq are kernel integral operators estimated from samples from p and q, respectively. This yields an RKHS-regularized, least-squares fit of Kp r to Kq 1 under L2,p (and variants), with closed-form solutions via the representer theorem.

- Type II (one-sample-with-values): Kt,p r ≈ q, using a δ-like kernel kt, so convolution under p approximates the identity. This allows learning r from a sample from p and function values q(xi), which may be available in MCMC or unnormalized density contexts.

The authors derive explicit matrix formulas (FIRE) for several losses (L2,p, mixtures of L2,p and L2,q, and RKHS norm), discuss connections to KMM (Gretton et al.) and LSIF/KLIEP (Sugiyama et al.), and argue that their formulation clarifies KMM as solving an operator equation in H and provides an out-of-sample extension and regularization. They present finite-sample bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and compact manifolds, using spectral analysis and concentration. They also introduce an unsupervised model selection scheme (CD-CV), which chooses kernel width t and regularization λ by minimizing discrepancies in moment-matching identities Ep[u]=Eq[u r] across random linear or indicator test functions. Experiments compare FIRE to TIKDE, LSIF (and discuss KMM), on USPS, CPUsmall, and Kin8nm for ratio estimation and covariate shift tasks.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: Casting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation and solving with RKHS Tikhonov regularization is elegant and connects the problem to classical operator theory, enabling spectral analysis and clear algorithmic derivations.

- Algorithmic simplicity: The resulting estimators are closed-form linear systems in kernel matrices; the out-of-sample extension is immediate. The explicit Type I/II taxonomy is helpful.

- Theory: Nonasymptotic bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and manifolds are valuable. The dependence on n, m, t, λ is made explicit, and the manifold treatment is a nontrivial extension.

- Model selection: The CD-CV criterion is a practical, unsupervised way to tune hyperparameters in ratio estimation, and it is well-motivated by the importance sampling identity.

- Empirical evaluation: Reasonable comparisons to LSIF and a KDE-based baseline; clear runtime advantages from closed-form solvers are plausible.

Weaknesses
- Empirical scope and baselines: Experiments are relatively small-scale and use classic datasets. Comparisons omit strong, widely-used baselines such as uLSIF/KuLSIF/RuLSIF variants, logistic-regression-based propensity modeling, and modern importance-weighted cross-validation (IWCV). KMM is discussed but not evaluated with an out-of-sample extension or with regularization, which weakens the empirical case.

- Constraints and stability: The estimator does not enforce non-negativity or normalization of r, which can produce negative or miscalibrated weights. This is a practical concern for downstream importance weighting and could be addressed with simple parametrizations (e.g., exponential link) or inequality constraints.

- Model selection justification: CD-CV is intuitive but lacks theoretical guarantees (e.g., conditions under which minimizing JCD correlates with L2,p error or target risk under covariate shift). Sensitivity to the test function family U, and leakage in cross-validation splits, are not deeply analyzed.

- Assumptions and rates: The theoretical results assume smoothness (Sobolev) and boundedness, and for Type II include an approximation error term ‖Kt,q1−q‖. It is not clear whether the rates are minimax-optimal in the regimes considered (cf. Nguyen et al. 2007). The influence of kernel-width t and its interaction with λ is somewhat opaque and could be clarified with oracle choices and practical guidance.

- Clarity and organization: Notation is heavy (Kp, Kt,p, KH, Kp,p, etc.), with occasional overloading; some editorial issues remain. Proofs are deferred; boundary-domain remarks are brief.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The operator-theoretic derivations and empirical estimators are correct, and the bounds follow standard regularization/operator learning techniques. Practical issues (positivity/normalization constraints, computational complexity O(n^3), and hyperparameter tuning) deserve more attention. Experiments support the claims but could be much stronger.

Clarity
Generally clear but dense. The paper would benefit from:
- Unifying notation and consistently distinguishing the operator kernel and the RKHS kernel, especially since the theory often sets them equal.
- A short schematic for Type I vs. Type II pipelines, with assumptions and available data.
- Clear statements on out-of-sample prediction, complexity, and numerical stability, plus implementation notes (e.g., regularization of kernel matrices, Nyström/random features).

Originality
Moderate. The operator view linking KMM to a Fredholm equation is insightful; Type II with δ-kernels and manifold rates add novelty. However, the core idea—RKHS-regularized least-squares fitting for density ratios—relates closely to LSIF/KuLSIF and prior inverse-problem perspectives in ML.

Significance
Potentially useful, especially as a unifying framework and for applications where q is known up to a constant (Type II). Impact would be strengthened by more comprehensive experiments and practical constraints.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, unifying operator-theoretic perspective with concrete, simple algorithms.
- Theoretical guarantees on Rd and manifolds.
- Practical unsupervised model selection method.
- Clear connection and differentiation from KMM/LSIF.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical validation and missing strong baselines and constraints crucial for practice.
- No positivity/normalization of weights; potential instability in downstream tasks.
- CD-CV lacks theoretical backing and robustness analysis.
- Some clarity/notation and editorial issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Enforce or discuss positivity/normalization (e.g., exponential parametrization, inequality-constrained QP, or post-hoc clipping plus reweighting).
- Include uLSIF/KuLSIF/RuLSIF, logistic propensity modeling, and regularized KMM with an out-of-sample extension as baselines. Report target risk under covariate shift with IWCV.
- Provide scalability via Nyström or random features and complexity/accuracy trade-offs.
- Offer theoretical or empirical support for CD-CV (consistency under simple models; sensitivity to U; ablation on function families).
- Clarify the interaction between t and λ; provide oracle choices and practical guidelines.
- Tighten notation and add a table summarizing settings, assumptions, and available losses.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper offers a solid and conceptually clean framework with theory and workable algorithms. While the empirical section and practical considerations could be stronger, the contributions are likely of interest to the NeurIPS community and can be improved in revision.
