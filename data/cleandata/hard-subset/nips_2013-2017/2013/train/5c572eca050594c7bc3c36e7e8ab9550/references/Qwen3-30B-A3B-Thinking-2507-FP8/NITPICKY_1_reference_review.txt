

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by formulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, solved via regularization in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors introduce two formulations (Type I and Type II) for different settings and develop the FIRE algorithm. The paper claims theoretical error bounds for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings and proposes a novel unsupervised parameter selection method called CD-CV.

## Strengths
The paper makes a valuable connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems, providing a theoretically grounded framework. The FIRE algorithm is well-motivated and leads to explicit, implementable solutions. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive for the Gaussian kernel case, and the CD-CV method addresses an important practical challenge in unsupervised learning.

## Major Technical Issues

1. **Notational inconsistencies and errors**: The paper contains fundamental notational errors that undermine its technical rigor. In Equation (1), the expression "Ep(h(x) q(x) p(x))" should be "Ep(h(x) q(x)/p(x))" - the parentheses are misplaced, and the division is incorrectly written. Similarly, Equation (2) contains inconsistent notation with "q(y) p(y) p(y)" which should be "q(y)/p(y)".

2. **Theoretical gaps**: Theorems 1 and 3 claim error bounds, but the constants C1, C2, etc. are not defined. The manifold case in Theorem 1 references an undefined ε (0 < ε < 1) and the exponent in Corollary 2 appears to contain a typographical error: "n^(-1/(3.5(1-ε)+d/2))" should likely be "n^(-1/(3.5(1-ε)) + d/2)" or similar. More critically, the paper states "‖Kt,q1− q‖2,p = O(t)" without proof, which is essential to the error bounds but not properly justified.

3. **Algorithmic notation**: The paper uses ambiguous notation throughout, such as "K2p,p" in Equation (8) (is this (Kp,p)^2 or Kp,p composed with itself?), and "K2KH" in the Type II setting. These notational inconsistencies make the algorithms difficult to implement and reproduce.

4. **Theoretical scope**: The paper claims to provide "a detailed theoretical analysis for the case of the Gaussian kernel," but fails to discuss whether the approach generalizes to other kernels, which is a significant limitation for a kernel-based method.

## Minor Issues

1. **Experimental presentation**: The paper references "TIKDE" without a clear definition, only providing a vague footnote. The threshold α used in TIKDE is not specified, making it impossible to reproduce the comparison. Table 1 reports results with "N" as the number of random functions but doesn't specify the actual values used.

2. **Related work**: The connection to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) is mentioned but not thoroughly developed. The paper claims to be the first to formulate density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation, but this appears to be an overstatement given prior work on integral equation approaches to density estimation.

3. **Formatting inconsistencies**: The references have inconsistent formatting (e.g., "ICML, ," with extra commas), and the notation "L2,p" is used without consistent definition.

4. **Experimental details**: The paper lacks standard deviations for experimental results, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the claimed performance improvements.

## Recommendation

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations, the numerous technical errors, notational inconsistencies, and theoretical gaps undermine its contribution. The paper would benefit from a careful rewrite to address these issues before being considered for publication. The theoretical claims are not sufficiently supported, and the experimental results lack sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The paper's theoretical framework is promising, but the current presentation contains too many technical flaws to be considered a strong contribution. The authors should address the notational inconsistencies, provide proper proofs for the asymptotic claims, and clarify the experimental setup before resubmission.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial technical flaws that prevent the reader from confidently evaluating the paper's claims. The theoretical analysis contains critical gaps, and the experimental presentation lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. While the core idea has potential, the current version does not meet NIPS's standards for technical rigor and clarity.
