## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper attempts to recast density ratio estimation as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm equations and regularization techniques from the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors propose two formulations (Type I & II) and derive algorithms (FIRE) based on Tikhonov regularization. Theoretical analysis, including convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, is presented, alongside an unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV) and empirical evaluation.

While the idea of framing density ratio estimation through the lens of inverse problems and operator theory is interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, ultimately undermining its impact and making acceptance problematic. The presentation is dense and often lacks sufficient motivation for certain choices.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Perspective:** The connection to Fredholm integral equations provides a novel perspective on density ratio estimation, potentially opening avenues for leveraging tools from functional analysis.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The effort to provide theoretical guarantees, even under restrictive assumptions (Gaussian kernel, smoothness conditions), is commendable.
*   **Algorithm Flexibility:** The proposed FIRE framework is flexible, accommodating different norms and loss functions.
*   **Unsupervised Parameter Selection:** The CD-CV method addresses a crucial issue in unsupervised learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Clarity & Motivation:** The introduction, while outlining the goal, feels rushed. The justification for choosing the kernel approach *specifically* as a route to solving the Fredholm equation isn't fully explained. Why is this framing inherently advantageous? The paper repeatedly *states* connections to classical analysis, but doesn't convincingly demonstrate how this translates to practical benefits over existing methods.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** The core idea – applying regularization to an integral equation formulation of density ratio estimation – feels incremental. Many kernel methods implicitly rely on solving inverse problems; the novelty lies in the specific framing, but the gains aren't immediately apparent.
*   **Overly Detailed and Repetitive:** The paper is excessively detailed, with significant overlap in the presentation of Type I and Type II settings. Mathematical notation is frequently repeated without substantial change, adding to the density. The inclusion of all derivations of the algorithms is unnecessary in a conference paper.
*   **Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions (smoothness, Gaussian kernel) that limit its generalizability. The error bounds, while presented, are difficult to interpret in practice without further context. The dependence on *n* in the error bounds feels high, and not competitive with other approaches.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is weak. The parameter search space is limited. The comparison to baselines (TIKDE, LSIF, KMM) is insufficient. The description of the resampling methods (PCA, label-based) is vague. The error tables are difficult to interpret without more detailed analysis of the results and ablation studies. The claims of computational efficiency are not substantiated. It is unclear what the choice of functions U1 and U2 represent for the evaluation.
*   **Formatting & Typos:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent spacing around equations, missing punctuation) and typos are present throughout the paper. Equation (1) is incomplete. The remark about L2,q norm is followed by a '1.' which makes it look like the start of a numbered list.
*   **Unnecessary Complexity:** Introducing multiple norms and variations within the FIRE framework (e.g., γL2,p + (1-γ)L2,q) adds complexity without a clear justification for their benefits.

**Specific Concerns:**

*   The justification for approximating the integral operator Kp with a discrete sum is weak. Concentration inequalities are mentioned but not explicitly used to bound the error of the approximation.
*   The connection between KMM and the Type I formulation is stated but not fully explored. The differences in optimization procedures aren't sufficiently highlighted.
*   The claim that the FIRE algorithms can be implemented as linear combinations of kernels is not new and applies to many RKHS-based methods.
*   The practical implications of the theoretical results are not discussed. For example, how do the convergence rates guide the choice of regularization parameter λ?

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Provides a novel perspective on density ratio estimation.
*   Offers theoretical analysis, albeit under strong assumptions.
*   Proposes a flexible algorithm framework.

**Con:**

*   Incremental contribution.
*   Lack of clarity and motivation.
*   Weak empirical evaluation.
*   Numerous minor flaws in presentation and formatting.
*   Overly detailed and repetitive.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting idea, its execution is flawed. The lack of clarity, weak empirical evaluation, and numerous minor issues outweigh the novelty of the approach. The theoretical contribution, while valuable, is limited by strong assumptions.  The paper feels more like an exploration of a potential direction than a polished, impactful contribution to the field.



**Recommendation:** **Reject**. The paper requires substantial revision and a stronger empirical validation before it can be considered for publication in a venue like NIPS. A more focused presentation, a clearer articulation of the benefits of the proposed approach, and a more thorough experimental evaluation are essential.
