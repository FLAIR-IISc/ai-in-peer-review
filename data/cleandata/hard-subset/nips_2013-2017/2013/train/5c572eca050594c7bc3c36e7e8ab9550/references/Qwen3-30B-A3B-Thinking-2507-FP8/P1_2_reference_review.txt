

# Peer Review: "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation (q(x)/p(x)) by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, a classical inverse problem. The authors establish a connection between density ratio estimation and operator theory, enabling them to apply regularization techniques within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). They develop two algorithms, FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), for Type I (when samples from both p and q are available) and Type II (when q(x) is known at sample points from p) settings. The paper provides theoretical error bounds for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and on manifolds, and proposes a new unsupervised model selection method called CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation).

This work relates to previous NIPS contributions on density ratio estimation, particularly Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10]. While KMM can be viewed as a special case of their Type I formulation (with RKHS norm as loss), the authors' approach provides a more principled regularization framework with theoretical guarantees.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty**: The paper's core contribution—formulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation—is intellectually significant. This connection to classical inverse problem theory provides a fresh perspective that unifies and extends existing approaches. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, providing error bounds and convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold settings.

2. **Algorithmic Contribution**: The FIRE algorithms are well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and practically implementable. The Type I and Type II settings address complementary scenarios in density ratio estimation, with the Type II setting being particularly valuable when sampling from q is infeasible.

3. **Model Selection**: The CD-CV method for parameter selection is a significant practical contribution that addresses a key challenge in unsupervised learning. It's cleverly designed to leverage the unique properties of density ratio estimation.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The paper provides thorough experimental results comparing FIRE with multiple baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across regression and classification tasks. The results consistently show competitive performance, and the error analysis using different test functions demonstrates the robustness of the approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Accessibility**: The paper assumes substantial familiarity with Fredholm equations and RKHS theory, which may make it less accessible to a broader NIPS audience. A brief introduction to these concepts would improve readability without sacrificing technical depth.

2. **Type II Limitation**: The Type II setting has an inherent error term O(t) from the kernel approximation, which is acknowledged but not thoroughly explored in the experimental section. It would be valuable to see how this error affects performance in practice, especially compared to Type I.

3. **Kernel Selection**: The paper focuses primarily on Gaussian kernels, which is understandable for the theoretical analysis. However, the experimental section doesn't explore how the method performs with other kernel choices, which could limit its practical applicability.

4. **Comparison with Related Work**: While the paper references KMM and LSIF, the comparison could be strengthened by discussing how the FIRE framework relates to other recent approaches like KLIEP [23] and the theoretical connections to [26] (which analyzes KMM) could be elaborated.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The experimental design is appropriate, and the results support the claims. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., Type II's error term, the need for specific smoothness assumptions).

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with precise mathematical formulations. The main ideas are presented logically. However, the introduction could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why Fredholm equations are relevant to density ratio estimation.

**Originality**: The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is novel, providing a fresh perspective on an established problem. The theoretical analysis for manifold settings extends previous work in this area.

**Significance**: Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem in transfer learning and covariate shift adaptation. The paper provides a principled framework that unifies and extends existing approaches, with theoretical guarantees and practical algorithms. The CD-CV method addresses a key practical challenge in unsupervised learning. The results demonstrate strong performance compared to existing methods, suggesting the approach will be valuable to practitioners.

## Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The theoretical framework is novel and well-supported, the algorithms are practical, and the experimental results are convincing. While the paper could benefit from slightly more accessibility for a broader audience, the technical depth is appropriate for NIPS.

The paper advances the state of the art in density ratio estimation by providing a principled framework that connects to classical inverse problem theory, with concrete algorithms and theoretical guarantees. The CD-CV method is particularly valuable for addressing a key challenge in unsupervised learning.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. The work is of high quality, original, and significant, and the paper is well-written and technically sound.

## Final Assessment

*Quality*: Strong - Theoretical and experimental results are well-supported.

*Clarity*: Good - Well-organized with precise mathematical formulations, though could be more accessible.

*Originality*: High - Novel connection to Fredholm equations and inverse problem theory.

*Significance*: High - Addresses a fundamental problem with practical implications for transfer learning and importance sampling.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and deserves to be published at NIPS.
