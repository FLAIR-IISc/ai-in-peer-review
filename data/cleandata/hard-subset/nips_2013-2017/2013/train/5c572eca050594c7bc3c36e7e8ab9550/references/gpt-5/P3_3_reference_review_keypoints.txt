- Proposal: Cast density-ratio estimation r(x)=q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm first-kind integral equation and solve via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding FIRE algorithms for two settings (Type I: samples from p and q; Type II: q known/evaluable, samples from p).  
- Implementation: Replace integrals with sample averages and use the representer theorem to obtain closed-form kernel solutions with natural out-of-sample extensions.  
- Theory: Provide non-asymptotic concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean domains and smooth manifolds using spectral techniques and heat-kernel approximations.  
- Model selection: Propose CD-CV, an unsupervised hyperparameter selection that minimizes violations of the importance-sampling equality over a family of test functions.  
- Relation to prior work: Closely connected to KMM and LSIF/KLIEP; Type II relates to convolution/heat-kernel approximations and conditional mean embeddings.  
- Missing references: Should cite direct ratio estimators like uLSIF/RuLSIF and MMD-based two-sample tests to better position CD-CV.  
- Strength — conceptual: The Fredholm/inverse-problem framing is elegant and unifies prior methods under an operator-theoretic view.  
- Strength — algorithmic: Closed-form kernel solutions are simple, practical, and offer out-of-sample prediction unlike KMM’s weight-only QP.  
- Strength — theory: Nontrivial theoretical analysis on both Euclidean domains and manifolds broadens applicability.  
- Strength — model selection: CD-CV is a fully unsupervised selection mechanism aligned with the importance-weighting objective.  
- Strength — flexibility: Framework accommodates multiple loss norms and both sampled-q and evaluable-q scenarios.  
- Weakness — constraints: Estimators are unconstrained (no nonnegativity or normalization), risking negative or poorly scaled weights that harm downstream tasks.  
- Weakness — experiments: Limited empirical comparison to KMM and uLSIF, no synthetic ground-truth ratio experiments, and missing runtime/memory benchmarks.  
- Weakness — CD-CV: Validation depends on chosen test-function family U with little guidance/theory on sensitivity or the number of functions needed.  
- Weakness — theory/rates: Derived rates (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2)) look conservative/unusual; assumptions (like q/p2 ∈ W2^2) and λ−1/6 factors are not fully explained or justified.  
- Weakness — presentation: Notational ambiguity (empirical operators), typos, and unclear kernel roles reduce readability.  
- Quality: Methodologically sound and plausible spectral arguments, but practical impact limited by missing constraints and narrow empirical evaluation; theoretical rates useful but possibly suboptimal.  
- Clarity: Good high-level exposition but dense technical sections; needs clearer notation, an algorithm box, positivity/normalization discussion, and computational-complexity details.  
- Originality: Moderately high — the inverse-problem lens and manifold-aware analysis are novel despite similarities to KMM/LSIF.  
- Significance: Potentially influential if strengthened, but current significance limited by empirical breadth and omission of practical constraints.  
- Suggestion: Add Ep[f]=1 constraint or positivity-enforcing parameterizations (e.g., exponential link) and discuss projection approaches.  
- Suggestion: Empirically compare to KMM (with/without positivity), uLSIF, and KDE baselines on synthetic and real covariate-shift tasks, reporting runtime and memory.  
- Suggestion: Consider MMD-based CD-CV with a characteristic kernel for a more universal validation criterion and analyze its consistency.  
- Suggestion: Address computational scaling (O(n^3)) and provide Nyström/sketching or early-stopping/spectral regularization alternatives.  
- Suggestion: Clarify theoretical roles of assumptions (e.g., q/p2 ∈ W2^2) and discuss optimality/tightness of rates.  
- Pros for acceptance: Elegant unifying framework, solid theory on Euclidean/manifolds, and a practical unsupervised model-selection idea.  
- Cons for acceptance: Missing positivity/normalization handling, limited/unfair experiments, CD-CV lacks theory, and clarity/notation issues.  
- Recommendation: Borderline reject (leaning reject) — strong ideas and theory but needs stronger empirical validation, constraint treatment, and clearer presentation.  
- Reviewer confidence: High.
