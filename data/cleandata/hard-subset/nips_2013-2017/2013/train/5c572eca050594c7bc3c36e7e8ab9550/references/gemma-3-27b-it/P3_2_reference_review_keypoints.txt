- Reformulates density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation, connecting DRE to operator theory and RKHS.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis: concentration inequalities, error bounds, and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings.  
- Introduces a flexible FIRE framework supporting different norms, losses, kernels, and two data-availability formulations (Type I and Type II).  
- Proposes CD-CV, an unsupervised cross-density cross-validation method for parameter selection.  
- Carefully positions the work relative to KMM, LSIF, and related transfer-learning approaches, noting KMM’s out-of-sample and stability limitations.  
- Generally well-written and logically organized, with a motivating introduction.  
- Computational complexity concerns: solving the integral equations (matrix inversions) may not scale to large datasets and lacks detailed discussion of approximations.  
- Practical performance gains over KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE appear marginal in reported experiments; more extensive empirical validation is needed.  
- Theoretical results are largely restricted to Gaussian kernels; extension to other kernels would strengthen the contribution.  
- CD-CV implementation details (choice and sensitivity of test functions like linear or half-space) are insufficiently explored.  
- Some dense and hard-to-follow sections (notably Section 2) would benefit from clearer, more concise explanations.  
- Comment on L2,q norm in Type I: need expansion on how sampling from q affects practical performance and bias.  
- Type II formulation is a strength because it applies when q is not necessarily a density, broadening applicability.  
- Key theorem constants (C1, C2, etc.) are not fully characterized; their dependence on problem parameters should be clarified.  
- CD-CV needs ablation studies to analyze impact of different test-function families and validation schemes.  
- Table 1 is cluttered and unclear (e.g., what 'N' denotes); highlight best methods per setting for readability.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded approach with significant analytical contributions and useful unsupervised parameter selection.  
- Arguments for rejection: insufficient practical advantage to justify added complexity, unaddressed scalability, and limited kernel-theory scope.  
- Overall: reviewer leans toward strong accept conditional on addressing computational complexity concerns and providing more compelling empirical evidence.
