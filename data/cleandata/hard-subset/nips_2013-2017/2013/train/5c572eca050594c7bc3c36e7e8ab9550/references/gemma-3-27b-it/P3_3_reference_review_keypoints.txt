- Frames density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral inverse problem solved with Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS (FIRE).
- Strong theoretical foundation connecting operator theory and RKHS, yielding convergence rates and error bounds.
- Results for Gaussian kernels are particularly strong under the paper's assumptions.
- Provides a flexible framework supporting different kernels, norms (L2,p, RKHS), and loss functions.
- Introduces an unsupervised cross-validation method (CD-CV) for parameter selection.
- Clear, well-organized presentation and accessible mathematical derivations.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope and datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) and doesn’t clearly beat state-of-the-art.
- Comparisons to baselines (TIKDE, Importance Sampling, KMM, LSIF) are not consistently favorable or decisive.
- Scalability concerns due to kernel matrix inversions are not sufficiently addressed.
- Theoretical results depend on restrictive assumptions (density smoothness, Gaussian kernel) with unclear robustness to violations.
- Performance sensitive to regularization parameter λ and kernel width t; CD-CV’s robustness in high dimensions is uncertain.
- Practical relevance is not convincingly demonstrated; improvements may be insufficient to drive adoption.
- Requests for experiments on higher-dimensional or more challenging covariate-shift datasets to test applicability.
- Suggests exploring approximations for kernel inversions (e.g., Nyström) to improve scalability.
- Asks for analysis of CD-CV sensitivity to choice of test functions and its computational scaling.
- Wants clearer guidance on when to use Type I vs Type II formulations and their relative advantages.
- Pro: novel inverse-problem formulation, strong theory, flexible RKHS framework, and practical CD-CV idea.
- Con: limited empirical validation, scalability issues, and potentially restrictive theoretical assumptions.
- Recommendation: weak accept contingent on expanded experiments, scalability analysis, and stronger practical demonstrations.
