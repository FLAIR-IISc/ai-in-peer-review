Summary
This paper tackles density-ratio estimation q(x)/p(x) by recasting it as an inverse problem—specifically, a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind—and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two formulations are proposed: Type I uses samples from both p and q and enforces Kp(q/p) = Kq1; Type II assumes q is known (or its values are known at the p-sample) and uses a δ-kernel approximation Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q. Using the representer theorem, both yield simple linear-algebraic estimators (FIRE). The paper analyzes convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and on compact manifolds, derives concentration bounds, and proposes an unsupervised model selection scheme (CD-CV) based on cross-density moment matching with random test functions. Experiments compare FIRE to LSIF, KMM (partially), and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline on standard covariate shift setups.

Relation to prior work
The work relates closely to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), LSIF/KLIEP, and operator-theoretic RKHS methods. The authors correctly highlight that KMM can be seen as enforcing an integral identity in H, while FIRE regularizes an operator inversion in H and produces a natural out-of-sample function; they also connect to inverse problem regularization (Engl et al.) and related operator inversion views (e.g., Smola & Schölkopf, De Vito et al.). The Type II setting (when q is known pointwise) aligns with scenarios in MCMC/unnormalized models. The CD-CV criterion is reminiscent of importance-weighted cross-validation and moment matching, but used solely for validation rather than as the modeling basis.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual framing: Casting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and unifies the task with a rich body of operator theory and regularization. This perspective feels genuinely fresh relative to purely empirical risk formulations.
- Algorithmic simplicity and flexibility: Closed-form, representer-theorem solutions; supports different loss norms (L2,p, L2,q, mixtures, RKHS norm) with simple matrix expressions; a clean out-of-sample function unlike standard KMM.
- Theoretical analysis: Nonasymptotic error bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels, including manifold support. The spectral/operator lens provides a principled analytic toolkit.
- Practical contribution: An unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV) tailored to this setting; a Type II variant that handles non-density q and unnormalized models.
- Clarity of connections: The paper helpfully shows how KMM arises as a special case/limit of this operator equation viewed in H, and positions FIRE as a regularized alternative.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is somewhat limited: Results include USPS/CPUsmall/Kin8nm under synthetic shift but miss broader benchmarks (e.g., covariate shift suites), off-policy evaluation, or unnormalized model scenarios where Type II should shine. The KMM comparison is incomplete (“no out-of-sample”)—in practice, out-of-sample can be handled (e.g., via Nyström, random features), so a fairer comparison would strengthen the case.
- Positivity/normalization: The estimator can produce negative or non-normalized ratios. There is little discussion of post-hoc correction, constraints, or impact on downstream weighted learners. This contrasts with many density-ratio methods that enforce positivity.
- Theory scope and sharpness: Assumptions (Sobolev smoothness of q/p and q/p^2; Gaussian kernel; matching kernel for operator and H) are somewhat restrictive; derived rates look conservative and depend on nontransparent conditions (e.g., m > λ^(1/3) n). The role of the δ-kernel bias (O(t)) in Type II is acknowledged but not fully optimized in the tuning discussion.
- CD-CV justification: The selection criterion is intuitive but lacks theoretical guarantees (consistency, robustness to the choice of test functions). Sensitivity to the choice/distribution of test functions is only empirically probed. A connection to MMD-based validation or importance-weighted CV (Sugiyama et al., 2007) could be elaborated.
- Presentation: The manuscript is notation-heavy and occasionally rough (typos, long equations in prose). The distinction between kernels k and kH and their roles sometimes blurs; the empirical risk definitions could be streamlined.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with reasonable derivations and careful links to operator theory. The bounds appear plausible though not necessarily tight; proofs are deferred. The experimental section supports the claims but would benefit from stronger baselines and broader scenarios.

Clarity
Generally understandable, but the paper would benefit from tightening notation, separating intuition from formalism, and improving organization (e.g., collect all empirical matrix formulas; provide a concise algorithm box for each variant; clarify when k = kH). A brief discussion on enforcing positivity and normalization would improve practical clarity.

Originality
Good. The Fredholm inverse problem perspective for density ratio estimation, the Type II setting, and the manifold-aware rates give this a distinctive flavor beyond KMM/LSIF. The CD-CV idea is incremental but useful.

Significance
Moderate to high. Density ratios are central in covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, and likelihood-free inference. Providing a principled operator-regularized estimator with theory and a practical tuning method can influence both research and practice.

Pros
- Conceptually bold reformulation tying density ratios to classical inverse problems.
- Simple, general, and extensible algorithms with out-of-sample functions.
- Theoretical rates including manifold supports.
- Practical unsupervised hyperparameter selection.

Cons
- Limited and partially unfair empirical comparisons (esp. KMM).
- No positivity/normalization constraints or ablation on their impact.
- Theory under specific kernels and somewhat strong smoothness assumptions; rates not clearly optimal.
- CD-CV lacks theoretical backing; sensitivity to test functions not fully explored.

Questions for authors
- Can you enforce nonnegativity/normalization in FIRE without losing closed-form solvability (e.g., via projection or exponential link)?
- How does FIRE compare to KMM with standard out-of-sample extensions (Nyström/features) in accuracy and runtime?
- Any experiments for Type II on unnormalized models (e.g., AIS/tempered posteriors)?
- Can you relate CD-CV to MMD and give conditions under which it is a consistent selector?

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a compelling and novel operator-theoretic lens on density-ratio estimation, yields practical algorithms with theory, and proposes a useful tuning heuristic. Despite some experimental and presentational gaps, the conceptual contribution is strong enough to merit inclusion, and the shortcomings are addressable.
