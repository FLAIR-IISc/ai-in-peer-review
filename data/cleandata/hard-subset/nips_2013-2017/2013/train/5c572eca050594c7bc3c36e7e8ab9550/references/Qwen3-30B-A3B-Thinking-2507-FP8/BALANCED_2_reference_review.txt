

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation problem, which the authors solve using regularization techniques within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors develop two main algorithms (Type I and Type II) called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), provide theoretical convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, and introduce an unsupervised parameter selection method called CD-CV.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

Density ratio estimation has been extensively studied in machine learning, particularly in the context of covariate shift and transfer learning. The paper correctly identifies related work including Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10]. The authors' key insight is recognizing that density ratio estimation can be framed as an inverse problem (Fredholm equation of the first kind), which connects it to classical operator theory. This is a significant theoretical contribution that provides a unified perspective on existing approaches, particularly KMM, which the authors show can be viewed as a special case of their Type I formulation. The paper also relates to broader work on kernel methods in statistical estimation [21, 17, 16].

## Strengths

1. **Novel Theoretical Framework**: The paper's main contribution is the elegant reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation problem. This connection to classical operator theory is insightful and provides a principled way to analyze the problem.

2. **Comprehensive Theoretical Analysis**: The paper provides detailed convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold settings, which is rare for density ratio estimation methods. The bounds in Theorems 1 and 3 are rigorous and address important cases beyond just the standard Euclidean setting.

3. **Practical Algorithms**: The FIRE algorithms are simple, explicit, and directly implementable using standard kernel methods. The authors derive closed-form solutions using the representer theorem, making the approach computationally feasible.

4. **Novel Parameter Selection Method**: The CD-CV approach for unsupervised parameter selection is a valuable practical contribution that addresses a significant challenge in density ratio estimation.

5. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of FIRE compared to KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE across multiple datasets and problem types (regression and classification).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to Key Baselines**: While the paper compares with KMM and LSIF, it doesn't adequately address how their approach differs from other important methods like KLIEP [23] or the recently proposed methods in [26]. The relationship to these works needs more thorough discussion.

2. **Lack of Practical Guidance**: The paper doesn't provide clear guidance on how to choose the kernel type (beyond Gaussian) or how to handle high-dimensional data where the manifold assumption might not hold. The theoretical results assume specific smoothness conditions that might be difficult to verify in practice.

3. **Experimental Depth**: The experimental section would benefit from more detailed analysis of the parameter sensitivity (e.g., how CD-CV performs across different data distributions) and computational complexity comparisons. The paper claims "better computational efficiency" but doesn't quantify this.

4. **Manifold Analysis Gap**: While the theoretical analysis covers manifold settings, the experiments are limited to Euclidean spaces (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS). It would strengthen the paper to include at least one experiment on a non-Euclidean dataset where manifold assumptions might be valid.

5. **Theoretical Assumptions**: The Sobolev space assumptions in the theoretical analysis (Theorem 1) are quite strong and might not hold in many practical applications. The paper could better discuss the practical implications of these assumptions.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that provides a novel perspective on density ratio estimation. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of density ratio estimation. The FIRE algorithms are elegant and well-motivated, and the CD-CV method is a practical advancement.

The main weaknesses are the limited comparison to key baselines, insufficient discussion of practical implementation considerations, and lack of experiments on manifold data. While the theoretical results are impressive, the paper would be stronger with more detailed guidance on practical application and a deeper exploration of the empirical performance across a wider range of settings.

## Recommendations for Improvement

1. Expand the comparison section to include a more thorough discussion of how the proposed approach relates to and differs from KLIEP [23] and the recent theoretical analysis of KMM in [26].

2. Add a subsection discussing practical considerations for implementing the method, including guidance on kernel selection and handling high-dimensional data.

3. Include at least one experiment on a dataset where the manifold assumption is likely to hold (e.g., data on a known manifold like the sphere) to validate the theoretical results for manifold settings.

4. Provide more detailed analysis of the parameter sensitivity in the experiments, particularly how CD-CV performs across different data distributions.

5. Add a discussion of the practical limitations of the theoretical assumptions (e.g., Sobolev space requirements) and how they might be relaxed in practice.

## Final Recommendation

The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of density ratio estimation with a novel perspective that connects to classical operator theory. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and rigorous, and the proposed algorithms are well-motivated and effective in practice. While the paper has some weaknesses in experimental depth and practical guidance, the theoretical contribution is substantial and would be valuable to the NIPS community.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points above, particularly expanding the comparison to key baselines and adding more practical guidance for implementation. The paper makes a clear contribution to the theoretical understanding of density ratio estimation and provides a unified framework that could influence future work in this area.
