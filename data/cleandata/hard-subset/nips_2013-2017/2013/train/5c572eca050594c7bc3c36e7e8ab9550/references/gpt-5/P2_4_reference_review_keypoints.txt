- Proposes FIRE: kernel-based density-ratio estimators by casting q/p estimation as a Fredholm integral equation solved with Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS.  
- Treats two settings: Type I (samples from p and q; solve Kp(q/p)=Kq1) and Type II (q known pointwise on p-samples; use δ-kernel formulation).  
- Uses the representer theorem to obtain closed-form linear systems over kernel matrices for the estimators.  
- Introduces CD-CV, an unsupervised hyperparameter selection method using the identity Ep(u q/p)=Eq(u) over random test functions.  
- Provides concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean domains and manifolds.  
- Empirically compares against KMM, LSIF, and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline on datasets like USPS, CPUsmall, and Kin8nm.  
- Clarifies relation to prior work: connects to KMM and LSIF and frames KMM as solving a related integral equation in H.  
- Notes novelty: Type II setting and unsupervised CD-CV are less common and are novel contributions.  
- Theory is principled and provides finite-sample bounds, but corollary rates are unusual and not contextualized against minimax results.  
- Theoretical assumptions are strong (bounded densities, Sobolev regularity, same Gaussian kernel for operator and RKHS) and differ from algorithmic flexibility.  
- Important constants and optimal tuning choices for t and λ are not specified.  
- Positivity and normalization of estimated ratios are not enforced, which can harm importance weighting stability.  
- Algorithms are simple and closed-form, with sensible extensions (mixed norms, RKHS loss), but computational cost is O(n^3) with dense kernels.  
- No discussion of scalability techniques (Nyström, random features, iterative solvers), limiting practical impact on large data.  
- CD-CV is an interesting unsupervised idea, but its sensitivity to test-function choices and hyperparameter grids is not analyzed.  
- Experiments are promising but lack runtime/memory benchmarks and omit stronger baselines (e.g., RuLSIF/KuLSIF, IWCV); KMM is omitted in some settings without sufficient justification.  
- No assessment of weight nonnegativity/normalization effects on downstream tasks is provided.  
- Paper is generally well written, but notation is heavy/overloaded and would benefit from pseudocode and a complexity summary.  
- The inverse-problem framing is a neat, unifying perspective and manifold-aware rates are a useful theoretical addition.  
- Caveat: Type I approach is close in spirit to existing methods (KMM/LSIF); main novelty is the inverse-problem/regularized functional viewpoint.  
- Significance: could influence future RKHS inverse-problem work and importance weighting, but empirical scope and scalability limit immediate impact.  
- Pros summarized: clean formulation, closed-form algorithms, theoretical bounds (including manifolds), novel Type II and CD-CV.  
- Cons summarized: limited baseline comparisons, no positivity/normalization constraints, scalability issues, Gaussian-only theory assumptions, not fully clarified tuning and rates.  
- Suggested improvements: add positivity/normalization handling, provide runtime/memory benchmarks and scalable approximations, compare to RuLSIF/KuLSIF/IWCV and include KMM where relevant.  
- Additional suggestions: analyze CD-CV sensitivity and relate it to MMD/GMM with consistency discussion; clarify kernel-role vs Gaussian-only theory and recommend tuning strategies; streamline notation and add pseudocode.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — interesting and principled contribution with useful theory and algorithms, but empirical and scalability aspects need strengthening.
