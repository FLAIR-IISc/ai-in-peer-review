

# Review: "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

## Summary

This paper introduces a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, an inverse problem from classical analysis. The authors propose two algorithms (Type I and Type II) that solve this inverse problem using Tikhonov regularization within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The key insight is that density ratio estimation can be viewed as "linearizing" density properties through associated integral operators. The authors develop the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) algorithms, provide theoretical convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, and introduce CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) for unsupervised parameter selection. The work connects density ratio estimation to classical operator theory, offering a principled framework that differs from existing approaches like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and provides a solid theoretical foundation for density ratio estimation. The authors successfully establish the connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations, which is a novel perspective not commonly explored in the machine learning literature. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, deriving convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings in Euclidean space and on manifolds. The assumptions are reasonable and well-motivated (e.g., bounded densities, Sobolev smoothness conditions), and the proofs appear rigorous. The experimental evaluation demonstrates competitive performance against strong baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across multiple datasets and tasks (regression, classification), with careful attention to parameter selection via CD-CV.

The paper carefully addresses limitations, noting that Type II has a kernel-dependent error term (O(t) for Gaussian kernels) and that q does not need to be a density in Type II. The authors also acknowledge that while their approach provides a principled framework, the Gaussian kernel assumption might limit practical applicability in some scenarios.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly establishes the problem, motivation, and contributions. The mathematical formulation is precise, with careful definitions of the function spaces, operators, and algorithms. The paper effectively uses the connection to classical analysis to provide intuition for why the proposed approach works. The theoretical results are stated with appropriate precision, and the experimental section provides sufficient detail for reproducibility (including specific parameter ranges and validation procedures). The comparison to related work (particularly KMM) is thorough and helps situate the contribution within the literature.

The only minor clarity issue is that the paper occasionally uses "q(x)p(x)" notation when they mean "q(x)/p(x)" (density ratio), which could be confusing. However, this is a minor notational inconsistency that doesn't significantly impact the understanding of the paper.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution by framing density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem. While density ratio estimation has been studied extensively (e.g., [9, 10, 22]), the Fredholm equation perspective is novel and provides a fresh theoretical foundation. The connection to spectral theory and classical operator theory is particularly insightful and not commonly explored in the machine learning community.

The FIRE algorithms themselves are new and distinct from existing methods. The authors demonstrate how their approach relates to KMM (as a special case with a different optimization procedure) but emphasize key differences: FIRE provides a natural out-of-sample extension and better stability due to the regularization term. The CD-CV method for unsupervised parameter selection is also a novel contribution that addresses a common challenge in unsupervised learning.

The theoretical analysis for manifold settings (Corollaries 2 and 4) extends previous work on density ratio estimation in a meaningful way, showing how the convergence rates depend on the geometry of the underlying space.

## Significance

Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem in machine learning with applications in covariate shift adaptation, importance sampling, and transfer learning. The paper addresses this problem with a unified theoretical framework that connects it to classical mathematical analysis, which is both elegant and practically useful. The theoretical guarantees provide valuable insights into the behavior of the algorithms, and the empirical results demonstrate strong performance.

The CD-CV parameter selection method is particularly significant, as it provides a practical solution to a persistent challenge in unsupervised learning. The paper shows that their method outperforms standard approaches in parameter selection, which is a substantial contribution to the field.

While the theoretical analysis is limited to Gaussian kernels (a common assumption in kernel methods), the framework itself is general and could be extended to other kernels. The paper's connection to classical analysis may also inspire future work on other density estimation problems.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Novel theoretical connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations.
2. Comprehensive theoretical analysis with convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold settings.
3. Practical algorithm (FIRE) with simple implementation and clear out-of-sample extension.
4. CD-CV for unsupervised parameter selection is a valuable contribution to the field.
5. Well-written, well-organized, and clearly motivated.

**Weaknesses:**
1. Theoretical analysis is limited to Gaussian kernels; the paper doesn't discuss how the results might generalize to other kernels.
2. The experimental section could include more datasets or more comprehensive comparisons with the most recent density ratio estimation methods.
3. The computational complexity of FIRE isn't discussed in detail, which would be useful for practitioners.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of density ratio estimation by providing a novel theoretical framework grounded in classical analysis. The theoretical results are well-supported, the algorithms are practical and well-motivated, and the experimental evaluation demonstrates competitive performance. The connection to Fredholm equations is particularly insightful and offers a new perspective on a well-studied problem.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria at a high level. It is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), makes a significant theoretical contribution (Originality), and addresses an important problem with meaningful results (Significance).

I recommend **acceptance** for NIPS. This paper would be a valuable contribution to the conference, providing both theoretical insights and practical algorithms for density ratio estimation that could benefit a wide range of machine learning applications.
