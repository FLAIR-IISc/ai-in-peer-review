This paper addresses the problem of density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques from RKHS theory. The core idea – leveraging operator theory to analyze density ratio estimation – is a novel perspective within a well-studied area. The authors propose a family of algorithms (FIRE) and provide theoretical analysis, including convergence rates, and empirical validation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The Fredholm equation formulation is a genuinely new angle on density ratio estimation. It connects a practical machine learning problem to a rich theoretical framework.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper delivers substantial theoretical results, including concentration and error bounds for the Gaussian kernel, covering Euclidean spaces and manifolds. This is a significant contribution.
*   **Generality:** The framework is quite general, accommodating various norms and loss functions, and offering both Type I and Type II formulations catering to different data availability scenarios.
*   **Algorithm Design:** The derivation of the FIRE algorithms is clear and leads to computationally tractable solutions based on kernel methods.
*   **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper includes a thorough experimental evaluation comparing FIRE with established methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) on multiple datasets and using different resampling strategies.
*   **Unsupervised Parameter Selection:** The CD-CV method for parameter selection is a valuable addition, addressing a common challenge in unsupervised learning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity (Theoretical Sections):** While the core idea is clear, the theoretical sections (Section 3 especially) are dense and challenging to follow for readers not deeply versed in functional analysis and operator theory. More intuitive explanations and intermediate steps would improve accessibility.
*   **Overly Broad Scope:** The paper attempts to cover a lot of ground – different norms, loss functions, settings (Type I/II), and theoretical results. This breadth sometimes comes at the expense of depth in specific areas.
*   **Limited Practical Advantage in Experiments:**  While the experiments show competitive performance, FIRE doesn’t consistently and significantly outperform existing methods.  The gains are often marginal, and the practical benefits of the theoretical advantages aren’t always clear from the empirical results. The error bars/variance in the experimental results could be more explicitly reported to demonstrate the statistical significance of the findings.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** Although the related work section is extensive, the paper could more explicitly highlight the limitations of existing approaches that FIRE addresses and how the proposed formulation overcomes those limitations beyond simply stating it's a different approach.

**Specific Comments & Questions:**

*   Equation (3): The “O(t)” approximation could benefit from a clearer explanation of the smoothness conditions required for it to hold.
*   Section 2: The discussions on Type I and Type II algorithms could be more concisely presented, perhaps using a table to summarize the differences and applicability.
*   Theorem 1 & 3: The dependence of the error bounds on the kernel parameter 't' seems crucial. How sensitive is the performance of FIRE to the choice of 't' in practice?  Could the CD-CV method reliably handle this sensitivity?
*   CD-CV: How does the choice of the test function families U1 and U2 in CD-CV affect the parameter selection process? Is there a systematic way to choose these families?
*   Table 1: Providing standard deviations or confidence intervals alongside the average errors would significantly strengthen the conclusions drawn from the experimental results.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to density ratio estimation.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the properties of the proposed algorithms.
*   The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates the competitiveness of FIRE.
*   The CD-CV method is a useful contribution to the field of unsupervised learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The theoretical sections may be inaccessible to a broad audience.
*   The practical advantages of FIRE over existing methods are not consistently demonstrated.
*   The paper's breadth could be reduced to allow for more in-depth exploration of key aspects.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong submission** with the potential to be a valuable contribution to the NIPS conference. The novelty of the approach and the depth of the theoretical analysis are significant strengths. While the clarity of the theoretical sections and the practical impact of the results could be improved, the paper's overall quality is high. I recommend **acceptance**, contingent on the authors addressing the clarity concerns in the theoretical sections and providing more detailed analysis of the experimental results to better demonstrate the practical benefits of FIRE.
