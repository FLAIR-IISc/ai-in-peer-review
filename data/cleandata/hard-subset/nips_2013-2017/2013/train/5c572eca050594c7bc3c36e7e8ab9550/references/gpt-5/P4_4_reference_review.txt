Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) via an inverse-problem formulation. Starting from the importance sampling identity Eq(h) = Ep(h r), the authors obtain two Fredholm-type equations of the first kind:
- Type I: Kp r = Kq 1, where Kp and Kq are integral operators with kernel k acting against p and q, respectively. This applies when samples from both p and q are available.
- Type II: Kt,p r ≈ q, where Kt,p uses a “local” kernel (e.g., Gaussian) that approximates the identity; this applies when q is known (not necessarily a density) at points sampled from p.

Both are solved by Tikhonov regularization over an RKHS H with kernel kH, yielding closed-form solutions via the representer theorem. The authors present several loss choices (L2,p, mixed γL2,p+(1−γ)L2,q, and an RKHS loss) and derive explicit linear-algebraic forms for the solutions (FIRE).

The paper analyzes statistical error for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean domains and compact manifolds, giving bounds and rates as functions of sample sizes, kernel bandwidth t, and regularization λ. It also proposes an unsupervised model-selection method (CD-CV) based on testing the importance identity against random test functions, enabling cross-validation without labeled targets. Experiments compare FIRE to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), LSIF, and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline (TIKDE), with additional downstream regression/classification under covariate shift.

This work connects to a substantial literature on density-ratio estimation and covariate shift: KMM (Gretton et al., 2009), LSIF/KLIEP (Kanamori et al., 2009; Sugiyama et al.), penalized likelihood-ratio estimation (Nguyen et al., 2007), and inverse-problem perspectives in RKHS (Smola & Schölkopf, 1998; De Vito et al., 2005). The Type I Kp r = Kq 1 identity is equivalent to matching mean embeddings; the RKHS-loss variant is closely related to KMM. The proposed Type II approximate-identity formulation is in line with classical integral operator theory.

Strengths
- Clear and principled inverse-problem formulation of density-ratio estimation that unifies several existing approaches under Fredholm integral equations and Tikhonov regularization.
- Derivation of simple closed-form estimators via RKHS machinery; the solutions are explicit linear systems with standard kernel matrices.
- Theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels, including concentration bounds and convergence rates, on Euclidean domains and manifolds, linking to operator spectra.
- The CD-CV unsupervised model-selection criterion is practical and leverages the importance identity without requiring labeled targets; it is compatible with Type I settings and broadly applicable.
- Empirical evaluation against representative baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) and downstream tasks demonstrates feasibility.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
Quality/technical soundness
- Positivity/normalization: The RKHS-regularized solutions are unconstrained, so estimated ratios can be negative or mis-normalized. This is not discussed, despite practical implications. Many competitors (KMM/LSIF variants) enforce non-negativity and sometimes normalization; an analysis of these constraints’ impact is missing.
- Parameter selection in theory: Bounds assume “small enough” t and provide rates via λ,t, but do not give explicit oracle or data-dependent scaling (t(n), λ(n)) achieving the stated rates. Comparing resulting rates to known minimax rates for likelihood/density ratios (e.g., Nguyen et al., 2007) would contextualize optimality or suboptimality.
- Rates appear conservative. The exponents in the corollaries (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2) or n−1/(4+5d/6) in squared L2,p error up to logs) seem weaker than classical nonparametric rates under Sobolev smoothness. A discussion of the gap and whether it is due to operator estimation, RKHS source conditions, or proof techniques would strengthen the contribution.
- Type II approximation error: The Kt,p r ≈ q(·) step introduces O(t) (or t1−ε on manifolds) bias. The bounds include ‖Kt,q 1 − q‖ terms, but practical guidance on choosing t to balance bias/variance is not provided. It would help to state precise bias constants/conditions (e.g., boundary effects, kernel order).
- Claims on computational efficiency are not substantiated with complexity analysis. FIRE requires solving dense linear systems (O(n3)) similarly to KMM/LSIF variants; in practice, preconditioning, low-rank approximations, or scalability strategies should be discussed.

Clarity
- Notation is dense and occasionally overloaded (K, KH, Kp,p, Kp,q, etc.). A concise algorithm box for each variant (input/output/complexity) would improve readability.
- The empirical losses combining L2,p and L2,q are well-motivated, but the role and selection of γ are not discussed experimentally.
- For experiments, more details would aid reproducibility: exact sample sizes (n,m), bandwidth and λ chosen by CD-CV, and variance of results across trials.

Originality
- Framing density-ratio estimation explicitly as a Fredholm inverse problem and deriving multiple RKHS-regularized variants is a coherent perspective that clarifies relationships to KMM and LSIF. However, the Type I RKHS-loss formulation is essentially a regularized version of KMM; novelty resides mainly in unifying the formulations and in the Type II approximate-identity route plus the manifold analysis.
- CD-CV is related to existing importance-weighted cross-validation and LSIF/KLIEP validation, but the use of random linear/half-space test functions as generic moment conditions is a practical, simple twist.

Significance
- The operator-theoretic perspective and explicit RKHS-regularized formulations are useful for both analysis and implementation. The manifold setting broadens applicability.
- Without stronger empirical advantages or tighter rates, practical impact relative to mature alternatives (e.g., uLSIF/KuLSIF) remains uncertain. Addressing negativity/normalization and scalability would increase adoption.

Pros for acceptance
- Unifying inverse-problem formulation with closed-form estimators and theoretical guarantees.
- Extension to manifold-supported distributions.
- Practical unsupervised model selection based on the importance identity.
- Clear connections to and reinterpretations of KMM/LSIF.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to KMM/LSIF for Type I; Type II is incremental from approximate identity theory.
- Bounds likely suboptimal with limited guidance on tuning to achieve near-optimal rates.
- Lack of treatment of positivity/normalization constraints and limited discussion of scalability.
- Experimental comparisons omit strong LSIF variants (e.g., uLSIF/KuLSIF) and provide limited evidence of clear advantage.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add constrained variants enforcing non-negativity and (optional) normalization, and analyze their effect.
- Provide explicit data-dependent tuning rules for t and λ (possibly via CD-CV) and compare achieved rates to known minimax results.
- Include comparisons to uLSIF/KuLSIF and report weight negativity statistics and downstream performance sensitivity.
- Clarify computational complexity and consider low-rank/Nyström approximations.
- Streamline notation and add algorithm summaries; expand experimental details for reproducibility.

Overall, the paper presents a coherent operator-theoretic framework with explicit algorithms and analysis. Addressing the above issues would strengthen both theoretical and practical contributions.
