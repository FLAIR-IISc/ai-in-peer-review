This paper presents a fascinating and novel perspective on density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable through Fredholm equations and regularization techniques. The connection to operator theory and spectral analysis is a strong point, offering a principled framework and potential for theoretical advancement—something often lacking in this area. The authors successfully bridge concepts from classical analysis and kernel methods, which is a significant achievement. The introduction of FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) and the exploration of different norms and loss functions demonstrate a good understanding of the underlying mathematical structure. The empirical results, while not overwhelmingly superior to existing methods, provide initial validation and show competitive performance.

However, the paper feels somewhat stretched thin across its ambition. The theoretical results, while present, are dense and rely on assumptions that might limit their immediate applicability. While the authors claim convergence rates, the dependence on parameters like *t* and *n* could be prohibitive in practice. The discussion of manifold learning is promising, but more concrete examples and results on non-Euclidean data would strengthen this aspect. Furthermore, the experimental section, while comprehensive, lacks a clear demonstration of *when* FIRE significantly outperforms established methods like KMM or LSIF. The CD-CV technique is a valuable addition, but its effectiveness appears somewhat data-dependent.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The Fredholm equation formulation is genuinely novel and offers a fresh perspective on density ratio estimation. This is the paper’s strongest aspect.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding in operator theory and RKHS provides a rigorous framework for analysis and potential generalization.
*   **Flexibility:** The ability to incorporate different norms and loss functions adds flexibility and adaptability to the approach.
*   **CD-CV:** The proposed unsupervised model selection technique is a valuable contribution.
*   **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper clearly positions its work in relation to KMM and LSIF, acknowledging their contributions and highlighting the differences.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Complexity:** The theoretical results are dense and may be difficult for many readers to fully grasp. The assumptions required for the convergence rates may be restrictive.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results are not conclusive. While competitive, FIRE doesn’t consistently outperform other methods across all datasets and settings.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of FIRE appears sensitive to the choice of kernel width *t* and regularization parameter λ.
*   **Limited Manifold Results:** The discussion of manifold learning is promising, but lacks sufficient experimental validation.
*   **Density Requirement:** The applicability of Type I to cases where q is not a valid density, as noted by the authors, feels somewhat underdeveloped.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the approach is significant. It presents a fundamentally different way of thinking about density ratio estimation.
*   The strong theoretical foundation provides a solid basis for future research.
*   The potential for generalization and adaptation through different norms and loss functions is appealing.
*   Even if the practical gains are modest at this stage, the theoretical contribution and potential for future improvement are substantial.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical results are complex and may not be readily applicable in practice.
*   The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the superiority of FIRE over existing methods.
*   The paper feels somewhat incomplete, with areas such as manifold learning and Type I setting application needing further development.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field, primarily due to its novelty and theoretical depth. While the experimental results are not conclusive, the potential for future development and the strong theoretical foundation outweigh the weaknesses. The paper offers a bold and innovative approach to a challenging problem, and I would lean towards **acceptance**, despite acknowledging the current limitations. I encourage the authors to focus on simplifying the theoretical presentation, providing more compelling experimental evidence, and further exploring the applications of their framework to non-Euclidean data and scenarios where q is not a density function.
