Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses estimation of the density ratio r(x) = q(x)/p(x), a key quantity for importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation. The central idea is to cast ratio estimation as an inverse problem for integral operators (Fredholm equations of the first kind), and to solve it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS.

Two formulations are proposed:
- Type I: Kp r = Kq 1, where Kp and Kq are kernel integral operators under p and q. This is applicable when samples from both p and q are available. Several loss choices are considered: L2,p, a mixture γL2,p + (1−γ)L2,q, and an RKHS-norm loss, all with RKHS-norm regularization.
- Type II: Kt,p r ≈ q, where Kt,p uses a “local” kernel (e.g., Gaussian with variance t) to approximate the identity operator, applicable when q is known (or its values are known at the p-sample points) but sampling from q is expensive.

Representer-theorem arguments yield closed-form, out-of-sample estimators as linear combinations of a chosen kernel on the p-sample. The resulting family of algorithms is termed FIRE.

Theoretical analysis provides finite-sample high-probability bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on compact d-dimensional manifolds (without boundary), under smoothness assumptions on r and boundedness of p and q. The rates account for approximation bias O(t) or O(t1−ε), regularization λ, and sampling errors.

For model selection, the paper proposes Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which uses the identity Eq[u] = Ep[r u] to define an unsupervised validation loss over random test functions (e.g., linear or half-space indicators), enabling selection of kernel bandwidth t and regularization λ.

Experiments compare FIRE to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF), a thresholded inverse KDE baseline (TIKDE), and importance sampling where available, on USPS digits and two regression datasets under synthetic covariate shift.

The work connects to and generalizes several lines of prior research. KMM enforces Eq[Φ] = Ep[r Φ] in an RKHS; the Type I formulation with RKHS loss recovers a regularized KMM-like objective with closed-form solution and natural out-of-sample behavior. LSIF minimizes L2,p error over a parametric basis; FIRE uses an RKHS with operator-based losses and Tikhonov regularization. The operator-inverse and Tikhonov perspective builds on earlier RKHS inverse problem analyses. The manifold support analysis relates to work on kernel methods over manifolds.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean and unifying operator-theoretic framing of density ratio estimation, leading to simple, explicit estimators via standard RKHS machinery.
- Clear derivations of closed-form solutions for multiple losses, including a combined L2,p/L2,q loss that leverages both samples in the objective while keeping out-of-sample estimation straightforward.
- Theoretical results with finite-sample bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in both Euclidean and manifold settings; analysis separates bias (t, λ) and variance terms and leverages spectral properties of integral operators.
- CD-CV provides a practical, unsupervised hyperparameter selection strategy grounded in the importance identity, and is easy to implement.
- Discussion of relationships to KMM and LSIF clarifies similarities/differences and positions the contribution.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to existing RKHS/operator-inversion views and to KMM/LSIF is partly incremental. In Type I with RKHS loss and same kernels, the estimator is essentially a regularized variant of KMM; the L2,p-based Type I objective is close in spirit to LSIF but with an operator-side formulation. The paper would benefit from a sharper delineation of genuinely new elements and when they yield different behavior.
- The estimator does not enforce positivity or normalization Ep[r]=1, so negative or mis-normalized ratios are possible. KMM explicitly constrains weights; here, the practical impact and possible remedies (e.g., constrained variants) are not analyzed.
- Theoretical assumptions (smoothness in W2 2, bounded densities, Gaussian kernels, small t) may limit scope; achieved rates have unusual exponents and are not compared to known minimax bounds for likelihood ratio estimation, leaving optimality unclear.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in breadth and ablations. Several experiments omit KMM citing out-of-sample issues, yet OOS predictions can be obtained via kernel representers over the p-sample. Comparisons to stronger baselines (e.g., uLSIF/RuLSIF), real-world covariate shift benchmarks, and wall-clock/memory measurements are missing.
- Scalability is not discussed; the presented solutions require solving linear systems with kernel matrices, which can be cubic in n. No use of approximations (Nyström, random features) is considered.
- CD-CV depends on the choice of test function family; sensitivity analyses are limited.

Quality
- The optimization problems and solutions are technically sound, with correct use of RKHS theory and empirical operator approximations. The theory is carefully structured, though many proofs and constants are deferred.
- The experimental protocol outlines datasets, resampling, and hyperparameter grids, but more comprehensive baselines and scalability analyses are needed for a convincing empirical case.

Clarity
- Generally well organized with clear derivations of algorithms. Some notation is heavy, and several references lack publication years. Assumptions for the theorems and guidance on practical selection of t, λ could be clarified. Minor typos exist.

Originality
- The Fredholm framing and the Type II formulation (q known, sampling hard) are interesting; the overall approach is a synthesis of known ingredients (operator inversion, RKHS Tikhonov) applied to density ratio estimation. Novelty is moderate.

Significance
- Density ratio estimation is important in covariate shift and Monte Carlo methods; a principled, closed-form, and theoretically grounded estimator with unsupervised model selection can be practically useful. Strengthening empirical evidence and addressing constraints/scalability would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Unifying and principled formulation with simple, out-of-sample estimators.
- Nontrivial theory including manifold support.
- Practical unsupervised hyperparameter selection.

Cons for acceptance
- Marginal novelty relative to KMM/LSIF/operator inversion.
- Lack of positivity/normalization constraints and limited discussion of practical consequences.
- Limited and uneven empirical comparisons; no scalability discussion.

Suggestions for improvement
- Introduce and evaluate constrained variants enforcing r≥0 and Ep[r]=1; analyze their effect on bias/variance and stability.
- Expand experiments: include KMM with standard OOS implementation, uLSIF/RuLSIF, larger datasets, real shift scenarios; report runtimes and memory; add sensitivity analyses for CD-CV test function choices.
- Provide guidance linking theory to practice for choosing t and λ; discuss rates vs. known lower bounds.
- Improve exposition and references; clarify assumptions and constants; discuss computational complexity and potential approximations.
