

# Review: Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach

This paper proposes a novel perspective on density ratio estimation by framing it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors develop two types of algorithms (Type I and Type II) called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) and propose a new unsupervised parameter selection method called CD-CV.

## Strengths

The theoretical connection to Fredholm equations is intellectually interesting and provides a new perspective on density ratio estimation. The paper demonstrates a clear mathematical foundation for their approach, showing how standard density ratio estimation problems can be viewed through the lens of classical inverse problem theory. The theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels in both Euclidean and manifold settings is well-structured and provides meaningful convergence rates. The CD-CV method for parameter selection is a practical contribution that addresses a common challenge in unsupervised learning.

## Weaknesses

Despite the interesting theoretical foundation, the paper suffers from several significant shortcomings that undermine its contribution:

1. **Lack of substantial empirical validation**: The experimental section is severely limited, using only two regression datasets (CPUsmall and Kin8nm) and one classification dataset (USPS). This is insufficient to demonstrate the claimed advantages over existing methods. The paper lacks comparisons with several recent state-of-the-art density ratio estimation methods that have appeared in the last two years.

2. **Questionable novelty**: The connection to Fredholm equations appears superficial. The integral equations they derive (Equations 2 and 3) are essentially standard in the density ratio estimation literature. For example, the Type I formulation closely resembles Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9], which is explicitly mentioned in the paper but not thoroughly compared. The authors acknowledge this connection but fail to demonstrate how their regularization approach provides meaningful advantages over KMM.

3. **Limited theoretical novelty**: The convergence rates they derive (Theorems 1 and 3) follow standard patterns for kernel-based regularization methods. The error bounds are comparable to what could be obtained using standard techniques in kernel methods, without demonstrating significant improvements over existing theoretical results for density ratio estimation.

4. **Theoretical assumptions are restrictive**: The analysis assumes that q/p and q/p² are in Sobolev space W²₂(Ω), which is a strong assumption not commonly satisfied in real-world applications. The paper does not discuss the practical implications of these assumptions.

5. **Insufficient comparison with baselines**: The comparison with KMM is incomplete because the authors omit out-of-sample extension comparisons (which are a key advantage of KMM). The comparison with LSIF and TIKDE is limited to the CD-CV metric without showing how their method performs on standard evaluation metrics for density ratio estimation (e.g., KL divergence, Hellinger distance).

6. **Type II setting lacks practical motivation**: The paper acknowledges that Type II is less general than Type I but fails to provide compelling examples where Type II would be useful in practice, particularly since Type II requires q to be known at the sample points, which is uncommon.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical perspective on density ratio estimation, it falls short of demonstrating a meaningful contribution to the field. The connection to Fredholm equations does not translate into substantial theoretical or empirical advantages over established methods. The theoretical analysis is standard for kernel methods, and the empirical validation is insufficient to support the claims of "strong performance" and "better computational efficiency."

The paper would benefit from more comprehensive experiments comparing with the most recent state-of-the-art methods, clearer demonstration of why the Fredholm equation perspective provides practical advantages, and a more thorough discussion of the limitations of their approach. Without these improvements, the paper does not advance the field significantly beyond existing density ratio estimation techniques.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper has a theoretically grounded approach, it fails to provide sufficient evidence that this new perspective leads to meaningful improvements over existing methods. The empirical validation is insufficient, and the novelty claim is not well-supported by the evidence presented. For a paper to be accepted at NIPS, it must demonstrate a clear advancement over the state of the art, which this paper does not do. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their empirical validation and provide a more compelling argument for the novelty and practical advantages of their approach.
