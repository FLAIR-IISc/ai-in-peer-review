

# Peer Review: "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper presents a novel perspective on density ratio estimation by framing it as a Fredholm integral equation (inverse problem), leading to a theoretically grounded and computationally efficient algorithm called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The authors establish a connection between density ratio estimation and classical operator theory, which allows them to apply spectral analysis and regularization techniques from functional analysis to derive error bounds and convergence rates.

## Summary and Context

Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem with applications in transfer learning, covariate shift adaptation, and importance sampling. While several approaches exist (KMM, LSIF, KLIEP), the authors propose a fresh perspective by recognizing that density ratio estimation can be formulated as a Fredholm equation of the first kind. They develop two types of formulations (Type I for when samples from both distributions are available, Type II for when function values of q are known at p-sampled points) and solve them using Tikhonov regularization with RKHS norms.

The key theoretical contribution is the derivation of error bounds for both Euclidean and manifold settings using Gaussian kernels. The authors also introduce CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), an unsupervised parameter selection method that leverages the importance sampling identity for validation.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Depth**: The paper's core contribution is the elegant connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations. This perspective provides a unified framework that explains and extends existing methods like KMM (which emerges as a special case of their Type I formulation with RKHS norm). The theoretical analysis is thorough, providing convergence rates for both Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds - a significant advancement over most existing work that focuses solely on Euclidean domains.

2. **Algorithmic Simplicity**: The FIRE algorithms follow directly from the representer theorem, resulting in simple, explicit formulas that are easy to implement. The Type II formulation is particularly valuable for cases where q is known explicitly but sampling from q is difficult.

3. **Practical Innovation**: CD-CV is a clever unsupervised parameter selection method that addresses a critical challenge in density ratio estimation. The experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness compared to traditional cross-validation approaches.

4. **Comprehensive Comparison**: The paper provides a detailed comparison with relevant baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across multiple datasets and tasks (regression, classification), showing competitive performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Limitation**: While the theoretical analysis is strong, it's limited to Gaussian kernels. The authors acknowledge this but don't sufficiently discuss how the results might extend to other kernels or whether their approach would be equally effective with other kernel choices. This is a significant limitation for a paper claiming to be a "flexible and principled framework."

2. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The related work section mentions [14] and [26] but doesn't deeply analyze how their theoretical results compare with these recent advances. The paper should clarify how their bounds improve upon or differ from minimax rates established in [14] or the KMM analysis in [26].

3. **Computational Complexity**: The paper mentions computational efficiency but doesn't provide a detailed complexity analysis or comparison with alternatives. For large-scale applications, this is a crucial consideration that's missing.

4. **Type II Limitations**: While the Type II formulation is valuable for certain applications, the paper doesn't sufficiently address the trade-offs. The O(t) approximation error term (in Theorem 3) could be substantial for practical kernel bandwidths, and the paper doesn't discuss how to select t in practice for Type II.

## Critical Assessment

The paper's greatest strength is its theoretical framework, which provides a new lens through which to view density ratio estimation. The connection to Fredholm equations is both intellectually satisfying and practically useful, as it allows the authors to apply the rich machinery of spectral theory to derive meaningful error bounds.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the following points:

1. A more detailed discussion of how their theoretical results compare with [14] and [26], particularly regarding the minimax rates and assumptions.

2. A clearer explanation of how the Gaussian kernel limitation affects the practical applicability of their framework.

3. A more comprehensive comparison of computational complexity with KMM and LSIF, especially for large datasets.

4. A more thorough discussion of the trade-offs in Type II setting, particularly regarding the choice of kernel bandwidth t.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of density ratio estimation, but the practical implications would be enhanced with a more nuanced discussion of the limitations and trade-offs.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field. The main areas for improvement are:

1. Expanding the discussion of theoretical limitations and connections to recent work
2. Adding a computational complexity analysis
3. Providing more detailed guidance on practical implementation choices (particularly for Type II)

The paper's novel theoretical framework and strong empirical results make it a compelling contribution to NIPS. The authors have successfully bridged classical analysis with modern machine learning, providing both a deeper theoretical understanding and practical algorithms for a fundamental problem in machine learning.

This paper advances the state of the art in density ratio estimation by providing a unified theoretical framework that connects to classical inverse problems, while also delivering practical algorithms with strong empirical performance. The CD-CV method is particularly noteworthy as it addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning. With the suggested revisions, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
