Summary and relation to prior work
The paper reframes density-ratio estimation r(x)=q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solves it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two formulations are proposed: (Type I) Kp r = Kq 1, which uses samples from both p and q and does not require a δ-kernel, and (Type II) Kt,p r ≈ q, which uses a δ-family kernel (e.g., Gaussian) and requires q to be known (or evaluable) on points drawn from p. By empirical approximations of the operators and the L2,p norm, the representer theorem yields explicit closed-form solutions (FIRE) that are linear combinations of kernel evaluations on the p-sample. The paper also proposes an unsupervised hyperparameter selection method, cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV), which minimizes discrepancies of empirical moment-matching Ep[u(x)r̂(x)] ≈ Eq[u(x)] over random families of test functions. Theoretical results provide finite-sample error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean domains and smooth manifolds.

The formulation is closely connected to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF/KLIEP. Indeed, Type I with RKHS loss is a regularized variant of KMM (with a different optimization route) where the equality Eq[Φ(x)] = Ep[r(x)Φ(x)] is expressed as an integral equation in H. Compared to LSIF/KLIEP, FIRE uses an infinite-dimensional RKHS hypothesis space rather than a finite parametric basis, while CD-CV echoes LSIF’s idea of choosing parameters by matching moments on held-out data. The operator-theoretic perspective also relates to prior work on inverse problems and spectral regularization in RKHS.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean re-derivation: Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation ties the problem to a mature theory of inverse problems, clarifying the role of regularization and enabling use of operator tools.
- Simple, explicit algorithms: The representer-theorem solutions lead to closed-form linear systems with natural out-of-sample extension, addressing a known limitation of KMM.
- Flexibility: The framework accommodates several loss choices (L2,p, L2,q, their mixtures, and an RKHS loss) and both Type I and Type II scenarios (the latter applicable when q is known up to scaling).
- Theoretical analysis: Nonasymptotic bounds and rates are provided for Gaussian kernels, including manifold settings—valuable for readers interested in the spectral/operator viewpoint.
- Unsupervised model selection: CD-CV is practical and easy to implement, and its connection to moment matching is intuitive.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
Quality/Technical soundness
- Positivity and normalization: For density ratios, r ≥ 0 and Ep[r]=1 are key. FIRE imposes neither, which may yield negative or poorly calibrated weights in importance weighting. This is a practical concern not analyzed theoretically or ablated empirically. KMM and LSIF often include constraints/regularizers addressing this.
- Theoretical rates: The presented rates involve nonstandard exponents (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2), and “56/d” appears to be a typesetting artifact). Guidance on optimal tuning of t and λ to attain the stated rates is limited, and it is unclear how close these are to known minimax rates for ratio estimation. Assumptions (e.g., qp ∈ W2
2 and bounded densities) are strong and could be discussed more critically.
- Type II approximation bias: The O(t) (or t1−ε) bias term is acknowledged but practical selection of t balancing bias and variance is not analyzed in depth.

Clarity
- Notation is heavy and sometimes inconsistent. Several kernel matrices (Kp,p, Kp,q, KH, etc.) and scaling constants (1/n, 1/m) appear; collecting all definitions in one place and providing pseudocode would improve readability.
- A number of typographical issues in the bounds/rates make them hard to parse; please verify exponents and constants.

Originality
- The core equality Kp r = Kq 1 with RKHS machinery and the representer theorem largely recapitulates the KMM perspective, albeit with a different loss/regularization. The main novelty is the explicit inverse-problem framing, the Type II variant, and the CD-CV procedure. Overall, novelty feels moderate.

Significance/Empirical support
- Experimental scope is limited to standard small/medium datasets; claims of computational advantages are not substantiated with complexity/runtimes. Solving n×n systems scales as O(n^3) without approximations; discussion of Nyström/random features would help.
- Baselines: While LSIF is included, comparisons to KMM are inconsistent (at one point omitted due to out-of-sample extension issues, though one can still evaluate reweighting on training points), and modern strong baselines such as uLSIF/KuLSIF or logistic regression-based ratio estimation are missing. Ablations on positivity/normalization constraints and on CD-CV choices are absent.
- CD-CV: The method is plausible but lacks theory (e.g., excess-risk bounds linking JCD to ratio estimation error with characteristic kernels/test families) or sensitivity analyses (choice of function family U, number F, split stability).

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Clear operator-theoretic framing and derivations
- Explicit, practical algorithms with out-of-sample extension
- Some new variants (Type II) and an unsupervised model selection heuristic
- Theoretical bounds including manifold cases

Cons:
- Limited novelty beyond existing RKHS-based ratio estimation (esp. KMM/LSIF)
- Missing positivity/normalization constraints and analysis
- Theoretical rates unclear and possibly loose; tuning guidance limited
- Experimental evaluation modest, missing key baselines and scalability analysis
- CD-CV lacks theoretical justification and thorough empirical stress-testing

Suggestions for improvement
- Enforce or analyze nonnegativity and normalization (e.g., constrained QP, projection, exponential parametrization) and show their impact on downstream IW regression/classification.
- Clarify and correct the bounds, provide data-driven prescriptions for λ and t that achieve the stated rates, and discuss optimality relative to known lower bounds.
- Provide theory for CD-CV (e.g., with characteristic kernels, JCD upper-bounds an integral probability metric), and include sensitivity/ablation studies.
- Expand experiments: add uLSIF/KuLSIF/KLIEP and logistic regression baselines; include KMM (with kernel ridge for out-of-sample if needed); report runtimes and memory; evaluate on larger-scale/high-dimensional settings; include synthetic data with known ground-truth r(x).
- Discuss and, if possible, implement scalability via Nyström or random features; include pseudocode and complexity.
- Improve clarity and unify notation; gather all kernel/operator/matrix definitions; fix typographical issues.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The operator-theoretic perspective and closed-form algorithms are appealing, but the novelty over established RKHS methods is limited, the theory needs clarification, positivity/normalization are unaddressed, and the empirical evaluation is not yet strong enough for NeurIPS. With the suggested improvements, this work could become a solid contribution.
