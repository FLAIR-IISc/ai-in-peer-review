This paper addresses the problem of density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques from RKHS theory. The core idea is to leverage operator theory to provide a principled and theoretically sound approach to a problem prevalent in transfer learning and importance sampling. The authors propose two formulations (Type I and Type II) depending on the availability of samples from both densities or just knowledge of the target density’s values. They develop algorithms (FIRE) based on Tikhonov regularization and provide theoretical convergence guarantees for Gaussian kernels in various settings, including manifolds. An unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV) and empirical validation are also presented.

The paper is well-motivated, connecting density ratio estimation to classical integral equation theory. The approach of formulating the problem as solving a Fredholm equation is novel and provides a solid mathematical foundation. The use of RKHS regularization is a natural fit and leads to relatively simple algorithms. The theoretical analysis, providing convergence rates, is a significant strength, and the extension to manifold settings is a valuable addition. The inclusion of an unsupervised parameter selection method is practical and addresses a common challenge in these types of problems. The extensive experimental evaluation, comparing against several existing methods, strengthens the claims made in the paper.

However, several weaknesses exist. While the connection to Fredholm equations is mathematically sound, its practical benefit beyond providing a theoretical framework is not entirely clear. The presented algorithms, while explicit, don't appear to offer significant computational advantages over existing methods like KMM or LSIF in many scenarios, particularly given the need for kernel matrix inversions. The theoretical analysis, while rigorous, relies on several assumptions (smoothness of densities, Gaussian kernels) that may limit its applicability. The experimental results, while comprehensive, don’t demonstrate overwhelming superiority of FIRE across all datasets and settings. The discussion of related work is very thorough, but sometimes feels disconnected from directly positioning the contributions of FIRE.  The motivation for certain choices, such as the specific forms of test functions used in CD-CV, could be further clarified.  Finally, the paper is quite dense and could benefit from some streamlining for clarity. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The Fredholm equation formulation offers a novel perspective on density ratio estimation.
*   **Theoretical soundness:**  Rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees.
*   **Principled approach:** RKHS regularization provides a well-founded framework.
*   **Comprehensive evaluation:**  Extensive experimental validation against relevant baselines.
*   **Manifold Extension:** The theoretical results extend to distributions on manifolds.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Practical benefits unclear:**  The Fredholm formulation's practical advantage over existing methods is not fully demonstrated.
*   **Computational complexity:** Kernel matrix inversions can be computationally expensive.
*   **Strong assumptions:** Theoretical results rely on potentially restrictive assumptions.
*   **Incremental improvement:** Empirical results show performance comparable to, rather than exceeding, existing state-of-the-art methods.
* **Density and Clarity:** The paper is quite dense and potentially difficult for readers not already deeply familiar with the relevant mathematical tools.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound and well-researched paper. However, its practical impact relative to existing methods is not definitively established. The theoretical contributions are valuable, but the complexity of the mathematical framework may limit its wider adoption.  

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **weak accept**. While not a groundbreaking advance, the paper presents a novel and mathematically rigorous approach to density ratio estimation, supported by theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation.  Addressing the clarity and highlighting more strongly the specific advantages of the approach in practical scenarios (beyond the theoretical framework) could substantially improve the paper.
