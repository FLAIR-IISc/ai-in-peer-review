## Peer Review: Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as solving a Fredholm integral equation. The authors leverage tools from operator theory, specifically regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), to develop algorithms (FIRE) for this task. They propose two types of formulations, Type I leveraging samples from both densities and Type II requiring only samples from one density and knowledge of the other density’s values at those points.  Theoretical analysis, including concentration and error bounds for the Gaussian kernel, is provided, alongside an unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV) and empirical comparisons against existing methods like KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly situates its contribution within the growing body of work on density ratio estimation, motivated by applications like transfer learning and covariate shift adaptation (citing key works like [6, 10, 9, 22, 2]).  The connection to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] is well-articulated, with the authors highlighting how KMM can be viewed as a special case within their framework.  They also accurately position their work relative to LSIF [10] and other related techniques, emphasizing the advantages of their approach regarding theoretical analysis and flexibility. The authors demonstrate a strong awareness of the relevant literature in kernel methods and regularization theory.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:**  The Fredholm equation perspective offers a fresh and mathematically rigorous approach to density ratio estimation, connecting it to a well-established field of mathematical analysis. This provides a strong theoretical foundation.
* **Generality and Flexibility:** The framework is quite general, accommodating various kernels, norms, and loss functions.  The two types of formulations (I & II) offer flexibility depending on available data.
* **Theoretical Guarantees:** The inclusion of theoretical analysis, including convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel, is a significant strength. These bounds provide valuable insight into the performance of the algorithms.
* **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The CD-CV method is a practical contribution addressing the often-challenging issue of parameter tuning in unsupervised learning.
* **Clear Algorithm Derivation:** The derivation of the FIRE algorithms is clear and well-explained, making them relatively easy to implement.
* **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section provides a thorough comparison with several relevant baselines on different datasets and settings.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the algorithms are presented with clear formulas, the computation of kernel matrices (especially in Type I) can become computationally expensive for large datasets. The paper doesn't extensively discuss scalability concerns or potential approximations.
* **Practicality of Type II:** The assumption in Type II that *q(x)* is known at the sample points *x<sub>i</sub>* drawn from *p* might limit its applicability in many real-world scenarios. The authors acknowledge this but could further elaborate on use cases where this assumption is reasonable.
* **Limited Scope of Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical guarantees are currently limited to the Gaussian kernel and Euclidean/manifold settings. Expanding these results to other kernels or more complex data distributions would be beneficial.
* **Experiment Details:** Some experimental details are lacking.  For instance, a discussion of the hyperparameter selection for the baseline methods (TIKDE, LSIF) would be helpful for a fair comparison. The rationale behind the chosen test function families for CD-CV is somewhat lacking.
* **CD-CV Sensitivity:** The performance of CD-CV could be sensitive to the choice of test functions. The paper does not provide guidance on how to choose suitable test functions for different problems.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*  The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a fundamental problem in machine learning.
*  The connection to operator theory provides a powerful framework for analysis and algorithm design.
*  The theoretical results and experimental validation demonstrate the potential of the proposed methods.
*  The unsupervised model selection technique is a valuable practical contribution.
*  The writing is generally clear and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the proposed algorithms might be a limitation in practice.
* The assumption underlying Type II formulation might restrict its applicability.
* The theoretical analysis is currently limited in scope.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The novel formulation, strong theoretical foundations, and comprehensive experimental validation outweigh the identified weaknesses. While the computational complexity and limitations of Type II formulation are concerns, they do not negate the overall value of the work.  The paper is of high quality and I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Specific Recommendations for Revision:**

*   **Scalability Discussion:**  Add a discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithms and potential methods for addressing scalability issues (e.g., Nyström methods, other approximation techniques).
*   **Type II Application Scenarios:** Provide more concrete examples of real-world scenarios where the assumption of knowing *q(x)* at sample points *x<sub>i</sub>* is reasonable.
*   **Expand Theoretical Analysis:** Consider extending the theoretical analysis to other kernels or data distributions (even if preliminary).
*   **Experiment Details:** Provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process for the baseline methods. Explain the rationale behind the choice of test functions for CD-CV.
*   **CD-CV Robustness:** Investigate the sensitivity of CD-CV to the choice of test functions and provide guidance on how to select appropriate functions for different problems.
