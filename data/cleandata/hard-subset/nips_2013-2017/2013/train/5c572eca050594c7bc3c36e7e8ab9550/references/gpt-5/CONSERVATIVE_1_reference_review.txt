Summary
The paper reformulates density-ratio estimation q/p as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solves it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding closed-form kernel algorithms (FIRE). Two settings are considered: (I) when samples from both p and q are available, solve Kp f ≈ Kq 1; (II) when function values of q are known on points drawn from p, solve Kt,p f ≈ q with a δ-family kernel. The representer theorem leads to explicit linear system solutions. The authors provide concentration/error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds. They also propose an unsupervised parameter selection scheme (CD-CV) based on matching moments across distributions using random test functions. Empirical comparisons are presented against KMM, LSIF, a thresholded inverse KDE baseline (TIKDE), and importance sampling where applicable, on standard datasets under synthetic covariate shift.

Relation to prior work
The approach is closely related to KMM (which also uses Eq[Φ(x)] = Ep[βΦ(x)]) and to inverse-problem regularization in RKHS (e.g., De Vito et al.). LSIF/KLIEP minimize divergences to the ratio in linear/RKHS families with closed-form or convex programs. The paper positions FIRE as a regularized, out-of-sample-friendly alternative to KMM with an operator-theoretic perspective, and introduces CD-CV for unsupervised model selection.

Strengths
- Unifying operator-theoretic formulation that makes clear the connection between density-ratio estimation and first-kind Fredholm equations; provides clean closed-form RKHS estimators.
- Explicit linear-system solutions and natural out-of-sample extension (unlike KMM’s weights-only formulation).
- Theoretical bounds for Gaussian kernels, including manifold support; attention to concentration and rates.
- CD-CV is a practical idea for unsupervised hyperparameter tuning, with a simple implementation.
- The paper is generally well organized; derivations leverage standard RKHS machinery.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty relative to established work appears incremental. The Type I formulation essentially regularizes the same identity exploited by KMM; the use of Tikhonov regularization in RKHS for inverse problems is well known. The paper’s main novelty seems to be packaging and some variants (γ L2,p+(1−γ)L2,q, RKHS loss), plus the specific CD-CV heuristic.
- Missing key baselines. The widely used classification-based ratio estimator (logistic regression/probability ratio with calibration) is absent; so are more recent and competitive variants such as uLSIF/KuLSIF/RuLSIF. TIKDE is a weak baseline. Without these, empirical claims of superiority are not convincing.
- No constraints to ensure a valid ratio. Unlike KMM/KLIEP, FIRE does not enforce non-negativity or normalization (Ep[f]=1). The estimators can produce negative ratios, which is problematic for importance weighting. The paper does not discuss whether clipping or other constraints are used in practice, nor their impact.
- Type II setting is under-validated. Although motivated by scenarios where q is known (e.g., MCMC/unnormalized models), there is no dedicated experiment demonstrating this advantage. All datasets are standard covariate-shift benchmarks constructed by resampling; no example with known q and expensive sampling.
- Theory appears specialized and potentially suboptimal. The rates n^{-1/(3.5+d/2)} (Type I) and n^{-1/(4+5d/6)} (Type II; up to logs/parameters) degrade quickly with dimension and seem worse than known minimax rates for related problems (e.g., Nguyen et al., 2008). Assumptions (q/p ∈ W^2_2, bounded densities, same Gaussian kernel for operator and RKHS) are strong. There is no comparison or discussion of optimality.
- Empirical evaluation lacks depth. The datasets and shifts are relatively simple; there is little analysis of sensitivity to kernel width t and λ, no ablation on the loss choices (L2,p vs mixed norms vs RKHS loss), and no runtime/memory comparisons despite claims of improved efficiency. Error bars/variances are not consistently reported.
- CD-CV seems close in spirit to existing importance-weighted CV (IWCV) and moment-matching criteria; the distinction and advantages over Sugiyama et al. (2007) are not clearly established. Risk of overfitting to random test functions is not analyzed.
- Clarity and notation: multiple overloaded kernel matrices (Kp,p, KH, K) with embedded 1/n, 1/m scalings make it hard to check dimensions and conditioning. It would help to factor out sampling operators to avoid K^2, K^3 expressions with scaling constants.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but important practical aspects (validity constraints, robustness) are not addressed. The theory is careful but specialized and not benchmarked against optimal rates. Empirics are not sufficiently rigorous to substantiate strong performance claims.

Clarity
Generally readable, but notation is heavy and sometimes opaque. More guidance on choosing t, λ, on enforcing positivity/normalization, and on computational complexity would improve reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. The operator view is elegant but largely overlaps with prior RKHS inverse problem and KMM perspectives; the main additions are regularized closed-form estimators and a specific CV heuristic.

Significance
Potentially useful unification, but current evidence is insufficient to shift practice away from established methods (LSIF/KLIEP/KuLSIF or classification-based estimators). The unvalidated Type II use-case weakens the impact.

Questions and suggestions
- How do you ensure non-negativity and Ep[f]=1 in practice? Do you clip or re-normalize? Provide results showing the effect.
- Add baselines: logistic regression-based ratio estimation with calibration; uLSIF/KuLSIF/RuLSIF; KLIEP.
- Include a Type II experiment where q is known (e.g., unnormalized target) and demonstrate advantages over alternatives.
- Provide runtime and memory comparisons, and condition-number analysis of the involved linear systems (K^2, K^3 terms).
- Discuss how your rates compare to known minimax results and whether the dependence on d is improvable.
- Clarify the relationship between CD-CV and IWCV; analyze overfitting to test functions; report sensitivity to the choice/number of functions.
- Improve notation by separating sampling operators from kernels and removing embedded 1/n factors in matrix powers.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean unifying formulation with closed-form estimators and some theory.
- Practical, easy-to-implement algorithms with out-of-sample extension.
- CD-CV is a simple unsupervised tuning tool.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty over established approaches.
- Missing key baselines and insufficient empirical validation, especially for Type II.
- Lack of validity constraints for ratio estimates.
- Suboptimal-looking rates and strong assumptions without discussion.

Recommendation
Lean reject. With stronger empirical validation against standard baselines (including Type II), handling of constraints, and clearer positioning relative to minimax theory and IWCV, the paper could become more compelling.

Reviewer confidence: High.
