Summary and relation to prior work
The paper reframes density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as an inverse problem: a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two formulations are proposed. Type I uses the identity Kp r = Kq 1 (with a general positive definite kernel k), which can be estimated from samples from p and q; Type II uses an approximate identity with a δ-family kernel kt: Kt,p r ≈ q, which applies when q is known or can be evaluated at points drawn from p. Both are solved via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS with kernel kH, leading to closed-form representer-theorem solutions written in terms of kernel matrices on the p-sample (and optionally q-sample). The authors analyze statistical error and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and on compact submanifolds, and propose an unsupervised model selection scheme (CD-CV) that tunes t and λ by matching empirical expectations Eq[u] ≈ Ep[r u] over random test functions u (linear and half-space indicators). They compare the proposed FIRE estimators to KMM, LSIF, and a thresholded inverse KDE on USPS, CPUsmall, and Kin8nm.

Connections to existing work are well articulated. KMM enforces Ep[rΦ] = Eq[Φ] in an RKHS, which corresponds to the same integral identity as Type I; the paper argues FIRE regularizes the function r directly in an RKHS and provides a natural out-of-sample extension. LSIF/KLIEP minimize f-divergence or L2,p directly over a parametric family, whereas FIRE uses operator inversion plus RKHS regularization. The positioning within classical inverse problem theory (Tikhonov–Phillips, spectral analysis) is apt and builds on prior RKHS/inverse problems work (e.g., De Vito et al.). Theoretical rates are discussed relative to Gaussian convolutions on Rd and manifolds.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Casting density-ratio estimation as an operator inversion problem is elegant and unifies several existing approaches; the Type II formulation (q known at sampled p-points) is particularly useful for MCMC/unnormalized models.
- Simple, explicit algorithms: Closed-form solutions via kernel matrices make implementation straightforward; out-of-sample prediction is natural.
- Theoretical development: Nonasymptotic bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, with separation of approximation (t), regularization (λ), and sampling errors.
- Practical model selection: CD-CV is a reasonable, task-agnostic scheme grounded in the importance sampling identity and does not require labeled data.
- Relation to prior art: The paper situates FIRE relative to KMM, LSIF/KLIEP, and inverse problem literature, clarifying similarities and differences.

Weaknesses
- Positivity/normalization: r(x) ≥ 0 and Ep[r]=1 are not enforced. Unconstrained RKHS regression can yield negative or highly variable ratios, which is problematic for importance weighting. This contrasts with LSIF/KLIEP and KMM variants that impose non-negativity and sometimes normalization/clipping.
- Theory–algorithm gap: The theory mostly assumes the same Gaussian kernel for the operator and RKHS and focuses on Type I/II with L2,p. The paper introduces additional losses (mixed L2,p/L2,q and RKHS loss), but these are not analyzed theoretically, nor are the implications of using different kernels in practice discussed in depth.
- Approximation bias in Type II: The error due to Kt,p f ≈ q is controlled by t, but practical guidance for balancing t vs. λ vs. sample size is limited beyond asymptotic rates; sensitivity to t is not empirically probed.
- Empirical evaluation: 
  - Baselines could be expanded (e.g., RuLSIF/KuLSIF, IWCV variants). 
  - Many evaluations use the surrogate JCD metric rather than ground-truth r or downstream risk; synthetic datasets with known r or standard covariate-shift benchmarks would strengthen conclusions. 
  - Negativity/clipping behavior and robustness to heavy-tailed ratios are not reported.
  - Runtime/complexity and scalability (O(n^3) solves plus cross-validation) are not quantified.
- Clarity and reproducibility: While mathematically sound, notation is dense and algorithms could benefit from pseudocode. Details of CD-CV (number and scaling of test functions, random seeds, sensitivity) are sparse; code availability is not mentioned.

Quality
Technically solid overall. The operator identities are correct, empirical operators are standard, and the representer-based solutions are derived cleanly. The concentration bounds and rates appear plausible and informative, though some constants/conditions (e.g., boundary cases, dependence on Sobolev smoothness) could be discussed more transparently. Missing constraints on r are a practical concern.

Clarity
Generally well written but heavy on notation. Sections 2–3 would benefit from clearer separation between the core methods (Type I/II with L2,p) and variants, and from algorithm summaries. The CD-CV section needs more implementation detail.

Originality
Moderate to strong. The Fredholm operator perspective and Type II formulation within RKHS regularization, together with manifold extensions and the CD-CV criterion, provide a novel combination beyond KMM/LSIF. The link to inverse problems is conceptually valuable.

Significance
Potentially impactful for covariate shift, importance sampling with unnormalized densities, and broader operator-theoretic kernel methods. Practical significance hinges on handling positivity/normalization and demonstrating robust gains across tasks.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean unifying formulation with explicit, usable algorithms.
- Theoretical guarantees including manifold settings.
- Sensible unsupervised model selection.
- Clear relation to and extension of KMM/LSIF.

Cons for acceptance
- No enforcement of r ≥ 0 or Ep[r]=1; potential instability in practice.
- Limited empirical validation (few datasets, surrogate metrics, missing modern baselines, no runtime).
- Theory does not cover all proposed loss variants or differing kernels, leaving practical guidance thin.

Suggestions for improvement
- Enforce non-negativity and (optionally) normalization: e.g., constrained quadratic programs on coefficients, exponential parameterization r=exp(g), or post-hoc clipping/renormalization with analysis of induced bias–variance trade-offs.
- Expand experiments: include synthetic data with known r, standard covariate-shift benchmarks, and recent baselines (RuLSIF/KuLSIF, IWCV). Report downstream risks (regression/classification), negativity rates, clipping effects, and runtime/memory.
- Provide guidance on choosing t vs λ, including ablations and sensitivity. Clarify when to use Type I vs Type II in practice.
- Analyze the mixed L2,p/L2,q and RKHS-loss variants, or restrict focus to the core setting to streamline the paper.
- Improve clarity with algorithm boxes, precise CD-CV protocol (number/scaling of test functions, folds), and discussion of kernel choices (operator vs RKHS).

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a coherent and theoretically grounded perspective with practical algorithms and a useful tuning criterion. Strengthening empirical validation and addressing positivity/normalization would considerably improve its impact. My recommendation would move to clear accept if the experimental section were expanded and constraints on r were incorporated or systematically studied. Confidence: medium.
