This paper presents a novel and principled approach to the problem of density ratio estimation, q(x)/p(x), by reformulating it as a classical inverse problem. The core idea is to frame the estimation as the task of solving a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors propose two main formulations: Type I, `Kp(q/p) = Kq(1)`, which is suitable when samples from both distributions p and q are available; and Type II, `Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q`, which applies when q is a known function but may be difficult to sample from. The resulting ill-posed integral equations are solved using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework, leading to a family of algorithms dubbed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels and introduces a practical, unsupervised model selection method called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV). This work connects to a significant body of existing literature on density ratio estimation, particularly methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF, but provides a new theoretical lens and a flexible algorithmic framework.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of the paper is its novel formulation. Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is an elegant and powerful idea. It connects a popular machine learning problem to a well-understood area of classical analysis and operator theory. This new perspective is a significant conceptual contribution and opens the door for applying established tools from inverse problems and spectral theory to this domain, as demonstrated by the authors' theoretical analysis.

2.  **Quality (Theoretical):** The paper is technically sound and provides a rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors derive concentration and error bounds, along with convergence rates for the FIRE estimator using Gaussian kernels (Theorems 1 & 3, Corollaries 2 & 4). The analysis considers distributions on both Euclidean space and compact manifolds, which adds to its generality and depth. This level of theoretical support is a major advantage over more heuristic approaches.

3.  **Quality (Algorithmic):** The proposed FIRE algorithms are simple, direct, and practical. By leveraging the RKHS framework and the Representer Theorem, the optimization problems yield closed-form solutions involving standard kernel matrix operations (e.g., Eq. 8, 10). This makes the methods easy to implement and computationally efficient compared to iterative optimization schemes. The framework is also flexible, allowing for different loss functions (L2,p, L2,q, RKHS norm) to be incorporated seamlessly.

4.  **Practical Contribution (Model Selection):** The paper addresses the crucial and often-overlooked problem of unsupervised model selection for density ratio estimation. The proposed CD-CV method, based on checking the importance sampling equality with a set of test functions, is a principled and practical solution. This is a highly valuable contribution, as hyperparameter tuning (e.g., kernel width, regularization) is a major challenge in unsupervised settings.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Exposition:** While generally well-written, the paper is quite dense. The transition from the continuous integral formulations to the empirical, sample-based optimization problems could be explained more explicitly. For example, a clearer derivation of how the L2,p norm `‖Kpf −Kq1‖^2_L2,p` becomes the empirical sum in Equation 7 would benefit readers less familiar with these techniques. A bit more intuition behind the matrix forms in Equation 8 would also enhance readability.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is solid but could be strengthened.
    *   **Comparison with KMM:** The paper correctly identifies KMM as the most closely related work and discusses the connection in the introduction. However, KMM is absent from the quantitative comparisons in the experiments section, with the authors noting KMM lacks a natural out-of-sample extension. While this is true, its omission is conspicuous. A more thorough comparison, perhaps including in-sample performance or discussing a simple out-of-sample extension for KMM, would provide a more complete picture of FIRE's advantages.
    *   **CD-CV Test Functions:** The choice of random linear and half-space functions for CD-CV feels somewhat arbitrary. A discussion on the principles for selecting a good family of test functions `U` would be valuable. For instance, how does the performance of CD-CV depend on the complexity or diversity of these functions?
    *   **Baselines:** The TIKDE (Thresholded Inverse Kernel Density Estimator) baseline feels like a strawman. Its performance is highly dependent on a manually chosen threshold, and it is known to have issues. Including more modern or robust baselines could provide a more compelling case for FIRE's performance.

3.  **Relationship to KMM:** The paper states that one of its formulations (using the RKHS norm) "can be viewed as a regularized version of KMM (with a different optimization procedure)." This is a key insight that deserves more exploration. The difference in the optimization procedure is not fully elaborated. Is the main advantage of FIRE's approach simply the inclusion of a regularization term for stability, or are there more fundamental benefits to the Tikhonov-style solution over the constrained quadratic program of KMM? A clearer head-to-head discussion on this point would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The central idea of using Fredholm integral equations is novel and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithms. The work is technically sound, with strong theoretical guarantees and a practical, well-motivated method for model selection. The weaknesses are primarily in the experimental section and points of clarity, which are addressable and do not detract from the core contribution. The paper successfully advances the state of the art by providing a new, powerful, and flexible framework for a fundamental machine learning problem. It is likely to be influential and inspire follow-up work.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
