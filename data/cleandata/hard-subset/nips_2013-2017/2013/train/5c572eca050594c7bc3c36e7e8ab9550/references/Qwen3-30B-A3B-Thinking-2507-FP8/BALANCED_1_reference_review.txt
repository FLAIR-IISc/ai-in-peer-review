

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation by formulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using regularization within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors develop two main formulations (Type I and Type II) and propose a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), along with a new unsupervised model selection method called CD-CV. The work connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problem theory, offering a fresh perspective on an important problem in machine learning with applications to transfer learning and importance sampling.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Novelty:** The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing a clear connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations, a classical inverse problem in mathematical analysis. This theoretical perspective is novel and provides a fresh framework for understanding existing methods.

2. **Comprehensive Theoretical Analysis:** The authors provide thorough error bounds and convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings in Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and addresses important aspects like the role of kernel bandwidth and regularization parameters.

3. **Practical Algorithm Development:** The paper successfully bridges theory and practice by deriving explicit, implementable algorithms from the theoretical framework. The FIRE algorithms are well-motivated and the CD-CV model selection method addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning.

4. **Clear Comparisons with Existing Work:** The paper effectively differentiates its approach from related methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), highlighting both theoretical and practical distinctions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section is somewhat limited. Only three datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) are used, and the resampling methods (PCA(a,b) and label-based) are not sufficiently justified or explained. The comparison with KMM is incomplete since the authors state they "do not compare with KMM as out-of-sample extension is necessary for fair comparison," but this claim needs stronger justification.

2. **Computational Scalability Concerns:** The paper does not address the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, particularly the need to solve linear systems of size n×n (where n is the number of samples). This is a significant limitation for practical applications with large datasets, which are common in modern machine learning.

3. **Narrow Scope of Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical results are limited to Gaussian kernels, which restricts the generalizability of the findings. The paper could benefit from discussing how the results might extend to other kernel families.

4. **Underdeveloped Manifold Learning Connection:** While the paper mentions theoretical results for distributions on sub-manifolds, the experimental evaluation does not leverage this capability, missing an opportunity to demonstrate the practical value of the manifold analysis.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to the field of density ratio estimation, establishing a valuable connection between classical inverse problem theory and a key machine learning problem. The theoretical analysis is strong, and the proposed FIRE algorithms are well-motivated and implementable. However, the limited experimental evaluation and lack of discussion about computational scalability are significant drawbacks that prevent the paper from making a more substantial contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend rejection, but with constructive suggestions for improvement. The paper has strong theoretical foundations but would benefit from:

1. Expanding the experimental evaluation to include larger datasets and more comprehensive comparisons (including KMM with proper out-of-sample extension).

2. Addressing computational scalability concerns by analyzing the complexity of the proposed algorithms and potentially suggesting approximations for large-scale settings.

3. Providing more detailed justification for the resampling methods used in experiments.

4. Exploring the practical implications of the manifold analysis in the experimental section.

5. Discussing how the theoretical results might extend to non-Gaussian kernels.

The paper's core idea is valuable and potentially impactful, but it requires more thorough experimental validation and discussion of practical implementation challenges to meet the high standards of NeurIPS. With these improvements, the paper could make a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation and inverse problems in machine learning.
