Summary and relation to prior work
The paper reframes density ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as solving a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two formulations are proposed:
- Type I: Kp r = Kq 1, where Kρ is a kernel integral operator with respect to ρ. This setting assumes samples from both p and q (q being a density).
- Type II: Kt,p r ≈ q for a local kernel kt (e.g., Gaussian), leveraging the approximate identity property. This setting assumes samples from p and pointwise access to q (not necessarily a density).

Both are solved via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS H, yielding closed-form solutions (linear systems) by the representer theorem. The resulting methods (FIRE) are simple to implement and have natural out-of-sample extension.

Theoretically, the paper provides concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on compact manifolds, with explicit dependencies on kernel bandwidth t, regularization λ, and sample sizes n (from p) and m (from q). An unsupervised model selection procedure (CD-CV) is proposed that uses the importance sampling identity to define validation losses via families of test functions, avoiding labels.

The work is related to KMM (Gretton et al.), which can be viewed as the H-norm version of the Type I equation, and LSIF/KLIEP (Sugiyama et al.), which minimize L2,p or KL risks over parametric families. The authors position FIRE as a regularized operator-theoretic approach that keeps the ratio itself in an RKHS, allowing stability, closed-form solutions, and out-of-sample evaluation.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The operator-theoretic derivations are standard and carefully connected to RKHS regularization. The empirical surrogates and representer-theorem-based solutions are correct. The approximation Kt * f ≈ f for local kernels is used appropriately; the stated O(t) bias is standard.
- Theory: The paper gives non-asymptotic bounds with explicit dependencies for both Type I and Type II, including manifold extensions. This is a substantial technical contribution. That said, parts of the statements contain typographical issues (e.g., “56 d” presumably means 5/6·d), and the rates degrade with dimension as expected for kernel methods. The assumptions (bounded densities; Sobolev smoothness of r and related quantities) are reasonable but should be stated more explicitly early on.
- Model selection: CD-CV is a sensible unsupervised criterion derived from the same identities; the connection to LSIF/KLIEP risk proxies is acknowledged. A theoretical justification (e.g., consistency of selected hyperparameters) is not provided, which limits guarantees.
- Experiments: The procedure is tested on several datasets with covariate shift, comparing against LSIF and a KDE-based baseline. Some experiments omit KMM on the grounds of out-of-sample extension; while KMM indeed optimizes weights at training points, practical out-of-sample approximations exist. The empirical section would be stronger with broader baselines and clearer reporting of variance, effect sizes, and computational costs. Nevertheless, the results are indicative and match the claims about stability and efficiency.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with a clear operator viewpoint and explicit algorithms. The notation is heavy and occasionally overloaded (K, KH, Kp,p, Kp,q), which makes following some derivations harder. There are typos (e.g., “TiknonovPhilips”) and formatting issues in theorems/corollaries (ambiguous exponents), and some footnotes intrude into the text flow. Providing a concise notation table and proofreading the bounds would improve readability. The experimental section could be more explicit about dataset sizes, hyperparameter grids, and computational complexity (time/memory).

Originality
Recasting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation and solving it via RKHS Tikhonov is an elegant and, to my knowledge, novel synthesis, even if closely related to KMM’s moment-matching identity. The Type II formulation (q known pointwise; only p is sampled) and the manifold-specific analysis are particularly original components. The CD-CV procedure is closely related to prior risk surrogates but with a different use: test functions need not span the ratio, which is a clean conceptual shift.

Significance
Density ratio estimation is central to covariate shift and importance sampling. Providing a unified, regularized operator view with closed-form solutions, theoretical rates (including manifolds), and a practical unsupervised model selection strategy is valuable. The empirical evaluation suggests FIRE is competitive or better than LSIF/TIKDE and more stable than KMM (where compared in other parts of the paper). Broader and more thorough experiments would make the practical impact clearer, but the conceptual contribution is significant.

Pros
- Elegant, unified operator-theoretic framing (Type I/II) with simple kernel algorithms (closed-form linear systems).
- Solid theoretical analysis with explicit rates on Rd and manifolds.
- Practical, unsupervised model selection criterion aligned with the problem’s identities.
- Natural out-of-sample extension; connection and contrast to KMM and LSIF are well discussed.

Cons
- Experimental evaluation is limited: incomplete baselines (e.g., KMM omitted in places; no KLIEP/uLSIF variants; classifier-based ratio estimators), and limited ablations.
- No positivity constraint on r; negative estimates may arise and can matter in downstream weighting.
- CD-CV lacks theoretical guarantees; sensitivity to choice of test functions is only empirically explored.
- Notational heaviness and some typos/ambiguous exponents in bounds; complexity analysis is absent.
- Type II usefulness when q is known only up to a constant is mentioned but not addressed: the equation would be biased by the unknown scale.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can you enforce nonnegativity or normalization of r (e.g., via constrained KRR or exponential link) and does it help?
- How sensitive is CD-CV to the choice/number of test functions, and can you provide theory (e.g., uniform deviation bounds for JCD)?
- Please clarify the exact differences vs. KMM in the H-norm variant, and include a fair KMM baseline with a standard out-of-sample extension.
- Discuss computational complexity and scalability (n^3 linear solves) and any low-rank/Nyström variants.
- Clarify applicability of Type II when q is known only up to a multiplicative constant.

Overall assessment
This is a strong, well-motivated paper that advances density ratio estimation by tying it to classical inverse problems and RKHS regularization, with meaningful theoretical and practical contributions. With improved empirical breadth and clarity, it would be a solid fit for NeurIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
