

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper presents a novel perspective on density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors propose a unified framework, called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), that leverages regularization in RKHS to solve this inverse problem. The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) establishing the connection between density ratio estimation and classical integral equations, (2) providing a principled framework for solving the problem using regularization techniques, (3) delivering comprehensive theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, and (4) introducing CD-CV, an unsupervised model selection technique for parameter tuning.

## Strengths

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical contribution. The connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations is insightful and provides a fresh perspective on an important problem in machine learning. The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is particularly strong, providing detailed convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings in Euclidean space and on manifolds, which is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of density ratio estimation.

The FIRE algorithms are well-motivated, mathematically sound, and offer clear out-of-sample extensions, addressing a key limitation of KMM. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the related work, particularly in their detailed comparison with KMM. The CD-CV technique is a valuable practical contribution that addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing FIRE against multiple baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across regression, classification, and density ratio estimation tasks. The results demonstrate consistent competitive performance, particularly highlighting the advantages of their CD-CV model selection method.

## Weaknesses

While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper could better articulate the practical advantages of the Fredholm equation approach beyond theoretical guarantees. The connection to existing methods like LSIF and KLIEP could be more explicitly discussed, as these are also kernel-based density ratio estimation methods that might share similar theoretical properties.

The Type II setting introduces an approximation error term O(t) from the kernel approximation (Equation 3), which is not adequately addressed in the experimental section. The paper does not provide guidance on how to choose t to balance this error with regularization, which is crucial for practical implementation.

The theoretical analysis assumes that q(x)/p(x) belongs to Sobolev space W²₂(Ω), which may be restrictive in practice. The paper would benefit from discussing the implications of this assumption and whether it holds for typical applications of density ratio estimation.

The experimental section lacks sufficient detail on the CD-CV parameter selection process. While the method is proposed as unsupervised, the paper doesn't explain how the range of kernel widths and regularization parameters was determined, or how CD-CV compares to other model selection approaches in terms of computational efficiency.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Expand the discussion of the practical advantages of the Fredholm equation approach over existing methods. Provide specific examples where this perspective leads to better performance or simpler implementations.

2. Add a comparison with LSIF and KLIEP in the related work section, highlighting how the Fredholm equation framework relates to their approaches.

3. Include a brief discussion of the Sobolev space assumption and its practical implications. Consider adding a small experiment showing performance when this assumption is violated.

4. Provide more details on the CD-CV parameter selection process, including how the range of parameters was chosen and how the method compares to other model selection techniques in terms of computational cost.

5. Consider adding a brief case study where q(x) is explicitly known (as mentioned in the paper) but sampling from q is difficult, demonstrating the practical advantage of the Type II setting.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically strong paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The connection to Fredholm equations is novel and insightful, and the theoretical analysis is comprehensive. The FIRE algorithms are well-motivated and the experimental results support the theoretical claims.

The paper meets NeurIPS standards for quality, clarity, and significance. While the practical advantages of the approach could be better articulated, the theoretical foundation is solid and the contributions are substantial. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the minor weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding the practical implications of the theoretical assumptions and the implementation details of the CD-CV method.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the suggestions above. The paper makes a significant contribution to the understanding of density ratio estimation and offers a principled framework that advances the state of the art in this important area of machine learning.
