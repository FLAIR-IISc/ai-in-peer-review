

# Peer Review: "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

## Summary
This paper addresses density ratio estimation (q(x)/p(x)) by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, a classical inverse problem in operator theory. The authors propose two main algorithms (Type I and Type II) that use RKHS regularization to solve this inverse problem, resulting in the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) framework. Type I requires samples from both distributions p and q, while Type II only requires samples from p and known values of q at those points. The paper provides theoretical error bounds for Gaussian kernels in both Euclidean and manifold settings, and introduces an unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV) for parameter tuning. The work connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problem theory, which is not commonly used in this context, while maintaining the practicality of kernel methods.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contributions:** The paper's most significant strength is its novel theoretical framing of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation. This connection to classical inverse problem theory provides a principled foundation that allows for rigorous theoretical analysis using spectral methods. The authors derive comprehensive convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings in Euclidean space and on manifolds, which is a substantial theoretical contribution. The bounds (Theorems 1-4) are well-motivated and address important practical considerations like manifold structure and kernel bandwidth.

**Practical Algorithm Design:** The FIRE algorithms are well-motivated and directly implementable. The authors provide explicit formulas for the solutions using the representer theorem, making the algorithms straightforward to implement. The CD-CV model selection method is particularly valuable, as it addresses a persistent challenge in unsupervised learning: how to tune hyperparameters without labeled data.

**Experimental Validation:** The experimental section demonstrates strong empirical performance compared to several baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across multiple datasets and tasks (regression, classification). The authors show that their method is both more accurate and computationally efficient, which is crucial for practical adoption. The use of multiple performance metrics and test function families adds credibility to the results.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Comparison with Recent Work:** While the paper mentions connections to KMM and LSIF, it doesn't provide a detailed comparison of how the theoretical guarantees differ from recent work. For example, [26] provides theoretical analysis of KMM, and it would strengthen the paper to discuss how the FIRE framework's guarantees differ or improve upon these. The comparison with LSIF is also somewhat superficial, as LSIF has been extended in several recent papers.

**Theoretical Scope:** The theoretical analysis is limited to Gaussian kernels. While Gaussian kernels are widely used, it would be valuable to discuss how the framework might extend to other kernels (e.g., polynomial, Laplacian) or to non-parametric settings. The paper mentions "other norms and loss functions" but doesn't explore these in depth.

**Implementation Details:** The paper doesn't provide sufficient details about computational complexity or scalability to large datasets. The theoretical bounds suggest certain dependencies on dimensionality (d), but the paper doesn't discuss how the method scales with data size or dimension in practice. This is particularly important for the manifold case, where computational costs could be prohibitive.

**Connection to Related Work:** The paper could better contextualize its contribution relative to other inverse problem approaches to density estimation. For example, [12] and [4] use inverse problem frameworks for density estimation, but the paper doesn't clearly differentiate its approach from these works.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance Assessment

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The proofs are referenced to supplemental material, and the experimental results are comprehensive. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., Type II has an error term depending on the kernel).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the connection between the Fredholm equation formulation and the practical algorithms is well-explained. The experimental setup is described with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is novel in this context. While density ratio estimation is well-studied, the connection to classical inverse problem theory provides a fresh perspective that has not been previously explored in the machine learning literature. The CD-CV model selection method is also a notable contribution.

**Significance:** Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem in transfer learning, covariate shift, and importance sampling. The FIRE framework provides a principled, theoretically grounded approach that outperforms existing methods in experiments. The unsupervised CD-CV method addresses a critical practical challenge in the field. These contributions have clear potential for adoption by practitioners.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution by connecting density ratio estimation to classical inverse problem theory, providing strong theoretical guarantees, and introducing a practical, efficient algorithm with a novel unsupervised model selection method. The theoretical and empirical results are compelling, and the work addresses a problem of clear significance in machine learning.

While the paper could be strengthened by more thorough comparisons with recent theoretical work on density ratio estimation and more detailed discussion of computational aspects, these are relatively minor concerns compared to the paper's substantial contributions. The work is well-suited for NIPS as it combines theoretical depth with practical significance, and it advances the state of the art in density ratio estimation.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper is of high quality, presents novel theoretical insights, and offers a practical approach to an important problem. It would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
