

# Peer Review: "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation (q(x)/p(x)) by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using Tikhonov regularization with RKHS norm penalty. The authors develop two algorithms (Type I and Type II settings) within the kernel learning framework, called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). They provide theoretical error bounds for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, and introduce an unsupervised model selection technique called CD-CV. The work connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problem theory, which is a fresh perspective compared to the standard approaches in the field.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Theoretical Framework**: The paper's core contribution is framing density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem (Fredholm equation), establishing a meaningful connection to operator theory and spectral analysis. This provides a principled foundation for understanding and solving density ratio estimation, which is distinct from most prior work that treats it as an optimization problem without theoretical grounding.

2. **Clear Algorithmic Development**: The authors successfully translate the theoretical framework into practical algorithms (FIRE) with explicit, implementable formulas. The derivation of the Representer Theorem solutions for both Type I and Type II settings is technically sound and well-explained.

3. **Theoretical Rigor**: The error bounds and convergence rates (Theorems 1-4) for Gaussian kernels in both Euclidean and manifold settings are substantial contributions. The analysis properly accounts for the different behaviors on manifolds versus Euclidean space, which is a sophisticated treatment.

4. **Practical Model Selection**: The CD-CV method for unsupervised parameter selection is a valuable contribution that addresses a critical practical challenge in density ratio estimation. The connection to importance sampling principles makes it theoretically grounded.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Assumptions**: The theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions (e.g., q/p, q/p² ∈ W²₂(Ω)) that are not justified or discussed in terms of practical validity. For real-world applications, these Sobolev space conditions may be difficult to verify.

2. **Limited Experimental Comparison**: The experiments only compare with KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE, but omit more recent methods like KLIEP (Kullback-Leibler Importance Estimation Procedure) and other advanced density ratio estimators. The paper claims "strong performance" but doesn't provide sufficient statistical evidence or comparison with the state-of-the-art.

3. **Ambiguity in Type II Setting**: The Type II setting has an error term depending on the kernel (O(t) for Gaussian), but the paper doesn't clearly explain how this affects practical implementation. The trade-off between kernel bandwidth and approximation error isn't sufficiently discussed.

4. **Lack of Theoretical Parameter Selection Guidance**: The paper relies on CD-CV for parameter selection, but the theoretical error bounds (Theorems 1-4) don't provide clear guidance on how to choose λ and t to minimize the bound. This disconnect between theory and practice weakens the contribution.

## Originality and Significance

The paper's main contribution - framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation - is genuinely novel within the context of machine learning. While density ratio estimation has been extensively studied (e.g., [9, 10, 23]), this operator-theoretic perspective is fresh and provides a new lens for understanding the problem. The connection to classical inverse problems (e.g., [5]) is particularly valuable.

However, the significance is somewhat limited by the fact that density ratio estimation is already a well-studied problem with several effective methods. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the theoretical framework leads to substantial practical advantages over existing methods. The experimental results show "better computational efficiency," but don't provide clear evidence that this translates to meaningful improvements in real-world applications.

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper with a novel theoretical perspective that could be valuable for researchers interested in the mathematical foundations of density ratio estimation. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and addresses important settings (manifolds, Euclidean space). However, the paper would be significantly stronger with:

1. More thorough experimental comparisons with recent state-of-the-art methods
2. Clearer discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical assumptions
3. Better integration between the theoretical error bounds and the CD-CV parameter selection method
4. More explicit discussion of how this framework differs from KMM beyond the optimization procedure

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of density ratio estimation, but falls short in demonstrating clear practical advantages over existing methods. The theoretical framework is interesting, but the paper would benefit from stronger empirical validation of its claims.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept** (with reservations)

The paper's theoretical contribution is strong and novel, but the experimental validation is insufficient to fully support the claims of superior performance. With additional experiments comparing with more recent methods and better integration between theory and practice, this would be a strong NIPS paper. As it stands, it makes an important theoretical contribution but doesn't fully demonstrate its practical significance.

## Specific Recommendations for Authors

1. Expand the experimental section to include comparisons with KLIEP and more recent density ratio estimation methods.
2. Provide a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical assumptions (e.g., how to check if q/p ∈ W²₂(Ω)).
3. Clarify the relationship between the theoretical error bounds and the CD-CV parameter selection method.
4. Discuss the Type II setting's kernel bandwidth trade-off more thoroughly, as it's a practical concern that affects implementation.
