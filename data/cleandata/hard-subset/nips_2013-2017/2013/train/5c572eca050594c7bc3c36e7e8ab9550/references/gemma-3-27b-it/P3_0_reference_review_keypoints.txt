- Novel formulation: reframes density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation enabling operator-theoretic treatment.  
- Method (FIRE): applies Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding two formulations (Type I & II) depending on sample availability.  
- Algorithms: provides explicit, easily implementable solutions via the Representer Theorem.  
- Theoretical rigor: derives convergence rates and error bounds (notably for Gaussian kernels).  
- Connection to prior work: clearly relates to and differentiates from KMM and LSIF, highlighting regularization and stability advantages.  
- CD-CV: proposes cross-density cross-validation for unsupervised parameter selection.  
- Empirical evaluation: comprehensive across datasets and baselines but lacks focused scenarios showing clear superiority.  
- Strength: flexibility to handle cases with/without samples from both distributions.  
- Strength: strong linkage between classical inverse problems and modern kernel methods.  
- Strength: practical relevance due to unsupervised parameter tuning and implementable algorithms.  
- Weakness: empirical analysis depth — needs focused experiments (e.g., high-dimensional or small-sample regimes).  
- Weakness: limited computational complexity analysis; matrix inversions could be a bottleneck.  
- Weakness: theoretical results mainly for Gaussian kernels; generality to other kernels is unclear.  
- Weakness: CD-CV intuition and choice of test functions need clearer explanation.  
- Suggested improvement: concentrate experiments on scenarios where FIRE should outperform competitors.  
- Suggested improvement: add detailed time and space complexity discussion, especially for matrix operations.  
- Suggested improvement: discuss and/or evaluate other kernel choices empirically.  
- Suggested improvement: elaborate on CD-CV rationale and how test functions affect performance.
