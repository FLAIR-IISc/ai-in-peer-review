## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and mathematically rigorous approach to density ratio estimation, framing it as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging tools from regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and offers a compelling connection between classical operator theory and modern kernel methods. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the empirical evaluation, while comprehensive, could benefit from a more focused analysis on the advantages of FIRE compared to established methods in specific scenarios. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper proposes a new perspective on density ratio estimation (crucial for importance sampling, transfer learning, etc.) by reformulating the problem as solving a Fredholm integral equation. This allows the authors to apply tools from functional analysis, specifically Tikhonov regularization within an RKHS framework, leading to two formulations (Type I & Type II) depending on the availability of samples from both densities or just knowledge of the target density’s values. They derive algorithms (FIRE) based on these formulations, demonstrate their connection to existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), and provide theoretical guarantees on convergence and error bounds, particularly for Gaussian kernels.  Crucially, they also propose a cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) technique for parameter selection.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the growing body of work on density ratio estimation, citing relevant papers like [6, 9, 10, 22]. The connection to KMM [9] is particularly well-highlighted, and the authors clearly articulate how their approach differs – specifically emphasizing the regularization aspect and the resulting stability and theoretical tractability not present in KMM. The comparison to LSIF [10] is also relevant, and the discussion of related work in kernel methods and inverse problems is thorough. The authors acknowledge the influence of spectral regularization approaches [4, 11, 12] and highlight the originality of their framework.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:**  The framing of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is insightful and opens up avenues for applying powerful tools from classical analysis.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides solid theoretical guarantees for the proposed algorithms, including convergence rates and error bounds for Gaussian kernels. This is a significant strength, differentiating it from many purely empirical approaches.
* **Flexibility:** The two formulations (Type I and Type II) offer adaptability to different scenarios – when samples from both distributions are available or when the target density is known pointwise.
* **Connection to Existing Methods:**  The authors clearly articulate the relationship between FIRE and KMM, providing valuable context and demonstrating a deeper understanding of the field.
* **Practical Algorithm (FIRE):** The resulting algorithms are explicit and easily implementable, thanks to the Representer Theorem.
* **Unsupervised Parameter Selection:** The CD-CV technique addresses a crucial challenge in unsupervised learning and demonstrates the paper’s practical relevance.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The paper evaluates FIRE on multiple datasets and compares it against several baselines, offering a good overview of its performance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Empirical Analysis Depth:** While the experiments are broad, the analysis sometimes lacks depth.  It would be beneficial to focus on specific scenarios where FIRE demonstrably *outperforms* existing methods (e.g., high-dimensional data, limited samples, etc.).  Simply showing competitive performance isn’t as convincing as highlighting clear advantages.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper briefly mentions computational efficiency, but a more detailed analysis of the time and space complexity of FIRE compared to KMM and LSIF would be valuable.  The matrix inversions involved in solving for the coefficients (Eq. 8) could be a bottleneck for large datasets.
* **Choice of Kernel:** The theoretical analysis primarily focuses on Gaussian kernels. While this is a common and well-understood choice, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss the implications of using other kernels and potentially provide some empirical results with different kernel choices.
* **CD-CV explanation:** A slightly more detailed explanation of the intuition behind the CD-CV method and how the choice of test functions impacts its effectiveness would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and mathematically sound approach to a fundamental problem in machine learning.
* The theoretical analysis is strong and provides valuable insights into the behavior of the proposed algorithms.
* The practical algorithms are well-defined and easy to implement.
* The CD-CV technique addresses a practical challenge in unsupervised learning.
* The empirical evaluation, while not perfect, demonstrates competitive performance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical evaluation could be more focused on demonstrating clear advantages of FIRE over existing methods.
* The computational complexity analysis is limited.
* The theoretical focus on Gaussian kernels might limit the generality of the results.




**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Empirical Focus:** In the experimental section, focus on scenarios where FIRE is expected to shine (e.g., high-dimensional data, small sample sizes).
* **Complexity Analysis:**  Add a section discussing the computational complexity of FIRE, particularly highlighting the cost of the matrix inversions.
* **Kernel Exploration:** Briefly discuss the implications of using different kernels and potentially provide some empirical results.
* **CD-CV Details:** Elaborate on the rationale behind the CD-CV method and the choice of test functions.




**Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The novel formulation, rigorous theoretical analysis, and practical algorithms make it a worthy addition to the NIPS program. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above.**
