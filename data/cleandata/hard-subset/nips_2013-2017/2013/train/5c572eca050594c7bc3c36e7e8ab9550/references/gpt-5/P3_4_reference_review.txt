Summary
The paper proposes to estimate the density (or function) ratio r(x) = q(x)/p(x) by recasting it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it with Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two variants are studied:
- Type I: Kp r = Kq 1, where Kρ is an integral operator defined by a (not necessarily “local”) kernel k. This setting applies when samples from both p and q are available.
- Type II: Kt,p r ≈ q, where kt is a δ-like kernel (e.g., Gaussian with small bandwidth), and only samples from p with q(xi) known at those locations are needed.

Using empirical approximations of Kρ and the L2,p loss (and, for Type I, also mixed L2,p/L2,q and RKHS losses), the authors derive closed-form representer-theorem solutions (linear systems in the n points from p). They call the method FIRE. They analyze convergence for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and compact manifolds, giving high-probability L2,p error bounds and rates. Finally, they propose an unsupervised model-selection heuristic (CD-CV) based on moment-matching of random test functions, and present experiments comparing to LSIF, KMM (limited), a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, and importance sampling when applicable.

Relation to prior work
The operator-theoretic view connects to learning-as-inverse-problem (De Vito et al.) and kernel regularization. The formulation unifies and contrasts with KMM (Gretton et al.)—which minimizes an RKHS discrepancy of kernel means but solves for weights at sample points with constraints—and LSIF/KLIEP (Sugiyama et al.)—which minimize an L2,p or KL risk over a parametric function class. The CD-CV idea is related to importance-weighted cross-validation and moment-matching criteria used in LSIF/KLIEP, and to two-sample/IPM/MMD tests, though the connection is not explicitly made.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Casting ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation yields a clean linear inverse problem amenable to standard regularization and spectral analysis.
- Practical algorithms: Representer-theorem solutions give simple closed forms with natural out-of-sample evaluation (unlike classical KMM).
- Theoretical analysis: Nonasymptotic high-probability L2,p bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels, including the manifold setting, are valuable. The decomposition of error terms (kernel approximation t, bias λ, and sample/concentration) is informative.
- Scope: Type II handles cases where q is known pointwise but sampling from q is hard (e.g., some MCMC/unnormalized models), which is underexplored in the literature.
- Unsupervised model selection: CD-CV is simple and practical, and empirically effective in the provided examples.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Positivity/normalization: The estimator is unconstrained; it can output negative ratios and does not enforce Ep[r]=1. This is a practical issue for importance weighting (variance blow-up, invalid weights). LSIF/KLIEP and KMM explicitly encode constraints (nonnegativity, sum). Please discuss and, ideally, incorporate positivity/normalization (e.g., via nonnegativity constraints, exponential link, or post-hoc clipping/renormalization) and assess its impact.
- Experimental scope: Comparisons to KMM are limited (“no out-of-sample extension”), which is not compelling; KMM can be evaluated at training points and used for reweighting. Also missing are strong baselines such as probabilistic classification-based ratio estimation (logistic regression estimating q/p via odds), and recent LSIF variants (RuLSIF) and IWCV. Scalability and runtime comparisons (linear solve vs QP) are not reported.
- Clarity/derivations: Several typos/notation issues (e.g., exponents like “− 1 4+ 56 d” apparently meaning 1/(4+5d/6); inconsistent equation numbering; overloaded k, kt, kH; unclear normalization constants in Gram matrices). The linear-algebra steps leading to v = (K^2KH + nλI)−1 K⋯ should be shown once in detail (dimensions, normalizations). Assumptions in the theory (e.g., q/p and q/p^2 in W2
2, bounded densities, t→0 regime, equality of k and kH) deserve more discussion and interpretation.
- CD-CV: The proposed criterion is essentially matching moments of random test functions; it relates to IPMs/MMD. The choice of test classes (linear, halfspaces) and number F are important; please study sensitivity and link to theory (e.g., what objective is optimized in the limit?).
- Type II approximation error: The O(t) kernel bias is acknowledged; practical guidance on balancing t vs λ is limited. In higher dimensions, t must be small, which magnifies variance; empirical sensitivity analyses would help.

Originality
- The Fredholm-equation framing and RKHS-Tikhonov solution are a nice synthesis; Type I with RKHS loss also clarifies the relation to KMM and yields a regularized closed form. The manifold case in the theory is a plus. However, the overall approach remains close in spirit to existing kernel mean matching/density-ratio methods; clearly stating what is genuinely new (beyond the operator-theoretic lens and rates) would strengthen the novelty claim.

Significance
- If coupled with positivity/normalization and broader comparisons, FIRE could be a practical alternative to KMM/LSIF with stronger theoretical underpinnings and simple implementation. The Type II use case is promising. As presented, the empirical validation is not yet sufficient to establish state-of-the-art performance across regimes.

Clarity
- The paper would benefit from careful proofreading and notation cleanup, a brief algorithm box for each variant, and a discussion of computational complexity and numerics (regularization path, kernel width search, linear solver).

Questions for the authors
1. How often does FIRE output negative or highly variable weights? Do you recommend clipping/normalization, and how does that affect theory/empirics?
2. Can you connect CD-CV to IPM/MMD minimization, and justify the choice of test function families?
3. How do your rates compare to those in [Nguyen et al. 2007] and recent KMM analyses (e.g., Yu & Szepesvári), both in dependence on d and sample sizes?
4. Any results on large n (e.g., n>10^4) with iterative solvers or Nyström approximations?
5. Can the theory cover the case k ≠ kH (as used in parts of Section 2)?

Pros
- Clean formulation via Fredholm equation and RKHS regularization
- Closed-form representer solutions with out-of-sample evaluation
- Nontrivial theory including manifold case
- Practical unsupervised model selection heuristic

Cons
- No positivity/normalization constraints; practical risk for importance weighting
- Limited and incomplete empirical comparisons; weak justification for omitting KMM
- Notational/typographical issues; some derivations omitted
- Sensitivity to kernel/tuning not fully explored; scalability unaddressed

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper has solid ideas and theory, but the empirical section and practical considerations (positivity, baselines, clarity) need strengthening. With revisions addressing these points, it could be a strong contribution. Confidence: high.
