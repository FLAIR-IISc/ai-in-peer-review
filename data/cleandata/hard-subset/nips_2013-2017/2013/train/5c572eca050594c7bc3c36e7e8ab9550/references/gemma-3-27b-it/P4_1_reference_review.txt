This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques from RKHS theory. The core idea—connecting density ratio estimation to operator theory—is insightful and allows leveraging established theoretical tools. The authors present two types of formulations (Type I and Type II) and propose corresponding algorithms (FIRE) with theoretical guarantees, including concentration and error bounds for Gaussian kernels. An unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV) and comprehensive experimental results complete the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is a novel contribution. It bridges the gap between density estimation and classical operator theory, opening avenues for theoretical analysis and algorithmic development.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, including concentration and error bounds, especially for the Gaussian kernel.  The analysis extends to distributions on manifolds, enhancing the theoretical contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and generally clearly written. The motivation is well-explained, and the mathematical derivations are presented in a logical manner. The notation is consistent throughout.
*   **Completeness:** The paper provides a complete framework, including algorithms, theoretical analysis, a model selection technique, and experimental validation.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors clearly position their work within the existing literature on density ratio estimation, covariate shift, and kernel methods, drawing connections to KMM and LSIF. They highlight the advantages of their approach over these existing methods.
*   **Practical Algorithm:** The FIRE algorithms derived from the theoretical framework are explicit and easily implementable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While the algorithms are explicit, the need to invert kernel matrices (even with approximations) could pose computational challenges in high dimensions or with large datasets. This aspect is not thoroughly discussed in the paper.
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are extensive, they are primarily focused on a few datasets.  Exploring more diverse datasets and higher-dimensional problems would further strengthen the empirical validation. The performance comparison to state-of-the-art methods feels somewhat limited by the chosen settings (e.g., KMM requires out-of-sample extension which is why it's not directly comparable).
*   **Choice of Gaussian Kernel:** The theoretical analysis heavily relies on the Gaussian kernel. While reasonable, it’s unclear how well the results generalize to other kernels.  A discussion of the limitations and potential extensions to other kernels would be beneficial.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the algorithms might be sensitive to the choice of regularization parameter λ and kernel width t. The CD-CV method addresses this, but a sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of these parameters would be valuable.
*   **Type II Limitation:** The Type II setting's restriction that *q* doesn't have to be a density is noted but not fully explored in terms of potential applications beyond standard density ratio estimation.  Highlighting specific scenarios where this is advantageous would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to density ratio estimation.
*   The rigorous theoretical analysis, including convergence rates and error bounds, significantly contributes to the understanding of the proposed methods.
*   The FIRE algorithms are well-defined and can be readily implemented.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach compared to existing methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The computational complexity of the method could be a limiting factor in practical applications.
*   The heavy reliance on the Gaussian kernel restricts the generality of the theoretical results.
*   The experimental evaluation, while thorough, could benefit from a wider range of datasets and dimensions.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and theoretically well-grounded approach to density ratio estimation. The connection to Fredholm integral equations and RKHS theory is a significant contribution, and the theoretical analysis is impressive. While the computational complexity and reliance on the Gaussian kernel are potential limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding computational complexity and kernel generalization. Specifically, discussing scalability, hinting at potential approximations for large datasets, and acknowledging the limitations of the Gaussian kernel assumptions would enhance the paper’s impact.
