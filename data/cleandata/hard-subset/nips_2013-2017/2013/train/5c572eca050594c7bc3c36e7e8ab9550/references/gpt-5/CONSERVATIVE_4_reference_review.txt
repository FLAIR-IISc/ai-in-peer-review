Summary
This paper proposes to cast density ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and to solve it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two settings are considered: Type I (samples from both p and q; solve Kp r = Kq 1) and Type II (only samples from p and pointwise access to q at those samples; solve Kt,p r ≈ q). The representer theorem yields closed-form kernel ridge–like estimators (FIRE). The authors provide generalization bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, and introduce an unsupervised model selection heuristic (CD-CV) based on moment-matching with random test functions. Empirical comparisons to KMM, LSIF, and a thresholded inverse KDE are reported on several datasets.

Strengths
- Clear operator-theoretic framing that unifies several prior ideas (KMM, LSIF) under a well-known inverse problem perspective.
- Simple closed-form estimators derived via standard RKHS tools; easy to implement.
- Finite-sample bounds and convergence rates are provided, including manifold support cases.
- The CD-CV criterion is practical and aligns with the estimation objective (moment matching).
- The Type II setting could be relevant when q is known up to a constant but sampling is hard (e.g., MCMC contexts).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established literature: KMM explicitly uses Eq[Φ]=Ep[rΦ], which is the same operator identity; LSIF/ KLIEP minimize L2,p/KL discrepancies for r. Similar operator inversion in RKHS has a long history [3,20]. The manuscript does not sufficiently delineate what is substantively new beyond a repackaging and some specific empirical solvers.
- Weak empirical validation: no comparisons to strong and widely used baselines such as probabilistic classification-based ratio estimation (e.g., logistic regression discriminating samples to get r(x)), uLSIF/RuLSIF variants, or IWCV-based model selection [22]. The claim that KMM lacks an out-of-sample extension is debatable and, more importantly, not a reason to omit it from tasks (e.g., reweighting training losses) where only training-point weights are needed.
- Positivity and normalization are not enforced. Density ratios are nonnegative and often constrained by Ep[r]=1. FIRE may output negative values; this is not discussed, nor are remedies (e.g., nonnegativity constraints, exponential link, projection). This is a central practical issue for importance weighting.
- Support mismatch is not addressed: standard covariate shift requires supp(q) ⊆ supp(p). The paper doesn’t clarify assumptions or robustness when this is violated; thresholding in TIKDE suggests issues at low p(x), but FIRE’s behavior there is unclear.
- Theoretical results are upper bounds with unusual exponents (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2)) and are not compared to known minimax benchmarks for likelihood ratio estimation (e.g., [14]). No lower bounds or discussion of tightness. The role/choice of t and λ in achieving those rates is not practically connected to CD-CV.
- CD-CV largely reiterates established importance-weighted cross-validation ideas (moment matching), but the connection to prior IWCV [22] and to LSIF/KLIEP model selection is not fully drawn, nor is there a direct empirical comparison.
- Computational claims are not substantiated. FIRE requires solving n×n linear systems (O(n^3) naive). Runtime/scale comparisons vs. LSIF/KMM QP solvers are missing.
- Clarity issues: notation is overloaded (K denotes several objects), some equations mix spaces and norms without clearly stated domains, and assumptions (Sobolev regularity, bounds, “small enough t”) are only informally motivated.

Quality
Technically mostly sound at a high level (standard Tikhonov in RKHS). However, important practical aspects (nonnegativity, normalization, support considerations) are omitted. Theory appears standard for kernel inverse problems; without proofs here, I cannot verify edge conditions. Empirical evaluation does not rigorously establish superiority or robustness; ablations and sensitivity analyses are minimal.

Clarity
Readable overall but notation could be streamlined; several typos and informal arguments (e.g., δ-kernel approximations) would benefit from precise conditions. The link between the theory (choice of t, λ) and the CD-CV procedure is not clearly explained.

Originality
Moderate. The operator equation perspective is well known, and KMM is directly equivalent to the Type I identity in H. The specific formulations are incremental combinations of established components (Fredholm eqn + RKHS Tikhonov + representer theorem).

Significance
Potentially useful as a unifying exposition and for practitioners who want closed-form RKHS estimators; Type II might be relevant in specific applications. However, given limited novelty and insufficiently strong empirical evidence against baselines, impact seems modest.

Detailed suggestions
- Enforce r ≥ 0 (and possibly Ep[r]=1) via constraints or reparameterization; analyze its effect on stability and theory.
- Compare to classification-based ratio estimation (logistic/boosting with probability calibration) and to uLSIF/RuLSIF, KLIEP with their recommended CV. Include IWCV [22] as a model-selection baseline against CD-CV.
- Report computational complexity and wall-clock times; include larger n to test scalability.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for t and λ; examine robustness to support mismatch (e.g., when q puts mass in low-p regions).
- Discuss relations and differences to [3,20] and conditional mean embeddings [8], and to [14] minimax rates; position your rates relative to known results.
- Clarify Type II use cases and include a realistic experiment where q is known up to a constant and sampling is difficult (e.g., AIS-like settings).
- Improve notation and clearly separate kernels (kt vs kH), operators (Kp vs Kt,p), and spaces (L2,p vs H).

Pros
- Conceptually coherent operator-theoretic framing.
- Simple, closed-form estimators with general applicability.
- Some theoretical guarantees including manifold case.
- Practical, unsupervised model selection heuristic.

Cons
- Novelty over KMM/LSIF and prior inverse problem work is limited.
- Missing key practical constraints (nonnegativity/normalization).
- Weak/missing baselines and fairness concerns in experiments.
- Theory not tied to practice, and rates not contextualized.
- Scalability and stability claims not empirically supported.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The paper has a clean framework and some analysis, but the advances over established methods are incremental, and the empirical evidence and practical considerations are not sufficiently thorough for NeurIPS. Strengthening the baselines, enforcing proper constraints, clarifying theory–practice connections, and demonstrating clear advantages would improve the case for acceptance.
