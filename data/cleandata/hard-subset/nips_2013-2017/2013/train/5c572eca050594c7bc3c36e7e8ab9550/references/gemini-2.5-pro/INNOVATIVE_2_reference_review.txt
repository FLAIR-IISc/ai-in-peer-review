### **Summary**

This paper recasts the problem of density ratio estimation (estimating q(x)/p(x)) as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The key insight is to use the importance sampling identity, `Eq(h) = Ep(h * q/p)`, and replace the test function `h` with a kernel function `k(x, y)`. This "linearizes" the problem, yielding an operator equation `K_q 1 = K_p (q/p)`, where `K_p` and `K_q` are integral operators associated with densities p and q. The unknown density ratio `q/p` can then be solved for. Since this is an ill-posed inverse problem, the authors propose solving it using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This leads to a family of simple, closed-form kernel-based algorithms, which they name FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The authors provide two main settings: Type I, where samples from both p and q are available, and Type II, where samples from p are available and q is a known function. They provide a thorough theoretical analysis with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, including on manifolds. Finally, they introduce a novel unsupervised model selection technique, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), and demonstrate strong empirical performance against related methods like LSIF and a baseline kernel density estimator approach.

### **Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty and conceptual elegance. The reframing of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is a significant and beautiful contribution. It connects a popular problem in modern machine learning to a deep and well-understood area of classical analysis and operator theory. This is not merely an incremental improvement on existing methods; it is a fundamentally new perspective that opens up new avenues for theoretical analysis and algorithm design.

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core idea is highly original. While kernel methods are standard for this problem (e.g., KMM, LSIF), the explicit formulation as an integral equation to be inverted is a bold and powerful conceptual leap. This connection allows the authors to leverage the mature toolkit of inverse problems and spectral theory, leading to a principled and rigorous treatment of the problem. This work has the potential to influence how researchers think about not just density ratio estimation, but other related estimation problems.

2.  **Principled Framework:** The Fredholm approach provides a flexible and clear framework. It elegantly separates the core inverse problem from the choice of regularization (RKHS norm) and loss function (L2,p, L2,q, RKHS norm, etc.). This modularity is a significant advantage, leading to a family of related algorithms and making it clear where future variations could be introduced.

3.  **Theoretical Depth:** The paper is technically sound, providing a detailed theoretical analysis with convergence rates. The analysis covers not just the standard Euclidean case but also distributions on manifolds, which is a non-trivial and valuable extension. These results place the proposed algorithms on a firm theoretical footing.

4.  **Practical Contributions:** Beyond the core theoretical idea, the paper makes a strong practical contribution with the introduction of the Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) method. Unsupervised hyperparameter selection is a major challenge in this domain, and CD-CV offers a simple, intuitive, and effective solution. The empirical results demonstrate that FIRE, equipped with CD-CV, is competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art methods like LSIF.

### **Weaknesses**

While the paper is excellent, there are a few minor points that could be addressed. These do not detract from the overall significance of the contribution.

1.  **Experimental Comparison:** The experiments are well-executed, but the comparison could be slightly broader. For instance, while the authors correctly note that Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) does not have a natural out-of-sample extension, a more direct comparison on the training data or through some other metric might have been insightful, given its close relationship to the formulation using an RKHS loss. The performance gains over LSIF are solid but not always dramatic, which is acceptable given the novelty of the approach but worth noting.

2.  **Clarity of Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on assumptions that the density ratio `q/p` lies in a Sobolev space. While this is a standard assumption for this type of analysis, a brief discussion of its practical implications and when it might be violated would be helpful for the practitioner.

3.  **Algorithmic Novelty:** The final algorithmic solutions, involving the inversion of a regularized kernel matrix, are structurally similar to other kernel methods like kernel ridge regression. This is not a major weakness, as the novelty lies in the *derivation* and the underlying framework, not the final matrix equation. However, it's worth acknowledging that the power of the Fredholm formulation lies more in the new perspective and analytical tools it provides than in producing an algorithm with a completely novel computational form.

### **Recommendation**

I strongly recommend acceptance.

This paper is a prime example of what a NIPS paper should be: it introduces a bold, novel, and conceptually powerful idea that bridges different fields of mathematics and computer science. The reframing of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem is a significant contribution that advances our understanding of the problem. While the empirical gains are not an order of magnitude, the method is competitive, and the theoretical foundation and the proposed CD-CV model selection are substantial contributions in their own right. The paper prioritizes a significant new direction over incremental methodological perfection, and for this, it should be highly commended. This is an elegant piece of work that is likely to be influential and inspire follow-up research.
