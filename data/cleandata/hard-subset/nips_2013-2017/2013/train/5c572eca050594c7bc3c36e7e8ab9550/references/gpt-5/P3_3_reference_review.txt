Summary
The paper proposes to estimate the density ratio r(x)=q(x)/p(x) by recasting it as a linear inverse problem for an integral operator (a Fredholm equation of the first kind), and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two settings are treated: Type I, when samples from both p and q are available, uses the identity Kp r = Kq 1; Type II, when q is known (not necessarily a density) but only samples from p are available, uses Kt,p r ≈ q with a δ-kernel kt. Empirical objectives are constructed by replacing integrals with averages over samples, leading (via the representer theorem) to closed-form kernel algorithms (FIRE). The authors analyze these estimators using spectral techniques for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean domains and smooth manifolds, deriving concentration bounds and convergence rates. They also propose an unsupervised model selection criterion (CD-CV), which chooses hyperparameters by minimizing violations of the importance-sampling equality against a family of test functions, and present experiments comparing FIRE to LSIF, KMM (partially), and a KDE-based baseline.

Relation to prior work
The work is closely connected to kernel mean matching (KMM) and least-squares density ratio (LSIF/KLIEP). The Type I equality Eqq(Φ)=EprΦ underlies KMM; the paper correctly notes that their RKHS-loss variant with regularization is an operator-theoretic view and resembles a regularized KMM with a different optimization. The Type II formulation relates to convolution/heat-kernel approximations and the conditional mean embeddings literature. Connections to inverse problems and spectral regularization in RKHS are appropriate. Some additional related direct ratio estimators (e.g., uLSIF/RuLSIF) and MMD-based two-sample tests could be referenced to position CD-CV more precisely.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm first-kind inverse problem is elegant and unifies several existing methods under an operator-theoretic umbrella.
- Algorithmic simplicity: Closed-form kernel solutions via the representer theorem lead to practical estimators with natural out-of-sample extensions, unlike KMM’s weight-only QP.
- Theoretical analysis: Non-asymptotic error bounds and convergence rates are provided for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on smooth manifolds, leveraging heat-kernel approximations—this is technically interesting and broadens applicability beyond Euclidean settings.
- Practical model selection: CD-CV gives a fully unsupervised hyperparameter selection mechanism that is aligned with the target use (importance weighting).
- Flexibility: The framework allows different loss norms (L2,p, mixed L2,p/L2,q, RKHS) and accommodates both settings where q is sampled (Type I) and where q can be evaluated but not sampled (Type II).

Weaknesses
- Constraints/identifiability: Density ratios are nonnegative and satisfy Ep[r]=1, yet the proposed estimators are unconstrained. Without positivity or normalization, weights can be negative or poorly scaled, potentially harming downstream importance-weighted learners. The paper should discuss projections, positivity-preserving parameterizations (e.g., exp(g)), or adding Ep[f]=1 as a soft/hard constraint.
- Experimental evaluation: The comparison to KMM is limited; for many tasks (importance-weighted ERM), one only needs weights on p-sample points, so “out-of-sample extension” is not a blocker. Runtime and accuracy comparisons against KMM (and uLSIF) on standard covariate shift benchmarks are needed. Synthetic data with known ground-truth ratios would help quantify estimation error directly.
- CD-CV analysis: The validation criterion depends on a chosen function family U; guidance on how many functions are needed and sensitivity to U is limited, and no theory is provided. Using a characteristic kernel and MMD between the reweighted p and q (i.e., sup over an RKHS unit ball) would provide a more universal, function-class-agnostic criterion.
- Rates and assumptions: The exponents in the rates (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2)) appear conservative and unusual relative to known minimax rates for smooth ratio estimation; more discussion of optimality and spectral conditions would help. Some assumptions (e.g., q/p, q/p2 ∈ W2
2) are stated but their roles are not fully explained. The λ−1/6 factors in the bounds deserve interpretation.
- Notational/clarity issues: The presentation around empirical operators (e.g., Kp,p, K^2KH) could be streamlined; some typos (Tikhonov misspelled) and occasional ambiguity (kernel roles in loss vs. regularizer) impede readability.

Quality
Technically sound overall: the operator equalities are correct, the empirical formulations are standard, and the spectral arguments for Gaussian kernels on manifolds are plausible. However, the lack of constraints and limited empirical comparisons reduce the practical impact. The theoretical rates are informative but likely suboptimal; a discussion of tightness would improve the contribution.

Clarity
Good high-level exposition; sections 2–3 are dense and could benefit from clearer notation (dimensions of matrices, kernel roles) and a concise algorithm box. A short subsection on enforcing positivity/normalization and a more explicit computational complexity discussion (and potential Nyström/CG approximations) would help practitioners.

Originality
Moderately high. The Fredholm/inverse-problem lens and the unified kernel-regularized derivations are novel and insightful, even if closely related to KMM/LSIF. The manifold-aware analysis is a nice addition.

Significance
If strengthened, this framework could influence how density ratio estimation is taught and used, offering a principled, extensible view and practical estimators. As is, significance is limited by experimental breadth and by the omission of constraints crucial in applications.

Suggestions and questions
- Add Ep[f]=1 as a constraint or penalty and discuss positivity (e.g., nonnegativity constraints, exponential link).
- Evaluate against KMM (with and without positivity constraints) and uLSIF on synthetic and real covariate shift tasks; report runtime and memory.
- Consider MMD-based CD-CV with a characteristic kernel, and analyze its selection consistency.
- Discuss computational scaling (O(n^3)) and provide results with Nyström/sketching or early stopping (spectral regularization).
- Clarify the role of q/p2 ∈ W2
2 in the theory and comment on optimality of rates.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant unifying operator-theoretic framework with closed-form kernel algorithms
- Nontrivial theory on Euclidean domains and manifolds
- Practical unsupervised model selection idea aligned with use-case

Cons for acceptance
- Missing positivity/normalization constraints; practical impact unclear
- Limited and somewhat unfair experimental comparison (KMM excluded; no synthetic ground truth)
- CD-CV lacks theoretical support and may be sensitive to test-function choice
- Clarity and notation issues

Overall recommendation: Borderline reject (leaning reject). The paper has strong ideas and solid theory, but needs stronger empirical validation, treatment of constraints, and a clearer presentation to meet the NeurIPS bar.

Confidence: High.
