

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, a classical inverse problem in mathematics. The authors develop the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) algorithms within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework, providing a principled approach that connects density ratio estimation to operator theory. The paper establishes two main formulations (Type I and Type II) and provides theoretical guarantees for both Euclidean and manifold domains using spectral analysis. The authors also introduce CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), an unsupervised parameter selection method that addresses a key practical challenge in density ratio estimation.

The work builds upon and significantly extends previous approaches like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10]. While KMM can be viewed as a special case of their Type I formulation, the authors clarify that FIRE provides a more stable solution with natural out-of-sample extension and better theoretical guarantees. The paper also properly contextualizes the work within the broader literature on inverse problems in machine learning [3, 20] and density estimation [12, 4].

Strengths:
1. The theoretical contribution is strong, with rigorous error bounds and convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings in various domains (Euclidean space, compact sub-manifolds). Theorem 1 and Theorem 3 provide precise convergence rates that are valuable for practitioners.
2. The framework is flexible, allowing different norms (L2,p, L2,q, RKHS) and loss functions, making it adaptable to diverse scenarios.
3. The CD-CV method for parameter selection is a significant practical contribution that addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning.
4. The experimental evaluation demonstrates competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) on multiple datasets and tasks (regression, classification), with strong computational efficiency.

Weaknesses:
1. The theoretical analysis focuses primarily on the Gaussian kernel, which limits the generality of the theoretical results, though this is common practice in the literature.
2. The computational complexity comparison between FIRE and existing methods is not sufficiently detailed, making it difficult to assess practical scalability.
3. The Type II setting has an inherent error term (O(t) for Gaussian kernel), which might limit its applicability in certain scenarios, though the authors properly acknowledge this limitation.
4. The connection to manifold learning is mentioned in the theoretical analysis but not elaborated upon in the experimental section, missing an opportunity to demonstrate the approach's value in a practical manifold learning context.

The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear mathematical derivations, logical organization, and sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results. The related work section properly contextualizes the contributions within the machine learning literature on density ratio estimation.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by framing density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem with connections to operator theory.
- The proposed FIRE algorithms are theoretically grounded with explicit error bounds and convergence rates.
- The CD-CV method addresses a practical challenge in parameter selection that is often overlooked in the literature.
- The empirical results demonstrate strong performance compared to existing methods across multiple datasets and tasks.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The theoretical results are limited to the Gaussian kernel, which might not generalize to other kernels without additional assumptions.
- The computational complexity comparison is insufficient for practitioners to assess practical utility.
- The Type II setting's inherent error term might limit applicability in some scenarios, though this is properly acknowledged.

The paper addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel perspective that bridges classical mathematics with modern machine learning. The theoretical analysis provides valuable guarantees, the framework is flexible, and the experimental results demonstrate practical utility. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it advances both the theoretical understanding and practical applicability of density ratio estimation.
