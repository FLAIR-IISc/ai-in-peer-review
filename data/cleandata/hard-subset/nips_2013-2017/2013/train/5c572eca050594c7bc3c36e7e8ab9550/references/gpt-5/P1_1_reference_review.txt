Summary
The paper proposes to estimate density ratios β(x)=q(x)/p(x) by recasting the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two settings are considered:
- Type I (two-sample): Kp β = Kq 1 for a general kernel k; both p- and q-samples are available, and the loss is measured in L2(p) (with variants combining L2(p) and L2(q), or using an RKHS loss).
- Type II (one-sample with known q): Kt,p β ≈ q for a δ-kernel (e.g., Gaussian with bandwidth t); only p-samples are required, and q(xi) is assumed known at those points.

By the representer theorem, the solutions reduce to kernel ridge-type linear systems with explicit closed-form expressions. The authors analyze error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on compact manifolds, and propose an unsupervised model selection method, cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV), that validates β by testing the moment-matching equality Ep[uβ] ≈ Eq[u] over random test functions. Experiments on regression/classification under covariate shift and on USPS illustrate the approach, with comparisons to LSIF and a baseline KDE-based ratio estimator (TIKDE).

Relation to prior work
The operator-theoretic view connects to Tikhonov regularization for inverse problems and kernel methods (e.g., De Vito et al., Smola & Schölkopf). The Type I formulation is closely related to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), which enforces Eq[Φ]=Ep[βΦ] in RKHS; here, instead of constraining β pointwise and the moment vector, the authors regularize β as a function in an RKHS and allow several choices of loss (L2(p), mix with L2(q), or RKHS norm). The approach is also related to direct ratio estimation (LSIF/KLIEP) but avoids designing a basis for β. The theoretical rates are positioned against general likelihood ratio estimation theory (e.g., Nguyen et al.), and the manifold analysis builds on heat kernel approximations.

Strengths
- Clear unifying perspective: viewing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem provides a principled route to regularization, stability, and analysis.
- Simple algorithms: closed-form kernel-based estimators with out-of-sample extension follow immediately; multiple loss choices (including mixed L2(p)/L2(q)) are supported.
- Theory: nonasymptotic bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and compact manifolds; decomposition clarifies approximation (t), regularization (λ), and sampling error contributions.
- CD-CV: a practical, unsupervised model selection criterion derived from the defining moment equality that does not require labeled targets and is flexible in the choice of test functions.
- Empirical results: the approach appears competitive against LSIF and a KDE baseline; the method’s stability vs. KMM is well motivated.

Weaknesses and questions
- Positivity/normalization: β need not be nonnegative or integrate to one under p. This can cause negative importance weights and instability in downstream reweighting. KMM enforces β≥0 and Ep[β]=1; FIRE does not. The paper should discuss or incorporate constraints (e.g., exponential parametrization β=exp g, nonnegativity constraints, or equality constraints Ep[β]=1).
- Relation and novelty w.r.t. KMM: The Type I RKHS-loss variant (Eq. 10) is essentially a regularized version of KMM in function space. The paper should more sharply delineate differences (solution properties, statistical and computational trade-offs) and compare empirically to regularized KMM variants with out-of-sample extension.
- Theory details: Rates such as O(n−1/(3.5+d/2)) and O(n−1/(4+5d/6)) are unusual; it would help to (i) optimize t and λ explicitly to clarify how the exponents arise, (ii) compare to known minimax rates for ratio estimation, and (iii) discuss tightness and dependence on smoothness assumptions (W22) and kernel choices. Boundary conditions are only mentioned; a precise statement for domains with boundary is missing.
- Practicality of Type II: Assuming q(xi) known at p-sample locations fits some MCMC scenarios but is uncommon in covariate shift; this limitation should be clearer in positioning and experiments.
- Experiments: Some inconsistencies: earlier the paper claims comparisons to KMM; later it omits KMM due to out-of-sample extension issues. Since FIRE yields a function, one can construct an OOS extension for KMM via kernel regression on β at p-sample points; a fair KMM comparison would strengthen the empirical section. Also, results focus on the CD-CV objective; more end-task metrics and ablations (e.g., positivity of β, weight variance, sensitivity to t, λ) would be valuable. The λ search range seems oddly small and not scaled by n.
- Clarity and presentation: Notation is heavy; some equations and subscripts are hard to parse, and several references lack years or proper formatting. It would help to clearly separate k (integral operator) and kH (regularizer), and to provide intuitive guidance on when to use L2 vs RKHS loss.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with careful operator formulation and derivations. The theoretical development is substantive, though some assumptions and rate interpretations could be clearer. Empirical comparisons could be broader and more consistent.

Clarity
Generally readable but dense; could benefit from tighter notation, a running algorithm box, explicit computational complexity, and more guidance on hyperparameter scaling. Clean up typos and references.

Originality
Moderately original: the explicit Fredholm/Tikhonov formulation for density ratio estimation and manifold rates are novel and useful, though the connection to KMM/LSIF is close.

Significance
Meaningful: density ratio estimation is central to covariate shift and importance sampling. The framework, theory, and CD-CV are likely to influence future work and practice, especially given the simplicity of the estimators.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add constraints or transformations to ensure β≥0 and Ep[β]=1, and evaluate the impact.
- Provide full KMM comparisons with a reasonable OOS extension and regularization.
- Include complexity analysis and large-scale approximations (Nyström, random features).
- Clarify optimal t,λ choices and compare rates to known lower/upper bounds.
- Expand experiments: report weight variance, negativity rates, downstream task performance, and sensitivity to γ in mixed norms.
- Improve exposition and references; add guidance on choosing k vs kH.

Pros
- Unifying inverse-problem perspective; simple kernel algorithms with OOS extension.
- Solid theoretical analysis on Rd and manifolds.
- Practical unsupervised model selection (CD-CV).

Cons
- No positivity/normalization guarantees for β.
- Limited/inconsistent empirical comparison to KMM.
- Some clarity and presentation issues; unusual rates not deeply interpreted.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
The paper presents a coherent and useful framework with nontrivial theory and practical algorithms. Despite issues around constraints and experimental breadth, the contribution is of interest to the NeurIPS community and worth inclusion with revisions.
