## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm equation, leveraging tools from regularization theory and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors present two formulations (Type I and Type II) and derive corresponding algorithms, termed FIRE, with theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. While the core idea of framing density ratio estimation within the well-established framework of integral equations is intriguing, the paper suffers from a plethora of minor – yet pervasive – issues that collectively detract from its overall impact and, in my assessment, warrant rejection.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Formulation:** The connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm integral equations is a fresh perspective and provides a new lens through which to view this problem.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The attempt to provide theoretical bounds (albeit under simplifying assumptions) is commendable.  The extension to manifolds is a positive aspect.
* **RKHS Framework:** Leveraging RKHS provides a natural and flexible framework for algorithm development.
* **CD-CV Method:**  The proposed unsupervised parameter selection technique appears reasonable and practical.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Clarity & Motivation:** The introduction is somewhat convoluted. The rationale for choosing the specific kernel formulations isn't always clear, and the potential benefits over existing approaches are not convincingly articulated upfront.  Why is *this* approach superior to, for instance, direct optimization within the RKHS as done in KMM? This is never fully addressed.
* **Overabundance of Notations & Equations:** The paper is exceptionally dense with equations.  While mathematical rigor is appreciated, the sheer volume obfuscates the core ideas and makes the paper difficult to follow. Many equations are stated without sufficient explanation or intuitive justification.
* **Minor Technical Flaws & Inconsistencies:**  A number of small, but persistent, technical issues arise throughout the paper. For example, the discussion of Type II settings assuming *q* does not need to be a density is true, but this is not a significant advantage. More critically, the discussion of approximating integrals via sample averages lacks sufficient rigor. Simply stating “can be made precise” is insufficient; concentration inequalities *need* to be explicitly stated and referenced for each approximation. The approximations used in deriving the algorithms are also not consistently justified.
* **Incremental Contribution:** While the framing is novel, the algorithms themselves (FIRE) appear largely as slight variations of existing techniques (KMM, LSIF). The practical gains of using FIRE over established methods are not convincingly demonstrated, despite the extensive experimental section.
* **Experimental Shortcomings:** The experimental section is overly complex.  The various resampling strategies and the multitude of parameter settings make it difficult to discern clear trends or draw definitive conclusions.  The presentation of results is also somewhat haphazard and lacks sufficient statistical significance testing.  The comparisons to KMM are also limited by the lack of out-of-sample extension in the baseline KMM implementations.
* **Repetitive Content:** Some concepts and equations are repeated multiple times throughout the paper, adding to the overall length and hindering readability.
* **Formatting & Style:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies exist (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface, variable spacing). The writing style is often overly formal and lacks conciseness.

**Detailed Comments:**

* **Equation (1):** The notation `Ep( h(x) q(x) p(x) )` is unusual and potentially misleading.  It's not immediately clear what this represents.
* **Type I vs. Type II:** The distinction between Type I and Type II isn't always sharp, and the conditions under which each is applicable could be clarified.
* **Remark 2 in the introduction:** The connection to Kernel Mean Matching is stated, but the limitations of the KMM optimization (lack of regularization, no natural out-of-sample extension) aren't sufficiently emphasized.
* **Section 2:** The derivation of the empirical versions of the equations (e.g., Eq. 7) lacks sufficient detail.  The justification for the approximations used needs to be more explicit.
* **Theorem 1 & 3:** The conditions required for the convergence rates are overly restrictive and may not hold in many practical scenarios. The constants C1, C2, C3, etc., are introduced without any indication of their dependence on problem parameters.
* **CD-CV:** The choice of test functions (linear, half-space) seems arbitrary.  A more systematic approach to selecting these functions would be beneficial.
* **Table 1:** The table is difficult to interpret. The notation is unclear, and the results are not presented in a concise and informative manner.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Against Acceptance:**

* Overly dense and difficult to read.
* Incremental contribution relative to existing methods.
* Numerous minor technical flaws and inconsistencies.
* Lack of compelling experimental results.
* Excessive notation and mathematical complexity.

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel formulation of density ratio estimation.
* Attempted theoretical analysis (although limited in scope).
* RKHS framework provides flexibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novel framing of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation, the paper suffers from numerous shortcomings that outweigh its strengths. The lack of clarity, the abundance of technical details, and the incremental contribution make this paper unsuitable for acceptance at NIPS.  I recommend rejection.  The authors should consider significantly simplifying the presentation, focusing on the most important theoretical results, and providing more compelling experimental evidence before resubmitting this work.  A greater emphasis on highlighting the practical advantages of FIRE over existing methods is also crucial.
