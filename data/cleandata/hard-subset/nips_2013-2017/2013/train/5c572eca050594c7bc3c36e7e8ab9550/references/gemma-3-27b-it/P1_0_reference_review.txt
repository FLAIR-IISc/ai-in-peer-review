## Peer Review: Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel framework for density ratio estimation (DRE) by reformulating the problem as solving a Fredholm integral equation. The authors leverage tools from operator theory, specifically regularization techniques within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS), to develop algorithms – dubbed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) – for estimating the density ratio. The work provides theoretical analysis, including concentration and error bounds for Gaussian kernels, and proposes a cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) method for parameter selection. The authors position their approach as complementary to existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), emphasizing its flexibility and theoretical grounding.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper acknowledges the growing body of work on DRE motivated by covariate shift and transfer learning (citing relevant works). It correctly positions itself in relation to KMM, highlighting similarities in the underlying integral equation but emphasizing the difference in optimization approach and regularization. The connection to LSIF is also appropriately noted, with the authors acknowledging shared concepts in parameter selection. The paper also references relevant literature on kernel methods, regularization theory, and density estimation, demonstrating a good understanding of the broader field.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:**  Framing DRE as a Fredholm integral equation provides a fresh perspective and opens the door to applying established techniques from operator theory, a compelling and original contribution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a significant theoretical contribution, presenting concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel case. This is a strong point and adds substantial value compared to purely empirical approaches. The generalization to manifolds is also noteworthy.
* **Flexibility:** The FIRE framework appears flexible, accommodating different norms and loss functions, offering a range of possible algorithms. The two types of formulations (I & II) cater to different data availability scenarios.
* **Clear Algorithm Derivation:** The derivation of the FIRE algorithms from the theoretical framework is well-explained and provides explicit formulas, making implementation straightforward.
* **Practical Parameter Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method addresses the crucial issue of parameter tuning in unsupervised learning, potentially improving the robustness of the approach.
* **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation comparing FIRE to several baselines, demonstrating competitive performance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Practical Advantage Demonstrated:** While theoretically appealing and competitive in experiments, the paper doesn't definitively showcase a *significant* practical advantage over existing state-of-the-art methods like KMM or LSIF. The improvements shown in the experiments are often marginal and highly dependent on the dataset and parameter settings. More compelling results demonstrating a clear win in specific scenarios would strengthen the paper.
* **Complexity of Theoretical Results:** The theoretical analysis, while impressive, is quite involved and may be difficult for readers unfamiliar with operator theory to fully grasp.  Simplifying some of the derivations or providing more intuitive explanations could improve accessibility.
* **CD-CV Details:** The details of the CD-CV method, particularly the selection of test functions (linear, half-space, etc.), are somewhat ad-hoc. A more systematic exploration of different test function families and their impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Scalability Considerations:** The paper does not address the scalability of the approach to very large datasets. The computational cost of kernel matrix inversion could become a bottleneck in such scenarios.
* **Assumptions on Smoothness:** The theoretical guarantees rely on strong smoothness assumptions on the densities (Sobolev spaces). Relaxing these assumptions, or at least discussing the impact of violating them, would improve the robustness of the theoretical results.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to a well-studied problem.
* The theoretical results are significant and provide valuable insights into the properties of the proposed algorithms.
* The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates competitive performance.
* The work is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical advantage over existing methods is not definitively demonstrated.
* The theoretical analysis is complex and may limit accessibility.
* Scalability concerns are not addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The authors successfully frame the problem as an inverse problem and leverage tools from operator theory to develop a flexible and well-defined framework. While the practical advantage over existing methods is not overwhelming, the theoretical results and the potential for further development justify acceptance.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**

I recommend accepting this paper, contingent on the authors addressing the following minor revisions:

1. **Clarify Practical Benefits:** Strengthen the discussion of the practical advantages of FIRE compared to existing methods, perhaps by focusing on specific scenarios where it excels.
2. **Simplify Theoretical Presentation:**  Consider simplifying some of the theoretical derivations or providing more intuitive explanations to improve accessibility.
3. **Expand CD-CV Discussion:**  Provide a more thorough discussion of the CD-CV method, including a justification for the chosen test function families and an analysis of their impact on performance.
4. **Address Scalability:** Briefly discuss scalability considerations and potential strategies for addressing them (e.g., using approximate kernel methods).
