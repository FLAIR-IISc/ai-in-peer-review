Summary
The paper proposes estimating the density ratio r(x)=q(x)/p(x) by recasting it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two formulations are considered: Type I uses the identity Kp r = Kq 1, and Type II uses a δ-kernel approximation Kt,p r ≈ q. The estimation is posed as Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding explicit kernel solutions via the representer theorem (FIRE). The authors also introduce an unsupervised model selection criterion (CD-CV) based on matching expectations of random test functions across the two distributions. They present concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, and compare FIRE with KMM, LSIF, and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline.

Relation to prior work
The re-expression of density ratio estimation as an operator equation is a natural lens that connects to KMM (Eq[Φ]=Ep[rΦ]) and to LSIF/KLIEP (minimizing L2,p or KL). The RKHS/Tikhonov machinery and spectral/operator view are well-established in inverse problems and kernel methods. The paper’s Type I can be seen as a regularized, functional version of KMM; Type II resembles convolved deconvolution-style estimation. The CD-CV idea is conceptually close to importance-weighted cross-validation and unsupervised model selection in uLSIF/IWCV.

Strengths
- Unifying operator-theoretic framing of density ratio estimation; links to classical inverse problems.
- Explicit kernel algorithms with out-of-sample extension (unlike standard KMM).
- Error bounds/convergence rates (for Gaussian kernels) in both Euclidean and manifold settings.
- Unsurvised parameter selection (CD-CV) is practical and broadly applicable.

Weaknesses (technical and presentation; many are minor but numerous)
Major issues
1) Notation errors and ambiguity throughout: the core object is the ratio q/p, but the manuscript alternates between “q p”, “qp”, and “q p2” (presumably q/p and q/p^2?). This severely hampers readability and may invalidate theorem statements as written. E.g., Assumptions: “qp , q p2 are in Sobolev space W2
2(Ω)”—unclear what is meant.

2) Theorems and rates contain typographical/numeric errors that make them impossible to verify:
- Corollary 4 exponents like “56 d” appear (likely 5/6·d?), decimals like “3.5” (7/2) are mixed with missing slashes, and optimization over t,λ is not spelled out. Such misprints undermine the credibility of the stated rates.
- Conditions such as “m > λ1/3 n” are unusual and not motivated.

3) Incorrect or misleading statements about RKHS requirements:
- In “Algorithms for the RKHS norm,” it says “applicable only in Type I since it requires q to belong to H′.” In fact, the requirement is that the mean embedding Kq 1 exists (e.g., bounded kernel and q has finite mass); q need not be a function in H′. This conceptual mistake affects both method and theory.

4) Positivity/normalization of the ratio: FIRE returns an unconstrained function; density ratios must be nonnegative and satisfy Ep[r]=1. The paper neither enforces these constraints nor analyzes their impact. This is a central modeling gap relative to KMM (which enforces positivity and normalization) and methods like uLSIF (which can clip/positivity-regularize).

5) Empirical evaluation is underspecified and incomplete:
- Key results (e.g., Table 1) are referenced but not included; parameter ranges are given but selection details (e.g., F in CD-CV) and runtime/memory are absent.
- The justification for excluding KMM in some comparisons (“out-of-sample extension”) is weak; KMM’s intended use is reweighting for risk estimation, which can be evaluated without an explicit functional ratio on test inputs.

6) Double-blind violation: explicit acknowledgments with grants. This must be removed for review.

Methodological/detail issues
- Discretization and algebra: Several matrix formulas are dimensionally non-transparent (e.g., v=(Kp,p^2 KH + nλI)^{-1} Kp,p Kp,q 1). Powers and order of multiplications are ambiguous; consistent shapes and normalizations should be verified and stated.
- Empirical L2,p is approximated by (1/n)∑ f(x_i); should be squared values; elsewhere the squared form is used. Inconsistency indicates carelessness.
- The Type II bound includes ‖Kt,q 1−q‖2,p = O(t). This depends on measuring in L2,p; assumptions linking p and q (e.g., absolute continuity, boundedness) needed. Only upper bounds p(x)<Γ, q(x)<Γ are stated; no lower bound on p, which allows unbounded q/p.
- Kernel choices: theory is for Gaussian with k=kH; algorithms allow k≠kH, but no theory in that case.
- No discussion of computational complexity or scalability. FIRE involves inverting n×n systems; claims of “better computational efficiency” are unsupported by complexity or wall-clock comparisons.
- The “γ L2,p+(1−γ) L2,q” variant has no theoretical analysis, yet is positioned as a key practical option.

Clarity and formatting
- Numerous typos: “TiknonovPhilips,” “a unsupervised,” missing squares in norms, inconsistent K/k capitalization, stray and missing subscripts/superscripts, and punctuation.
- Many references are incomplete (missing years, pages), inconsistent, or malformed. Please standardize and complete citations.
- The paper oscillates between operator and sample notation without a clear, self-contained derivation of the closed-form coefficients; a concise derivation (with dimensions) is needed.

Questions for authors
- Can you clarify exactly which function classes (q/p or variants) are assumed to lie in Sobolev spaces, and under what conditions on p is q/p bounded?
- Can you provide corrected theorem statements (with precise exponents and optimized choices of t,λ) and a proof sketch for at least one main result?
- How do you handle negativity of the estimated ratio in practice? Any post-processing or constraints?
- What are the computational complexities and runtimes vs. LSIF/KMM on your datasets?
- How sensitive is CD-CV to the choice/number of test functions? Is it equivalent to IWCV/uLSIF CV in some regimes?

Pros
- Conceptually appealing operator-theoretic framing with RKHS regularization.
- Out-of-sample ratio function; flexible losses.
- Attempts at rigorous rates, including manifold setting.
- Practical unsupervised model selection idea.

Cons
- Pervasive notation/typo errors; problematic theorem statements.
- Misstatement about RKHS requirements; missing positivity/normalization.
- Evaluation incomplete; limited and uneven comparison to baselines.
- Double-blind violation; reference and formatting issues.

Recommendation: Reject
While the core idea has merit and could be significant, the manuscript in its current form contains too many technical, notational, and presentation flaws to be reliable. I encourage a thorough rewrite: fix notation, correct and justify theorems, address positivity/normalization, provide complete experiments with runtime and fair baselines, and ensure anonymity. With these addressed, the paper could be a strong contribution.
