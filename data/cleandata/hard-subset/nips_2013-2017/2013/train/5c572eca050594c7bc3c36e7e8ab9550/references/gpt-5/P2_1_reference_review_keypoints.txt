- Reframes density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation and solves it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS (FIRE) with closed-form, out-of-sample estimators.
- Proposes two settings: Type I (samples from p and q leading to Kp r ≈ Kq 1) and Type II (q known on p-samples using a δ-like kernel to get Kt,p r ≈ q).
- Provides nonasymptotic concentration and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and on compact manifolds.
- Introduces CD-CV, an unsupervised model-selection criterion based on the importance-sampling identity using random linear and half-space test functions.
- Connects to prior work on importance weighting (Shimodaira; KMM; LSIF/KLIEP), inverse problems and RKHS regularization, and recent ratio-estimation theory.
- Correctly notes KMM as an RKHS identity version of the same Fredholm equation but lacking explicit out-of-sample functions and regularization.
- Variational formulations and empirical algorithms are technically sound and follow standard Tikhonov/representer results with explicit linear systems.
- Theoretical assumptions (bounded densities, Sobolev smoothness) and explicit dependence on bandwidth t and regularization λ are clearly stated.
- Derived rates appear conservative (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2)) and are not compared to known minimax lower bounds; faster rates under stronger assumptions should be discussed.
- Theory primarily treats identical Gaussian kernels for operator and RKHS, while algorithms allow different kernels—extension of guarantees needs clarification.
- CD-CV lacks statistical justification showing it correlates with L2,p error; guidance on choosing test functions and their number is missing.
- Experiments validate CD-CV on USPS and downstream weighted tasks but omit Type II experiments and a synthetic ground-truth study for direct L2 error measurement.
- KMM comparisons are incomplete (omitted in places); fair comparisons via held-out evaluation or KMM out-of-sample extension should be provided.
- Runtime and memory/complexity are not reported despite O(n^3) matrix inversions; scalability techniques (Nyström/sketching) and runtimes should be included.
- Paper is generally clear and well organized, but notation is heavy and inconsistent (kernels and operators), and a compact notation block plus pseudocode would aid reproducibility.
- Minor typographical issues and unclear treatment of boundary cases and the γ parameter in mixed norms need fixing.
- Core idea is a novel and useful synthesis; linking to KMM and the Type II δ-kernel extension are insightful contributions.
- Manifold-support analysis and the practical CD-CV selection method add originality beyond most density-ratio work.
- Significance is moderate-to-high: FIRE could impact covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, and MCMC applications where q is known but unnormalized.
- Major weakness: no enforcement of positivity/normalization, risking negative weights; suggest constrained estimators or link functions (e.g., r=exp g) and evaluate clipping effects.
- Empirical suggestions: add Type II experiments (e.g., posterior ratios), synthetic ground-truth cases, runtime/memory benchmarks, and fair KMM comparisons.
- Theoretical suggestions: discuss optimality relative to lower bounds, when Type I vs Type II is preferable, and provide heuristic/consistency arguments for CD-CV.
- Clarity suggestions: consolidate notation, provide pseudocode, and clarify effects of k=kH versus k≠kH on guarantees.
- Pros: clean operator-theoretic unification, simple closed-form algorithms with out-of-sample prediction, nonasymptotic manifold bounds, and a practical unsupervised model-selection method.
- Cons: no positivity/normalization constraints, conservative rates with limited optimality discussion, missing Type II and ground-truth experiments, partial KMM comparisons, scalability not addressed, and dense/inconsistent notation.
- Recommendation: Weak accept—principled, useful perspective with solid theory; improving empirical coverage (Type II), enforcing positivity/normalization, clarifying notation, and addressing rate optimality would strengthen the paper.
