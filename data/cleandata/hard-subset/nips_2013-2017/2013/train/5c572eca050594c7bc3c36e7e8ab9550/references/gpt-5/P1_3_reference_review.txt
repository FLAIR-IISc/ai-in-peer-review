Summary
The paper reframes density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solves it via Tikhonov regularization in RKHS, yielding simple kernel algorithms (FIRE). Two settings are proposed:
- Type I: Given samples from p and q, use the identity Kp r = Kq 1 (for a general kernel k) and minimize an empirical L2,p (or mixtures of L2,p and L2,q) loss with RKHS penalty. A variant replaces the loss by the RKHS norm, linking to KMM.
- Type II: When q is known pointwise (not necessarily a density) at the p-sample locations, use a δ-kernel kt and the approximation Kt,p r ≈ q to obtain a regression problem with RKHS penalty.

Representer-theorem leads to closed-form linear system solutions. The authors provide finite-sample bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on compact smooth manifolds (and remarks for domains with boundary). They also propose an unsupervised model-selection criterion, CD-CV, that cross-validates hyperparameters by checking moment-matching over random test functions (linear or half-space indicators). Experiments on standard datasets compare FIRE to LSIF and a thresholded inverse KDE heuristic (TIKDE), with some discussion of KMM.

Relation to prior work
- KMM: The authors clarify that Eq(Φ) = Ep(rΦ) is the H-identity underlying KMM and show how it corresponds to their Type I Fredholm formulation in H; their RKHS-loss variant is a regularized KMM-like procedure with a direct out-of-sample representation.
- LSIF/KLIEP: FIRE differs in that the unknown is directly a function in RKHS solved by inverse-problem regularization rather than basis expansion minimizing L2,p or KL. CD-CV resembles the moment-matching perspective used in LSIF/KLIEP but is used solely for validation.
- Theory: Builds on operator-theoretic regularization (Engl et al.) and RKHS learning theory, contributing concentration-based finite-sample bounds (including manifold settings).

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: Casting density-ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem is elegant and connects modern kernel methods to operator-theoretic regularization.
- Algorithmic simplicity: Closed-form linear solvers via representer theorem; flexible losses (L2,p, mixtures, RKHS) and kernels; natural out-of-sample form.
- Theory: Nonasymptotic error bounds with explicit dependencies on t, λ, n, m for Gaussian kernels; extension to compact manifolds is novel and useful.
- Practical model selection: CD-CV provides an unsupervised, task-agnostic hyperparameter selection procedure that can be reused in other ratio estimators.
- Scope: Type II covers settings where q is known pointwise (e.g., integrands, some MCMC contexts), broadening applicability beyond covariate shift.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Positivity and normalization: The ratio r in importance weighting should be nonnegative, but FIRE produces an unrestricted RKHS function. Negative weights can harm importance sampling and weighted ERM. The paper should discuss enforcing nonnegativity (e.g., constrained RKHS optimization, link functions such as exp(g), or post-hoc clipping) and the impact on theory.
- Type II scale: If q is known only up to a multiplicative constant (as in many MCMC scenarios), minimizing ||Kt,p f − q|| yields f scaled by that unknown constant, not the true ratio. Please clarify how the method handles unknown normalization (e.g., re-scaling f by enforcing Ep[f]=1, or another constraint) and its effect on theory/empirics.
- Assumptions: Bounds assume bounded p, q and smoothness of r and even r/p (q/p and q/p2 in W2
2). These are strong and atypical for density ratios near low-density regions; no lower bound on p is stated though division by small p is central. Please justify necessity/tightness and discuss robustness when p has light tails or near-zeros.
- Rates and tightness: The exponents (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2)) are unusual; a comparison to known minimax results for likelihood/density ratio estimation (e.g., Nguyen et al. 2007) would help position the rates (are they optimal or conservative?).
- Analysis/applicability gap: Theory is given only when the operator and RKHS use the same Gaussian kernel. Algorithms allow different k and kH and several loss variants; the paper should discuss whether analogous guarantees hold or how sensitive performance is to kernel mismatch.
- Experimental evaluation: 
  - Baselines: KMM is omitted due to out-of-sample issues, but many applications only need weights on the p-sample; a comparison at training points (where KMM natively operates) would be fair. Consider adding KLIEP and RuLSIF/KuLSIF as well.
  - Scale and scope: Datasets are small to medium; no large-scale, high-dim or heavy-covariate-shift tests; no ablation on the impact of nonnegativity or normalization.
  - CD-CV: While appealing, more evidence on its reliability (variance across random test-function families, sensitivity to U, and correlation with downstream task performance) would strengthen the claim.
- Practicalities: Complexity and numerical stability (conditioning of Kp,pKH etc.), memory requirements, and any regularization tricks (e.g., diagonal loading) are not discussed.

Quality
Technically solid overall with clear operator-theoretic grounding and correct derivations for the empirical objectives. Theoretical results are substantial though assumptions may be restrictive; some key practical issues (nonnegativity, unknown normalization) should be addressed more explicitly. Empirics support the approach but could be broader and include stronger baselines.

Clarity
Generally well organized, but notation is heavy and occasionally ambiguous (k vs kH; K, Kp,p, KH; r = q/p written as q p). A notation table and consistent ratio notation would help. Please correct minor typos and clearly state assumptions (bounds on p, lower bounds, Sobolev class) where first used.

Originality
Moderate to strong. While close in spirit to KMM/LSIF moment-matching, the explicit Fredholm framing, the Type II operator regression, and manifold-domain theory provide novel contributions.

Significance
Moderate. The approach is likely to influence how density-ratio estimation is taught and analyzed; the manifold results and CD-CV are useful. Wider impact would be enhanced by addressing positivity/normalization and by stronger empirical comparisons.

Pros
- Elegant inverse-problem formulation with simple, closed-form kernel solvers
- Nontrivial theory (rates, manifolds)
- Practical unsupervised model selection (CD-CV)
- Clear connections to KMM and LSIF

Cons
- No positivity/normalization constraints; potential negative weights
- Type II scale ambiguity when q known up to a constant
- Restrictive assumptions and unusual rates without comparison to minimax
- Limited baselines and scope of experiments; omission of KMM comparison at training points

Questions for authors
1) How do you enforce or encourage nonnegativity of r in practice? What is the impact on theory?
2) How do you handle q known up to scale in Type II? Can you re-normalize f to have Ep[f]=1?
3) Can you compare the obtained rates to known minimax rates?
4) What is the computational complexity and conditioning behavior of the linear systems? Any scalability strategies (Nyström, CG, preconditioning)?
5) Can you add KMM (at training points), KLIEP, and RuLSIF baselines?

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying positivity/normalization, adding a KMM baseline at training points, and improving the discussion of assumptions/rates.
