

# Peer Review: "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper presents a novel approach to density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, a classical inverse problem in mathematical analysis. The authors develop two formulations (Type I and Type II) for estimating q(x)/p(x), where p is given by samples and q is either a known function or another density. They solve these inverse problems using Tikhonov regularization within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework, leading to simple and implementable algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The paper makes a significant connection between classical operator theory and modern machine learning approaches to density ratio estimation.

The work relates to several important lines of research at NIPS, including covariate shift adaptation (e.g., [6, 9, 10, 22]), kernel methods for density estimation (e.g., [12, 11]), and kernel mean matching (KMM) [9]. The authors clearly position their work relative to these approaches, particularly noting that while KMM can be viewed as a special case of their Type I formulation, their approach provides a more stable solution with natural out-of-sample extension.

## Strengths

**Originality (High):** The core contribution of reformulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is genuinely novel. While density ratio estimation has been studied extensively, this mathematical perspective has not been previously applied in this context. The connection to classical inverse problem theory provides a fresh theoretical framework that is both elegant and powerful.

**Quality (High):** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with convergence rates provided for both Euclidean and manifold cases. The authors carefully consider different settings (Type I vs Type II) and provide detailed error bounds. The experimental results are comprehensive, comparing with relevant baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across multiple datasets and problem types (regression, classification). The CD-CV model selection technique is particularly innovative and addresses a significant practical challenge in unsupervised learning.

**Clarity (High):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical formulation is precise, and the connection between classical analysis and machine learning is clearly explained. The algorithms are presented with explicit formulas, making them straightforward to implement. The experimental section is well-structured and provides meaningful comparisons.

**Significance (High):** Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem with broad applications in transfer learning, covariate shift adaptation, and importance sampling. The FIRE algorithm provides a principled approach with theoretical guarantees, and the CD-CV model selection technique addresses a key practical challenge. The paper's theoretical framework could inspire new approaches to related problems in machine learning.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Scope (Moderate):** While the theoretical analysis is strong for the Gaussian kernel, the paper doesn't sufficiently discuss the limitations of this assumption. For real-world applications, the choice of kernel significantly impacts performance, and the paper doesn't provide guidance on selecting the most appropriate kernel for different scenarios.

**Comparison with Related Work (Moderate):** The connection to KMM is mentioned, but the paper could more clearly articulate the practical differences between the two approaches. Specifically, it would be valuable to include a comparison of computational efficiency, as this is a critical factor in real-world applications.

**Experimental Depth (Moderate):** While the experiments are comprehensive, they could benefit from more detailed analysis of when Type I versus Type II is preferable. The paper mentions that Type II is useful when sampling from q is expensive, but doesn't provide concrete examples where this scenario arises in practice.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is novel and well-executed, providing both theoretical insights and practical algorithms. The FIRE algorithms are simple, implementable, and backed by strong theoretical guarantees. The CD-CV model selection technique is particularly impressive for its unsupervised nature and practical utility.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria:
- **Quality:** Strong technical content with rigorous theoretical analysis and comprehensive experiments
- **Clarity:** Exceptionally well-written with clear mathematical formulations and organization
- **Originality:** Novel mathematical perspective on a well-studied problem
- **Significance:** Addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with broad applications

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field, with a novel theoretical framework that connects classical inverse problem theory with modern machine learning approaches. The practical algorithms and theoretical guarantees provide both immediate utility and long-term theoretical value. The work is clearly superior to previous approaches like KMM and LSIF in both theoretical foundation and practical implementation.

The paper would benefit from slightly more discussion about kernel selection and computational efficiency, but these are minor points that don't detract from the overall strength of the contribution. This work represents a substantial advance in density ratio estimation that will be valuable to both researchers and practitioners in machine learning.

**Overall Rating: Strong Accept**
