

# Review: Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using regularization techniques within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors develop two main algorithms (Type I and Type II) that address different scenarios: Type I when samples from both distributions are available, and Type II when q is known at sample points from p. They also introduce CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) for unsupervised parameter selection.

The paper connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems in mathematical analysis, establishing a novel theoretical foundation. This approach differs from existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) by framing the problem within the well-established framework of Fredholm equations and regularization theory. The authors demonstrate how their formulation encompasses KMM as a special case while providing a more principled regularization framework with better theoretical guarantees.

**Quality:** The paper presents technically sound work with rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors derive error bounds and convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings in Euclidean space and on manifolds for Gaussian kernels. The theoretical results are well-motivated and the proofs (referenced to supplemental material) appear solid. The experimental section thoroughly compares their method (FIRE) with KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE on multiple datasets, providing strong empirical validation. The authors carefully discuss limitations, such as the error term in Type II setting depending on the kernel parameter.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the connection to related work (particularly KMM) is clearly articulated. The theoretical analysis is presented in a logical sequence, building from the integral equation formulation to the algorithm derivation and then to the error bounds. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The paper's core contribution is the novel perspective of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem. This is a significant departure from existing approaches, which typically treat the problem as a direct optimization task. The connection to classical operator theory and the application of spectral analysis to this machine learning problem represents a genuine novelty. The CD-CV model selection technique is also a useful contribution that addresses a practical challenge in unsupervised learning.

**Significance:** Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem with wide applications in transfer learning, covariate shift adaptation, and importance sampling. The proposed framework provides a unified approach that combines the strengths of classical inverse problem theory with modern machine learning techniques. The theoretical guarantees and empirical results demonstrate that the approach advances the state of the art, with competitive performance and better computational efficiency compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
1. Novel theoretical connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm integral equations
2. Comprehensive theoretical analysis with error bounds for multiple settings
3. Practical unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV)
4. Clear exposition of the connection to related work, particularly KMM
5. Strong experimental validation across multiple datasets and settings

**Weaknesses:**
1. The theoretical results could better emphasize the practical significance of the improved convergence rates compared to existing methods
2. The paper doesn't provide concrete examples of real-world applications where the Type II setting (q known explicitly) is particularly advantageous
3. The comparison with KMM could more thoroughly address computational complexity and scalability
4. The connection to manifold learning (beyond just theoretical analysis) could be strengthened with more practical insights

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

**Pro:**
- Presents a novel theoretical framework that connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems
- Provides rigorous theoretical guarantees with convergence rates for multiple settings
- Introduces CD-CV, a useful unsupervised method for parameter selection
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance against multiple baselines
- Well-written with clear mathematical exposition

**Con:**
- The practical significance of the theoretical improvements isn't fully articulated
- The Type II setting's real-world applicability isn't sufficiently demonstrated
- The comparison with KMM could be more comprehensive regarding computational aspects

**Conclusion:**
This paper makes a significant contribution by establishing a novel theoretical framework for density ratio estimation through the lens of Fredholm integral equations. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and provides both theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. While the practical significance of the theoretical improvements could be more explicitly articulated, the paper's contribution is substantial and represents a meaningful advance in the field. The proposed FIRE algorithms with CD-CV offer a principled and effective approach to density ratio estimation that should be of interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
