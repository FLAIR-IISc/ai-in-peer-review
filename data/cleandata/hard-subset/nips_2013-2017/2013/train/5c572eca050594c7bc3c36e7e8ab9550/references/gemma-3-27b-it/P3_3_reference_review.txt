## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and mathematically grounded approach to density ratio estimation by framing it as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging tools from regularization theory and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The connection to classical operator theory is a significant strength, offering a powerful framework for analysis and algorithm design. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the empirical results presented feel somewhat limited in scope and impact, and lack a clear demonstration of superiority over existing state-of-the-art methods beyond specific theoretical advantages. I recommend **weak accept** contingent on the authors expanding their experimental evaluation and providing a more compelling demonstration of practical benefit.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two probability densities, a crucial task in importance sampling, transfer learning (covariate shift), and related fields.  Instead of directly estimating the ratio, the authors cleverly recast the problem as solving a Fredholm integral equation. This allows them to apply the well-developed machinery of Tikhonov regularization within an RKHS framework, resulting in a family of algorithms (FIRE) with strong theoretical guarantees.  The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers in density ratio estimation (KMM, LSIF, KLIEP) and highlighting the connections and distinctions. The emphasis on the operator theory perspective and the resultant analytical tractability represent a novel and valuable contribution.  The connection to KMM is well-explained, and the authors rightly point out the stability benefits of their regularization approach compared to KMM's unregularized optimization.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Foundation:** The primary strength of the paper lies in its rigorous mathematical formulation and analysis. Framing density ratio estimation as an inverse problem with well-defined properties opens up avenues for theoretical guarantees (convergence rates, error bounds) that are often lacking in purely empirical approaches.  The results for Gaussian kernels are particularly strong.
* **Generality & Flexibility:**  The RKHS framework provides a flexible platform for incorporating different kernels, norms, and loss functions. The authors explicitly explore variations with different norms (L2,p, RKHS) and demonstrate the adaptability of the FIRE algorithms.
* **Connection to Operator Theory:**  The use of Fredholm equations and regularization theory provides a powerful and elegant perspective on the problem, potentially leading to further theoretical advancements.
* **CD-CV for Parameter Selection:** The proposed unsupervised cross-validation technique (CD-CV) is a practical contribution, addressing a common challenge in unsupervised learning.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the derivations are presented in a digestible manner.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the authors present experimental results, they feel somewhat cursory. The comparisons to baseline methods (TIKDE, Importance Sampling) are not consistently favorable, and the performance gains over KMM and LSIF are not always decisive.  The dataset selection (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) is relatively standard but doesn't represent a particularly challenging test bed for these methods.
* **Lack of Scalability Discussion:** The computational complexity of the FIRE algorithms, especially concerning kernel matrix inversions, is not thoroughly discussed.  Scalability to high-dimensional datasets or large sample sizes is a critical practical consideration that needs to be addressed.
* **Theoretical Assumptions:** The theoretical results rely on specific assumptions about the smoothness of the densities and the choice of the Gaussian kernel. The implications of relaxing these assumptions are not fully explored.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The choice of regularization parameter (λ) and kernel width (t) can significantly impact performance. While CD-CV is proposed, its robustness and efficiency in high-dimensional settings warrant further investigation.
* **Practical Relevance:** The improvements demonstrated in the experiments are not substantial enough to clearly motivate the adoption of FIRE over existing methods in a wide range of applications.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3 (Theoretical Analysis):** While the theoretical results are impressive, the conditions under which they hold (smoothness of densities, choice of Gaussian kernel) are quite restrictive.  Can the authors comment on the robustness of their algorithms to violations of these assumptions?
* **Section 4 (Experiments):** The experiments feel somewhat limited.  Could the authors include results on higher-dimensional datasets (e.g., image classification datasets)?  A more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art density ratio estimation methods would strengthen the evaluation. Specifically, how does FIRE perform on datasets where covariate shift is particularly severe?
* **Algorithm Efficiency:** The kernel matrix inversions in the FIRE algorithms can be computationally expensive.  Are there any strategies for approximating these inversions or using alternative kernel methods (e.g., Nyström approximation) to improve scalability?
* **CD-CV:**  Is the performance of CD-CV sensitive to the choice of test functions (U)?  How does the computational cost of CD-CV scale with the size of the datasets?
* **Type I vs Type II:** The advantages and disadvantages of Type I and Type II formulations aren't always clearly articulated in the experimental section. What types of problems would most benefit from each approach?

**5. Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and analysis.
* Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
* Flexible RKHS framework allows for various kernel choices and regularization techniques.
* CD-CV provides a practical solution for parameter selection.

**Con:**

* Limited empirical evaluation and lack of compelling performance gains over existing methods.
* Scalability concerns due to kernel matrix inversions.
* Theoretical assumptions may be restrictive in real-world applications.




**Recommendation:** **Weak Accept.**  The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. However, the lack of a strong empirical validation limits its overall impact. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors expanding their experimental evaluation with more challenging datasets and providing a clearer demonstration of the practical advantages of the FIRE algorithms, especially regarding scalability and robustness.  Addressing the points raised in the "Detailed Comments & Questions" section would also significantly strengthen the paper.
