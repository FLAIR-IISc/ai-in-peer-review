

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper makes a significant contribution by reframing density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem through the lens of Fredholm integral equations. The core novelty lies in connecting the well-established field of inverse problems (specifically, Fredholm equations of the first kind) to the problem of estimating density ratios, which has been primarily approached through machine learning and statistics methods. The authors develop a principled framework that leverages regularization techniques from inverse problems within the RKHS setting, resulting in simple and elegant algorithms they call FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator).

The paper's main strength is its bold theoretical perspective. Instead of proposing yet another incremental improvement to density ratio estimation methods, the authors establish a fundamental connection between two seemingly disparate fields: the theory of integral equations and density ratio estimation. This connection is not just a mathematical curiosity but leads to practical algorithms with strong theoretical guarantees. The authors show how existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) can be viewed within their framework (Type I setting with RKHS norm), but their approach provides a more unified and theoretically grounded perspective.

The paper's theoretical analysis is impressive, providing detailed error bounds and convergence rates for both Euclidean space and manifold settings. The results for Type I and Type II settings are particularly compelling, with the Type II formulation being especially interesting as it allows q(x) to be any function (not necessarily a density), which could be valuable in many applications where q(x) is known explicitly but sampling from q is difficult.

The proposed CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) method for unsupervised parameter selection is a particularly valuable contribution. It addresses a major pain point in unsupervised learning where traditional cross-validation is not applicable. The authors demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments, showing that it outperforms other methods in parameter selection for their FIRE algorithm.

While the paper builds on existing work in density ratio estimation (KMM, LSIF), the novelty lies in the theoretical framework rather than just algorithmic improvements. The authors clearly position their work in relation to previous methods, showing how their framework subsumes and generalizes some existing approaches while providing stronger theoretical guarantees.

The paper is well-written, with clear exposition of the theoretical concepts and algorithms. The experimental section, while somewhat limited to two regression and one classification dataset, provides solid evidence of the method's practical utility. The comparison to KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE is fair and demonstrates the advantages of the proposed approach.

The main limitation is that the theoretical analysis focuses primarily on Gaussian kernels, though the framework itself is more general. However, given the practical importance of Gaussian kernels in machine learning, this is a reasonable focus. The paper also could have explored more applications where the Type II setting would be particularly valuable (where q(x) is known explicitly but sampling from q is difficult).

What makes this paper stand out is the bold theoretical perspective it brings to a well-studied problem. Rather than tweaking existing algorithms, the authors reframe the problem in a way that opens up new theoretical and practical possibilities. This is exactly the kind of novel approach NIPS should prioritize.

The paper's contributions are substantial: (1) the fundamental connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations, (2) the FIRE algorithms that emerge from this perspective, (3) the comprehensive theoretical analysis, and (4) the novel CD-CV method for parameter selection. These contributions collectively represent a significant step forward in the field, not just an incremental improvement.

I believe this paper has the potential to influence future research in density ratio estimation, transfer learning, and related areas by providing a new theoretical lens through which to view these problems. The authors have successfully bridged classical mathematical analysis with modern machine learning, creating a framework that is both theoretically sound and practically useful.

In summary, this is a highly original paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field. The bold perspective on density ratio estimation as an inverse problem is exactly the kind of novel approach that NIPS should celebrate. While the experimental section could be expanded, the theoretical contributions are substantial enough to outweigh this limitation. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with a fresh perspective.

**Recommendation: Accept**
