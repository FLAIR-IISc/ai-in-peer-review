Summary
The paper proposes framing density ratio estimation q(x)/p(x) as an inverse problem via Fredholm integral equations of the first kind, leading to kernel-based Tikhonov-regularized estimators (FIRE). Two formulations are presented: (Type I) Kp(q/p) = Kq 1 using samples from both p and q; and (Type II) Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q with a local kernel kt, useful when q is known or can be evaluated at points sampled from p. By working in an RKHS and invoking the representer theorem, the authors derive closed-form linear-algebraic solutions for several losses (L2,p, mixed L2,p/L2,q, and an RKHS loss). They analyze concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean domains and smooth manifolds, and propose an unsupervised model selection procedure (CD-CV) that matches empirical expectations of random test functions under p and q. Experiments on covariate shift scenarios compare FIRE to LSIF, KMM (partially), and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects density ratio estimation methods (KMM, LSIF/KLIEP) to operator-theoretic inverse problems and places them in the classical Tikhonov/RKHS framework (cf. De Vito et al.; Smola & Schölkopf). The equivalence Kp(q/p) = Kq1 clarifies KMM as an identity in H; the proposed formulations add explicit regularization and out-of-sample functions in an RKHS. The Type II route—using approximate identity properties of local kernels to obtain Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q—is a noteworthy twist not standard in KMM/LSIF. The theoretical analysis leverages spectral tools and manifold kernels, in line with prior work on Laplacian/kernel integral operators.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm first-kind inverse problem is elegant and unifying; the Type II formulation is particularly fresh and potentially impactful for scenarios where q is evaluable but not samplable (e.g., MCMC, AIS).
- Algorithmic simplicity: Closed-form linear systems with natural out-of-sample extension in RKHS. The flexibility to use different losses (L2,p, mixed L2,p/L2,q, RKHS) is practical.
- Theory: Nontrivial finite-sample bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, including manifold settings. The separation of inverse problem and regularization is clean and aligns with operator learning literature.
- Model selection: CD-CV is a neat, unsupervised criterion based on expectation matching with random test functions; useful in practice when no labels are available.
- Empirical comparisons suggest competitive performance and stability relative to LSIF and baseline TIKDE.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is limited. The choice not to compare with KMM in some settings due to lack of out-of-sample extension is unconvincing: in standard covariate shift, one only needs weights on the p-sample, so KMM should be included more broadly. Claims of better computational efficiency need clearer complexity/scale evidence.
- Constraints: The estimators do not enforce nonnegativity or normalization of q/p, unlike KMM/LSIF variants. Potential negative weights may harm downstream tasks; the paper should quantify frequency/impact and discuss remedies (projection, constrained optimization).
- Clarity/notation: Several derivations are difficult to parse. Some matrix expressions (e.g., “K^3 + λI”) suggest ill-conditioned compositions; simplify or restate clearly. The dependence on t and λ in the bounds is complex; guidance on practical tuning beyond CD-CV would help.
- Rates/assumptions: The sample complexity exponents (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2)) and auxiliary conditions (m > λ1/3 n) are unusual. The effect of the approximation error ‖Kt,q1 − q‖ (Type II) on optimal t is only briefly addressed; more intuition or simulations would be valuable.
- CD-CV overlap: While useful, the idea echoes LSIF/KLIEP validation via test functions. Emphasize what is genuinely new (e.g., using test functions not intended as bases) and provide sensitivity analyses to the choice/number of functions.

Quality
The work is technically sound overall and grounded in established operator/RKHS theory. Proofs are deferred to the supplement; given the unusual rates and constants, a brief proof sketch in the main text would help. Experiments are adequate but not exhaustive.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but dense. Improve notation consistency (K, KH, Kp,p, etc.), carefully define all matrices, and avoid ambiguous powers of kernel matrices. Summarize algorithmic complexity and numerical stability considerations. Provide a concise algorithm box for each variant.

Originality
Moderate to high. The unifying Fredholm viewpoint and Type II estimator are genuinely interesting contributions; the CD-CV criterion is a practical addition, albeit related to prior validation schemes.

Significance
Potentially significant. The formulation could influence how density-dependent operators and importance weights are estimated, with implications for covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, and MCMC/likelihood-free inference. The manifold analysis adds breadth.

Pros
- Fresh inverse-problem framing of density ratio estimation; Type II is novel and useful.
- Simple RKHS-based algorithms with closed-form solutions and out-of-sample capability.
- Theoretical bounds including manifold settings.
- Practical unsupervised model selection.

Cons
- Limited/uneven comparison to KMM; computational claims not substantiated.
- Lack of positivity/normalization constraints on ratios.
- Some clarity/notation issues; unusual rate expressions with limited intuition.
- Experiments confined to modest-sized datasets and synthetic shift procedures.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include comprehensive KMM baselines where appropriate; report wall-clock and scaling vs. LSIF/KMM.
- Add a constrained variant (e.g., nonnegativity, normalization) or analyze projection post-processing.
- Provide guidance on choosing t, λ, and F (number of test functions) and sensitivity plots.
- Use real-world importance sampling or off-policy tasks (e.g., AIS, covariate shift benchmarks) to demonstrate practical gains.
- Clarify matrix equations, condition numbers, and numerics; include algorithm boxes and complexity.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
The paper introduces a compelling, unifying perspective with concrete algorithms and analysis. Despite some empirical and presentation shortcomings, the conceptual novelty (especially Type II) and the operator-theoretic connection merit presentation and are likely to spur follow-up work.

Confidence: 3/5
