

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes reformulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it using regularization with RKHS norms. The authors introduce two settings (Type I and Type II) and develop algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). They claim to provide a connection between classical inverse problem theory and density ratio estimation, with theoretical guarantees for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds.

## Major Technical Flaws

The paper contains a fundamental notational error that appears throughout the manuscript: the authors consistently write "q(x)p(x)" to represent the density ratio, when they clearly mean "q(x)/p(x)" (e.g., in the abstract, equations 1-4, and throughout the paper). This is a critical error that would confuse readers and undermine the paper's technical credibility. For instance, in equation (1), they write "Eq(h(x)) = ∫ h(x)q(x)dx = ∫ h(x) q(x) p(x) p(x)dx", which should be "q(x)/p(x)".

The paper also contains several other technical inconsistencies:
- In the introduction, they write "q(x)p(x)" when they mean "q(x)/p(x)" multiple times.
- The paper refers to "TiknonovPhilips regularization" instead of the standard "Tikhonov-Phillips regularization".
- The notation for the integral operators is inconsistent (e.g., "Kp q p (x)" vs "Kp(q/p)(x)").
- In Theorem 1, the bound has a term "1/λ^(1/6)" which is unusual for such error bounds and lacks justification.

## Theoretical Concerns

The theoretical analysis has several questionable aspects:
- The error bounds in Theorems 1 and 3 have dependencies on parameters (like λ) that are not typical for similar results in the literature. For example, the term "1/λ^(1/6)" appears in the bound without explanation.
- The paper claims to provide "detailed theoretical analysis" but relies heavily on the supplemental material for proofs, which is not acceptable for a NIPS paper.
- The convergence rates in Corollaries 2 and 4 are not clearly superior to existing methods (e.g., LSIF or KMM), and the paper doesn't provide a clear comparison of rates.

## Experimental and Methodological Issues

- The paper claims to have "a completely unsupervised technique, CD-CV," but the method described in Section 4 requires knowledge of the density q at sample points (for Type II) or samples from q (for Type I), making it not truly unsupervised.
- The experimental section lacks sufficient detail to verify the claims. For example, the paper states "strong performance and better computational efficiency" but doesn't provide enough information about the experimental setup, parameter ranges, or statistical significance.
- The comparison to KMM is problematic because the paper states they don't compare with KMM "as out-of-sample extension is necessary for fair comparison," yet the FIRE method they propose does have out-of-sample extension (unlike KMM), which is a key advantage.

## Minor Issues

- The paper has inconsistent notation for kernel matrices (e.g., sometimes "Kp,p", sometimes "Kp,p").
- The paper uses "L2,p" and "L2,ρ" interchangeably without explanation.
- The reference list contains several formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent capitalization, missing page numbers).
- The paper states "q(x) does not have to be a density function at all" but then uses it in a context where it's implicitly assumed to be a density (e.g., in the Type II setting where they use "q(x)" as a function).

## Overall Assessment

While the paper identifies a potentially useful connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations, the fundamental notational error, inconsistent mathematical presentation, and questionable theoretical claims significantly undermine the paper's technical quality. The paper does not provide sufficient evidence that its approach offers significant advantages over existing methods, and the theoretical analysis is not as rigorous as required for NIPS.

The paper's contribution appears to be a restatement of existing methods (KMM, LSIF) in the language of Fredholm equations without providing meaningful new theoretical insights or empirical advantages. The errors in notation and presentation make it difficult to evaluate the technical content, which is particularly concerning for a paper that relies heavily on mathematical formalism.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper contains critical notational errors that would confuse readers, has inconsistent mathematical presentation, and the theoretical claims are not sufficiently supported. While the idea of connecting density ratio estimation to Fredholm equations is potentially interesting, the execution in this paper is flawed and lacks the technical rigor required for NIPS. The authors should thoroughly revise the paper to correct the notational errors and provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis before resubmission.
