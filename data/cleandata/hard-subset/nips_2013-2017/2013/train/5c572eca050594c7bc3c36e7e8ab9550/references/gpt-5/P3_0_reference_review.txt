Summary
The paper proposes FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), a family of kernel methods for density ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) by recasting the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two formulations are developed:

- Type I: Kp r = Kq 1, where Kρf(x) = ∫ k(x,y) f(y) dρ(y). With samples from p and q, the equation is solved via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS H, yielding closed-form solutions via the representer theorem.

- Type II: Using a local kernel kt that approximates the identity, Kt,p r ≈ q, hence one can solve for r when function values q(xi) are available at points sampled from p.

Several empirical losses are considered (L2,p, mixtures γL2,p + (1−γ)L2,q, and RKHS loss), all leading to linear systems in kernel matrices. The paper provides nonasymptotic error bounds and convergence rates under Gaussian kernels for Euclidean domains and compact manifolds. For model selection, an unsupervised cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) is proposed: choose (t, λ) to minimize discrepancies of reweighted expectations Ep[u(x) r̂(x)] and Eq[u(x)] over test function families U (random linear or half-space indicators). Experiments compare FIRE to LSIF, KMM (in some settings), a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, and importance sampling proxies on a few datasets.

Relation to prior work
The approach is closely related to operator-theoretic views of kernel methods (e.g., Tikhonov regularization, operator inversion in RKHS), importance weighting under covariate shift, and direct ratio estimation (LSIF/KLIEP). The Type I RKHS-loss variant is essentially a regularized, function-valued analogue of KMM (matching kernel means), clarifying KMM as an instance of solving Kp r = Kq 1 in H. The Type II formulation leverages the approximate identity property of local kernels and is reminiscent of convolution-based approximations in nonparametrics. The paper also touches connections to manifold learning via heat kernel asymptotics.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: reframing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem unifies several methods (KMM, LSIF) under a standard regularization framework and gives a principled route to out-of-sample function estimates.
- Methodological flexibility: multiple empirical losses and RKHS choices; simple, closed-form solutions amenable to implementation.
- Theoretical development: finite-sample error bounds for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds; explicit dependence on t, λ, n, m; use of spectral/operator tools.
- Unsupervised model selection: CD-CV is reasonable and practical, leveraging the importance sampling identity with user-chosen test function classes.
- Discussion of practical scenarios where Type II is attractive (q evaluable but costly to sample, e.g., MCMC targets).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Statistical rates appear suboptimal. The corollary rates (e.g., for Type I, L2,p error squared decaying like n−1/(3.5 + d/2)) are very slow and substantially worse than standard nonparametric rates for smooth (e.g., Sobolev s=2) function estimation r = q/p, where rates of order n−4/(4+d) are typical under L2 losses. No lower bounds are provided, making it hard to gauge optimality. The unusual exponents (e.g., λ−1/6) suggest technical looseness in concentration/approximation steps or suboptimal parameter balancing.
- Lack of constraints on the ratio. FIRE solves an unconstrained Tikhonov problem; r̂ can be negative or violate Ep[r̂]=1. In practice, positivity and normalization are important for reweighting. KMM, LSIF, and uLSIF usually enforce non-negativity and sometimes normalization; the empirical comparison should discuss this and consider constrained variants or penalizations to ensure valid weights, especially when p is small in the tail where ratios blow up.
- Type II realism and analysis. The assumption that q(xi) is available on p-sampled points is restrictive outside MCMC/unnormalized models. The approximation bias ‖Kt,q 1 − q‖ is treated as O(t), but the interaction between this bias and λ, and the required smoothness assumptions, are not fully explored or optimized. Boundary effects are only briefly mentioned.
- Experimental section is underpowered. 
  - Baselines: comparison is limited; widely used variants like uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF and divergence-minimization approaches are not included. The KMM comparison is sometimes omitted “for lack of out-of-sample extension,” but many tasks only require weights on p’s sample, where KMM is well-defined.
  - Ground truth: there are no synthetic experiments with known p, q to directly measure ratio error and the impact of positivity/normalization constraints.
  - Metrics: The CD-CV metric is a test-function IPM/MMD-like discrepancy; results depend on U. Robustness to the choice of U (and to random seeds) is not thoroughly explored. More downstream metrics (e.g., covariate-shift adaptation performance with confidence intervals) and ablations (γ, kernel choices) are needed.
  - Scalability: No complexity analysis or large-scale experiments are provided. FIRE requires solving dense linear systems in n; discussion of Nyström/CG/preconditioning would be helpful.
- Presentation issues.
  - Some notational slips (e.g., missing square in the empirical L2,p norm statement) and dense algebra without enough intuition for the linear systems may hinder reproducibility.
  - Assumptions like absolute continuity (support of q within support of p) and conditions ensuring q/p, q/p^2 ∈ W2
2 are critical but not emphasized upfront.

Suggestions for improvement
- Tighten the analysis: optimize over t and λ explicitly, state rates in terms of smoothness s, and compare to known minimax rates (e.g., Nguyen et al., 2007). If suboptimal, explain trade-offs or provide improved bounds (e.g., via spectral filtering beyond Tikhonov).
- Incorporate constraints: positivity and (optionally) normalization, either via convex constraints, barrier penalties, or post-hoc calibration (e.g., clipping and renormalization), and analyze their effects.
- Expand experiments: add synthetic settings with known ratios; include uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF and recent ratio estimators; report standard errors; vary U; test large n with approximations; provide timing/conditioning details.
- Clarify the relationship to MMD/IPMs: CD-CV is effectively a squared IPM over U; discuss its statistical properties and potential overfitting if U is rich and validation folds are small.
- Discuss support mismatch and small-p regions; propose strategies (e.g., thresholding, localized regularization) analogous to TIKDE but principled within the FIRE framework.
- Clean up notation and provide a short derivation sketch of the closed-form solutions, with dimensions and scaling.

Pros
- Elegant operator-theoretic unification; principled RKHS regularization; out-of-sample ratio function.
- Simple algorithms with closed forms and multiple loss options.
- Nontrivial theory including manifold case.
- Practical unsupervised hyperparameter selection idea.

Cons
- Statistically weak rates and no optimality discussion.
- No positivity/normalization; potential practical issues when p is small.
- Experimental evaluation insufficient and missing key baselines/ablations.
- Some clarity/notation issues.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The conceptual contribution is interesting and potentially impactful, but the empirical evidence and statistical guarantees need strengthening, and practical aspects (constraints, robustness) should be addressed. With improved theory (tighter rates or clarity on limits), stronger experiments, and constrained variants, this work could be a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
