### Summary of the Paper

This paper recasts the problem of density ratio estimation, $q(x)/p(x)$, as a classical inverse problem. The authors formulate this estimation task as solving a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. They propose two primary settings based on this formulation:
1.  **Type I:** Based on the equality $K_p [q/p] = K_q [1]$, where $K_p$ and $K_q$ are integral operators associated with a kernel $k$. This setting is applicable when samples from both distributions $p$ and $q$ are available.
2.  **Type II:** Based on the approximation $K_{t,p} [q/p] \approx q$, where $K_{t,p}$ is an integral operator with a "local" kernel (e.g., a Gaussian). This setting is useful when one has samples from $p$ and can evaluate the function $q$ at those sample points.

To solve these ill-posed integral equations, the authors employ Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This approach, which they term FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), leads to a family of simple and flexible algorithms. The application of the representer theorem yields closed-form solutions that involve inverting kernel matrices, making the algorithms straightforward to implement.

The paper provides a significant theoretical contribution by deriving error bounds and convergence rates for the proposed estimators using Gaussian kernels. The analysis is comprehensive, covering cases where the data lies on Euclidean spaces as well as on compact manifolds. A key practical contribution is the proposal of a novel, fully unsupervised method for hyperparameter selection called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which leverages the importance sampling identity to create a validation metric.

The work is well-situated within the existing literature on density ratio estimation and covariate shift, drawing clear connections to and distinctions from prior methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF). The authors demonstrate experimentally that FIRE performs competitively against these alternatives on several benchmark datasets.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The central contribution—framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation—is elegant and powerful. It provides a new theoretical lens through which to view this important problem, connecting it to the rich literature on inverse problems and operator theory. This is a significant conceptual advance.

2.  **Flexible and Unifying Framework:** The FIRE framework is highly flexible. By varying the loss function (L2,p, mixed L2,p/L2,q, or RKHS norm) and the problem setting (Type I vs. Type II), the framework generates a family of related algorithms. This provides a unifying perspective on a range of estimation scenarios.

3.  **Algorithmic Simplicity:** Despite the sophisticated theoretical underpinnings, the resulting algorithms are remarkably simple. They have non-iterative, closed-form solutions derived from standard kernel matrix operations, making them easy to implement and computationally deterministic.

4.  **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The paper includes a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed methods. The derivation of error bounds and convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold settings (Theorems 1 & 3, Corollaries 2 & 4) adds significant weight to the paper's claims and demonstrates the utility of the inverse problem formulation for theoretical study.

5.  **Practical Solution for Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method for unsupervised hyperparameter selection is a major practical strength. Model selection is a notoriously difficult problem in unsupervised settings, and CD-CV provides an intuitive and effective solution that does not require labeled data. The paper shows how this can be used to select both the kernel width and the regularization parameter.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Scalability:** The proposed algorithms require the inversion of an $n \times n$ matrix, where $n$ is the number of samples from the distribution $p$. This results in a computational complexity of $O(n^3)$ and memory complexity of $O(n^2)$, which is a significant barrier for large datasets. The paper does not discuss this limitation or potential strategies to mitigate it (e.g., low-rank approximations like the Nyström method or random features).

2.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The experimental evaluation is solid but could be strengthened. A direct comparison with Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) is notably absent, with the authors citing the difficulty of out-of-sample extension for KMM. However, given that the paper frames one of its variants as a regularized version of KMM, an experiment designed to specifically highlight the benefits of regularization (e.g., stability or performance on noisy data), even if limited to in-sample evaluation, would have been highly insightful.

3.  **Choice of Test Functions for CD-CV:** The CD-CV method relies on a family of test functions $U$. The paper suggests using random linear functions or half-spaces, but the choice feels somewhat arbitrary. A more detailed discussion on the properties an effective family of test functions should have, or an analysis of the sensitivity of the results to this choice, would improve the paper's practical guidance.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

*   **On Scalability:** It would be valuable to add a paragraph in the conclusion or a future work section acknowledging the $O(n^3)$ complexity as a limitation and briefly mentioning potential scalable approximations. This would manage reader expectations and position the work more comprehensively within the broader machine learning landscape.

*   **On the RKHS Norm Algorithm:** The formulation using an RKHS norm as the loss function (leading to Eq. 10) is presented as a regularized version of KMM. This connection is very interesting. Could the authors comment on whether the Tikhonov regularization term $\lambda ||f||^2_H$ provides a clear advantage over the constraints used in the original KMM formulation (e.g., $f(x) \ge 0$, $\mathbb{E}_p[f(x)] = 1$)? A small experiment demonstrating this on a toy problem would be compelling.

*   **On Theory:** The theoretical analysis is a highlight. However, it is focused on Gaussian kernels. A brief discussion on the challenges or possibilities of extending the analysis to other common kernel types (e.g., polynomial, Matérn) would be a welcome addition.

*   **Minor Clarification:** In Section 4, the range for the regularization parameter $\lambda$ is given as $(1e-5, 1e-6, \dots, 1e-10)$. This seems to be a decreasing sequence. Is this a typo, or is the grid search performed in this order for a specific reason?

### Overall Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field. The reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is both novel and insightful, leading to a simple, flexible, and theoretically well-founded family of algorithms. The inclusion of a practical, unsupervised model selection technique further enhances the value of the work. While there are limitations regarding scalability, these are common to many kernel methods and do not detract from the significance of the core contributions. The paper is well-written, the claims are well-supported, and the results are significant. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and elegant formulation of a fundamental problem.
*   Theoretically rigorous, with convergence rates provided for multiple settings.
*   Leads to simple, closed-form algorithms.
*   Includes a practical and effective method for unsupervised hyperparameter tuning (CD-CV).
*   Clearly explains connections to important prior work (KMM, LSIF).

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The method does not scale well to large datasets ($O(n^3)$ complexity), a limitation that is not discussed.
*   The experimental comparison could have been more comprehensive, particularly with respect to KMM.
