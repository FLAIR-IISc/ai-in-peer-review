- Paper reframes density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral inverse problem and solves it with Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS (FIRE), giving closed-form kernel algorithms and several variants (Type I/II).

- Core identities: (I) Kp r = Kq 1 and (II) Kt,p r ≈ q with “delta-like” kernels; estimators derived via representer theorem.

- Theoretical contribution: nonasymptotic bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on compact smooth manifolds, with error decomposition into smoothing bias, regularization bias, and sample error.

- Practical contribution: unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) using random test functions and empirical evaluation under covariate shift against LSIF, KMM, and a thresholded KDE ratio.

- Relation to prior work: properly acknowledges links — Type I RKHS-loss akin to KMM, L2,p relates to LSIF/KLIEP, and ties to RKHS inverse problem theory and manifold kernel operator approximation.

- Strength: conceptual clarity — unifies several existing methods under operator-theoretic framing.

- Strength: algorithmic simplicity — FIRE reduces to ridge-like linear systems yielding out-of-sample predictions, avoiding KMM’s QP and training-point-only weights.

- Strength: theoretical analysis — provides transparent nonasymptotic rates and decomposition of errors.

- Strength: practical model selection — CD-CV is simple, unsupervised, and broadly applicable.

- Strength: empirical results — FIRE variants are competitive or superior to baselines and computationally efficient.

- Weakness: novelty limited — Type I formulations are close to existing KMM/LSIF/Tikhonov approaches; need clearer contrasts with KuLSIF/uLSIF/RuLSIF and Bregman methods.

- Weakness: positivity/normalization not enforced — FIRE can produce negative or unnormalized weights; need discussion and experiments on constrained RKHS methods or projections.

- Weakness: theory gaps — unusual rates without comparison to minimax lower bounds (e.g., Nguyen et al.), unclear constants, choices of t and λ, and Sobolev assumption roles; identifiability concerns for Type II when q is known up to a constant.

- Weakness: CD-CV needs stronger justification — relate to IPMs/MMD and IWCV and analyze stability/consistency and choice of test functions.

- Weakness: experimental coverage limited — missing KuLSIF/RuLSIF/KLIEP baselines, dataset/run details, runtime/complexity reporting, ablations (γ, kernel choices), and stronger KDE baselines.

- Weakness: clarity/notation issues — typos, inconsistent notation, unclear equations, and missing complexity/memory discussion; CD-CV pseudocode and fold description need polish.

- Quality: technically plausible and grounded in RKHS inverse-problem theory; proofs deferred but theorems appear reasonable; empirical section promising but needs broader baselines and runtime discussion.

- Clarity: overall understandable but requires consistent notation, equation numbering, and streamlined presentation of FIRE variants; CD-CV description should be clearer.

- Originality: moderate — operator-theoretic framing and Type II are novel, but core Type I methods are incremental relative to existing kernel Tikhonov/moment-matching work.

- Significance: relevant for covariate shift and off-policy evaluation; closed-form out-of-sample estimators with theory and unsupervised model selection are useful but impact would increase with stronger experiments and minimax connections.

- Pros for acceptance: elegant unification, out-of-sample kernel algorithms, nonasymptotic analysis on Euclidean/manifold domains, practical CD-CV, and competitive empirical performance.

- Cons for acceptance: limited empirical breadth and missing strong baselines, no handling of positivity/normalization, incremental novelty, and presentation/notation problems.

- Key questions for authors: how are nonnegativity and normalization handled; relation of rates to minimax and recommended t/λ schedules; empirical comparison of CD-CV to IWCV; handling of q known up to constant in Type II; and inclusion of KuLSIF/RuLSIF and runtime comparisons.

- Recommendation: Weak accept, conditional on improved clarity and stronger experimental comparisons.
