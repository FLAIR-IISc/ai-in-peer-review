### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio `r(x) = q(x)/p(x)` from samples. The authors propose a novel and principled approach by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The key idea stems from the importance sampling identity `Eq[h(x)] = Ep[h(x)r(x)]`. By choosing the test function `h` to be a kernel function `k(x, y)`, this identity is transformed into an integral equation `Kq1 = Kp r`, where `Kp` and `Kq` are integral operators associated with densities `p` and `q`. This leads to the "Type I" formulation, suitable when samples from both distributions are available. A second, "Type II" formulation is derived from the property that for a delta-like kernel `kt`, `Kt,p r ≈ q`, which is useful when `q` is known analytically but may be difficult to sample from.

To solve these ill-posed inverse problems, the authors apply Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. By formulating empirical versions of the objective functions and applying the Representer Theorem, they derive simple, closed-form solutions for the resulting algorithms, which they name Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE). The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis for the case of Gaussian kernels, establishing concentration bounds and convergence rates for the proposed estimators on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds. Furthermore, the paper introduces a practical and unsupervised method for model selection called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV). Experimental results on several datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of FIRE compared to methods like Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF) and a kernel density-based baseline.

The proposed framework provides a new perspective on density ratio estimation, connecting it to the classical theory of inverse problems. It positions the work relative to prior art such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF, noting that KMM can be seen as an unregularized variant of one of their formulations, while LSIF uses a parametric approach in contrast to their non-parametric kernel method.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is mathematically sound and elegant. The application of Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS is a standard and appropriate technique for solving such ill-posed problems, and its application here is carried out correctly. The derivation of the FIRE algorithms is clear and the resulting closed-form solutions are a significant practical advantage.

The theoretical analysis presented in Section 3 is a major strength of the paper. It provides rigorous error bounds and convergence rates for the FIRE estimators under standard assumptions on the densities and the ratio function. The analysis covers both Euclidean and manifold settings, which broadens the applicability of the results. The claims made in the paper are well-supported by this theoretical foundation.

The experimental evaluation is solid and supports the practical utility of the proposed methods. The authors are careful in their experimental design, particularly in the development and validation of the CD-CV model selection procedure.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The core idea is introduced logically, progressing from the fundamental integral identities to the regularized optimization problems and the final algorithmic solutions. The distinction between Type I and Type II settings is clear and well-motivated. The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations are easy to follow. The explicit formulas for the solutions (e.g., Eq. 8, 10) make the algorithms straightforward to understand and implement. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

### Originality

The paper makes several original contributions. The primary novelty lies in the formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem. This provides a new conceptual framework that connects this machine learning problem to a rich area of classical analysis and operator theory. While kernel-based methods for this problem exist (e.g., KMM, LSIF), the inverse problem perspective is new and leads to a principled regularization scheme that is absent in KMM.

The development of the FIRE family of algorithms is a direct and novel outcome of this formulation. The framework's flexibility in accommodating different loss functions (L2,p, L2,q, RKHS norm) is also a valuable and original aspect. Finally, the proposed Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) method is a novel and practical contribution for unsupervised hyperparameter selection in this specific context, addressing a significant challenge for unsupervised methods. The work adequately references and differentiates itself from prior contributions.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are significant for both researchers and practitioners.
1.  **Conceptual Advance:** The paper provides a new and insightful way to think about density ratio estimation, which may inspire further theoretical and algorithmic developments.
2.  **Practical Algorithms:** The FIRE algorithms are simple to implement, have closed-form solutions, and are shown to be effective empirically. They provide a strong alternative to existing methods.
3.  **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The CD-CV method is a significant practical contribution. The ability to select hyperparameters (`λ` and kernel width `t`) without labeled data is crucial for the unsupervised settings where density ratio estimation is often applied.
4.  **Theoretical Guarantees:** The rigorous convergence rates provide confidence in the method's behavior and performance, which is a key requirement for a mature statistical estimation technique.

The problem of density ratio estimation is fundamental to many areas of machine learning, including covariate shift adaptation, transfer learning, and anomaly detection. This work provides a valuable addition to the tools available for tackling this problem.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Novel and Principled Formulation:** The reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant, insightful, and original.
*   **Strong Theoretical Support:** The paper includes a rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence rates, which lends significant credibility to the proposed method.
*   **Practical and Effective Algorithms:** The resulting FIRE algorithms are simple, have closed-form solutions, and demonstrate strong empirical performance.
*   **Solution to a Key Practical Problem:** The proposed CD-CV method for unsupervised hyperparameter selection is a significant practical contribution.
*   **High Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   **Computational Scalability:** The proposed algorithms require the inversion of an `n x n` matrix, leading to `O(n^3)` computational complexity. This may limit applicability to very large datasets. A discussion of potential approximations (e.g., Nyström, random features) to improve scalability would strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Experimental Comparison:** While the comparison to LSIF is valuable, a more direct and thorough comparison with KMM would be beneficial, as it is the most closely related method. The authors acknowledge the difficulty of out-of-sample extension for KMM, but addressing this challenge (e.g., by fitting a regressor to KMM's weights) would provide a more complete picture.
*   **Scope of Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical analysis is limited to Gaussian kernels. While this is a common and important case, it is unclear how the results generalize to other popular kernels.
