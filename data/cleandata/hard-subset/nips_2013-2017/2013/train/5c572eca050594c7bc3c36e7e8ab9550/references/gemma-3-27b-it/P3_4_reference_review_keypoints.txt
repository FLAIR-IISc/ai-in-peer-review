- Novel formulation: Recasts density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation, enabling operator-theoretic analysis.  
- Theoretical rigor: Provides convergence rates and error bounds (for Gaussian kernel) and extends analysis to manifolds.  
- Algorithmic framework: Introduces the FIRE family using Tikhonov regularization and RKHS, with explicit representer-theorem solutions.  
- Flexibility: Supports different norms, loss functions, and kernel choices, making the approach broadly applicable.  
- Unsupervised parameter selection: Proposes CD-CV for model selection and shows good empirical performance.  
- Relation to prior work: Clearly contrasts FIRE with KMM and LSIF, highlighting differences and advantages.  
- Empirical evaluation: Comprehensive experiments comparing FIRE to several baselines across datasets and tasks.  
- Computational cost concern: Kernel-matrix inversions in Eqs. 8 and 10 may be prohibitive for large datasets; scalability not deeply addressed.  
- Practicality of assumptions: Theoretical results depend on smoothness and bounded-density assumptions; sensitivity to violations is unclear.  
- Experimental novelty: Experiments follow standard protocols and do not showcase a unique application where FIRE’s advantages are pronounced.  
- Type II limitations: Type II setting is less flexible (only L2,p norm); guidance on when it’s advantageous is limited.  
- Notational clarity: Dense equations and numerous subscripts can be difficult to follow in places.  
- Constants in bounds: Need clarification on dependence of constants C1–C4 (e.g., on dimensionality) and their practical impact.  
- CD-CV sensitivity: Suggests investigating sensitivity of CD-CV to choice of test functions U1 and U2.  
- Scalability strategies: Recommends discussing approximations (e.g., Nyström) or other methods to mitigate matrix inversion cost.  
- Regularization comparison: Calls for deeper discussion of how FIRE’s regularization compares to KMM and why FIRE may be more stable.  
- Generalizing Type II: Asks whether Type II can be extended to other norms/losses (even approximately) and trade-offs versus Type I.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound formulation; strong guarantees; flexible algorithms; good parameter selection; solid experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Potential scalability issues; reliance on possibly restrictive assumptions; experiments not fully demonstrating unique strengths.  
- Overall assessment and recommendation: Strong paper; accept with minor revisions to address computational complexity, assumption sensitivity, and notation clarity.
