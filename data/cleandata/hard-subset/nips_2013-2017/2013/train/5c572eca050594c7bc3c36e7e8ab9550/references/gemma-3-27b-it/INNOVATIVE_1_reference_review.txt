This paper presents a novel and compelling reformulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm equation. The connection to classical operator theory is a significant strength, allowing the authors to leverage well-established tools like regularization and RKHS theory. The resulting “FIRE” algorithms are conceptually clean and offer a flexible framework. I particularly appreciate the exploration of different norms and loss functions, and the unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV) is a valuable addition. While the theoretical analysis feels somewhat dense and the experimental validation isn’t overwhelmingly conclusive, the boldness of the approach and the potential for future work are substantial.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is genuinely novel.  It shifts the perspective from a purely statistical estimation problem to an inverse problem with a rich theoretical foundation.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The use of RKHS theory and Tikhonov regularization provides a strong theoretical underpinning for the proposed algorithms. The convergence rates and error bounds, though complex, demonstrate a commitment to rigorous analysis.
* **Flexibility:** The framework allows for different kernels, norms, and loss functions, providing adaptability to various scenarios. The two types of formulations (I and II) address different practical situations.
* **CD-CV:** The unsupervised parameter selection technique is a practical contribution, addressing a common challenge in unsupervised learning.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper carefully places the FIRE algorithms in relation to KMM and LSIF, highlighting their advantages and disadvantages.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Density:** The theoretical analysis is mathematically sophisticated, potentially making it inaccessible to a broader audience. While rigor is appreciated, more intuitive explanations of the convergence rates would be helpful.
* **Experimental Validation:**  The experimental results, while demonstrating competitive performance, don’t definitively showcase a clear and consistent advantage over existing methods. The comparisons could be more extensive and the datasets more diverse. The choice of test functions for CD-CV seems somewhat arbitrary.
* **Limited Empirical Novelty:** While theoretically sound, the FIRE algorithms in practice seem to lead to solutions similar to existing techniques (e.g., relationship to KMM). The added benefit from this reformulation isn’t always immediately apparent in the experiments.
* **Implementation Details:** The description of implementation could be more detailed.  For example, how sensitive are the results to the choice of kernel parameters (beyond ‘t’)?
* **Boundary Case:** The mention of domains with boundaries in the theoretical analysis feels underdeveloped.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a fundamentally new perspective on a well-studied problem.
* The theoretical analysis, even if dense, establishes a solid mathematical foundation.
* The framework is flexible and offers potential for future extensions.
* The CD-CV technique is a valuable practical contribution.
* The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of related work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The experimental results are not overwhelmingly convincing.
* The theoretical complexity may limit the paper’s impact.
* The practical benefits of the FIRE algorithms over existing methods aren’t always clear.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards acceptance, despite the weaknesses. The novelty and theoretical depth of this work are significant. It represents a bold attempt to connect density ratio estimation to classical operator theory, and I believe this connection could open up new avenues for research. The paper isn’t perfect, and the experimental validation could be stronger, but the potential for impact outweighs these concerns. I would encourage the authors to address the clarity of the theoretical sections and to expand the experimental evaluation in a revised version. The authors should also consider elaborating on the practical benefits relative to the computational cost of these methods.



**Rating:** 7/10 (Accept with minor revisions)
