

# Peer Review: "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, solved via Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors introduce two formulations: Type I (when samples from both distributions are available) and Type II (when q(x) values are known at p-sampled points). They provide theoretical error bounds for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean space and on manifolds, and propose a cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) method for parameter selection. The resulting algorithms are called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator).

## Strengths
1. **Theoretical Novelty**: The connection to Fredholm equations and classical inverse problem theory is a significant conceptual contribution. This provides a fresh perspective on density ratio estimation that bridges classical analysis with modern machine learning.

2. **Comprehensive Theoretical Analysis**: The paper provides rigorous error bounds for both Type I and Type II settings across different domains (Euclidean space, manifolds). The convergence rates (Corollaries 2 and 4) are well-motivated and demonstrate how the approach scales with dimensionality.

3. **Practical Algorithmic Framework**: The FIRE algorithms are simple, explicit, and easily implementable. The CD-CV method for parameter selection is particularly valuable, addressing a key challenge in unsupervised learning.

4. **Clear Exposition**: The paper is well-organized, with careful mathematical development and clear connections to related work (KMM, LSIF). The representer theorem application is correctly handled.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Experimental Comparison**: The paper claims to compare with KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE, but fails to include KMM in their experiments (despite calling it "the most closely related algorithm"). This omission undermines their theoretical comparison and makes it difficult to assess the practical advantages of their approach. For example, they state that KMM "does not have a natural out-of-sample extension" but don't demonstrate this empirically.

2. **Type II Setting Limitation**: The Type II approach has an inherent error term O(t) from the kernel approximation (Eq. 3). The paper doesn't adequately address how this affects practical performance or how the error compares to Type I. The theoretical analysis (Theorem 3) shows this error term contributes to the bound, but the experimental section doesn't measure this effect.

3. **Overstated Generality**: While the paper claims their framework is general, the theoretical analysis is restricted to Gaussian kernels. The paper doesn't discuss how their approach would generalize to other kernel families or why Gaussian is particularly advantageous.

4. **Insufficient Differentiation from KMM**: The paper acknowledges the connection to KMM (Remark 2) but doesn't clearly articulate how their regularization approach improves upon KMM's optimization procedure. The statement that "the solution is still a linear combination of kernel functions centered on the sample from p" is true but doesn't explain why their approach is superior.

5. **Computational Complexity**: The paper claims "better computational efficiency" but provides no analysis of computational complexity or runtime comparisons with existing methods.

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a theoretically rich perspective on density ratio estimation that connects machine learning with classical inverse problem theory. The theoretical contributions are strong and well-executed, and the CD-CV parameter selection method is a practical contribution. However, the experimental evaluation is incomplete, particularly the omission of KMM, which is the most directly comparable method.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Including KMM in the experimental comparisons
2. Providing a clear analysis of the Type II error term (O(t)) in practical settings
3. More detailed discussion of computational complexity
4. Better differentiation of the theoretical and practical advantages over KMM

While the theoretical framework is compelling and the approach is novel, the incomplete experimental evaluation limits the paper's impact. The connection to Fredholm equations is a genuine contribution to the literature, but the lack of comprehensive empirical validation makes it difficult to assess the practical value.

## Recommendation
**Weak Accept** (borderline for NIPS)

The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution to density ratio estimation, and the Fredholm equation perspective is novel and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and comprehensive. However, the experimental section is incomplete and fails to compare with the most relevant method (KMM), which is a significant weakness for a machine learning paper. The authors should address these issues before the paper can be confidently accepted at NIPS. If they can provide the missing experimental comparisons and clarify the relationship to KMM, this would be a strong contribution to the field.
