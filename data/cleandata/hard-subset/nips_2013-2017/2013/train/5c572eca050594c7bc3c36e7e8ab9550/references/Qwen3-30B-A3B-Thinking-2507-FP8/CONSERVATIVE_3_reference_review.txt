

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The authors introduce two types of formulations (Type I and Type II) and develop the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) algorithms. The paper also proposes a cross-validation method called CD-CV for parameter selection and provides theoretical error bounds for Gaussian kernels.

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

The paper makes a connection between density ratio estimation (a well-studied problem in machine learning, particularly in covariate shift adaptation) and Fredholm integral equations, which is an interesting perspective. The authors correctly identify that Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) can be viewed within their Type I framework, though they don't thoroughly analyze the differences in optimization procedures. The paper also relates to LSIF and KLIEP, though the distinctions aren't clearly articulated.

The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as an inverse problem is not entirely new - several papers have approached similar problems through operator theory (e.g., [3, 20] cited in the paper). What's novel is the specific formulation using Fredholm equations and the proposed regularization approach, though the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how this differs substantially from existing methods.

## Strengths

1. The paper provides a clear mathematical framework that connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems, which is theoretically interesting.

2. The theoretical analysis for the Gaussian kernel case is comprehensive, providing error bounds and convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold settings.

3. The CD-CV method for unsupervised parameter selection is a novel contribution that addresses a genuine challenge in density ratio estimation.

4. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and technically sound in its mathematical derivations.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of compelling evidence for superiority over established methods:** The empirical results are limited to three datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) and don't demonstrate consistent, significant advantages over existing methods. Table 1 shows FIRE performs similarly to LSIF and TIKDE across different test functions, with no clear pattern of consistent superiority. The paper claims "better computational efficiency" but provides no runtime comparisons or computational complexity analysis.

2. **Overstated novelty:** The paper's main contribution is framed as a "new approach," but the connection to KMM is acknowledged in the related work section. The Type I formulation with L2,p norm is nearly identical to KMM's mathematical formulation (as the authors note), differing only in the optimization procedure. The theoretical analysis doesn't provide new insights that couldn't be derived from existing KMM analysis.

3. **Limited theoretical scope:** The theoretical analysis is restricted to Gaussian kernels, despite the paper's claim of being "flexible and principled." This severely limits the generality of the results. The paper doesn't address how the method would behave with other kernel choices, which is a significant limitation for a method that claims to be within the "popular kernel learning framework."

4. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art:** The paper compares with KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE, but doesn't include more recent methods like those in [14, 26] that have provided better theoretical guarantees. The comparison with KMM is particularly weak since the authors acknowledge the connection but don't clearly articulate the differences in performance or theoretical properties.

5. **Lack of practical guidance:** The paper doesn't provide clear guidance on when to use Type I versus Type II settings in practice. The theoretical analysis for Type II includes an additional error term (O(t)), which the authors don't adequately address in the experimental section. The paper claims Type II is useful when q(x) is known explicitly, but doesn't demonstrate this in a meaningful way.

## Specific Concerns

The paper states: "Our method for unsupervised parameter selection in Section 4 is related to their ideas" (referring to LSIF), but doesn't clearly explain how the CD-CV method differs from or improves upon existing cross-validation approaches. The experiments don't convincingly demonstrate that CD-CV outperforms standard cross-validation techniques.

The theoretical error bounds (Theorems 1 and 3) are interesting, but the constants involved (C1, C2, C3) are not quantified, making it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the bounds. The paper also doesn't provide guidance on how to choose the parameters t and λ in practice, despite claiming these are "key" parameters.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents an interesting mathematical perspective on density ratio estimation, but fails to demonstrate sufficient empirical and theoretical advantages over established methods to justify its novelty claim. The theoretical analysis is limited in scope (Gaussian kernels only), and the empirical results don't provide strong evidence of the method's superiority. The connection to KMM is acknowledged but not leveraged to show clear advantages.

The authors have made a technically sound contribution to the mathematical understanding of density ratio estimation, but the practical significance is limited. The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, clearer demonstration of the advantages of the Fredholm equation approach over existing formulations, and more comprehensive experimental validation across diverse datasets and settings.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, the contributions don't represent a significant advance over the state of the art. The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that the Fredholm equation approach offers meaningful advantages over established methods like KMM and LSIF. The theoretical analysis is limited in scope, and the empirical validation is insufficient to support the claims of improved performance and computational efficiency. The field would benefit more from a paper that makes a clear, empirically validated contribution rather than a rephrasing of existing methods with limited new insights.
