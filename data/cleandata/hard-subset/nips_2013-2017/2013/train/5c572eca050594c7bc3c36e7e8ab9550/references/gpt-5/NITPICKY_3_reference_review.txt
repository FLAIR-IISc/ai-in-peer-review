Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes to recast density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind by exploiting identities of the form Eq[h] = Ep[r h] and, more specifically, Eq. 2 and Eq. 3 obtained by inserting kernel functions. Two settings are introduced: Type I, where both samples from p and q are available and one fits r in an RKHS by Tikhonov regularization to match Kp r ≈ Kq 1; and Type II, where only samples from p are available but q is known (not necessarily a density) and one solves Kt,p r ≈ q for a δ-like kernel. Representer-theorem solutions yield closed-form kernel algorithms the authors call FIRE. They also propose an unsupervised model-selection scheme (CD-CV) that matches weighted expectations across distributions on random test functions, and they provide concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean domains and compact manifolds.

The approach connects to operator-theoretic views of learning (Tikhonov, integral operators, spectral analysis) and is related to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF/KLIEP. The paper positions FIRE as a regularized RKHS alternative with explicit out-of-sample extension and provides some empirical comparisons.

Strengths
- Interesting and conceptually clean reformulation of density-ratio estimation as an inverse problem for integral operators; brings classical regularization tools to bear.
- Derivation of closed-form solutions via the representer theorem, with clear dependence on kernel matrices and regularization.
- Inclusion of a Type II setting (q known, sample from p) that is rarely handled explicitly in ratio estimation.
- Theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels with finite-sample bounds and rates, including manifold support, is valuable in this literature.
- A practical, fully unsupervised model selection heuristic (CD-CV) that is simple to implement and broadly applicable.

Weaknesses and technical issues
Quality and correctness
- Notational inconsistencies throughout: the “ratio” is described as q(x)p(x) in several places, but later consistently used as q/p. The symbol qp is used to mean q/p without a clear declaration; “q p2” appears (likely q/p^2). This seriously hinders readability and risks mathematical confusion.
- Eq. 2 and the surrounding notation Kp q p (x) are ambiguous. Please standardize Kρ f notation and use parentheses to avoid misparsing.
- Approximation of the L2,p norm is incorrect: the text claims ‖f‖^2_{L2,p} ≈ (1/n)∑ f(x_i). The correct empirical approximation requires squares, (1/n)∑ |f(x_i)|^2.
- In “Type II setting comes from the fact Kt,q f(x) ≈ f(x)p(x) + O(t).” This appears wrong: the operator is Kt,p, not Kt,q, since the integration is with respect to p.
- Typos in integral approximations: sometimes Kt,p f ≈ f, elsewhere ≈ p f; with kernels normalized over dx and integration against p(x)dx, ∫ k_t(x,y) f(x) p(x) dx ≈ f(y) p(y). Please make this precise and consistent.
- The empirical solutions include expressions like v = (K_p,p^2 K_H + nλI)^−1 K_p,p K_p,q 1; the placement of transposes and the meaning of “squares” of kernel matrices are not clear. Carefully derive and present these with unambiguous dimensions and definitions.
- No positivity or normalization constraints for the ratio are enforced. This can yield negative “importance weights” and unnormalized weights, which are problematic in downstream tasks. The practical and theoretical implications should be discussed, and constrained variants should be considered.
- The theoretical results have numerous typographical anomalies in exponents (e.g., n^{-1/(4+ 56 d)}, n^{-1/(3.5+d/2)}). These look like typesetting errors (perhaps 5/6 d), but as written they are uninterpretable. Clarification is essential to assess significance.
- Assumptions state “qp, q p2 ∈ W^2_2” which appears to be q/p and q/p^2; please correct.
- The resampling scheme “PCA(a,b)” defines Pi using a malformed logistic-like expression that simplifies incorrectly; denominator is duplicated and does not define a valid probability. This undermines the experimental setup.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous spelling/formatting issues: “TiknonovPhilips” (should be Tikhonov–Phillips), inconsistent spacing and punctuation, missing articles, and many undefined symbols (e.g., 1_zq). References are incomplete (missing years/pages) and inconsistent.
- Equations often omit domain, measures, and assumptions; some quantities are introduced without definitions (e.g., KH vs K, meaning of K^2).
- The paper repeatedly promises a table (Table 1) and specific comparisons, but the provided text contains no actual tables, figures, or numerical results.

Originality
- The operator-theoretic perspective is interesting but overlaps with prior RKHS inverse problem formulations (De Vito et al., Smola/Schölkopf 2000, related works on conditional mean embeddings and kernel spectral regularization). The main novelty appears to be the precise Fredholm framing for ratio estimation and the Type II case; nonetheless, please more clearly distinguish from LSIF/KLIEP/uLSIF/KuLSIF and MMD-based moment matching. Several recent ratio estimators (e.g., uLSIF and KuLSIF variants) and Bregman divergence approaches are not discussed sufficiently.

Significance and empirical evaluation
- The empirical section is too thin for NeurIPS: lack of quantitative results, missing baselines in some settings (e.g., KMM is excluded due to out-of-sample, but straightforward function approximation or Nyström makes OOS feasible), no runtime or scalability analysis despite cubic solvers, and no sensitivity analyses for λ and t. The CD-CV heuristic is plausible but lacks theoretical justification or robustness analysis (choice of test function families, F, variance control).
- The Type II setting, which is a stated contribution, has no dedicated experiments.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually appealing operator-theoretic framing with regularized RKHS solutions.
- Theoretical rates for Gaussian kernels on manifolds are potentially impactful.
- Simple, explicit algorithms and a pragmatic unsupervised model selection idea.

Cons for acceptance
- Numerous mathematical and typographical errors, several of which affect core definitions and theorems.
- Ambiguous/incorrect derivations and notation in key formulas.
- Empirical section is incomplete: no actual tables shown here, limited baselines, no Type II experiments, and no computational analysis.
- Missing or insufficient discussion of positivity/normalization constraints and their impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Rigorously clean up notation, fix all typos, and restate theorems with correct exponents and assumptions. Provide a self-contained derivation of the empirical solutions with clear matrix dimensions.
- Address positivity and normalization (e.g., constrained Tikhonov, post-hoc projection/clipping) and discuss impact on theory and practice.
- Provide comprehensive experiments: include KMM with an OOS extension, LSIF/uLSIF/KuLSIF baselines, ablations of CD-CV (function families, F), runtime and memory scaling, and Type II scenarios where q is known up to a constant.
- Justify CD-CV theoretically (e.g., relation to MMD between weighted p and q) and provide guidelines for choosing test functions.
- Correct and complete the references.

Recommendation
At present, I recommend rejection. The core idea is promising, but the accumulation of technical and presentation issues, plus insufficient and partially inconsistent experiments, prevents a confident assessment of the contribution. A thoroughly revised version addressing the above could be competitive.
