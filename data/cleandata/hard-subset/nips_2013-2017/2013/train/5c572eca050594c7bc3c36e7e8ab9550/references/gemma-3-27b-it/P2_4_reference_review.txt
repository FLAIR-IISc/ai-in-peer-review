## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel perspective on density ratio estimation by framing it as an inverse problem solvable through Fredholm integral equations. The authors leverage tools from operator theory, regularization, and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to develop algorithms (FIRE) for estimating the density ratio, offering two main formulations (Type I and Type II) based on available data. They provide theoretical analysis (convergence rates, error bounds for Gaussian kernels) and an unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV), demonstrating performance on several datasets. The work builds on and connects to existing literature in density ratio estimation (KMM, LSIF) and kernel methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Theoretical Foundation:**  The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is a compelling and novel approach. It connects a practical machine learning problem to a well-established mathematical framework, opening avenues for theoretical analysis.
* **Generality & Flexibility:** The two types of formulations (I and II) offer flexibility depending on data availability (samples from both densities vs. known values of q). The use of RKHS and regularization provides a principled approach adaptable to different norms and loss functions.
* **Theoretical Analysis:**  The paper presents theoretical results (error bounds, convergence rates) which, while limited to Gaussian kernels and specific domains, are a significant contribution.  Providing these bounds lends credibility and understanding to the algorithm's behavior.
* **Practical Algorithm & Evaluation:** The FIRE algorithms are clearly defined and computationally feasible.  The empirical evaluation demonstrates competitive performance compared to existing methods like KMM and LSIF. The CD-CV method is a particularly valuable contribution for practical use.
* **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The paper does a good job of situating itself within the existing literature, specifically acknowledging and contrasting its approach with related methods like KMM and LSIF.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Theoretical Results:** The theoretical analysis is restricted to Gaussian kernels and relatively simple domain geometries (Euclidean space, manifolds).  Extending these results to other kernels or more complex domains would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Complexity of Theoretical Presentation:** The theoretical sections are dense and require a strong background in functional analysis. While mathematically rigorous, they could be made more accessible with clearer explanations and motivations.
* **Experimental Scope:** While the experiments are thorough, the datasets used are relatively standard. Evaluation on more challenging and high-dimensional datasets would further demonstrate the robustness and scalability of the FIRE algorithms.
* **Practical Improvements over Existing Methods:** The performance improvements demonstrated in the experiments seem modest. A more detailed analysis of the scenarios where FIRE significantly outperforms other methods would be beneficial.  The advantage of FIRE beyond the theoretical framework is not always compellingly shown.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, and the claims are generally well-supported by the theoretical analysis and experimental results.  However, the limitations of the theoretical results (kernel and domain restrictions) reduce the overall quality. The work is mostly complete, although extending the theoretical analysis would be a strong improvement.
* **Clarity:**  The writing is generally clear, though the theoretical sections are challenging. The organization is logical, and the algorithm descriptions are well-defined. Some sections could benefit from more illustrative examples.
* **Originality:**  The framing of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation *is* original and provides a novel perspective. The combination of operator theory and kernel methods in this context is also a valuable contribution.
* **Significance:** The results are important, as they provide a new theoretical understanding of density ratio estimation.  The FIRE algorithms have the potential to be useful for practitioners, but their practical advantage over existing methods isn't overwhelmingly demonstrated. This work could encourage further research at the intersection of operator theory and machine learning.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically grounded approach to density ratio estimation.
* Clear and well-defined algorithms (FIRE).
* Comprehensive theoretical analysis (albeit limited in scope).
* Empirical validation demonstrating competitive performance.
* Useful unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV).

**Against Acceptance:**

* Theoretical results are limited to Gaussian kernels and simple domains.
* Theoretical sections can be challenging to follow.
* Performance gains over existing methods are not consistently substantial.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, combined with the theoretical analysis and practical algorithms, makes it a worthy candidate for acceptance. While the limitations of the theoretical results and the modest performance improvements are concerns, the overall quality and potential impact of the work are significant.

**Recommendation:** **Accept (with minor revisions)**.

I recommend acceptance with the following minor revisions:

*   **Expand Discussion of Limitations:**  More explicitly acknowledge the limitations of the theoretical analysis and discuss future work to address these limitations.
*   **Improve Accessibility of Theory:**  Consider adding more intuitive explanations and motivations to the theoretical sections.
*   **Further Analysis of Experimental Results:** Analyze the experiments in more detail to identify specific scenarios where FIRE performs particularly well and provide insights into the reasons for its performance.
