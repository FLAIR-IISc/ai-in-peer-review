## Peer Review: Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel perspective on density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations. The authors leverage tools from operator theory, regularization, and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to develop a family of algorithms – FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) – for tackling this problem. They propose two formulations (Type I and Type II) depending on data availability, and provide theoretical analysis, including concentration and error bounds for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds.  The paper also introduces a cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) technique for parameter selection and demonstrates competitive performance against existing methods like KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE on several datasets.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a significant body of work in density ratio estimation, acknowledging prior approaches like covariate shift adaptation, importance weighting, and kernel methods (KMM, LSIF). It distinguishes itself by explicitly formulating the problem as an inverse problem, enabling the application of tools from functional analysis. The connection to KMM is particularly well highlighted, positioning FIRE as a generalization with a more stable regularization scheme. The authors correctly situate their work within the broader context of kernel methods and regularization techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:**  Framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is a genuinely novel and insightful contribution. This provides a strong theoretical foundation and opens avenues for applying well-established tools from operator theory.
* **Generality and Flexibility:** The FIRE framework offers two distinct formulations (Type I and Type II) catering to different data availability scenarios. The flexibility in choosing kernels, norms, and regularization parameters is a significant advantage.
* **Theoretical Analysis:**  The inclusion of theoretical bounds and convergence rates, even if limited to the Gaussian kernel, strengthens the paper and provides a basis for understanding the algorithm’s behavior. The extension to manifolds is particularly valuable.
* **CD-CV for Parameter Selection:** The unsupervised CD-CV method is a practical and valuable addition, addressing a common challenge in unsupervised learning.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical derivations are presented clearly, and the notation is consistent.
* **Comprehensive Experimentation:** The paper provides a good set of experiments across different datasets and techniques, with comparison to several baselines.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical analysis is currently limited to the Gaussian kernel and relatively simple domains (Euclidean space, manifolds). While this is a good starting point, extending the analysis to other kernels and more complex domains would significantly enhance the paper.
* **Practical Considerations of Type II:** The usefulness of Type II formulation is highly dependent on having accurate knowledge of *q* at the sampled points *x<sub>i</sub>*. This assumption may not always hold in practice, and the paper could discuss this limitation more explicitly.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper does not delve into the computational complexity of the proposed algorithms, particularly for large datasets. A discussion of scalability and potential optimizations would be beneficial.
* **Empirical Results Could Be More In-Depth:** While the empirical results demonstrate competitive performance, the analysis could be more detailed. For example, providing statistical significance tests for the observed improvements would strengthen the conclusions. Investigating the sensitivity of the algorithm to the choice of regularization parameter and kernel width more thoroughly would be valuable.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** Some sections (e.g., the discussion of different norms in the introduction) could be more concise and focused.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The mathematical derivations appear correct, and the claims are generally supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results.  It represents a complete piece of work, going beyond a simple position paper by providing algorithms and empirical evaluation.  The authors demonstrate carefulness in acknowledging limitations.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized. However, as noted above, some sections could benefit from more conciseness. The mathematical notation is generally clear, but the initial introduction of the framework could be made more accessible.

**Originality:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is novel and represents a significant contribution. The combination of operator theory, RKHS regularization, and the CD-CV method is also original. The authors adequately reference related work.

**Significance:** The results are important, as they provide a new theoretical framework and practical algorithms for density ratio estimation.  The FIRE framework has the potential to be used by researchers and practitioners working on transfer learning, domain adaptation, and related areas. The CD-CV method addresses a crucial challenge in unsupervised learning.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically grounded approach to density ratio estimation.
* Flexible and general framework (FIRE) with two formulations.
* Solid theoretical analysis (albeit limited in scope).
* Effective unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV).
* Competitive experimental results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited scope of theoretical analysis (Gaussian kernel only).
* Practical limitations of Type II formulation.
* Lack of discussion regarding computational complexity.
*  Empirical results could be more in-depth.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a **strong contribution** to the field. The novel formulation, theoretical insights, and practical algorithms make it a valuable addition to the NIPS program. While there are areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept with minor revisions.**

I recommend the authors address the following points in a revision:

1.  Expand the discussion of the practical limitations of the Type II formulation.
2.  Include a discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithms.
3.  Provide more detailed analysis of the empirical results, including statistical significance tests and sensitivity analysis.
4.  Consider exploring the extension of the theoretical analysis to other kernels or domains (even briefly).
5.  Improve the clarity of certain sections (e.g., the discussion of different norms in the introduction).
