## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel perspective on density ratio estimation (DRE), framing it as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging tools from operator theory and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to develop a family of algorithms, termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The authors demonstrate theoretical results regarding convergence and error bounds for Gaussian kernels and propose a cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) technique for parameter selection. The work offers a principled and flexible framework for DRE, connecting it to a rich theoretical foundation and providing a fresh angle compared to existing approaches like KMM and LSIF.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The core contribution—reformulating DRE as an inverse problem solvable through Fredholm equations—is a significant conceptual advance. This provides a bridge to a well-developed area of mathematical physics and allows for application of powerful analytical tools.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides detailed theoretical analysis, including concentration inequalities, error bounds, and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings. This is a substantial accomplishment and distinguishes the work from many empirical DRE papers.
* **Flexibility & Generality:** The FIRE framework is highly flexible, accommodating different norms, loss functions, and kernel choices. The Type I and Type II formulations address varying data availability scenarios, expanding the applicability of the method.
* **CD-CV for Parameter Selection:**  The proposed unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV) is a practical contribution, addressing a challenging aspect of unsupervised learning.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper carefully positions itself within the landscape of DRE and related fields (transfer learning, covariate shift), explicitly highlighting connections and differences with KMM and LSIF. The authors correctly identify limitations of KMM regarding out-of-sample extension and stability.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the theoretical framework is elegant, the paper doesn’t thoroughly address the computational complexity of solving the resulting integral equations, especially for large datasets. The matrix inversions involved (e.g., in Eq. 8) can be computationally expensive. A more detailed discussion of scalability and potential approximations would be valuable.
* **Practical Performance:** While experimental results are presented, the performance gains over established methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) appear marginal in some cases. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation, including a wider range of datasets and hyperparameters, is needed to convincingly demonstrate the practical superiority of FIRE.  Specifically, the advantage of FIRE over KMM, given the added complexity of regularization, is not clearly established in the reported results.
* **Limited Scope of Theoretical Results:**  The theoretical analysis is largely confined to Gaussian kernels. While justifiable given its popularity, extending the analysis to other kernels (e.g., polynomial, sigmoid) would significantly enhance the paper’s impact.
* **CD-CV Details:** The implementation details of CD-CV, particularly the choice of test functions (linear, half-space), and their impact on performance are not fully explored. It’s unclear if the choice of test functions is sensitive, and how to select them appropriately in practice.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** Some sections (especially Section 2 with the multiple formulations) can feel dense and require careful reading. More concise and streamlined explanations would improve readability.




**Detailed Comments:**

* **Page 3, Remark:**  The comment about L2,q norm being interesting in Type I is worth expanding upon. Specifically, how does utilizing a sample from q influence the practical performance and potential biases?
* **Section 2, Type II:** The explanation of Type II formulation and its applicability when q is not necessarily a density is a key strength. This expands the use case of the framework beyond standard density ratio estimation.
* **Section 3, Theorem 1 & 3:** The error bounds are impressive.  However, they contain constants (C1, C2, etc.) that are not fully characterized. Understanding the dependence of these constants on the problem parameters would provide deeper insights.
* **Section 4, CD-CV:**  The motivation for CD-CV is sound. However, a more detailed ablation study, analyzing the impact of different test function families and validation schemes, would be beneficial.
* **Table 1:** The table feels somewhat cluttered.  Highlighting the best-performing method for each setting would make the results easier to interpret.  It's also unclear what 'N' represents in the column headers – clarification is needed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to density ratio estimation.
* The analytical results are significant and contribute to a deeper understanding of the problem.
* The flexible framework and unsupervised parameter selection method are valuable additions to the field.
* The paper is well-written (overall) and thoroughly contextualizes its contributions within related work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The practical performance improvements over existing methods are not substantial enough to justify the increased complexity.
* The computational complexity issues are not adequately addressed.
* The theoretical analysis is limited to Gaussian kernels.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**, leaning towards a *conditional accept* contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding computational complexity and providing a more compelling empirical demonstration of the FIRE algorithm’s advantages. The novelty of the formulation, the theoretical rigor, and the potential for future extensions make this a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.  The authors have presented a solid piece of work that, with a few improvements, has the potential to significantly impact the field of density ratio estimation.  I encourage the authors to address the reviewer’s comments and strengthen the experimental validation.
