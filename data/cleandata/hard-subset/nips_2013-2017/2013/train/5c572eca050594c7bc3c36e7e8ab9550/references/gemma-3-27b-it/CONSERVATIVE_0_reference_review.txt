This paper proposes a novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm equation, and utilizes tools from regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to develop algorithms – FIRE – for its solution. While the connection to operator theory is interesting, and the potential for theoretical analysis appealing, I find the paper ultimately lacking in substantial novelty and insufficiently validated against established methods.

The core idea—reformulating density ratio estimation as solving a Fredholm equation—feels largely like a reinterpretation of existing kernel methods. The connection to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) is acknowledged, and the authors correctly point out that their Type I formulation is closely related. However, framing KMM *as* a specific case of solving a Fredholm equation doesn’t, in itself, constitute a significant advance. The contribution appears to be primarily a different perspective and a unification within a broader mathematical framework rather than a genuinely new algorithmic approach. 

The theoretical analysis, while present, is fairly standard for RKHS-based methods and doesn’t offer groundbreaking insights. The convergence rates presented (Theorems 1 & 3) are typical for kernel methods, and the assumptions required (smoothness, bounded densities) are quite restrictive.  The authors acknowledge the dependence on kernel parameters and the need for regularization, issues inherent to the kernel method paradigm itself. The extension to manifolds is appreciated, but the dependence on ε in Theorem 3 makes the rate less attractive in practice.

My primary concern lies with the experimental evaluation. While the authors compare against several baselines (TIKDE, LSIF, and KMM), the experimental setup feels somewhat superficial. The reliance on resampling techniques like PCA for creating covariate shift scenarios is not a standard practice and raises concerns about the realism of the evaluation. The table showcasing results (Table 1) lacks sufficient detail regarding parameter tuning and statistical significance. It’s unclear whether observed differences are statistically meaningful or simply due to random fluctuations. Specifically, the comparison against KMM is hindered by the lack of out-of-sample extension in KMM, which the authors admit. A fair comparison necessitates a method that addresses this limitation.  The comparison lacks a clear justification for the choice of test functions (U1, U2) used for CD-CV and their relevance to the underlying problem.

The unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV) is potentially useful, but its effectiveness hinges on the choice of test functions, which the authors don't fully justify.

**Strengths:**

*   Formalization of density ratio estimation within the framework of Fredholm equations provides a unifying perspective.
*   Theoretical analysis, although standard, offers some guarantees on performance.
*   The FIRE algorithms are well-defined and conceptually simple.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty; largely a reinterpretation of existing kernel methods.
*   Experimental evaluation is superficial and lacks statistical rigor.
*   Reliance on non-standard resampling techniques.
*   Insufficient justification for the choice of test functions in CD-CV.
*   Theoretical results depend on strong assumptions.
*   The advantage over KMM isn’t convincingly demonstrated due to KMM’s limitations and the absence of out-of-sample extensions for fair comparison.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a new perspective on a well-studied problem.
*   The mathematical framework could potentially facilitate further theoretical analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of significant novelty.
*   Insufficient experimental validation.
*   The theoretical results are incremental and rely on strong assumptions.
*   The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a practical advantage over existing methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is technically sound and clearly written, it falls short of the originality and significance thresholds for NIPS. The contribution is primarily a re-framing of existing ideas rather than a substantial advancement. The experimental validation is insufficient to justify the claims made, and the theoretical results, while correct, are not groundbreaking. The paper feels more like a preliminary investigation into a potential research direction than a complete and compelling contribution to the field. The authors would need to significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation and demonstrate a clear advantage over established methods to warrant acceptance.
