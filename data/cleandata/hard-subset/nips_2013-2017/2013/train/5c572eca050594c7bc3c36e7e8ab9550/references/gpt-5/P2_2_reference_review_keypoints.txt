- Reframes density ratio estimation r(x)=q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation and proposes two formulations: Type I (Kp r = Kq 1) and Type II (Kt,p r ≈ q).
- Type I assumes samples from both p and q; Type II assumes samples from p and pointwise access to q (not necessarily a density).
- Both formulations are solved by Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS yielding closed-form solutions via the representer theorem (FIRE) with natural out-of-sample extension.
- Provides concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and compact manifolds, with explicit dependence on bandwidth t, regularization λ, and sample sizes n and m.
- Proposes an unsupervised model selection procedure (CD-CV) using importance sampling identities and families of test functions to define validation losses.
- Positions FIRE relative to prior work: relates to KMM as an H-norm version of Type I and to LSIF/KLIEP which minimize L2,p or KL risks; emphasizes operator-theoretic regularized view.
- Technical derivations and empirical surrogates are sound; approximation Kt * f ≈ f and stated O(t) bias are used appropriately.
- Non-asymptotic theoretical contributions are substantial, including manifold extensions, though some statements contain typographical issues and rates degrade with dimension as expected.
- CD-CV is sensible and related to LSIF/KLIEP risk proxies but lacks theoretical guarantees (e.g., consistency of selected hyperparameters).
- Experiments test FIRE on covariate-shift datasets against LSIF and a KDE baseline; results indicate stability and efficiency but baselines are incomplete.
- Experimental weaknesses include omission or limited use of KMM, lack of KLIEP/uLSIF/classifier-based baselines, unclear variance/effect-size reporting, and missing computational-cost analysis.
- Notation is heavy and occasionally overloaded (multiple K variants); typos and ambiguous exponents in theorems reduce readability.
- Originality: novel synthesis of Fredholm equation framing with RKHS Tikhonov regularization; Type II formulation and manifold analysis are particularly original.
- Significance: offers a unified, regularized operator view with closed-form solutions and theoretical rates, valuable for covariate shift and importance sampling.
- Pros: elegant operator-theoretic framing, solid theoretical analysis, practical unsupervised model selection, and out-of-sample extension.
- Cons: limited experimental breadth, no enforced positivity on r (allowing negative estimates), CD-CV lacks theory and may be sensitive to test-function choice, notational/typo issues, and Type II scale ambiguity when q is known only up to a constant.
- Key suggestions: explore nonnegativity/normalization constraints, analyze CD-CV sensitivity and provide uniform-deviation theory, clarify differences vs. KMM and include fair KMM baseline, discuss computational complexity and low-rank/Nyström variants, and address Type II scaling when q is known up to a constant.
- Overall assessment: strong, well-motivated paper with meaningful theoretical and practical contributions; recommend acceptance conditional on broader experiments and improved clarity.
