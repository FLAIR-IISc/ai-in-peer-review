- Novel framing: Density ratio estimation is formulated as a Fredholm integral equation, yielding the FIRE estimator using Tikhonov regularization in RKHS.  
- Two problem formulations: Type I (samples from both distributions) and Type II (q known at p-sampled points) with explicit representer-theorem solutions.  
- Theoretical contribution: Derivation of error bounds and convergence rates using spectral analysis for Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds with Gaussian kernels.  
- CD-CV: Introduces an unsupervised Cross-Density Cross-Validation method for parameter selection using the importance sampling identity.  
- Empirical evaluation: Comprehensive comparisons to KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE across regression and classification tasks show competitive performance.  
- Strength — theoretical novelty: Connects density ratio estimation to operator theory and unifies/extends existing methods (e.g., KMM as a special case).  
- Strength — algorithmic simplicity: FIRE algorithms yield simple, explicit formulas that are easy to implement.  
- Strength — practical innovation: CD-CV addresses unsupervised parameter tuning effectively.  
- Strength — empirical breadth: Thorough baseline comparisons across multiple datasets and tasks.  
- Weakness — kernel limitation: Theoretical analysis is restricted to Gaussian kernels with limited discussion of other kernels’ applicability.  
- Weakness — related work comparison: Insufficient depth in comparing and contrasting theoretical results with recent works [14] and [26], especially minimax rates.  
- Weakness — computational analysis: Lacks detailed computational complexity analysis and large-scale comparisons to KMM/LSIF.  
- Weakness — Type II trade-offs: O(t) approximation error in Type II and practical selection of bandwidth t are not sufficiently addressed.  
- Requested revisions: Add detailed comparisons to [14]/[26], discuss Gaussian-kernel limitations, provide computational complexity analysis, and offer guidance on Type II bandwidth selection.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions — technically sound, well-written, significant theoretical contribution with practical algorithms, pending the suggested clarifications.
