- Proposes framing density ratio estimation q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation and develops FIRE Type I/II algorithms using Tikhonov regularization in RKHS.
- Provides rigorous theoretical analysis with error bounds and convergence rates (Theorems 1–4) for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds.
- Distinguishes clearly between the two data settings (samples from both p and q vs. q known at p samples) and tailors algorithms accordingly.
- Introduces CD-CV, a novel unsupervised parameter selection method addressing a key practical challenge.
- Connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems and spectral/operator theory, offering a unifying perspective distinct from existing methods.
- Empirically competitive with state-of-the-art methods (LSIF, TIKDE) across regression and classification tasks, with CD-CV performing well in practice.
- Theoretical contributions are technically sound and well-motivated, with precise conditions for the stated bounds.
- Originality is high: reformulation as a Fredholm equation and the FIRE algorithms are novel contributions.
- Significance is strong given the importance of density ratio estimation for transfer learning, importance sampling, and covariate shift.
- Clarity could be improved: inconsistent notation (L2,p norm), vague practical guidance (e.g., selecting “small enough t”), and limited implementation details.
- Theoretical scope is limited to Gaussian kernels, with little discussion of extensions to other kernels or practical implications.
- Lacks a thorough comparison of computational complexity between FIRE and existing methods, which affects practical adoption assessment.
- Experimental depth is somewhat limited: incomplete direct comparison with KMM, fewer datasets, and insufficient parameter-sensitivity analysis.
- Overall assessment: high-quality, novel, and significant work that meets NIPS standards; recommendation: Accept.
- Justification for acceptance: solid theoretical framework, well-designed algorithms, strong empirical results, and a valuable CD-CV method despite some limitations.
