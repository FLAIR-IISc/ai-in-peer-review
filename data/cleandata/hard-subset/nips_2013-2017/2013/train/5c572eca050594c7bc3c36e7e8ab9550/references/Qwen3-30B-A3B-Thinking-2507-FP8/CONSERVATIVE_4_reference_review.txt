

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a new method called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) for density ratio estimation, framing it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors argue that this perspective provides a "classical inverse problem" framework for the problem, which they solve using Tikhonov regularization with RKHS norms. The paper presents two formulations (Type I and Type II), provides theoretical error bounds for Gaussian kernels, and proposes a new unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV).

The main strength of the paper is its theoretical grounding. The connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations is mathematically sound, and the theoretical analysis provides meaningful error bounds for both Euclidean and manifold settings. The paper correctly situates its work in relation to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), noting that KMM can be viewed as a special case of their Type I formulation (with a different optimization procedure). This theoretical contribution is solid and well-executed.

However, the paper's primary claims of novelty and significance are problematic. The core idea of using RKHS regularization for density ratio estimation is not new. KMM (Gretton et al., 2009), which is cited as related work, already operates within a similar RKHS framework. The authors acknowledge this connection but don't convincingly demonstrate how their Fredholm equation perspective leads to substantially different or superior algorithms. The Type I formulation with L2,p norm is essentially a rephrasing of KMM with a different optimization procedure, and the Type II formulation appears to be a special case of a known approach.

The experimental validation is insufficient to support the paper's claims of "strong performance" and "better computational efficiency." The comparison with KMM is incomplete (the authors state they don't compare with KMM because "out-of-sample extension is necessary for fair comparison," but KMM does have out-of-sample extension properties). The comparison with LSIF and TIKDE is limited to a few datasets without sufficient statistical significance testing or detailed parameter sensitivity analysis. The CD-CV model selection method is interesting but lacks thorough validation - it's not clear how much better it performs compared to standard cross-validation approaches.

The theoretical analysis, while mathematically rigorous, doesn't clearly translate to practical advantages. The error bounds involve terms like t (kernel width) that aren't well-justified in the experimental section, and the assumptions (e.g., q/p belonging to Sobolev spaces) are quite strong. The paper doesn't demonstrate how these theoretical results inform practical implementation choices.

The paper's main weakness is its overstated novelty claim. The Fredholm equation framing is a mathematical reinterpretation rather than a substantive methodological advance. The paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that this reinterpretation leads to meaningful improvements in performance, computational efficiency, or theoretical understanding beyond existing methods.

The writing is clear and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical analysis to experiments. The related work section is appropriate, though it could better highlight the specific differences between FIRE and KMM.

In terms of significance, the paper makes a modest contribution to the literature on density ratio estimation. The CD-CV method for model selection has potential, but it's not sufficiently validated to claim it's a major advance. The core algorithmic contribution is incremental at best.

Pro:
- Solid theoretical foundation with meaningful error bounds
- Well-written and logically organized
- Correctly situates work in relation to KMM and other related methods
- CD-CV for model selection is an interesting idea

Con:
- Overstated novelty claim - essentially rephrasing existing RKHS-based approaches
- Insufficient experimental validation to support claims of superior performance
- Theoretical analysis doesn't clearly translate to practical advantages
- Comparison with KMM is incomplete and misleading
- CD-CV method lacks sufficient validation

The paper presents a theoretically sound approach that builds on existing work, but the novelty is minimal and the experimental evidence for practical advantages is insufficient. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why this particular formulation leads to meaningful improvements over established methods like KMM. The authors would need to provide more compelling experimental evidence showing clear advantages over existing methods in practical scenarios to justify acceptance. As it stands, the paper is a minor incremental contribution that doesn't significantly advance the state of the art in density ratio estimation.
