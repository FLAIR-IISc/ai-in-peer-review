### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel framework for estimating the density ratio q(x)/p(x), a fundamental problem in areas like covariate shift correction and importance sampling. The central idea is to reframe this estimation problem as a classical inverse problem, specifically a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors derive two types of integral equations:
1.  **Type I:** `∫ k(x, y) [q(y)/p(y)] p(y) dy = ∫ k(x, y) q(y) dy`. This formulation is suitable when samples from both distributions p and q are available. The integral operator `Kp` and the right-hand side `Kq1` are estimated empirically.
2.  **Type II:** `∫ kt(x, y) [q(x)/p(x)] p(x) dx ≈ q(y)`. This formulation uses a delta-like kernel `kt` and is applicable when the function q(x) is known at sample points drawn from p.

To solve these ill-posed integral equations, the authors employ Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This approach leads to a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), which have simple, closed-form solutions derived via the Representer Theorem. The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis, establishing concentration and error bounds with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds. Furthermore, a practical and unsupervised method for model selection, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), is introduced and validated. Experimental results on several datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods compared to existing approaches like LSIF and a baseline TIKDE.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The core contribution—casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation—is highly original and insightful. It provides a new, principled perspective on the problem, connecting it to the rich mathematical literature on inverse problems and operator theory. This is a significant conceptual advance over existing methods.

2.  **Principled and Flexible Framework:** Using Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS to solve the inverse problem is a methodologically sound and powerful choice. It naturally handles the ill-posed nature of the problem and provides a flexible framework where different loss functions (e.g., L2,p, L2,q, RKHS norm) and regularization kernels can be incorporated. This modularity is a clear advantage.

3.  **Strong Theoretical Support:** The paper is backed by a rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors provide explicit error bounds and convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings, considering not only the standard Euclidean case but also the more challenging setting of distributions on manifolds. This level of theoretical depth is commendable and adds significant value.

4.  **Practical and Unsupervised Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method for hyperparameter selection is a major practical contribution. Hyperparameter tuning is a notorious challenge in unsupervised learning settings like density ratio estimation. CD-CV provides a well-motivated, data-driven solution that does not require labeled data, making the overall method much more usable in practice. The experimental validation of this approach is convincing.

5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the mathematical derivations are easy to follow, and the distinction between the different settings and algorithms is laid out logically.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

1.  **Scalability:** The proposed FIRE algorithms require solving a linear system involving an n x n matrix, where n is the number of samples from p. The computational complexity is O(n^3), which is prohibitive for large datasets common in modern machine learning. The paper does not address this limitation. While this is a common issue for many kernel methods, it would strengthen the paper to acknowledge this and briefly discuss potential solutions, such as using kernel approximation techniques (e.g., Nyström method, random features).

2.  **Assumptions in Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical results rely on the assumption that the true density ratio `q/p` and its square are in the Sobolev space W^2_2. This is a strong smoothness assumption that may not hold in many practical scenarios, for instance, when the supports of p and q are mismatched. A discussion on the implications of this assumption and the behavior of the method when it is violated would be beneficial. How robust is the estimator to non-smooth or even discontinuous ratios?

3.  **Comparison with KMM:** The paper positions itself as a regularized alternative to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) but omits a direct experimental comparison in the supervised learning tasks. The provided reason ("out-of-sample extension is necessary") is not entirely convincing, as KMM's instance weights can be used to train any downstream model. Given the close conceptual relationship highlighted by the authors, a direct performance comparison would be very informative to understand the practical benefits of the proposed regularization and optimization scheme.

4.  **Discussion of Convergence Rates:** The derived convergence rates are a key theoretical result. However, the paper could further contextualize their significance. How do these rates compare to the known minimax optimal rates for density ratio/likelihood ratio estimation (e.g., as in [14])? A brief discussion would help readers appreciate the theoretical optimality (or lack thereof) of the proposed estimator.

5.  **Choice of Test Functions for CD-CV:** The CD-CV method's performance may depend on the choice of the test function family U. The paper demonstrates its effectiveness with linear and half-space functions, which is good. However, a more in-depth discussion on the principles for selecting this family of functions would be valuable. For instance, what are the trade-offs between using simple vs. complex test functions?

### Overall Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and multi-faceted contribution to the field. The novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is both theoretically elegant and practically fruitful. The combination of this principled framework with rigorous theoretical analysis and a practical solution for model selection makes for a very complete and high-quality piece of work. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly relate to opportunities for further discussion and future work, rather than fundamental flaws. The paper is of high quality, original, significant, and clearly presented. I strongly recommend acceptance.
