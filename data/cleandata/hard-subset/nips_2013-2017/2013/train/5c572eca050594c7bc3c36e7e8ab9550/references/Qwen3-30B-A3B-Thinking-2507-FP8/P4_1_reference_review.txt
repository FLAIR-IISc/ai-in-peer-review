

# Review: Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach

This paper proposes a novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which the authors solve using regularization techniques within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors develop two main algorithms (Type I and Type II) that provide a unified approach to density ratio estimation under different assumptions about available data. The work connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problem theory, offering a fresh perspective on an important problem in machine learning.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio q(x)/p(x) where p is given by samples and q is either a known function or another density. This problem arises in importance sampling and covariate shift settings. The authors reformulate density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation, which they solve using RKHS-based regularization. The work builds on previous approaches like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), but provides a more unified theoretical framework. Unlike KMM, which uses RKHS norm for the optimization but not for the weight function, the authors' approach (FIRE) provides a natural out-of-sample extension and better stability. The paper also relates to a broader line of work on kernel methods for inverse problems in machine learning.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and provides a complete theoretical analysis. The authors establish error bounds and convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings in Euclidean space and on manifolds, using Gaussian kernels. The theoretical results are rigorous, with proofs provided in supplementary material. The experimental section demonstrates competitive performance against KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE on regression and classification tasks. The authors carefully address the limitations of their approach, particularly noting the error term in Type II setting and the dependence on kernel parameters.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the connection between the density ratio estimation problem and Fredholm equations is effectively explained. The algorithm descriptions are explicit with clear formulas, and the supplementary material provides additional details. The paper adequately informs the reader about both the theoretical framework and practical implementation. The experimental section provides sufficient details for reproducibility, including parameter ranges and data resampling procedures.

## Originality

The paper makes a significant contribution by connecting density ratio estimation to Fredholm integral equations, a perspective that has not been previously explored in this context. The authors provide a unified framework that encompasses various norms and regularization techniques, which is novel compared to previous approaches. The development of the CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) model selection technique for unsupervised parameter tuning is particularly innovative and addresses a key challenge in density ratio estimation. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly KMM, by providing a more principled regularization framework.

## Significance

Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem with wide applications in transfer learning, covariate shift adaptation, and importance sampling. The authors' approach provides both theoretical guarantees and practical algorithms that perform well in experiments. The theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels on manifolds is particularly significant as it extends the applicability of density ratio estimation to more complex data structures. The unsupervised CD-CV method addresses a critical practical challenge in the field, making the approach more broadly applicable.

## Strengths

1. Novel theoretical framework connecting density ratio estimation to Fredholm equations and inverse problem theory.
2. Comprehensive theoretical analysis with error bounds and convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold settings.
3. Development of a flexible RKHS-based regularization framework that accommodates various norms.
4. Introduction of CD-CV, a novel unsupervised model selection technique for this problem.
5. Strong experimental results demonstrating competitive performance against existing methods.

## Weaknesses

1. The theoretical analysis is limited to Gaussian kernels, which may restrict the generalizability of the results to other kernel choices.
2. The experimental comparison could be expanded to include more recent methods beyond KMM and LSIF.
3. The paper does not thoroughly discuss how the proposed approach handles high-dimensional data, which is a common challenge in density ratio estimation.
4. While the connection to previous work is generally good, the paper could more explicitly contrast with the recent "Direct Importance Estimation with Model Selection" (Sugiyama et al., 2013).

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation by providing a novel theoretical framework and practical algorithms. The work is technically sound, well-organized, and addresses an important problem with clear significance. The theoretical guarantees are rigorous, and the experimental results demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed approach.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning with connections to statistical learning theory, kernel methods, and inverse problems. The authors have carefully positioned their work within the context of previous literature and clearly articulated their contributions.

The strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. The novel theoretical perspective, comprehensive analysis, and practical contributions make it a valuable addition to the field. The paper meets all four criteria for quality at NIPS: it is technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant.

**Recommendation: Accept**
