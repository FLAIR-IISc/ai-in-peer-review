Summary and context
The paper proposes to recast density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as solving a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two formulations are developed:
- Type I: use an arbitrary positive definite kernel k to express Kp r = Kq 1, which follows from Eq(h) = Ep(r h) applied to the kernel sections. This yields an inverse problem for r through the linear operator Kp.
- Type II: use a δ-like kernel kt to exploit Kt,p r ≈ q, giving an approximate integral equation for r when q(x) is known (possibly up to a constant) at sample points from p. 

Both formulations are solved via Tikhonov-Philips regularization in an RKHS H with kernel kH, leading to closed-form solutions through the representer theorem. The authors call the estimators FIRE and provide several variants depending on the choice of loss (L2,p, mixed γL2,p + (1−γ)L2,q, or RKHS norm) and whether KH equals the integral kernel. They present error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean domains and compact submanifolds, and introduce an unsupervised model-selection procedure (CD-CV) based on validating the importance-sampling identity with families of test functions. Experiments compare FIRE to LSIF, KMM (where possible), a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, and standard importance sampling on regression and classification tasks with covariate shift.

Relation to prior work
- The approach is closely related to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), which enforces Eq[Φ] = Ep[r Φ] in an RKHS. The paper correctly observes that this identity is equivalent to the Type I Fredholm equation written in H. The differences are: (i) FIRE directly solves a regularized inverse problem for a function in RKHS (with natural out-of-sample extension), whereas KMM optimizes sample weights without explicit regularization; (ii) FIRE admits alternative losses (L2,p, mixed L2,·) beyond RKHS norm.
- LSIF minimizes L2,p distance between a parametric ratio model and r. FIRE’s Type I-L2 formulation minimizes the discrepancy between smoothed quantities Kp f and Kq 1 rather than f and r; this can improve stability at the cost of introducing an additional smoothing operator to invert. The RKHS-loss variant is essentially a regularized version of KMM with a different optimization path.
- The operator-theoretic framing is in the spirit of inverse problems and spectral regularization in RKHS (e.g., Smola & Schölkopf 1998; De Vito et al.; Steinwart & Christmann). The manifold setting builds on diffusion/heat kernel approximation results.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: casting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem makes the connections to operator theory explicit and clarifies why regularization is necessary. This yields a family of simple, closed-form RKHS estimators.
- Theoretical analysis: nonasymptotic error bounds are provided for both types, with explicit dependence on Gaussian bandwidth t, regularization λ, sample sizes, and dimension, including manifold support. This is more explicit than typical analyses for KMM-like methods.
- Practical flexibility: multiple loss choices (L2,p, mixed L2,p/L2,q, and RKHS) and the Type II case where q is known pointwise (useful in MCMC contexts) are covered.
- Unsupervised model selection: CD-CV is a practical idea, leveraging the importance-sampling identity with random test functions for hyperparameter tuning without labels.
- Algorithms are computationally simpler than KMM’s QP and have natural out-of-sample extension.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty vs. existing methods: While the Fredholm framing is elegant, Type I with RKHS-loss reduces to a regularized KMM; the L2,p-loss variant is a smoothed moment-matching objective. The contribution is more a unifying perspective and regularized formulation than a fundamentally new estimator. The relation to LSIF variants (e.g., KuLSIF/uLSIF) and Bregman-based methods (Nguyen et al., Sugiyama et al.) could be discussed more thoroughly, including conditioning/variance properties.
- Statistical rates: The derived rates (e.g., n−1/(3.5 + d/2) up to logs) are slow and their optimality is unclear relative to known minimax rates for ratio/likelihood estimation under smoothness assumptions (Nguyen et al., 2007). The paper does not position these rates against known lower bounds, nor discuss whether the smoothing/inverse operator makes the problem intrinsically harder than direct L2,p regression on r.
- Identifiability and stability: Kp is a compact smoothing operator, making the inverse ill-posed. The paper regularizes, but conditions under which r is identifiable in H (e.g., injectivity of Kp on H, role of spectral decay, Mercer eigenfunctions) are not articulated. Similarly, the role of choosing k versus kH and the impact on bias are only briefly discussed.
- Practical constraints: The ratio can be negative under the proposed unconstrained regression. This can be problematic for importance weighting; the paper does not address positivity or normalization constraints or post-hoc corrections (clipping, exponentiation) and their effect on theory and practice.
- Type II practicality: Knowing q(xi) at p-sampled points is realistic in some MCMC/unnormalized density scenarios, but the experiments do not include such settings. The bias term O(t) is acknowledged but there is limited guidance on tuning t versus λ in finite samples beyond CD-CV.
- Experiments: 
  - Comparisons omit strong baselines such as KuLSIF/RuLSIF and logistic regression–based ratio estimators. 
  - The datasets are moderate-sized; scaling to large n is not addressed (e.g., Nyström, random features, conjugate gradients).
  - Reporting is somewhat sparse: effect sizes, confidence intervals, and ablations (e.g., γ in mixed loss) would strengthen empirical claims.
  - CD-CV relies on chosen function families (random linear, half-spaces). Sensitivity to these choices and to the number of functions F is not systematically studied; overfitting to the validation function class is a risk.

Clarity
The paper is generally well structured and readable. Some notation is overloaded (k vs. kH, Kp vs. Kp,p, etc.), making formulas hard to parse; a consolidated notation table would help. Assumptions (Sobolev smoothness, boundedness) should be stated once early and referenced in theorems. The manifold boundary case is only mentioned; either include a brief result or remove. Typographical issues (spacing, equation numbering consistency) should be fixed.

Significance
The operator viewpoint and regularized closed-form estimators are useful and likely to interest the community working on covariate shift, kernel methods, and inverse problems. The unsupervised model selection idea is practical. That said, the incremental novelty and limited empirical scope temper the impact.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level; proofs are deferred. The bounds are plausible but conservative; tighter analyses or comparisons to minimax benchmarks would strengthen the work. Empirical evaluation needs broader baselines and more diagnostics.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Clarify identifiability: under what conditions on k (and kH) and on H is r uniquely recoverable? Provide a spectral perspective (Mercer expansion) and link bias to eigenvalue decay.
- Discuss positivity constraints or practical post-processing of r̂ for importance weighting, and how that interacts with theory.
- Compare against KuLSIF/uLSIF and logistic/probabilistic classification–based ratio estimators; include runtime and conditioning comparisons.
- Provide a large-scale variant (Nyström or random features) and report complexity/memory.
- For Type II, include a realistic unnormalized-density or MCMC application and study t-λ trade-offs.
- Analyze CD-CV statistically (consistency, variance) and evaluate robustness to the choice of test-function family and F.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, unifying operator-theoretic formulation with simple algorithms.
- Solid, if conservative, theoretical bounds including manifold support.
- Practical unsupervised model selection.
- Natural out-of-sample extension and stability via regularization.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty relative to KMM/LSIF and related RKHS inverse problems.
- Slow rates and no optimality discussion; limited theoretical comparison to existing bounds.
- Missing strong baselines and limited experimental analysis; no Type II real-world use case.
- Positivity/normalization and identifiability aspects under-discussed.

Recommendation: borderline reject. With stronger empirical comparisons (including KuLSIF and classification-based estimators), a Type II application, and clearer identifiability/positivity discussion, this would be a solid contribution.
