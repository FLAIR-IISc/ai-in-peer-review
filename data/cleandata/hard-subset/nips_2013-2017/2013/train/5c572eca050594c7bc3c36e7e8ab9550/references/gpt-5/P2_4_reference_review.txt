Summary
The paper proposes FIRE, a family of kernel-based estimators for the density ratio q(x)/p(x) by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two settings are treated: Type I (both p and q are distributions with samples; solve Kp(q/p)=Kq1) and Type II (q is known pointwise on samples from p; solve Kt,p(q/p)≈q using a δ-kernel). By applying the representer theorem, the estimators reduce to closed-form linear systems over kernel matrices. The authors also propose CD-CV, an unsupervised hyperparameter selection method that leverages the identity Eq(u)=Ep(u q/p) over random test functions. They provide concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels for Euclidean domains and manifolds, and present experiments against KMM, LSIF, and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline.

Relation to prior work
The work is closely related to KMM (Gretton et al.), which matches RKHS means under covariate shift, and LSIF/KLIEP (Sugiyama et al.), which directly regress q/p in L2,p or with KL loss. The paper usefully clarifies that KMM can be viewed as solving an instance of the same integral equation in H, whereas FIRE solves a Tikhonov-regularized version with an explicit functional parameterization (and thus natural out-of-sample evaluation). The Type II setting (q known, sampling from q difficult) is less commonly treated in density ratio estimation and is a novel angle. The analysis connects to the operator-theoretic perspective in RKHS/inverse problems (e.g., De Vito et al.) and manifold learning.

Quality
- Theory: The operator-based formulation is sound, and building estimators via Tikhonov regularization in RKHS is principled. The paper states finite-sample bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, including Euclidean and manifold cases, which is a notable theoretical contribution. However, (i) the rates in the corollaries (e.g., n^{-1/(3.5+d/2)}) are unusual and not contextualized against known minimax rates (e.g., Nguyen et al., 2007); (ii) the assumptions (bounded densities, Sobolev regularity of ratios) and using the same Gaussian for both the operator and RKHS are strong and differ from the more flexible algorithmic section; (iii) constants and optimal tuning choices for t and λ are not spelled out; and (iv) positivity/normalization of the estimated ratio is not enforced, which can matter for importance weighting and stability.
- Algorithms: The closed-form solutions are clear and easy to implement, and the extension to mixed norms (γL2,p+(1−γ)L2,q) and to an RKHS loss is sensible. Complexity is O(n^3) with dense kernels; there is no scalability discussion (e.g., Nyström, random features, conjugate gradients with preconditioning), which limits practical impact on large datasets.
- Experiments: The CD-CV idea is interesting and genuinely unsupervised. The evaluations on USPS, CPUsmall, and Kin8nm show promising performance and some efficiency claims; however, (i) runtime/memory comparisons are not quantified; (ii) comparisons omit stronger baselines such as (Ru)LSIF/KuLSIF and more recent importance weighting methods; (iii) KMM is omitted in some settings with the rationale of out-of-sample extension, but for training reweighting one typically only needs weights on training points; (iv) sensitivity of CD-CV to the choice and number of test functions and to hyperparameter grids is not analyzed; and (v) no assessment is provided of weight nonnegativity or normalization and their effect on downstream tasks.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with a clear derivation of the empirical objectives and solutions. That said, the notation is heavy and sometimes overloaded (k vs kH vs kt, Kp vs Kt,p), which could be streamlined. It would help to provide pseudocode, a brief complexity summary, and a clearer separation between the algorithmic flexibility (kernels and norms) and the theoretical setting (Gaussian-only, same kernel in operator and RKHS). The CD-CV procedure would benefit from a tighter connection to moment matching/GMM/MMD and a discussion of its consistency.

Originality
- Positive: Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation and treating it as an inverse problem with RKHS Tikhonov regularization is a neat and unifying perspective. The Type II setting and the unsupervised CD-CV procedure are novel contributions. The manifold-aware rates are also a nice addition.
- Caveats: The Type I method is close in spirit to KMM/LSIF and related operator-learning approaches; the main novelty is the inverse-problem framing and regularized functional solution rather than a fundamentally new objective.

Significance
The operator-theoretic lens may influence future work on importance weighting and other inverse problems in RKHS. The availability of closed-form estimators with theoretical guarantees is useful. CD-CV could be practically valuable when labels are unavailable. However, the experimental scope and scalability limitations reduce immediate practical impact, and the improvements over strong baselines are not yet decisively demonstrated.

Pros
- Conceptually clean inverse-problem formulation; clear algorithms with closed-form solutions.
- Theoretical bounds for Gaussian kernels, including manifold support.
- Type II setting addresses cases with known q but no q-samples.
- Unsupervised CD-CV is practical and easy to implement.

Cons
- Limited comparison to stronger and more recent baselines; no runtime/memory analysis.
- No constraints for positivity/normalization of weights; potential instability not addressed.
- Scalability not discussed; O(n^3) complexity.
- Theory assumes Gaussian kernel and identical operator/regularizer; rate optimality and tuning not fully clarified.
- Clarity issues in notation; limited ablation on CD-CV choices.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add positivity and (optionally) normalization constraints or discuss post-processing (e.g., nonnegativity projection) and their effect on theory/algorithms.
- Provide runtime/memory benchmarks and discuss scalable approximations (Nyström, random features, iterative solvers).
- Compare against RuLSIF/KuLSIF and importance-weighted cross-validation (IWCV) methods, and include KMM where appropriate (weights on training points suffice).
- Analyze sensitivity of CD-CV to the set and number of test functions; relate it to MMD/GMM and, if possible, provide consistency guarantees.
- Clarify the role of kernel choices in practice versus the Gaussian-only theory; discuss recommended tuning strategies and provide guidance on t and λ.
- Streamline notation and add pseudocode.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper presents a sound and interesting viewpoint with practical algorithms and nontrivial theory. While empirical scope and scalability could be stronger and some design choices (positivity, hyperparameter selection) merit deeper treatment, the contribution is likely to be of interest to the NeurIPS community and could stimulate further work at the intersection of inverse problems and density ratio estimation.
