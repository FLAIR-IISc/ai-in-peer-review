

# NIPS Review: "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses the fundamental problem of estimating the density ratio q(x)/p(x) where p is given by a sample and q is either a known function or another density given by a sample. The authors propose a novel framework by reformulating this problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it using regularization in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). This perspective establishes a connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems, which is distinct from existing approaches that typically frame the problem as a direct optimization task.

The paper builds on a significant body of work in density ratio estimation (e.g., KMM [9], LSIF [10], KLIEP [23]), but introduces a fresh mathematical perspective by recognizing that the problem is fundamentally an inverse problem for integral operators. The authors also extend the Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) algorithm within their framework, highlighting how their approach provides a more principled regularization and theoretical foundation.

## Quality Assessment

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors provide a precise mathematical formulation of the density ratio estimation problem as a Fredholm equation, which is well-motivated by the importance sampling identity. They develop two types of algorithms (Type I and Type II) that cover different practical scenarios and provide a unified theoretical analysis for both settings.

The theoretical results (Theorems 1-4) are particularly impressive, offering convergence rates for both Euclidean spaces and manifolds under realistic assumptions. The bounds carefully separate approximation error (related to kernel width t), estimation error (related to sample size), and regularization error, which is crucial for understanding the trade-offs in practical applications. The analysis is thorough and well-justified, with appropriate concentration inequalities and spectral analysis techniques.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the proposed FIRE algorithms with multiple baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across several datasets and tasks (regression, classification). The results consistently show that FIRE performs competitively, and the introduction of CD-CV for model selection is a practical contribution that addresses a key challenge in unsupervised learning.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors take care to explain the connections between their framework and existing methods, making it accessible to machine learning researchers. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the algorithms are presented with clear pseudocode and explicit formulas.

The paper effectively distinguishes between Type I and Type II settings, which is crucial for understanding when each approach should be applied. The theoretical analysis is presented in a way that is digestible for machine learning researchers familiar with RKHS theory, without excessive mathematical jargon.

## Originality Assessment

The paper's main contribution is its novel perspective on density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem. While density ratio estimation has been extensively studied, this connection to classical integral equation theory is fresh and provides a unifying framework that explains and extends existing methods. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from KMM (which uses RKHS norm as a loss function but lacks proper regularization) and LSIF (which uses a parametric family of functions).

The CD-CV model selection technique is also original and addresses a significant practical challenge in unsupervised learning. The paper's theoretical analysis for manifold settings (Theorem 1(2)) is another strong original contribution, as most prior work focuses solely on Euclidean domains.

## Significance Assessment

The significance of this work is substantial. Density ratio estimation is a core problem in transfer learning, covariate shift adaptation, and importance sampling, with broad applications in machine learning. The proposed FIRE algorithms provide a principled, theoretically grounded framework that addresses key limitations of existing methods.

The theoretical guarantees, particularly for manifold domains, are valuable for practitioners dealing with real-world data that often lies on lower-dimensional manifolds. The CD-CV technique is particularly significant as it provides a practical, unsupervised method for parameter selection in an otherwise challenging unsupervised learning problem.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Novel mathematical perspective connecting density ratio estimation to Fredholm equations
2. Comprehensive theoretical analysis with convergence rates for both Euclidean spaces and manifolds
3. Practical, well-implemented algorithms (FIRE) with strong experimental results
4. Introduces CD-CV, a valuable unsupervised model selection technique
5. Clear distinction between Type I and Type II settings with appropriate applications

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper could better articulate why the Fredholm formulation provides advantages beyond mathematical elegance (e.g., specific scenarios where it outperforms existing methods)
2. The experimental section could include more detailed comparisons with recent density ratio estimation methods (e.g., [23] KLIEP, [26] recent theoretical analysis of KMM)
3. While the Type II setting is theoretically interesting, the paper could provide more concrete examples where q(x) is known but sampling from q is difficult

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation by providing a novel theoretical framework grounded in classical inverse problem theory. The quality of the technical content is excellent, with strong theoretical guarantees and practical algorithms. The clarity of presentation is outstanding, making the mathematical content accessible to the NIPS audience.

The paper's main contribution is not merely a new algorithm but a new perspective that unifies existing methods and provides a principled foundation for future work. This theoretical advancement, combined with the practical FIRE algorithms and CD-CV technique, makes this a highly significant contribution to the field.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written with excellent organization, substantially original in its mathematical perspective, and significant in both theoretical and practical contributions to density ratio estimation.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, with contributions that advance the state-of-the-art in density ratio estimation while providing a deeper theoretical understanding of the problem. The authors have made a compelling case for the value of viewing density ratio estimation through the lens of inverse problems, and their framework provides both theoretical insights and practical benefits for practitioners.
