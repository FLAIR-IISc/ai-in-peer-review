**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper presents a novel and principled framework for estimating the density ratio `q(x)/p(x)`, a fundamental problem in areas like covariate shift adaptation, importance sampling, and transfer learning. The central idea is to reframe the estimation problem as a classical Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors derive two types of integral equations: Type I, `Kp(q/p) = Kq(1)`, which is applicable when samples from both distributions `p` and `q` are available, and Type II, `Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q`, which is suitable when `q` is a known function.

Recognizing that these are ill-posed inverse problems, the authors propose solving them using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). This approach, termed Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE), is highly flexible, allowing for various loss functions (L2,p, a mix of L2,p and L2,q, and the RKHS norm) and regularization choices. A key advantage of this formulation is that it leads to simple, closed-form solutions for the density ratio estimator, which take the familiar form of a linear combination of kernel functions centered on the data points sampled from `p`.

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing error bounds and convergence rates for the FIRE estimators using Gaussian kernels, covering cases where data lies in Euclidean space or on a compact manifold. Furthermore, the authors address the crucial practical problem of hyperparameter selection (e.g., kernel width, regularization parameter) in this unsupervised setting by introducing a novel method called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV).

This work is well-situated within the existing literature. It explicitly connects to and differentiates itself from prior influential methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF). The authors elegantly show that KMM can be viewed as a special, unregularized instance of their Type I framework, while their CD-CV model selection criterion is conceptually related to the objective function used in LSIF. The primary novelty lies in the "inverse problem" perspective, which provides a unified and theoretically grounded foundation for density ratio estimation, distinct from previous approaches.

**Strengths**

1.  **Elegant and Principled Formulation:** The core contribution—framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem—is both novel and theoretically powerful. It connects a core machine learning problem to a rich, classical area of mathematics, providing new insights and a principled basis for algorithm development.

2.  **Flexibility and Unification:** The FIRE framework is remarkably flexible. It naturally handles different problem settings (samples from both densities vs. samples from one and function values for the other), various loss functions, and different choices of kernels. This provides a unified view that encompasses and extends ideas from prior work.

3.  **Rigorous Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper includes a thorough theoretical analysis, providing concentration and error bounds as well as convergence rates. The analysis for both Euclidean and manifold settings is a significant contribution that adds to the credibility and understanding of the proposed method.

4.  **Practical and Novel Model Selection:** The proposed Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) method is a major practical strength. Hyperparameter tuning is a notoriously difficult problem in unsupervised learning. CD-CV provides a well-motivated, data-driven solution that appears effective in the experiments. This contribution significantly enhances the usability of the proposed algorithms.

5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the mathematical derivations are presented logically, and the relationships to prior work are explicitly discussed, which is very helpful for the reader.

**Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions**

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The proposed algorithms require solving a linear system involving an `n x n` kernel matrix, where `n` is the number of samples from `p`. This leads to a computational complexity of O(n³) and memory complexity of O(n²), which is prohibitive for large-scale datasets.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should explicitly discuss this limitation and suggest potential avenues for scaling the method. Standard kernel approximation techniques like the Nyström method or random features seem directly applicable and would be valuable to mention as future work.

2.  **Clarity on Kernel Choices:** The framework allows for two distinct kernels: one defining the integral operator (`k`) and another for the RKHS regularization (`kH`). While the theoretical analysis and experiments seem to assume these are the same, the paper could benefit from a discussion on the roles of these two kernels.
    *   **Suggestion:** A brief discussion on the practical implications of choosing `k` and `kH` would be helpful. For instance, are there benefits to using a different regularization kernel, and how might one guide this choice?

3.  **Empirical Comparison with KMM:** The experiments are informative but omit a direct comparison with KMM in the main results, citing the lack of a natural out-of-sample extension for KMM. However, given that the paper frames KMM as a special case of its own framework (which does have an out-of-sample extension), a more direct comparison would strengthen the empirical evaluation.
    *   **Suggestion:** It would be more compelling to include KMM in the supervised learning experiments. One could use a standard out-of-sample extension for KMM (e.g., nearest neighbor) or compare the methods on an in-sample task, such as the quality of the importance weights themselves for correcting training set loss.

4.  **Guidance on CD-CV Test Functions:** The CD-CV method relies on a family of test functions `U`. The paper suggests using random linear functions or half-spaces. The performance of the model selection could depend on this choice.
    *   **Suggestion:** While a full analysis may be beyond the scope, it would be beneficial to add a brief discussion providing intuition or practical guidance on selecting the family and number of test functions for CD-CV. An ablation study in the appendix would be even more valuable.

**Decision**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, theoretically sound, and practical contribution to the important problem of density ratio estimation. The core idea of using a Fredholm equation framework is insightful and leads to a flexible family of simple yet powerful algorithms. The solid theoretical analysis and the introduction of the practical CD-CV method for model selection are significant strengths. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate primarily to scalability and further empirical details, rather than any fundamental flaws in the methodology. The work is of high quality, originality, and significance, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS.
