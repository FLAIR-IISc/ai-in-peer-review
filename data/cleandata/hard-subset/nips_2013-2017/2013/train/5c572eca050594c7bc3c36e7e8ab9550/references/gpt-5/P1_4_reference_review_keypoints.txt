- Reformulates density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation and solves it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, producing closed-form kernel algorithms (FIRE) with out-of-sample evaluation.  
- Proposes two formulations: Type I (both p and q sampled) using Kp r = Kq 1, and Type II (q known pointwise on p-samples) using a δ-kernel approximation Kt,p r ≈ q.  
- Empirical solutions reduce to linear systems over kernel matrices and admit representer-theorem solutions.  
- Introduces cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV), an unsupervised model-selection method matching empirical expectations of random test functions under q and reweighted p.  
- Provides theoretical concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean domains and manifolds, with explicit dependence on t, λ, n, m, and d.  
- Connects to prior work: inverse problems literature, RKHS regularization, KMM, LSIF/KLIEP, manifold/operator viewpoints, and recent minimax analyses.  
- Conceptual strength: clear operator-theoretic framing that unifies and clarifies density-ratio estimation as an inverse problem.  
- Algorithmic strength: closed-form representer solutions, out-of-sample evaluation, and multiple loss choices with simple matrix forms.  
- Theoretical strength: nontrivial generalization bounds and valuable manifold extensions.  
- Practical strength: CD-CV addresses unsupervised parameter selection under covariate shift.  
- Breadth: handles both sampled-q (Type I) and pointwise-known-q (Type II) settings.  
- Major weakness: estimated ratios can be negative and need not satisfy Ep[f]=1, which is problematic for importance weighting; no constrained or projection solution evaluated.  
- Notation/clartiy issues: multiple kernels (integral vs RKHS) and operators Kp, Kq, Kt,p are dense and sometimes unclear; derivations of key equations could be clearer.  
- Theory vs practice mismatch: strong assumptions (Gaussian kernels, k = kH, smoothness) not fully analyzed for robustness when violated; condition m > λ1/3 n needs practical guidance.  
- Rates are unusual (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2)) and not compared to known minimax or existing likelihood-ratio rates.  
- Computational concerns: linear systems can be cubic and ill-conditioned for large n; no runtime, memory, or approximation (Nyström/random features) analysis.  
- Baselines: experiments omit KMM and stronger baselines (uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF); TIKDE baseline is weak and thresholding-sensitive.  
- CD-CV limitations: choice/number/distribution of test functions U and robustness to mismatch with downstream tasks are not deeply analyzed; variance/bias trade-offs unaddressed.  
- Experimental scope limited: modest datasets, no large-scale/high-dimensional benchmarks, and no timing or scalability evidence.  
- Overall quality: technically sound with substantial theoretical work, but theory relies on strong assumptions and experiments are limited.  
- Clarity: generally organized but notation is dense; recommend a symbol glossary and bound derivation sketch.  
- Originality: moderate–high — novel Fredholm/inverse-problem viewpoint, Type II formulation, and CD-CV are interesting contributions.  
- Significance: potentially significant if robustness, stronger baselines, and scalability are demonstrated.  
- Key questions for authors: can non-negativity and Ep[f]=1 be enforced; sensitivity to k ≠ kH; runtime/memory comparisons; CD-CV robustness/theory; justification for excluding KMM.  
- Suggested improvements: add positivity/normalization handling, compare to uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF and KMM, provide timing/large-n experiments and approximations, expand sensitivity analyses, and clarify notation.  
- Pros summarized: elegant operator view, simple closed-form RKHS solutions, manifold theory, CD-CV, and out-of-sample estimates.  
- Cons summarized: no positivity/normalization, limited baselines and scalability evidence, dense presentation, and theory-practice gaps.  
- Recommendation: borderline accept pending stronger empirical validation, scalability analysis, constrained handling, and clearer presentation.
