- Proposes estimating density ratios by casting the problem as a Fredholm integral equation and solving via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding two formulations (Type I and Type II) and closed-form kernel solutions called FIRE.  
- Introduces an unsupervised cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) criterion to select bandwidth and regularization by matching empirical expectations of test functions under p and q.  
- Provides theoretical concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean space and on compact manifolds, with explicit dependence on bandwidth t, regularization λ, sample sizes n,m, and dimension.  
- Connects the approach to KMM and LSIF/KLIEP: Type I resembles a regularized KMM variant, Type II uses kernel integral approximations and handles known q values without q-sampling.  
- Theory is technically sound and insightful but uses strong assumptions, focuses on matched operator/RKHS kernels, and yields rates that may be suboptimal relative to minimax results.  
- Algorithmic derivations and matrix forms are clear; FIRE supports out-of-sample prediction and regularization likely improves stability versus original KMM.  
- CD-CV is practical but lacks theoretical guarantees of consistency or explicit linkage to established IPM/MMD criteria; choice and coverage of test functions are unanalyzed.  
- Experiments use reasonable datasets (USPS, CPUsmall, Kin8nm) and protocols but lack detailed quantitative results, error bars, runtime/conditioning info, and a fair KMM baseline.  
- Notation is heavy and sometimes overloaded (multiple k/K variants); a concise notation table and consistent subscripts are needed.  
- The distinction and practical tradeoffs between t (approximation bias) and λ (regularization bias) are under-explained and need clearer guidance.  
- Some references are incomplete and there are typos; pseudocode for FIRE variants and a complexity analysis would aid reproducibility.  
- Originality: framing density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem with RKHS Tikhonov regularization is a novel, unifying perspective; Type II and CD-CV add useful extensions.  
- Significance: offers a principled alternative to KMM/LSIF with nontrivial manifold theory that could influence follow-up work, but practical impact depends on stronger empirical/scalability evidence.  
- Pros: principled operator-theoretic formulation, closed-form regularized estimators with out-of-sample capability, manifold-level theory, and an unsupervised hyperparameter selection proposal.  
- Cons: does not enforce positivity/normalization of ratios, limited empirical detail and scalability analysis, strong assumptions and possibly suboptimal rates, clarity/notation issues, no large-scale approximations discussed.  
- Suggested improvements: enforce or project-to-nonnegativity and normalization, provide complexity/conditioning analysis and scalable solvers (Nyström/random features), strengthen experiments (synthetic known-ratio tests, ablations, fair KMM/LSIF baselines, runtimes), analyze or relate CD-CV to MMD/IPM, and clarify use of different kernels plus polish notation/citations.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — compelling operator-theoretic viewpoint and theory but needs stronger empirical validation and presentation polish.
