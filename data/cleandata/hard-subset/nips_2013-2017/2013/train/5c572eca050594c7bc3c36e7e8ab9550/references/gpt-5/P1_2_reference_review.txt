Summary and relation to prior work
The paper reframes density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as an inverse problem for integral operators. Two core identities are used: (I) Kp r = Kq 1, and (II) with “delta-like” kernels kt, Kt,p r ≈ q. These are Fredholm equations of the first kind in which the unknown is r. The authors solve them with Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding closed-form kernel algorithms via the representer theorem (FIRE). They give several variants: Type I with L2,p loss (and a γL2,p+(1−γ)L2,q variant), a Type I RKHS-loss variant, and Type II for the case when q is known at sample points from p. They analyze the estimators under Gaussian kernels on Rd and on compact smooth manifolds, giving nonasymptotic bounds and rates. They also propose an unsupervised model selection criterion (CD-CV) based on cross-density moment matching using random test functions, and evaluate on covariate-shifted versions of USPS, CPUsmall, and Kin8nm, comparing against LSIF, KMM (when appropriate), and a thresholded ratio of KDEs.

The connection to prior work is properly acknowledged. The Type I RKHS-loss formulation is closely related to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), essentially regularizing the same operator equation but delivering an explicit out-of-sample function. The L2,p formulation relates to LSIF/KLIEP, but here the function class is a full RKHS rather than a finite parametric basis. The operator-theoretic framing ties to classical inverse problems and existing RKHS regularization theory (e.g., De Vito et al.). The manifold case links to kernel integral operator approximation results in learning on manifolds.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: recasting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and unifies several existing methods.
- Algorithmic simplicity: the FIRE estimators reduce to solving linear systems (ridge-like), yielding out-of-sample predictions, unlike KMM’s training-point-only weights and QP.
- Theoretical analysis: nonasymptotic bounds and rates are provided for Gaussian kernels in both Euclidean and manifold settings. The decomposition of errors into smoothing bias O(t), regularization bias, and sample error is transparent.
- Practical model selection: CD-CV is simple, unsupervised, and broadly applicable; it operationalizes the importance-sampling identity across random test function classes.
- Empirical results: on several datasets, FIRE variants are competitive or better than LSIF/KMM and more efficient computationally (no QP).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Novelty relative to KMM/LSIF: The Type I RKHS-loss objective is essentially a regularized KMM-like moment-matching in H; the L2,p objective corresponds to kernel ridge regression on the operator equation. The operator view is insightful, but the practical algorithms are close to known kernel Tikhonov formulations for inverse problems. Please sharpen the novelty claims and contrast more explicitly with KuLSIF/uLSIF/RuLSIF and Bregman divergence approaches, which are missing from the comparisons and related work.
- Positivity and normalization: FIRE does not enforce non-negativity or unit expectation of r. Many applications require nonnegative importance weights. Discuss how to incorporate positivity/normalization constraints (e.g., projected or constrained RKHS optimization), and show the effect of possible negative weights in experiments.
- Theory: The rates (e.g., n^{-1/(3.5 + d/2)}) are unusual; there is no comparison to known minimax rates for likelihood/density-ratio estimation (e.g., Nguyen et al.). Provide intuition for the exponents, recommended choices of t and λ, and how close the rates are to known lower bounds. Clarify dependence of constants and the role of Sobolev assumptions. For Type II, discuss identifiability when q is known only up to a constant (common in unnormalized models) and whether renormalization of r suffices for downstream use.
- CD-CV justification: The criterion matches moments for random test functions; relate it more explicitly to IPMs/MMD and to importance-weighted cross-validation (IWCV) (Sugiyama et al., 2007), and provide a rationale for the chosen function families. A brief analysis of its stability or consistency would strengthen the section.
- Experimental coverage: Comparisons omit (K)uLSIF/RuLSIF and KLIEP; include them for fairness. Provide more details on datasets (dimensions, n, m), runtime/complexity comparisons (e.g., O(n3) linear solves vs QP), and ablations on the impact of γ and choice of k vs kH. The TIKDE baseline is weak; consider stronger KDE-based ratios (e.g., variable bandwidth, boundary correction) or recent conditional mean embedding approaches. For KMM, while out-of-sample is nontrivial, a standard workaround is to regress training weights, enabling test-time evaluation; explain why this was not adopted.
- Clarity/notation: The manuscript contains typos, inconsistent notation, and formatting issues (e.g., q/p ≈ argmin f; missing years in references; undefined constants). Carefully standardize operators/matrices, factor of 1/n scaling, and clearly distinguish k vs kH. Some equations (e.g., products like Kp,p^2 KH) need shape/definition checks in the text. Add a short complexity and memory section.

Quality
Technically plausible and grounded in established RKHS inverse problem theory. The theorems are reasonable given the setting, though detailed proofs are deferred. The empirical section is promising but could be stronger with broader baselines, runtime reporting, and discussion of constraint handling.

Clarity
Overall understandable, but polish is needed: consistent notation, equation numbering, and a streamlined presentation of the different FIRE variants. The CD-CV procedure should be described with clearer pseudocode and fold splits.

Originality
Moderate. The operator-theoretic framing and Type II formulation are nice, and the unsupervised model selection via random test functions is practical. However, the core Type I formulations are close to known moment-matching/Tikhonov approaches.

Significance
Estimating density ratios is important for covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, and integration with mismatched samples. Providing closed-form, out-of-sample estimators with theory and a usable unsupervised model selection criterion is useful. Strengthening empirical evidence and connections to minimax theory would enhance impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant operator-theoretic unification with simple kernel algorithms and out-of-sample solutions
- Nonasymptotic analysis on Euclidean/manifold domains
- Practical unsupervised model selection (CD-CV)
- Competitive empirical performance; potentially better stability than KMM

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical breadth and missing strong baselines (KuLSIF/RuLSIF/KLIEP), no runtime analysis
- No positivity/normalization constraints; practical implications underexplored
- Novelty over prior kernel inverse problem methods is incremental
- Presentation/notation issues

Questions for authors
1) How do you ensure/use nonnegativity and normalization of r in practice? Do negative weights occur, and how do they affect downstream tasks?  
2) Can you relate your rates to minimax results (Nguyen et al.) and provide recommended t, λ schedules?  
3) How does CD-CV compare empirically to IWCV?  
4) In Type II, if q is known only up to a constant, do you renormalize r during use?  
5) Can you include KuLSIF/RuLSIF and runtime comparisons?

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on improved clarity and stronger experimental comparisons.
