## Review of “Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach”

This paper proposes a novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem, specifically framing it as a Fredholm equation of the first kind. The authors leverage tools from regularization theory and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) to develop algorithms (FIRE) for solving this equation, offering both theoretical analysis and experimental validation. While the connection to classical operator theory is interesting and the proposed framework offers a principled approach, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of rigorous justification in certain areas. These shortcomings collectively detract from the overall impact and clarity, leading me to recommend rejection.

**Summary of Ideas and Relation to Previous Work:**

The core idea – representing density ratio estimation as solving a Fredholm integral equation – is conceptually appealing. The authors correctly identify the connection to importance sampling and position their work within the broader context of covariate shift adaptation. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, citing relevant papers on density ratio estimation (e.g., KMM, LSIF) and regularization techniques. However, the discussion often feels superficial, simply stating connections without deeply contrasting the proposed approach with existing methods. For instance, the distinction between FIRE and KMM is outlined but doesn’t fully convey the practical advantages or disadvantages.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The Fredholm equation perspective is a unique and potentially valuable way to view the density ratio estimation problem.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The attempt to ground the algorithms in a solid theoretical framework using spectral analysis and concentration inequalities is commendable.
* **RKHS Framework:** The use of RKHS provides a flexible and principled way to incorporate regularization.
* **Algorithm FIRE:** The resulting algorithms, FIRE, are presented in a clear, explicit manner and have a relatively simple implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Notational Inconsistencies & Lack of Precision:** Throughout the paper, the notation is often inconsistent. For example, the subscript ‘p’ and ‘q’ on operators (Kp, Kq) are used seemingly interchangeably with arguments representing sampling distributions. This ambiguity makes it difficult to follow the derivations. The definition of Kq1(x) is presented without sufficient context - is this a kernel operator acting on the density q(x)? Moreover, the meaning of ‘approximately’ (denoted by ≈) is used liberally without proper quantification or conditions.
2. **Justification of Integral Approximations:** The claim that ∫ Rd kt(x, y)f(x)dx ≈ f(y) + O(t) (referencing [24]) is stated without fully explaining the smoothness conditions required for this approximation to hold.  The authors need to explicitly state these conditions and their implications for the applicability of their approach.
3. **Type I vs. Type II Formulation:** The distinction between Type I and Type II formulations is not always clear. The authors state q doesn’t *have* to be a density in Type II, but this point isn’t thoroughly explored regarding its theoretical consequences or practical implications.  The assumption that knowing q(xi) at sampled points is sufficient for Type II lacks justification.
4. **Empirical Implementation Details:**  The approximations of the integral operator and L2,p norm using discrete sums are standard, but the discussion lacks a nuanced treatment of the impact of sample size on the accuracy of these approximations. The authors state these can be "made precise" with concentration inequalities, but the inequalities themselves aren’t explicitly stated or used to derive bounds on the approximation error.
5. **Theoretical Results – Limited Scope:** The theoretical results (Theorems 1-4) are presented for the Gaussian kernel and specific domain assumptions (Euclidean space or manifolds). The authors acknowledge this limitation, but don’t adequately discuss the extent to which these results generalize to other kernels or domain structures.  The convergence rates appear relatively weak and aren’t convincingly justified.
6. **Experimental Evaluation – Insufficient Comparison:** The experimental evaluation feels incomplete.  While comparisons are made to TIKDE and LSIF, the parameter tuning strategies for these baselines aren’t fully described, making it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparison. The use of different function families (U1, U2) for parameter selection and evaluation is interesting, but lacks detailed explanation. The role of PCA resampling is not clearly motivated. The tables are hard to interpret, lacking statistical significance tests.
7. **Minor Errors and Typos:** Numerous minor errors and typos throughout the manuscript detract from its professionalism (e.g., "Eq(h(x))" should be simply "E[h(x)]").

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel perspective on density ratio estimation.
* Theoretical foundation based on well-established mathematical tools.
* Clear algorithm formulation.

**Con:**

* Significant notational inconsistencies and lack of precision.
* Insufficient justification for key approximations.
* Limited scope of theoretical results.
* Incomplete and potentially biased experimental evaluation.
* Numerous minor errors.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential of the proposed approach, the numerous weaknesses outlined above prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper lacks the rigor and clarity expected of a NIPS publication. While the idea of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is interesting, the execution falls short of delivering a truly impactful contribution. A major revision addressing the points raised above – particularly the notational clarity, justification of approximations, and more thorough experimental evaluation – would be necessary for reconsideration.
