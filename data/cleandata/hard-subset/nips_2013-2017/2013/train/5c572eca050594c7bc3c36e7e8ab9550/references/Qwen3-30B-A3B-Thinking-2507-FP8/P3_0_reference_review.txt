

# Peer Review: "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by formulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using Tikhonov regularization in Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors introduce two formulations: Type I (when samples from both distributions p and q are available) and Type II (when q is known at points sampled from p). They develop the FIRE algorithm, provide theoretical convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, and introduce CD-CV for parameter selection. The work connects density ratio estimation to classical inverse problem theory, offering a principled approach with clear theoretical guarantees.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty**: The paper's core contribution—viewing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem—is conceptually fresh. This connection to classical operator theory provides a unified framework that clarifies the relationship between existing methods (like KMM) and offers new theoretical insights.

2. **Comprehensive Theoretical Analysis**: The convergence rate analysis (Theorems 1-4) is rigorous and covers both Euclidean and manifold settings. The error bounds explicitly account for sampling errors and regularization, which is valuable for practitioners.

3. **Practical Contribution**: The CD-CV parameter selection method is well-motivated and addresses a critical gap in unsupervised density ratio estimation. The experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over TIKDE and competitive performance with LSIF.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear mathematical exposition and logical flow. The connection to related work (KMM, LSIF) is well-explained, highlighting both similarities and differences.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Limited Experimental Comparison**: The experiments only compare with KMM (which is not directly comparable due to out-of-sample extension issues), LSIF, and TIKDE. Recent methods like KLIEP or the more sophisticated MMD-based approaches should be included for a more complete picture.

2. **Theoretical Assumptions**: The theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions (e.g., q/p and q/p² in Sobolev space W²₂(Ω)), which may not hold for many practical density ratio estimation problems. The paper doesn't discuss how these assumptions impact real-world performance.

3. **Type II Setting Limitations**: The Type II formulation has an inherent error term O(t) (or O(t¹⁻ᵋ) for manifolds) that depends on the kernel bandwidth. The paper doesn't provide guidance on selecting t to balance this approximation error with regularization error, which could be problematic in practice.

4. **Scalability Concerns**: The paper doesn't address computational complexity for large-scale datasets. The kernel matrix inversion in FIRE (O(n³)) may become prohibitive for large n, unlike some recent scalable density ratio estimators.

5. **Ambiguous Practical Guidance**: While CD-CV is introduced, the paper doesn't provide clear guidance on choosing the test functions U for different application domains. The experiments use random linear functions, but it's unclear whether this generalizes to other settings.

## Critical Analysis

The paper makes a valuable contribution by bridging classical inverse problem theory with modern density ratio estimation. The theoretical foundation is strong, but the practical implications could be better articulated. For instance, the paper claims FIRE has "better computational efficiency" than alternatives, but doesn't provide runtime comparisons.

The connection to KMM is particularly insightful: the paper shows KMM is equivalent to their Type I formulation with RKHS norm as loss function (Eq. 10), but with a different optimization procedure. This clarifies why KMM lacks out-of-sample extension (since the weight function β isn't in the RKHS), a known limitation of KMM that the authors correctly identify.

However, the paper could have better positioned itself against the recent theoretical work on KMM by Yu and Szepesvári (2009) and the more recent analysis by [26]. The authors acknowledge [26] but don't engage deeply with its findings about KMM's convergence rates.

## Significance and Originality

The paper's main contribution—reformulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation—is highly original and provides a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem. The theoretical analysis for manifold settings is particularly valuable, as most existing work focuses on Euclidean spaces.

While density ratio estimation has been extensively studied (e.g., [9, 10, 23]), this paper offers a unified framework that connects disparate approaches through classical operator theory. This conceptual unification is significant for both theoretical understanding and practical algorithm design.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to density ratio estimation. The theoretical foundation is rigorous, the novel perspective is valuable, and the empirical results support the claims. The main weaknesses are limited experimental comparison with recent methods and insufficient discussion of practical implementation challenges.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the field. With minor revisions to address the experimental comparison and scalability concerns (e.g., adding runtime comparisons, discussing practical parameter selection for Type II), it would be an excellent addition to NIPS. The theoretical insights and CD-CV method alone would be valuable contributions to the field.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses**:
- *Strengths*: Novel theoretical framework, rigorous analysis, clear exposition, practical CD-CV method
- *Weaknesses*: Limited comparison with recent methods, strong theoretical assumptions, scalability concerns unaddressed, Type II setting limitations not fully explored
