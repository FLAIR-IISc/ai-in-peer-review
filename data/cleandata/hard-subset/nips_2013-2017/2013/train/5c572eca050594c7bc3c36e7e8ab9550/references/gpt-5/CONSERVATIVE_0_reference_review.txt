Summary
The paper proposes viewing density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two settings are considered: Type I uses the identity Ep[k(x,·)r(x)] = Eq[k(x,·)1] and requires samples from both p and q; Type II uses a δ-kernel approximation Ep[kt(x,·)r(x)] ≈ q(·), assuming access to q(·) at sample points from p (e.g., when q is known up to normalization). Representer-theorem-based closed forms yield the FIRE estimators. The authors provide finite-sample error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean space and on manifolds. They also propose an unsupervised model selection heuristic (CD-CV) that matches moments of random test functions across reweighted p and q samples. Experiments compare FIRE to LSIF and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, and partially discuss KMM.

Relation to prior work
The approach is closely related to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM): the core identity Eq[Φ] = Ep[rΦ] with Φ the RKHS feature map is equivalent to the authors’ Type I operator equation, and the paper’s RKHS-loss variant is essentially a regularized KMM-like problem with different optimization. It is also related to LSIF/KLIEP (minimizing L2,p or KL to the true ratio using a linear function class), and to broader RKHS inverse problems and learning-as-regularization literature. The operator-theoretic framing is classical; the novelty is positioning density-ratio estimation explicitly as a Fredholm inverse problem and treating both Type I/II in a unified kernel-regularization framework.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: clean operator-theoretic formulation that ties density-ratio estimation to classical inverse problems and RKHS regularization.
- Closed-form kernel solutions with natural out-of-sample extension, addressing a practical limitation of KMM.
- Theoretical results: finite-sample bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings; separation of approximation (t, δ-kernel) and estimation (λ, sample sizes) errors.
- The CD-CV idea is practical and simple to implement; the connection to moment matching is clear.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established methods. Type I is essentially a regularized rephrasing of KMM; the L2,p formulation and use of RKHS regularization are standard. Type II relies on δ-kernel approximation with known q, a setting not central to covariate shift and not evaluated in realistic scenarios where it matters (e.g., MCMC with unnormalized targets).
- Empirical validation is not sufficiently rigorous:
  - Baselines are weak or missing. Excluding KMM because of “out-of-sample” is not convincing; one can evaluate KMM at training points or use Nyström/feature maps for extension. Stronger baselines like uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF and classification-based ratio estimation (e.g., logistic regression density ratio) are absent. No comparison to importance-weighted cross-validation (IWCV) for hyperparameter selection.
  - No synthetic experiments with known ground-truth r(x) to report L2,p errors and examine behavior in low-density p regions—central for ratio estimation.
  - The claimed computational efficiency is not supported by timing/memory comparisons; solving the presented linear systems is O(n^3) and memory heavy, similar to other kernel methods.
  - The Type II scenario (q known at sample points) is not demonstrated in applications where it would be compelling (e.g., AIS or unnormalized models).
- Technical assumptions and rates:
  - Assumptions on q/p and q/p^2 in Sobolev spaces and bounded densities are strong; the analysis does not address what happens near regions where p is small (instability of ratio estimates is a known issue). No positivity or normalization constraints on r are enforced; practical remedies (clipping) are not analyzed.
  - The convergence rates (with unusual exponents like n^{-1/(3.5 + d/2)}) are hard to interpret and are not compared against known minimax results (e.g., Nguyen et al., 2007). Optimality/suboptimality is unclear.
  - Most proofs are deferred, and some matrix formulas (e.g., Kp,p^3 terms) are presented tersely; it is not obvious that all systems are well-conditioned or PSD without additional explanation.
- CD-CV is close in spirit to moment-discrepancy/IWCV approaches from prior work; its novelty is limited and empirical advantage over IWCV is not demonstrated.

Quality
Technically sound in its high-level formulation; however, the lack of strong baselines, missing realistic Type II experiments, and absence of careful analysis around low-density regions and constraints on r reduce confidence that the method advances practice. The theory is developed but restricted (Gaussian kernels, smoothness) and not positioned against known optimal rates.

Clarity
Generally well written; however, notation is occasionally inconsistent (r vs q/p), and some derivations are compressed. A clearer mapping to KMM/LSIF formulations and explicit discussion of constraints on r would improve readability.

Originality
Moderate. The operator-theoretic framing is interesting, but the resulting methods largely repackage known identities with standard RKHS regularization. CD-CV is a familiar idea in disguise.

Significance
Potentially useful as a unifying perspective and for deriving out-of-sample estimators akin to regularized KMM. As presented, empirical significance is limited by weak baselines and lack of compelling application for Type II.

Questions for the authors
- Can you include strong baselines (KMM with practical out-of-sample extension, uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF, and a classification-based ratio estimator)? Also include IWCV for model selection.
- Provide synthetic experiments with known r(x), reporting L2,p error and robustness when p is small; analyze clipping/nonnegativity constraints.
- How do your rates compare to known minimax results? Are they optimal under your assumptions?
- Can you demonstrate Type II on an MCMC/unnormalized-density task?
- Provide time/memory benchmarks and an ablation of CD-CV vs alternative tuning strategies.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean, unified operator-RKHS view with closed-form estimators and some theory.
- Natural out-of-sample extension versus KMM.
- Manifold-case analysis is a nice addition.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty over KMM/LSIF; CD-CV closely related to existing IWCV/moment matching.
- Empirical evidence is insufficient and omits strong baselines; Type II not validated in its motivating use cases.
- Theoretical rates not contextualized against minimax results; practical issues (nonnegativity, instability in low-density regions) not addressed.

Overall rating: 4 (weak reject)
