### Summary

This paper presents a novel framework for estimating the density ratio $q(x)/p(x)$, a fundamental problem in areas like covariate shift correction and importance sampling. The central idea is to reformulate the estimation problem as a classical inverse problem, specifically solving a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors derive two types of integral equations from core statistical principles. The first (Type I) is based on the importance sampling identity and is applicable when samples from both distributions $p$ and $q$ are available. The second (Type II) leverages the properties of local kernels (e.g., Gaussian) and is suitable when $q(x)$ is known analytically, but samples are only available from $p$.

To solve these ill-posed integral equations, the paper proposes using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This principled approach leads to a family of algorithms, dubbed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), which have simple, closed-form solutions derived from the Representer Theorem. The framework is flexible, accommodating various loss functions ($L_{2,p}$, combined $L_{2,p}$ and $L_{2,q}$, and the RKHS norm).

The paper's contributions are significant and multi-faceted. Beyond the novel formulation, it provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing error bounds and convergence rates for FIRE with Gaussian kernels on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds. Crucially, it addresses the practical challenge of hyperparameter selection in this unsupervised setting by introducing a Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) method. The paper situates its contributions clearly with respect to related work such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF), and demonstrates strong empirical performance on several datasets.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Principled Formulation:** The reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is a core strength. It is an insightful connection that bridges machine learning with classical functional analysis and inverse problem theory. This provides a new, theoretically grounded perspective on the problem, distinct from methods that directly model the ratio.

2.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper is technically sound and provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed FIRE estimators. The derivation of error bounds and convergence rates (Theorems 1 & 3, Corollaries 2 & 4) for both Euclidean and manifold settings adds significant value and depth. This level of analysis is a strong point and provides confidence in the method's properties.

3.  **Practical and Flexible Algorithms:** Despite the sophisticated theoretical underpinnings, the resulting FIRE algorithms are simple, elegant, and easy to implement. The closed-form solutions involving kernel matrix operations fit naturally into the standard kernel methods toolkit. The framework's flexibility in handling different problem settings (Type I vs. Type II) and loss functions is also a major advantage.

4.  **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The proposal of the Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) method is a major practical contribution. Hyperparameter tuning is a notoriously difficult problem for unsupervised methods, and CD-CV provides a well-motivated and empirically effective solution. This significantly enhances the usability of the proposed method.

5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the derivations are easy to follow, and the relationship to prior work (especially KMM and LSIF) is explained precisely and fairly. This clarity makes the paper's novel ideas accessible and convincing.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is an excellent paper, there are a few areas where additional discussion or clarification could strengthen it further.

1.  **Computational Scalability:** The proposed algorithms rely on inverting an $n \times n$ kernel matrix, where $n$ is the number of samples from $p$. This leads to a computational complexity of $O(n^3)$, which is a potential limitation for large datasets. While this is common for many kernel methods, it would be beneficial for the authors to briefly discuss this limitation and mention potential avenues for scaling up the method, such as using low-rank approximations (e.g., Nyström method) or iterative solvers.

2.  **Choice of Test Functions for CD-CV:** The CD-CV method's performance depends on the choice of the family of test functions, $\mathcal{U}$. The paper demonstrates its effectiveness using random linear functions and half-spaces. It would be helpful to include a brief discussion on the properties of a good test family. For example, how sensitive is hyperparameter selection to this choice? Does the family need to be rich enough to detect discrepancies, and could a poor choice lead to suboptimal parameters? A few sentences of guidance or intuition would be valuable for practitioners.

3.  **Empirical Comparison with KMM:** The paper provides a clear theoretical connection to KMM, positioning FIRE as a regularized alternative that is potentially more stable and has a natural out-of-sample extension. However, a direct empirical comparison to KMM is absent from the experiments. The text mentions that KMM was excluded from Table 1 due to the out-of-sample extension issue, but it is not clear why it was also omitted from the supervised learning experiments in Tables 2 and 3, where all methods are used to compute weights for a downstream task. A direct comparison on a task that highlights the benefits of regularization (e.g., with noisy data or a small sample size) would have made the paper's claims even more compelling.

4.  **Contextualizing Theoretical Rates:** The convergence rates derived in the corollaries are a key theoretical result. It would be interesting to see these rates put into a broader context. For instance, how do they compare to the known minimax optimal rates for density ratio/likelihood ratio estimation (e.g., as discussed in [14])? A short remark on this would help readers better appreciate the theoretical contribution.

### Recommendation: Accept

This is a high-quality paper that makes significant contributions to the field. The novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem is both theoretically elegant and leads to practical, effective algorithms. The combination of a strong theoretical foundation, a practical model selection procedure, and solid empirical results makes this a standout submission. The identified weaknesses are minor and represent opportunities for further strengthening an already excellent paper. I strongly recommend acceptance.
