Summary
The paper proposes to estimate a density (or function) ratio r(x) = q(x)/p(x) by viewing it as the solution of a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind: Kp r = Kq 1, and by solving the inverse problem via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two settings are given: Type I (samples from both p and q are available) and Type II (values of q are known at sample points from p, using a δ-kernel approximation Kt). Representer-theorem-based closed-form solutions lead to algorithms termed FIRE. The authors provide high-level concentration/error bounds and rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings and propose an unsupervised parameter selection method (CD-CV) based on matching importance-weighted and unweighted expectations of random test functions. Experiments compare against LSIF, KMM (partially), and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline.

Relation to prior work
The paper is closely related to kernel mean matching (KMM), uLSIF/LSIF, KLIEP, and earlier operator-theoretic perspectives on learning with Tikhonov regularization in RKHS. The key identity Kp(q/p) = Kq 1 is a restatement of importance weighting in feature space; the manuscript makes this operator inversion viewpoint explicit and extends it with Type II (when q is known) and δ-kernel approximations. The connections to de Vito et al., Smola & Schölkopf, and conditional mean embeddings are noted, though several links could be deepened.

Strengths
- Clear high-level idea: casting density-ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem is conceptually elegant and connects to rich operator-theoretic tools.
- Simple closed-form algorithms in RKHS with standard linear algebra; potentially practical implementations.
- Theory attempts to provide rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings.
- The CD-CV criterion is an intuitive, general-purpose unsupervised model selection tool that could be of independent interest.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and derivations
- Several formulae contain inconsistencies that undermine confidence.
  - The empirical L2,p norm approximation is written as ‖f‖^2 ≈ (1/n)∑i f(xi) (missing the square). This is not a typo of little consequence; it indicates carelessness in key steps.
  - Operator discretizations mix k and K notation and insert 1/n factors inside kernel matrices in ways that lead to opaque scalings. In the linear systems, expressions like (Kp,p^2 KH + nλI) appear nonsymmetric and potentially indefinite; there is no discussion of ensuring SPD structure for numerical stability.
  - In Type II, defining Kij = (1/n)kt(xi, xj) and then using K^2 KH + nλI yields poorly motivated scalings.
- The δ-kernel approximation Kt,q f ≈ f p + O(t) is cited without conditions; required smoothness and boundary behavior are not carefully stated. In Theorem 3, the additional bias term ‖Kt,q1 − q‖ is said to be O(t) in Rd and O(t^{1−η}) on manifolds, but assumptions and constants are not made explicit.
- Assumptions include q/p and q/p^2 in W^2_2(Ω). The requirement on q/p^2 is unusual and strong; no intuition or necessity is provided.
- The rates (e.g., n^{-1/(3.5 + d/2)}) are nonstandard and not compared to known minimax rates for likelihood ratio estimation (e.g., Nguyen et al., 2007). No lower bounds or discussion of optimality are given.
- Support mismatch is not addressed. If q is not absolutely continuous w.r.t. p or places mass where p is tiny, the operator equation and empirical approximations can break; safeguards or assumptions should be explicit.
- No constraints are enforced on r(x) (e.g., nonnegativity or normalization), unlike KMM and LSIF variants. Negative ratios are possible and may be problematic in practice; implications are not discussed.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typos and notation issues: “TiknonovPhilips,” inconsistent subscripts/superscripts, qp vs q/p, qp2 presumably q/p^2, double 2’s in norms, inconsistent equation numbering, and several symbols undefined where they first appear. This repeatedly hinders readability.
- Theorems depend on “small enough t” and unspecified constants C1–C4; guidance on choosing t and λ from data is deferred to CD-CV but not tied theoretically to the bounds.
- In Eq. 16, the text motivates matching (1/n)∑ u(xp) f(xp) ≈ (1/m)∑ u(xq), but the formula shown drops the 1/n and 1/m factors. This discrepancy materially changes the criterion’s scaling and could bias selection.

Experiments
- Important details are missing: actual tables/figures are not provided here, hyperparameters beyond ranges, F (number of test functions), and variance/error bars. Reproducibility is limited.
- KMM is excluded in some comparisons due to “out-of-sample extension,” but many practical uses require weights only on p-samples; this rationale is unconvincing and weakens the empirical comparison.
- The resampling using labels violates covariate shift (p(y|x) invariant) and blends label shift scenarios; this should be clarified and analyzed, as methods tailored to covariate shift may behave differently.
- No discussion of computational complexity and numerical stability (matrix conditioning, effect of tiny λ, scaling by 1/n in kernels).

Originality and significance
- The operator inversion framing is a neat synthesis, but technically it is close to KMM/conditional mean embeddings identities. The Type II variant when q is known at sample points is a useful niche. Without stronger theory or clearer empirical advantages, incremental novelty.
- CD-CV is practical but lacks theoretical justification for consistency or robustness; sensitivity to choice of U is only lightly explored.

Pros
- Conceptually appealing operator-theoretic perspective.
- Simple closed-form algorithms; flexible losses (e.g., γL2,p + (1−γ)L2,q).
- Attempts to handle manifold settings and provide finite-sample bounds.
- Unsupervised model selection criterion is practical.

Cons
- Multiple methodological and notational inaccuracies; derivations lack rigor and clarity.
- Strong/atypical assumptions and unusual rates without optimality discussion.
- Insufficient and partly unfair empirical evaluation; missing details and ablations.
- No constraints on the ratio; potential negativity and instability not addressed.
- CD-CV definition inconsistency (normalization), and no theoretical support.

Questions for authors
- Can you clarify and correct the empirical norm approximation and all scaling factors in the Gram matrices so that the linear systems are symmetric PSD?
- What precise conditions ensure Kt,q1 − q = O(t) in your settings, and how do boundaries affect bias?
- Why is q/p^2 ∈ W^2_2 required? Can this be relaxed?
- Can you justify the rate exponents and compare to known minimax rates?
- Please reconcile Eq. 16 with the earlier normalized equality and provide a consistent, unbiased criterion.
- How do you prevent/handle negative ratio estimates? Any practical clipping/constraints?

Recommendation
Reject. While the core idea is interesting and potentially useful, the paper contains a substantial number of technical inconsistencies, unclear derivations, and presentation issues that collectively undermine confidence. The experimental evaluation is not fully convincing, and theoretical results are stated with strong assumptions and unusual rates without adequate justification. With careful revision—fixing notation and derivations, adding constraints or discussing negativity, strengthening and fairly extending experiments (including KMM), and tightening theory—the work could become a solid contribution.
