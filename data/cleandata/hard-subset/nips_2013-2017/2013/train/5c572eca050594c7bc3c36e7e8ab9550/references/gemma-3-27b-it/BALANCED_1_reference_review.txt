This paper tackles the problem of density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm equations and regularization techniques from Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The core idea is compelling: leveraging tools from operator theory to provide a theoretically grounded and flexible approach to a widely studied problem in machine learning, particularly relevant in transfer learning and covariate shift scenarios. The authors present two formulations (Type I & II) accommodating different data availability scenarios and derive algorithms (FIRE) with explicit solutions. A significant contribution lies in the theoretical analysis, providing concentration and error bounds for Gaussian kernels on various domains, alongside a novel unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV).

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** Reinterpreting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is a genuinely novel perspective, opening up a connection to a rich mathematical framework. This allows for the application of established tools for analysis and solution.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis, including error bounds and convergence rates, a crucial aspect often lacking in empirical studies of density ratio estimation. The coverage of both Euclidean and manifold domains enhances its generality.
* **Algorithmic Clarity:** The FIRE algorithms are presented clearly, with explicit formulas for the solutions, making them readily implementable. The separation of the inverse problem from the regularization process is a strong point.
* **Comprehensive Comparison:** The experimental evaluation compares FIRE against relevant state-of-the-art methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) on various datasets and tasks, providing a good assessment of its performance.
* **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The CD-CV method addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning: robust parameter tuning without labeled data.
* **Well-written & Structured:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, making it accessible to researchers familiar with kernel methods and functional analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Impact & Scalability:** While theoretically sound, the practical advantages of FIRE over established methods like KMM or LSIF are not overwhelmingly demonstrated. The scalability of the method, particularly the matrix inversions involved in solving for the optimal coefficients, is not thoroughly discussed.  The computational complexity is implicitly high and could be a significant limitation for large datasets.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** Although CD-CV is proposed, the performance of the algorithms might still be sensitive to the choice of kernel parameters and regularization strength. A more detailed discussion of the practical challenges in tuning these parameters would be valuable.
* **Limited Novelty in Experiments:** While the experimental results are solid, some of the datasets and experimental setups are standard in the density ratio estimation literature. Demonstrating FIRE's superiority on more challenging or real-world problems would strengthen the paper's impact.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors mention connections to existing literature, but a deeper discussion of how FIRE differs fundamentally from related approaches in terms of computational cost, robustness, or theoretical guarantees would be beneficial. For example, the discussion of KMM is somewhat dismissive of its theoretical analysis ([6, 26] are cited but not deeply integrated into the discussion).
* **Type II limitations:** The authors acknowledge that Type II settings are limited to the L2 norm. Expanding this could be interesting.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically well-grounded approach to a significant problem in machine learning.
* The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the properties of the proposed algorithms.
* The experimental evaluation demonstrates competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* The practical advantages of FIRE over existing methods are not convincingly demonstrated.
* Scalability and computational complexity are potential concerns.
* The novelty of the experimental results is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The novel formulation, rigorous theoretical analysis, and clear algorithmic presentation are commendable. However, the lack of a clear and substantial practical advantage over existing methods, along with potential scalability concerns, prevents me from giving a strong recommendation for acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject.

**Suggestions for improvement:**

* **Scalability Analysis:** Include a discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithms and potential strategies for improving scalability (e.g., approximations, efficient matrix solvers).
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the impact of different components of the algorithm (e.g., choice of kernel, regularization strength) on performance.
* **Broader Experimental Evaluation:** Evaluate FIRE on more challenging datasets or real-world applications to demonstrate its robustness and generalizability.
* **Deeper Comparison:** Provide a more nuanced comparison to related methods, highlighting the trade-offs between FIRE and existing approaches.
* **Address KMM's Theoretical Advances:** Acknowledge and thoroughly address the recent theoretical advancements in KMM analysis ([6, 26]) and clarify how FIRE offers distinct advantages.
* **Explore Type II generalizations**: Investigate if alternative norms can be used in the Type II setting.
