

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by formulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The authors introduce two main formulations (Type I and Type II) that can be solved efficiently using kernel methods, and they propose a novel unsupervised model selection technique called CD-CV. The paper provides theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and sub-manifolds, and demonstrates competitive performance compared to existing methods.

## Strengths
The paper makes an interesting connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems in operator theory. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive for the Gaussian kernel case, and the proposed FIRE algorithms are well-motivated. The CD-CV model selection technique is novel and addresses an important practical challenge in unsupervised learning. The experimental section is thorough, comparing with several relevant baselines across multiple datasets.

## Weaknesses

1. **Consistent Notation Errors**: The paper contains a critical and pervasive notational error throughout: it uses "q(x)p(x)" to denote the density ratio q(x)/p(x), instead of the correct "q(x)/p(x)". This appears in the abstract, introduction, equations (1), (2), (3), and numerous other places. For example, equation (1) states "Ep(h(x) q(x) p(x))" when it should be "Ep(h(x) q(x)/p(x))". This is not merely a typo but a fundamental error in notation that could confuse readers and undermine the paper's credibility.

2. **Inconsistent Mathematical Notation**: 
   - The Sobolev space is written as "W 2 2 (Ω)" (with spaces) instead of the standard "W^2_2(Ω)".
   - The L^2 norm is written inconsistently as "L2,p", "L2,p", "L2,p" (with and without spaces, and with inconsistent subscript formatting).
   - Kernel matrices are denoted inconsistently as "Kp,p", "Kp,p", and "Kp,p" throughout the paper.

3. **Technical Inconsistencies**:
   - In the Type II setting explanation, the paper states "Kt,qf(x) ≈ f(x)p(x) + O(t)" but earlier establishes that "∫ Rd kt(x, y)f(x)dx = f(y) + O(t)" which would imply Kt,qf(x) ≈ f(x) + O(t), not f(x)p(x) + O(t).
   - The definition of TIKDE (Thresholded Inverse Kernel Density Estimator) in the introduction is unclear: "the standard kernel density estimator for q divided by a thresholded kernel density estimator for p" should specify "q̂(x)/p̂(x)" where p̂ is thresholded.

4. **Formatting and Presentation Issues**:
   - The references contain inconsistent punctuation (e.g., "NIPS, pages 487–495, , " has an extra comma).
   - The paper uses "δ-kernels" (with a hyphen) in some places and "δ-kernels" (without) in others.
   - Equation (8) has inconsistent spacing: "K2p,pKH" should be "K^2_{p,p} K_H".
   - In the related work section, the reference list mentions "19" but the reference [19] is not properly cited in the text.

5. **Missing Clarifications**:
   - The paper mentions "q does not have to be a density function" in the Type II setting but doesn't clarify what properties q must have (e.g., boundedness, integrability).
   - The theoretical analysis assumes "qp, q p2 are in Sobolev space W^2_2(Ω)" but doesn't define "qp" or "q p2" clearly (should be "q/p" and "q/p^2").
   - The paper claims "the solution is still a linear combination of kernel functions centered on the sample from p" in the mixed norm case, but doesn't explicitly state this in the algorithm description.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations, and the theoretical analysis is generally sound, the pervasive notation errors and inconsistent mathematical presentation significantly undermine the paper's quality. The consistent use of "q(x)p(x)" instead of "q(x)/p(x)" is particularly concerning as it represents a fundamental misunderstanding of the problem formulation. These are not minor typographical errors but represent a serious lack of attention to detail in a mathematical paper.

The paper's quality is diminished by these formatting inconsistencies, which make it difficult to assess the technical content with confidence. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these issues are substantial enough to warrant rejection, despite the paper's potentially interesting core idea. A well-executed paper on this topic would have caught these errors before submission.

The paper would benefit from a thorough proofreading to fix these notation issues, clarify the mathematical expressions, and ensure consistent presentation throughout. Without these corrections, the paper's contribution is obscured by its own presentation errors.
