Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper formulates density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solves it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two settings are developed:

- Type I: Use the identity Kp r = Kq 1, where Kρ is an integral operator with kernel k and integration against ρ. Given samples from p and q, this yields a regularized inverse problem in L2(p).
- Type II: Use a δ-kernel kt and the approximation Kt,p r ≈ q, yielding a different inverse problem when q is known (possibly up to normalization) at sample points from p.

By representer theorems, both lead to closed-form kernel solutions (FIRE) as linear systems in Gram matrices. The paper further proposes variants that use L2(p), L2(q), and mixed losses γL2(p)+(1−γ)L2(q) and an RKHS loss; the latter connects to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] as a regularized variant with natural out-of-sample extension. The authors give concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and compact manifolds, analyzing bias from δ-kernel approximation and sampling errors. They also propose an unsupervised model-selection scheme, cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV), which chooses kernel width t and regularization λ by matching expectations of random test functions under reweighted p and q. Experiments on covariate shift constructed from CPUsmall, Kin8nm, and USPS compare FIRE to LSIF [10], KMM [9] (in some settings), a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, and importance sampling when applicable.

This work relates closely to prior importance-weight estimation under covariate shift (KMM [9], LSIF/KLIEP [10,23]) and RKHS-based inverse problems [3,20]. The identity underlying KMM can be viewed as the same Fredholm equation in H; FIRE differs by explicitly regularizing the weight function in an RKHS with closed-form solutions and by covering a Type II regime where q is known but not samplable (relevant for unnormalized models).

Strengths:
- Clear operator-theoretic formulation that unifies several density-ratio approaches as inverse problems.
- Simple, closed-form kernel algorithms with natural out-of-sample extension and a variety of losses.
- Theoretical analysis providing finite-sample bounds and rates in both Euclidean and manifold settings for Gaussian kernels, with explicit dependence on t, λ, d, and sample sizes.
- Practical unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) that is aligned with the objective of matching reweighted expectations between p and q.
- Empirical evidence indicating competitive or improved performance and stability versus LSIF and baselines; discussion of KMM’s stability and out-of-sample issues is accurate.

Weaknesses:
- Constraints important for importance weighting (non-negativity and normalization Ep[r]=1) are not enforced; the practical impact and remedies (e.g., clipping, constrained optimization) are not analyzed.
- Theoretical rates appear suboptimal relative to existing minimax results for likelihood ratio estimation [14]; no direct comparison or discussion of optimality is provided.
- Scalability is not addressed: the solutions involve O(n^3) linear algebra with matrices such as K^2KH or K^3; no complexity analysis, memory footprint, or approximation strategies (Nyström, random features) are discussed, despite claims of better computational efficiency.
- Type II is theoretically interesting but empirically underrepresented. Demonstrations on unnormalized targets (e.g., MCMC, AIS [13]) would strengthen the motivation.
- CD-CV is closely related to integral probability metrics/MMD-style matching; the connection and potential variance/overfitting issues when using random function classes are not analyzed.
- Some experimental comparisons omit KMM due to out-of-sample issues; stronger baselines (e.g., KuLSIF/RULSIF variants) and fair transductive evaluation for KMM would improve completeness.
- Assumptions and constants in bounds (e.g., dependence on λ−1/6, choice of t) are intricate; guidance on selecting t, λ beyond CD-CV to balance bias and variance is limited.

Quality:
- The methodology is technically sound as a regularized inverse problem with RKHS penalties. Derivations of empirical objectives and representer-theorem solutions are standard and correct.
- The theoretical analysis is meaningful and reasonably careful for Gaussian kernels, with separate treatment of Rd and manifolds. However, optimality of the rates is unclear, and practical constraints on the ratio are not incorporated.
- Experimental evaluation supports the claims but could be strengthened with stronger baselines, runtime/memory metrics, and Type II use cases.

Clarity:
- Overall well-organized with explicit problem statements, algorithmic formulas, and settings (Type I/II). Some notation is heavy; collecting all kernel matrices and normalizations early and consistently would help.
- The relation to KMM is explained clearly; the RKHS-loss variant makes the connection concrete.
- Minor issues: missing years in references (as provided), brief treatment of boundary domains, and limited discussion on enforcing valid weights.

Originality:
- The Fredholm equation viewpoint provides a clear, unified lens and leads to new variants (Type II; mixed L2 losses; RKHS-loss regularized KMM). While closely connected to KMM/LSIF, the explicit inverse-problem framing, manifold-aware analysis, and CD-CV selection present a novel package.
- The theory for manifold-supported distributions and δ-kernel bias in this context is a notable addition.

Significance:
- Density-ratio estimation is central in covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, and Monte Carlo with unnormalized targets. The proposed framework and theory are likely to be useful and buildable.
- Practical impact depends on addressing constraints and scalability; nonetheless, the conceptual unification and analytical results are valuable.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Incorporate non-negativity and normalization constraints or provide a principled post-processing (e.g., projection) with theoretical guarantees.
- Provide complexity and memory analysis, and include scalable approximations (Nyström, random features) with empirical runtime comparisons.
- Add experiments demonstrating Type II on unnormalized models (e.g., MCMC/AIS) to validate that setting.
- Discuss rate optimality versus [14], and clarify conditions under which the presented rates are tight or could be improved.
- Analyze CD-CV statistically (bias/variance) and relate it formally to IPMs/MMD; consider adaptive choices of test-function classes.
- Expand comparisons to stronger modern baselines and include KMM variants with regularization to ensure fair out-of-sample comparisons.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, unifying formulation; simple algorithms with out-of-sample extension.
- Theoretical bounds in both Euclidean and manifold settings.
- Practical, unsupervised model selection method aligned with the task.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of constraints for valid weights; limited discussion of scalability.
- Suboptimal or uncontextualized rates; incomplete experimental comparisons and few Type II demonstrations.

Overall, the paper provides a coherent and technically solid framework with promising theory and practice, but would benefit from addressing constraints, scalability, and broader empirical validation.
