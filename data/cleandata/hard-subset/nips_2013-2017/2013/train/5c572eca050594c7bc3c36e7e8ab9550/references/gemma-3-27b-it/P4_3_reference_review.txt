This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by recasting it as an inverse problem solvable via Fredholm integral equations and regularization techniques from Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The approach yields two algorithms, FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), offering flexibility in terms of kernels, norms, and regularization. The paper provides a theoretical analysis including concentration and error bounds for Gaussian kernels, and proposes a novel unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV). Extensive experimental validation on several datasets demonstrates competitive performance compared to existing methods like KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE.

The core idea of reformulating density ratio estimation as an inverse problem is intriguing and represents a non-trivial connection to classical operator theory. The resulting algorithms, leveraging the representer theorem, are straightforward to implement. The theoretical analysis, providing convergence rates and error bounds, adds substantial value and differentiates this work from purely empirical approaches. The proposed CD-CV method is a practical contribution, addressing the often-challenging problem of parameter selection in unsupervised learning. The comparison with established methods highlights the efficacy and, in some cases, the improved computational efficiency of FIRE.

However, the paper’s extensive reliance on existing kernel methods, while a strength in terms of implementation simplicity, also limits its originality to a degree. While the *application* of Fredholm equations to this problem is novel, the underlying tools are well-established within the kernel learning community. The theoretical analysis, though solid, is currently limited to the Gaussian kernel and Euclidean/manifold settings. Expanding these bounds to other kernels or more complex data distributions would significantly enhance the work. Furthermore, the experimental section is lengthy and presents many results, but the significance of some improvements appears marginal.  The paper would benefit from a more focused discussion of the scenarios where FIRE demonstrably outperforms other methods.  The presentation also suffers from occasional redundancy (e.g., repeated explanations of the core idea) and could benefit from more concise writing. The connection between the two FIRE formulations (Type I & II) and their respective advantages isn't always clearly articulated. Finally, the discussion of related work, while comprehensive, could benefit from a more critical assessment of the limitations of existing methods, explicitly positioning FIRE as addressing specific shortcomings.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is a novel perspective.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Rigorous theoretical analysis, including convergence rates and error bounds, supports the proposed approach.
*   **Practicality:** The algorithms are straightforward to implement and computationally efficient.
*   **Experimental Validation:** Comprehensive experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on several datasets.
*   **Unsupervised Model Selection:** The CD-CV method provides a valuable unsupervised approach to parameter tuning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Incremental Originality:**  The approach builds heavily on existing kernel methods, limiting the overall novelty.
*   **Limited Scope of Theoretical Results:**  The theoretical analysis is currently restricted to Gaussian kernels and specific data settings.
*   **Verbose Presentation:** The paper could benefit from more concise writing and a more focused discussion of results.
*   **Marginal Improvements:** Some experimental results show only minor improvements over existing methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-researched paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation.  While the originality isn't groundbreaking, the novel formulation, rigorous theoretical analysis, practical algorithms, and comprehensive experimental validation justify acceptance. The paper is clearly written, although it could benefit from some streamlining. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of both the problem domain and the relevant theoretical tools. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, focusing on conciseness and potentially expanding the theoretical analysis.
