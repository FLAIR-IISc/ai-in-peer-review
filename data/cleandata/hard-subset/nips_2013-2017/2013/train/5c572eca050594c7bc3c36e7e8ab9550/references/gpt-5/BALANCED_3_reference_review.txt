Summary
The paper proposes to estimate density ratios r(x) = q(x)/p(x) by casting the problem as an inverse problem for a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two formulations are presented:

- Type I: use the exact identity Kp r = Kq 1, where Kρ is an integral operator with kernel k and measure ρ; solve for r ∈ H with squared L2(p) loss plus RKHS penalty. This setting assumes samples from both p and q.

- Type II: use an approximate identity Kt,p r ≈ q with a δ-like kernel kt (e.g., Gaussian with bandwidth t), yielding a biased but direct regression of q against Kt,p r; this applies when q is known at sample points from p, even if q is not a density.

Closed-form solutions follow from the representer theorem, leading to simple kernel algorithms (FIRE). The authors analyze estimation error for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean domains and manifolds, providing rates in terms of t, λ, n, and m. They also introduce CD-CV, an unsupervised model selection procedure that validates r by checking importance sampling equalities over random test functions u, and present experiments comparing FIRE to LSIF, Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), and a thresholded ratio of KDEs.

Relation to prior work: The approach connects to operator-theoretic views of learning and inverse problems (e.g., De Vito et al.; spectral regularization), and to density ratio estimation methods such as KMM (matching RKHS means) and LSIF/KLIEP (minimizing L2(p) or KL divergences). The Type I formulation can be seen as a regularized variant of moment matching in H, while Type II is a novel approximate-identity route. The CD-CV criterion relates to moment-matching/MMD-style discrepancies used in two-sample testing and importance-weighted cross-validation.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: recasting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem builds a bridge to classical operator theory and yields a unifying view of kernel-based ratio estimation.
- Practical algorithms: closed-form solutions via kernel matrices are simple to implement; both L2(p) and mixed L2 losses are considered, and the method works directly in function space (not only on sample weights).
- Theoretical analysis: non-asymptotic bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels are provided, including manifold-supported distributions; the decomposition of bias (t, approximate identity) and variance (sampling, regularization) is clear.
- Unsupervised model selection: CD-CV is a sensible, task-agnostic way to choose t and λ when labels are unavailable, leveraging the importance sampling identity.

Weaknesses
- Empirical evaluation is limited: comparisons to KMM are omitted in key settings, and head-to-head with established methods (e.g., uLSIF, IWCV) on standard covariate shift benchmarks is sparse. Claims of improved computational efficiency are not quantified.
- Positivity and normalization: FIRE does not enforce r ≥ 0 (or Ep[r]=1 when appropriate), which can matter in practice; competing methods often include these constraints.
- Clarity/notation: the distinction among k, kt, kH and the multiple uses of K for operators and Gram matrices is sometimes confusing. Some algorithmic choices in experiments (e.g., not comparing KMM in downstream tasks where only training weights are needed) deserve clearer justification.
- CD-CV novelty vs. existing criteria: the proposed validation resembles importance-weighted cross-validation (IWCV) and MMD-based discrepancies. The relationship and differences could be articulated and empirically compared.

Quality
- The optimization formulations and closed-form solutions follow standard RKHS arguments and appear sound. The Type II bias term and its control via t are properly acknowledged.
- The bounds look plausible for ill-posed inverse problems; however, they are derived under using the same Gaussian kernel for the operator and RKHS, whereas experiments use more general pairings—discussion of robustness to kernel mismatch would help.
- Some rate exponents (e.g., λ−1/6, n−1/(3.5+d/2)) are unusual; a brief intuition and comparison to known minimax rates for likelihood/density ratio estimation (e.g., Nguyen et al., 2007; Sugiyama et al., 2012) would strengthen the theory section.

Clarity
- Overall well-written, but sections 2–3 could benefit from a streamlined notation table, separating operators (calligraphic K), kernels (k, kt), and Gram matrices (bold K), and from explicit algorithm pseudocode with complexity.
- The experimental setup (resampling schemes, parameter grids) is described, but effect sizes and runtime/memory are not reported.

Originality
- The Fredholm-equation framing and the Type II approximate-identity route are novel and interesting. The Type I formulation is closely related to KMM but differs in using Tikhonov regularization to recover a function in H with an out-of-sample extension.
- CD-CV is a reasonable twist on existing unsupervised selection; its connection to MMD/moment-matching should be made explicit.

Significance
- Density ratio estimation is central in covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, and Monte Carlo methods. A principled inverse-problem perspective with theory and practical algorithms is valuable.
- The current experimental evidence is promising but not yet compelling; broader evaluations and ablations would help establish impact.

Specific suggestions
- Compare against KMM in settings where only training-sample weights are needed (regression/classification under covariate shift), and include uLSIF and IWCV baselines.
- Enforce non-negativity (and optionally normalization) via simple post-processing (clipping) or constrained optimization; report the impact.
- Discuss and, if possible, test robustness to kernel mismatch between k (integral operator) and kH (regularizer).
- Clarify CD-CV’s relation to MMD: if U is drawn from an RKHS ball (e.g., random Fourier features), JCD approximates an MMD-like discrepancy; consider using that for stronger validation.
- Provide runtime/complexity comparisons and scalability strategies (e.g., Nyström, random features).
- Include a Type II experiment where q is known up to a normalizing constant (e.g., MCMC/AIS), to demonstrate that use case.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, principled formulation unifying density ratio estimation and inverse problems.
- Solid theoretical analysis including manifold cases.
- Simple, closed-form kernel algorithms with out-of-sample functions.
- Practical unsupervised model selection idea.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited empirical comparisons; missing strong baselines in key settings.
- No constraints to ensure a valid ratio; potential practical instability.
- Some clarity and notation issues; theory focused on a special kernel configuration.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a compelling and well-founded perspective with clear potential; with stronger empirical validation and minor clarifications, it would make a valuable contribution.
