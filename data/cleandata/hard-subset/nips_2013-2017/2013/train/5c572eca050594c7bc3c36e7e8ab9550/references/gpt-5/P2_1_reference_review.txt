Summary and relation to prior work
The paper reframes density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solves it by Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two settings are proposed: Type I, where both p and q are densities with samples from each, leading to Kp r ≈ Kq 1; and Type II, where q is known (not necessarily a density) on sample points from p and one uses a “δ-like” kernel kt to obtain Kt,p r ≈ q. By applying representer theorems, the resulting estimators (FIRE) have closed-form linear-algebraic solutions with out-of-sample evaluation. The authors analyze concentration and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and on compact manifolds, and introduce an unsupervised model-selection criterion (CD-CV) based on the importance-sampling identity, using random linear and half-space test functions. Experiments compare FIRE to LSIF, KMM (partially), and a thresholded ratio of KDEs.

This connects naturally to prior work on importance weighting under covariate shift (Shimodaira; Gretton et al. KMM; Sugiyama et al. LSIF/KLIEP), inverse problems and RKHS regularization (Smola & Schölkopf; De Vito et al.), and recent theory for ratio estimation (Nguyen et al.). The authors correctly note that KMM corresponds to an RKHS identity version of the same Fredholm equation but lacks an explicit out-of-sample function and regularization; LSIF minimizes L2,p error over a linear model. The operator-theoretic perspective is a clean unification.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The variational formulations are standard Tikhonov problems; the empirical algorithms follow from the representer theorem and are stated with explicit linear systems. The theoretical results provide nonasymptotic bounds and rates, including manifold support. Assumptions (bounded densities, Sobolev smoothness of r and its powers) are clearly stated. The dependence on kernel bandwidth t and regularization λ is explicit.
- Limitations in analysis: (i) The derived rates (e.g., n−1/(3.5+d/2) in Type I) appear slower than known minimax rates in related settings and are not compared to lower bounds (e.g., Nguyen et al., 2007). It would help to discuss whether conservatism comes from controlling both operator estimation and inverse stability, and whether faster rates are attainable under stronger assumptions (e.g., source conditions, eigenvalue decay). (ii) The theory largely treats the case where the same Gaussian kernel is used for operator and RKHS; the algorithms allow different kernels—clarify how the guarantees extend. (iii) CD-CV, while intuitively compelling, lacks a statistical justification (e.g., that minimizing JCD over a sufficiently rich test class correlates with L2,p error of r), and guidance on choosing the test function family and its size.
- Experiments: The CD-CV idea is validated on USPS and downstream tasks (weighted regression and classification) on standard datasets. However: (i) There is no experiment for the Type II setting (where q is known at sample points), although that is a main selling point for MCMC-like applications. (ii) There is no synthetic low-dimensional setting with known ground-truth r(x) to directly measure L2 error and bias-variance tradeoffs with t and λ. (iii) KMM is omitted in some comparisons due to lack of out-of-sample extension; nevertheless, one can compare on held-out points via a simple kernel regression of KMM weights or by reporting training-point performance fairly across methods. (iv) Runtime and memory are not reported; matrix inversions scale as O(n^3), whereas LSIF and KMM often exploit sparsity/QP structure—please provide complexity and wall-clock comparisons, and consider Nyström/sketching for scalability.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a helpful overview of both settings and explicit algorithms. That said, the notation can be heavy and sometimes inconsistent:
- Use consistent symbols for kernels (k vs kH vs kt) and operators (Kp vs Kp,p vs K), and clarify products such as “K2KH” (is this K K KH?). A compact notation block and pseudocode for each variant would aid reproducibility.
- There are a few typographical issues (spacing in q/p, “TiknonovPhilips,” etc.).
- Be explicit about boundary cases and the role of γ in mixed norms, and provide practical guidance for choosing t and λ beyond the CD-CV grid.

Originality
The core idea—casting density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation and solving with RKHS Tikhonov—is a novel and useful synthesis. The link to KMM via operator identities is insightful, and the extension to “δ-kernel” approximations (Type II) is a fresh angle that broadens applicability beyond densities. The manifold-support analysis adds originality relative to most density-ratio papers. The CD-CV criterion is a pragmatic contribution to unsupervised model selection, distinct from importance-weighted CV.

Significance
Moderate to high. The operator-theoretic lens unifies and clarifies existing methods and yields simple, closed-form estimators with out-of-sample predictions. If widely adopted, FIRE could influence practice in covariate shift, off-policy evaluation, and MCMC scenarios where q is known but unnormalized. The theoretical bounds on manifolds are of independent interest. The lack of positivity/normalization constraints and limited empirical scope reduce immediate impact but are addressable.

Key weaknesses and suggestions
- Positivity/normalization: The estimator can output negative “weights” and does not enforce Ep[r]=1, which is critical for importance sampling and stable weighting. Consider adding constraints (nonnegativity and normalization) or a link function (e.g., r=exp g) with RKHS regularization, and report the practical effect (clipping vs unconstrained).
- Empirical coverage: Add (i) Type II experiments (e.g., Bayesian posterior ratios or AIS-like settings), (ii) a synthetic case with known r to report direct error, (iii) runtime/memory and scalability, and (iv) a fair KMM comparison on shared evaluation points or with an out-of-sample extension.
- Theory: Discuss optimality relative to known lower bounds; delineate when Type I vs Type II is preferable; provide at least a heuristic justification of CD-CV’s consistency.
- Clarity: Consolidate notation, provide algorithm pseudocode, and detail the effect of choosing k=kH versus k≠kH.

Pros
- Clean operator-theoretic formulation unifying several density-ratio estimators.
- Simple, closed-form RKHS algorithms with out-of-sample evaluation.
- Nonasymptotic bounds and rates, including manifold support.
- Practical unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV) with empirical validation.

Cons
- No positivity/normalization constraints; potential for negative weights.
- Rates appear conservative; limited discussion of optimality.
- Experiments miss Type II and ground-truth error; partial comparisons to KMM; no scalability analysis.
- Dense and occasionally inconsistent notation.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a principled and useful perspective with concrete algorithms and nontrivial theory. Strengthening empirical evaluation (especially Type II), addressing positivity/normalization, clarifying notation, and discussing rate optimality would make this a strong accept.
