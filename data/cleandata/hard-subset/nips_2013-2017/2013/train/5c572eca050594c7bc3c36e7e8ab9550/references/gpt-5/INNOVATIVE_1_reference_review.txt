Summary
This paper reframes density ratio estimation r(x)=q(x)/p(x) as an inverse problem for integral operators, yielding Fredholm equations of the first kind. Two settings are studied: Type I uses the identity Kp r = Kq 1, estimable from samples of p and q, and Type II uses a δ-kernel identity Kt,p r ≈ q that applies when q is known (not necessarily a density) at sample points from p. Both are solved via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, leading to closed-form kernel solutions (FIRE). The operator-theoretic formulation enables spectral analysis; the paper provides finite-sample error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean space and on smooth manifolds. An unsupervised model-selection procedure (CD-CV) based on test-function moment matching is proposed. Experiments compare FIRE to KMM, LSIF, and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline on several datasets, and show competitive performance and computational simplicity.

Relation to prior work
The approach connects to KMM, which imposes Eq(Φ)=Ep(βΦ) in an RKHS; here that equality is cast as a Fredholm equation, and solved with explicit ridge-type operators (and optional RKHS loss), rather than a constrained QP. LSIF/KLIEP estimate r by minimizing L2,p or KL divergences in parameterized families; FIRE uses operator equations to avoid basis approximation, with representer-theorem expansions over p-samples. The use of δ-kernel approximations and heat-kernel operators is standard in nonparametric analysis, but applying it to ratio estimation (Type II) is interesting. The paper also builds on inverse problem regularization (Engl et al.) and learning-as-inverse-problem literature (De Vito et al.). The manifold setting and Gaussian-kernel analysis relate to diffusion operators and manifold regularization.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is a clean, unifying view that ties importance weighting to operator equations. This is a bold and appealing perspective with potential to organize a family of methods.
- Simple, explicit algorithms: Closed-form kernel solutions with standard linear algebra; flexible loss choices (L2,p, L2,q, mixed, RKHS) and clear out-of-sample forms.
- Type II setting: Practical when q is known at p-sample points (e.g., unnormalized targets), extending beyond density ratios per se; addresses a regime under-served by KMM/LSIF.
- Theory: Concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings; explicit dependence on t, λ, n, m, with discussion of approximation bias from δ-kernels.
- Unsupervised model selection (CD-CV): Pragmatic and well-motivated via test-function moment matching; decouples basis choice from estimator.
- Empirical evidence across tasks (ratio validation, regression, classification), with favorable performance and stability compared to baselines.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Novelty vs. existing work: While the Fredholm framing is elegant, the Type I RKHS-loss formulation is very close to regularized KMM in operator form. Please better delineate what FIRE enables that KMM/KuLSIF/uLSIF cannot, beyond different optimization and explicit out-of-sample expressions.
- Constraints: The estimator does not enforce nonnegativity or normalization constraints (Ep[f]=1) common in KMM/LSIF. This may yield negative or ill-behaved weights in practice. Can simple convex constraints be incorporated without losing closed forms, or do you observe issues empirically?
- Rates and sharpness: The derived rates (e.g., n^(-1/(3.5 + d/2))) are unusual and appear weaker than known minimax rates for likelihood-ratio estimation under smoothness assumptions. A discussion comparing to Nguyen et al. (NIPS’07) and Sugiyama’s uLSIF/RuLSIF theory would help position the contributions.
- δ-kernel bias: Type II introduces O(t) (or t^(1−ε)) bias via Kt approximations. How sensitive is FIRE to the t–λ trade-off in practice? Any adaptive t selection beyond grid search? Please add experiments illustrating the bias-variance curve.
- Experimental scope: Datasets are modest; covariate shift scenarios are synthetic (PCA resampling). It would strengthen the paper to include:
  - High-dimensional settings and stress tests for scalability (n≥10^4).
  - A scenario where q is known up to a constant (e.g., AIS/logistic regression under shift), to highlight Type II’s value.
  - Direct comparisons with uLSIF/RuLSIF/KuLSIF and KLIEP, including runtime and stability.
- Fairness of KMM comparison: The statement that KMM lacks an out-of-sample extension is partly context-dependent; for covariate shift, weights on training samples suffice for reweighting, and OOS is often unnecessary. Please nuance this critique.
- Clarity: Notation is dense and sometimes overloaded (K, KH, Kt), and some matrices (e.g., K2KH) appear without explicit operator ordering. A concise algorithm box with input/output/complexity and a consistent notation table would help. Spell out assumptions in theorems (Sobolev order, boundary conditions) and provide brief proof sketches to orient readers.
- Computational aspects: Complexity is cubic in the number of p-samples due to kernel matrix inversions. Any possibility of Nyström/sketching/preconditioned solvers? Memory costs for mixed-loss (γL2,p+(1−γ)L2,q) also deserve discussion.

Quality
Technically solid overall; the operator-based derivations and empirical risk surrogates are sound, and the theoretical analysis is careful within its assumptions. Empirical evaluation supports claims but could be broadened and benchmarked against more recent ratio estimators.

Clarity
Generally clear but heavy notation and some missing details hinder readability. Improving exposition and adding algorithmic summaries would help reproducibility.

Originality
High on the conceptual framing (inverse Fredholm equation for density ratios) and on integrating δ-kernel approximations into a practical ratio estimator. Some overlap with prior RKHS inverse problem views and KMM-family methods remains.

Significance
Potentially significant: provides a unifying operator-theoretic lens with simple algorithms and theory, including manifold settings and an unsupervised model selection strategy. Likely to interest researchers in covariate shift, kernel methods, and inverse problems.

Pros
- Bold, unifying formulation (Fredholm inverse problem) with practical kernel algorithms.
- Theoretical guarantees in Euclidean/manifold cases.
- Useful unsupervised parameter selection (CD-CV).
- Empirically competitive and simple to implement.

Cons
- Overlap with KMM/uLSIF lines; need clearer differentiation and broader comparisons.
- No positivity/normalization constraints; potential practical issues.
- Rates may be suboptimal; limited discussion versus known theory.
- Scalability and high-dimensional performance not demonstrated.

Recommendation: Accept (7/10). I value the conceptual novelty and the clean operator-theoretic derivation, which outweigh the current experimental and comparative limitations. With improved positioning and additional experiments, this can be an influential contribution.

Confidence: 3/5.
