- Proposes reformulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation solved with Tikhonov regularization in RKHS (FIRE algorithm).
- Introduces two settings: Type I (samples from both p and q) and Type II (q known at points sampled from p).
- Provides theoretical convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, with explicit sampling and regularization error terms.
- Introduces CD-CV, a parameter-selection method for unsupervised density ratio estimation.
- Connects and clarifies relationships to existing methods like KMM, showing KMM as a special case lacking out-of-sample extension.
- Theoretical novelty: casts density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem, offering unified operator-theoretic insights.
- Comprehensive analysis: rigorous Theorems 1–4 with error bounds that account for sampling and regularization.
- Practical contribution: CD-CV and experiments show advantages over TIKDE and competitive performance with LSIF.
- Well-written and clearly organized, with lucid comparisons to related work.
- Limited experimental comparison: omits recent methods such as KLIEP and advanced MMD-based estimators.
- Strong theoretical assumptions (e.g., Sobolev-space conditions on q/p) may not hold in practice and are not discussed in depth.
- Type II has inherent O(t) (or O(t^{1-ε}) on manifolds) approximation error and lacks guidance on selecting bandwidth t to balance errors.
- Scalability concerns: kernel matrix inversion is O(n^3), and large-scale computational costs and runtime comparisons are not addressed.
- Ambiguous practical guidance: unclear how to choose test functions U for CD-CV beyond random linear functions used in experiments.
- Could better engage with prior theoretical work on KMM (e.g., Yu & Szepesvári 2009 and [26]) regarding convergence rates.
- Significant and original: novel Fredholm formulation and manifold analysis offer valuable theoretical and conceptual contributions.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to expand experimental comparisons, address scalability, and provide practical guidance for Type II and CD-CV.
