

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel perspective on density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, connecting it to classical inverse problem theory. The authors develop two types of formulations (Type I and Type II) for estimating q(x)/p(x), where p is given by samples and q is either known or given by samples. They solve these using Tikhonov regularization with RKHS norms, leading to the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) algorithms. The paper provides theoretical error bounds for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and manifolds, and proposes CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation) for unsupervised parameter selection.

The work builds upon recent NIPS contributions on density ratio estimation (e.g., [9, 10, 23]) while offering a fundamentally different theoretical perspective. It connects to the broader literature on kernel methods and regularization [21, 17, 16], but positions itself as a novel application of inverse problem theory to density estimation. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from Kernel Mean Matching [9] by using a different optimization framework that provides better out-of-sample extension properties, and from LSIF [10] by using a different theoretical foundation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Theoretical Framework:** The paper's core contribution is the reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation, establishing a new connection between classical operator theory and modern machine learning. This perspective is novel and well-motivated, offering a fresh way to view an important problem.

2. **Comprehensive Theoretical Analysis:** The authors provide detailed error bounds and convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings in Euclidean spaces and manifolds. The analysis extends beyond the standard Euclidean case to sub-manifolds, which is a significant technical contribution. The bounds are carefully derived and appropriately contextualized within the literature.

3. **Practical Algorithms:** The FIRE algorithms are explicitly derived using the representer theorem, resulting in simple and implementable methods. The CD-CV model selection technique is particularly valuable as it addresses a key challenge in unsupervised learning without requiring labeled data.

4. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate clear performance advantages over KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE across multiple datasets and settings. The use of multiple error measures (linear, half-space, kernel-based functions) provides a comprehensive evaluation of the method's robustness.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work:** While the paper references [14, 26] as related theoretical work, it does not sufficiently compare with more recent density ratio estimation methods published since these works. The paper could better position itself within the most current state-of-the-art.

2. **Practical Significance of Theoretical Results:** The theoretical error bounds are well-developed but the paper does not clearly demonstrate how these bounds guide practical parameter selection. The CD-CV method is introduced as a practical solution, but the theoretical analysis does not directly inform this choice.

3. **Experimental Scope:** The experiments focus on a limited set of datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS). More diverse datasets, especially those with complex manifold structures, would better demonstrate the advantages of the manifold analysis.

4. **Computational Complexity Analysis:** The paper does not provide a thorough comparison of computational complexity between FIRE and competing methods, which would be valuable for practitioners.

**Significance Assessment:**

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by connecting density ratio estimation to classical inverse problem theory, which has not been done before. This framework provides a principled way to approach density ratio estimation that is both mathematically rigorous and practically implementable. The proposed algorithms show strong empirical performance, and the CD-CV method addresses a key practical challenge in unsupervised learning. The theoretical results for manifold settings are particularly valuable as they extend the applicability of density ratio estimation to more complex data structures.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically sound paper with a novel theoretical perspective that advances the field of density ratio estimation. The theoretical analysis is comprehensive and well-executed, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The paper is well-written with clear mathematical development and good organization.

The main limitations are the limited comparison with the very latest methods in the field and the somewhat narrow scope of the experimental validation. However, these do not significantly undermine the paper's strong technical contribution and theoretical novelty.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Pro-Acceptance Arguments:**
- Novel theoretical framework connecting density ratio estimation to Fredholm equations
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with error bounds for Euclidean and manifold settings
- Practical algorithms with explicit implementation details
- Strong empirical results demonstrating clear advantages over competitors
- Novel unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV)

**Con-Acceptance Arguments:**
- Limited comparison with the most recent density ratio estimation methods
- Theoretical analysis does not clearly inform practical parameter selection
- Experimental validation could be expanded to more diverse datasets

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by establishing a new theoretical foundation for density ratio estimation that is both mathematically rigorous and practically useful. The results advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way and are likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners working on transfer learning, covariate shift, and related problems.
