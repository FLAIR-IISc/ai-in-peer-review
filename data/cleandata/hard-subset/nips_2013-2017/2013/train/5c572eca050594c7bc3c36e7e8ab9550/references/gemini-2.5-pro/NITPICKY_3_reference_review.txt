### Summary of the Paper
This paper formulates the problem of density ratio estimation, q(x)/p(x), as a classical inverse problem. The authors reframe the estimation task as solving a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two primary settings are proposed: Type I, where samples from both distributions p and q are available, and Type II, where samples from p are available along with the values of q at those sample points. The solution approach relies on Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework, leading to a family of algorithms collectively named FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). The paper provides closed-form solutions for the resulting optimization problems. A theoretical analysis is presented, establishing error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds. Finally, the authors introduce a cross-validation procedure, CD-CV, for unsupervised hyperparameter selection and present experimental results comparing FIRE to methods like LSIF and a kernel density-based baseline.

### Quality
The paper's technical quality has several significant issues that prevent a confident assessment of its soundness.

**Major Concerns:**
The derivations of the core algorithms in Section 2 appear to be incorrect or at least inadequately explained. For the Type I algorithm with an L2,p norm (Eq. 7), the provided closed-form solution for the coefficient vector `v` (Eq. 8) does not seem to follow from a standard derivation for the stated optimization problem. Minimizing the objective in Eq. 7, which is a generalized kernel ridge regression problem, leads to a more complex "sandwich" estimator of the form `(A^T A + λ K_H)^{-1} A^T y`, where `A` involves a product of kernel matrices. The paper's solution `(K^2_{p,p} K_H + nλI)^{-1} ...` is much simpler and likely arises from unstated assumptions or an error in the derivation. This is a critical flaw that casts doubt on the validity of the algorithms that were implemented and tested.

**Minor Concerns:**
1.  The theoretical analysis, while a welcome addition, reports convergence rates (e.g., `n^{-1/(3.5+d/2)}`) that are quite slow, especially in higher dimensions. The paper does not provide any discussion contextualizing these rates. Are they optimal for this class of ill-posed problems? How do they compare to rates for other methods like KMM or LSIF? Without this context, the value of the theoretical contribution is diminished.
2.  The assumptions for the theory, such as `q/p` belonging to the Sobolev space `W^2_2`, are strong and their implications are not discussed.
3.  The experimental results in Table 1 lack error bars or any measure of statistical significance (e.g., standard deviations over the 5 trials). This makes it impossible to determine if the observed performance differences between methods are meaningful or simply due to random variation.

### Clarity
The paper's clarity is hampered by imprecise notation, organizational choices, and poor formatting.

1.  **Notation:** The notation is often dense and can be ambiguous. For example, `Kq1(x)` is used to denote the function resulting from applying the integral operator `Kq` to the constant function `1`. While defined, this is not standard and requires the reader to constantly track custom definitions. The expression `‖Kpf−Kq1(x)‖^2_{L2,p}` in the introduction is syntactically unusual, with the variable `(x)` appearing inside the norm.
2.  **Organization and Flow:** The derivation of the algorithms in Section 2 is presented too hastily. Given that these explicit formulas are a key contribution, a more detailed, step-by-step derivation is warranted to build confidence in their correctness.
3.  **Formatting:** The manuscript exhibits a lack of attention to detail.
    *   Footnotes are presented inline as part of the main text (e.g., footnote 1 and 2 in the introduction), which is non-standard for a formal publication and disrupts the reading flow.
    *   The bibliography is exceptionally sloppy. Many entries are missing publication years (e.g., [1], [2], [3], [9]), page numbers, or have inconsistent formatting. For a top-tier venue like NIPS, this level of carelessness is unacceptable.

### Originality
The core idea of framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation is elegant and provides a novel connection between a common machine learning problem and classical operator theory. This is the paper's main strength. While other methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) are based on a similar integral identity (as the authors correctly point out), the explicit formulation as an inverse problem to be solved via Tikhonov regularization is a distinct and valuable perspective. The proposed CD-CV method for parameter selection is a practical idea, though it builds on concepts similar to the objective functions used in LSIF and KLIEP.

### Significance
The significance of the work is potentially high. The proposed framework could unify several existing approaches and open up new avenues for theoretical analysis by leveraging the rich literature on inverse problems. A simple, robust, and theoretically-grounded algorithm for density ratio estimation would be of great interest to researchers in transfer learning, causal inference, and reinforcement learning.

However, the potential significance is severely undermined by the paper's flaws. The questionable correctness of the main algorithms, the lack of a convincing experimental comparison, and the overall poor presentation prevent the current manuscript from realizing this potential. The experimental evaluation is particularly weak in its significance because it omits a direct comparison with KMM, which the authors themselves identify as the most closely related method. The justification provided (difficulty of out-of-sample extension) is not a strong enough reason to exclude such a critical baseline.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is novel, principled, and intellectually appealing.
2.  The paper provides a theoretical analysis with explicit convergence rates, which adds a level of rigor often missing in more heuristic approaches.
3.  The framework is flexible and encompasses several variants (different norms, Type I/II settings).

### Arguments for Rejection:
1.  **Major Technical Flaw:** There is a strong reason to believe the closed-form solutions for the proposed FIRE algorithms (e.g., Eq. 8) are incorrect as they do not appear to follow from the stated optimization problems. This is a fatal flaw.
2.  **Weak Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments lack a comparison to the most relevant baseline (KMM) and do not report measures of statistical significance (e.g., error bars), making the results difficult to interpret.
3.  **Poor Presentation and Lack of Care:** The paper is marred by unclear notation, non-standard formatting, and a very poorly prepared reference list. This accumulation of minor flaws suggests a rushed and careless preparation, which does not meet the high standards of NIPS.

### Recommendation: Reject
While the core idea is interesting and potentially significant, the execution is critically flawed. The likely error in the derivation of the main algorithms is a disqualifying issue on its own. Combined with the weak experimental evaluation and the numerous presentation errors, the paper is not ready for publication. I would encourage the authors to carefully re-derive their algorithms, perform a more thorough and convincing experimental comparison (including KMM), and meticulously proofread and format their manuscript before resubmission.
