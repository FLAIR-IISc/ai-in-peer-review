

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using regularization techniques within Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors develop a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) that address both Type I (when samples from both distributions are available) and Type II (when only function values of q are known) settings. The paper makes several significant contributions: a theoretical connection to classical inverse problems, comprehensive error analysis for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, and a novel unsupervised model selection technique called CD-CV.

The paper builds upon and connects to several relevant works, particularly Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10]. The authors correctly note that KMM can be viewed within their Type I framework but with a different optimization procedure, which provides valuable context for their work. The connection to classical operator theory and spectral analysis represents a meaningful theoretical contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical Novelty and Soundness:** The paper's core contribution—framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem—is conceptually fresh and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis is thorough, providing error bounds and convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold settings, which is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of density ratio estimation.

2. **Algorithmic Simplicity and Flexibility:** The FIRE algorithms are straightforward to implement and generalize to different norms (L2,p, L2,q, and RKHS), offering flexibility that existing methods lack. The derivation of explicit solutions using the representer theorem is well-executed.

3. **Practical Contribution:** The CD-CV method for model selection is particularly valuable as it addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning. The experimental results demonstrate that FIRE outperforms KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE on several benchmarks, with strong computational efficiency.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Practical Motivation Gap:** While the paper demonstrates strong theoretical and empirical results, it doesn't sufficiently articulate *why* this approach would be preferred in real-world applications compared to existing methods. The Type II setting's error term (O(t)) and its practical implications for kernel selection need clearer discussion.

2. **Limited Theoretical Scope:** The theoretical analysis is restricted to Gaussian kernels, which limits the generalizability of the convergence rates. The paper could better discuss how their framework might extend to other kernel families.

3. **Experimental Comparison Depth:** The experiments are well-executed but could be strengthened by including more challenging datasets or exploring the performance of FIRE under more severe covariate shift scenarios. The comparison with KMM is limited due to out-of-sample extension considerations, which might be addressed more thoroughly.

4. **Related Work Context:** The paper could better distinguish itself from other recent RKHS-based approaches to density ratio estimation (e.g., [26] on KMM analysis) by more explicitly highlighting the theoretical and practical differences.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a high-quality paper with a strong theoretical foundation, clear algorithms, and promising experimental results. The connection to classical inverse problems provides a valuable new perspective on density ratio estimation, and the CD-CV method is a significant practical contribution. The theoretical analysis is thorough and addresses important settings (Euclidean and manifold domains).

**Recommendation:**
Accept. This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of density ratio estimation by connecting it to classical inverse problems in operator theory. The theoretical guarantees, algorithmic simplicity, and empirical performance demonstrate clear significance for the NIPS community.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. In the introduction and related work, more clearly articulate the practical advantages of the FIRE framework over existing methods (beyond just empirical performance).
2. Expand the discussion of the Type II setting's error term (O(t)) and its practical implications for kernel selection in real applications.
3. Consider briefly discussing how the theoretical results might extend to other kernel families beyond Gaussian.
4. Add a few more challenging experimental scenarios to better demonstrate the robustness of FIRE under severe covariate shift.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a valuable contribution to the field of density ratio estimation, which is fundamental to many transfer learning and importance sampling applications. The authors have provided a novel perspective that connects kernel methods to classical operator theory, offering both theoretical insights and practical algorithms.
