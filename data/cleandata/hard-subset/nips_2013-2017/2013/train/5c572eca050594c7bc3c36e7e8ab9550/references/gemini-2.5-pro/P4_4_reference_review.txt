**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio `q(x)/p(x)`, a fundamental task in areas such as importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation. The central contribution is a novel reformulation of this problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, thereby connecting it to the classical theory of inverse problems. The authors derive two main formulations. "Type I" is based on the identity `∫ k(x, y) (q(y)/p(y)) p(y) dy = ∫ k(x, y) q(y) dy`, which is applicable when samples from both distributions `p` and `q` are available. "Type II" uses the approximation `∫ kt(x, y) (q(x)/p(x)) p(x) dx ≈ q(y)` for a delta-like kernel `kt`, suitable when values of `q(x)` are known at points sampled from `p`.

To solve these ill-posed integral equations, the paper proposes using Tikhonov regularization with a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) norm as the penalty term. This approach, named Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE), leads to simple, explicit, and easily implementable algorithms whose solutions are linear combinations of kernel functions centered on the data points. The framework is flexible, accommodating various loss functions (L2,p, a mix of L2,p and L2,q, and the RKHS norm).

The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis for the Gaussian kernel case, establishing error bounds and convergence rates for both Euclidean and manifold data settings. A significant practical contribution is the proposal of an unsupervised model selection technique, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), which leverages the importance sampling identity to tune hyperparameters without labeled data. The work is positioned in relation to existing methods, noting that Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) can be viewed as a special, unregularized case within the FIRE framework. Experimental results on several datasets demonstrate the proposed method's strong performance and computational efficiency compared to LSIF, KMM, and a kernel density-based baseline.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:** The mathematical foundation, which grounds density ratio estimation in the well-established theory of Fredholm integral equations and Tikhonov regularization, is solid and elegant. The derivations of the algorithms from the optimization problems are sound and follow standard procedures in kernel methods. The paper is a complete piece of work, providing a novel framework, concrete algorithms, a detailed theoretical analysis with convergence rates (Theorems 1 & 3), and a practical solution for model selection.
*   **Weaknesses:** The theoretical analysis relies on assumptions that `q/p` and its square belong to a Sobolev space. A brief discussion on the practical implications of these assumptions would be beneficial—for instance, when might they be violated in practice (e.g., by very sharp or discontinuous ratios) and what might be the consequences?

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
*   **Strengths:** The introduction clearly articulates the problem, the core idea, and the paper's contributions. The distinction between the Type I and Type II settings is well-motivated and easy to follow. The relationship to prior work, particularly KMM and LSIF, is explained clearly, helping to situate the contributions within the existing literature. The presentation of the final algorithms as closed-form solutions (e.g., Eq. 8) greatly enhances reproducibility.
*   **Weaknesses:** The description of the TIKDE (Thresholded Inverse Kernel Density Estimator) baseline in the experiments is brief. Clarifying how the crucial threshold parameter `α` was chosen would strengthen the empirical comparison.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel and insightful perspective on a well-studied problem.
*   **Strengths:** The primary contribution—formulating density ratio estimation as an inverse problem solvable via a Fredholm equation—is highly original. This reframing provides new theoretical and algorithmic tools for the problem. While the building blocks (RKHS, regularization) are familiar, their application in this context is novel. The proposed CD-CV method for unsupervised parameter selection is also a significant and novel contribution, addressing a key practical challenge in this area. The development of a unified framework that can accommodate different norms and settings is a testament to the originality of the underlying idea.
*   **Weaknesses:** It is correctly noted that the RKHS-norm variant of the FIRE algorithm is closely related to KMM, effectively serving as a regularized version. While this regularization is a valuable addition, this specific algorithm could be seen as an incremental improvement rather than a completely separate approach. However, this does not detract from the novelty of the overall Fredholm framework, which is much broader.

**Significance:** The paper's contributions are likely to be impactful for both researchers and practitioners.
*   **Strengths:** Density ratio estimation is a problem of great importance in machine learning. The proposed FIRE algorithms are simple to implement (requiring only standard kernel matrix manipulations and solving a linear system) and shown to be computationally efficient. These properties make them highly attractive for practical use. The CD-CV method for hyperparameter tuning is a particularly valuable contribution, as it provides a principled way to perform model selection in the common unsupervised setting of covariate shift. The theoretical framework itself may inspire similar operator-theoretic approaches to other estimation problems in machine learning.
*   **Weaknesses:** The experimental comparison, while solid, could be strengthened by including other relevant methods like KLIEP [23].

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides a novel, principled, and elegant formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation.
2.  The resulting FIRE algorithms are simple, computationally efficient, and have closed-form solutions.
3.  Includes a rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence rates on both Euclidean and manifold domains.
4.  Introduces CD-CV, a highly practical and novel method for unsupervised hyperparameter selection.
5.  The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
6.  Empirical results demonstrate strong performance against established methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  One variant of the proposed algorithm is a close, albeit regularized, relative of the existing KMM method.
2.  The practical implications of the theoretical assumptions could be discussed in more detail.
