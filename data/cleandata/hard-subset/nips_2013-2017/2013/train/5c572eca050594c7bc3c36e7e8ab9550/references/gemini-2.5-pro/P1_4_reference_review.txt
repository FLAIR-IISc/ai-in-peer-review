### Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

**Summary**

This paper presents a novel framework for estimating the density ratio (or importance function) $q(x)/p(x)$. The core idea is to re-frame this estimation problem as a classical inverse problem, specifically, solving a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors derive two main integral equations: one (Type I) that relates the density ratio to integral operators applied to samples from both $p$ and $q$, and another (Type II) that relates the ratio to an integral operator and the function $q$ itself.

Since Fredholm equations of the first kind are ill-posed, the authors propose solving them using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This approach, which they name FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), leads to simple, closed-form solutions that are easily implementable within the standard kernel methods paradigm. The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, establishing error bounds and convergence rates for the FIRE estimator using Gaussian kernels, both in Euclidean space and on manifolds. A significant practical contribution is the introduction of a fully unsupervised model selection technique called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) for choosing the kernel and regularization parameters. The authors conduct experiments on several datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of their method and the CD-CV procedure compared to alternatives like a thresholded kernel density estimator (TIKDE) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF).

The work connects to a significant body of recent literature on density ratio estimation, particularly in the context of covariate shift, such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF. The authors clearly position their work relative to these methods, arguing that their regularized Fredholm approach offers greater stability and a more principled framework than KMM, and a different use of test functions compared to LSIF.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is a major strength. It provides concentration and error bounds with explicit convergence rates, lending strong theoretical support to the proposed algorithms. The consideration of both Euclidean and manifold settings is comprehensive.
*   The claims are well-supported by both the theoretical derivations and the experimental results. The experiments, though not on massive datasets, are well-designed to test the different components of the contribution, especially the CD-CV model selection.
*   The authors are honest about the limitations and assumptions (e.g., the O(t) error in the Type II formulation).

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized.
*   The introduction clearly motivates the problem and lays out the core idea of using Fredholm equations. The progression from the integral equations to the regularized RKHS formulation and the final algorithms is logical and easy to follow.
*   The distinction between Type I and Type II settings is clear and highlights the flexibility of the framework for different problem scenarios (e.g., when sampling from $q$ is difficult but evaluating it is possible).
*   The derivation of the final algorithms (Eqs. 8, 10, etc.) from the optimization problems is standard for kernel methods, making it accessible to the NIPS audience.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions.
*   The primary conceptual contribution—formulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation—is novel and elegant. It provides a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem and connects it to a rich body of literature in classical analysis and inverse problems.
*   While Tikhonov regularization and RKHS methods are standard tools, their application in this specific framework to derive the FIRE algorithm is new.
*   The Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) technique for unsupervised hyperparameter selection is a clever and highly practical innovation. It addresses one of the most significant challenges in applying density ratio estimators in practice.
*   The paper clearly differentiates its approach from prior work. The relationship to KMM is particularly well-articulated, framing KMM as a special, unregularized case of the FIRE framework, which helps to contextualize both methods.

**Significance:** The results are significant and likely to be influential.
*   Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem with broad applications in covariate shift adaptation, transfer learning, and importance sampling. This paper provides a powerful, flexible, and theoretically-grounded new tool for practitioners and researchers.
*   The connection to inverse problems may open up new avenues for analysis and algorithm design, allowing tools from spectral theory and operator theory to be applied more directly.
*   The CD-CV method is a standalone contribution that could be adopted for tuning other density ratio estimators. Given the difficulty of unsupervised model selection, this is a valuable result.
*   The FIRE algorithm itself is simple to implement, relying only on standard kernel matrix operations, which increases its potential for widespread adoption.

**Areas for Improvement / Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Scalability:** The proposed algorithms require inverting an $n \times n$ matrix, where $n$ is the number of samples from $p$. This has a computational complexity of $O(n^3)$, which is prohibitive for large datasets. This is a major practical limitation that is not discussed in the paper. Could the authors comment on potential approximation techniques (e.g., Nyström method, random features) to scale FIRE to larger problems?
2.  **Experimental Comparisons:** The comparison with KMM is avoided in the experiments due to its lack of a natural out-of-sample extension. While this is a valid point, a comparison of the *regularized* version of KMM proposed by the authors (Eq. 10) with the other FIRE variants would have been insightful. This would help clarify the practical impact of choosing different loss functions (L2 vs. RKHS norm) within the same framework.
3.  **Sensitivity of CD-CV:** The performance of CD-CV relies on a set of test functions $\mathcal{U}$. The paper suggests random linear functions or half-spaces. How sensitive is the parameter selection to the choice of this function class? A brief discussion or an ablation study on this point would strengthen the paper.
4.  **Assumptions in Theory:** The theoretical analysis assumes the density ratio $q/p$ lies in a Sobolev space. It would be helpful to provide some intuition on when this assumption holds or fails in practice. For instance, what happens if the support of $p$ is not contained within the support of $q$?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and principled formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
*   Presents a family of simple, flexible, and effective algorithms (FIRE) with closed-form solutions.
*   Includes a rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence rates.
*   Introduces a highly practical and unsupervised method for model selection (CD-CV).
*   Clearly positions the work within the existing literature and improves upon related methods like KMM by introducing regularization in a principled way.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The computational complexity of $O(n^3)$ is a significant drawback for large-scale applications, and this limitation is not addressed.
*   The experimental evaluation is performed on relatively small datasets, which may not fully expose the practical challenges of the method.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes significant contributions on conceptual, theoretical, and practical fronts. The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is both novel and insightful. The resulting FIRE algorithms are elegant and backed by strong theoretical guarantees. The proposed CD-CV method for model selection is a key practical contribution that addresses a critical need. Despite the unaddressed issue of scalability, the strengths of this work far outweigh its weaknesses. The paper is well-written, the ideas are significant, and it is likely to have a lasting impact on the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
