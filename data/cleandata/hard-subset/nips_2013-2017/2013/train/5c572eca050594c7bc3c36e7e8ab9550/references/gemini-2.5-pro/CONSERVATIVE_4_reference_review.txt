**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of density ratio estimation, q(x)/p(x), a key task in areas such as covariate shift adaptation and importance sampling. The authors propose a novel framework by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two primary settings are considered: Type I, where samples from both distributions p and q are available, and Type II, where samples from p are available along with the values of the function q at those sample points. The resulting ill-posed inverse problems are solved using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This approach, termed Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE), yields simple, closed-form solutions. The paper provides a theoretical analysis with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels and introduces an unsupervised cross-validation procedure (CD-CV) for model selection. The method is experimentally compared against established baselines like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF).

**Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Soundness:** The primary strength of this paper is its principled approach. Casting density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem provides an elegant and well-understood mathematical foundation. The use of Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS is a standard and robust technique for dealing with such ill-posed problems, and its application here is methodologically sound.

2.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis, including concentration and error bounds, as well as convergence rates. Deriving these guarantees using spectral theory is a significant contribution that adds a level of rigor often missing in more heuristic approaches. This analysis helps to characterize the behavior of the proposed estimator.

3.  **Algorithmic Simplicity:** The proposed FIRE algorithms have simple, analytical solutions expressed in terms of kernel matrices. This is a practical advantage over methods like KMM, which require solving a constrained quadratic program, potentially leading to better computational efficiency and easier implementation.

4.  **Practical Considerations:** The paper addresses the crucial and often-overlooked problem of hyperparameter selection in an unsupervised setting by proposing the CD-CV method. While the method's novelty is debatable (as it is based on the same identity used by LSIF for fitting), its application to model selection is a useful practical contribution.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Formulation:** While the framing in terms of Fredholm equations is elegant, the underlying integral identity is not new. As the authors correctly point out, the equality `Eq(Φ(x)) = Ep((q/p)Φ(x))` is the foundation of the Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) algorithm. The proposed Type I method can therefore be seen as a direct, Tikhonov-regularized variant of KMM. The contribution is thus more of a thoughtful reformulation and application of a standard regularization technique to an existing idea, rather than a fundamentally new approach to the problem.

2.  **Insufficient Validation of Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method for hyperparameter selection is a new heuristic introduced in this paper. However, its effectiveness is not independently validated. The experiments use CD-CV to select parameters for the authors' own method and then compare its performance to baselines. This creates a potential for biased evaluation. A more convincing validation would involve demonstrating CD-CV's reliability on synthetic problems where the optimal parameters are known, or showing that it consistently selects better parameters than standard heuristics for a range of different algorithms, not just FIRE. Without this, it is difficult to disentangle the performance of the FIRE estimator from the performance of the CD-CV selector.

3.  **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental evaluation, while showing promising results, could be strengthened.
    *   The comparisons are made against a limited set of baselines. While KMM and LSIF are appropriate, other relevant methods like KLIEP [23] are mentioned but not included in the comparison.
    *   The baseline TIKDE (Thresholded Inverse Kernel Density Estimator) appears to be a weak competitor, and its performance relies heavily on a heuristically chosen threshold `α`. Strong performance against such a baseline is not particularly compelling.
    *   The results presented in Table 1 lack standard deviations or other measures of variance, making it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the reported improvements over the baselines. Given that the performance differences are in some cases modest, this is a notable omission.

4.  **Theoretical Discussion:** The derived convergence rates show a strong dependence on the input dimensionality `d`. This is a common feature of kernel methods but warrants a more detailed discussion regarding the practical implications for high-dimensional problems. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from comparing its derived rates to known minimax optimal rates for density ratio estimation (e.g., [14]) to better contextualize the theoretical contribution.

**Conclusion**

The paper presents a methodologically sound and well-analyzed approach to density ratio estimation. Its main contribution is the formalization of the problem within the established framework of Fredholm integral equations and Tikhonov regularization, leading to a simple and efficient algorithm. However, the novelty of the core idea is limited when compared to prior work like KMM. The experimental validation, particularly concerning the proposed model selection procedure, is not sufficiently rigorous to fully support the claims of superior performance. While this is a solid piece of work, its contribution is more incremental than transformative. I am cautiously leaning towards acceptance but would strongly urge the authors to strengthen the experimental validation and temper the claims of novelty in a revised version.
