**Summary of the Paper**

This paper recasts the problem of density ratio estimation, `q(x)/p(x)`, as a classical inverse problem. The authors formulate this as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two primary formulations are proposed: Type I, `Kp(q/p) = Kq(1)`, which is suitable when samples from both `p` and `q` are available; and Type II, `Kt,p(q/p) ≈ q`, which applies when `q` is a known function. These ill-posed integral equations are solved using Tikhonov regularization with a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) norm as the penalty term. This approach, termed Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator (FIRE), leads to a family of simple kernel-based algorithms with closed-form solutions. The paper provides a theoretical analysis with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds. Additionally, a practical, unsupervised model selection method, Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), is introduced. The work is positioned relative to existing methods like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF), arguing that the Fredholm perspective offers a more principled and flexible framework.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Formulation:** The central contribution—framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation—is elegant and conceptually appealing. It establishes a clear connection to the well-studied field of inverse problems, allowing the application of classical tools like Tikhonov regularization in a principled manner.

2.  **Flexibility and Simplicity:** The proposed framework is versatile, accommodating different loss functions (L2,p, mixed L2,p/L2,q, RKHS norm) and settings (Type I and Type II). The resulting FIRE algorithms are straightforward to implement, benefiting from closed-form solutions derived via the representer theorem.

3.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis, deriving concentration and error bounds as well as convergence rates for the proposed estimators. The analysis covers both Euclidean and manifold domains, which adds to its comprehensiveness.

4.  **Practical Model Selection:** The proposal of the CD-CV method for unsupervised hyperparameter selection is a valuable practical contribution, addressing a notoriously difficult problem in this setting.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is strong, the paper is undermined by a significant number of flaws ranging from minor presentational issues to a critical methodological gap.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript suffers from a lack of polish.
    *   **Typos:** There are distracting typos, most notably in the introduction where the target function is repeatedly written as `q(x)p(x)` instead of the correct `q(x)/p(x)`. This fundamentally misrepresents the problem being solved in the motivating sentences.
    *   **Ambiguous Notation:** The notation for kernel matrices and vectors in Section 2 is inconsistent and confusing. For instance, the distinction between matrices with and without the `1/n` scaling (`Kp,p` vs. `KH`) is not clearly handled, and the meaning of `1zq` in Equation (8) is not defined. The reader is forced to re-derive the equations to verify their correctness.
    *   **Anonymity Violation:** The acknowledgements section includes NSF grant numbers, which is a clear violation of the double-blind review policy for NIPS.
    *   **Underdeveloped Sections:** The experimental section is cursory. Table 1 is dense and poorly captioned; terms like "Xerr," "f cv," and "f err" are not defined. There is almost no qualitative discussion or interpretation of the experimental results.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:**
    *   **Justification of Type II Formulation:** The motivation for the Type II equation, `∫ kt(x, y) (q(x)/p(x)) p(x)dx ≈ q(y)`, appears to be flawed. The paper cites a standard result for kernel smoothers, `∫ kt(x, y)f(x)dx ≈ f(y)`. However, the integral in Equation (3) is `Ep[kt(x,y) * w(x)]` where `w(x)=q(x)/p(x)`. This approximates `w(y)p(y) = q(y)` only if the function `p(x)` is approximately constant in the neighborhood of `y`. This is a very strong and unstated assumption that severely limits the generality of the Type II approach. The validity of this formulation is questionable without a more rigorous justification.
    *   **Undiscussed Assumptions in Theory:** The theoretical analysis assumes that `q/p` and `(q/p)^2` belong to a Sobolev space. A necessary condition for this is that the density `p(x)` must be bounded away from zero on the support of `q(x)`. This is a critical assumption in density ratio estimation and a major practical limitation, yet it is not explicitly stated or discussed.

3.  **Evaluation:**
    *   **Lack of Context for Theoretical Rates:** The derived convergence rates are presented without comparison to established minimax optimal rates for this problem (e.g., from Nguyen et al. [14]). This makes it difficult to judge the tightness of the results and the theoretical quality of the estimator.
    *   **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The experiments omit a direct comparison with KMM in the main results table (Table 1), one of the most closely related methods. While the authors justify this based on KMM's lack of a natural out-of-sample extension, this omission weakens the empirical evaluation. An alternative comparison on an in-sample metric could have been provided.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and elegant perspective on density ratio estimation.
*   The resulting FIRE framework is flexible and produces simple, closed-form algorithms.
*   The paper includes a non-trivial theoretical analysis and a practical method for model selection.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The manuscript contains serious presentation flaws, including violations of the review policy, numerous typos, and unclear notation.
*   A core part of the methodology (the Type II formulation) rests on a questionable and unjustified approximation.
*   The theoretical analysis omits discussion of crucial underlying assumptions and fails to benchmark the derived rates against known optimal ones.
*   The experimental section is underdeveloped and provides an incomplete comparison to prior art.

**Decision:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the core idea of formulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is promising and intellectually appealing, the paper in its current form is not ready for publication. The combination of a critical methodological gap in the justification of the Type II method, the failure to discuss important theoretical assumptions, and numerous presentation issues, including a breach of anonymity, is too substantial to overlook. The work has the potential to be a strong contribution, but it requires a major revision to address these fundamental problems.
