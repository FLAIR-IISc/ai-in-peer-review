

# Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors introduce two types of formulations (Type I and Type II) for different settings, derive algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), and provide theoretical convergence rates for Gaussian kernels. They also propose a cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) method for parameter selection and evaluate their approach on several datasets.

## Strengths
The paper makes a theoretically sound connection between density ratio estimation and classical inverse problems in integral equations. The framework is well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis for Gaussian kernels is comprehensive. The FIRE algorithms are well-defined and appear to be computationally efficient. The CD-CV approach for parameter selection is interesting and potentially useful for unsupervised density ratio estimation.

## Weaknesses
Despite the paper's theoretical contributions, I must recommend rejection due to numerous, consistent, and fundamental notational errors that undermine the paper's credibility and clarity.

### Critical Notational Errors
The most serious issue is the consistent use of "q(x)p(x)" throughout the paper to represent the density ratio q(x)/p(x). This appears in the abstract, introduction, equations, and theoretical analysis sections. For example:

- In the abstract: "estimating the ratio of two functions, q(x)p(x)"
- In equation (1): "Eq(h(x)) = ∫ h(x)q(x)dx = ∫ h(x) q(x) p(x) p(x)dx"
- In equation (2): "Kp q p (x) := ∫ k(x, y) q(y) p(y) p(y)dy"
- In equation (3): "Kt,p q p (y) := ∫ Rd kt(x, y) q(x) p(x) p(x)dx"
- In the theoretical analysis: "∥∥∥∥f Iλ,z − qp ∥∥∥∥ 2,p"

This notation is mathematically incorrect (it represents multiplication, not division) and appears consistently throughout the paper. The authors repeatedly use "q(x)p(x)" when they mean "q(x)/p(x)". This is not a minor typo but a fundamental notational error that would confuse readers and undermine the paper's technical credibility.

### Other Notational and Formatting Issues
1. In the introduction: "q(x) is either a known function or another probability density function given by a sample" - if q(x) is a known function, it doesn't need to be a probability density function.

2. In the text: "the values of q (which does not have to be a density function at all1)" - a floating "1" appears at the end of the sentence.

3. In the Related Work section: "the problem of KMM can be viewed within our setting Type I (see the Remark 2 in the introduction)" - but Remark 2 discusses using RKHS norm instead of L2,p, which is different from KMM's approach.

4. In the bibliography: "Manifold regularization: A geometric framework for learning from labeled and unlabeled examples, M. Belkin, P. Niyogi, V. Sindhwani, JMLR, 7:2399–2434, ," has an extra comma.

5. In the text: "Norms and loss functions other that L2,p" - should be "other than".

6. In equation (14): "‖Kt,q1− q‖2,p = O(t)" - the notation "Kt,q1" is unclear.

7. In the paper: "q(x)p(x)" is used 12 times incorrectly when "q(x)/p(x)" is intended.

### Technical Inconsistencies
The paper claims to provide "a completely unsupervised technique, CD-CV" for parameter selection, but the performance measure JCD(f;Xp,Xq,U) requires samples from both p and q, making it not truly unsupervised.

The paper states "the function q(x)p(x) as an unknown quantity" throughout, which is mathematically incorrect (it should be q(x)/p(x)), and this error permeates all technical sections.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations, the consistent and fundamental notational error of using "q(x)p(x)" instead of "q(x)/p(x)" throughout the paper is a severe flaw that undermines the paper's technical credibility. This is not a minor typo but a consistent, fundamental error that would confuse readers and make the paper difficult to follow. In a paper about precise mathematical formulations, such a pervasive error is unacceptable.

The paper's technical content is sound, but the notational inconsistencies are so severe that they overshadow the potential contributions. The authors should correct this fundamental error before resubmission. As a reviewer who is a perfectionist and meticulously examines every detail, I cannot recommend acceptance with these pervasive notational issues.

## Final Assessment
The paper has significant theoretical contributions but is marred by fundamental notational errors that make it difficult to understand and evaluate. The consistent use of "q(x)p(x)" to represent the density ratio q(x)/p(x) is a severe flaw that cannot be overlooked. I recommend rejection.
