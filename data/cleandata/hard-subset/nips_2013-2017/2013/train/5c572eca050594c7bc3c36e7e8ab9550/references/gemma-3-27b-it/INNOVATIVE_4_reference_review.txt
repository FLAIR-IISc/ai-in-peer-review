This paper presents a fascinating and novel perspective on density ratio estimation, framing it as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging tools from operator theory and RKHS. The core idea of connecting importance weighting to inverse problems is genuinely insightful and represents a significant conceptual leap. While the theoretical analysis and empirical validation are not exhaustive, the boldness of the approach and the potential for further development are compelling. This is a paper that prioritizes a new direction over immediate, polished perfection, and I believe that’s a strength in the context of NIPS.

**Summary:** The authors reformulate density ratio estimation as solving a Fredholm integral equation. They propose two formulations (Type I and Type II) and solve them using Tikhonov regularization within an RKHS framework, leading to the FIRE algorithm. They present theoretical results for Gaussian kernels and an unsupervised model selection technique (CD-CV) with experimental results demonstrating competitive performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is highly original.  This provides a completely different lens through which to view the problem and opens doors to techniques from the field of integral equations.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The connection to operator theory provides a strong theoretical framework. The analysis, particularly for Gaussian kernels, offers valuable insights into convergence rates and error bounds.
*   **Generality & Flexibility:** The framework is flexible, allowing for different norms and loss functions, and adaptable to situations where *q* is known only at sampled points (Type II formulation). This addresses a practical challenge in many applications.
*   **Explicit Algorithms:** The use of RKHS leads to easily implementable algorithms with explicit solutions, a significant practical advantage.
*   **CD-CV:** The unsupervised model selection technique is a valuable contribution, addressing a common challenge in density ratio estimation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Scope:** The theoretical analysis is largely confined to Gaussian kernels and Euclidean/manifold spaces. Extending this to other kernels and more complex domains would strengthen the work.
*   **Empirical Validation:** While the experiments demonstrate competitive performance, the results aren’t overwhelmingly superior to existing methods like KMM and LSIF. More extensive and diverse datasets, and a more thorough ablation study (e.g., varying regularization parameters more systematically), would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't explicitly address the computational complexity of the FIRE algorithms, especially concerning kernel matrix inversion.  This is particularly important for large datasets.
*   **Limited Discussion of Practical Challenges:** While the Type II formulation is valuable, the paper doesn't deeply discuss practical challenges in determining *q(x)* when it's not a density.
*   **Connection to KMM:** While the relationship to KMM is acknowledged, a deeper discussion on why FIRE might be preferred (e.g., stability, out-of-sample extension) would be useful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a fundamentally new and compelling perspective on a well-studied problem.
*   The theoretical framework is solid and provides a foundation for future research.
*   The algorithms are practical and implementable.
*   The paper’s novelty outweighs the limitations in its current form. Even if the empirical gains aren’t dramatic *now*, the potential for improvement and the originality of the approach justify acceptance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical results, while competitive, do not demonstrate a clear and substantial advantage over existing methods.
*   The theoretical analysis is relatively limited in scope.
*   Some practical considerations (computational complexity, challenges in determining *q(x)*) are not fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper represents a significant step forward in the way we think about density ratio estimation. The boldness of the approach, the strong theoretical grounding, and the potential for future development make it a valuable contribution to the field. While further work is needed to address the limitations, the novelty and potential impact of this work are sufficiently high to warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Expand the discussion of computational complexity.
2.  Include a more detailed ablation study in the experiments.
3.  Discuss potential extensions of the theoretical analysis to other kernels and domains.
4.  Clarify the practical implications of using the Type II formulation when *q(x)* is not a density.
