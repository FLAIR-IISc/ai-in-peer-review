

# Peer Review: "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

## Summary
This paper reframes density ratio estimation (also known as importance function estimation) as a classical Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, establishing a novel connection between machine learning and operator theory. The authors propose solving this inverse problem using Tikhonov regularization with RKHS norm penalties, leading to their "FIRE" (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) algorithms. They develop two main types of algorithms: Type I (when samples from both distributions p and q are available) and Type II (when q is known explicitly at points sampled from p). The paper provides theoretical analysis with error bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, and introduces a novel unsupervised model selection technique called CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation). The work connects to related approaches like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF, but offers a more unified theoretical framework.

## Strengths

**Originality:** The paper's core contribution—reformulating density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation—is highly novel. While density ratio estimation has been studied extensively (e.g., KMM, LSIF), this is the first to frame it within classical inverse problem theory. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from existing work, particularly noting that KMM can be viewed as a special case of their Type I formulation but with a different optimization procedure.

**Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical analysis is impressive. The authors provide precise error bounds and convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings in Euclidean space and on manifolds, using techniques from spectral theory and concentration inequalities. The bounds are comprehensive and well-motivated, showing how different factors (kernel width, regularization, sample size) affect performance.

**Practical Impact:** The FIRE algorithms are simple to implement and yield strong empirical results. The CD-CV model selection technique is particularly valuable, addressing a major challenge in unsupervised learning. The paper demonstrates FIRE's superiority over baselines (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across multiple datasets and tasks (regression, classification).

**Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical development is clear, with careful definitions of key concepts. The connection between the classical operator theory and practical machine learning algorithms is made transparent. The experimental section is thorough and well-structured.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Theoretical Scope:** The theoretical analysis is restricted to Gaussian kernels, which may limit the general applicability of the theoretical results. While Gaussian kernels are widely used, extending the analysis to other kernels (e.g., polynomial, Laplacian) would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

**Comparison with KMM:** The paper notes that KMM can be viewed as a special case of their Type I formulation but doesn't provide a thorough experimental comparison between the two methods. Since KMM is a major competitor in the density ratio estimation literature, the paper would benefit from a direct comparison of FIRE and KMM across multiple datasets, particularly regarding out-of-sample performance and stability.

**Computational Complexity Discussion:** While the algorithms are presented as computationally efficient, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of computational complexity, especially for large-scale applications. This is a significant omission given that many density ratio estimation methods struggle with scalability.

**Manifold Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis for manifolds assumes a compact smooth Riemannian sub-manifold, which may not cover all practical scenarios where manifold learning is applied. The paper would benefit from discussing how the method might handle more complex manifold structures.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper. It makes a significant contribution by bridging classical inverse problem theory with modern density ratio estimation methods, offering both theoretical insights and practical algorithms. The paper's strengths—particularly its novel theoretical framework, rigorous analysis, and strong empirical results—significantly outweigh the weaknesses.

The paper advances the state of the art in density ratio estimation by providing a principled framework that unifies and generalizes existing approaches. The FIRE algorithms are not only theoretically sound but also practically useful, as demonstrated by the comprehensive experimental evaluation. The CD-CV model selection technique is especially valuable for addressing a common challenge in unsupervised learning.

The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the field. While the theoretical scope could be expanded, the current analysis is thorough and appropriate for the Gaussian kernel case, which is widely used in practice. The connection to classical operator theory is particularly valuable for the broader ML community, as it provides a new perspective on an important problem.

## Final Thoughts

This is a high-quality paper that makes a substantial contribution to the field of density ratio estimation and inverse problems. The authors have successfully demonstrated how classical mathematical tools can provide new insights and practical algorithms for a well-studied machine learning problem. The paper's theoretical depth, clear presentation, and strong empirical results make it a compelling contribution to NIPS.

The paper is within the scope of NIPS, as it addresses an important problem in machine learning (density ratio estimation) with novel theoretical insights and practical algorithms, fitting squarely within the conference's focus on machine learning theory and applications.

I believe this paper will be of significant interest to the NIPS community, particularly to researchers working on transfer learning, covariate shift, and kernel methods. The FIRE algorithms and CD-CV technique are likely to be adopted and built upon by other researchers.
