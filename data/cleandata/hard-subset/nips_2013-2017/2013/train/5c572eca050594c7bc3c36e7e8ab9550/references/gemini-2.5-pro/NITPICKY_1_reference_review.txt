**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel framework for estimating the density ratio q(x)/p(x), a fundamental problem in areas like covariate shift adaptation and importance sampling. The central idea is to reframe this estimation task as a classical inverse problem, specifically, solving a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. The authors propose two main formulations: Type I, where samples from both distributions p and q are available, leading to the integral equation K_p[q/p] = K_q[1]; and Type II, where samples are from p but q is a known function, leading to the approximate equation K_{t,p}[q/p] ≈ q.

To solve these ill-posed inverse problems, the paper employs Tikhonov regularization with a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) norm penalty. This approach yields a family of algorithms, termed FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), which have simple, closed-form solutions based on the representer theorem. The framework is flexible, accommodating various loss functions (L2,p, a mixed L2,p/L2,q norm, and the RKHS norm). A key strength is the detailed theoretical analysis, providing concentration and error bounds with convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds. Furthermore, the paper introduces a practical, unsupervised model selection technique called Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV). Experimental results compare FIRE against established methods like KMM and LSIF, demonstrating competitive performance.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Formulation:** The core contribution—framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation—is highly original and intellectually appealing. It establishes a principled connection between a common machine learning problem and the well-developed fields of operator theory and inverse problems. This "linearization" approach is a significant conceptual advance.

2.  **Principled and Flexible Framework:** The use of Tikhonov regularization within an RKHS framework is a natural and powerful way to solve the resulting inverse problem. It provides a clear separation between the problem formulation and the regularization, and allows for considerable flexibility in the choice of kernels and loss functions, leading to a family of related algorithms.

3.  **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a substantive theoretical analysis, deriving error bounds and convergence rates for the proposed estimators. The consideration of both Euclidean and manifold settings is commendable and adds to the completeness of the work.

4.  **Practical Model Selection:** The proposed CD-CV method addresses the critical and often difficult problem of hyperparameter selection in an unsupervised setting. This is a valuable practical contribution that makes the proposed method more readily applicable.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the significant strengths, the paper suffers from a considerable number of flaws in clarity, methodological detail, and presentation that detract from its overall quality.

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Notational Precision:**
    *   The notation is frequently dense and ambiguous. For example, in Equation (7), the empirical operator `K_zp f(x_i)` is `1/n \sum_j k(x_j, x_i) f(x_j)`. When expressed in matrix form with `(K_{p,p})_{ij} = 1/n k(x_i, x_j)`, the squared term in the loss becomes `(1/n || K_{p,p} K_H v - K_{p,q} 1_m ||^2)`, which seems to introduce scaling factors (`1/n^2`) that are not explicitly handled in the text leading to the final solution in Equation (8). While the final formula appears correct upon re-derivation, the path presented in the paper is confusing.
    *   The term `1_zq` in Equation (8) is not defined; one must infer it is a vector of ones of appropriate dimension.
    *   The use of `≈ q(y)` and `+ o(1)` in core definitional equations (Eq. 3, 4) is overly informal. The conditions on the functions and kernels for these approximations to hold should be stated upfront, not deferred to the theory section.
    *   The name of the regularization method is misspelled as "TiknonovPhilips" in the abstract; the correct name is Tikhonov-Phillips.

2.  **Methodological Gaps:**
    *   The framework uses two kernels: `k` for the integral operator and `k_H` for the regularization penalty. The paper provides no guidance on how to choose these kernels. In most derivations and the theory, they are assumed to be identical, but the implications of choosing them differently—a key flexibility of the framework—are never discussed.
    *   In the experiments, the choice of the regularization parameter range `λ \in (1e-5, ..., 1e-10)` is extremely narrow and small. No justification is provided for this choice, raising questions about the method's sensitivity and the generality of the experimental findings.
    *   The TIKDE baseline relies on a threshold parameter `α`. The paper notes this is "key to reasonable performance" but fails to state how `α` was selected for the experiments. This omission undermines the comparison and may unfairly disadvantage the baseline.

3.  **Insufficient Discussion of Theoretical Results:**
    *   The theoretical analysis relies on strong smoothness assumptions, namely that `q/p` and `(q/p)^2` are in the Sobolev space `W^2_2`. The practical implications of these assumptions are not discussed. What happens when the density ratio is less smooth, as may often be the case in practice?
    *   The derived convergence rates are not contextualized. How do they compare to the known minimax optimal rates for this problem (e.g., from [14])? Without this comparison, it is difficult to judge the quality of the derived bounds.
    *   The condition `m > λ^(1/3) n` in Corollary 2 is not interpreted. Given the optimal scaling of `λ` with `n`, this translates to a condition on the relative sample sizes of `p` and `q`, which should be explicitly stated and discussed.

4.  **Presentation and Experimental Reporting:**
    *   The reference list is poorly formatted, with numerous inconsistencies, missing years/venues (e.g., [13], [23]), and typographical errors (e.g., trailing commas in [1]).
    *   Table 1 is extremely dense and difficult to read due to cryptic abbreviations (L., H.S., K., K.I.). It is not self-contained.
    *   The main results tables for regression and classification lack error bars or standard deviations. Reporting only average performance over 5 trials is insufficient to assess the statistical significance of the results.
    *   Crucial experimental details are missing. The `PCA(a, b)` resampling procedure is opaque, as the values of `a` and `b` are not specified.

**Recommendation**

This paper is built on a strong, novel, and significant core idea. The formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem is a valuable contribution, and the accompanying theoretical analysis is substantial. However, the execution and presentation of these ideas are marred by a large number of minor and moderate flaws. The lack of notational clarity, gaps in methodological details, incomplete discussion of theoretical results, and poor experimental reporting collectively undermine the reader's confidence in the work. While any single one of these issues might be minor, their sheer number suggests a lack of care and polish that is not up to the standards of NIPS.

Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The underlying work has the potential for a very strong paper, and I would strongly encourage the authors to thoroughly revise the manuscript to address the issues raised above and resubmit to a future conference.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Highly original and elegant formulation of an important problem.
*   Provides a flexible framework with simple, closed-form algorithms.
*   Includes a strong theoretical analysis with convergence rates.
*   Introduces a practical unsupervised model selection method.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Numerous issues with clarity, precision, and notation throughout the paper.
*   Significant methodological details are missing, particularly regarding experimental settings and baseline comparisons, hindering reproducibility and fair evaluation.
*   The theoretical analysis, while present, is not sufficiently contextualized or discussed.
*   The overall presentation, including tables and references, lacks the polish and rigor expected for a top-tier publication.
