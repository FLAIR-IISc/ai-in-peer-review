

This paper presents a novel reformulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, establishing a connection between classical inverse problem theory and modern machine learning approaches. The authors propose the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) framework, which leverages Tikhonov regularization with RKHS norms to solve this inverse problem, providing a unified theoretical foundation for density ratio estimation. The paper makes several key contributions: a principled mathematical formulation, detailed theoretical analysis including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, and a novel unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV) that addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS literature, building upon but significantly extending previous work on density ratio estimation. While Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10] have been prominent approaches, this work provides a more comprehensive theoretical foundation by connecting to classical operator theory. The authors correctly identify that KMM can be viewed within their Type I framework but with different optimization procedures, and they provide a clear theoretical justification for their approach.

The quality of the paper is excellent. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with detailed convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings across different domains (Euclidean space, manifolds). The experimental results demonstrate strong performance compared to existing methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) on classification and regression tasks, with CD-CV proving particularly effective for model selection. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The originality is strong, as the Fredholm equation perspective provides a fresh and principled approach to a well-studied problem. The connection to inverse problem theory is novel in this context and opens new avenues for theoretical analysis. The significance is high, as density ratio estimation is fundamental to covariate shift adaptation, importance sampling, and transfer learning, with the FIRE framework offering both theoretical guarantees and practical advantages.

A minor weakness is that the paper could better highlight the computational complexity of FIRE compared to simpler methods like LSIF, though the experimental section does compare computational efficiency. Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in density ratio estimation with principled theory and strong empirical results. I recommend acceptance.
