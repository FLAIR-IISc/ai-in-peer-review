

# Review: Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach

This paper introduces a novel approach to density ratio estimation by formulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, then solving it using regularization techniques from inverse problems theory. The authors propose the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) framework that leverages Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) regularization to solve these inverse problems.

The paper makes several key contributions: (1) a new formulation of density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem, (2) a principled framework for constructing algorithms using regularization with RKHS norms, (3) detailed theoretical analysis with concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, and (4) a novel unsupervised model selection method called CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation).

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical analysis to experimental evaluation. The connection to previous work is well-explained, particularly the relationship to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10]. The authors demonstrate how their approach differs from KMM, noting that KMM does not have a natural out-of-sample extension and lacks regularization, which can lead to instability.

The theoretical analysis is a significant strength of the paper. The authors provide detailed convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings, covering Euclidean spaces and compact manifolds. The bounds (Theorems 1 and 3) are carefully derived and account for the trade-offs between approximation error, estimation error, and regularization. The analysis is thorough and provides meaningful insights into the behavior of the algorithms.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing FIRE with several state-of-the-art methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) on multiple datasets (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS). The authors use a novel performance measure JCD(f; Xp, Xq, U) for parameter selection, which is well-motivated and effectively demonstrates the utility of their CD-CV method. The results show consistent improvements over baselines, particularly in classification tasks.

The paper is technically sound with claims well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors carefully discuss both strengths and weaknesses of their approach, and the manuscript is complete as a scientific contribution rather than a position paper.

The novelty of the approach is significant - the Fredholm equation formulation of density ratio estimation is not commonly used in the machine learning community. The connection to classical inverse problem theory provides a fresh perspective that advances the understanding of this problem. The CD-CV method for unsupervised model selection addresses a key challenge in unsupervised learning.

The significance of the work is substantial. Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem with applications in transfer learning, importance sampling, and covariate shift adaptation. The theoretical insights and practical algorithms presented have the potential to influence future work in these areas.

While the paper is strong overall, it could benefit from more thorough discussion of limitations, particularly regarding the assumptions required for the theoretical guarantees (e.g., Sobolev space conditions). The computational efficiency analysis compared to alternatives could also be more detailed.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by bridging classical inverse problem theory with modern machine learning techniques. The theoretical analysis provides a solid foundation for understanding the behavior of their algorithms, while the experimental results demonstrate practical utility. The approach is generalizable to different kernel functions and settings, making it a flexible tool for practitioners.

This work meets all the NIPS review criteria: it is technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant. The paper advances the state of the art in density ratio estimation with a novel theoretical perspective and practical algorithms. It is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with a scientifically rigorous approach.

Pro: Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation, rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence rates, practical CD-CV method for model selection, comprehensive experimental evaluation.

Con: Limited discussion of approach limitations, computational efficiency analysis could be more detailed.

Given the paper's technical soundness, novelty, and significance, I recommend acceptance.
