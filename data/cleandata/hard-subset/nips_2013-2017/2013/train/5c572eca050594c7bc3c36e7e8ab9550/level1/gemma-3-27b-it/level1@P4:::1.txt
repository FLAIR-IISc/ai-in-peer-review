This paper addresses the problem of density ratio estimation, framing it as a Fredholm integral equation and proposing a family of algorithms (FIRE) based on Tikhonov regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The authors provide theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates, and introduce a cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) method for model selection.

The core idea of reformulating density ratio estimation as an inverse problem is novel and potentially insightful, connecting it to a well-established area of mathematics (Fredholm theory) and enabling the application of tools from spectral analysis. The FIRE algorithms are presented as a flexible and implementable framework within kernel learning. The theoretical analysis, while complex, attempts to provide rigorous guarantees for the proposed methods. The CD-CV method is a practical contribution, addressing a common challenge in unsupervised learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The Fredholm equation approach to density ratio estimation is a unique perspective, offering a different lens through which to analyze and solve this problem.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates, which is a significant strength. The analysis considers both Euclidean and manifold settings.
*   **Flexibility:** The FIRE algorithms are presented as a flexible framework, allowing for different norms and regularization techniques.
*   **Practical Contribution:** The CD-CV method is a valuable addition for model selection, especially in unsupervised settings.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors clearly position their work relative to related approaches, such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), highlighting the advantages and disadvantages of each.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** While the paper aims for rigor, the mathematical notation and explanations are dense and can be challenging to follow. The jump from the integral equation formulation to the concrete algorithms could be made more explicit. A more intuitive explanation of the underlying intuition would be beneficial.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results, while present, lack a clear demonstration of the FIRE algorithms’ superiority over existing methods in all scenarios. The comparisons with TIKDE, LSIF, and KMM could be more thorough, including a more detailed discussion of parameter tuning and statistical significance.  The choice of test functions in CD-CV and their impact on performance isn’t fully explored.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper does not explicitly discuss the computational complexity of the FIRE algorithms, which is an important consideration for practical applications, especially in high-dimensional settings. The matrix inversions involved (e.g., in Eq. 8) could be a bottleneck.
*   **Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on certain assumptions (e.g., smoothness of densities, Gaussian kernels), which may not hold in all real-world applications. The robustness of the algorithms to violations of these assumptions is not investigated.
*   **Limited Scope of Type II Setting:** The theoretical analysis for the Type II setting contains several caveats regarding convergence rates dependent on the kernel and function properties that might limit its practicality.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to density ratio estimation with a strong theoretical foundation.
*   The FIRE algorithms offer a flexible and principled framework for solving this problem.
*   The CD-CV method provides a practical solution for model selection.
*   The connections to classical operator theory and RKHS methods are valuable.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The clarity of the paper could be improved, making it more accessible to a wider audience.
*   The experimental evaluation is not entirely convincing, and more comprehensive comparisons with existing methods are needed.
*   The computational complexity of the algorithms is not discussed.
*   The reliance on specific assumptions may limit the applicability of the methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting and potentially significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The novelty of the approach, the theoretical rigor, and the practical contribution of CD-CV are commendable. However, the lack of clarity and the limited scope of experimental validation are drawbacks.  I would lean towards **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent upon the authors addressing the clarity concerns, providing more compelling experimental results (including a more detailed discussion of computational cost), and perhaps acknowledging the limitations of the theoretical assumptions. Specifically, the authors should clearly demonstrate in experiments that FIRE consistently outperforms or provides a worthwhile trade-off compared to existing methods like KMM and LSIF. A discussion of scalability to larger datasets would also be beneficial.
