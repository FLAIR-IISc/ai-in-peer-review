This paper tackles the important problem of density ratio estimation, framing it as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging regularization techniques from operator theory. The approach is well-motivated, building on the connection to importance sampling and covariate shift, and offers a principled framework for algorithm design and theoretical analysis. The FIRE algorithms proposed are conceptually simple and the introduction of the CD-CV method for model selection is a valuable contribution.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem as a Fredholm equation is clever and allows for the application of a rich set of mathematical tools. The theoretical analysis, providing concentration bounds and convergence rates, is a significant strength. However, the presentation of the theoretical results could be more concise.  The convergence rates (Corollary 2 & 4) are expressed as `O(√ τn − ...)` which is somewhat unusual and obscures the dependency on *n* at a glance.  The claims are generally well-supported by the analysis. It feels complete as a research paper, although the experimental section could benefit from more extensive comparisons and analysis (see below). The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the dependence on the choice of kernel).

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but suffers from a degree of mathematical density. While the core ideas are clear, the numerous equations and technical details can be overwhelming for readers not deeply familiar with operator theory and functional analysis.  More intuitive explanations alongside the mathematical derivations would improve readability.  The notation is mostly consistent but could be improved in places; the multiple uses of subscripts (e.g.,  `f Iλ,z`) can become confusing.  The connection between the two FIRE types (I & II) and their practical application could be highlighted more clearly.

**Originality:** The core idea of reframing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is novel, though it builds upon existing work in kernel methods and regularization. The authors convincingly demonstrate how this perspective enables a more rigorous theoretical analysis and leads to a flexible algorithmic framework. While KMM is acknowledged as closely related, the authors correctly point out the benefits of their approach, particularly the regularization term that enhances stability and enables a clearer theoretical treatment.  The CD-CV method is a novel and practically useful contribution.

**Significance:**  The results are significant. Providing a theoretical foundation for density ratio estimation is valuable, particularly in the context of covariate shift and transfer learning. The FIRE algorithms offer a competitive alternative to existing methods, and the CD-CV method provides a practical solution for model selection. The paper’s contribution is likely to be of interest to researchers working in machine learning, statistics, and related fields. However, the practical impact depends on the scalability and performance of the algorithms on real-world datasets, which the experimental section doesn't fully demonstrate.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem.
*   Rigorous theoretical analysis with convergence rates.
*   Principled algorithmic framework (FIRE) based on regularization.
*   Practical CD-CV method for model selection.
*   Clear connection to established problems like importance sampling and covariate shift.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Mathematical density can hinder readability for some readers.
*   Experimental section is somewhat limited; lacks comparisons to stronger baselines and larger datasets.
*   Convergence rates are expressed in a less-than-intuitive manner.
*   The discussion of the choice of kernel and its impact on performance could be expanded.
*   Lack of discussion regarding computational complexity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to a significant problem in machine learning.
*   The theoretical analysis is a major strength, providing valuable insights into the behavior of the proposed algorithms.
*   The CD-CV method is a practical and useful contribution.
*   The paper is well-written (despite its mathematical density) and clearly presents its ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is limited and doesn’t fully demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed approach over state-of-the-art methods.  A comparison to more recent and sophisticated algorithms for density ratio estimation (beyond KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE) would be beneficial.
*   The mathematical density might make the paper inaccessible to some readers.




**Overall Assessment:**  I believe this paper is a strong contribution and deserves acceptance. The novelty of the approach, the rigor of the theoretical analysis, and the practical value of the CD-CV method outweigh the weaknesses. However, I recommend the authors address the comments regarding clarity and expand the experimental evaluation to further strengthen the paper. A more thorough discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithms would also be valuable. I rate this as a **Strong Accept** contingent on minor revisions addressing the clarity and experimental evaluation issues.
