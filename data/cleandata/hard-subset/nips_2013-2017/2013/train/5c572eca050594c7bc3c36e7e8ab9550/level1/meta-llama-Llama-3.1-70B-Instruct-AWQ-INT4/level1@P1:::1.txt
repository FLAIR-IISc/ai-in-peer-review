This paper addresses the problem of estimating the ratio of two functions, q(x)/p(x), where p is a density function and q is another density or an arbitrary function. The authors reformulate this problem as an inverse problem using the Fredholm equation of the first kind and solve it using regularization and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS). The resulting family of algorithms, called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator), is flexible, simple, and easy to implement.

The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel, for densities defined on R and smooth d-dimensional sub-manifolds of the Euclidean space. The authors also introduce a completely unsupervised method for model selection, called CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation), and demonstrate its usefulness in experimental results.

The strengths of the paper include:

* A novel formulation of the density ratio estimation problem as an inverse problem using the Fredholm equation of the first kind.
* A flexible and principled framework for constructing algorithms and analyzing them theoretically using regularization and RKHS.
* A detailed theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel.
* A completely unsupervised method for model selection, CD-CV, which is useful in practice.

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The paper assumes that the kernel k(x, y) is symmetric and positive definite, which may not always be the case in practice.
* The authors do not provide a comprehensive comparison of their method with other existing methods for density ratio estimation, such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF).
* The experimental results are limited to a few datasets, and more extensive experiments are needed to fully evaluate the performance of the proposed method.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper provides a novel and principled approach to density ratio estimation, which is a fundamental problem in statistical inference.
* The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the behavior of the proposed algorithm.
* The CD-CV method for model selection is a useful contribution to the field.

Arguments con acceptance:
