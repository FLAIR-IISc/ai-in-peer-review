## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper tackles the problem of density ratio estimation, a crucial task in areas like importance sampling and transfer learning. The authors propose a novel approach by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation and leveraging tools from regularization theory, specifically within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This leads to a family of algorithms, FIRE, and a novel unsupervised model selection method, CD-CV.

The work builds upon a significant body of research in density ratio estimation, acknowledging related approaches like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9], Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10], and covariate shift correction methods [19, 27]. The key contribution lies in framing the problem as an inverse problem, enabling the application of well-established theoretical tools for analysis. This is a strong point, as it provides a principled foundation for algorithm design and theoretical guarantees. 

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound, presenting a clear mathematical formulation and deriving concentration bounds and convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel. The connection to classical operator theory is insightful and allows for a rigorous analysis. The FIRE algorithms appear relatively simple to implement, and the CD-CV method offers a practical solution for parameter selection. The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance against existing methods. The detailed theoretical analysis, including bounds for both Euclidean and manifold settings, is a significant achievement.

**Weaknesses:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the assumptions required for the derived bounds (smoothness, specific kernel choice) might limit the practical applicability. The comparison with KMM is somewhat limited, as the authors acknowledge the need for out-of-sample extension for a fair comparison. The presentation could be more concise; some sections feel repetitive. While the paper mentions connections to a vast literature, a more focused discussion on how FIRE specifically improves upon existing methods in practical scenarios would be beneficial. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
*   Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees.
*   Practical algorithms (FIRE) and model selection method (CD-CV).
*   Competitive experimental results.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Theoretical results rely on potentially restrictive assumptions.
*   Limited comparative analysis against KMM, particularly regarding out-of-sample performance.
*   Presentation could be more concise.



**Overall:** This is a solid contribution to the field. The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to density ratio estimation. While some limitations exist, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised regarding the comparative analysis and presentation.
