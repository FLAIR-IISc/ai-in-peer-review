Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes estimating the density ratio w(x) = q(x)/p(x) by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two settings are introduced:
- Type I: Kp w = Kq 1, where Kp and Kq are integral operators with respect to p and q using a chosen kernel k. This is applicable when samples from both p and q are available.
- Type II: Kt,p w ≈ q for a δ-like kernel kt (e.g., Gaussian), yielding an approximate identity with a controllable kernel bias term; this applies when q is known pointwise (not necessarily a density) at sample points from p.

Using representer-theorem arguments, closed-form solutions are derived for several loss choices (L2,p; mixed γL2,p+(1−γ)L2,q; and RKHS norm), leading to a family of estimators termed FIRE. The paper analyzes these estimators for Gaussian kernels, giving concentration bounds and convergence rates in Euclidean spaces and on compact smooth submanifolds. It also proposes an unsupervised model selection method, CD-CV, which selects kernel width and regularization by validating the importance-sampling identity on random test functions. Experiments compare FIRE to LSIF and a thresholded ratio of kernel density estimates (TIKDE), and to some extent discuss KMM. Connections to prior work are drawn: KMM is shown to correspond to the same integral identity in H; LSIF/KLIEP relate to minimizing L2,p/KL divergence with finite bases; and the approach aligns with operator-based kernel methods and inverse problem regularization.

Quality
- Methodology: The formulation as an inverse problem is sound, and the empirical objective functions are well-posed convex problems in RKHS with explicit solutions. The separation between the inverse problem (Fredholm equation) and regularization is clear. The Type II formulation properly accounts for kernel bias O(t).
- Theory: For Gaussian kernels, the error bounds decompose into approximation (kernel bias O(t) or t1−ε), regularization (O(λ1/2)), and sample terms depending on n, m, t, and λ. Manifold cases are addressed, which is nontrivial and valuable. However, the final rates in the corollaries are presented with unusual exponents (e.g., “3.5” and “56 d”), likely typographical/formatting issues (e.g., 7/2 + d/2, 5/6·d). Clarification is needed. The paper does not compare its rates to known minimax rates for likelihood ratio estimation (e.g., Nguyen et al., 2007), so it is unclear whether the obtained rates are optimal or loose.
- Empirics: The CD-CV validation criterion is appropriate for ratio estimation when both samples are available, and the use of independent random function families for selection and evaluation is a sound stress test. Experiments (USPS, CPUsmall, Kin8nm) show encouraging results and highlight computational simplicity. Yet, comparisons to KMM are limited, and more recent least-squares variants (e.g., unconstrained/KuLSIF) are not included. Runtime analyses and sensitivity studies (e.g., to kernel width and λ) are not fully detailed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-structured and explains the two formulations, empirical objectives, and representer-based solutions clearly, with explicit matrix forms. Some notation is heavy and occasionally overloaded (K, KH, Kt with different normalizations), and there are minor typos (e.g., “Tiknonov”). The presentation of convergence rates needs careful proofreading to eliminate exponent formatting issues. The connection to KMM is insightful, but the discussion of out-of-sample issues could be sharpened (KMM yields weights at p-sample points, which suffices for many covariate-shift uses).

Originality
Casting density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem and solving it via RKHS Tikhonov regularization provides a unifying operator-theoretic perspective. While related to KMM and LSIF, the paper’s derivation yields simple closed-form estimators for multiple losses, covers a Type II setting where q is known pointwise, and includes manifold analyses. The CD-CV criterion is a practical, unsupervised way to tune hyperparameters specific to ratio estimation. Overall, the combination of the inverse-problem framing, explicit solutions, and manifold theory appears novel and useful.

Significance
Density ratio estimation underlies importance weighting and covariate shift. A flexible, stable, and efficient estimator with theoretical guarantees and an unsupervised selection rule is valuable. The manifold results may stimulate further work in non-Euclidean settings. However, the experimental scope is modest, and the lack of stronger baselines and rigorous comparison of statistical rates tempers the immediate impact. The absence of positivity or normalization constraints for the ratio (important in importance weighting) is a practical concern; empirical implications are not explored.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation as a Fredholm inverse problem with RKHS regularization.
- Closed-form estimators in multiple settings (Type I/II; several losses), with natural out-of-sample evaluation.
- Theoretical guarantees for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings, with explicit bias–variance trade-offs.
- Practical, unsupervised CD-CV model selection tailored to ratio estimation.
- Simple algorithms with favorable computational profile compared to QP-based methods.

Cons
- Presentation issues in the convergence rates (ambiguous exponents) and minor notation/typo problems.
- No constraints to enforce nonnegativity or normalization of the estimated ratio; implications not analyzed.
- Experimental evaluation is limited in datasets, metrics, and baselines (e.g., KMM, KuLSIF/uLSIF, KLIEP) and lacks runtime and sensitivity analyses.
- Limited theoretical discussion of optimality of rates relative to known minimax results; no guarantees for CD-CV selection.
- Type II bias–variance trade-off (choice of t vs λ) is not practically guided beyond CD-CV.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix typographical/formatting issues in theoretical results; clearly state assumptions and final rates, and compare to minimax benchmarks.
- Discuss or incorporate simple positivity/normalization mechanisms (e.g., clipping, constrained optimization) and evaluate their effect.
- Expand experiments: include KMM and modern least-squares baselines (e.g., KuLSIF), larger datasets, runtime, and robustness analyses; evaluate in downstream covariate-shift tasks with test accuracy gains.
- Provide guidance on selecting t and λ jointly (analytical heuristics or theoretically grounded schedules).
- Include brief pseudocode to aid reproducibility.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and reasonably clear framework with novel aspects and useful theory. Strengthening empirical comparisons and clarifying the theoretical rates would significantly improve its impact.
