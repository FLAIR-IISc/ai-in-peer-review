## Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper tackles the important problem of density ratio estimation, framing it as an inverse problem solvable through Fredholm integral equations and Tikhonov regularization. The authors leverage tools from operator theory and RKHS to propose a family of algorithms, FIRE, and provide theoretical guarantees for convergence rates under Gaussian kernels, considering both Euclidean and manifold settings. A novel unsupervised model selection technique, CD-CV, is also presented and empirically evaluated.

The work builds upon a significant body of related research, particularly Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9] and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF) [10], accurately positioning itself within the existing landscape. The authors rightly highlight how their formulation allows for the application of standard spectral and Fourier analysis for theoretical results, a benefit not readily available in other approaches. The connection to classical operator theory is a strong point, offering a principled framework for analysis. 

**Strengths:** The paper's key strength lies in its solid theoretical foundation. The derivation of concentration bounds and convergence rates is a significant contribution, providing a strong justification for the proposed algorithms. The framework is flexible, easily accommodating different norms and loss functions. The introduction of CD-CV for unsupervised model selection is a practical and valuable addition, and the experimental results demonstrate competitive performance. Clarity is good, with a logical flow and clear explanations of core concepts.

**Weaknesses:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, it is limited to the Gaussian kernel. Expanding these results to other commonly used kernels would significantly broaden the applicability of the framework. The experimental section, while demonstrating good results, could be more extensive, particularly in comparisons to KMM with careful parameter tuning. The paper lacks a discussion on computational complexity, an important consideration when comparing algorithms. The presentation of the experimental results is a bit dense and could benefit from clearer visualization and statistical significance testing. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation with convergence rate analysis.
*   Novel formulation of density ratio estimation as an inverse problem.
*   Flexible framework with potential for customization.
*   Practical unsupervised model selection technique.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Theoretical results limited to Gaussian kernel.
*   Experimental section could be more comprehensive.
*   Lack of discussion on computational complexity.



**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a technically sound and original contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The rigorous theoretical analysis and well-motivated algorithmic framework are commendable. While there are areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, making it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend **acceptance**, with the suggestion that the authors consider expanding the theoretical analysis to other kernels and providing a more detailed experimental evaluation.
