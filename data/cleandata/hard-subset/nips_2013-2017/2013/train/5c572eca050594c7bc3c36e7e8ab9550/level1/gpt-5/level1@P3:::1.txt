Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a new perspective on density-ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) by casting it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. Two complementary formulations are introduced: (Type I) Kp r = Kq 1, where Kp and Kq are integral operators induced by a kernel and the measures p and q; and (Type II) Kt,p r ≈ q, where Kt,p uses a δ-approximate kernel (e.g., Gaussian) so that ∫ k_t(x,y) r(x) p(x) dx ≈ q(y) as t→0. Both are solved via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding closed-form linear systems (FIRE estimators) via the representer theorem. The authors analyze statistical error and derive convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in R^d and for measures supported on smooth manifolds. They also propose a fully unsupervised hyperparameter selection method, CD-CV, which chooses kernel width and regularization by matching empirical expectations across the two samples (a moment-matching/IPM-type criterion). Experiments compare against LSIF and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline, and show applications to covariate shift classification/regression.

This work connects density-ratio estimation and covariate shift adaptation—studied at NIPS and elsewhere—most directly to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [Huang et al. NIPS’06; Gretton et al. 2009], KLIEP/LSIF [Sugiyama et al.], and to inverse-problem regularization in RKHS [De Vito et al.; Smola & Schölkopf]. The Type I formulation with RKHS loss clarifies KMM as solving a Fredholm equation in H; the Type II formulation resembles approximate-identity arguments standard in analysis. The theory leverages spectral properties of Gaussian convolution, extending to manifold-supported measures, resonating with manifold regularization and kernel operator methods (Belkin & Niyogi; Shi, Belkin & Yu).

Strengths:
- Conceptual unification: Casting density-ratio estimation as solving a Fredholm equation yields a principled operator-theoretic view, naturally motivating regularization and enabling spectral analysis. This clarifies how KMM fits into a broader inverse-problem framework and why regularization is beneficial.
- Algorithmic simplicity and out-of-sample extensibility: Closed-form solutions as kernel expansions over p-sample points; unlike KMM, the target function itself lies in the RKHS, providing natural out-of-sample evaluation.
- Theoretical analysis: Finite-sample concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, including the manifold case. The decomposition of error terms (bias in t, regularization λ, and stochastic terms) is transparent and useful.
- Practical model selection: CD-CV is a sensible, fully unsupervised criterion based on matching expectations across samples, related to integral probability metrics and importance-weighted validation; easy to implement.
- Applications: Demonstrations on covariate shift tasks (weighted SVM, weighted least squares) are appropriate.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Assumptions and well-posedness: The analysis assumes q/p and q/p^2 ∈ W^2_2 and only an upper bound p(x) < Γ without a lower bound away from zero. This combination permits arbitrarily large ratios where p is small, yet requires square-integrability of r and r/p, which is a strong condition. Clarify assumptions ensuring absolute continuity (q ≪ p) and discuss the impact of near-zero p. Consider either requiring p ≥ c > 0 on support of q, or making explicit how regularization and kernel smoothing mitigate ill-posedness when supports mismatch.
- Non-negativity and normalization: FIRE does not enforce r ≥ 0 or Ep[r] = 1 (when q is a density). Negative estimates can be problematic in importance sampling/classification. Discuss practical remedies (clipping, positivity/linear constraints, logarithmic parameterization) and their theoretical implications. Comparing to KMM’s box constraints and LSIF’s nonnegativity is important.
- Rates and optimality: The derived rates (e.g., n^{-1/(3.5 + d/2)} in Cor. 2) are relatively slow and their optimality is unclear compared to minimax results for likelihood ratio estimation (Nguyen et al., 2007) or related deconvolution problems. Provide a discussion of whether suboptimality is due to proof technique (Gaussian RKHS approximation, δ-kernel bias) or inherent to the operator inversion, and if faster rates arise under source conditions or stronger smoothness.
- Hyperparameter conditions: Corollaries impose conditions like m > λ^{1/3} n and include non-intuitive exponents (λ^{-1/6} terms). Give guidance on feasible scaling with n,m and practical tuning (e.g., balancing t and λ) to achieve the stated rates.
- Relation to IPMs/MMD: CD-CV with random linear or half-space test functions is an IPM over a function class U. This could be framed as MMD with a characteristic kernel, which would give a stronger signal and a single, canonical choice of U. Consider including MMD-based validation and discussing its statistical power vs. random U choices.
- Experimental coverage: KMM is omitted in some comparisons due to out-of-sample issues, but for covariate shift in classification/regression, KMM’s weights on training points suffice—so a fair comparison is feasible. Include KMM, KLIEP, uLSIF/RuLSIF, and importance-weighted CV baselines (Sugiyama et al., JMLR’07). TIKDE is a weak baseline. Also report sensitivity to positivity clipping and to support mismatch.
- Computational aspects: The methods require solving dense linear systems with O(n^3) time and O(n^2) memory. Discuss scalability (Nyström, random features, conjugate gradients with preconditioning) and how CD-CV’s parameter grid search impacts runtime.

Quality:
- Generally sound and carefully argued. The operator-theoretic derivations are correct and insightful. However, some assumptions are stronger than needed and could be better justified. The theory would benefit from clearer guidance on tuning t,λ and a discussion of optimality.

Clarity:
- Well-written and organized. Some notation is dense (many K-matrices); providing explicit pseudocode, complexity, and a summary table of variants would improve readability. Clarify the settings where Type II is appropriate (e.g., known q up to a constant) and emphasize absolute continuity requirements.

Originality:
- The Fredholm-equation formulation and unification with RKHS inverse problems is original and elegant. The manifold extensions and CD-CV criterion add novelty beyond KMM/LSIF.

Significance:
- Potentially impactful, offering a principled framework and analysis for an important problem (covariate shift, importance sampling). If empirical comparisons are strengthened and positivity/robustness issues addressed, the approach could be adopted widely.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptual unification of ratio estimation with inverse problems; clear algorithms with out-of-sample extension.
- Nontrivial theory (finite-sample bounds, manifold case).
- Practical, unsupervised hyperparameter selection.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing key baselines (KMM, KLIEP/uLSIF/RuLSIF) and positivity constraints in practice.
- Strong/opaque assumptions and somewhat slow rates without clear optimality discussion.
- Limited scalability discussion and partial experimental validation.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Enforce or post-process for non-negativity and normalization; analyze clipping.
- Add KMM, KLIEP/uLSIF/RuLSIF, and IWCV comparisons; include MMD-based CD-CV.
- Provide tuning guidance (t vs. λ) and discuss rate optimality.
- Clarify assumptions regarding support, absolute continuity, and lower bounds on p.
- Discuss scalable solvers/approximations and provide runtime comparisons.
