This paper presents a novel framework for estimating the density ratio q/p, a fundamental problem in importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation. The central idea is to reformulate the estimation problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. This reformulation provides a principled connection to classical operator theory and inverse problems.

The authors propose two main settings. In "Type I," where samples from both distributions p and q are available, the density ratio f = q/p is found by solving the integral equation `K_p f = K_q 1`, where `K_p` is the integral operator with a given kernel k, with respect to the measure p. In "Type II," applicable when q is a known function and samples are drawn from p, the ratio is found by solving the approximate equation `K_{t,p} f ≈ q`, where `K_{t,p}` is an operator with a delta-like kernel `k_t`.

To solve these ill-posed inverse problems, the paper employs Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. This leads to a family of algorithms, termed Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimators (FIRE). By applying the representer theorem, the paper derives closed-form solutions for the estimator, which take the form of standard kernel methods involving the inversion of kernel matrices. The framework is shown to be flexible, accommodating various loss functions (L2,p, L2,q, and RKHS norms).

This work builds upon a significant body of research on direct density ratio estimation. It explicitly relates its framework to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9], showing that KMM can be viewed as a specific instance of the Type I setting with an RKHS norm loss and no regularization term. It also draws connections to Least-Squares Importance Fitting (LSIF) [10] and KLIEP [23], particularly in the context of model selection. By framing the problem in the language of inverse problems, the work aligns with prior machine learning research that views learning as operator inversion [3, 20].

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Principled Formulation:** The primary strength of the paper is its novel formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem. This provides an elegant and theoretically grounded perspective that unifies different approaches and connects the problem to a mature field of classical analysis. This conceptual contribution is significant.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Theoretical Analysis:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the FIRE algorithms from the regularization framework is clear and correct. A major contribution is the detailed theoretical analysis, which provides concentration bounds and convergence rates for the proposed estimators using Gaussian kernels. The analysis is comprehensive, covering data on both Euclidean spaces and smooth sub-manifolds, which is a non-trivial extension. These results (Theorems 1 and 3, Corollaries 2 and 4) provide strong theoretical support for the method.

3.  **Practical Contribution in Model Selection:** A key challenge in unsupervised learning tasks like density ratio estimation is model selection. The proposed Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV) method is a simple yet effective approach to this problem. It leverages the fundamental importance sampling identity to create an objective function for hyperparameter tuning without requiring labeled data. This is a highly valuable practical contribution.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. It clearly distinguishes between the Type I and Type II settings, explains the underlying integral equations, and presents the resulting algorithms with explicit formulas. The connections to related work are clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation, while encouraging, could be more comprehensive.
    *   The datasets used (CPUsmall, Kin8nm, USPS) are relatively low-dimensional. The derived theoretical rates show a dependency on dimensionality (`d`), and it remains unclear how the method's practical performance scales to high-dimensional problems where density estimation is notoriously difficult.
    *   KMM is discussed as the most closely related algorithm, yet it is absent from the quantitative experiments. The authors' reasoning (the need for out-of-sample extension) is valid but omitting the comparison misses an opportunity to empirically demonstrate a key advantage of FIRE.
    *   The description of the baseline TIKDE (Thresholded Inverse Kernel Density Estimator) lacks detail on how its crucial threshold parameter `α` was selected, making it difficult to fully assess the fairness of the comparison.

2.  **Discussion of Theoretical Rates:** The paper presents convergence rates but does not extensively discuss their implications. For instance, the rates in Corollary 4 for the Type II setting appear quite slow, especially with the `(5/6)d` term in the exponent's denominator. A discussion on whether these rates are tight or how they compare to minimax rates for this problem [14] would provide valuable context.

3.  **Computational Complexity:** The algorithms involve inverting an `n x n` matrix (where `n` is the number of samples from p), resulting in `O(n^3)` complexity. While the paper notes better computational efficiency compared to alternatives in experiments, this cubic scaling can be a bottleneck for large datasets. A brief discussion of potential approximations or scalability would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a novel and principled framework for density ratio estimation based on Fredholm integral equations.
*   Provides strong theoretical guarantees, including convergence rates on manifolds.
*   Proposes CD-CV, a practical and well-justified method for unsupervised model selection.
*   The framework is flexible and leads to simple, implementable kernel-based algorithms.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope, particularly concerning high-dimensional data and direct comparison with KMM.
*   The practical implications of the derived convergence rates and the method's computational scaling are not fully discussed.

In summary, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The novel formulation as an inverse problem is both theoretically elegant and practically useful, leading to a flexible family of algorithms with solid theoretical backing. The proposed unsupervised model selection technique addresses a critical practical challenge. While the experimental evaluation could be expanded, the core conceptual, theoretical, and methodological contributions are significant and justify acceptance.
