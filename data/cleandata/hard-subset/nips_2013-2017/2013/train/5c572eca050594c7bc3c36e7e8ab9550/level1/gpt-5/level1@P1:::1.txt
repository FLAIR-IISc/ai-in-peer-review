Summary
The paper proposes a new viewpoint on density ratio estimation r(x) = q(x)/p(x) by casting it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solving it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two formulations are given: Type I uses Kp r = Kq 1 (exact identity), applicable when samples from both p and q are available; Type II uses approximate identity Kt,p r ≈ q with a δ-kernel (e.g., Gaussian), applicable when q is known or evaluable on the sample from p (not necessarily a density). Using empirical approximations and the representer theorem yields closed-form kernel algorithms (FIRE) with explicit linear system solutions. The authors derive concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on compact d-dimensional manifolds. They also propose an unsupervised hyperparameter selection method, CD-CV, that chooses kernel width and regularization by matching cross-moments between p and q over random test functions. Experiments on regression and classification under covariate shift compare to LSIF and a thresholded inverse KDE baseline; some commentary on KMM is provided.

Relation to prior work
The approach is closely related to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), which enforces Eq(Φ) = Ep(rΦ) in RKHS, and to LSIF/KLIEP that fit r by minimizing f-divergence or L2,p in a parametric basis. The key novelties here are: using a Fredholm operator viewpoint that unifies multiple losses and operators; explicit closed-form solutions via RKHS regularization; analysis on manifolds; and a simple unsupervised model selection criterion that avoids labeled data. The work connects to inverse problems and spectral regularization literatures.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulations (Eqs. 5/7/11) are standard RKHS Tikhonov problems with consistent empirical approximations to L2,p norms and kernel integral operators. The derivations of closed-form solutions are correct. The spectral/concentration analysis leads to nontrivial rates that depend on kernel bandwidth t, regularization λ, sample sizes n,m, and dimension d. The manifold extension is a welcome generalization.
- Theory: Bounds in Theorems 1 and 3 disentangle approximation (O(t) or O(t1−ε)) and estimation errors (λ, n, m). Corollaries provide rates such as O(n−1/(3.5+d/2)) for Type I and O(n−1/(4+5d/6)) for Type II after parameter tuning. These are plausible albeit not obviously minimax; a brief comparison to known minimax results (e.g., Nguyen et al. 2008) would help interpret tightness.
- Algorithms: Solutions require inverting n×n systems built from kernel matrices; this is computationally simpler and more stable than KMM’s QP, though still O(n3) without low-rank tricks.
- Experiments: The CD-CV criterion is evaluated on USPS and used in regression/classification on CPUsmall and Kin8nm under covariate shift. Comparisons include LSIF and TIKDE; the omission of KMM is partially justified for some settings, but for supervised reweighting tasks where only weights at training points are needed, KMM can be included fairly.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with helpful derivations and explicit formulas. Notation is heavy but consistent. The roles of k (integral operator) and kH (RKHS) are sometimes conflated in theory (set equal) but differ in algorithms; clarifying when they can differ would help. Pseudocode or a concise algorithm box for Type I/II would aid reproducibility. Proofs are deferred to the supplement.

Originality
Moderately high. While related to KMM/LSIF and inverse problems in RKHS, the Fredholm lens, the resulting family of estimators (including Type II for known q), and the manifold error analysis are original. The unsupervised CD-CV criterion is a simple, practical contribution.

Significance
Density ratio estimation is central to importance sampling and covariate shift. The FIRE family is flexible, has closed-form solutions, and comes with theory that covers manifold settings. CD-CV addresses a thorny practical issue. If broadly adopted, these methods could influence practical covariate shift correction and importance weighting. The empirical section is promising but limited in scope; larger-scale and more diverse benchmarks would strengthen claims.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation as an inverse problem, leading to simple closed-form kernel algorithms.
- Theoretical analysis with finite-sample bounds and rates in Rd and on manifolds.
- Practical unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) requiring only samples from p and q.
- Flexibility: multiple loss norms, ability to separate operator and regularizer kernels, Type II applicability when q is known up to scale.
- Discussion of relations to KMM and LSIF is thoughtful.

Weaknesses and questions
- No positivity or normalization constraints on r̂. Negative weights can arise, which is problematic for importance sampling. A projection to the positive cone or simple clipping could be discussed and analyzed.
- CD-CV lacks theoretical guarantees (e.g., consistency for hyperparameter selection) and its sensitivity to the choice of test function family U and number F is not fully quantified.
- Experimental scope is modest; comparisons to KMM are incomplete. For supervised reweighting tasks, KMM can be compared without out-of-sample extension.
- Computational complexity is O(n3); discussion of scalability (e.g., Nyström, random features, conjugate gradients) is missing.
- Theory assumes using the same Gaussian kernel for operator and RKHS; practical algorithms sometimes decouple them. Guidance on when mismatched kernels preserve guarantees would be useful.
- Boundary effects are only remarked upon; details of assumptions for domains with boundary are absent.
- The rates are unusual; a comparison to known lower bounds or to LSIF/KMM rates would contextualize performance.

Pro-accept arguments
- Conceptually neat unification via Fredholm operators with solid theoretical backing.
- Simple, closed-form estimators likely more stable and easier to implement than KMM’s QP.
- Manifold analysis is novel and potentially impactful.
- CD-CV is a practical contribution for unsupervised hyperparameter tuning.

Pro-reject arguments
- Limited empirical validation and incomplete baselines weaken the practical impact.
- Lack of non-negativity constraints could hinder use in importance sampling.
- Scalability concerns and absent discussion of approximations for large n.
- Theoretical guarantees for CD-CV are missing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Enforce non-negativity and, if desired, normalization of r̂, and assess impact on theory and practice.
- Provide more comprehensive experiments, including KMM where applicable, and larger-scale datasets; report runtime and memory.
- Analyze or at least empirically study the sensitivity of CD-CV to U and F; relate it more directly to importance-weighted cross-validation (Sugiyama et al., 2007).
- Discuss kernel mismatch (k vs. kH) and provide practical guidance for choosing them.
- Add algorithmic accelerations (Nyström, random features) and iterative solvers with preconditioning.
- Clarify boundary conditions and assumptions for domains with boundary.
- Compare derived rates to minimax benchmarks and to existing methods.

Overall recommendation
The paper offers a principled and elegant framework with theory and practical utility. With stronger empirical validation and minor practical refinements (positivity, scalability), it could be a strong contribution. I lean toward a weak accept.
