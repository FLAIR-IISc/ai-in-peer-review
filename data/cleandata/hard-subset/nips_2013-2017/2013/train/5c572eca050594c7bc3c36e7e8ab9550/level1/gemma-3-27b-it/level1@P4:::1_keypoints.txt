- Reformulates density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation and proposes FIRE algorithms based on Tikhonov regularization in RKHS.
- Provides theoretical analysis including concentration bounds and convergence rates for Euclidean and manifold settings.
- Introduces cross-density cross-validation (CD-CV) as a model selection method for unsupervised settings.
- Novelty: connects density ratio estimation to Fredholm theory and spectral analysis, offering a new analytical perspective.
- FIRE framework: flexible and implementable within kernel learning, allowing different norms and regularization choices.
- Theoretical rigor: detailed, complex analysis attempting to give rigorous guarantees.
- Practical contribution: CD-CV addresses model selection challenges in unsupervised learning.
- Good positioning: clearly relates to and contrasts with existing methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE).
- Clarity issue: dense mathematical notation and explanations; the transition from theory to algorithms is not explicit enough.
- Experiments: evaluation lacks conclusive evidence of FIRE’s superiority and needs more thorough comparisons, tuning details, and statistical analysis.
- CD-CV evaluation: choice of test functions and their impact on performance are not fully explored.
- Computational complexity: paper omits explicit discussion of runtime and potential bottlenecks from matrix inversions.
- Assumptions: reliance on smoothness and Gaussian kernels may limit real-world applicability; robustness to assumption violations is not studied.
- Type II setting: theoretical results contain caveats tied to kernel and function properties, limiting practicality.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, strong theoretical foundation, flexible framework, and useful CD-CV method.
- Arguments against acceptance: clarity issues, unconvincing experimental validation, missing complexity discussion, and restrictive assumptions.
- Overall recommendation: lean toward acceptance with minor revisions contingent on improved clarity, stronger experiments (including scalability and comparisons), and acknowledgment of theoretical limitations.
