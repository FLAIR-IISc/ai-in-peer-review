This paper recasts density-ratio estimation q/p as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solves it via Tikhonov regularization in RKHS, yielding a family of closed-form kernel algorithms (FIRE). Two settings are treated: Type I, which leverages samples from both p and q to match Kp(q/p) to Kq1; and Type II, which approximates an identity via a δ-kernel to match Kt,p(q/p) to q when q(x) is known at p-sampled points. The authors derive explicit solutions via the representer theorem, propose an unsupervised model-selection criterion (CD-CV) based on cross-density moment matching, and provide nonasymptotic bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and smooth manifolds. Experiments compare against KMM (conceptually), LSIF, and a KDE baseline, and demonstrate utility under covariate shift. The work connects to prior NIPS and related literature on KMM (Gretton et al.), LSIF/KLIEP (Sugiyama et al.), operator-theoretic learning and inverse problems (Smola & Schölkopf; De Vito et al.), and covariate shift/IWCV (Shimodaira; Sugiyama et al.).

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: a principled operator-theoretic view that subsumes and clarifies connections to KMM and least-squares ratio methods; explicit out-of-sample estimators.
- Technical depth: finite-sample concentration and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels, including manifold support, are valuable and nontrivial.
- Practicality: simple closed-form solvers, flexibility in losses (L2,p, mixed p/q, RKHS), and an unsupervised CD-CV selection heuristic tied to the problem’s moment identities.
- Empirical evidence: competitive performance and apparent stability advantages over unregularized KMM and TIKDE; computationally straightforward.

Weaknesses
- Assumptions and scope: rates rely on Gaussian δ-kernels and smoothness (Sobolev) assumptions; Type II incurs O(t) bias and requires q(xi), which limits applicability.
- Lack of constraints: FIRE does not enforce nonnegativity or normalization of q/p; practical impact and possible rectifications (e.g., positivity penalties or clipping) are not analyzed.
- Empirics: datasets are modest; comparisons omit strong modern baselines (e.g., uLSIF/RuLSIF variants) and do not probe high-dimensional or heavy-tailed shifts; ablations on CD-CV robustness are limited.
- Clarity: notation (K vs KH), equation numbering/typos, and computational complexity discussion (matrix inversions, scalability, Nyström/CG options) could be improved; brief pseudocode would aid reproducibility.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Generally sound with clear derivations and stated assumptions; proofs relegated to supplement.
- Clarity: Good overall, but could be streamlined and tightened as noted.
- Originality: High; the Fredholm framing and manifold-rate analysis are novel and insightful.
- Significance: Solid for covariate shift and density-ratio estimation; likely to influence kernel-based importance weighting.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, unifying framework with theory and algorithms.
- Manifold-aware rates and clear operator connections to prior work.
- Practical model selection (CD-CV) and simple implementations.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing constraints/analysis on ratio positivity/normalization.
- Limited empirical breadth and scalability discussion.
- Type II applicability narrower in practice.

Overall: Weak accept.
