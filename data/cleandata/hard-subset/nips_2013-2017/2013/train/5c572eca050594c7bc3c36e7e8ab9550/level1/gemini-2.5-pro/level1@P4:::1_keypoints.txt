- Novel formulation: density-ratio estimation is cast as solving a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, linking the problem to operator theory and inverse problems.  
- Two settings: Type I uses samples from p and q solving K_p f = K_q 1; Type II uses samples from p and known q solving K_{t,p} f ≈ q with delta-like kernel.  
- Regularization and estimator: Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS yields the FIRE family of estimators with closed-form kernel-matrix solutions via the representer theorem.  
- Flexibility: Framework accommodates multiple loss functions (L2,p, L2,q, RKHS norms) and yields standard kernel-method formulas.  
- Connections to prior work: Relates explicitly to KMM (as a special Type I case without regularization), LSIF, KLIEP, and the view of learning as operator inversion.  
- Originality and principle: The Fredholm inverse perspective is a significant, theoretically grounded conceptual contribution that unifies approaches.  
- Theoretical strength: Provides detailed concentration bounds and convergence rates (including for Gaussian kernels) and extends analysis to data on manifolds (Theorems 1/3, Corollaries 2/4).  
- Practical model selection: Proposes Cross-Density Cross-Validation (CD-CV), an unsupervised, importance-sampling–based method for hyperparameter tuning.  
- Clarity and organization: Paper is well-written, clearly differentiates Type I/II, explains equations, and articulates related work.  
- Limited experiments: Empirical evaluation uses low-dimensional datasets, leaving open how performance scales to high dimensions where rates depend on d.  
- Missing KMM comparison: KMM is not included in quantitative experiments despite being a closely related baseline, which weakens empirical claims.  
- Baseline details lacking: Selection procedure for TIKDE’s threshold α is insufficiently described, making comparisons hard to assess.  
- Rate discussion insufficient: Paper presents convergence rates (notably slow rates in Type II) but lacks discussion of tightness or comparison to minimax results.  
- Computational scalability: Estimators require inverting n×n matrices (O(n^3)), and the paper does not adequately address approximations or scalability for large n.  
- Acceptance arguments: Novel, principled framework with strong theoretical guarantees, manifold results, practical CD-CV selection, and implementable algorithms justify acceptance.  
- Rejection arguments: Limited empirical scope (esp. high-dimensional and KMM comparison) and incomplete discussion of rate implications and computational scaling argue against acceptance.
