- Reframes density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation (Type I and Type II identities).
- Proposes FIRE: Tikhonov-regularized RKHS solutions yielding closed-form kernel expansions.
- Core contributions: theoretical connection to integral equations, FIRE algorithms, convergence bounds (Rd and manifolds), CD-CV unsupervised model selection, and strong experiments.
- Shows KMM is a special case of Type I; FIRE adds explicit regularization and natural out-of-sample extension.
- Relates to LSIF/KLIEP: CD-CV is conceptually linked to LSIF but used for validating nonparametric estimates rather than fitting parametric models.
- Places the work within broader ML inverse-problem literature, leveraging classical analysis tools.
- Strength: novel, principled, and elegant formulation connecting classical analysis to a practical ML problem.
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis with explicit concentration bounds and rates.
- Strength: practical CD-CV method for unsupervised hyperparameter selection based on importance-sampling identity.
- Strength: flexible, simple algorithms with implementable closed-form kernel-matrix solutions.
- Weakness: computational cost O(n^3) time and O(n^2) memory; scalability to large datasets is not addressed.
- Weakness: theoretical analysis restricted to the Gaussian kernel; extension to other kernels is not discussed.
- Weakness: choice of test functions for CD-CV (random linear functions/half-spaces) is ad-hoc and lacks formal justification.
- Weakness: no direct experimental comparison to KMM despite close conceptual relationship.
- Overall assessment: high-quality, well-motivated, and well-supported work; recommend acceptance with minor scope caveats.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel formulation, principled/flexible algorithms, strong theory, effective unsupervised model selection, convincing experiments.
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability concerns and missing KMM empirical comparison.
