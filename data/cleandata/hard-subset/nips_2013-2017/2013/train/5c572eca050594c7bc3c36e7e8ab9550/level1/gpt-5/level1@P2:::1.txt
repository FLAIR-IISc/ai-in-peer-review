Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes to estimate the density ratio r(x) = q(x)/p(x) by recasting it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, and to solve it via Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS. Two formulations are given: (Type I) when samples from both p and q are available, solving Kp r ≈ Kq 1; and (Type II) when a δ-family kernel is used so Kt,p r ≈ q, applicable when q is known pointwise at the p-sample. By empirical approximation of integral operators and L2 norms, representer theorems yield closed-form kernel solutions (FIRE). The paper analyzes concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on smooth manifolds, and proposes an unsupervised model selection procedure (CD-CV) based on testing importance-sampling identities with random test functions. Experiments compare to LSIF/KLIEP-style approaches and a thresholded KDE ratio baseline, and show applications to covariate shift regression/classification.

This connects naturally to: Kernel Mean Matching (KMM), which also leverages Eq[Φ(x)] under covariate shift but without explicit regularization of the weight function; LSIF/KLIEP, which minimize L2,p or KL discrepancies in a parametric basis; inverse-problem views of kernel methods (e.g., De Vito et al.; Smola & Schölkopf); and kernel conditional mean embeddings. The proposed Fredholm framing provides a clean operator-theoretic lens and unifies variants within RKHS regularization.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria

Quality (technical soundness, support of claims)
- The formulations are well-posed inverse problems with standard Tikhonov regularization and clear empirical estimators. The closed-form solutions follow from the representer theorem.
- Theoretical results give finite-sample high-probability L2,p error bounds with explicit dependence on kernel bandwidth t, regularization λ, sample sizes, and dimension, for both Euclidean domains and manifolds. The bias terms O(t) (or t1−ε) for δ-kernels are correctly identified and propagated.
- However, the rates in the corollaries appear relatively weak compared to minimax results for likelihood ratio estimation (e.g., Nguyen et al. 2007), and the dependence on d through td/2 exposes the curse of dimensionality. The paper could better discuss optimal choices of (t, λ) to achieve the stated rates and whether the rates are sharp under the given smoothness.
- The algorithms do not enforce positivity or normalization of r, unlike KMM/LSIF variants that include β ≥ 0 and Ep[β]=1 constraints. Potential negative weights and variance explosion in importance sampling are not analyzed; clipping or constraints could improve stability.
- Experimental evaluation demonstrates feasibility and proposes a sensible unsupervised CV proxy (CD-CV), but quantitative details are limited in the excerpt: effect sizes, variances, runtimes, and comparisons to KMM (with an out-of-sample extension workaround) are not fully developed. Still, the design is coherent.

Clarity (writing, organization)
- The paper is generally well organized: problem setup, algorithms, theory, model selection, experiments. The operator notation is heavy but standard; some matrices (Kp,p, KH, etc.) are clearly defined.
- Suggestions: streamline notation in Section 2; summarize the different loss/regularizer pairings (Type I L2,p and RKHS-loss variants, γ-combinations) with a compact list. Clarify assumptions (e.g., boundedness, Sobolev class) upfront. In the theory section, explicitly state recommended scaling of t and λ. In experiments, provide more comprehensive tables (numbers, standard errors, runtimes, memory).

Originality (novelty, relation to prior work)
- Framing density ratio estimation explicitly as a Fredholm inverse problem and solving with RKHS Tikhonov is a neat and, to my knowledge, not previously articulated perspective in this form; it ties KMM as a special case and yields a family of closed-form algorithms. The Type II formulation (q known pointwise; only p-sample available) is practically relevant and less explored in prior ratio-estimation work.
- The manifold analysis and unsupervised CD-CV selection add novel components. That said, the core ingredients (operator equations, RKHS regularization) build on established ideas, so the overall novelty is moderate.

Significance (impact, usefulness)
- Density ratio estimation is central to covariate shift and importance sampling; a principled, regularized, out-of-sample-capable approach is valuable. The operator view may facilitate further analysis and extensions (e.g., spectral regularization, other losses, constraints).
- The manifold results and unsupervised model selection are potentially impactful in practice.
- Limitations include computational scaling O(n^3) in naive form, sensitivity to high dimension and bandwidth selection, and lack of constraints on r.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clean, unified operator-theoretic formulation with simple closed-form kernel solutions.
- Provides nontrivial theory: finite-sample bounds and convergence rates on Rd and manifolds.
- Addresses an important problem with practical implications for covariate shift and IS.
- CD-CV is a practical, unsupervised model selection strategy grounded in test-function identities.
- Clarifies relation to KMM and LSIF; offers out-of-sample extension lacking in KMM.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Novelty is incremental relative to existing RKHS inverse-problem and ratio-estimation literatures; KMM can be seen as a special case.
- No enforcement of nonnegativity/normalization; potential instability of importance weights not addressed theoretically.
- Reported convergence rates are relatively weak; optimal parameter selection and sharpness not fully discussed.
- Experimental evaluation could be stronger: broader baselines (e.g., uLSIF/RuLSIF), fuller comparisons to KMM with practical out-of-sample schemes, runtime/memory analysis, and downstream impact metrics.
- Dependence on δ-kernel approximation introduces bias; guidance on balancing (t, λ) is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Incorporate constraints (β ≥ 0, Ep[β] ≈ 1) or analyze clipped versions; study variance control of weights.
- Provide explicit prescriptions for choosing t and λ to attain the theoretical rates; discuss adaptivity.
- Expand experiments with uLSIF/RuLSIF and KLIEP, include KMM with Nystrom/feature-based out-of-sample extension; report runtimes and memory.
- Analyze sensitivity to kernel mismatch (operator kernel vs RKHS kernel) and to γ in the mixed L2 loss.
- Discuss scalability (e.g., low-rank approximations) and provide ablations of CD-CV (linear vs half-space vs kernel test functions).

Overall assessment
- Quality: good, with solid algorithms and meaningful theory, but with gaps in constraints and experimental depth.
- Clarity: good, though notation-heavy; could be tightened.
- Originality: moderate.
- Significance: moderate to high for covariate shift and IS; the operator framing may influence future work.

On balance, I view this as a solid contribution with a principled framework and useful insights, meriting consideration if the committee values theoretically grounded methods for density ratio estimation and covariate shift.
