

# Peer Review: "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"

This paper presents a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating the problem as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, which is then solved using regularization techniques from the theory of inverse problems. The authors introduce the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) family of algorithms, providing both theoretical analysis and practical implementation details. The work connects classical mathematical analysis to modern machine learning problems, offering a unified perspective on density ratio estimation.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) a reformulation of density ratio estimation as a classical inverse problem; (2) a principled framework using RKHS regularization; (3) theoretical convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings; and (4) a novel unsupervised model selection method called CD-CV. The authors relate their work to Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9], noting that KMM can be viewed as a special case of their Type I formulation with an RKHS norm but without regularization. They also connect to LSIF [10] and KLIEP [23], noting similarities in their unsupervised parameter selection approach.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical Novelty:** The connection between density ratio estimation and Fredholm equations is fresh and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis provides meaningful convergence rates for both Type I and Type II settings in Euclidean and manifold domains, filling a gap in the literature. The error bounds in Theorems 1 and 3 are particularly strong, with clear dependence on the kernel bandwidth and regularization parameters.

2. **Practical Contribution:** The FIRE algorithms are simple to implement and provide a unified framework that encompasses several existing methods as special cases. The CD-CV model selection method is particularly valuable, as unsupervised parameter tuning for density ratio estimation is notoriously difficult.

3. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate competitive performance against state-of-the-art methods (KMM, LSIF, TIKDE) across multiple datasets and tasks (regression, classification). The use of diverse test functions for CD-CV (linear, half-space, kernel-based) is well-justified and shows the robustness of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Theoretical Comparison:** While the paper provides strong theoretical results, it doesn't clearly articulate how these rates compare to existing methods. For instance, the convergence rates in Corollary 2 for Type I setting (O(n^{-1/(3.5+d/2)}) for Euclidean domains) should be compared to minimax rates established in [14] to better establish the theoretical contribution.

2. **Manifold Implementation Details:** The paper mentions analysis for d-dimensional sub-manifolds but doesn't explain how this affects practical implementation. The manifold setting is important for real-world data, but the paper lacks discussion of how to estimate the manifold structure from data when it's unknown.

3. **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of computational complexity, which is crucial for understanding the practical scalability of the method. For instance, the matrix inversion in Equation 8 has O(n^3) complexity, which could be prohibitive for large datasets.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. The connection between classical inverse problem theory and modern machine learning is well-executed. The authors appropriately discuss the limitations of their approach, such as the Type II setting's error term depending on the kernel.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear motivation, precise mathematical formulations, and logical flow. The connection between the Fredholm equation and density ratio estimation is well-explained, and the algorithms are described with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality:** The core idea of viewing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation is novel. While related to works like [9] and [10], the paper provides a distinct theoretical framework that generalizes previous approaches. The CD-CV method represents a significant practical innovation.

**Significance:** Density ratio estimation is a fundamental problem with applications in transfer learning, covariate shift adaptation, and importance sampling. This paper advances the state-of-the-art by providing a unified theoretical framework and practical algorithms with strong empirical performance. The work has clear potential for impact in both theoretical and applied machine learning.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**
*Pro:*
- Novel theoretical connection between classical inverse problems and density ratio estimation
- Comprehensive theoretical analysis with convergence rates for multiple settings
- Practical, easy-to-implement algorithms with strong empirical results
- Introduction of CD-CV, a valuable unsupervised model selection technique

*Con:*
- Limited discussion of computational complexity
- Insufficient theoretical comparison to existing minimax rates
- Limited exploration of practical implementation challenges for the manifold setting

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical novelty is compelling, and the practical algorithms are well-motivated and validated. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's overall quality, which meets NIPS' standards for technical soundness, clarity, and significance. I recommend acceptance.
