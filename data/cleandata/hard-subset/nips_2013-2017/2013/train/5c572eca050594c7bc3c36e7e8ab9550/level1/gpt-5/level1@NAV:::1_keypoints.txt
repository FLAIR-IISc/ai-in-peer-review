- Recasts density-ratio estimation q/p as a Fredholm integral equation solved by Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, producing the FIRE family of kernel algorithms.  
- Treats two settings: Type I uses samples from both p and q to match K_p(q/p) to K_q 1; Type II uses a δ-kernel to approximate the identity when q(x) is known at p-sampled points.  
- Derives explicit closed-form solutions via the representer theorem and provides out-of-sample estimators.  
- Proposes CD-CV, an unsupervised model-selection criterion based on cross-density moment matching.  
- Proves nonasymptotic concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on R^d and on smooth manifolds.  
- Empirical comparisons to KMM, LSIF, and a KDE baseline show competitive performance and stability under covariate shift.  
- Conceptual unification: offers an operator-theoretic view that clarifies connections to KMM and least-squares ratio methods.  
- Technical depth: finite-sample and manifold-aware rates are substantial and nontrivial contributions.  
- Practicality: simple closed-form solvers, loss flexibility (L2,p, mixed p/q, RKHS), and a problem-tied unsupervised model-selection heuristic.  
- Empirical practicality: appears computationally straightforward and more stable than unregularized KMM and TIKDE.  
- Assumptions and scope: theoretical rates rely on Gaussian δ-kernels and Sobolev smoothness; Type II incurs O(t) bias and requires q(x_i) values.  
- Missing constraints: FIRE does not enforce nonnegativity or normalization of q/p, and remedies (e.g., positivity penalties/clipping) are not analyzed.  
- Empirics limitations: datasets are modest, omit strong modern baselines (uLSIF/RuLSIF), and do not test high-dimensional or heavy-tailed shifts; CD-CV robustness ablations are limited.  
- Clarity and reproducibility issues: confusing notation (K vs K_H), equation numbering/typos, and weak discussion of computational complexity and scalability (matrix inversions, Nyström/CG); lacking pseudocode.  
- Assessment — Quality: generally sound with clear derivations (proofs in supplement).  
- Assessment — Clarity: overall good but could be streamlined and tightened.  
- Assessment — Originality: high; Fredholm framing and manifold-rate analysis are novel.  
- Assessment — Significance: solid contribution to covariate shift and density-ratio estimation; likely to influence kernel-based importance weighting.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled unifying framework with theory and algorithms, manifold-aware rates, and practical model selection plus simple implementations.  
- Cons for acceptance: no analysis of ratio positivity/normalization, limited empirical breadth and scalability discussion, and narrower practical applicability of Type II.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept.
