This paper addresses the important problem of estimating the density ratio q/p, a key component in tasks like importance sampling and covariate shift adaptation. The central and novel contribution is the reformulation of this estimation problem as a classical Fredholm integral equation of the first kind. By applying Tikhonov regularization within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework to this integral equation, the authors derive a family of simple and flexible algorithms, named FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator). This approach provides a principled theoretical underpinning for density ratio estimation, connecting it to the classical analysis of inverse problems. The paper builds upon and provides a broader context for prior work such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and LSIF, positioning them within this new framework while also highlighting the stability benefits of explicit regularization.

The paper has several significant strengths.
**Originality and Significance:** The formulation of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm inverse problem is elegant and insightful. It establishes a strong connection between a modern machine learning problem and a well-understood area of applied mathematics, which is a valuable conceptual contribution that could inspire future work.
**Quality:** The work is technically sound. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis, deriving concentration bounds and convergence rates for their algorithm on both Euclidean spaces and manifolds. Furthermore, they propose a practical and well-motivated unsupervised method for model selection, CD-CV, which addresses a key challenge in this domain.
**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured, making the core ideas and the derivation of the algorithms accessible. The distinction between the Type I and Type II settings is helpful for understanding the method's applicability.

The paper also has minor weaknesses that could be addressed.
**Quality:** While the theoretical results are a major strength, the main text could benefit from more intuition regarding the error bounds and the interplay between the regularization parameter λ, the kernel width t, and the sample sizes. The experimental evaluation is solid, but it could be further strengthened by including experiments on synthetic high-dimensional data where the ground-truth ratio is known, allowing for a more direct assessment of accuracy.
**Originality:** While the formulation is novel, the resulting algorithmic solutions are variants of kernel ridge regression. This is not a major flaw, as the novelty lies in the derivation and framework, but it is worth noting.

In summary, this paper presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field. The novel formulation, supported by strong theoretical analysis and a practical model selection procedure, advances the state of the art in density ratio estimation. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, and this work is likely to be influential for both researchers and practitioners.
