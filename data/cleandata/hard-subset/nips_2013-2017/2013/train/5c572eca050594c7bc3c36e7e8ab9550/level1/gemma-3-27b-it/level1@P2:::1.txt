This paper proposes a novel framework for density ratio estimation by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation and solving it using regularization techniques from the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) literature. The resulting algorithms, FIRE, are presented along with theoretical analysis and experimental validation.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a common problem in machine learning and statistics (importance sampling, covariate shift). The connection to Fredholm equations is interesting and provides a solid mathematical foundation.  The authors correctly position their work relative to recent advances like Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) and Least Squares Importance Sampling (LSIF), highlighting the differences and advantages of their approach. The theoretical analysis, providing concentration bounds and convergence rates, is a significant strength, particularly for the Gaussian kernel and distributions on manifolds. The introduction of the CD-CV model selection technique is a valuable contribution, offering an unsupervised approach to parameter tuning. The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance.

However, there are areas for improvement. While the mathematical exposition is generally clear, some parts are dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The discussion of the different FIRE variants (Type I vs. Type II) and their respective advantages and disadvantages could be more concise and focused. The experimental section feels somewhat fragmented, lacking a cohesive narrative. A more thorough comparison with existing methods, potentially including a more detailed ablation study of the FIRE variants, would strengthen the empirical evaluation.  The connection to existing work on integral equations and regularization is acknowledged but could be more deeply explored. For example, relating the convergence rates to known results from integral equation theory could be valuable. The notation can be a bit heavy and could be simplified in places.  The dependence on the choice of kernel (specifically Gaussian) for the theoretical analysis is a limitation, and exploring other kernels or providing more general theoretical results would be beneficial. The paper also lacks a discussion of computational complexity of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Framing density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation provides a fresh perspective.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** Rigorous analysis with concentration bounds and convergence rates.
*   **Practicality:** FIRE algorithms are relatively simple to implement.
*   **Unsupervised Model Selection:** CD-CV offers a convenient way to tune parameters.
*   **Competitive Performance:** Empirical results demonstrate strong performance compared to existing methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Density of Mathematical Exposition:** Some sections are challenging to follow without a strong background in functional analysis.
*   **Fragmented Experimental Evaluation:**  The experimental section could be more cohesive and provide more insights.
*   **Kernel Dependence:** Theoretical results are primarily focused on the Gaussian kernel.
*   **Lack of Complexity Analysis:** Computational costs are not discussed.
*   **Notation:** Some notation is heavy and could be simplified.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and mathematically grounded approach to a challenging problem.
*   The theoretical analysis is a significant contribution, providing guarantees on the performance of the proposed algorithms.
*   The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of the FIRE framework.
*   The unsupervised model selection technique is a valuable addition.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper could benefit from improved clarity and conciseness in certain sections.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more thorough and insightful.
*   The reliance on the Gaussian kernel limits the generality of the theoretical results.
*   The lack of computational complexity analysis is a drawback.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, combined with the rigorous theoretical analysis and promising experimental results, makes it worthy of acceptance. While there are areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly focusing on clarifying the presentation, strengthening the experimental evaluation, and discussing the computational aspects of the proposed methods.
