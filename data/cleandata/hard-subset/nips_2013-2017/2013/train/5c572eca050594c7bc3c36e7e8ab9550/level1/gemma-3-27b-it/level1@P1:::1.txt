This paper proposes a novel approach to density ratio estimation (DRE) by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation and solving it using regularization techniques from the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The resulting algorithms, dubbed FIRE, are presented with theoretical analysis (concentration bounds and convergence rates) and experimental evaluation, including a novel unsupervised model selection method (CD-CV).

The core idea of framing DRE as an inverse problem is compelling and connects the problem to a well-studied area of mathematics, allowing for the application of powerful analytical tools. The paper clearly articulates the connection to importance sampling and covariate shift, placing the work in a relevant context.  The authors correctly identify Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) as the closest related work and clearly differentiate FIRE from KMM, highlighting the benefits of explicit regularization.  The theoretical analysis, providing convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean and manifold domains, is a significant contribution. The introduction of CD-CV is a valuable addition, addressing the practical challenge of model selection in unsupervised settings.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The Fredholm equation formulation of DRE is a novel and insightful approach.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The rigorous theoretical analysis, including concentration bounds and convergence rates, provides a strong justification for the method.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the mathematical concepts and algorithmic details. The notation is consistent.
* **Completeness:** The paper includes a thorough discussion of related work, algorithm details, theoretical results, experimental setup, and comparisons with existing methods.
* **Practical Contribution:** The FIRE algorithms and the CD-CV method offer practical tools for DRE.
* **Flexibility:** The framework allows for different norms and loss functions, providing flexibility in adapting the method to specific applications.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the algorithms are presented as simple to implement, the matrix inversions involved in solving the regularized problems (see Eq. 8 and 10) can be computationally expensive for large datasets. The paper doesn’t thoroughly discuss scalability.
* **Kernel Choice:** The theoretical analysis focuses on the Gaussian kernel. The performance of FIRE with other kernels is not explored in detail, and the sensitivity to kernel choice is not adequately addressed.  The impact of kernel parameter (bandwidth) selection, beyond the range considered in experiments, is also unclear.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, primarily focuses on relatively small datasets.  Demonstrating the scalability and performance on larger, more complex datasets would strengthen the claims.
* **CD-CV Details:** While CD-CV is introduced, more details about its sensitivity to the choice of test functions `U` would be helpful. A more in-depth analysis of the properties of the best test functions would be beneficial.
* **Limited Discussion on Type II Limitations:** The Type II setting, while offering advantages, has a strong assumption about knowing `q` at sample points. This limitation should be emphasized more explicitly.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem (density ratio estimation).
* The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of the proposed algorithms.
* The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance compared to existing methods.
* The introduction of CD-CV is a practical contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the algorithms could limit their applicability to large-scale datasets.
* The limited exploration of different kernels and parameter settings may not fully reveal the potential and limitations of the method.
* The experiments on small datasets may not fully demonstrate the scalability of the proposed approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of density ratio estimation. The novel formulation, rigorous theoretical analysis, and practical algorithms make it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. While some concerns remain regarding computational complexity and scalability, the strengths of the paper outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

*  Discuss the computational complexity of the algorithms and potential strategies for improving scalability (e.g., using approximation techniques for matrix inversion).
*  Explore the performance of FIRE with other kernels beyond the Gaussian kernel.
*  Perform experiments on larger and more complex datasets to demonstrate scalability.
*  Provide a more detailed analysis of the CD-CV method, including sensitivity analysis to the choice of test functions.
*  Explicitly discuss the limitations of the Type II setting.
