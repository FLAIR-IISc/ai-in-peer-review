- Summary: Casts density-ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation solved by Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS, yielding closed-form kernel estimators (FIRE) for two formulations (Type I using samples from p and q; Type II using known/evaluable q).
- Summary: Derives concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Rd and on compact manifolds, and proposes CD-CV, an unsupervised hyperparameter selection by matching cross-moments.
- Relation to prior work: Connects closely to KMM and LSIF/KLIEP but frames the problem as an inverse problem, unifying losses/operators and linking to spectral regularization literatures.
- Novelty: Main contributions are the Fredholm/operator viewpoint, closed-form RKHS Tikhonov estimators (including Type II), manifold error analysis, and a practical unsupervised model selection criterion.
- Technical soundness: Formulations and closed-form derivations are correct; spectral/concentration analysis yields nontrivial rates depending on bandwidth, regularization, and sample sizes.
- Theory: Theorems separate approximation (O(t) or O(t1−ε)) and estimation errors; corollaries give concrete rates (e.g., O(n−1/(3.5+d/2)) for Type I) though comparison to minimax results is lacking.
- Algorithms: FIRE requires solving n×n linear systems (simpler and more stable than KMM QP) but remains O(n3) without low-rank/approximation tricks.
- Experiments: CD-CV evaluated on USPS and used in regression/classification under covariate shift with comparisons to LSIF and TIKDE; empirical scope is limited and KMM comparisons are incomplete.
- Clarity: Paper is generally clear with consistent notation, but conflation of operator kernel k and RKHS kernel kH and lack of pseudocode hinder reproducibility.
- Originality: Moderately high—builds on existing ideas but contributes a useful new perspective, manifold analysis, and a simple unsupervised CV method.
- Significance: Offers flexible, closed-form estimators with theory for manifold settings and a practical hyperparameter tuning approach, potentially impactful for covariate shift correction.
- Strength: Principled inverse-problem formulation leading to simple, closed-form kernel algorithms and finite-sample bounds in Euclidean and manifold settings.
- Strength: Practical unsupervised model selection (CD-CV) that only needs samples from p and q.
- Strength: Flexibility to choose different loss norms and separate operator and regularizer kernels; Type II handles known q up to scale.
- Weakness: No enforcement of non-negativity or normalization of estimated r̂, which can produce negative weights harmful for importance sampling.
- Weakness: CD-CV has no theoretical guarantees and its sensitivity to test function choices (U, F) is unexplored.
- Weakness: Experimental validation is modest and lacks comprehensive baselines (notably KMM in supervised reweighting settings); scalability experiments are missing.
- Weakness: Scalability concerns unaddressed—no discussion of Nyström, random features, or iterative solvers for large n.
- Weakness: Theory assumes same Gaussian kernel for operator and RKHS; the impact of kernel mismatch used in practice is unclear.
- Weakness: Boundary conditions and domain-boundary assumptions are only briefly mentioned and not fully detailed.
- Weakness: Obtained convergence rates are unusual and not compared to known minimax lower bounds or rates for LSIF/KMM.
- Pro-accept: Elegant unification via Fredholm operators with solid theoretical backing, practical closed-form estimators, novel manifold analysis, and useful CD-CV criterion.
- Pro-reject: Limited empirical validation, lack of non-negativity constraints, scalability issues, and missing theoretical guarantees for CD-CV weaken practical impact.
- Suggestions: Enforce/clamp positivity and normalization and analyze effects; expand experiments to include KMM and larger datasets with runtime/memory; study CD-CV sensitivity to U and F; discuss kernel mismatch and provide guidance; add scalable approximations and iterative solvers; clarify boundary assumptions and compare rates to minimax benchmarks.
- Overall recommendation: Principled and elegant work with solid theory and practical potential but needs stronger empirical validation and practical refinements; reviewer leans toward weak accept.
