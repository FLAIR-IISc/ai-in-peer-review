- Reformulates density ratio estimation as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind and solves it via inverse-problem regularization.  
- Introduces the FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) family with RKHS regularization and practical algorithms.  
- Provides theoretical convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean and manifold settings.  
- Proposes CD-CV, a novel unsupervised model selection method for density ratio estimation.  
- Connects and generalizes existing methods: KMM as a special case of Type I without regularization, and relations to LSIF and KLIEP.  
- Theoretical novelty: links density ratio estimation to Fredholm equations and gives strong error bounds (Theorems 1 and 3).  
- Practical contribution: FIRE algorithms are simple to implement and unify several existing approaches.  
- CD-CV addresses the difficult problem of unsupervised parameter tuning and is practically valuable.  
- Experimental validation: competitive performance versus KMM, LSIF, TIKDE across regression and classification tasks.  
- Robustness shown by using diverse test functions (linear, half-space, kernel-based) in CD-CV.  
- Lacks clear comparison of derived convergence rates to existing minimax rates (e.g., [14]).  
- Manifold setting analysis is given theoretically but lacks guidance on estimating manifold structure in practice.  
- Computational complexity is not thoroughly analyzed; matrix inversion implies O(n^3) costs that may not scale.  
- Paper is technically sound with rigorous analysis and well-designed experiments.  
- Exceptionally clear writing, precise formulations, and reproducible algorithm descriptions.  
- Original idea: novel framing of density ratio estimation as a Fredholm equation, extending prior work.  
- CD-CV is a significant practical innovation for unsupervised model selection.  
- Significance: advances theory and practice for density ratio estimation with applications in transfer learning and covariate shift.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel theory, comprehensive analysis, practical algorithms, and strong empirical results.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited complexity analysis, insufficient theoretical benchmarking to minimax rates, and limited practical manifold implementation discussion.
