

**Review of "Inverse Density as an Inverse Problem: the Fredholm Equation Approach"**

This paper addresses the problem of estimating the density ratio q(x)/p(x) by reformulating it as a Fredholm integral equation of the first kind, an inverse problem solvable via regularization in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework. The authors develop a family of algorithms called FIRE (Fredholm Inverse Regularized Estimator) and propose a novel unsupervised model selection method called CD-CV (Cross-Density Cross-Validation). The paper provides theoretical analysis of convergence rates for Gaussian kernels on Euclidean spaces and manifolds, and demonstrates competitive performance on various datasets compared to existing methods like KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE.

The paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by connecting density ratio estimation to classical inverse problem theory. This approach differs from previous work such as Kernel Mean Matching (KMM) [9], which is framed as a Hilbert space optimization problem without the inverse problem perspective, and LSIF [10], which uses parametric linear families for density ratio estimation. The authors clearly position their work within the literature, noting how KMM can be viewed as a special case of their Type I formulation with a different optimization procedure.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides comprehensive convergence rate analysis for both Type I and Type II settings on Euclidean spaces and d-dimensional sub-manifolds. Theorems 1 and 3 establish error bounds with precise dependencies on kernel bandwidth, regularization parameter, and sample size, which is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of density ratio estimation. The analysis for manifold settings (Corollary 2) is particularly valuable as it extends the applicability of density ratio estimation to non-Euclidean domains.

2. **Novel Contributions:** The CD-CV model selection method is a major practical contribution that addresses a critical challenge in unsupervised learning. Unlike existing methods that require labeled data for validation, CD-CV leverages the unique properties of density ratio estimation to perform model selection without supervision. This is well-justified and demonstrated in the experiments.

3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-structured with clear definitions, theorems, and algorithm descriptions. The connection between the Fredholm equation formulation and existing methods is explicitly explained, making it accessible to readers familiar with kernel methods.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Practical Performance Justification:** While the theoretical analysis is strong, the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why the Fredholm equation approach leads to better practical performance beyond the theoretical guarantees. The experimental results show competitive performance, but the paper lacks a detailed comparison of computational complexity and scalability to larger datasets, which would be crucial for practitioners.

2. **Theoretical Accessibility:** The theoretical results, while rigorous, are quite technical and may be challenging for readers without a strong background in functional analysis. More intuitive explanations of the key insights (e.g., why the Fredholm approach provides better convergence rates) would enhance the paper's accessibility.

3. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper compares with KMM, LSIF, and TIKDE, but doesn't include a comparison with more recent density ratio estimation methods like those in [26] (which provides theoretical analysis of KMM) or the more recent work on density ratio estimation using neural networks. The paper should clarify how their method compares to the current state-of-the-art.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper establishes a novel theoretical framework connecting density ratio estimation to classical inverse problems, which is a significant conceptual contribution.
- The FIRE algorithms and CD-CV method are practical, well-motivated contributions that address important challenges in the field.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides new convergence rates for the Gaussian kernel setting, particularly for manifold domains.
- The experimental results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate competitive performance across multiple datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address computational complexity compared to existing approaches, which is a critical consideration for practical applications.
- The practical advantages of the Fredholm approach over simpler methods aren't clearly articulated beyond theoretical guarantees.
- The experimental section could be strengthened with more detailed comparisons of running times and scalability.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the literature on density ratio estimation. The theoretical framework is novel and rigorously analyzed, and the practical contributions (FIRE algorithms and CD-CV) address important challenges in the field. While the paper could benefit from more detailed discussion of computational complexity and comparison with the most recent methods, these are minor concerns that don't detract from the paper's overall quality and significance. The work is technically sound, well-written, and represents a meaningful advancement in the field.

**Recommendation: Accept**
