- Proposes estimating density ratio w(x)=q(x)/p(x) by casting it as a Fredholm integral equation and solving with Tikhonov regularization in an RKHS.
- Introduces two settings: Type I (both p and q samples available: Kp w = Kq 1) and Type II (q known pointwise via approximate identity Kt,p w ≈ q).
- Derives closed-form representer-theorem solutions for several losses (L2,p, mixed, RKHS norm), forming the FIRE estimator family.
- Provides theoretical concentration bounds and convergence rates for Gaussian kernels in Euclidean spaces and on compact smooth manifolds.
- Proposes CD-CV, an unsupervised model selection method validating importance-sampling identity on random test functions to choose kernel width and regularization.
- Empirical comparisons to LSIF and TIKDE show encouraging results and computational simplicity; limited discussion of KMM.
- Connects to prior work: relates to KMM, LSIF/KLIEP, operator-based kernel methods, and inverse problem regularization.
- Methodology is sound: well-posed convex RKHS objectives and clear separation of inverse problem and regularization.
- Type II formulation correctly accounts for kernel bias O(t); theoretical decomposition into approximation, regularization, and sample terms is sensible.
- Manifold analysis is nontrivial and valuable, but reported rates contain typographical/exponent formatting issues that need fixing.
- Does not compare obtained rates to known minimax rates (e.g., Nguyen et al. 2007), leaving optimality unclear.
- CD-CV is an appropriate validation criterion when both samples are available and testing with independent random functions is a robust stress test.
- Experimental evaluation is limited: missing comparisons to KMM and modern least-squares variants (KuLSIF/uLSIF), and lacks runtime/sensitivity analyses.
- Presentation is generally clear with explicit matrix forms, but notation is heavy/overloaded and contains minor typos (e.g., “Tiknonov”).
- Originality: unifying inverse-problem RKHS framing, explicit closed-form estimators, Type II setting, and manifold theory are novel and useful.
- Significance: valuable for importance weighting and covariate shift; manifold results could spur further work, but modest experiments temper immediate impact.
- Practical concern: estimator does not enforce positivity or normalization of w(x); implications and fixes are not analyzed.
- Key pros: principled formulation, closed-form estimators, theoretical guarantees, practical CD-CV selection, and simple computational profile.
- Key cons: ambiguous convergence exponents, no nonnegativity constraints, limited experimental baselines/analyses, and no guarantees for CD-CV.
- Suggestions: fix theoretical typos and compare to minimax rates, discuss/include positivity/normalization fixes, expand experiments (KMM/KuLSIF, runtime, robustness), provide guidance for choosing t and λ, and add pseudocode for reproducibility.
