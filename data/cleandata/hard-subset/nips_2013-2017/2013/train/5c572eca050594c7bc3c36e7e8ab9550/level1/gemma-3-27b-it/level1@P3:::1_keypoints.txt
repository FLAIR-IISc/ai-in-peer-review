- Tackles density ratio estimation by framing it as a Fredholm integral equation and applying operator-theoretic regularization.
- Well-motivated connection to importance sampling and covariate shift, providing a principled algorithmic framework.
- Proposes conceptually simple FIRE algorithms for density ratio estimation.
- Introduces CD-CV as a practical and novel method for model selection.
- Technically sound formulation that enables application of rich mathematical tools.
- Strong theoretical analysis with concentration bounds and convergence rates.
- Presentation of theoretical results is dense and could be more concise.
- Convergence-rate notation (e.g., Corollary 2 & 4) is unusual and obscures n-dependence.
- Experimental section is limited and needs stronger baselines and larger datasets.
- Authors acknowledge limitations such as kernel choice dependence.
- Mathematical density and heavy technical detail hinder accessibility for non-experts.
- Notation (multiple subscripts like f_Iλ,z) can be confusing and should be clarified.
- Relationship and practical differences between FIRE types I & II need clearer exposition.
- Novelty: reframing as a Fredholm inverse problem is original though building on kernel/regression work.
- Correctly situates relation to KMM and highlights benefits of the regularization term.
- Significance: provides valuable theoretical foundation for covariate shift and transfer learning applications.
- Practical impact hinges on algorithm scalability and real-world performance, which are not fully demonstrated.
- Strengths summarized: novel formulation, rigorous theory, principled FIRE framework, CD-CV, clear relevance to importance sampling/covariate shift.
- Weaknesses summarized: dense math, limited experiments, unclear convergence-rate presentation, kernel discussion lacking, no complexity analysis.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, useful CD-CV, well-presented core ideas.
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient experimental validation and potential inaccessibility due to mathematical density.
- Overall assessment: strong contribution deserving acceptance, recommended minor revisions to improve clarity, expand experiments, and discuss computational complexity.
