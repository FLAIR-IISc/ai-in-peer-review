- Paper proposes inference by projecting an Ising MRF onto the set of fast-mixing models (defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute edge strengths) and performing inference on that projected model.  
- Projection is constrained to preserve the original graph structure.  
- "Projection" is defined via divergences between Gibbs distributions (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL).  
- Euclidean projection is solved by dualizing and using L-BFGS-B; other divergences use a projected-gradient algorithm.  
- Reversed KL projection requires running Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model inside the projection procedure.  
- Experiments on small random models compare approximated marginals to true marginals and evaluate runtime vs accuracy.  
- Methods compared: proposed projections (all divergences), loopy BP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs sampling on the original model.  
- Results: proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy; for reasonable runtimes they also beat Gibbs on the original model; reversed KL performs best among proposed methods.  
- Request clarification: projected-gradient algorithm in Sec.5.1 has nested loops (inner L-BFGS-B) — specify stopping criteria for inner iterations.  
- Request clarification: x-axis in Figure 2 labeled "number of samples" is unclear (sampling used only for reversed KL); likely should be runtime — ask for absolute running times in seconds and runtime reporting for LBP, TRW, MF for fair comparison.  
- Suggestion: include experiments on larger models, e.g., using tractable random-field models from Boris Flach to test scalability.  
- Minor edits: replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T" (line 222) and use "subgradient" instead of "derivative" (line 226).  
- Overall assessment: interesting and convincing empirical results, but practical utility may be limited by high runtime (needs clarification in rebuttal).
