- Paper aims to speed up Gibbs mixing in strongly coupled pairwise Ising models by projecting parameters onto a nearby “fast-mixing” model that satisfies spectral bounds.  
- Proposes several projection methods that find parameters close to the original but weak enough to ensure rapid mixing.  
- Experiments on small Ising models show regimes where projected models give better marginal estimates within fixed computation time than variational methods and original-model Gibbs.  
- Technical development builds heavily on prior rapid-mixing results [4,8], though projecting onto the nearest rapidly-mixing model as an inference tool appears novel.  
- Main concern: unclear whether gains will scale to larger models—mixing-time comparisons may not generalize with problem size.  
- Computational cost of the projection step appears high and is not accounted for in timing comparisons, biasing results in favor of the proposed method.  
- Unclear generalizability beyond binary states since convergence bounds are harder for non-binary variables.  
- Clarity issues: several “standard results” are stated without references or proof sketches (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5 missing/partial proof).  
- Dependency matrix R needs discussion—its maximization makes tractability unclear and the paper should state/address this.  
- Section 4 is hard to follow: notation (g, M, Lambda) and Theorem 4 are introduced unclearly and require reorganization and explanation.  
- Notational inconsistency in Sec. 1: KL(q||p) usage flips conventionally used roles of p and q and should be fixed.  
- Experimental flaws: projection computation likely omitted from Figure 2 timings—results must include projection time or clarify.  
- Experiments lack larger/realistic models; suggested alternatives include symmetric models with tractable marginals or using long-chain sampling as ground truth.  
- Missing exploration of KL(θ||ψ) projection as a potential “best case” (junction tree could be used on toy models).  
- Mean-field is noted as reverse-KL special case but the observed large error gap versus reverse-KL projection is unexplained and needs discussion.  
- Overall: elegant idea leveraging recent mixing bounds, but experimental comparisons and limited applicability raise significant concerns.
