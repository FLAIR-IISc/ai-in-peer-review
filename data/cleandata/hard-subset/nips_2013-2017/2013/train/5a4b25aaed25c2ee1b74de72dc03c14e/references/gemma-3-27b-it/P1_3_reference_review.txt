## NIPS Review: Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing

**Summary:**

This paper explores a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically the Ising model. Instead of approximating the distribution directly (e.g., via mean-field), the authors propose *projecting* intractable parameters onto a set guaranteed to induce fast mixing under Gibbs sampling. The core idea leverages the well-established connection between spectral norm of the dependency matrix and mixing time. The paper investigates several divergence measures for performing this projection: Euclidean distance (via SVD thresholding), KL-divergence (theoretically interesting but intractable), a piecewise KL-divergence approximating on low-treewidth subgraphs, and a reversed KL-divergence amenable to stochastic approximation.  Experimental results demonstrate that this approach often outperforms standard variational methods like loopy belief propagation and naive mean-field, and offers a complementary trade-off to running Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing parameters.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on a substantial body of work in approximate inference for graphical models. It explicitly positions itself within the landscape of methods like mean-field inference [14, 16, 20], loopy/tree-reweighted belief propagation [19, 21], and planar graph methods [6]. However, it diverges from these by focusing on ensuring fast mixing of an underlying Markov chain rather than directly approximating the target distribution.  The connection to spectral graph theory and mixing time bounds [4, 8, 10, 11] is central, and the paper correctly cites relevant literature. The use of stochastic approximation for KL-divergence minimization is reminiscent of variational autoencoder training and related techniques. The piecewise KL-divergence draws inspiration from work on piecewise likelihood and tree-based approximations [15, 17].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of parameter projection to guarantee fast mixing is a fresh and insightful perspective on approximate inference.  It shifts the focus from distributional approximation to ensuring the efficiency of sampling.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is well-grounded in the theoretical literature on Markov chain mixing times, particularly the connection between spectral norm and convergence rate. The theorems regarding projection are clearly stated and provide a solid mathematical basis for the proposed methods.
* **Multiple Divergence Measures:** Exploring different divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL) demonstrates a thorough investigation of the problem and highlights the trade-offs between accuracy and computational tractability.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation provides compelling evidence that the proposed methods can achieve improved accuracy compared to standard variational methods. The comparison with both original Gibbs sampling and its fast-mixing counterpart is particularly insightful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas. The mathematical notation is mostly clear and consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Spectral Norm Conservatism:** As acknowledged by the authors, the spectral norm bound for fast mixing may be overly conservative, particularly for simpler Ising models. While the empirical results are good, tighter bounds would strengthen the theoretical justification. The reported improvement in performance using a spectral norm of 2.5 over 1.0 raises questions about the optimality of this choice, and its sensitivity to problem specifics.
* **Piecewise KL Performance:** The piecewise KL-divergence, while a clever idea, does not perform as well as the other methods in the experiments. More investigation into the choice of subgraphs or approximation strategies might be beneficial. The rationale for selecting horizontal and vertical chains for grid graphs isn't fully explained.
* **Stochastic Approximation Tuning:** The stochastic approximation for reversed KL-divergence uses fixed hyperparameters.  A more systematic tuning of these parameters could potentially improve performance and provide greater robustness. The explanation of the algorithm's connection to Ergodic Mirror Descent feels somewhat tacked on and could be expanded.
* **Limited Scope:** The paper focuses almost exclusively on the Ising model. While this allows for a detailed analysis, it raises the question of how well the proposed approach generalizes to other graphical models.



**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound approach to approximate inference.
*  The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, outperforming standard variational techniques.
*  The paper is well-written and clearly presents its ideas.
*  The exploration of multiple divergence measures adds significant value.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  The spectral norm bound may be overly conservative.
*  The piecewise KL-divergence does not perform competitively.
*  The scope is limited to the Ising model.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of approximate inference. The idea of projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set is both novel and theoretically grounded. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths of the paper – particularly its originality, theoretical foundation, and empirical validation – outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggested Revisions:**

*   Further investigate the conservatism of the spectral norm bound and explore potential tighter bounds.
*   Provide a more detailed discussion of the performance of the piecewise KL-divergence and explore alternative subgraph selection strategies.
*   Consider discussing the potential generalization of the approach to other graphical models.
*   Expand the explanation of the stochastic approximation algorithm and its connection to Ergodic Mirror Descent.
*   Acknowledge the limited scope of experiments (i.e. only Ising model) in the discussion.
