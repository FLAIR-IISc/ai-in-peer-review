- Proposes "tractable approximations" based on parameter-space conditions guaranteeing rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs, and projects an intractable Ising model to a fast-mixing model by minimizing a divergence under a spectral-norm constraint.

- Uses spectral-norm condition |||β|||2 < 1 (from dependency matrix bounds) as a sufficient mixing condition and enforces it via projection.

- Studies four projection divergences: Euclidean (dual SVD-based projection with sparsity mask), inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ), piecewise KL over tractable subgraphs, and exclusive KL KL(ψ||θ) with a Monte Carlo gradient and projected stochastic gradient method.

- Derives a dual projection algorithm (Theorem 7) that enforces spectral-norm constraints on |B| while preserving sparsity via repeated SVD thresholding and LBFGS-B.

- Correctly derives the covariance-form gradient for KL(ψ||θ) and provides a practical Monte Carlo estimator (noting unbiasedness caveats).

- Empirical results on 8×8 grids and 10-node random graphs show projected models can outperform MF, LBP, TRW in marginal accuracy for comparable sampling budgets and can beat Gibbs on the original slow-mixing model in finite time.

- Major weakness: paper uses a relaxed spectral-norm bound (≈2.5) instead of the theoretical <1, undermining the claimed mixing guarantees and making the method heuristic without further evidence.

- Recommends providing evidence that the true dependency norm remains <1, or adding empirical mixing diagnostics (coupling bounds, autocorrelation, Gelman–Rubin, spectral gap proxies).

- Experimental concerns: small-scale experiments and unfair time–accuracy comparisons that exclude projection and optimization costs bias results toward projected methods.

- Theorem/notation errors: Theorem 6's singular-value thresholding statement is incorrect (typo about c^2/c/2), plus inconsistent KL ordering and other notation issues.

- Theorem 7 needs clearer derivation and explanation of how constraints (D = |B|, Z ⊙ D = 0) are enforced via dual variables M and Λ, and convergence/concavity conditions for LBFGS-B ascent should be discussed.

- Piecewise KL design is heuristic and non-convex (max_T KL), needs discussion of alternatives (sum/average, reweighting like TRW), computational cost, and number of subgraphs used.

- Evaluation protocol is unclear/conflicting about how marginals are computed (Gibbs chain vs exact junction tree); must clarify sampling details, scan type, sweep counts, and convergence criteria.

- Missing baselines/diagnostics: compare against block/tree Gibbs under same constraints, report mixing diagnostics for ψ vs θ, and show sensitivity to hyperparameters and c.

- Quality assessment: analytic derivations and gradients are sound aside from typos and missing dual-projection details; empirical evidence is promising but limited and partially undermined by relaxed constraints and accounting issues.

- Clarity: generally readable but needs tighter notation, corrected typos (especially Theorem 6), and a more explicit algorithmic description (step sizes, projection frequency).

- Originality: novel framing of tractability via fast-mixing set and an original dual projection algorithm; piecewise KL is related to prior work but used in a new projection context.

- Significance: potentially influential for MCMC-based inference if guarantees and experiments are strengthened; current impact limited by experimental scope and missing robustness.

- Suggested fixes: enforce theoretical mixing condition in experiments, correct Theorems and expand derivations, include total computational cost/wall-clock comparisons, scale up experiments, report mixing diagnostics, and unify notation/evaluation protocol.

- Acceptance arguments: conceptually novel, technically solid ideas, and promising empirical gains.

- Rejection arguments: key guarantees relaxed without sufficient evidence, limited experimental scale and unfair time accounting, and theoretical/expository issues with piecewise KL and dual derivations.
