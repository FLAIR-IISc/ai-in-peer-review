## Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

**Summary:**

This paper explores a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically the Ising model. The authors propose projecting parameters of a slow-mixing distribution onto a set of parameters guaranteed to induce fast mixing in Gibbs sampling. They investigate several divergence measures for this projection – Euclidean distance (using SVD thresholding), KL divergence (both theoretically and via piecewise approximations), and a reversed KL divergence amenable to stochastic approximation. The paper provides theoretical results for the Euclidean projection and empirical evaluation demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods compared to standard inference algorithms like LBP, TRW, and naive mean-field, and importantly, comparing to running Gibbs sampling on the original parameters.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of explicitly projecting parameters to ensure fast mixing is a conceptually fresh approach to approximate inference.  It frames tractability not as a property of the graph structure, but as a property of the parameter space. This is a valuable perspective.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a sound theoretical basis, leveraging existing results on mixing times and spectral norms, and extending them with Theorem 7 regarding Euclidean projection.  The derivations appear rigorous and well-motivated.
* **Multiple Divergence Measures:**  Investigating several divergence measures offers a nuanced understanding of the trade-offs between accuracy, computational cost, and theoretical properties. The piecewise KL divergence offers a pragmatic compromise.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental setup, leveraging configurations similar to Hazan and Shashua (2009), provides a good benchmark for comparison.  The results demonstrate that the proposed methods often outperform standard variational approximations.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively positions the approach within the context of existing approximation techniques like mean-field and belief propagation, highlighting the generalization offered by fast-mixing projections.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conservatism of Spectral Norm Bound:** The authors acknowledge, and the discussion highlights, that the spectral norm bound used to define “fast mixing” may be overly conservative.  Using a tighter bound (e.g., relating to the known critical value for the 2D Ising model) could significantly improve the performance of the projection.
* **Computational Cost:** While the experiments show improved accuracy over some methods, the computational cost of the projection itself (particularly for the zero-forcing KL divergence) isn't fully explored in comparison to simply running Gibbs sampling for a longer period.  A detailed cost-benefit analysis, especially in terms of wall-clock time to achieve a desired accuracy, would be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are largely confined to Ising models with relatively small grids or random graphs. Exploring larger, more complex graphs and potentially other graphical model families could strengthen the generality claim.
* **Clarity of "Piecewise" KL:** The description of the piecewise KL divergence and the selection of subgraphs could benefit from greater clarity. The rationale for choosing specific subgraphs isn't fully explained, and the impact of this choice on performance isn’t thoroughly investigated.
* **Dependence on Tuning:** The performance of stochastic gradient descent for the zero-forcing KL divergence relies heavily on parameter tuning (step size, pool size, iterations). The sensitivity of the results to these parameters should be discussed more explicitly.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with claims generally well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The analysis relating spectral norm to mixing time is correct. However, the potential conservatism of the spectral norm threshold weakens the overall quality somewhat. (Score: 4/5)
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, but the description of the piecewise KL divergence could be improved. Some sections (e.g., the motivation for the different divergences) could be made more accessible to a broader audience. (Score: 3.5/5)
* **Originality:** The concept of projecting parameters to enforce fast mixing is a novel contribution. While building on existing work on mixing times and spectral norms, it presents a fresh perspective on approximate inference. (Score: 4/5)
* **Significance:** The results are potentially significant. Providing a framework for guaranteeing rapid convergence of MCMC methods is valuable.  The success of the approach in outperforming standard variational methods suggests practical utility. However, the limited scope of experiments and the potential computational cost of projection temper the overall significance. (Score: 3.5/5)

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically grounded approach to approximate inference.
* Empirically demonstrates improvement over standard variational methods.
* Offers a new perspective on defining "tractability" in graphical models.
* Well-written and clear (with some minor areas for improvement).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Potential conservatism of the spectral norm bound.
* Limited scope of experiments.
* Computational cost of projection needs further investigation.
* Piecewise KL divergence explanation could be more detailed.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of approximate inference.  The idea of projecting parameters to ensure fast mixing is innovative and well-motivated. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding the conservatism of the spectral norm bound and the limited scope of experiments, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns.  

**Recommendation:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance with the following revisions:

1.  Discuss the potential for using a tighter bound on the spectral norm, relating it to known critical values for specific models like the 2D Ising model.
2.  Provide a more detailed cost-benefit analysis, comparing the computational time required for the projection methods to that of running Gibbs sampling on the original parameters to achieve a similar level of accuracy.
3.  Clarify the rationale for selecting specific subgraphs in the piecewise KL divergence, and investigate the impact of this choice on performance.
4. Discuss the sensitivity of the results with respect to parameters of the zero-forcing KL divergence optimization (step size, pool size, etc.).
