

# Review: Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing

This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models by focusing on "fast mixing" models rather than traditional low-treewidth approximations. The authors propose projecting parameters of slow-mixing Ising models onto a set of fast-mixing models where Gibbs sampling converges rapidly. They derive projection algorithms under several divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, and reversed KL), with theoretical guarantees based on spectral norm conditions of the dependency matrix. The work builds on prior mixing time literature (Dyer et al., Hayes) and differentiates from traditional variational methods (mean-field, tree-reweighted) by focusing on parameter-space conditions rather than model structure restrictions.

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical foundation (Theorem 4, Lemma 5) correctly establishes the spectral norm condition for fast mixing. The Euclidean projection algorithm (Theorem 7) is rigorously derived, and the experimental design is appropriate for evaluating marginal accuracy. The authors honestly acknowledge the looseness of the spectral norm bound compared to known Ising model thresholds (Section 7), which demonstrates appropriate scholarly honesty. The experimental results support the claim that their approach provides better marginal estimates than standard variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) for a given computational budget.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and logically organized. The background on mixing times and dependency matrices is clear and accessible to the NIPS audience. The theoretical results are presented with sufficient detail for experts to verify the derivations. The experimental setup is clearly described, though some algorithmic details (e.g., parameter tuning for the stochastic gradient descent) could be more explicit. The paper effectively differentiates their approach from related work, particularly noting that mean-field is a degenerate case of their reversed KL approach.

**Originality**: This work makes a significant contribution by introducing a new notion of "tractability" based on fast mixing conditions rather than low-treewidth structures. The paper clearly differentiates from previous NIPS work (e.g., Globerson and Jaakkola's planar graph approach, mean-field methods) by focusing on parameter-space conditions that guarantee rapid convergence. While the spectral norm bound is conservative, the paper's novel perspective on tractability represents a meaningful advance over traditional variational approaches.

**Significance**: The problem of approximate inference in high-treewidth models is fundamental to graphical models. The paper demonstrates that their approach provides better marginal estimates than standard variational methods for a given computational budget, which is significant for practitioners. The potential extension to learning (Section 7) suggests broader applicability. However, the practical utility is somewhat limited by the conservative spectral norm bound, which the authors acknowledge.

**Strengths**:
1. Novel perspective on tractability through fast mixing conditions
2. Rigorous theoretical foundation for the spectral norm condition
3. Well-designed experimental evaluation showing superior marginal accuracy compared to standard variational methods
4. Clear differentiation from existing work (mean-field as degenerate case)
5. Honest assessment of the spectral norm bound's conservatism

**Weaknesses**:
1. The spectral norm bound (1.0) is conservative compared to known Ising model thresholds (βc ≈ 0.44), requiring a practical bound of 2.5 (Section 6.1) without theoretical justification
2. Limited experimental scope (only 8x8 grids and 10-node graphs)
3. Insufficient comparison with other fast-mixing approaches (e.g., Globerson and Jaakkola's planar graph method)
4. Limited exploration of computational cost trade-offs between projection and sampling
5. The piecewise KL approach (Section 5.3) lacks theoretical justification for its effectiveness

**Arguments for acceptance**:
- Introduces a novel approach to approximate inference with clear theoretical foundation
- Demonstrates practical improvements over standard variational methods
- Well-structured and clearly written
- Addresses a fundamental problem in graphical models

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- The spectral norm bound's conservatism limits practical utility
- Experimental results are limited to small-scale problems
- Insufficient comparison with related fast-mixing approaches

**Overall assessment**: This paper makes a meaningful contribution by introducing a new perspective on tractable inference through fast mixing conditions. The theoretical results are sound, and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over standard variational methods for marginal estimation. While the conservative spectral norm bound is a limitation, the paper's novel approach and strong theoretical foundation justify its significance. The work advances the state of the art by expanding the notion of tractability beyond low-treewidth structures. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
