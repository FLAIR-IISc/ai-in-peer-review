

# Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set. The authors define fast-mixing as parameter-space conditions guaranteeing rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling, specifically for Ising models where the spectral norm of the interaction matrix is less than 1. They derive projection algorithms under four different divergences, including Euclidean distance, piecewise KL, and reversed KL.

**Strengths**: The paper addresses an important problem in approximate inference with clear theoretical motivation. The concept of "fast-mixing" as a tractability criterion is novel and well-motivated. The projection algorithm under Euclidean distance (Theorem 7) is technically sound, and the experimental comparisons with standard methods (LBP, TRW, MF) provide useful empirical validation.

**Weaknesses**: Despite the promising concept, the paper contains several significant technical and methodological issues that undermine its contribution.

First, there are critical notational and formatting errors. Theorem 4 states "τ(!) ≤ n 1− ||R||2 ln (n ! )" which is ambiguous and mathematically incorrect as written. It should be τ(!) ≤ n/(1−||R||2) ln(n/!). Similarly, Theorem 7 uses inconsistent notation like "Z'Z" (likely meant to be Z^T Z) and "Λ'Z" (likely Λ^T Z) without clarification.

Second, the paper's theoretical foundation is shaky. Section 3 claims that "an Ising model can be guaranteed to be fast mixing if the spectral norm of the absolute value of interactions terms is less than one." However, Lemma 5 only establishes Rij ≤ tanh |βij| ≤ |βij|, and the paper fails to connect this bound to the spectral norm condition. For the 2D Ising model, the known critical threshold βc ≈ 0.4407 is significantly higher than the 0.25 implied by their spectral norm bound (as noted in Section 7), but they don't provide a rigorous justification for using 2.5 instead of 1.0 in practice.

Third, Theorem 8 on the gradient of KL(ψ||θ) appears incorrect. The stated gradient ∑x p(x;ψ)(ψ − θ) · f(x) (f(x)− µ(ψ)) is mathematically problematic and doesn't match standard results for KL divergence gradients. The paper should either correct this or provide a proper derivation.

Fourth, the experimental section lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. They mention "a constant step size of 0.1, sample pool size of 500 and 60 total iterations" without explaining the rationale for these choices or the tuning process. The claim that "Gibbs sampling on the rapid-mixing approximation is guaranteed to converge rapidly but will result in less accurate marginals asymptotically" is not adequately supported by their experimental results.

Fifth, the paper fails to properly contextualize its contribution. While they claim their method is a generalization of mean-field, they don't adequately compare with other approaches that use more flexible tractable families (e.g., structured mean-field with tree structures). The reference to [16] on structured mean-field doesn't properly contrast with the authors' approach.

The paper also contains minor but pervasive formatting issues: inconsistent use of mathematical notation (e.g., "||R||2" vs "||R||"), missing parentheses in equations, and inconsistent reference formatting in the bibliography (e.g., some entries have page numbers, others don't).

**Overall Assessment**: While the paper presents an interesting conceptual contribution, the technical errors, insufficient theoretical justification, and lack of experimental detail significantly undermine its scientific quality. The spectral norm bound is overly conservative (as acknowledged in Section 7), and the paper doesn't provide a compelling reason to prefer their approach over existing methods for fast-mixing models.

The authors have identified a potentially valuable direction for approximate inference, but the current paper lacks the technical rigor and completeness required for publication at NIPS. The theoretical results are not sufficiently supported, the experimental validation is incomplete, and the writing contains numerous technical errors that would confuse readers.

**Recommendation**: Reject. The paper's technical shortcomings outweigh its conceptual novelty, and the current version does not meet the standards for a NIPS publication. The authors should address the mathematical errors, provide a tighter bound on the spectral norm condition, and include more detailed experimental validation before resubmission.
