This paper proposes a novel approach for approximate inference in Ising models by reformulating the notion of a "tractable" distribution. Instead of relying on low-treewidth structures, the authors define tractability based on the mixing time of Gibbs sampling. Specifically, they leverage a known result that an Ising model is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing" if the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths is less than one. The core contribution is to approximate an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing, Ising model by projecting its parameters onto this set of fast-mixing models.

The authors explore four different projection methods based on different divergences:
1.  **Euclidean Distance:** A method is derived to find the closest fast-mixing parameters in the L2 sense. This involves a non-trivial optimization problem, which the authors solve via a dual formulation that iteratively thresholds a singular value decomposition (SVD).
2.  **"Zero-avoiding" KL-divergence (KL(q||p)):** This is presented as a theoretically ideal but computationally intractable approach, as it requires computing expectations over the original, slow-mixing distribution `q`.
3.  **Piecewise KL-divergence:** A novel and tractable approximation to the KL divergence is proposed, where the KL is calculated on low-treewidth subgraphs (e.g., spanning trees) of the original model.
4.  **"Zero-forcing" KL-divergence (KL(p||q)):** This is the main practical, probabilistically-motivated method. Since it requires expectations over the approximating distribution `p`, which is constrained to be fast-mixing, these can be estimated via MCMC. The overall optimization is performed using projected stochastic gradient descent.

Experiments on 8x8 grids and small random graphs show that the proposed methods, particularly the projection under KL(p||q), outperform standard variational methods like Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), and Mean Field (MF) in terms of marginal accuracy. The paper also demonstrates a favorable time-accuracy trade-off compared to running Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing parameters.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of defining a tractable family of models based on MCMC mixing time rather than graphical structure is a fresh and compelling perspective. It directly addresses a fundamental bottleneck in MCMC-based inference. This shifts the focus from structural tractability (e.g., low treewidth) to dynamic tractability (fast convergence), which could open up a new line of research in approximate inference.
*   **Technical Quality:** The derivation of the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a solid technical contribution. It cleverly addresses the challenges of constraining the spectral norm of the *absolute value* of the parameters while preserving the graph's sparsity structure. The connection drawn between minimizing KL(p||q) and Mean Field (which corresponds to a zero spectral norm constraint) is also insightful and helps place the work in context.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It clearly motivates the problem, presents the necessary background on mixing times, details the proposed projection methods, and provides a clear experimental evaluation. The distinction between the different divergences and their computational trade-offs is explained well.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while on small-scale models, are well-designed. The proposed methods consistently outperform strong and widely used baselines (LBP, TRW, MF). The comparison against running Gibbs on the original model highlights the practical utility of the approach: for a fixed computational budget, it is often better to spend some time projecting to a fast-mixing model and then sampling, rather than sampling directly from the slow-mixing one.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

*   **Looseness of the Mixing Time Bound:** This is the most significant weakness of the paper. The theoretical guarantee of fast mixing is based on the condition `|| |β| ||_2 < 1`. However, the authors state in the experimental section that they use a bound of 2.5 because the theoretical bound is too conservative and gives poorer approximations. This discrepancy undermines the central claim of projecting onto a "guaranteed" fast-mixing set. While the authors are transparent about this in the discussion, it makes the experimental results somewhat ad-hoc. The work would be substantially stronger if it could:
    a) Provide some empirical justification (e.g., by plotting mixing diagnostics) that the models with a spectral norm of 2.5 are indeed fast-mixing in practice on the tested graphs.
    b) Discuss or incorporate tighter, albeit more complex, mixing time bounds from the literature. The current approach feels like a proof-of-concept whose theoretical underpinnings are disconnected from its practical implementation.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** The Euclidean projection algorithm relies on SVD, which has a cubic complexity (`O(n^3)`) in the number of variables. This would be prohibitive for the large-scale models where fast-mixing approximations are most needed. The paper lacks a discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed projection methods. While the per-iteration cost of the stochastic method for KL(p||q) is lower, it requires many iterations, and each iteration still requires a full Euclidean projection.
*   **Details of Stochastic Optimization:** The procedure for minimizing KL(p||q) has several hyperparameters (step size, sample pool size, number of Gibbs updates per iteration). The paper states fixed values were chosen "without intensive tuning." For reproducibility and a fair assessment of the method's practicality, a more detailed discussion or sensitivity analysis of these parameters would be beneficial. Linking the method to Ergodic Mirror Descent is good, but the practical implications of this for setting the schedule of step sizes and MCMC steps are not explored.
*   **Missing Experimental Results:** The paper introduces the novel "piecewise KL-divergence" but then states it "does not perform as well as the true KL-divergence" without showing the results. For completeness and to evaluate the merit of this proposed divergence, its performance should be quantified and included in the experimental tables or figures.

### Summary and Recommendation

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a novel and significant core idea. The reframing of approximate inference around the concept of mixing time is a valuable contribution to the field. The technical derivations are sound, and the experimental results are promising, showing a clear advantage over established variational methods.

The primary weakness is the disconnect between the theoretical mixing time bound used for the formal problem definition and the much looser bound used in the experiments. This makes the "guarantee" of fast mixing tenuous. However, the conceptual contribution and the strong empirical performance are compelling enough to outweigh this concern, provided the authors can address it more thoroughly in the final version (e.g., by empirically verifying the rapid mixing for their chosen bound).

I recommend acceptance. The paper is likely to inspire further work on bridging the gap between MCMC theory and practical approximate inference.
