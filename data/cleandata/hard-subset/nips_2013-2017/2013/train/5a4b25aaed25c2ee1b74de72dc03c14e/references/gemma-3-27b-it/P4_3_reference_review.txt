This paper explores the idea of projecting Ising model parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set, aiming to accelerate Gibbs sampling for inference. The core concept is that Gibbs sampling converges quickly if the spectral norm of the dependency matrix is less than one. The authors investigate several methods for projecting parameters – Euclidean projection via SVD thresholding, KL-divergence based projections (theoretical and approximated via piecewise KL and a zero-forcing variant), and evaluate these approaches experimentally. The work is situated within a broader context of approximating intractable graphical models, contrasting with methods like mean-field and loopy belief propagation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of explicitly projecting parameters to ensure fast mixing is a novel contribution. It presents a different angle on approximating intractable distributions than existing methods focused on tractable graph structures.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation, leveraging results on mixing times and spectral norms, appears solid. Theorem 7, providing a dual method for Euclidean projection, is a significant technical contribution. The analysis of divergences and their gradients is also well-presented.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The authors perform a reasonably thorough empirical evaluation, comparing their approach against several baselines (LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling).  The experiments cover different graph topologies and interaction strengths.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The concepts are explained clearly, and the mathematical notation is consistent. The connection to existing literature is well-established.
*   **Addressing Weaknesses:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach, particularly the conservatism of the spectral norm bound and the computational cost of certain projections, which demonstrates a careful and honest evaluation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Significance:** While the approach shows promising results in the experiments, the practical advantage over existing methods, especially efficient implementations of TRW or well-tuned Gibbs sampling, isn't overwhelmingly demonstrated. The performance gains seem relatively modest, particularly given the added complexity of the projection step.
*   **Computational Cost:** Some of the proposed projections, especially those involving KL-divergence (even the piecewise approximation), are computationally expensive. The reliance on stochastic gradient descent and Gibbs sampling *within* the projection step adds to the overall complexity.
*   **Spectral Norm Bound:**  The authors correctly point out the potential conservatism of using a spectral norm of 1 as a threshold for fast mixing.  While acknowledging this, a more detailed discussion of alternative, tighter bounds would strengthen the work.  The empirical observation that a bound of 2.5 works well warrants further investigation.
*   **Limited Scope:** The paper primarily focuses on the Ising model. While the principles are potentially applicable to other MRFs, the generality of the approach isn't fully explored.  Extending the analysis to more complex models would be valuable.
*   **Dependency on Parameters:** The performance of the stochastic gradient descent for the zero-forcing KL-divergence is sensitive to parameter tuning (step size, sample pool size, iterations), and the chosen parameters might not generalize well.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to approximate inference.
*   The technical contributions, particularly Theorem 7, are valuable.
*   The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates the potential of the method.
*   The authors are honest about the limitations of their approach.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical advantages over existing methods are not substantial.
*   The computational cost of some projections is high.
*   The reliance on a potentially conservative spectral norm bound limits the applicability of the approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a novel idea and sound technical foundations. While the practical benefits are not overwhelming, the approach has the potential to be a valuable addition to the toolkit of approximate inference methods. The authors have addressed the limitations of their approach in a thoughtful manner. Given the novelty and technical quality, I lean towards **acceptance**, but suggest that the authors address the concerns regarding computational cost and the conservatism of the spectral norm bound in a revised version. A stronger emphasis on the theoretical implications and potential for generalization beyond the Ising model would also be beneficial.
