- Core idea: redefine tractability for approximate inference by projecting Ising model parameters into a parameter set that guarantees fast mixing of Gibbs sampling.
- Key theoretical claim: a sufficient condition for rapid mixing is that the spectral norm of the interaction matrix is < 1.
- Methods: develop projection algorithms onto the fast-mixing set using Euclidean distance (dual method via singular value thresholding), piecewise KL, and reversed KL (KL(ψ||θ)) with stochastic approximation.
- Empirical result: projected models yield better marginal accuracy than standard variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) and converge faster than Gibbs on original parameters.
- Novelty: introducing "fast mixing" as a tractability criterion is a fresh conceptual contribution beyond structural approximations like trees or mean-field.
- Theoretical foundation: provides clear links between spectral norm bounds and mixing time (Theorem 4, Lemma 5) supported by mixing-time literature.
- Algorithmic contribution: practical, implementable algorithms (Euclidean dual, stochastic gradient for reversed KL) with useful implementation details (sample pool).
- Experimental strength: convincing results on 2D grids and random graphs, including insightful comparisons to slow-but-exact Gibbs sampling.
- Honest limitations: authors acknowledge conservativeness of the spectral norm bound and practical adjustments (e.g., using ~2.5 instead of 1.0).
- Weakness — conservative bound: large gap between theoretical (<1) and practical (~2.5) spectral norms limits applicability and is unexplained.
- Weakness — scope: work focuses only on Ising models; extensions to general MRFs are not developed or discussed in depth.
- Weakness — piecewise KL: piecewise KL performs worse than reversed KL in experiments and the paper does not explain or remedy this.
- Weakness — complexity analysis missing: computational complexity of projection algorithms (dual method, stochastic approximation) is not analyzed.
- Weakness — literature context: related work on mixing-time guarantees for MCMC is not sufficiently contextualized despite citations.
- Overall quality: technically sound, well-proven results, and well-designed experiments.
- Clarity and writing: paper is clear, well-organized, and mathematically consistent.
- Originality and significance: novel approach likely to be influential; provides a practical new framework for approximate inference with guaranteed convergence rate.
- Recommendation: accept — merits outweigh limitations given novelty, theory, and strong empirical performance.
