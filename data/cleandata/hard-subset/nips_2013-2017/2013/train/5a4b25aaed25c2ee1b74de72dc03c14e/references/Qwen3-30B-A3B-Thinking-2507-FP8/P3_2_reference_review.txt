

# Peer Review: "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models by focusing on parameter-space conditions that guarantee fast mixing for Gibbs sampling, rather than restricting the model structure. For Ising models, the authors show that a sufficient condition for rapid mixing is that the spectral norm of the interaction matrix's absolute values is less than 1. They then develop projection methods to map arbitrary (slow-mixing) parameters to the fast-mixing set under various divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, and reversed KL). The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution that extends beyond standard variational approaches (mean-field, LBP, TRW) by offering a new notion of tractability.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a clear theoretical foundation for fast-mixing conditions using dependency matrices and spectral norms (Theorem 4). The key insight that spectral norm < 1 guarantees fast mixing for Gibbs sampling is well-established in the literature (Hayes [8]), and the authors properly contextualize this within existing mixing time bounds.

**Novel Projection Framework:** The dual algorithm for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is technically sound and represents a significant contribution. The authors successfully address the two challenges in projecting to the fast-mixing set: maintaining graph structure and controlling the spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix.

**Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing against standard baselines (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs) across multiple graph topologies and interaction strengths. The results consistently show that their methods outperform variational approximations in marginal accuracy, while being computationally more efficient than running Gibbs on the original slow-mixing parameters.

**Practical Relevance:** The paper makes a compelling case for using fast-mixing projections in practice, especially when one needs to obtain approximate marginals quickly without waiting for slow-mixing chains to converge.

## Weaknesses

**Conservative Mixing Bound:** The most significant limitation is the conservative nature of the spectral norm bound. The paper correctly notes that for 2D Ising models, the actual mixing threshold is βc ≈ 0.4407, while the spectral bound would require β < 0.25. The authors use a bound of 2.5 in practice, but provide no theoretical justification for this choice, making the practical utility of their method questionable. A tighter bound would be needed to make this approach truly competitive.

**Limited Scope:** The paper focuses exclusively on Ising models, despite claiming that "future work might extend this approach to general Markov random fields." Without theoretical extensions to general MRFs, the contribution remains somewhat narrow for a NIPS paper. The authors should clarify whether their spectral norm approach generalizes to other MRFs.

**Computational Cost:** The projection process adds substantial computational overhead (30,000 Gibbs iterations for Euclidean projection), which the authors acknowledge but don't adequately address. The paper should provide a more detailed cost-benefit analysis of their method versus simply running longer Gibbs chains on the original parameters.

**Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper doesn't sufficiently compare with recent NIPS work on fast-mixing conditions for MRFs (e.g., Dyer et al. [4] on matrix norms and rapid mixing, or recent work on fast-mixing for specific MRF structures). This weakens the novelty claim.

**Theoretical Justification for Practical Choices:** The paper uses a spectral norm bound of 2.5 in experiments, but doesn't explain why this particular value was chosen. The authors should provide theoretical or empirical justification for this practical choice.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a technically sound contribution with novel ideas about tractability in graphical models. The core idea of using fast-mixing conditions as a tractability criterion is original and well-motivated. However, the conservative spectral norm bound significantly limits the practical utility of the method, and the paper doesn't sufficiently address this limitation.

The experimental results are promising but would be stronger with a more thorough analysis of the trade-off between projection cost and marginal accuracy. The authors should also clarify the connection between their approach and recent work on fast-mixing conditions.

**Overall, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions** (specifically, addressing the conservative bound issue and providing more theoretical justification for their practical choices). The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on approximate inference and would be a good fit for NIPS.

## Key Strengths:
- Novel notion of "tractability" based on fast-mixing conditions rather than graph structure
- Theoretical grounding of fast-mixing conditions using spectral norms
- Well-designed experiments showing improved marginal accuracy over standard variational methods

## Key Weaknesses:
- Conservative spectral norm bound (theoretical and practical limitation)
- Limited scope to Ising models without clear path to generalization
- Insufficient comparison with recent work on fast-mixing conditions
- Unclear justification for practical choice of spectral norm bound (2.5)

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by expanding the notion of tractability beyond structural constraints, but would be stronger with a more thorough discussion of the practical limitations of their mixing condition.
