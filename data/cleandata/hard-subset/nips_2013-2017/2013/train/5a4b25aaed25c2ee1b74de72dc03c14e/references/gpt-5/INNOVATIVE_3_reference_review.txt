Summary
The paper proposes “fast-mixing” distributions as a new tractable family for approximate inference: instead of restricting graph structure (trees, planar, low treewidth), restrict parameters so that single-site Gibbs mixes rapidly. For Ising models, the sufficient condition used is ||R||2 < 1, with R the dependency matrix and Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|, leading to a spectral-norm bound on the absolute interaction matrix. Given an arbitrary parameter vector θ (possibly slow-mixing), the paper projects to a fast-mixing parameter set Ψ under several divergences:
- Euclidean (l2) projection: derive a dual formulation (Theorem 7) enabling projection that (a) controls the spectral norm of |B| and (b) preserves sparsity pattern. This uses iterative SVD thresholding inside a dual ascent with LBFGS-B.
- Inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ): gradient µ(ψ) − µ(θ); impractical at scale since µ(θ) is intractable for slow-mixing θ.
- A novel “piecewise KL”: drop edges to form low-treewidth subgraphs T and define D = maxT KL(θ(T)||ψ(T)). Tractable via exact inference on subgraphs.
- Exclusive KL KL(ψ||θ): derive a sampling-based gradient estimator (Eq. 8); optimize via projected stochastic approximation with a persistent sample pool (Ergodic Mirror Descent view).

Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare the projections (Euclidean, piecewise KL, KL(ψ||θ)) to MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs on the original parameters. Results indicate that fast-mixing projections typically yield better marginals than variational baselines for a fixed sampling budget; with unlimited time, Gibbs on the original model wins, as expected.

Strengths
- Novel direction: reframing “tractability” as fast mixing is a bold and appealing idea that can bridge variational and MCMC paradigms. Using Ψ as an approximation family generalizes MF (KL(ψ||θ) with stronger constraints than mean-field’s zero-dependence).
- Technical contribution: dual projection that enforces a spectral-norm constraint on |B| while preserving sparsity is nontrivial and potentially useful beyond this paper.
- Practicality: the KL(ψ||θ) approach is implementable by Gibbs since ψ stays fast-mixing; the sample-pool + projection procedure is simple and generic.
- Piecewise KL is an interesting, tractable surrogate that connects to piecewise training and mixtures of trees.
- Clear articulation of the trade-off: guarantees vs conservativeness of the spectral bound; and time-accuracy trade-offs vs running Gibbs on the original parameters.

Weaknesses and questions
- Conservativeness vs guarantees: the experiments adopt a spectral-norm bound of 2.5 (vs the theoretical <1 guarantee) to get better approximations, which sacrifices the core selling point (provable fast mixing). Please quantify mixing behavior (empirical coupling time, autocorrelation, or spectral gap proxies) as the bound is varied. An ablation showing accuracy vs spectral threshold and measured mixing time would be very valuable.
- Theorem 6/7 clarity: In Theorem 6 the projection onto a spectral-norm ball should clip singular values at c (S′ii = min(Sii, c)); the text shows “c 2”, which looks like a typo. Theorem 7 is central but the main text omits convexity and duality conditions. The constraint D = |B| is nonconvex; please clarify the problem’s convexity, exactness of the dual, and convergence guarantees of the LBFGS-B procedure. Providing a short proof sketch and conditions would strengthen the contribution.
- Gradient for KL(ψ||θ) (Theorem 8): the presented form is unusual; a brief derivation from KL(ψ||θ) = A(θ) − A(ψ) − (θ − ψ)·µ(ψ), or via score-function identities, would improve confidence. Also discuss variance and stability of the estimator in Eq. 8 and whether control variates/baselines were used.
- Experimental protocol clarity: At one point you say accuracy is measured using exact junction-tree marginals; elsewhere you say you estimate marginals from Gibbs samples. Please make explicit: what is the ground truth (exact via JT) and what is the evaluation estimator for each method. Also, a single chain for baselines can conflate mixing issues; report burn-in, thinning, ESS or R-hat where applicable.
- Baseline tuning and compute fairness: MF/LBP/TRW have tunable schedules; Gibbs on original parameters is run for the same post-projection sampling steps but projection itself consumed substantial compute. A compute-normalized comparison (e.g., wall-clock vs error) would make the time-accuracy narrative much more convincing.
- Piecewise KL: Results are said to be worse than true KL; please analyze sensitivity to the choice of subgraphs (trees vs width-2 chains) and whether averaging over subgraphs (sum or expectation) improves robustness compared to maxT.
- Scope: Current results are for binary pairwise Ising. A brief discussion or preliminary evidence for Potts or general MRFs (bounds on R and projection algorithms) would broaden impact.

Quality
- Generally solid, with correct use of known mixing criteria (Dyer et al., Hayes). Some derivations and optimization details need tightening (typo in Theorem 6, duality/convexity for Theorem 7, gradient derivation for Theorem 8). Experimental setup is standard but could be more rigorous in fairness/mixing diagnostics.

Clarity
- Well organized and easy to follow. Please fix typos, clarify the projection algorithm (pseudo-code), provide derivations/appendix proofs, and tighten the evaluation description.

Originality
- High. Using fast-mixing parameter sets as the approximating family and projecting onto them—especially via a sparsity-preserving spectral-norm projection—is a novel angle that goes beyond standard variational classes. The piecewise KL variant is also a fresh twist on piecewise ideas.

Significance
- Promising direction with potential to influence approximate inference where MCMC is desired but mixing is a bottleneck. Even if the current spectral condition is conservative, the framework can incorporate improved bounds, and the projection machinery is reusable.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptual novelty and a bold new tractability notion.
- Useful projection algorithm with potential independent value.
- Practical stochastic projection procedure with encouraging results.
- Clear relation to and generalization of mean-field.

Cons against acceptance
- Guarantees weakened in practice by relaxing the spectral bound.
- Theoretical details (dual exactness, gradient) need clarification.
- Limited evaluation rigor on mixing/time and baseline fairness.

Recommendation
- Accept (leaning). The paper opens a compelling direction and provides concrete algorithms; the minor theoretical and experimental issues are, in my view, addressable in revision and outweighed by the novelty. I encourage the authors to add the requested clarifications and empirical ablations.
