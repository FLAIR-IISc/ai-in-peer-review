Summary
The paper proposes a new “tractable family” for approximate inference: distributions whose parameters guarantee that single-site Gibbs sampling is fast mixing. For binary Ising models, the sufficient condition used is that the spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix |β| is less than one (via bounds on the dependency matrix and Theorem 4). Given an intractable target Ising model θ, the authors study projections onto the fast-mixing set Ψ using four divergences:

- Euclidean projection: minimize ||θ−ψ||2 subject to |||ψedge|||2 ≤ c and graph sparsity. They derive a dual procedure (Theorem 7) implemented with LBFGS-B that repeatedly thresholds an SVD and enforces sparsity via a mask.
- KL(θ||ψ) (“zero-avoiding”): theoretically attractive but requires expectations under θ, hence intractable for high treewidth.
- A “piecewise” KL approximation: project both θ and ψ onto tractable subgraphs and minimize maxT KL(θ(T)||ψ(T)).
- KL(ψ||θ) (“zero-forcing”): amenable to stochastic approximation because expectations are under ψ, which is constrained to be fast-mixing. They give a gradient form that equals Covψ[f,f](ψ−θ) and estimate it from a sample pool, alternating gradient steps with Euclidean projections.

Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare projected models (Euclidean, piecewise, reverse KL) against LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original parameters, evaluating marginal errors. The projected fast-mixing models, especially with KL(ψ||θ), outperform standard variational baselines and can be more accurate than finite-time Gibbs on the original parameters, while being guaranteed (in theory) to mix rapidly.

Relation to prior work
The work connects mixing-time theory for spin systems (Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum; Hayes) to approximate inference. It contrasts with variational methods (MF, structured MF, TRW, BP) and planar/tractable substructure approaches (Globerson & Jaakkola; piecewise/mixtures of trees). The link between KL(ψ||θ) with constraints on the dependency matrix and mean-field (zero spectral norm as a degenerate case) is insightful.

Strengths
- Clear framing of “fast mixing” as a tractable family, bridging MCMC guarantees and variational design.
- A practical projection mechanism onto a spectral-norm-constrained set with sparsity preservation (dual/SVD-thresholding); interesting technically.
- Using KL(ψ||θ) with projected stochastic approximation is a neat generalization of MF that leverages guaranteed mixing for sampling-based gradients.
- Experiments show consistent improvements over MF/LBP/TRW on tested settings; the time–accuracy comparison to finite-time Gibbs on original parameters is informative.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Use of a looser spectral norm bound in practice (2.5 instead of <1). This undermines the central guarantee. If looser bounds still “seem to mix,” please provide empirical mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, effective sample size, coupling time proxies, or Gelman–Rubin statistics) and report actual spectral norms after projection. Otherwise the main selling point (guaranteed fast mixing) is not upheld in experiments.
- Evaluation inconsistencies. Section 6 says marginals are “compared … by running a Gibbs chain on each” but also that “marginals [are] computed via the exact junction-tree algorithm.” Later it says marginals are estimated from Gibbs samples. Please clarify what was used for ground-truth and for the projected models, and ensure the same estimator is used when comparing methods, or clearly separate “true” vs “estimated” marginals.
- Computational budget fairness. The comparison “does not take into account the computational effort deployed during projection,” which can be substantial. A fair comparison should include wall-clock, total Gibbs updates (including those used during optimization), and number of SVDs; or provide accuracy-vs-time curves including projection/training cost.
- Technical clarity of the Euclidean projection. Theorem 6 appears to have a typo (threshold at c/2 instead of c for projection onto an operator-norm ball). Please correct and define the Hadamard products (‘, ·) consistently; e.g., Λ’Z vs Λ·Z. The dual objective in Theorem 7 seems to have a typographical issue (“1/ ||D−R||^2F” likely 1/2||…||^2). Since Πc is non-smooth at singular-value thresholds, discuss subgradient handling and convergence guarantees for LBFGS-B in the dual.
- Gradient estimation for KL(ψ||θ). The gradient simplifies to Covψ[f,f](ψ−θ); estimating the covariance and multiplying by (ψ−θ) is lower variance than the product-of-estimators in Eq. (8), and avoids bias from reusing the same samples. Please consider/report this estimator. Also discuss step-size schedules and convergence (ergodic mirror descent conditions) more concretely.
- Piecewise KL choice. Using maxT KL(θ(T)||ψ(T)) is a conservative criterion; alternatives (sum or average over a cover, or reweighting as in TRW) might be better behaved. The current variant underperforms in experiments—some analysis of why, and ablations over choices of T, would help.
- Scope and scale. Empirical tests are on small graphs (10-node random graphs, 8×8 grids). Complexity of repeated SVDs and projections is not discussed for larger n. Reporting runtime and scaling behavior would strengthen the significance claim.
- Related mixing bounds. The spectral norm bound is known conservative for grids; discussion could cite correlation decay/Weitz-style bounds and whether alternative dependency measures (e.g., max row sums, block updates) could tighten Ψ.

Quality
The core ideas are technically sound and mostly well supported. However, typos in statements, limited proofs (Theorem 7 deferred), and the relaxation of the main constraint in experiments weaken the rigor. The empirical methodology needs clearer, consistent evaluation and budget accounting.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with helpful background. Some notational ambiguities and typos should be fixed. Provide pseudocode for the projected SGD, define all operators, and state convergence/complexity results explicitly.

Originality
The idea of projecting onto a fast-mixing set and the dual projection preserving sparsity are novel, and the connection to MF via KL(ψ||θ) under dependency constraints is original and interesting.

Significance
Promising direction that could influence how practitioners trade off bias (approximation family) and variance (MCMC). Current experiments are modest; with stronger empirical validation, tighter mixing criteria, and scalability analysis, the impact could be substantial.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and timely idea linking mixing-time theory and approximate inference.
- Practical algorithms (projection + projected SGD) with promising empirical gains over standard variational methods.
- Conceptual unification: MF as a special case of constrained KL(ψ||θ).

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees not adhered to in experiments (spectral bound > 1); lack of mixing diagnostics.
- Evaluation inconsistencies and incomplete accounting of computational cost.
- Limited scale and scope; some technical statements contain typos/ambiguities.

Suggestions to improve
- Enforce the theoretical bound in at least a subset of experiments and report mixing metrics; if using looser bounds, justify with diagnostics.
- Use the covariance-form gradient; compare to Eq. (8) empirically.
- Provide accuracy vs total compute (including projection/training).
- Clarify evaluation methodology; add larger-scale experiments; discuss scalability.
- Fix typos, define notation rigorously, and sketch the proof/assumptions behind Theorem 7.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper introduces a compelling idea with solid initial algorithms and promising results, but needs clearer experimental validation of the claimed guarantees and stronger empirical methodology.
