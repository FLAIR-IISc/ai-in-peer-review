- Proposal: project Ising-model parameters onto a fast-mixing family (spectral-norm constrained) to enable guaranteed rapid-mixing Gibbs sampling as an approximate inference family.
- Practical sufficient condition: use |||β|||2 (spectral norm of absolute interactions) < 1 (practical relaxation via Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|).
- Euclidean projection: dual algorithm using iterated spectral-norm thresholding that preserves sparsity (technically interesting, uses SVD thresholding).
- Inclusive KL projection: principled but requires expectations under the original slow-mixing model, limiting practicality.
- Piecewise KL: uses tractable subgraphs but the max-over-subgraphs design is pessimistic and non-smooth.
- Reverse KL projection: optimized via stochastic approximation with samples from the fast-mixing surrogate; connects to mean-field as a degenerate case.
- Empirics: comparisons on 8×8 grids and small random graphs against LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs using exact marginals as ground truth.
- Novelty: using fast-mixing region as a tractable family is original and complementary to treewidth/tractable-class approaches.
- Strength: conceptually novel bridge between MCMC and variational inference, leveraging mixing-time theory for practical constraints.
- Strength: dual projection that preserves sparsity is broadly useful and technically nontrivial.
- Strength: stochastic reverse-KL approach leverages tractable expectations under the constraint and relates to ergodic mirror descent.
- Strength: empirical comparisons against strong baselines and ablations across graph types/interaction strengths.
- Weakness: main theoretical guarantee is undermined in experiments by using spectral-norm bound c=2.5 (>1), so fast-mixing guarantee no longer holds.
- Weakness: likely typo in Theorem 6 (S′ii = min(Sii, c^2) should be min(Sii, c)) needs clarification.
- Weakness: Theorem 7 dual derivation and notation are unclear/typos (missing factors, unclear operators), hurting reproducibility.
- Weakness: Reverse-KL gradient estimator is biased when using the same sample pool for μ̂ and product terms; independence assumption is misstated.
- Weakness: experimental scope is limited (small graphs); missing wall-clock, convergence diagnostics, and projection cost accounting limits practical claims.
- Weakness: piecewise KL formulation (max) may be suboptimal; alternatives like convex combinations/TRW-style averages should be considered.
- Weakness: additional related work on stronger uniqueness/correlation-decay conditions could contextualize and potentially tighten mixing criteria.
- Quality: technically sound overall but needs fixes to derivations/notation and addressing bias concerns.
- Clarity: generally well written, but dual derivation, projection notation, and algorithmic details (pseudocode, update schedules, stopping criteria) need clarification.
- Significance: promising approach with potential practical impact if guarantees align with experiments and scalability/runtime are demonstrated.
- Suggestions: fix Theorems 6/7 typos and provide full derivations or supplementary proofs.
- Suggestions: report post-projection spectral norms and mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, Gelman-Rubin, coupling) especially when c>1.
- Suggestions: eliminate gradient bias using independent sample pools or leave-one-out μ̂, and report sensitivity to step size, pool size, and Gibbs updates.
- Suggestions: explore alternative piecewise objectives (averages or TRW weights) and include wall-clock timing and larger graph experiments.
- Pros for acceptance: novel framing, nontrivial projection method, and promising empirical performance versus variational baselines.
- Cons for acceptance: guarantees not respected in main experiments, notation/derivation issues, gradient-bias not addressed, and limited empirical diagnostics.
- Recommendation: weak accept conditional on clarifying theory (Theorems 6/7), fixing unbiasedness/bias issues, and adding mixing/runtime diagnostics.
