Summary
This paper proposes a new notion of “tractable” approximations for high-treewidth graphical models: not low-treewidth structures, but parameter sets that guarantee rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs sampling. For Ising models with binary variables, the authors use a sufficient condition for fast mixing—spectral norm of the dependency matrix less than 1—leveraging bounds Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|. They study the projection of arbitrary Ising parameters onto this fast-mixing set under four divergences:
- Euclidean projection: they derive a dual algorithm that (i) projects via singular value thresholding while (ii) enforcing sparsity and the absolute-value spectral constraint (Theorem 7).
- Inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ): theoretically attractive but requires expectations under θ and is thus intractable generally.
- A novel “piecewise” KL approximation: remove edges to form tractable subgraphs and optimize the worst-case (max over subgraphs) KL; easy to compute but empirically weaker.
- Reverse KL KL(ψ||θ): enables stochastic optimization using samples from ψ (which is enforced to be fast-mixing), with projected gradient steps back onto the fast-mixing set. This can be viewed as a generalization of mean-field from fully factorized families to the larger “fast-mixing” family.

Experiments on 8×8 grids and 10-node random graphs compare these projections (followed by Gibbs) against LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original parameters. Results suggest the proposed projections yield better finite-time marginal accuracy than the variational baselines and, for fixed compute on sampling, often outperform Gibbs on the original parameters due to mixing issues.

Relation to prior work
The work ties mixing-time criteria (Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum; Hayes) to approximate inference, offering an alternative to mean-field, structured mean-field, TRW, and planar decompositions. The reverse-KL projection with stochastic approximation relates to Minka’s divergence perspectives and ergodic mirror descent. To my knowledge, directly projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set for inference is a novel and compelling direction.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: redefining “tractable” via fast mixing, and operationalizing it through projections. This is a bold, fresh direction beyond tree-structured approximations.
- Algorithmic contribution: a dual Euclidean projection method (Theorem 7) that enforces sparsity and the absolute-value spectral constraint via SVD thresholding in a principled way.
- Practicality: the reverse-KL projected SGD is implementable; using fast-mixing ψ makes sampling-based gradients feasible.
- Clear connection to MF: the approach generalizes MF (R=0) to a substantially richer approximation family with empirical benefits.
- Empirical relevance: finite-time comparisons capture what practitioners face; the reported gains over LBP/TRW/MF are meaningful.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Guarantees vs practice: While the theory requires ||R||2<1, the experiments sometimes use a bound of 2.5 “which still preserves rapid mixing.” This undermines the guarantee; please either (i) report diagnostics that confirm fast mixing under this looser bound (e.g., coupling/mixing-time surrogates), or (ii) keep experiments within the proven regime and separate the heuristic regime clearly.
- Tightness of bounds: Using |β| rather than tanh|β| in the projection is convenient but loose; this can materially shrink the feasible set. A projection under tanh-transformed parameters would be tighter, though more complex—worth discussing as future work or a partial implementation.
- Gradient estimator discussion: The product-of-unbiased-estimators claim is generally false. In this specific case, the reverse-KL gradient equals Covψ(f)(ψ−θ), and an unbiased estimator exists using centered features. Using the same sample set for μ̂ and the gradient yields bias unless carefully centered; your estimator is in fact unbiased if you use (ψ−θ)·(f−μ̂) times (f−μ̂). Please correct the discussion and, if possible, report the variance/bias behavior with and without centering.
- Dual derivation clarity: Theorem 7 is central but terse. Symbols like Λ′Z, the “triple dot product,” and the exact form of g(Λ,M) (which currently looks dimensionally odd: “1/||·||^2” vs a typical quadratic) need clearer definitions and sanity checks. Please fix potential typos (e.g., in Theorem 6 the singular values should be clipped at c, not c^2; TV distance typically has a 1/2 factor).
- Piecewise KL: The max-over-subgraphs objective is non-smooth and empirically underperforms. A weighted average or convex combination might be better behaved; subgraph selection could be learned or optimized.
- Experimental accounting: A fair wall-clock comparison including the optimization/projection overhead (vs time spent solely sampling) would strengthen the practical message. Also, describe mixing diagnostics for the “fast-mixing” ψ (autocorrelation, spectral gap proxies) to support the claim.

Quality
Overall technically sound with a few derivation/notation issues that should be corrected. The core idea and algorithms are coherent; experiments are reasonable, though runtime accounting and mixing diagnostics would improve the evaluation.

Clarity
The paper is well organized and readable. Some notation/typos and the dual derivation need tightening. Explicitly stating assumptions (minimal exponential family, overcomplete handling) is appreciated.

Originality
High. Projecting onto a fast-mixing set as an approximation family, and the dual Euclidean projection preserving sparsity under an absolute-value spectral constraint, are novel. The piecewise KL idea is also fresh, albeit less successful.

Significance
Promising direction likely to influence approximate inference and learning with MCMC-in-the-loop. Even if the current theory is limited to Ising and the bounds are conservative, the approach could seed further developments (tighter bounds, better projections, broader MRF classes).

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you provide empirical mixing evidence for ψ at bound 2.5 (e.g., PSRF, autocorrelation times), and an ablation of the spectral cap vs accuracy?
- Can you tighten the projection by using tanh(|β|) in the constraint, at least approximately?
- Please correct the KL(ψ||θ) gradient estimator discussion and center features to reduce variance.
- Include wall-clock comparisons that account for projection overhead.
- Clarify Theorem 7’s objective and notation; check Theorem 6’s clipping constant and Definition 1’s TV factor.

Pros
- Bold and novel framing of tractability via fast mixing.
- Practical algorithms; generalizes MF to a richer family.
- Empirical gains over strong variational baselines in finite time.

Cons
- Theoretical guarantees not always respected in experiments.
- Some derivation/notation inaccuracies.
- Limited to Ising; piecewise KL underperforms.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
The paper introduces an exciting and original direction with practical promise. With corrections and clearer experimental accounting, it would make a valuable NeurIPS contribution.
