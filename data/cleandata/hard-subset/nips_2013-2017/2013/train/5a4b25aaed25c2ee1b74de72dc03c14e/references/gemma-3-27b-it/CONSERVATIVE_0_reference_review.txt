This paper explores the idea of projecting parameters of Ising models onto a “fast-mixing” set, aiming to accelerate Gibbs sampling for inference. The core concept – leveraging parameter constraints to ensure rapid convergence of MCMC – is potentially interesting, connecting optimization and sampling techniques. However, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence and a somewhat convoluted presentation. While the authors investigate several divergence measures for the projection, the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over established methods, and the theoretical justifications sometimes feel underdeveloped.

The primary strength lies in the formalization of the fast-mixing property as a constraint and the attempt to project onto this constraint using different divergences. The theorem regarding Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a useful, though perhaps not groundbreaking, contribution. The connection to mean-field inference as a special case of zero-forcing KL-divergence is also insightful.

However, the weaknesses are more substantial. The experimental evaluation feels incomplete. While comparisons are made to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling, the configurations tested (8x8 grids and small random graphs) are relatively simple, and the reported improvements over existing methods are marginal. The sensitivity to the spectral norm threshold (using 2.5 instead of the theoretically motivated 1.0) is concerning and not thoroughly explained. The piecewise KL-divergence seems particularly unconvincing, performing worse than the other approaches. Furthermore, the choice of divergences feels somewhat arbitrary, and the justification for exploring them beyond theoretical interest is weak. The “zero-avoiding” KL-divergence is dismissed as intractable, but the discussion lacks a clear articulation of why approximating it is fundamentally difficult.

The writing is dense and often lacks clarity. The extensive background on mixing times (Section 2) feels disproportionately long compared to the depth of the contribution. While thoroughness is appreciated, a more focused presentation would benefit the reader. The notation is also sometimes cumbersome, making it difficult to follow the derivations.

In terms of originality, the idea of constraining parameters to improve mixing is not entirely novel. Prior work has explored similar concepts, though perhaps not with the same emphasis on spectral norm constraints. The specific divergences considered, and their projection methods, offer some incremental contributions, but are unlikely to represent a major breakthrough.

The significance of this work is questionable. While faster inference is always desirable, the marginal improvements demonstrated here, combined with the computational cost of projection, do not make a strong case for practical adoption. The paper doesn’t convincingly establish that this approach is superior to simply running Gibbs sampling for a longer period or employing more sophisticated sampling techniques.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Formalization of fast-mixing as a desirable property for parameter constraints.
*   Theoretical results on Euclidean projection.
*   Connection to mean-field inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited and unconvincing experimental results.
*   Convoluted and dense writing style.
*   Marginal originality and significance.
*   Lack of thorough justification for the choice of divergences.
*   Questionable sensitivity to the spectral norm threshold.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

The paper presents an interesting idea, but the execution and validation are insufficient. The experimental results do not demonstrate a clear benefit over existing methods, and the theoretical contributions are incremental. The paper lacks the clarity and impact necessary for acceptance at NIPS. I recommend the authors significantly expand the experimental evaluation, provide a more compelling justification for their choices, and revise the manuscript for improved clarity.
