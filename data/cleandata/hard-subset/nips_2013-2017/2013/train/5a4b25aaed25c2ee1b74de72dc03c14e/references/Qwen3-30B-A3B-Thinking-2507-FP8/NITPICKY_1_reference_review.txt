

# Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

This paper proposes a novel notion of "tractability" for graphical models based on fast mixing of Gibbs sampling, rather than low treewidth. The authors define a set of parameters for Ising models that guarantee fast mixing (spectral norm of interaction matrix < 1) and propose projecting arbitrary parameters onto this set using four different divergences. The main technical contribution is a dual method for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7), with extensions to KL divergences.

**Strengths:**
- The concept of "fast mixing" as a tractability criterion is conceptually fresh and addresses a practical limitation of traditional approaches.
- The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering grid graphs, random graphs, and various interaction strengths with multiple comparison methods.
- The paper demonstrates that fast-mixing projections can outperform variational methods like mean-field and loopy belief propagation in marginal accuracy.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical Inconsistency in Spectral Norm Bound:** The paper claims in Section 7 that "for β = .25, the spectral bound norm will be equal to one," but this is incorrect. For a 2D grid Ising model with uniform interaction strength β, the spectral norm of the interaction matrix scales with β times the spectral norm of the grid graph's adjacency matrix. The actual threshold βc ≈ 0.4407 (for criticality) is unrelated to the spectral norm of the interaction matrix being equal to 1 at β = 0.25. The paper's claim that "using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of 1.0 can still preserve the rapid-mixing property" lacks theoretical justification and contradicts the established theoretical bound. This is a significant theoretical flaw.

2. **Incorrect Gradient Calculation:** In Section 5.4, the gradient of KL(ψ||θ) is stated as "d/dψ D(θ,ψ) = ∑_x p(x;ψ)(ψ - θ) · f(x) (f(x) - μ(ψ))". This appears to be incorrect. The standard gradient for KL(ψ||θ) should be ∇_ψ KL(ψ||θ) = μ(ψ) - μ(θ) for exponential families. The provided expression seems to contain an extra factor that doesn't align with standard results, potentially leading to incorrect optimization.

3. **Formatting and Presentation Errors:**
   - The total variation distance definition (Section 2) is missing the crucial factor of 1/2: "||p(X)− q(X)||TV = 1 ∑ x |p(X = x)− q(X = x)|" should be "||p(X)− q(X)||TV = (1/2) ∑_x |p(X = x)− q(X = x)|".
   - The mixing time bound in Theorem 4 is written as "τ(!) ≤ n 1− ||R||2 ln (n ! )", which is ambiguous and should be "τ(!) ≤ n/(1-||R||2) ln(n/!)".
   - The reference to "Dyer et al. [4]" is inconsistent with the citation style used elsewhere in the paper.

4. **Experimental Design Concerns:** The paper states that "Gibbs sampling on the original parameters will produce highly accurate marginals if run long enough," but then claims their method "gives better results" with finite time. However, the experiments don't demonstrate a clear time-accuracy tradeoff where the fast-mixing projection outperforms Gibbs sampling on the original model within a reasonable time budget. The paper also doesn't provide sufficient justification for why the spectral norm bound was increased from 1.0 to 2.5.

**Quality:** The paper contains a fundamental theoretical error regarding the spectral norm bound, which undermines a core claim of the paper. The incorrect gradient calculation further weakens the technical contribution. The experimental results are well-presented but cannot compensate for these theoretical flaws.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized but contains multiple formatting inconsistencies and ambiguous mathematical expressions that reduce clarity. The missing factor of 1/2 in the total variation distance definition is particularly problematic.

**Originality:** The notion of fast-mixing as a tractability criterion is novel and interesting, but the technical implementation contains significant errors that diminish the contribution.

**Significance:** While the concept is promising, the theoretical inconsistencies prevent the work from making a meaningful contribution to the field. The practical implications are unclear without a sound theoretical foundation.

**Conclusion:** Despite the interesting conceptual contribution, the paper contains substantial theoretical errors (particularly regarding the spectral norm bound and gradient calculation) that invalidate key claims. The formatting inconsistencies and ambiguous mathematical expressions further detract from the paper's quality. While the experimental evaluation is thorough, it cannot compensate for the fundamental theoretical flaws. I recommend rejection due to these significant technical issues.
