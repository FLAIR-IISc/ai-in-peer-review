Summary
This paper proposes “fast-mixing” models as a tractable family for approximate inference: parameter settings that guarantee rapid convergence of univariate Gibbs sampling. Focusing on Ising models, the authors use the spectral norm of the dependency matrix as a sufficient condition (via Hayes/Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum), and develop methods to project arbitrary (possibly slow-mixing) Ising parameters onto the fast-mixing set under different divergences:
- Euclidean projection: a dual method that effectively clips singular values while preserving sparsity and constraining the absolute interaction matrix.
- KL(θ||ψ) (inclusive/zero-avoiding): theoretically appealing but requires expectations under θ (intractable for high treewidth), so only small graphs.
- Piecewise KL: compute KL on tractable subgraphs (trees or low-treewidth subgraphs) and take a max, yielding an easy but looser surrogate.
- KL(ψ||θ) (reverse/zero-forcing): feasible because expectations are under ψ (enforced to be fast-mixing), optimized by stochastic projected gradient using a sample pool (Ergodic Mirror Descent).

Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare marginals against LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original parameters. The projected fast-mixing models generally yield more accurate marginals than variational methods under a finite computational budget, while maintaining rapid-mixing chains.

Quality
- Theory: The use of the spectral norm bound on the dependency matrix and Lemma 5 (Rij ≤ tanh |βij| ≤ |βij|) is standard and sound. The Euclidean projection approach leveraging singular value clipping is well-motivated; the dual formulation (Theorem 7) that preserves sparsity and enforces constraints on |B| rather than B itself is a useful technical contribution. The gradient for KL(ψ||θ) matches the well-known expression ∇ψ KL = Covψ[f](ψ−θ), and the sampling-based estimator is reasonable.
- A few technical/notation issues need correction for rigor:
  - Theorem 6 likely has a typo: S′ii = min(Sii, c) (not “c 2”).
  - In Theorem 7, the objective expression appears typeset incorrectly (“1 / ||·||^2F” vs the expected ±(1/2)||·||^2F term). Please check signs and factors of 1/2; the provided gradients suggest a quadratic form.
  - In the introduction, “zero-forcing divergence KL(q||p)” contradicts Section 5.4 (zero-forcing is KL(ψ||θ), i.e., KL(p||q)). Please fix the direction consistently.
  - A stray “1” footnote marker in Section 5.3.
- Optimization: The projected stochastic gradient scheme is plausible, but some details are heuristic. The claim that the product of independent unbiased estimators is unbiased is correct, but the implementation reuses the same samples for both terms (introducing bias); the paper notes this but does not quantify the effect. A simple fix is to use two independent sample sets or control variates; at minimum, empirical validation that the bias is negligible would strengthen the claims.
- Experiments: Comparisons are sensible and the evaluation metric (mean absolute error of exact marginals) is appropriate. However:
  - The key guarantee hinges on the spectral norm bound < 1, but experiments often use 2.5 “which still preserves rapid mixing.” This undercuts the theoretical guarantee; if loosened in practice, the paper should provide diagnostics (autocorrelation time/effective sample size, Gelman–Rubin R̂) or empirical bounds to justify the choice, and quantify failure modes near criticality.
  - Computational budget comparisons exclude the cost of projection for projected methods; the paper acknowledges this. Offering a wall-clock/iteration-normalized comparison would be more informative.
  - The chosen settings (8×8 grid, n=10 random graphs) are reasonable but modest; exploring larger grids near critical coupling would better stress-test mixing.

Clarity
The paper is largely well-written and well-organized, with a clear motivation contrasting “inference-tractable” vs “mixing-tractable” families. That said, fix the noted typos/equation formatting and be explicit about algorithmic details:
- State the exact projection subproblem and constraints (on |B| and sparsity) and summarize the dual derivation, with complexity.
- Clarify the sample-pool algorithm (number of passes, refresh strategy, dependence across iterations) and provide a convergence schedule (step sizes and number of Gibbs steps) per ergodic mirror descent theory.
- Report mixing diagnostics to substantiate “rapid mixing” in practice when using looser thresholds.

Originality
- The idea of using parameter-space fast-mixing constraints as an approximating family and explicitly projecting onto this set is novel and conceptually appealing. The dual Euclidean projection under absolute-value spectral constraints with sparsity is technically interesting.
- Framing KL(ψ||θ) minimization under a fast-mixing constraint as a generalization of mean-field is insightful. The “piecewise KL” construction is a reasonable, if incremental, idea building on piecewise training/mixtures of trees.

Significance
- If robust, the approach offers a practical middle ground between variational approximations and MCMC: approximate the model to make sampling fast and reliable, then sample. Demonstrating superior marginal accuracy to LBP/TRW/MF under finite budgets is valuable to practitioners.
- The present scope is limited to Ising models with pairwise terms. Extending to general MRFs will require better dependency bounds and projection algorithms; the paper acknowledges this.
- The looseness of the spectral norm bound (and the empirical choice to exceed it) tempers the strength of the guarantees. Addressing this (either with tighter criteria or comprehensive empirical validation) would increase impact.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated new notion of tractability via fast mixing.
- Solid technical development for Euclidean projection with sparsity and absolute-value spectral constraints.
- Practical stochastic projected gradient scheme for KL(ψ||θ).
- Empirical gains over standard variational baselines under finite budgets.

Cons
- Reliance on a conservative mixing bound; experiments often exceed it, weakening guarantees without mixing diagnostics.
- Some technical typos/notation issues in key theorems/equations.
- Limited experimental scope; no end-to-end time/budget comparisons including projection cost.
- Piecewise KL shows limited benefit; analysis of when it helps is sparse.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide mixing diagnostics when using spectral thresholds > 1, and ablate performance vs the threshold.
- Include time-normalized comparisons that account for projection overhead.
- Clarify and correct Theorem 6/7 notation and provide a brief derivation sketch in the main text.
- Evaluate the bias from reusing the same samples in Eq. (8), or use two independent pools.
- Discuss extension beyond Ising (e.g., Potts, general MRFs) and alternative sufficient conditions (e.g., Dobrushin/Weitz/correlation decay bounds).

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a fresh and useful perspective with concrete algorithms and promising empirical results. Addressing clarity issues and providing stronger empirical evidence for mixing under relaxed thresholds would solidify the contribution.
