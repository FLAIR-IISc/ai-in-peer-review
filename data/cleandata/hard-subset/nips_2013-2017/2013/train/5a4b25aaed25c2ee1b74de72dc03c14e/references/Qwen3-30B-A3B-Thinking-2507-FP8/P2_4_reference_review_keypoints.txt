- Paper proposes shifting tractable graphical models from low-treewidth to "fast-mixing" models where Gibbs sampling converges rapidly.
- For Ising models, a sufficient condition for fast mixing is that the spectral norm of the interaction matrix is < 1.
- Authors develop projection methods to map arbitrary Ising parameters into the fast-mixing set by minimizing various divergences (Euclidean, zero-avoiding KL, piecewise KL, zero-forcing KL).
- Work builds on classic mixing-time theory (Hayes, Dyer et al.) and extends mean-field approaches by enlarging the tractable family.
- Experimental results show projected fast-mixing models outperform LBP, TRW, and MF in marginal accuracy while guaranteeing rapid convergence.
- Theorems (4–8) provide clear, well-supported conditions and methods for fast mixing and projection.
- Authors acknowledge limitations of the spectral norm bound (conservative, ~1.76 factor looser for 2D Ising) and empirically use a 2.5 threshold.
- Experimental design is thorough: multiple graph topologies (8×8 grids, random graphs), interaction strengths, and relevant baselines.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and reproducible with clear definitions and consistent notation.
- Novel contribution: redefining tractability via fast-mixing and introducing projection methods, notably the zero-forcing KL generalization of mean-field.
- Piecewise KL adaptation is a creative extension of piecewise likelihood/tree mixtures to the fast-mixing framework.
- The approach has substantial significance by providing better finite-time accuracy and guaranteed rapid Gibbs convergence for high-treewidth models.
- Strengths: novel perspective, solid theoretical foundation, multiple projection methods, comprehensive experiments, and transparent limitations.
- Weaknesses: conservative spectral norm bound, zero-avoiding KL is computationally intractable, piecewise KL underperforms true KL without deep analysis, and computational efficiency/time vs. accuracy comparisons could be clearer.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, impactful, and well-presented work that advances approximate inference.
- Recommendation: Accept.
