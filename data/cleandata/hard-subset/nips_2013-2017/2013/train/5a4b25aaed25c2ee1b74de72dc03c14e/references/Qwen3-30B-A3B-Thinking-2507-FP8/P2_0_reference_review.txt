

# Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models by redefining "tractability" not as structural simplicity (low-treewidth), but as the property of having parameters that guarantee rapid mixing for Gibbs sampling. The authors focus on Ising models, showing that if the spectral norm of the interaction matrix is less than 1, rapid mixing is guaranteed. They then develop projection methods to map arbitrary Ising model parameters onto this fast-mixing set under various divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, and reversed KL). The paper builds on prior work in mean-field inference and approximate inference methods like loopy belief propagation, but offers a fundamentally different perspective on tractability.

## Quality
The theoretical contributions are technically sound, with clear proofs for key results (Theorems 4, 7, 8). The connection between spectral norm and mixing time is well-established, and the projection method via singular value thresholding (Theorem 7) is a reasonable approach. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against standard methods (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs) on both grid and random graphs. 

However, the paper has a significant weakness: the spectral norm bound is too conservative. As acknowledged in Section 7, the theoretical bound (spectral norm < 1) is substantially looser than the known critical threshold for 2D Ising models (βc ≈ 0.4407), which would require a spectral norm bound of only 0.25 to guarantee mixing. The authors note they use a bound of 2.5 in practice, but don't adequately address why this works or how it relates to the theoretical bound. This undermines the theoretical foundation of their approach. The experimental results also don't sufficiently explore the trade-off between the spectral norm bound and approximation quality.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The background on mixing times and dependency matrices is clear, and the technical contributions flow logically. The notation is consistent, and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. The dual optimization approach in Theorem 7 is complex, but the paper provides sufficient explanation for experts. The writing is precise and avoids unnecessary jargon, making it accessible to NIPS readers.

## Originality
This is the paper's strongest aspect. The core idea of defining tractability through fast-mixing properties rather than structural properties is genuinely novel. Previous work (mean-field, structured mean-field, loopy BP) all relies on structural tractability, while this paper offers a fundamentally different perspective. The piecewise KL divergence approach is particularly creative and represents a meaningful extension of previous work on tractable subgraphs. The paper effectively contextualizes its contributions relative to prior work, with appropriate citations.

## Significance
The significance is moderately high. The problem of approximate inference in high-treewidth models is important, and the fast-mixing approach offers a potentially useful alternative to existing methods. The experimental results show the proposed methods outperform variational approaches (LBP, TRW, MF), which is promising. However, the practical impact is limited by the loose theoretical bound and the Ising-specific focus. The paper acknowledges these limitations (Section 7, Future Work), but doesn't sufficiently address how the looseness of the bound affects real-world applicability. The approach isn't immediately applicable to general MRFs without further theoretical work.

## Strengths vs. Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Novel concept of "fast-mixing" as tractability criterion (distinct from structural approaches)
2. Clear theoretical foundation with well-proven results
3. Well-executed experimental comparison against standard methods
4. Creative piecewise KL divergence approach
5. Exceptionally clear writing and organization

**Weaknesses:**
1. Theoretical bound (spectral norm < 1) is too conservative (loose by factor of ~1.76 for 2D Ising)
2. Limited to Ising models (not general MRFs), restricting broader applicability
3. Insufficient exploration of the trade-off between spectral norm bound and approximation quality
4. Piecewise KL approach doesn't perform as well as expected (Section 5.3)
5. Doesn't adequately address why the practical bound (2.5) works when the theoretical bound is 1

## Recommendation
This paper presents an interesting and novel perspective on approximate inference that merits attention at NIPS. The core idea of defining tractability through mixing time guarantees is significant and potentially influential. However, the looseness of the theoretical bound substantially limits the practical utility of the approach, and the paper doesn't sufficiently address this limitation. 

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a genuine contribution to the field, but the practical limitations and narrow scope prevent it from being a strong contribution. The authors should address the looseness of the spectral norm bound in a revision, perhaps by exploring tighter bounds or better justifying the practical choice of bound value.

The significance is moderate, as the approach is limited to Ising models and the theoretical bound is too conservative for practical use. However, the novel perspective on tractability is valuable, and with further work on tighter bounds, this could become a more significant contribution. The paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and practical performance.
