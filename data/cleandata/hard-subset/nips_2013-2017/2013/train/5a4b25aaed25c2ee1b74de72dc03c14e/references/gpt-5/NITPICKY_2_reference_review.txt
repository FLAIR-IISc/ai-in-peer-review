Summary
The paper proposes using “fast-mixing” parameter sets as a tractable approximation family for inference in Ising models. The key sufficient condition is a spectral-norm bound on the dependency matrix, upper-bounded by |β| via Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|. Given intractable parameters θ, the authors propose projecting onto a fast-mixing set Ψ under four divergences: (i) Euclidean (l2) distance with a dual projection method based on SVD thresholding; (ii) inclusive KL(θ||ψ) (impractical at scale due to expectations under θ); (iii) a novel piecewise KL that removes edges to form tractable subgraphs; and (iv) reverse KL(ψ||θ), optimized by stochastic approximation using Gibbs samples from ψ with repeated Euclidean projections to enforce the spectral-norm constraint. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare these projections to MF/LBP/TRW and Gibbs on original parameters; the authors claim improved marginal accuracy vs variational methods and better time–accuracy tradeoffs versus Gibbs, with a discussion of the looseness of the spectral-norm condition.

Strengths
- Interesting and original angle: using parameter-space fast-mixing constraints as the approximation family rather than low-treewidth or MF structure.
- Clear linkage to classical mixing results (Dyer et al., Hayes, Dobrushin), and sensible specialization to Ising via Rij ≤ tanh|βij|.
- Derivation for the reverse-KL gradient connects neatly to Covψ[f] (ψ−θ), enabling sampling-based optimization with ψ enforced to be fast-mixing.
- The “piecewise KL” idea is conceptually appealing and related to piecewise likelihood/train/tree mixtures.

Major weaknesses and technical concerns
- Apparent error in Theorem 6: for minB:||B||2≤c ||A−B||F, the optimal singular values are S′ii = min(Sii, c), not min(Sii, c^2). This is a serious technical mistake because it directly affects the projection.
- Definition 1 of total variation is missing the standard 1/2 factor. The displayed equation looks malformed (“1” on its own line).
- The primal projection (Eq. 1) imposes D=|B| exactly. This makes the feasible set likely nonconvex (absolute-value equality constraints). The paper claims an equivalent dual (Theorem 7) but provides neither a convincing convexity argument nor a proof in the main text; the appendix is not provided. LBFGS-B convergence and correctness for a nonconvex dual are not justified.
- The dual objective/gradient formulas in Theorem 7 contain notation and likely scaling errors: g(Λ,M) lacks the usual 1/2 factor for squared norms; “Λ′Z”/“Z′D” notation is unclear; the Πc[·] operator is under-specified in the constrained/sparse setting; and the triple-dot product is not consistently used. It is unclear how symmetry and zero-diagonal constraints are enforced for Ising β.
- The theory uses a mixing-time guarantee for random-scan Gibbs, but all experiments and the sample updates use systematic scan in fixed order. No discussion of how this affects guarantees or practical mixing.
- The authors explicitly choose a spectral norm bound 2.5 (>1.0) “since it preserves rapid mixing in practice,” which violates the stated sufficient condition. If guarantees are relaxed, diagnostics or empirical mixing time evidence should be provided; none is.
- Reverse-KL gradient estimator: the proposed estimator ĝ = (1/K) ∑ ((ψ−θ)·f(xk))(f(xk)−μ̂) equals a biased plug-in covariance estimator when using the same sample for μ̂. The text claims unbiasedness if independent estimates are used (true), then uses the same samples and states “makes little difference.” This should either use the standard unbiased covariance estimate or quantify the bias/variance tradeoff.
- Piecewise KL design choices are under-justified. Using maxT KL(θ(T)||ψ(T)) is non-smooth and potentially dominated by the worst subgraph; why not an average or weighted sum? How are T chosen to ensure adequate coverage and tightness?
- Experimental protocol is inconsistent and underspecified. The paper states both that marginals are computed by Gibbs and that “marginals [are] computed via the exact junction-tree algorithm.” For 8×8 grids, exact JT is feasible, but the procedure should be clearly defined and consistent across methods. Details such as LBP/TRW damping, iterations, stopping criteria, and whether TRW uses tree-reweighting with valid upper bounds are missing. Time–accuracy comparisons exclude the (potentially dominant) projection cost, biasing conclusions.
- No quantitative results are shown in the text (no tables/plots, no error bars), yet strong claims are made about superiority to MF/LBP/TRW and time–accuracy vs Gibbs. As written, these claims are unsupported.
- Numerous notational and formatting issues: missing or misplaced parentheses and factors (e.g., ln(n/ε) typeset as ln(n !)), undefined symbols, stray primes, missing symmetry constraint, and a dangling footnote marker “1” without content. References are incomplete (years/venues missing).

Quality
Given the errors in Theorem 6, the unclear/possibly incorrect dualization in Theorem 7, mismatch between theory (random scan) and practice (systematic scan), and unsupported empirical claims, the technical soundness is not at the standard required.

Clarity
The paper is difficult to follow in key technical sections due to notation errors and missing details. Many definitions and constraints (symmetry, diagonals) are not clearly stated. The experimental protocol is inconsistent.

Originality
The high-level idea is novel and interesting, and the reverse-KL projection with fast-mixing constraints is a nontrivial variant of MF. The piecewise-KL idea has precedents but is still a reasonable twist.

Significance
If made correct and empirically validated, this could be a useful addition to the toolbox for approximate inference. As presented, the impact is limited by correctness and empirical validation issues.

Pros
- Fresh perspective: tractability via fast-mixing sets.
- Potentially practical approach (reverse-KL with sampling) that generalizes MF.
- Honest discussion about looseness of spectral-norm bounds.

Cons
- Technical mistakes (SVD threshold), unclear/nonconvex dualization, and many notation issues.
- Guarantees are not respected in experiments (c>1, systematic scan); no diagnostics.
- Experimental evidence is insufficient and inconsistently described.
- Reproducibility is low.

Questions for authors
- Please correct Theorem 6 and clarify Theorem 7 with a rigorous derivation and convexity conditions; specify symmetry and diagonal constraints.
- How do you ensure fast mixing when using c=2.5? Can you provide empirical diagnostics or autocorrelation/mixing time estimates?
- Clarify whether marginals are computed by JT or Gibbs in all experiments; report exact settings for all baselines and include plots/tables with error bars and runtimes including projection cost.
- Justify using max over subgraphs in piecewise KL and discuss alternatives.
- Provide the unbiased covariance-based gradient estimator or quantify bias when reusing samples.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is promising, but the paper contains multiple technical errors, unclear derivations, inconsistent experimental methodology, and insufficient empirical evidence. I encourage a thorough revision addressing the above points; with corrected theory and solid experiments, this line of work could be impactful.

Confidence
High.
