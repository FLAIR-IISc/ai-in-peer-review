This paper introduces a fascinating and bold idea: approximating intractable distributions with “fast-mixing” counterparts. The core concept – leveraging the parameter space properties that guarantee rapid MCMC convergence – is genuinely novel and a significant departure from standard approximation techniques like mean-field or tree-based methods. The authors correctly identify a key weakness of many existing approaches: slow mixing can negate any gains from tractability. This work opens a new avenue for tackling intractable inference by shifting the focus from computational tractability of inference *algorithms* to properties of the distribution itself. 

The theoretical development, particularly Theorem 7 relating to Euclidean projection via SVD thresholding, is sound and well-presented. The exploration of different divergences (KL(q||p), piecewise KL, and KL(p||q)) and their associated computational challenges demonstrates a good understanding of the trade-offs involved.  The connection to existing mean-field methods via the zero-forcing KL-divergence is insightful.

However, the experimental evaluation feels somewhat underdeveloped given the potential of the idea. While the comparisons to LBP, TRW, and MF are useful, the gains aren’t dramatically shown. The sensitivity of performance to the spectral norm threshold (using 2.5 instead of the theoretically motivated 1.0) raises questions about the tightness of the theoretical guarantees and requires further investigation. It’s acknowledged that tuning the stochastic gradient descent parameters was not exhaustive, and this significantly limits the strength of the results. The limited scope of the experiments (small graphs, specific topologies) also makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of the approach. A more compelling demonstration on larger, more complex datasets would be crucial.

The writing is generally clear, but the technical details (particularly surrounding the divergences) could benefit from further simplification and intuitive explanation. The reference to Ergodic Mirror Descent feels somewhat tacked on, and a more detailed justification of its relevance would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High Novelty:** The core idea of leveraging fast-mixing properties for approximation is a genuinely new direction.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical development (particularly the projection theorem) is rigorous and well-motivated.
*   **Potential Significance:** This work could fundamentally shift how we approach intractable inference in graphical models.
*   **Bold Approach:**  The authors tackle a challenging problem with a creative and non-traditional solution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are not overwhelmingly convincing and lack breadth.
*   **Loose Theoretical Bound:** The observed need to relax the spectral norm constraint suggests the theoretical guarantees may be overly conservative.
*   **Complexity:** The various divergences and projection methods add complexity without clear demonstrable gains in all scenarios.
*   **Clarity in some sections:** Some of the theoretical explanations could be more accessible.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations in the empirical validation and some areas needing clarification, I strongly lean towards acceptance. The originality and potential significance of this work are exceptionally high. The authors have identified a crucial gap in the current approaches to approximate inference and propose a compelling alternative.  I believe the core idea deserves further exploration, and the paper’s contribution lies in sparking that exploration.  I recommend acceptance with a request for the authors to significantly expand the experimental evaluation, investigate the tightness of the spectral norm bound, and refine the clarity of certain sections.  The potential payoff of this research is substantial, and the minor flaws are outweighed by its innovative nature.
