**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in graphical models, specifically the Ising model. Instead of approximating an intractable, high-treewidth model with a structurally simple one (e.g., a fully-factorized or tree-structured distribution), the authors propose approximating it with a model from the set of "fast-mixing" distributions. A distribution is defined as fast-mixing if there are parameter-space conditions guaranteeing that Gibbs sampling will converge rapidly. For the Ising model, the authors leverage the known result that if the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths is less than one, the model is guaranteed to be fast-mixing.

The core of the paper is about "projecting" an arbitrary (potentially slow-mixing) set of Ising parameters onto this fast-mixing set. The authors investigate four different notions of distance or divergence for this projection:
1.  **Euclidean Distance:** They derive a novel and technically sound dual algorithm (Theorem 7) to find the closest fast-mixing parameter set in the Frobenius norm, while preserving the graph's sparsity.
2.  **"Zero-avoiding" KL(q||p):** This is probabilistically natural but computationally intractable for the target problem, as it requires expectations over the original slow-mixing distribution `q`.
3.  **Piecewise KL Divergence:** A novel tractable approximation to KL divergence, where the KL is computed on low-treewidth subgraphs (e.g., spanning trees or chains) of the original model.
4.  **"Zero-forcing" KL(p||q):** This divergence is more practical as it requires expectations over the approximating distribution `p`. Since `p` is constrained to be fast-mixing, these expectations can be estimated efficiently via Gibbs sampling. The overall optimization is performed using a stochastic projected gradient descent method.

The authors experimentally evaluate their projection methods on grid and random graphs, comparing the accuracy of marginal estimates against standard baselines like loopy belief propagation (LBP), tree-reweighted BP (TRW), naive mean-field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters. Their results show that the proposed methods, particularly the one based on the reversed KL-divergence, outperform the variational baselines and offer a better time-accuracy trade-off than running Gibbs on the original, slow-mixing model.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The central idea of defining a tractable family based on fast-mixing parameters rather than simple graph structure is highly original and significant. It opens up a new avenue for approximate inference that is conceptually different from the dominant variational paradigms. This could inspire a new line of research into other parameter-based conditions for tractability.
2.  **Strong Technical Contribution:** The derivation of the Euclidean projection algorithm in Theorem 7 is a solid technical achievement. The use of a dual formulation to handle the non-trivial constraints (spectral norm of the *absolute values* of the matrix, plus sparsity) is elegant and well-executed.
3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation, provides the necessary background on mixing times, and systematically introduces each of the four projection methods, clearly explaining the trade-offs between them. The experimental setup and results are also presented clearly.
4.  **Well-Designed Experiments:** The experiments are thorough and compare against the appropriate set of strong, widely-used baselines. The inclusion of a time-vs-accuracy comparison between sampling from the original model and the projected model is particularly insightful, as it directly validates the paper's core premise: for a finite computational budget, it can be better to sample from a slightly less accurate but fast-mixing model.
5.  **Intellectual Honesty:** The authors are transparent about the limitations of their work, particularly the conservative nature of the spectral norm bound for fast mixing (discussed in Section 7). This honesty strengthens the credibility of the paper.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The primary weakness is the disconnect between the theoretical guarantee and the experimental implementation. The theory guarantees fast mixing for a spectral norm less than 1.0. However, the authors state they use a bound of 2.5 in experiments because it "gives better approximation." While this is a reasonable pragmatic choice, it undermines the central claim of *guaranteed* fast mixing. The resulting model may be fast-mixing in practice, but it is no longer backed by the theory presented. A more detailed empirical analysis or discussion of why this much looser bound still works well in practice would have strengthened the paper.
2.  **Limited Scope (Ising Models):** The methods are developed and tested only for the pairwise binary Ising model. While the paper gestures towards future work on general MRFs, the current contribution is limited. Extending this work would require non-trivial effort, including deriving appropriate bounds on the dependency matrix for general potentials, which is a significant challenge.
3.  **Performance of Piecewise KL:** The proposed "piecewise" KL divergence is a novel idea, but the paper notes that it "does not perform as well." While its inclusion is fine, its practical utility seems limited based on the results, making it a less impactful contribution compared to the Euclidean and reversed-KL projection methods.
4.  **Scalability of Projection:** The Euclidean projection relies on an SVD, which has a cubic complexity (`O(n^3)`). While this is feasible for the graphs in the experiments (n=64 for grids), it would not scale to very large graphs with thousands of nodes. The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of the computational complexity and potential scalability limitations.

**Overall Recommendation**

This is a high-quality paper with a novel and compelling core idea. The proposal to use fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractable family for approximation is a significant conceptual contribution to the field of approximate inference. The technical execution is strong, the writing is clear, and the experiments convincingly demonstrate the value of the approach compared to established methods. While there is a notable gap between the theory and the practical implementation regarding the spectral norm bound, the authors are transparent about this, and the empirical results are strong regardless. The weaknesses are relatively minor compared to the strengths and mostly point towards interesting directions for future work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Highly original and significant core idea that could open a new research direction.
*   Strong technical contribution in the Euclidean projection algorithm.
*   Clear, well-written, and well-organized manuscript.
*   Convincing experimental results against strong baselines.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical implementation deviates from the theory by using a much looser spectral norm bound, weakening the "guarantee" of fast mixing.

This paper is a clear accept for NIPS. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and presents ideas that other researchers are likely to build upon.
