- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters into a fast-mixing set to enable rapid Gibbs sampling rather than restricting graph structure.  
- Shows a sufficient fast-mixing condition: spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix < 1, and develops projection methods under Euclidean, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences.  
- Theorem-based theoretical foundation linking dependency matrices and spectral norms to mixing guarantees (builds on Hayes).  
- Dual algorithm for Euclidean projection is technically sound and handles graph-structure constraints and spectral-norm control.  
- Experiments across graph types and interaction strengths show improved marginal accuracy over variational methods and are faster than running Gibbs on original slow-mixing parameters.  
- Argues practical relevance for quickly obtaining approximate marginals when chains mix slowly.  
- Major weakness: the spectral-norm bound is conservative (e.g., 2D Ising βc ≈ 0.4407 vs. spectral requirement β < 0.25), limiting practical utility.  
- Authors use a spectral-norm value of 2.5 in experiments without theoretical or empirical justification.  
- Scope is limited to Ising models; unclear if and how the approach generalizes to general MRFs.  
- Projection computation can be costly (e.g., 30,000 Gibbs iterations for Euclidean projection), with insufficient cost–benefit analysis versus running longer Gibbs chains.  
- Inadequate comparison to recent related work on fast-mixing conditions for MRFs (e.g., matrix-norm based results).  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on addressing the conservative bound, justifying practical choices, and better relating to recent literature.
