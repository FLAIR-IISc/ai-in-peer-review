### Summary ###

This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models. Instead of approximating an intractable high-treewidth model with a tractable low-treewidth one (as in mean-field or tree-reweighted BP), the authors define a "tractable" family of distributions as those that are guaranteed to be "fast-mixing" for Gibbs sampling. The paper leverages a known sufficient condition for rapid mixing: the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths, `|| |β| ||_2`, must be less than 1. The core contribution is to "project" an arbitrary (potentially slow-mixing) Ising model's parameters onto this fast-mixing set. The paper explores four projection methods based on different divergences: Euclidean distance, the standard KL-divergence (KL(q||p)), a novel "piecewise" KL approximation, and the reverse KL-divergence (KL(p||q)). For the Euclidean case, a concrete algorithm based on SVD thresholding is derived. For the reverse KL, a stochastic gradient descent approach is employed, where each step involves a Euclidean projection. Experiments on grid and random graphs compare these projection methods to standard baselines like LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs sampling on the original model, showing favorable performance in terms of marginal accuracy.

### Strengths ###

1.  **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The central idea of defining a tractable family based on dynamic properties (fast mixing) rather than static structural properties (low treewidth) is original and thought-provoking. It opens a new perspective on approximation in graphical models that connects the fields of variational inference and MCMC mixing time analysis.

2.  **Sound Technical Contribution:** The derivation of the Euclidean projection algorithm (Theorem 7) for finding the closest parameter matrix (in Frobenius norm) that respects a given sparsity pattern and satisfies the spectral norm constraint is a solid and non-trivial optimization result. This provides a methodologically sound building block for the other proposed techniques.

3.  **Connection to Established Methods:** The paper does a good job of contextualizing its work. Framing the reverse KL-projection as a generalization of naive mean-field (where the spectral norm is constrained to zero) is an insightful connection that helps ground the new approach in familiar concepts.

4.  **Reasonable Experimental Design:** The experiments are well-structured, comparing against a relevant and strong set of established baselines for approximate inference. The evaluation metric (mean absolute error of marginals computed via an exact solver) is appropriate and rigorous.

### Weaknesses ###

1.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice:** The paper's primary weakness lies in a significant disconnect between its theoretical motivation and its empirical execution. The entire premise rests on a formal guarantee of rapid mixing derived from Theorem 4, which requires `||R||_2 < 1`. However, the experiments are conducted with a spectral norm bound of 2.5. This choice is mentioned only briefly and justified with the hand-waving statement that it "can still preserve the rapid-mixing property and gives better approximation." This completely undermines the paper's core claim. The method is presented as one that *guarantees* fast mixing, but the experiments abandon this guarantee for an arbitrary, empirically chosen constant. The paper provides no evidence (e.g., convergence diagnostics, mixing time plots) that models with a spectral norm of 2.5 are, in fact, fast-mixing. This makes the central premise of the work feel unproven and ad-hoc.

2.  **Insufficient Validation of Proposed Methods:**
    *   The "piecewise KL" divergence is a novel idea but seems underdeveloped. The paper notes that "experimentally, this does not perform as well," but the experimental section provides scant detail on its performance, making it difficult to assess its utility. It feels more like a preliminary idea than a fully-vetted contribution.
    *   The stochastic gradient algorithm for the reverse KL projection involves several hyperparameters (step size, sample pool size, number of iterations) that were chosen without tuning or justification. The stability and performance of this method may be highly sensitive to these choices, which raises concerns about the robustness and reproducibility of the reported results.

3.  **Limited Scope and Practicality:** The work is confined to Ising models. While this is a common starting point, the specific bound `R_ij <= |β_ij|` is crucial to the method's simplicity. The paper acknowledges that extending this to general MRFs is a challenge but does not provide much insight into how this might be achieved. Furthermore, the core projection algorithm requires computing an SVD, which scales cubically with the number of variables, potentially limiting its application to the moderately-sized models where such approximations are most needed.

4.  **Looseness of the Bound:** As the authors correctly point out in the discussion, the spectral norm condition is known to be a loose, conservative bound for rapid mixing in many cases (e.g., the 2D Ising grid). While using a tighter, problem-specific bound would be ideal, the paper's solution is to discard the theoretical bound entirely in favor of an arbitrary larger one. This suggests that the underlying theoretical tool may not be sufficiently powerful for producing high-quality approximations, forcing the authors into an unprincipled empirical fix.

### Decision ###

This paper introduces an interesting and original idea for approximate inference. The technical derivation for the Euclidean projection is sound. However, the work is let down by a critical flaw in its experimental validation, where the theoretical guarantee that motivates the entire approach is abandoned in favor of an unjustified, arbitrary parameter choice. Without evidence that the resulting approximations are actually fast-mixing, the central claim of the paper is unsupported. The other proposed methods, particularly the piecewise KL, feel preliminary. While the direction is promising, the current work lacks the rigor and thoroughness expected for a NIPS publication. The gap between the theoretical promise and the empirical reality is too large to recommend acceptance in its present form.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a genuinely novel and creative idea: using fast-mixing as the criterion for a tractable approximate family.
*   Provides a solid technical result for the Euclidean projection problem (Theorem 7).
*   The connection to mean-field provides useful context.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental setup (using a spectral norm of 2.5) contradicts and undermines the paper's core theoretical motivation (using a bound of 1.0 to guarantee fast mixing).
*   No empirical evidence is provided to show that the resulting models are indeed "fast-mixing."
*   The novel piecewise KL method is not well-developed or validated.
*   The stochastic optimization procedure relies on untuned hyperparameters, casting doubt on the robustness of the results.
*   The underlying mixing time bound is acknowledged to be loose, which may limit the ultimate utility of the approach.
