This paper explores approximating intractable distributions with “fast-mixing” models, specifically within the Ising model framework. The core idea – projecting parameters onto a set guaranteeing rapid Gibbs sampling convergence – is conceptually interesting and connects to a long line of work on approximate inference, including mean-field variations and loopy belief propagation. However, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence that this approach meaningfully advances the state of the art, and relies heavily on theoretical explorations without sufficient validation against established baselines.

The paper is reasonably well-written and clearly lays out the theoretical groundwork, referencing relevant literature on Markov chain mixing times. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the work within the broader context of approximate inference. The mathematical derivations appear sound, particularly Theorem 7 regarding Euclidean projection. However, the numerous divergences considered feel somewhat disparate and lack a unifying principle beyond the goal of finding a “close” fast-mixing approximation.

My primary concern lies with the experimental evaluation. While the authors compare against LBP, TRW, and naive mean-field, the performance gains (or lack thereof) are not convincingly demonstrated. The statement that the proposed methods are “more accurate” than variational methods requires significant qualification, as the accuracy metric (mean absolute distance of marginals) isn’t thoroughly contextualized. The discussion of parameter tuning highlights a significant weakness: the sensitivity of the stochastic gradient descent approach to hyperparameters, and the lack of a systematic tuning strategy. Further, the assertion that the proposed method outperforms Gibbs sampling *given finite time* feels unsubstantiated. While Gibbs sampling can be slow, the comparison lacks a detailed analysis of computational cost versus accuracy trade-offs, making it difficult to assess the practical value of the projection. The experiments feel somewhat limited, primarily focusing on 8x8 grids and small random graphs. Scaling to larger, more complex graphs would be crucial to demonstrating the real-world applicability of this approach.

The looseness of the spectral norm bound, as acknowledged by the authors, is also concerning. Relying on a bound significantly exceeding the known critical threshold for mixing raises questions about the theoretical justification and practical effectiveness of the fast-mixing guarantee. While the authors suggest a relaxed bound works empirically, this weakens the core theoretical contribution of the work.

**Strengths:**

*   Conceptually interesting idea of leveraging fast-mixing properties for approximate inference.
*   Clear presentation of relevant theoretical background on Markov chain mixing times.
*   Derivation of a practical projection algorithm in the Euclidean norm (Theorem 7).

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited experimental validation and unclear performance gains.
*   Sensitivity to hyperparameters in the stochastic gradient descent approach.
*   Looseness of the spectral norm bound, diminishing the theoretical guarantee.
*   Lack of a unifying framework across the different divergence measures.
*   Insufficient scaling of experiments to more complex graphs.
*   The “piecewise” KL divergence does not perform as well as the true KL-divergence.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The idea of projecting onto a fast-mixing set is novel and potentially useful.
*   The theoretical analysis is sound and provides a foundation for further research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental results are not convincing and fail to demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods.
*   The practical applicability of the approach is questionable given the sensitivity to hyperparameters and the looseness of the theoretical bound.
*   The paper feels more like a preliminary investigation than a fully developed contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents an interesting idea and sound theoretical work, the lack of compelling experimental evidence and the practical concerns outweigh the novelty of the approach. The paper reads as a promising starting point for future research, but it does not currently meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. Substantial revisions, including more extensive experimental validation, a more rigorous analysis of computational cost, and a tighter theoretical justification for the spectral norm bound, would be required for reconsideration. The authors should focus on demonstrating a clear and measurable advantage over well-established inference algorithms on realistic problem instances.
