Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes “fast-mixing” parameter sets as a tractable family for approximate inference: instead of requiring exact inference on a low-treewidth structure, parameters are constrained so that single-site Gibbs sampling provably mixes rapidly. For Ising models, a sufficient condition is ||R||2 < 1, where R is the dependency matrix bounded by |β| elementwise (via tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|).
- Given arbitrary (possibly slow-mixing) parameters θ, the authors study projections ψ ∈ Ψ (the fast-mixing set) under several divergences: (i) Euclidean distance on parameters with a dual algorithm (Theorem 7) that enforces both a spectral norm constraint and sparsity; (ii) KL(θ||ψ) (“inclusive” KL)—theoretically appealing but impractical when θ is intractable; (iii) a novel piecewise KL based on exact KL on tractable subgraphs; and (iv) KL(ψ||θ) (“reverse” KL), minimized by stochastic projected gradient using Gibbs samples from ψ and Euclidean projections to enforce the mixing constraint. The reverse-KL gradient matches Covψ[f](ψ−θ).
- Experiments on 8×8 grids and 10-node random graphs compare the proposed projections to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original parameters. Results suggest the projected fast-mixing approximations yield more accurate marginals than standard variational methods and, for fixed sampling budgets, can outperform Gibbs on the original model. The paper is candid about the looseness of the spectral bound and the practical use of a larger threshold (2.5) than the theory (1.0) prescribes.

Relation to prior work
- Connects mixing-time theory (Dyer et al., Hayes; Lubetzky & Sly for 2D Ising thresholds) with approximate inference. Distinct from mean-field/structured mean field and TRW, which approximate via tractable graphs; here the tractable family is defined by dynamical (mixing) conditions.
- The Euclidean projection via SVD thresholding is standard; the novel contribution is the dual construction (Theorem 7) that enforces sparsity and constrains |B| rather than B directly. The reverse-KL projected SGD generalizes MF (recovered when R=0).
- The piecewise KL echoes piecewise likelihood and tree mixtures, but uses it as a divergence proxy for projection.

Quality
Strengths
- Theoretical underpinnings are sound where stated: the mixing-time condition via ||R||2, the dependency bound for Ising, and the reverse-KL gradient (covariance form) are correct. The projected SGD methodology and its relation to ergodic mirror descent are appropriate.
- Theorem 7 addresses two practical issues (sparsity preservation and controlling |B|) and provides a concrete optimization recipe (LBFGS-B with bounds).
- The experimental setup covers multiple graph families, interaction regimes (mixed/attractive), and compares to standard baselines.

Weaknesses
- The main guarantee (rapid mixing) is undermined in practice by using a spectral bound of 2.5 instead of 1.0. This is acknowledged but not quantified. Without diagnostics (e.g., autocorrelation times, spectral gap proxies, coupling bounds), it is unclear when the guarantee actually holds in experiments.
- Evaluation clarity: parts of Section 6 state marginals are computed via Gibbs sampling; elsewhere they say “with marginals computed via the exact junction-tree algorithm.” Please clarify: What serves as ground truth? How are approximate marginals estimated? Are the same estimators used across methods?
- Computational budget fairness: the reported accuracy omits the substantial cost of the projection (especially reverse-KL which runs many Gibbs updates internally). The time–accuracy discussion is qualitative; wall-clock comparisons or matched compute budgets would strengthen claims.
- Scalability and complexity are not analyzed. The dual projection still requires repeated SVDs and solving a constrained problem; complexity in n, edges, and impact on density after projection (even with the sparsity mask) need discussion.
- The piecewise KL divergence is interesting but underperforms; there is little analysis of why (e.g., choice of subgraphs, max vs average, sensitivity).
- Minor technical issues: Theorem 6 appears to set S′ii = min(Sii, c^2) although the spectral norm constraint suggests min(Sii, c); please correct. Section 3 cites “Section 3.2,” which does not exist; update cross-references.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; the background and motivation are clear. Proof details for Theorem 7 are deferred to an appendix (appropriate), but a brief sketch would help intuition.
- Please clarify: update scheme (random vs systematic scan) in both theory and experiments; treatment of fields α in the projection (fields unconstrained?); how the mask Z and absolute value constraint interact with signs in B and whether this biases attractive vs mixed couplings.

Originality
- The core idea—using fast-mixing parameter sets as the tractable family and projecting into them—is novel and interesting. The dual projection enforcing sparsity under a spectral constraint on |B| is technically nontrivial. The stochastic projected reverse-KL approach as a generalization of MF is a nice unification.

Significance
- This offers a new axis for approximate inference that practitioners might exploit, especially when they intend to use MCMC anyway and desire guaranteed (or at least promoted) fast mixing. However, the practical impact depends on (i) tighter mixing conditions (current bound is conservative), and (ii) clearer empirical evidence that the projections provide consistent gains under matched compute and maintain mixing guarantees.
- As a stepping stone, focusing on Ising is reasonable; path to general MRFs is outlined but open.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel perspective and algorithms (dual projection; projected reverse-KL).
- Solid theoretical framing; correct gradients; connection to MF.
- Empirical improvements over standard variational methods on common benchmarks.

Cons for acceptance
- Main guarantee relaxed in practice without adequate diagnostics.
- Ambiguities in evaluation protocol; compute fairness not established.
- Limited analysis of scalability and failure modes; piecewise KL underexplored.

Questions for authors
- Can you report effective sample sizes or integrated autocorrelation times to substantiate “fast mixing” under c=2.5?
- Please clarify the evaluation pipeline (ground truth vs estimates; per-method estimation).
- What is the computational complexity of one projection step (Euclidean and reverse-KL) and total runtime vs baselines?
- How sensitive are results to the spectral bound c and to sample-pool/stepsize choices?
- Does the projection alter signs or introduce bias for mixed vs attractive models due to the |B| constraint?

Minor comments/typos
- Theorem 6 likely needs S′ii = min(Sii, c), not c^2.
- Section number mismatch (“Section 3.2”).
- Small spelling/formatting issues: “zero-avoiding KL” vs “zero forcing.”

Overall recommendation
- Borderline/weak accept. The paper proposes a fresh and principled approximation paradigm with concrete algorithms and promising results. Strengthen the empirical evidence on mixing guarantees and clarify evaluation to solidify the contribution.
