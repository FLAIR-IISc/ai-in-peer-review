Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes using “fast-mixing” parameter sets as a tractable family for approximate inference in Ising models. The central idea is to take arbitrary (potentially slow-mixing) parameters θ and project them to ψ that are “close” under a chosen divergence while satisfying a sufficient condition for rapid mixing of single-site Gibbs. The condition is ||R||2 < 1, where R is a dependency matrix bounded by |β| in Ising models. The authors study four choices of divergence: (i) Euclidean distance in parameter space, with a dual projection algorithm (Theorem 7) based on SVD thresholding that aims to handle absolute values and preserve sparsity; (ii) KL(θ||ψ), deemed mostly theoretical due to the need for expectations under θ; (iii) a “piecewise” KL that operates on a set of low-treewidth subgraphs; and (iv) KL(ψ||θ), optimized by stochastic approximation using samples from ψ (since ψ is constrained to be fast-mixing).

Empirically, they compare their projections against LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original parameters on 8×8 grids and small random graphs, evaluating marginal accuracy. They also discuss time–accuracy trade-offs and acknowledge that the spectral-norm condition is conservative.

Strengths
- Conceptual bridge: The paper connects mixing-time theory (Dobrushin-type conditions, spectral norm bounds) with variational projection ideas, offering a tractable family that sits between fully factorized mean-field and the original model.
- Algorithmic idea: The dual formulation for Euclidean projection under a spectral-norm constraint with sparsity is interesting and, if correct, practically useful. The iterative projected-gradient framework for reverse-KL within the fast-mixing set is also natural.
- Awareness of limitations: The authors recognize the looseness of the spectral norm bound and the trade-offs between divergences. They reference relevant mixing-time and variational literature.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- Guarantees vs practice: The paper’s selling point is a guarantee of rapid mixing under ||R||2 < 1, yet the experiments use a bound of 2.5 “which still preserves the rapid-mixing property.” This directly undercuts the core guarantee. If the bound is violated, fast mixing is no longer ensured; the method becomes heuristic. At minimum, empirical mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation times, convergence of total variation bounds, coupling-based tests, or spectral-gap proxies) should be reported to justify the choice.
- Theorem 7 and optimization: The dual problem involves a projection Πc and absolute values with sparsity constraints. There is no discussion of convexity/concavity of the dual objective g or conditions under which LBFGS-B maximization converges to a global optimum. Since Πc is nonsmooth, the provided gradients require justification; subgradient vs gradient distinctions are not addressed. Complexity and runtime vs problem size are also not discussed.
- Reverse-KL gradient estimation: The claim about unbiasedness of the estimator when reusing the same samples is incorrect; even with independent sample sets, the estimator involves a product of random terms and needs care (control variates, independence, and finite variance conditions). Using a single Gibbs step per iteration and fixed stepsizes without schedule or convergence analysis weakens the methodological rigor. The connection to ergodic mirror descent is mentioned but not instantiated with the required schedules or proofs.
- Experimental methodology: There are inconsistencies. The text states both that marginals are computed via exact junction tree and that they are estimated by Gibbs; exact JT is infeasible on 8×8 grids with general interactions. Time–accuracy comparisons do not normalize for the potentially large cost of the projection step. Hyperparameters (stepsize, pool size) are fixed without sensitivity analysis. No quantitative results are shown here (means, variances, statistical tests), making it hard to assess claims of superiority over LBP/TRW/MF.

Clarity
- While the paper is mostly readable, key algorithmic details (pseudo-code, initialization, stopping criteria, handling of non-differentiability, computational complexity) are missing. The notation in the projection section (A,B,D,R,Z,M,Λ) is dense and would benefit from a clearer derivation and a self-contained proof sketch.
- The experimental section has contradictory statements about how “ground-truth” marginals are obtained.

Originality
- The notion of constraining to fast-mixing sets for inference is not new in spirit (Dobrushin-like conditions have long been used), but the projection viewpoint and the dual algorithm for spectral-norm-constrained Euclidean projection appear novel for Ising models. The piecewise KL is a variant of piecewise likelihood/trachtable subgraph approximations; the contribution here is its use for projection. The reverse-KL within the fast-mixing set can be viewed as extending mean-field (zero-norm R) to a larger class.

Significance
- Potentially useful idea, but as presented the impact is limited by the looseness of the condition and the lack of adherence to it in experiments, limited scale of evaluation, and unclear computational trade-offs. Without rigorous guarantees or strong empirical evidence on larger models, it is difficult to recommend adoption over established baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Either enforce ||R||2 ≤ 1 in all experiments or empirically certify mixing (e.g., coupling time bounds, Geweke/PSRF across multiple chains, integrated autocorrelation times) when using looser thresholds. Provide mixing-time vs threshold plots.
- Clarify Theorem 7: prove concavity of the dual or state nonconvexity and provide convergence guarantees to stationary points; justify gradients for Πc; give complexity analysis and pseudo-code.
- Clean up the experimental protocol: clarify how ground-truth marginals are obtained; report wall-clock time including projection; add sensitivity analyses for stepsize, pool size, number of Gibbs updates; include larger graphs; and provide quantitative summaries with confidence intervals.
- Analyze the piecewise KL: theoretical properties (bounds on true KL? conditions under which it is informative); ablate subgraph choices and coverage strategies.
- Correct the unbiasedness discussion for gradient estimation and consider variance-reduction techniques.

Arguments for acceptance
- Interesting and potentially practical notion of a fast-mixing tractable family for inference.
- Novel projection formulation with a dual algorithm that may be useful beyond this paper.
- Bridges MCMC mixing theory and variational inference.

Arguments against acceptance
- Core guarantee is not respected in experiments; no empirical mixing verification.
- Theoretical development (dual optimization) lacks rigor on convexity/smoothness and convergence.
- Experimental evaluation is small scale, methodologically inconsistent, and lacks fair time accounting and statistical rigor.
- The proposed divergences beyond Euclidean are ad hoc or insufficiently analyzed.

Recommendation: Reject. The idea is promising, but the paper requires stronger theoretical grounding of the projection method, adherence to or verification of mixing guarantees, and more rigorous, scalable empirical evaluation.
