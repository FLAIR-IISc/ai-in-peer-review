Summary
The paper proposes using “fast-mixing” distributions as a tractable family for approximate inference and approximation of intractable Ising models. The key idea is to project a given parameter vector θ onto a subset Ψ of parameter space where single-site Gibbs sampling is guaranteed (or at least intended) to mix rapidly. For Ising models with binary spins, the sufficient condition used is that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix R (bounded by |β| elementwise; Lemma 5) satisfies ∥R∥2 < 1, invoking Theorem 4 ([8]).

Four projection objectives are considered:
- Euclidean projection in parameter space: the authors derive a dual algorithm (Theorem 7) to project onto the fast-mixing set while preserving sparsity, based on thresholding singular values with a mask and optimizing a dual via L-BFGS-B.
- Inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ): theoretically appealing (“zero-avoiding”) but intractable at scale due to requiring expectations under θ.
- A novel “piecewise” KL built from exact KL on tractable subgraphs T (trees or low-treewidth subgraphs), using a max over T to define the divergence and optimizing via projected gradient.
- Reverse KL KL(ψ||θ): “zero-forcing,” optimized by stochastic projected gradient; gradients are estimated from samples from ψ (feasible because ψ is constrained to be fast-mixing). The gradient formula (Theorem 8) reduces to Covψ[f](ψ−θ).

Experiments on 8×8 grids and random graphs (n=10) compare Euclidean, piecewise, and reverse-KL projections against MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs on the original parameters. The authors report better accuracy than standard variational methods, and favorable time–accuracy comparisons with Gibbs under finite budgets. They also note that using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of 1.0 “preserves rapid-mixing in practice” and gives better approximations.

Relation to prior work
The fast-mixing condition relates to Dobrushin-type and spectral norm criteria ([4], [8]), and to known thresholds for 2D Ising ([11]). The piecewise idea is inspired by piecewise likelihood and tree mixtures ([15], [17]); connections to mean-field and TRW/Bethe approximations are discussed. The paper positions reverse-KL with fast-mixing constraints as a generalization of MF.

Strengths
- Conceptually fresh: defining tractability via mixing guarantees rather than graph structure adds a complementary axis to variational families.
- Technically interesting Euclidean projection (Theorem 7): sparsity-preserving projection under spectral norm on |B| via a dual formulation is a nontrivial contribution.
- Clear link between reverse-KL projection with fast-mixing constraints and mean-field; solid gradient derivation consistent with Covariance form.
- Pragmatic optimization framework: projected (stochastic) gradient with Euclidean projection; discussion of Ergodic Mirror Descent as a justification.
- The “piecewise KL” is novel and easy to compute; generality to low-treewidth subgraphs is flexible.

Weaknesses
- Core guarantee vs practice mismatch: the theoretical sufficient condition requires ∥R∥2 < 1, yet experiments often use a bound of 2.5 without justification. This undermines the central selling point (guaranteed fast mixing).
- Empirical scope is limited (8×8 grids, n=10 random graphs), with relatively light sensitivity analyses and no rigorous mixing diagnostics (e.g., spectral gaps, autocorrelation, PSRF) to validate the “fast-mixing” claim when using looser bounds.
- Fairness of the runtime comparison is unclear. The projection-based methods incur substantial precomputation (e.g., 30k Gibbs iterations for gradient estimates), but the reported time–accuracy comparisons focus on the final Gibbs stage only.
- The dual projection (Theorem 7) is insufficiently detailed: convexity of the constrained problem (due to D=|B|) and guarantees for dual ascent (existence/uniqueness, convergence) are not fully clarified. The practical behavior of the projection (e.g., how often it densifies parameters if sparsity constraint is enforced via Z) would benefit from analysis.
- The piecewise KL objective uses a max over subgraphs; subgradient handling and stability are not discussed, and the connection of this surrogate to the full KL is heuristic.
- Clarity/consistency issues in the experimental description: at points the text suggests using exact junction-tree marginals for evaluation but also mentions estimating marginals via a single Gibbs chain; this needs disambiguation.

Assessment by criteria
- Quality: Mixed. The idea is sound and some derivations are correct (reverse-KL gradient). However, the main guarantee is not respected in experiments; theoretical aspects of the Euclidean projection and convergence of the overall projected stochastic scheme are underdeveloped. Empirical evaluation is small and lacks robust diagnostics.
- Clarity: Generally well-written, with helpful background. Some notational details (triple dot product), missing proof details (Theorem 7), and experimental ambiguities detract.
- Originality: Good. Projecting onto fast-mixing sets and the dual sparsity-preserving projection are novel (to my knowledge), and “piecewise KL” is a creative surrogate.
- Significance: Potentially moderate. If guarantees can be preserved or empirically validated, the approach could become a useful addition to the inference toolbox, especially as a bridge between MCMC and variational methods. Current empirical scope and guarantee-practice gap limit impact.

Pros for acceptance
- New perspective on tractability (mixing-based) with concrete algorithms.
- Interesting dual projection method and connection to MF via reverse-KL.
- Empirical indications of improvements over MF/LBP/TRW on tested settings.

Cons for acceptance
- Violation/relaxation of the key sufficient condition in experiments without theoretical or empirical justification.
- Limited, small-scale experiments and lack of rigorous mixing diagnostics.
- Incomplete theoretical treatment of the projection’s properties and convergence.
- Unclear fairness in time–accuracy comparisons.

Suggestions for improvement
- Either adhere to ∥R∥2 < 1 in experiments, or justify larger thresholds: provide empirical mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation times, effective sample sizes, coupling bounds) across regimes, or theoretical arguments showing a broader safe region for specific graph classes.
- Expand experiments: larger grids, higher n for random graphs, attractive vs mixed regimes, ablations on sample pool size, step size, number of iterations, and projection frequency; include wall-clock comparisons that account for projection cost.
- Clarify evaluation protocol: whether marginals are computed by JT (when feasible) or approximated by Gibbs; ensure consistent and fair comparison across methods.
- Provide more details and proofs for Theorem 7: convexity, strong duality conditions, convergence guarantees; discuss computational complexity and scalability of the projection.
- Analyze the piecewise KL: subgradient method, sensitivity to the choice of subgraphs, and empirical correlation with full KL or marginal errors.
- Consider alternative sufficient conditions/metrics (e.g., Dobrushin’s ∥R∥1 < 1) and block Gibbs bounds; compare projections under different norms.
- Report mixing time bounds instantiated with the learned ∥R∥2 to give actionable guarantees, even if conservative.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper introduces a promising direction and some neat techniques, but the experimental methodology and theoretical underpinnings need strengthening—especially the disconnect between the stated guarantees and the conditions used in practice, and the limited empirical validation. With the suggested revisions, this could become a solid contribution.

Confidence: Medium-high
