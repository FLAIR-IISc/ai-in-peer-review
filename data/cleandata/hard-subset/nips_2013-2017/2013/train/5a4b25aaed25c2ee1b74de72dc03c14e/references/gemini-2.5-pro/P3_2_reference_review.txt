### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a new approach to approximate inference in intractable Ising models. Instead of approximating a high-treewidth model with a low-treewidth one (as in mean-field or structured variational methods), the authors propose approximating it with a "fast-mixing" model. A model is defined as fast-mixing if its parameters satisfy a condition—specifically, that the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths is less than one—which guarantees rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling.

The core of the paper is the idea of "projecting" the parameters of an arbitrary (potentially slow-mixing) Ising model onto this set of fast-mixing parameters. The authors explore four different projection methods based on four divergence measures:
1.  **Euclidean Distance:** An efficient algorithm based on a dual formulation and iterative SVD thresholding is derived to find the closest fast-mixing parameters in the L2 norm sense.
2.  **"Zero-avoiding" KL(q||p):** This is the standard KL divergence used in variational inference. It is theoretically appealing but intractable for this problem, as it requires computing expectations over the original, slow-mixing distribution `q`.
3.  **Piecewise KL:** A novel, tractable approximation to KL(q||p) that involves computing exact KL divergences on a collection of low-treewidth subgraphs of the original model.
4.  **"Zero-forcing" KL(p||q):** This divergence requires expectations over the approximating distribution `p`. Since `p` is constrained to be fast-mixing, these expectations can be estimated efficiently via MCMC. The overall optimization is performed using projected stochastic gradient descent.

Experiments on grid and random graphs show that the proposed methods, particularly the one based on minimizing KL(p||q), outperform standard baselines like Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted BP (TRW), and Naive Mean Field (MF) in terms of marginal accuracy. They also demonstrate a favorable time-accuracy trade-off compared to running Gibbs sampling directly on the original slow-mixing model.

### Strengths and Positive Aspects

1.  **Conceptual Novelty (Originality & Significance):** The central idea of defining a tractable family based on mixing time rather than graphical structure is highly original and significant. It reframes the problem of approximate inference in a new light, connecting the fields of variational inference and MCMC theory in a practical way. This is a genuinely new perspective that could inspire a new class of inference algorithms.

2.  **Technical Soundness (Quality):** The paper is technically solid. The derivation of the Euclidean projection via a dual formulation (Theorem 7) is elegant and non-trivial. The formulation of the KL(p||q) minimization as a projected stochastic gradient descent problem is well-conceived and connects the work to established optimization literature (e.g., Ergodic Mirror Descent). The theoretical motivation is well-grounded in classic results on MCMC mixing times [4, 8].

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The proposed methods, especially the KL(p||q) projection, consistently outperform widely-used and strong baselines (LBP, TRW, MF). The comparison showing that Gibbs sampling on the projected parameters can yield more accurate marginals in a fixed amount of time than Gibbs on the original parameters is a powerful demonstration of the method's practical utility.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It provides clear background on mixing time conditions, logically develops the different projection methods from simplest to most complex, and presents the experimental setup and results effectively.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

1.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice:** The most significant weakness is the discrepancy between the theoretical motivation and the experimental implementation. The theory guarantees fast mixing for a spectral norm bound of `c=1`, but the experiments use `c=2.5`, stating that this value "can still preserve the rapid-mixing property and gives better approximation." This is a major issue.
    *   It undermines the "guarantee" of fast mixing, which is the foundational premise of the work. While the bound of 1 is known to be conservative, using an empirically chosen value of 2.5 without any theoretical or even strong heuristic justification makes the method feel more like a heuristic than a principled projection.
    *   The paper would be much stronger if it included an analysis of this choice. For instance, an ablation study showing performance vs. the spectral norm bound would be very informative. A more in-depth discussion on the looseness of the Hayes [8] bound and why a larger value might be acceptable in practice is needed.

2.  **Computational Cost of Projection:** The paper does not adequately address the computational cost of the projection step itself, which is a critical aspect for practical applications.
    *   The Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) requires an SVD at each step of an iterative L-BFGS-B optimization. For a graph with `n` nodes, the SVD is O(n³), making it intractable for large-scale problems.
    *   The stochastic KL(p||q) projection requires running many MCMC steps within an optimization loop. While the paper mentions the parameters used (500 samples, 60 iterations), it's hard to gauge the total computational budget required to obtain the projection and how it scales. The final time-accuracy plots compare inference *after* projection, but the cost of the projection itself should be factored into the overall comparison.

3.  **Limited Scope and Generalizability:** The entire methodology is developed and tested for the pairwise binary Ising model. While a valuable starting point, the extension to general discrete MRFs (e.g., Potts models) is non-trivial. The key bound `Rij <= tanh|βij|` is specific to the Ising model. The authors mention this as future work, but a brief discussion of the specific challenges (e.g., how to bound the dependency matrix for general potentials) would strengthen the paper and manage reader expectations.

4.  **Underdeveloped Piecewise KL Divergence:** The piecewise KL is introduced as a novel idea but is quickly dismissed as not performing well experimentally, with no further analysis. Why does it not perform well? Is the issue with the choice of subgraphs? This idea seems promising and more principled than the Euclidean distance, so its poor performance warrants deeper investigation. As it stands, it feels like an interesting but incomplete contribution.

### Summary of Recommendation

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Highly novel and significant conceptual contribution: defining tractability via mixing time.
*   Strong technical derivations for the projection algorithms.
*   Convincing empirical results that demonstrate state-of-the-art performance against strong baselines.
*   The paper is well-written and clear.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   A critical disconnect between the theoretical guarantee (spectral norm < 1) and the practical implementation (spectral norm < 2.5), which undermines the core premise.
*   The computational cost of the projection step is potentially high and not fully analyzed.
*   The scope is limited to Ising models, with unclear generalization paths.

**Recommendation:** I lean towards **accepting** this paper. The novelty of the core idea and the quality of the results outweigh the weaknesses. The connection between variational inference and MCMC mixing time is a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. However, I strongly urge the authors to address the weaknesses in their camera-ready version, particularly by providing a more thorough justification or analysis of their choice of the spectral norm bound, and by discussing the computational complexity of the projection more explicitly. The paper introduces a promising research direction that is sure to stimulate further work.
