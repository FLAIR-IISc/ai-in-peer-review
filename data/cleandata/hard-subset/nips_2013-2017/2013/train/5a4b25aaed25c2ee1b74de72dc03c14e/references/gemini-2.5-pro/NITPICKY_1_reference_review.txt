This paper introduces a novel approach for approximate inference in Ising models. The central idea is to approximate an intractable (slow-mixing) distribution by projecting its parameters onto the set of "fast-mixing" distributions. The authors define this tractable set using a parameter-space condition—specifically, that the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths is less than one, which guarantees rapid convergence for Gibbs sampling. The paper explores this projection under four different divergences: Euclidean distance, the "zero-avoiding" KL(q||p), a novel piecewise KL approximation, and the "zero-forcing" KL(p||q). For the Euclidean case, a dual algorithm is derived. For the tractable KL(p||q) case, a stochastic projected gradient descent method is employed. This work relates to the broad field of variational inference but expands the notion of a "tractable family" from one defined by structural simplicity (e.g., fully factorized in mean-field) to one defined by parametric conditions that ensure efficient sampling.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this paper is its originality. The proposal to define a tractable family of distributions based on mixing-time guarantees, rather than graphical structure, is a conceptually novel and interesting contribution to the field of approximate inference. It provides a new perspective on the trade-offs between model expressiveness and computational tractability. The technical development for the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) appears solid and provides a necessary building block for the other methods. The paper is thorough in exploring several different divergences for performing the projection, which adds depth to the investigation.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the novelty of the core idea, the paper is undermined by a significant number of technical and expositional flaws, ranging from minor formatting issues to a fundamental contradiction in the experimental setup.

**Clarity and Organization:**
1.  The structure of the paper is somewhat convoluted. Section 4 is dedicated entirely to deriving the Euclidean projection, which is then presented in Section 5.1 as just one of four divergences. A clearer organization would have been to introduce the general optimization problem (Eq. 5) first, and then present the Euclidean projection as a general-purpose subroutine required for the projected gradient steps used to solve the optimization for other divergences.
2.  The notation `KL(θ||ψ)` is used throughout Section 5. This is imprecise; the KL divergence is defined between distributions, not parameter vectors. The correct notation would be `KL(p(x; θ)||p(x; ψ))`. While the meaning is inferable, this lack of precision is persistent.
3.  The motivation for the piecewise KL divergence in Section 5.3 is weak. The footnote "if θ and ψ define the same distribution, then if a certain set of edges are removed from both, they should continue to define the same distribution" is a tautology and does not justify the `max` formulation. A sum over subgraphs, akin to composite likelihood, would be a more standard choice, and the paper provides no justification for its `max` operator.
4.  The references are poorly formatted. There are inconsistencies in whether conference/journal names are included, and some entries (e.g., [13]) are incomplete. The proof for Theorem 7 is cited as being in the appendix, but no appendix was provided with the submission, making it impossible to verify this key result.

**Technical Soundness and Methodological Rigor:**
1.  **Unsupported Claims:** Lemma 5 states that a result from Hayes [8] for a specific case can be extended via "simple modifications." This is insufficient for a formal publication. Such a foundational result for the paper's method should be explicitly proven.
2.  **Stochastic Gradient Estimation:** In Section 5.4, the gradient for KL(ψ||θ) is estimated using the same set of samples to compute both the mean `µ̂` and the expectation in Eq. 8. The paper correctly notes that independence is required for an unbiased estimate and then dismisses the issue by claiming it "makes little difference" in practice. This is an unsupported empirical claim that ignores the known bias introduced by this procedure.
3.  **Convergence of Optimization:** The paper references Ergodic Mirror Descent [3] to support its stochastic optimization procedure, but then states it uses a constant step size. This violates the typical requirements for guaranteed convergence of stochastic gradient methods, which usually require a diminishing step-size schedule.
4.  **The Core Contradiction:** The most significant flaw is the disconnect between the paper's theory and its experiments. The entire premise is built on projecting parameters into a set guaranteed to be fast-mixing by Theorem 4, which requires `||R||₂ < 1`. However, Section 6.1 states: "we find in practice using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of 1.0 can still preserve the rapid-mixing property and gives better approximation." This decision fundamentally undermines the paper's central claim. If the method only works well in practice when the theoretical guarantee is violated, then the utility of the guarantee itself is questionable. The paper offers no empirical evidence (e.g., mixing-time plots) that a spectral norm of 2.5 "preserve[s] the rapid-mixing property," rendering the name "projection for fast mixing" misleading. The discussion acknowledges the bound is loose, but this does not excuse using a completely different, theoretically unjustified bound in the experiments that are meant to validate the approach.
5.  **Vague Experimental Details:** The description of the optimization parameters ("Without intensive tuning...") and the Gibbs sampling protocol for evaluation (unspecified number of samples and burn-in) makes the results difficult to interpret and reproduce.

**Summary of pros and cons:**

**Pro:**
*   Presents a novel and conceptually interesting idea: defining tractability via mixing-time conditions rather than graph structure.
*   The exploration of multiple divergences is comprehensive.
*   The method shows promising empirical results compared to standard baselines.

**Con:**
*   Major contradiction between the theoretical motivation (`||R||₂ < 1`) and the experimental implementation (`||R||₂ ≤ 2.5`), which invalidates the primary justification for the method.
*   Several technical claims are hand-wavy or lack rigorous support (e.g., Lemma 5, bias in gradient estimation).
*   The paper suffers from unclear exposition, imprecise notation, and poor formatting.
*   Key proofs are missing (appendix not included).

**Recommendation:**
While the core idea presented in this paper has merit and potential, the execution is too flawed for publication at NIPS in its current form. The contradiction between the theory and the experiments is a critical issue that invalidates the paper's central premise. Combined with the numerous minor issues regarding clarity, technical rigor, and reproducibility, the paper does not meet the high standards of the conference. I recommend **rejection**. The authors are encouraged to either justify their experimental choices with new theory or conduct new experiments that align with their existing theoretical claims.
