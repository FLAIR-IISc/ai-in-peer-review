This paper investigates approximating intractable distributions with "fast-mixing" distributions, specifically within the context of Ising models. The core idea is to project parameters onto a set that guarantees rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling, offering a potentially more efficient alternative to standard variational inference methods like mean-field or loopy belief propagation. The authors explore several projection strategies, including Euclidean projection via SVD thresholding and projections based on different KL divergences, with varying computational complexities.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical challenge in graphical model inference. The connection to existing work on mixing times and the exploration of different divergence measures for projection are strengths. The theoretical derivation of the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a valuable contribution, and the experimental evaluation provides a reasonable comparison to existing inference algorithms. The discussion of the limitations of the spectral norm bound is appreciated and demonstrates careful consideration of the work's scope.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in certain areas and could benefit from a more thorough exploration of the trade-offs between different approaches. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of explicitly targeting fast-mixing distributions as an approximation strategy is a novel contribution.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The paper provides a theoretical basis for the Euclidean projection, grounding the method in established results on singular value decomposition and spectral norms.
* **Comprehensive exploration:** The authors investigate multiple divergence measures for projection, offering a nuanced view of the problem.
* **Experimental validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods compared to several baselines.
* **Honest discussion of limitations:** The authors acknowledge the looseness of the spectral norm bound, indicating a realistic assessment of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of presentation:** The paper jumps between different concepts and notations quickly. For example, the introduction of the piecewise KL-divergence and its connection to tractable subgraphs is not fully explained. More intuitive explanations would improve understanding.
* **Practicality of KL divergence projections:** While the theoretical discussion of KL divergence projections is interesting, their practical utility is limited by the need for sampling, as acknowledged. The paper doesn’t fully explore practical approximations to overcome this.
* **Limited experimental scope:** The experiments focus primarily on Ising models. While this provides a clear evaluation setting, it doesn’t demonstrate the generalizability of the approach to other graphical models.
* **Lack of detailed analysis of computational cost:** The paper mentions computational complexity, but a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of each projection method, especially in relation to the size of the graph, would be valuable.
* **Connection to existing work:** While related work is cited, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how this work relates to, and differs from, existing approaches for accelerating MCMC, such as overrelaxation or preconditioning.  For example, the connection between the spectral norm bound and graph partitioning/clustering techniques for MCMC acceleration isn't explicitly discussed.

**Specific suggestions:**

* **Clarify the piecewise KL-divergence:** Provide a more detailed explanation of how the subgraphs are selected and how the KL divergence is calculated. Consider a small example to illustrate the process.
* **Explore approximations for KL divergence projections:** Investigate techniques for approximating the expectations required for the KL divergence projections, such as using importance sampling or variational inference.
* **Expand experimental evaluation:** Evaluate the methods on a wider range of graphical models and datasets.
* **Provide a computational complexity analysis:** Analyze the computational cost of each projection method as a function of the graph size and sparsity.
* **Discuss connections to related work:** Elaborate on the connections between this work and existing methods for accelerating MCMC.
* **The statement regarding spectral norm and βc discrepancy needs further investigation:** The empirical finding that a spectral norm of 2.5 works well, while the theoretical bound is ~0.44, is interesting but not sufficiently explored. More analysis is needed to understand why this happens and whether the spectral norm is actually a useful indicator in practice.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to approximate inference.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
* The authors acknowledge the limitations of the work and suggest directions for future research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The clarity of the presentation could be improved.
* The practical applicability of some of the proposed methods is limited.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of approximate inference. While there are some areas for improvement, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical grounding, and the experimental validation make it a worthwhile contribution. However, the weaknesses regarding clarity and practicality prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form.  I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit, addressing the concerns raised above.  Addressing the clarity issues and expanding the experimental evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper.
