## NIPS Review: Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing

**Summary:**

This paper explores a novel approach to approximating intractable distributions in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically the Ising model. Instead of relying on traditional tractable families like fully-factorized distributions or trees, the authors propose projecting onto the set of “fast-mixing” distributions – those with parameterizations guaranteeing rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling. They investigate several divergence measures (Euclidean, KL with different orientations, and a piecewise approximation) to define this projection, and provide theoretical results for the Euclidean case. The paper then presents experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach in approximating Ising models, comparing it to established inference methods like LBP, TRW, and MF.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon a rich history of approximating inference in intractable graphical models. It positions itself between mean-field approaches (like [14, 16, 20]), which aim to minimize KL-divergence to tractable families, and methods leveraging tree-based approximations (like [19, 6]).  Crucially, it diverges from these by defining tractability not by structural constraints on the graph but by properties of the parameter space guaranteeing fast mixing. The connection to work on mixing times [10, 4, 8, 11] is central and well-articulated. The piecewise KL-divergence draws inspiration from work on piecewise likelihoods [17, 15], and the use of stochastic gradient descent is reminiscent of Ergodic Mirror Descent [3]. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of leveraging fast-mixing properties as a notion of tractability is genuinely novel and offers a fresh perspective on approximate inference.  It provides a potentially useful alternative to standard structural approximations.
* **Theoretical Foundations:** The theoretical analysis, particularly Theorem 7 relating to Euclidean projection, is solid and provides a concrete algorithm.  The connections to mixing time bounds are clearly explained and correctly applied.
* **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing against a variety of established inference techniques across different graph topologies and interaction strengths.  The inclusion of time-accuracy tradeoffs is valuable.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, clearly outlining the motivation, methodology, and experimental setup.
* **Practicality:** The stochastic gradient descent approach for minimizing the zero-forcing KL divergence offers a potentially scalable solution, especially when exact computation is intractable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conservatism of Spectral Norm Bound:** The authors acknowledge the potential looseness of the spectral norm bound for fast mixing (discussed in Section 7), and this is a significant concern. While sufficient, a tighter bound would increase the practical utility of the approach. The experimental validation using a bound of 2.5 suggests the authors are aware of this limitation, but further investigation into tighter bounds would strengthen the work.
* **Piecewise KL Divergence Performance:** The piecewise KL divergence, while theoretically interesting, does not perform as well as other approaches in the experiments.  More investigation into how to effectively choose the subgraphs *T* might improve its performance, but the current results suggest limited practical value.
* **Computational Cost of Projection:** While Gibbs sampling on the projected parameters is faster, the projection itself can be computationally expensive, particularly for the KL-divergence based approaches. The paper doesn’t provide a detailed analysis of the overall computational cost compared to other inference methods.
* **Limited Scope:** The focus is primarily on the Ising model. While a natural starting point, it's unclear how easily these ideas generalize to other graphical model structures or energy functions.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a genuinely novel and theoretically sound approach to approximate inference.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, especially the zero-forcing KL-divergence approach, compared to established alternatives.
* The clear writing and thorough evaluation make the paper accessible and informative.
* The work opens up a new research direction by framing tractability in terms of mixing properties.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The conservatism of the spectral norm bound limits the practical applicability of the approach.
* The piecewise KL-divergence does not perform well and adds complexity without significant benefit.
* The computational cost of the projection step is not fully addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel idea and solid technical foundations.  While the spectral norm bound is a concern, the experimental results and clear presentation justify acceptance. The work provides a valuable contribution to the field of approximate inference, opening up a promising new avenue for research.

**Rating:**  **Accept (7/10)**

**Recommendations:**

* The authors should investigate tighter bounds on the spectral norm for fast mixing.
* A more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the projection step is needed.
* Future work should explore the generalization of these ideas to other graphical model structures.
* Consider including a brief discussion of potential applications where this method might be particularly beneficial.
