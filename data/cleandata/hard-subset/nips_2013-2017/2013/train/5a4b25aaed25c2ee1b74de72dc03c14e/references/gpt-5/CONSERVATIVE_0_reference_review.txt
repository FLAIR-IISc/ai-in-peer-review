Summary
The paper proposes using “fast-mixing” Ising models—those satisfying parameter-space conditions guaranteeing rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs—as a tractable approximation family. The key sufficient condition is ||R||2 < 1 for the dependency matrix R (Hayes; Dyer et al.). For Ising models, Lemma 5 bounds R in terms of pairwise couplings via tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|, yielding a practical spectral-norm constraint on the absolute pairwise interaction matrix. The authors then:

- Develop an Euclidean (l2) projection of arbitrary parameters onto the fast-mixing set, via a dual formulation that (a) preserves sparsity according to a mask and (b) uses SVD singular-value thresholding (Theorem 7).
- Explore several divergences for projection: l2, inclusive KL KL(q||p) (theoretical only), a novel “piecewise” KL based on tractable subgraphs, and the reversed KL KL(p||q), optimized via stochastic approximation using samples from the fast-mixing model (with an Ergodic Mirror Descent perspective).
- Empirically compare on 8×8 grids and small random graphs (n=10) against MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs on the original parameters, reporting improved marginal accuracy over variational baselines and favorable time-accuracy tradeoffs versus Gibbs for finite budgets.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of a different notion of “tractability” based on guaranteed fast mixing, and a concrete way to leverage it via projection.
- The Euclidean projection with sparsity mask and spectral-norm control is neat and practical for Ising; the dual derivation and use of Πc via SVD are reasonable tools.
- The reversed-KL gradient expression (Theorem 8) is correct and leads to a workable stochastic-approximation procedure using only samples from the fast-mixing model.
- The paper engages with several divergences and connects the reversed-KL projection to mean-field as a limiting case (R=0).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Guarantees are undermined in practice: the core premise is to ensure rapid mixing by enforcing || |β| ||2 ≤ c with c<1, but experiments use c=2.5 “as it still preserves rapid mixing” and “gives better approximation.” This invalidates the theoretical guarantee and makes the approach yet another heuristic unless the authors provide empirical evidence of mixing rates and a principled rationale for the relaxed bound.
- Limited and small-scale experiments: 8×8 grids and n=10 random graphs are modest; the study does not convincingly establish robustness across sizes/densities/interaction regimes (especially near criticality). Time-accuracy comparisons do not include projection cost in the budget, which biases against baselines; the paper admits this but does not compensate with alternative fair budgets.
- Evaluation clarity: It is stated that marginals are computed “via the exact junction-tree algorithm,” yet also that Gibbs chains are run “on each.” The protocol needs to be precise: what is used for ground truth vs. for estimating the approximations? If JT is used for ground truth on 8×8 grids, please justify complexity and settings.
- The dual projection (Theorem 7): no convergence or complexity guarantees for LBFGS-B on the dual are discussed; retrieving B from D and handling signs is not fully specified; the convexity of the feasible set (|| |B| ||2 ≤ c with sparsity mask) should be stated.
- Piecewise KL: The max over subgraphs induces a nonsmooth objective; optimization stability and convexity are not discussed. Empirically it “does not perform as well,” which weakens the contribution of this component.
- Stochastic gradient estimation: the estimator in Eq. (8) is biased when reusing the same samples for μ̂ and the product; the paper acknowledges this but does not quantify the impact (e.g., via control variates, separate chains, or diagnostics). The sample pool size, step-size, and number of iterations are arbitrarily chosen; sensitivity analyses are missing.
- Scope of prior work: The paper does not situate its condition relative to other contraction/uniqueness/correlation-decay criteria (e.g., Weitz’s SAW tree, stronger bounds for Ising on grids), nor compare to alternative fast-mixing sufficient conditions (Dobrushin vs. spectral norm vs. matrix infinity/one norms), which matters because the looseness of the spectral-norm bound is a central limitation (the authors themselves note a factor ≈1.76 gap on 2D grids).

Quality
Technically, the core ingredients are sound: using the spectral-norm sufficient condition via |β|, the dual form of the l2 projection with SVD thresholding, and the reversed-KL gradient. However, key claims about “rapid mixing” in practice are not supported when c>1 is used, and the experimental evidence is not extensive enough to compensate. The optimization details (dual convergence, projection stability) and evaluation protocol need strengthening.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. However, the experimental methodology needs clarification (ground truth vs. Gibbs estimates; time budgeting). The relationship between A, B, D, and recovering signs in the projection should be described more concretely. Discuss convexity of Ψ explicitly.

Originality
Projecting onto a fast-mixing set as an approximation family is a nice idea; I am not aware of prior work that operationalizes this with a spectral-norm constraint and a practical projection algorithm for Ising. The reversed-KL-with-projection connection to mean-field is interesting but not fundamentally new. The piecewise KL variant appears incremental and underperforming.

Significance
Potentially useful if the guarantees can be enforced without sacrificing accuracy. As presented, the reliance on a relaxed bound (c=2.5) and small-scale evaluation limit impact. Broader adoption would require stronger empirical validation, fair computational budgets, and perhaps tighter or adaptive mixing conditions.

Questions for the authors
- Please justify c=2.5: provide empirical mixing-time measurements (e.g., coupling diagnostics, autocorrelation times) showing rapid mixing still holds under your setups; otherwise, results should be clearly labeled as heuristic without guarantees.
- Clarify evaluation: how are ground-truth marginals obtained for 8×8 grids? Are Gibbs chains used only for approximations? How are burn-in, thinning, and convergence assessed?
- Provide details on recovering B from D in Theorem 7 (sign handling), and discuss convexity and convergence guarantees for the dual optimization.
- Sensitivity analyses: step size, pool size, iterations, and number of Gibbs updates in the stochastic approximation; robustness across attractive vs. mixed interactions and edge densities.
- Compare fairly on equal compute budgets that include projection costs.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting and plausibly impactful idea of fast-mixing projection as a tractable approximation family.
- Concrete algorithmic contribution (dual Euclidean projection with sparsity) that could be of independent use.
- Links between reversed KL projection and mean-field.

Cons for acceptance
- The main theoretical guarantee is not respected in experiments (c>1), undermining the core premise.
- Experimental scope is limited; fairness of time-accuracy comparisons is questionable.
- Some methodological details and clarity issues remain.
- Piecewise KL contribution is weak empirically and insufficiently analyzed.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is promising, but the current validation and adherence to the stated guarantees are insufficient for NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to tighten the condition or provide empirical mixing evidence under relaxed c, expand and clarify experiments, and ensure fair compute comparisons.

Confidence
High.
