This paper introduces a novel and compelling paradigm for approximate inference in graphical models. Instead of relying on the conventional notion of a tractable family defined by structural simplicity (e.g., trees or fully factorized distributions), the authors propose to define tractability by dynamic properties—specifically, parameter-space conditions that guarantee the rapid mixing of Gibbs sampling. The core idea is to take an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing Ising model and project its parameters onto the set of "fast-mixing" parameters. This projection is explored under four different divergences: the standard Euclidean norm, the "zero-avoiding" KL divergence KL(q||p), a novel "piecewise" KL divergence, and the "zero-forcing" KL divergence KL(p||q). For each, the paper provides theoretical insights and practical algorithms, positioning this work as a significant departure from standard variational inference techniques like mean-field or loopy belief propagation.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper lies in its conceptual boldness. The idea of using fast-mixing as the criterion for a tractable approximation family is, to my knowledge, new and highly insightful. It reframes the problem of approximate inference in a way that could open up an entirely new line of research. While the community has long focused on algebraic or structural properties for tractability, this work cleverly leverages a rich body of literature from statistical physics and MCMC theory to define a functional notion of tractability. This is precisely the kind of high-risk, high-reward idea that can push the field in new directions.

2.  **Solid Technical Contributions:** The paper is not merely a position piece; it backs up its core idea with solid technical work. The derivation of an algorithm for projecting onto the set of fast-mixing parameters under the Euclidean norm (Theorem 7) is a non-trivial and elegant result. Furthermore, the formulation for minimizing the reverse KL-divergence via projected stochastic gradient descent is well-motivated and provides a practical algorithm. This approach cleverly connects the proposed framework back to more familiar concepts, noting that standard mean-field can be seen as an extreme case where the spectral norm of the interactions is constrained to zero.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides a clear motivation for the problem, a concise background on mixing times and the relevant theoretical results, and a logical progression through the different proposed projection methods. The connection between the spectral norm of the dependency matrix and the interaction parameters of the Ising model is clearly established, making the core constraint understandable.

4.  **Promising Experimental Results:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach. By comparing against standard baselines like LBP, TRW, and MF, the authors show that their methods can achieve superior accuracy in estimating marginals. The comparison with Gibbs sampling on the original parameters is particularly insightful, highlighting the key trade-off: while the original chain is asymptotically exact, the proposed projection offers a better accuracy-for-computation budget, which is often what matters in practice.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Looseness of the Theoretical Bound:** The authors are commendably transparent about a key weakness: the spectral norm bound for guaranteeing fast mixing is known to be conservative. The fact that the experiments achieve better results by using an empirically chosen bound of 2.5 rather than the theoretically justified 1.0 highlights a disconnect between the theory and the optimal practice. While this does not invalidate the core idea, it does weaken the "guarantee" of fast mixing in the experimental setting and suggests that the theoretical underpinnings could be tightened. This is a crucial direction for future work.

2.  **Preliminary Aspects of the Methods:** While the overall framework is strong, some components feel less mature. The novel piecewise KL-divergence, while an interesting idea, is shown to be less effective, making its contribution somewhat marginal. The stochastic gradient descent algorithm for the reverse KL projection involves several hyperparameters (sample pool size, step size, etc.) whose tuning and impact are not deeply explored. A more thorough analysis of this optimization procedure would strengthen the paper's claims.

3.  **Limited Scope:** The entire development is focused on the Ising model. While this is a reasonable starting point, the extension to general discrete Markov random fields is non-trivial, as it would require both deriving new bounds on the dependency matrix and developing corresponding projection algorithms. This currently limits the immediate practical applicability of the work.

**Recommendation:**

Despite the weaknesses, which I view primarily as exciting avenues for future research, this paper is an outstanding contribution. Its core idea is a significant conceptual leap forward in the field of approximate inference. It challenges the community's conventional thinking about what constitutes a "tractable" model and provides the initial theoretical and algorithmic foundations for a new class of methods. This is the kind of creative, thought-provoking work that NIPS should champion. The methodological imperfections are minor in comparison to the novelty and potential impact of the central idea.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a highly novel and significant conceptual framework for approximate inference based on fast mixing.
*   Provides sound and non-trivial technical algorithms for realizing this framework.
*   Opens up a promising new research direction with clear paths for future work.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance against established variational methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical bound used to define the fast-mixing set is loose and not strictly adhered to in experiments.
*   The work is currently limited to Ising models.
*   Some of the proposed methods (e.g., piecewise KL) are not fully validated.

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper has the potential to be highly influential.
