This paper investigates projecting parameters of the Ising model onto a “fast mixing” set, aiming to accelerate convergence of Gibbs sampling. The core idea is to identify a tractable set of parameters guaranteed to yield rapid mixing, and then project arbitrary parameters onto this set using various divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL). The authors derive a projection algorithm for the Euclidean norm, and explore approximations for the KL divergence to make the projection tractable. Experimental evaluation on small graphs demonstrates improvements over standard variational inference methods and, in some cases, direct Gibbs sampling on the original parameters.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of explicitly projecting parameters onto a “fast mixing” set is a novel approach to accelerating MCMC convergence. This differs from standard variational inference, which typically focuses on approximating the target distribution directly.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the Euclidean projection algorithm (Theorem 7) appears sound and builds on established results regarding singular value decomposition. The proposed piecewise KL divergence provides a practical approximation for more complex settings. The connection to Ergodic Mirror Descent is also well-motivated.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while limited to small graphs, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods compared to existing techniques like LBP, TRW, and naive mean-field.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, technical details, and experimental setup.  Definitions and theorems are well-stated.
*   **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a comprehensive review of relevant literature on mixing times, variational inference, and related algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability:** The experiments are limited to relatively small graphs (8x8 grids and 10-node random graphs). The computational cost of the projections, especially the more accurate KL-based methods, is likely to become prohibitive for larger, more realistic models. The experiments do not fully explore the trade-off between projection accuracy and computational cost.
*   **Conservatism of Spectral Norm Bound:** The authors acknowledge that the spectral norm bound used to define the fast-mixing set might be conservative, potentially leading to unnecessary projection. A tighter bound could significantly improve the performance of the proposed methods.
*   **Practicality of KL Divergence Approximations:** While the piecewise KL divergence offers a practical approximation, its performance is not consistently superior to the simpler Euclidean projection. The choice of subgraphs (e.g., spanning trees) for the piecewise divergence seems somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from more principled selection criteria.
*   **Limited Scope:** The work focuses specifically on the Ising model. While the core idea of projecting onto a fast-mixing set is potentially applicable to other graphical models, the authors do not provide a general framework or demonstrate its applicability beyond the Ising model.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:**  The paper lacks a more thorough theoretical analysis of the convergence properties of the projected gradients. While it touches on Ergodic Mirror Descent, a more detailed convergence analysis would strengthen the work.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to accelerating MCMC convergence by explicitly projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods on small graphs, showing improvements over existing algorithms.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly explained, and provides a thorough review of related work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The scalability of the proposed methods is a concern, as the experiments are limited to small graphs.
*   The conservatism of the spectral norm bound could limit the performance of the approach.
*   The practicality of the KL divergence approximations is not fully established.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of approximate inference. While the scalability concerns and potential conservatism of the spectral norm bound are valid weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, the technical soundness of the derivations, and the promising experimental results justify acceptance. The paper is a good fit for NIPS, as it introduces a new perspective on accelerating MCMC convergence and provides a solid foundation for future research.

**Recommendation:** Accept.  I recommend the authors address the concerns regarding scalability and the spectral norm bound in future work.  Exploring extensions to other graphical models would also be valuable.
