Summary
This paper proposes “fast-mixing” Ising models as a tractable approximation family: instead of enforcing low treewidth for exact inference, it enforces parameter-space conditions that guarantee rapid mixing of single-site Gibbs sampling. Using the dependency matrix R and a sufficient condition for rapid mixing (||R||2 < 1), the authors derive simple bounds for Ising models (Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|), implying a sufficient condition || |β| ||2 < 1. They then study projections of an arbitrary (possibly slow-mixing) Ising model onto the fast-mixing set under four divergences: (i) Euclidean (l2) distance in parameter space; (ii) KL(q||p) (zero-avoiding), of mostly theoretical interest; (iii) a novel “piecewise” KL obtained by zeroing edges to form tractable subgraphs; and (iv) KL(p||q) (zero-forcing), optimized by stochastic approximation using Gibbs samples from the fast-mixing model. The Euclidean projection is addressed via a dual formulation (Theorem 7) that preserves sparsity and enforces a spectral norm constraint on |β|, implemented using LBFGS-B and iterative singular-value thresholding. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare these projections (with Gibbs on the projected model) against standard variational baselines (MF, LBP, TRW) and Gibbs on the original model, reporting marginal errors (exact marginals via junction tree) and some time-accuracy trade-offs.

Relation to prior work: The work builds on mixing-time results for single-site dynamics (Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum; Hayes; Levin-Peres-Wilmer) and connects to variational approximations (mean-field, TRW, structured/planar approximations). The “piecewise” KL relates to piecewise training and mixtures of trees. The reverse KL + sampling mirrors ideas behind mean-field (as an extreme constraint) and stochastic approximation (Ergodic Mirror Descent).

Strengths
- Clear articulation of a different notion of tractability (fast mixing) that sits between fully factorized and exact low-treewidth families and is practically motivated.
- Derivation of a practical Euclidean projection under a spectral norm constraint that preserves sparsity (Theorem 7) and is implementable via standard tools (SVD, LBFGS-B).
- Sensible exploration of multiple divergences and an algorithmic recipe (projected stochastic gradient) to optimize reverse KL under mixing guarantees.
- Conceptual unification: naive mean-field emerges as a degenerate case of the proposed framework with zero spectral norm bound.
- Empirical evaluation against strong and widely used variational baselines.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical guarantees vs practice: experiments use a spectral norm bound of 2.5 although the sufficient condition requires <1.0, so the claimed mixing guarantees do not apply to the experimental regime. No empirical verification of mixing times or deviation from the bound is provided.
- The projection objective (Eq. 1) includes D = |B| and a spectral norm on D, which makes the feasible set nontrivial; the convexity, dual derivation, and absence of duality gap in Theorem 7 need more justification. The projection operator Πc[·] is non-smooth; convergence properties of the LBFGS-B optimization are not discussed.
- Clarity issues in the experimental protocol: it is stated both that marginals are computed via exact junction tree and that they are estimated “by running a Gibbs chain,” which seems contradictory. The accounting of computational budget is inconsistent (projection effort vs downstream Gibbs), and the time-accuracy comparisons are anecdotal without standardization.
- Scale and generality: experiments are on small graphs (8×8 grids; n=10 random graphs). Claims about general utility would benefit from larger and more challenging instances. The approach is presented only for binary Ising models; generalization to higher-order/higher-arity MRFs is left as future work.
- The piecewise KL approach is underdeveloped: the choice of subgraphs and its connection to TRW-style bounds or calibration is not theoretically motivated, and performance is reported as inferior without analysis.

Quality
- Technical soundness is mixed. The mixing-time sufficient condition and bounding Rij are standard and correct, but the projection dual (Theorem 7) deserves a more careful treatment (convexity, KKT conditions, global optimality, subgradients of Πc). The reverse-KL stochastic optimization lacks convergence rate guarantees (fixed step size; single Gibbs sweep; reusing correlated samples).
- Experimental claims (e.g., “proposed methods are more accurate than variational approximations”) are not fully substantiated without plots/tables, standardized time budgets, and mixing diagnostics. Using c=2.5 undermines the core selling point of guaranteed fast mixing.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written, with helpful background on mixing and MCMC. However:
  - The theoretical section should explicitly state whether the projection problem is convex and whether strong duality holds.
  - Experimental protocol needs clarification (ground-truth marginals via junction tree vs Gibbs estimates; compute budget accounting).
  - Minor inconsistencies (e.g., references to Section “3.2” vs 3; typographical slips) should be corrected.

Originality
- The framing of projection onto a fast-mixing set and the dual Euclidean projection that preserves sparsity appear novel in the Ising context. The use of reverse KL with a fast-mixing constraint is a natural but nontrivial extension of mean-field. The piecewise KL is a modest twist on existing piecewise/mixture-of-trees ideas.

Significance
- Conceptually interesting: fast-mixing as a tractable family could influence approximate inference and learning with MCMC-in-the-loop. However, the current empirical scope and lack of guarantees in practice limit the immediate impact. Strengthening the theory and scaling experiments would enhance significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a rigorous analysis of the projection problem: convexity, conditions for strong duality, subgradient characterization of Πc, and convergence guarantees for the LBFGS-B-based solver.
- Empirically validate mixing: measure spectral norms post-projection; report autocorrelation times or coupling bounds; show how often c>1 still mixes fast and by how much.
- Standardize and report time-accuracy trade-offs: include wall-clock or iteration budgets across methods (including projection/training time) and show learning curves.
- Clarify the evaluation: explicitly state how ground-truth marginals are computed; remove contradictory descriptions; justify the feasibility of exact junction tree on 8×8 grids.
- Analyze sensitivity to the spectral bound c (1.0 vs 2.5): accuracy vs mixing trade-off curves.
- Expand to larger graphs and possibly block Gibbs or tree-block updates; discuss how fast-mixing conditions change.
- For piecewise KL, relate to TRW bounds/calibration and investigate principled selection of subgraphs.

Pros
- Novel and practical perspective on tractability via mixing guarantees.
- Implementable projection method preserving sparsity; clear algorithmic path.
- Bridges variational and sampling-based inference.

Cons
- Guarantees not respected in experiments (c=2.5), with no mixing diagnostics.
- Theoretical gaps in projection dual and stochastic optimization.
- Limited scale and somewhat unclear experimental methodology.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the current paper needs stronger theoretical justification of the projection, clearer and more rigorous experiments (with mixing diagnostics and standardized compute), and evidence that the guarantees matter in practice. Addressing these points could make this a strong contribution.
