

This paper introduces a novel framework for approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models by redefining "tractability" as *fast mixing* rather than low treewidth. The core idea is to project slow-mixing Ising model parameters onto a set of fast-mixing parameters (satisfying $\|R\|_2 < 1$, where $R$ is the dependency matrix) using four divergence measures: Euclidean distance (with a dual SVD-based projection), piecewise KL, reversed KL ($\text{KL}(\psi\|\theta)$), and zero-avoiding KL (theoretically intractable). The work connects to prior mixing-time literature (e.g., Hayes [8], Dyer et al. [4]) and extends variational methods (e.g., mean-field as a degenerate case with $\|R\|_2 = 0$).

**Strengths:**  
- **Quality & Originality:** The projection framework is technically sound, with a rigorous dual algorithm (Theorem 7) for Euclidean projection. The piecewise KL divergence and practical reversed KL approach (using stochastic gradient descent with Gibbs sampling) are novel contributions. The paper clearly distinguishes its fast-mixing tractability from prior work (e.g., mean-field, tree-reweighted), which focuses on graph structure rather than parameter constraints.  
- **Significance:** Experiments show the method outperforms standard variational approaches (LBP, TRW, MF) on marginal accuracy for finite-time inference, even on 8×8 grids. This addresses a key practical pain point: Gibbs sampling on original parameters often requires exponentially long runs to converge, while the proposed method guarantees rapid convergence.  
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions (e.g., dependency matrix, mixing time) and a logical flow from theory to experiments. The experimental setup (small grids, random graphs, 50 trials) is thorough and comparable to prior work (Hazan & Shashua [9]).

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Theoretical Looseness:** The spectral norm bound $\|R\|_2 < 1$ is overly conservative for Ising models. For 2D grids, the critical threshold $\beta_c \approx 0.44$ (Lubetzky & Sly [11]) is known to be tighter than the bound at $\beta = 0.25$. The paper uses $\|R\|_2 \leq 2.5$ empirically (Section 6.1) without justification, which risks including slow-mixing parameters. This undermines the core premise of guaranteed fast mixing.  
- **Experimental Gaps:**  
  - The paper claims "faster mixing" but provides no analysis of *actual mixing times* (e.g., iterations to reach $\epsilon$-accuracy). Without this, the practical advantage over Gibbs on original parameters (which may converge slowly but eventually) remains unclear.  
  - Piecewise KL experiments underperform (Section 6.1), yet the paper offers no guidance on optimal subgraph selection (e.g., spanning trees vs. treewidth-2 chains).  
- **Clarity Issues:** The reversed KL gradient derivation (Theorem 8) and stochastic approximation (Eq. 8) are technically sound but lack intuitive explanation. The claim that "using the same samples twice makes little difference" (Section 5.4) needs empirical validation.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**  
1. **Tighter Mixing Conditions:** Derive or cite a tighter bound for Ising models (e.g., using correlation decay [12]) to justify the spectral norm constraint. Compare the empirical bound (e.g., $\|R\|_2 \leq 2.5$) against known thresholds.  
2. **Mixing Time Analysis:** Include plots of marginal error vs. sampling time (not just final error) to demonstrate *speed* of convergence. Quantify the trade-off between projection accuracy and mixing time reduction.  
3. **Subgraph Selection:** Systematically evaluate subgraph choices (e.g., spanning trees vs. low-treewidth graphs) for piecewise KL. Provide a heuristic for selection based on graph topology.  
4. **Clarify Reversed KL:** Explicitly contrast the reversed KL approach with mean-field (e.g., "While mean-field enforces $\|R\|_2 = 0$, our method allows $\|R\|_2 \leq c$ for $c > 0$, yielding better approximations").  

**Decision:** **Weak Accept**. The work presents a novel and technically solid approach with strong empirical validation for a practical problem. However, the theoretical looseness of the mixing condition and lack of mixing-time analysis weaken the contribution. Addressing these gaps would significantly elevate the paper. The authors should revise to: (a) tighten the theoretical bound, (b) add mixing-time comparisons, and (c) clarify subgraph selection for piecewise KL. With these improvements, the paper would make a valuable contribution to NIPS.
