### **Summary**

This paper introduces a novel framework for approximate inference in Ising models. The central idea is to reframe the notion of a "tractable" model for approximation. Instead of using structurally simple models (e.g., fully factorized or tree-structured) as is common in variational inference, the authors propose using the family of "fast-mixing" distributions. These are distributions whose parameters guarantee that Gibbs sampling will rapidly converge to the true stationary distribution. The paper leverages a known result from the MCMC literature: an Ising model is provably fast-mixing if the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths is less than one.

The main contribution is a set of methods to project an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing Ising model onto this fast-mixing set. The authors explore four different notions of "closeness" or divergence for this projection:
1.  **Euclidean distance:** A direct projection of the parameter vectors, for which the authors derive an efficient dual optimization algorithm based on iterative SVD thresholding that preserves graph sparsity.
2.  **Inclusive KL-divergence (KL(q||p)):** A theoretically natural but computationally intractable projection, as it requires inference on the original slow-mixing distribution `q`.
3.  **Piecewise KL-divergence:** A novel and tractable approximation to the inclusive KL, calculated over a collection of low-treewidth subgraphs of the original model.
4.  **Exclusive KL-divergence (KL(p||q)):** A practical projection that can be optimized via projected stochastic gradient descent, as it only requires sampling from the fast-mixing approximate distribution `p`. This approach is presented as a generalization of mean-field inference.

Experiments on grid and random graphs show that sampling from the projected fast-mixing models provides more accurate marginal estimates than standard methods like Loopy Belief Propagation, Tree-Reweighted BP, and Mean Field, and often outperforms running Gibbs sampling on the original model for a limited time budget.

### **Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core concept of using "algorithmic tractability" (i.e., fast mixing) to define the approximating family is highly novel and conceptually elegant. It marks a significant departure from the standard structure-based families used in variational inference. This idea has the potential to open a new and fruitful direction of research in approximate inference, connecting MCMC theory more directly with variational methods.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of a dual algorithm for the sparsity-preserving Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a solid contribution. The formulation of the reverse KL projection as a stochastic optimization problem is well-motivated and leverages the fast-mixing property of the target distribution effectively.
*   **Thoroughness:** The exploration of four different divergences demonstrates a comprehensive treatment of the problem. This highlights the trade-offs between theoretical appeal (inclusive KL), computational feasibility (reverse KL), and practical simplicity (Euclidean). The introduction of the piecewise KL divergence is a creative attempt to find a middle ground.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It provides a clear background on mixing time conditions, logically motivates the need for projection, and walks the reader through each of the proposed methods. The connection to and generalization of mean-field is particularly insightful.
*   **Experimental Support:** The empirical evaluation is strong. The proposed methods are benchmarked against relevant and widely-used baselines on standard graph structures. The results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed approach can achieve a better accuracy-computation trade-off than both standard variational methods and direct MCMC on the intractable model.

### **Weaknesses**

*   **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice:** A primary weakness is the gap between the theoretical justification and the practical implementation. The theory guarantees fast mixing for a spectral norm bound of 1.0, yet the experiments use a bound of 2.5, which is reported to give better results. While the authors commendably acknowledge this in the discussion, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more rigorous investigation. Without it, the "guarantee" of fast mixing is undermined, and the choice of 2.5 seems arbitrary.
*   **Computational Cost of Projection:** The experiments focus on the efficiency of sampling from the *final* projected model but do not account for the computational cost of the projection itself. The stochastic optimization for the reverse KL projection, for example, requires many iterations, each involving MCMC sampling and a full Euclidean projection (which itself requires an iterative SVD-based optimization). A practitioner would need to know the total computational budget (projection + sampling) to make a fair comparison. The paper should be more transparent about this cost.
*   **Limited Scope:** The entire technical development is specific to pairwise, binary Ising models. The dependency bound `Rij ≤ |βij|` is key to the approach, and it is unclear how this would extend to general discrete MRFs with larger state spaces or non-pairwise factors. While this is noted as future work, the lack of discussion on the specific challenges involved makes it difficult to assess the broader applicability of the proposed framework.

### **Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Addressing the Spectral Norm Bound:** To bridge the gap between theory and practice, I suggest adding a small empirical study. For the models projected with a bound of 2.5, could you empirically measure the mixing time (e.g., by tracking the Total Variation distance from the true marginals on a small graph, or using a standard MCMC diagnostic like the potential scale reduction factor on larger ones)? This would provide concrete evidence that the resulting models are indeed fast-mixing in practice and would justify the choice of 2.5, even if it exceeds the theoretical sufficient condition.
2.  **Quantifying Projection Cost:** It would be very helpful to report the wall-clock time required for the projection step in the experiments. This would provide a complete picture of the method's computational footprint and allow for a fairer comparison of the total time-to-accuracy against baselines like running a longer MCMC chain on the original model.
3.  **Clarifying the Piecewise KL:** The description of how the tractable subgraphs `T` are selected for the piecewise KL divergence is brief. For the random graphs, the paper mentions using "sets of random spanning trees which can cover every edge." A few more details on this procedure (e.g., how many trees are used, how are they generated?) would improve the clarity and reproducibility of this specific method.
4.  **Discussion on Generalization:** While a full extension to general MRFs is beyond the scope of this paper, a paragraph in the discussion outlining the key technical hurdles would be valuable. Specifically, what would be required to bound the dependency matrix `R` for general potentials, and how might the projection algorithm need to be adapted? This would showcase a deeper consideration of the method's potential.

### **Decision**

This paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of approximate inference. The core idea of using fast-mixing as a criterion for tractability is compelling and well-executed. The technical contributions are solid, and the experiments convincingly demonstrate the value of the approach over established methods. While there are weaknesses, particularly concerning the gap between the theoretical bound and the one used in practice, they do not invalidate the core contribution and are addressable. The paper is well-written, thought-provoking, and likely to inspire follow-up work. I recommend acceptance.
