This paper explores the idea of projecting parameters of an Ising model onto a “fast-mixing” set, aiming to accelerate Gibbs sampling for inference. The core concept is to identify a tractable set of parameters where Gibbs sampling converges quickly—defined by a spectral norm constraint on the dependency matrix—and then project parameters from an arbitrary distribution onto this set using various divergence measures (Euclidean, KL-divergence, and piecewise approximations). The paper provides theoretical results for Euclidean projection via SVD thresholding and investigates practical approximations for KL-divergence minimization. The experimental evaluation compares performance to standard inference algorithms like LBP, TRW, and naive mean field, demonstrating competitive results.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of explicitly projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set is a conceptually interesting and potentially valuable alternative to traditional approximation techniques. It shifts the focus from approximating the distribution directly to approximating the sampling process.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds on solid theoretical foundations from Markov chain Monte Carlo literature, particularly regarding mixing times and dependency matrices. The derivation of the Euclidean projection theorem is well-presented.
* **Multiple Divergence Measures:** Investigating different divergence measures for projection (Euclidean, KL, piecewise) provides a comprehensive exploration of the approach and highlights the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods are competitive with existing inference algorithms, showcasing the practical potential of the approach. The comparison against Gibbs sampling on the original parameters is insightful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the key ideas and technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conservatism of Spectral Norm Bound:** As the authors themselves acknowledge, the spectral norm bound of 1.0 for fast mixing seems conservative compared to known thresholds for specific cases like the 2D Ising model. This limits the effectiveness of the projection, potentially removing valid parameters that would still lead to rapid mixing.  A more refined bound would significantly strengthen the approach.
* **Computational Cost of KL-Divergence Approximation:** The stochastic approximation approach for minimizing the KL-divergence, while theoretically sound, appears computationally demanding. The reliance on Gibbs sampling *within* the optimization loop introduces significant overhead, and the parameter tuning described is not particularly systematic.
* **Limited Scalability:** The experiments are limited to relatively small graphs (8x8 grids and 10-node random graphs). It’s unclear how well the approach would scale to larger, more complex models. The piecewise KL-divergence approach, while promising, may become intractable for very large graphs.
* **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees for Piecewise Approximation:** While the piecewise KL-divergence is intuitively appealing, there's limited theoretical justification for its accuracy or convergence properties. The choice of subgraphs (trees, low-treewidth graphs) isn't rigorously motivated.
* **Practical Significance of Zero-Forcing KL:** The authors appropriately note the potential drawbacks of using the zero-forcing KL, but the justification for including it feels somewhat weak, given its potential for high variance and non-convexity.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and potentially impactful approach to approximate inference based on fast-mixing Markov chains.
* The theoretical analysis is sound, and the derivation of the Euclidean projection is a valuable contribution.
* The experimental results demonstrate the competitiveness of the proposed methods.
* The work is well-written and clearly presents the key ideas and technical details.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The conservatism of the spectral norm bound limits the effectiveness of the projection.
* The computational cost of the KL-divergence approximation is a significant concern.
* The scalability of the approach is unclear.
* The lack of theoretical guarantees for the piecewise approximation is a weakness.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of approximate inference. The idea of projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set is novel and potentially valuable. However, the conservatism of the spectral norm bound, the computational cost of KL-divergence approximation, and the scalability concerns raise significant reservations.  

**Recommendation:**  **Reject with Encouragement for Revision.**

I recommend rejection in its current form, but with strong encouragement for the authors to revise and address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I suggest the following:

1. **Investigate tighter bounds on the spectral norm for fast mixing:** Explore recent literature on mixing times and dependency matrices to identify more accurate bounds, potentially specific to the Ising model.
2. **Optimize the KL-divergence approximation:** Explore more efficient stochastic gradient estimation techniques or alternative optimization algorithms to reduce the computational cost.
3. **Demonstrate scalability:** Conduct experiments on larger, more complex graphs to assess the scalability of the approach.
4. **Provide theoretical analysis for the piecewise approximation:** Investigate the accuracy and convergence properties of the piecewise KL-divergence, and provide guidance on selecting appropriate subgraphs.
5. **Consider alternative divergences:** Explore other divergences that may be more amenable to efficient computation and provide better theoretical guarantees.

Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
