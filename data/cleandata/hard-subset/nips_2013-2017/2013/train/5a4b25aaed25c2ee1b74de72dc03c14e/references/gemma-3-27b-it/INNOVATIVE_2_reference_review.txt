This paper explores a fascinating and potentially impactful idea: leveraging the theory of fast-mixing Markov chains to accelerate approximate inference in graphical models. The core concept – projecting intractable parameters onto a “tractable” space defined by fast mixing – is genuinely novel and represents a refreshing departure from standard variational or belief propagation techniques. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches and present a compelling motivation for their work. The exploration of different divergence measures for the projection (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL) is commendable, and the attempt to develop a stochastic approximation scheme for the KL divergence is particularly ambitious.

The paper's strength lies heavily in its conceptual novelty. It opens up a new avenue for tackling intractable inference, potentially offering guarantees on convergence speed that are absent in many existing methods. The theoretical groundwork, particularly the connection to spectral norm bounds for mixing times, is solid, and the derivation of the projection algorithm for the Euclidean norm (Theorem 7) is well-executed. The recognition that a tighter bound than ||R||2 < 1 might be necessary in practice is a valuable acknowledgement of the limitations.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses, primarily relating to the depth of experimental validation and the practical applicability of some of the proposed divergences. While the experiments provide a preliminary comparison to other inference algorithms, the setup feels somewhat limited. The comparison to Gibbs sampling on the original parameters, though insightful, lacks a more detailed analysis of the computational cost trade-offs. The “tuning” of the stochastic gradient descent for the zero-forcing KL-divergence is glossed over, and the sensitivity to parameter choices isn’t adequately discussed. More extensive experimentation on larger and more complex graphs is crucial to establish the scalability and robustness of the proposed methods.

The piecewise KL-divergence approach, while conceptually interesting, appears to perform worse than the Euclidean projection in the experiments. The authors attribute this to the approximation introduced by the graph simplification, but a more detailed investigation into the impact of the subgraph selection process is needed. The justification for using horizontal/vertical chains for grid graphs, and random spanning trees for random graphs, isn't particularly strong and might be a significant factor in the observed performance.

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the spectral norm as a proxy for fast mixing. The authors acknowledge the existence of tighter bounds for specific cases (e.g., the 2D Ising model), but a more general discussion of the challenges in characterizing fast mixing for arbitrary graphs and distributions would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **High Novelty:** The idea of projecting onto fast-mixing distributions is genuinely new and has the potential to significantly impact the field of approximate inference.
*   **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds on established results from Markov chain theory and provides a rigorous derivation of the projection algorithm for the Euclidean norm.
*   **Exploration of Multiple Divergences:** The authors explore a variety of divergence measures, demonstrating a breadth of thinking.
*   **Potential for Future Work:** The paper opens up several promising avenues for future research, including extending the approach to general MRFs and incorporating it into learning algorithms.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope and lack a detailed analysis of computational cost and parameter sensitivity.
*   **Practical Concerns:** The performance of the piecewise KL-divergence is disappointing, and the stochastic approximation scheme for the KL divergence is not fully explored.
*   **Looseness of Spectral Norm Bound:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of the spectral norm as a sufficient condition for fast mixing.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its weaknesses, I lean towards **acceptance**. The novelty of the idea and the potential impact of the work outweigh the current limitations. This paper presents a bold and promising new direction for approximate inference, and I believe it deserves to be presented at NIPS. The authors should be encouraged to address the weaknesses identified in this review in future work. I believe this work could have a significant impact if developed further. A minor revision addressing the concerns about experimental scope and parameter tuning would be sufficient.
