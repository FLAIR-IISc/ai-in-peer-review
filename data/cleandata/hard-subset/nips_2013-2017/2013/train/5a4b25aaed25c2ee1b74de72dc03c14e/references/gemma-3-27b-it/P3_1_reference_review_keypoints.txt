- Projects Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set to enable rapid Gibbs sampling as an alternative to traditional distribution approximations.
- Considers multiple projection divergences: Euclidean, KL-divergence, piecewise KL, and reversed KL.
- Grounded in MCMC theory using dependency matrices and spectral-norm-based fast-mixing conditions; provides Theorem 7 (dual algorithm for Euclidean projection).
- Novel perspective: modify parameters to guarantee fast sampling rather than directly approximating the distribution.
- Related work well-situated (mean-field, loopy BP, tree-reweighted BP, Dobrushin’s criterion, mixing-time results).
- Clear explanations of core concepts, making the paper accessible to graphical-model researchers.
- Empirical evaluation compares methods to standard inference algorithms on small Ising models.
- Concern: the spectral norm ≤1 condition is overly conservative; tighter bounds (e.g., Lubetzky & Sly) deserve deeper consideration.
- KL-based projections (especially zero-avoiding) are computationally prohibitive due to intractable expectations, limiting practicality.
- Experiments are limited to small graphs and the paper lacks a detailed scalability and complexity analysis (including sample-generation cost).
- Stochastic gradient descent approaches show sensitivity to hyperparameters (step size, pool size); more systematic tuning or schedules needed.
- Piecewise KL-divergence underperforms true KL in experiments, leaving its practical relevance unclear.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, promising alternative to variational methods, and well-written.
- Arguments for rejection: conservative mixing guarantee, high computational cost for some methods, inadequate scalability discussion, and weak piecewise KL results.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on addressing spectral-norm bounds, scalability analysis, and clarifying or justifying piecewise KL use.
- Reviewer confidence: 7/10.
