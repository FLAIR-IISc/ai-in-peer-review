- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a set that guarantees fast mixing for Gibbs sampling, instead of approximating the distribution directly.  
- Investigates four projection methods: Euclidean (SVD thresholding), zero-avoiding KL (theoretical), piecewise KL on low-treewidth subgraphs, and stochastic zero-forcing KL.  
- Provides theoretical foundation for the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) and derives gradients for divergence-based projections.  
- Empirically compares projection methods to LBP, TRW, mean-field, and direct Gibbs sampling on grid and random graphs.  
- Positions work relative to variational inference and mixing-time literature, citing Hayes and related results.  
- Novel idea shifting focus from tractable distributions to parameters that enable efficient sampling.  
- Strong theoretical contribution in the Euclidean projection and clear derivations for projection gradients.  
- Experiments demonstrate benefits of the projection approach across different topologies.  
- Authors clearly connect to existing MCMC and variational inference work.  
- Paper acknowledges limitations, particularly the looseness of the spectral norm bound.  
- Spectral norm bound (threshold = 1.0) is overly conservative, limiting projection effectiveness; choice of threshold needs better justification.  
- Piecewise KL divergence method consistently underperforms and lacks sufficient analysis explaining why.  
- Projection step can be computationally expensive (notably zero-forcing KL); computational trade-offs need clearer discussion.  
- Experiments are small-scale (8x8 grids, 10-node graphs), leaving scalability to larger models unproven.  
- Stochastic SGD for zero-forcing KL uses fixed hyperparameters without sensitivity analysis; results may depend on tuning.  
- Overall: solid, novel, and potentially impactful work; recommend accept with minor revisions to address spectral bound, piecewise KL analysis, computational complexity, and scalability.
