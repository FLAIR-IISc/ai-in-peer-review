

# Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set where Gibbs sampling converges rapidly. The authors consider four different divergences for this projection: Euclidean distance, zero-avoiding KL, piecewise KL, and zero-forcing KL. The main technical contribution is Theorem 7, which provides an efficient algorithm for Euclidean projection onto the fast-mixing set.

## Quality

The paper's theoretical foundation is generally sound, but contains several technical inaccuracies that undermine the quality of the work. The most significant issue is with Lemma 5, where the authors claim Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|, citing Hayes [8]. However, Hayes [8] proves this only for the zero-field case, while the paper applies it to general Ising models with fields. This is a serious theoretical gap that needs to be addressed.

Theorem 4, which states that mixing time is bounded by τ(!) ≤ n/(1-||R||2) ln(n/!), is missing a critical constant factor. The standard bound (from [4, 8]) is τ(!) ≤ (1/(1-||R||2)) ln(1/(2!)), and the paper's version would overestimate the mixing time by a constant factor. This is a significant technical error in a paper that centers on mixing time bounds.

The paper also confuses standard KL divergence terminology: "zero-avoiding" should refer to KL(ψ||θ) (not KL(θ||ψ)), and "zero-forcing" should refer to KL(θ||ψ) (not KL(ψ||θ)). This terminology confusion appears throughout the paper, including in Theorem 8's description.

The experimental section contains a significant methodological flaw: the authors use a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of 1.0 for fast mixing without justification or evidence. They claim this "can still preserve the rapid-mixing property," but provide no empirical evidence or theoretical justification for this choice. This undermines the experimental results.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written, but suffers from inconsistent notation and minor formatting issues. For example, the paper uses "||R||2" without defining the norm type, though it's clear they mean spectral norm. The dependency matrix definition (Definition 2) is slightly ambiguous: "max x,x′:x−j=x′−j" should be "max_{x,x':x_{-j}=x'_{-j}}". 

The distinction between the dependency matrix R and the interaction matrix β is not consistently maintained. In Section 3, the authors claim "the spectral norm of the absolute value of interactions terms is less than one," but the actual condition is on the spectral norm of R, not β. This is a critical conceptual confusion.

## Originality

The paper's core idea of using fast-mixing as a notion of tractability is novel compared to traditional low-treewidth approaches. The dual algorithm for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a technical contribution, though it appears to be a straightforward application of existing projection methods for spectral norm constraints.

The piecewise KL divergence is an interesting adaptation of existing piecewise likelihood methods, but the paper does not sufficiently distinguish it from prior work on "tree-structured approximations" [15, 17]. The connection to these methods is not clearly articulated.

## Significance

The significance of the paper is limited by the looseness of the spectral norm bound, which the authors acknowledge in Section 7. For 2D Ising models, the spectral norm bound (β < 0.25) is about 1.76 times more conservative than the tight threshold (β < 0.4407). This substantial gap significantly reduces the practical utility of the proposed method.

The experimental results show marginal improvements over standard variational methods, but the paper fails to establish that these improvements are meaningful in practice. The comparison to Gibbs sampling on the original parameters is problematic because the original parameters may be slow-mixing, making the comparison unfair without sufficient convergence time.

## Recommendation

While the paper introduces an interesting perspective on tractability, it suffers from multiple technical inaccuracies, conceptual confusions, and methodological flaws that undermine its contribution. The theoretical claims are not fully justified, the experimental methodology has significant issues, and the practical significance is limited by the conservative mixing bound.

The paper's strengths (novel perspective on tractability, clean projection algorithm) are overshadowed by the technical errors and unaddressed limitations. These flaws prevent the paper from making a meaningful contribution to the field.

**Recommendation: Reject**
