This paper proposes a novel approach for approximate inference in graphical models, specifically Ising models. Instead of approximating an intractable, high-treewidth model with a structurally tractable one (e.g., a tree or a fully-factorized distribution), the authors propose approximating it with a model that is "tractable" in the sense that Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to mix rapidly. The core idea is to identify a set of "fast-mixing" parameters—those for which the spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix is less than one—and then project an arbitrary (potentially slow-mixing) parameter vector onto this set. The paper explores four different projection methods based on different divergences: Euclidean distance, the "zero-avoiding" KL divergence KL(θ||ψ), a novel piecewise KL divergence, and the "zero-forcing" KL divergence KL(ψ||θ). For the Euclidean case, a dual optimization algorithm is derived. For the zero-forcing KL, a stochastic projected gradient descent method is employed. Experiments on grids and random graphs show that the proposed methods can outperform standard variational techniques like LBP, TRW, and mean-field in terms of marginal accuracy.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of defining tractability by fast-mixing dynamics rather than graphical structure is a genuinely novel and thought-provoking contribution. It opens a new avenue for approximate inference that is conceptually different from the dominant variational paradigms. If this line of work proves fruitful, it could significantly impact how we approach inference and learning in complex models.

2.  **Technical Contributions:** The paper provides a non-trivial algorithm for projecting Ising parameters onto the fast-mixing set under the Euclidean norm (Theorem 7). The proposed "piecewise KL-divergence" is also an interesting and practical idea for creating a tractable proxy for the full KL divergence.

3.  **Empirical Soundness:** The experimental comparison against established baselines (LBP, TRW, MF) is valuable and demonstrates the potential of the approach. The use of exact junction-tree marginals for evaluation provides a solid ground truth.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper, while built on a strong core idea, is undermined by a significant number of technical inaccuracies, inconsistencies, and a critical contradiction between its theory and experimental practice.

1.  **Major Contradiction in Experimental Setup:** The entire theoretical motivation of the paper rests on Theorem 4, which guarantees rapid mixing if the spectral norm of the dependency matrix is less than 1.0. However, in the final paragraph of Section 6.1, the authors state: "we find in practice using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of 1.0 can still preserve the rapid-mixing property and gives better approximation". This is a critical flaw. By using a bound of 2.5, the authors abandon the one theoretical guarantee their method provides. The resulting distribution is no longer guaranteed to be "fast-mixing" by their own definition, which invalidates the central premise of the work. While the bound of 1.0 may be loose (as noted in the discussion), arbitrarily choosing 2.5 without any theoretical justification turns the method into a purely empirical heuristic.

2.  **Technical Inaccuracies and Unsubstantiated Claims:**
    *   In Section 5.4, the justification for the unbiasedness of the gradient estimator for KL(ψ||θ) is flawed. The authors state, "It is a standard result that if two estimators are unbiased and independent, the product of the two estimators will also be unbiased." This is incorrect; the expectation of a product is the product of expectations for independent variables, but this does not imply the product of *estimators* is an unbiased estimator of the product of the true values. The subsequent admission that they use the same dependent samples in practice and it "makes little difference" is an unsubstantiated empirical claim that brushes over a statistical error in their reasoning.
    *   Theorem 6 appears to contain a typo. The projection of singular values is typically `S'_ii = min(S_ii, c)`, not `min(S_ii, c^2)`.
    *   Lemma 5 is an extension of a result from Hayes [8], justified by "simple modifications to the proof". For a result this central to the paper's setup, a sketch of these modifications should be provided in an appendix.
    *   Theorem 4 contains a typo: `ln(n!)` should almost certainly be `ln(n/ε)`. This suggests a lack of careful proofreading.

3.  **Lack of Clarity and Inconsistent Notation:**
    *   The paper is inconsistent in its terminology for KL divergences. The introduction (paragraph 3) claims the "zero forcing" divergence is KL(q||p), but Section 5.4, which introduces the method, correctly identifies the zero-forcing divergence as KL(p||q) (or KL(ψ||θ) in their notation). This is a confusing contradiction on a key concept.
    *   The notation in Theorem 7 is non-standard and poorly defined. The use of a prime (e.g., `Λ'Z`) for what appears to be an element-wise product is unconventional and confusing.
    *   The experimental description of the computational budget (Section 6.1, last paragraph before 6.2) is convoluted and difficult to parse, making it hard to understand the true cost-benefit trade-off of the proposed methods.

4.  **Minor Flaws:**
    *   The formatting of the reference list is highly inconsistent. Some entries include journal names and page numbers, while others are incomplete (e.g., [13] is just "Yuval Peres, Peter Winkler, , ,"). This reflects a general lack of attention to detail.
    *   There are numerous small typos, such as "systematical scan" (Section 6.1) and "KL-divergnce" (Section 7).

**Recommendation:** Reject.

While the core idea presented in this paper is novel and potentially significant, the execution is unfortunately not up to the standards of NIPS. The work is marred by a fatal contradiction between its theoretical foundation and its experimental implementation, several technical inaccuracies in its mathematical claims, and a pervasive lack of clarity and attention to detail in its presentation. The sheer number of minor and major flaws suggests the paper was hastily prepared and not thoroughly vetted. I would encourage the authors to pursue this interesting research direction, but the manuscript requires a major revision to correct the theoretical inconsistencies, fix the technical errors, clarify the notation, and present a more rigorous and carefully proofread paper.
