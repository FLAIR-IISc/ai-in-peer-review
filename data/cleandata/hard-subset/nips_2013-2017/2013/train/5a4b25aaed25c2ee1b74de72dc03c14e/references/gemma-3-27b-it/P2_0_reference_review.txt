## Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

**Summary:**

This paper explores a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically Ising models. The core idea is to project parameters from an arbitrary distribution onto a "fast-mixing" set – those guaranteed to allow for rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling. The authors investigate several divergence measures for performing this projection, including Euclidean distance (solved via SVD thresholding), KL divergence (both directly and approximated with piecewise methods), and a reversed KL divergence minimized via stochastic approximation.  They demonstrate empirically that projecting parameters towards the fast-mixing set can improve inference accuracy compared to standard methods like loopy belief propagation and naive mean field, though not surpassing Gibbs sampling given sufficient time.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on the long line of work addressing inference in intractable graphical models. It positions itself between mean-field approaches (e.g., [14, 16, 20]) which seek tractable approximations to the original distribution, and methods like loopy belief propagation [21] and tree-reweighted BP [19] that operate directly on the intractable graph.  The core contribution differentiates itself by focusing on modifying the *parameters* to ensure rapid mixing, rather than directly approximating the distribution. The work acknowledges connections to related ideas like structured mean field and references relevant literature on mixing times [4, 8, 10, 11]. The connection to Ergodic Mirror Descent [3] in the stochastic approximation section is also well noted.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of directly projecting parameters towards a fast-mixing set is a compelling and relatively unexplored idea. It offers a different perspective on approximating intractable distributions.
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, clearly defining fast mixing, the dependency matrix, and relevant theorems regarding mixing time bounds.  The derivation of the projection algorithm for the Euclidean distance is particularly well-presented.
* **Multiple Divergence Approaches:**  The exploration of different divergence measures for projection, including the pragmatic piecewise KL divergence, demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide evidence that the proposed methods often outperform standard variational inference techniques in terms of marginal accuracy, and that the rapid-mixing approximation can accelerate convergence compared to Gibbs sampling with original parameters.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.  The connections to existing literature are explicitly stated.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conservatism of the Spectral Norm Bound:** As the authors themselves acknowledge, the theoretical guarantee of fast mixing (based on the spectral norm of the interaction matrix) appears to be quite conservative. Using a higher threshold (2.5 in the experiments) yields better results, suggesting the bound needs refinement. This limits the practical impact of the theoretical results.
* **Computational Cost of Stochastic Approximation:** The stochastic approximation method for the reversed KL-divergence, while promising, requires careful tuning of parameters (step size, pool size, Gibbs steps) and can be computationally expensive. The experimental setup lacks a detailed cost analysis to compare it against the other approaches.
* **Piecewise KL Divergence Performance:** The piecewise KL divergence, while computationally attractive, does not consistently outperform the Euclidean projection in the experiments.  The rationale for the specific subgraph choices (e.g., horizontal/vertical chains) could be more thoroughly justified.
* **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While experiments are conducted on moderate-sized graphs, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of the scalability of the proposed methods to larger, more complex networks.  The computational complexity of the projection algorithms should be more clearly outlined.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to approximate inference.
* The empirical results demonstrate a clear benefit over existing variational methods.
* The exploration of multiple divergence measures showcases a thoughtful research process.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The conservative theoretical guarantee (spectral norm bound) limits the practical impact.
* The stochastic approximation method is computationally demanding and lacks detailed cost analysis.
* The piecewise KL divergence does not consistently perform well.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is **above average** and a worthwhile contribution to the field.  The core idea of projecting parameters to ensure fast mixing is innovative, and the theoretical and empirical work supports the feasibility of this approach.  While the theoretical bound could be tighter, and the computational cost of some methods is a concern, the potential for improved inference accuracy warrants acceptance. The authors have clearly identified avenues for future work, such as refining the fast-mixing condition and extending the approach to more general graphical models.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. I would suggest the authors:

*   Address the conservatism of the spectral norm bound, perhaps by exploring tighter conditions for fast mixing or adapting the projection threshold.
*   Provide a more detailed cost analysis for the stochastic approximation method, comparing its computational requirements to other approaches.
*   Further investigate the performance of the piecewise KL divergence, potentially exploring alternative subgraph choices or optimization strategies.
*   Discuss the scalability of the methods to larger networks.
