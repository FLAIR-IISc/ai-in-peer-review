Summary
This paper proposes “fast-mixing distributions” as an alternative tractable family for approximate inference: instead of restricting graphical structure (trees, planar, etc.), the authors restrict parameters to a set that guarantees rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs. For Ising models, they use the sufficient condition ||R||2 < 1 where R_ij ≤ tanh|β_ij|, hence controlling the spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix. Given an arbitrary (possibly slow-mixing) Ising model, they project its parameters onto the fast-mixing set under several divergences:
- Euclidean projection: a dual algorithm (Theorem 7) that preserves sparsity while enforcing a spectral-norm constraint on |β|.
- KL(θ||ψ) (“zero-avoiding”): theoretically appealing but requires expectations under θ and is intractable in general.
- Piecewise KL via tractable subgraphs (trees/low-treewidth), taking a max over subgraphs.
- KL(ψ||θ) (“zero-forcing”): amenable to stochastic approximation since expectations are under ψ, which is constrained to be fast-mixing. They implement projected stochastic gradient using a sample pool and repeated Euclidean projections.

Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare their projected models (Euclidean, piecewise, KL(ψ||θ)) with LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original parameters. They report better marginal accuracy than variational baselines and favorable time-accuracy tradeoffs vs Gibbs on the original model within a finite budget.

Relation to prior work: The approach connects mixing-time theory (Dyer et al., Hayes) with approximate inference, complementing variational methods (MF, structured MF, TRW, GBP), planar graph inference, and tree/mixture-of-tree approximations. The projected-gradient scheme resonates with Ergodic Mirror Descent for dependent-sample optimization. The piecewise KL echoes piecewise likelihood/training and tree-based decompositions.

Strengths
- Novelty: Framing “tractability” via fast mixing is a fresh and bold idea. Projecting to a fast-mixing set is a conceptually clean bridge between sampling guarantees and approximation.
- Technical contribution: A dual procedure for Euclidean projection that (i) enforces a spectral-norm constraint on |β| and (ii) respects sparsity is nontrivial; the iterative SVD thresholding embedded in a constrained dual is interesting and potentially reusable.
- Algorithmic design: The projected stochastic optimization for KL(ψ||θ) is practical and generalizes MF (zero spectral norm) to a richer family with mixing guarantees.
- Empirical signal: On small-to-moderate instances, fast-mixing projections outperform standard variational baselines and can beat finite-time Gibbs on the original parameters.

Weaknesses
- Guarantee vs practice gap: The paper’s main theoretical guarantee hinges on ||R||2<1, yet experiments often use a spectral norm bound of 2.5, explicitly violating the stated sufficient condition. This undermines the “guaranteed fast mixing” narrative; at minimum, mixing diagnostics or empirical spectral-gap/autocorrelation evidence should be provided to justify the relaxed bound.
- Theory clarity/rigor: 
  - Lemma 5’s extension of Hayes’ bound to general Ising with fields is plausible but not fully derived here.
  - Theorem 6 appears to have a typographical error (singular value threshold should be min(Sii, c), not c/2).
  - The dual objective in Theorem 7 has odd formatting (g includes “1/||D−R||^2_F”), which is likely a typo; the convexity and feasibility handling of D=|B| merit more careful exposition. As written, it’s hard to verify correctness without the appendix.
- Scope of experiments: 
  - The graphs are relatively small; the 8×8 grid is standard but not large, and random graphs are n=10. The main bottleneck (repeated SVDs + sampling) raises scalability questions that are not addressed.
  - Computational accounting is inconsistent: accuracy-vs-time comparisons exclude projection cost, which can be substantial; this makes some conclusions less compelling.
  - No comparison to other “high-temperature” or correlation-decay-based approximations (e.g., SAW-tree/uniqueness-region methods), which are close in spirit.
- Diagnostics: Since the core selling point is fast mixing, reporting effective sample sizes, autocorrelations, or convergence-rate proxies for the projected models would strengthen the case substantially.

Quality
Technically solid overall with innovative elements, but there are important presentation issues (typos in key theorems) and an empirical-theory mismatch (using bounds above 1). The stochastic optimization procedure is plausible but lacks a convergence schedule compatible with the cited ergodic mirror descent guarantees (fixed step size and one Gibbs sweep per update are heuristic). Still, the paper is a complete piece of work with honest discussion of looseness in the spectral norm bound.

Clarity
Readable and well-motivated, but some equations likely contain typos; the dual derivation and constraints (D=|B|, sparsity via Z, and how B is recovered from D) should be clarified. Experimental protocol is described, though the time accounting caveat is important.

Originality
High. The projection-onto-fast-mixing viewpoint, the dual Euclidean projection respecting sparsity under an absolute-value spectral constraint, and the projected stochastic scheme for KL(ψ||θ) represent a novel direction distinct from standard variational families.

Significance
Promising. If developed further (tighter mixing conditions, scalable solvers, better diagnostics), this could open a new class of approximate inference methods with principled MCMC convergence behavior. As presented, it is an impactful idea with preliminary but encouraging evidence.

Questions and suggestions
- Please correct and clarify Theorems 6 and 7 (objective, gradients, and recovery of ψ from D). Is the optimization convex? How is D=|B| enforced in practice?
- Provide empirical mixing diagnostics for projected models and justify the choice of spectral norm bound >1. Can you relate empirical mixing time to ||R||2?
- Report end-to-end time including projection for time-accuracy comparisons.
- Explore tighter conditions (e.g., Dobrushin, per-node bounds, or graph-dependent norms) and whether your projection method can target them.
- Discuss scalability: cost per projection step (SVD) and per gradient iteration; any structure-exploiting variants?
- Could block Gibbs or tree/block updates enlarge the guaranteed set and improve approximation?

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, original direction redefining tractability via fast mixing.
- Useful dual projection algorithm with sparsity preservation.
- Practical projected stochastic method generalizing mean-field.
- Encouraging empirical results vs standard baselines.

Cons against acceptance
- Key theoretical guarantee not respected in main experiments; lack of mixing diagnostics.
- Some theorem statements appear incorrect/typo-laden; dual derivation clarity is lacking.
- Limited scalability evaluation and incomplete computational accounting.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite imperfections, the core idea is novel and potentially influential, and the paper offers concrete algorithms and evidence. With clarifications and stronger diagnostics, this could be a valuable contribution.

Confidence
High on overall assessment; medium on verifying the dual derivation due to missing appendix and typos.
