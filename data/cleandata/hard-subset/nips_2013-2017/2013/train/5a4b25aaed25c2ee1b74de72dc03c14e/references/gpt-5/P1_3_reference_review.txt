Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a new “tractable family” for approximate inference: parameter settings that guarantee rapid mixing of single-site Gibbs sampling. For binary Ising models, a sufficient condition is that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix R satisfies ||R||2 < 1; using Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij| yields a practical condition on the absolute interaction matrix |β|. Given an arbitrary (possibly slow-mixing) Ising model, the authors “project” its parameters onto the fast-mixing set Ψ under various divergences: (a) Euclidean distance in parameter space, for which they derive a dual algorithm that amounts to iterated spectral-norm thresholding while preserving graph sparsity; (b) the inclusive KL(q||p) (zero-avoiding), which is principled but requires expectations under the original slow-mixing model; (c) a piecewise KL based on tractable subgraphs; and (d) the reverse KL(p||q) (zero-forcing), optimized via stochastic approximation using samples from the fast-mixing model. This last choice connects to mean-field as the degenerate case with zero spectral norm. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare these projections to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original parameters, using exact marginals (junction tree) as ground truth.

The work relates to classical mixing-time results (Dobrushin, spectral criteria), variational approximations (MF, TRW), and tractable substructure methods (trees, planar zero-field). Using fast-mixing constraints as a tractable family appears novel and complementary to prior approximations based on low treewidth or specific tractable classes.

Strengths
- Conceptually novel bridge between MCMC and variational inference: approximate an intractable model by projecting to a class with guaranteed rapid mixing, then sample efficiently.
- Clear use of mixing-time theory to derive a practical constraint (|| |β| ||2 ≤ c) and a corresponding projection operator.
- Dual formulation for Euclidean projection that preserves graph sparsity and uses standard SVD-thresholding; this is technically interesting and potentially broadly useful.
- Reverse-KL optimization with stochastic gradients leverages the fast-mixing constraint to make expectations tractable; the connection to ergodic mirror descent is appropriate.
- Empirical comparisons against strong baselines (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs) and ablations across graph types and interaction strengths.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The main selling point—guaranteed rapid mixing—is weakened in practice: experiments often use a spectral-norm bound of 2.5 (vs. the stated sufficient condition of 1.0), so the guarantee no longer holds. While the authors are transparent, this undermines the theoretical-experimental alignment.
- Theorem 6 seems to contain a likely typo: S′ii = min(Sii, c^2) should be min(Sii, c) for a spectral-norm constraint ||B||2 ≤ c. Please clarify.
- The dual in Theorem 7 is not fully transparent. The objective g(Λ,M) appears to have typesetting issues (e.g., missing 1/2 factor, unclear operators such as Λ’Z and triple-dot products). A more explicit derivation and precise notation (Hadamard product, constants) would improve reproducibility.
- Reverse-KL gradient estimation (Eq. 8): using the same sample pool to estimate μ̂ and the product term introduces bias. The text claims unbiasedness if “two unbiased estimators are independent,” but here the unbiasedness relies on using an independent estimate for μ(ψ), not on a generic product-of-estimators property. Suggested fix: use two independent pools or leave-one-out μ̂ to reduce bias, and report variance-control techniques.
- Experimental scope is modest (8×8 grids; n=10 random graphs). No wall-clock or convergence diagnostics for the projected-sampling pipeline are reported, and the cost of projection (LBFGS-B + SVDs + MCMC for gradients) is not accounted for in time-accuracy tradeoffs. This limits claims about practical advantages over running Gibbs on the original model.
- The piecewise KL design (max over subgraphs) is arguably pessimistic and non-smooth; comparisons to alternatives such as convex combinations (TRW-style weights) or averages might be more favorable and theoretically motivated (e.g., providing bounds).
- Some related theoretical results on correlation decay and stronger uniqueness/rapid-mixing regions for Ising models could be discussed to contextualize the looseness of the spectral norm bound and possible improvements.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with appropriate references to mixing-time theory and variational methods. The Euclidean projection and the stochastic reverse-KL approach are well motivated. However, several derivations/notational issues (Theorem 6/7) and the bias discussion for Eq. 8 should be corrected/clarified. Experimental methodology is reasonable but limited in scope and lacks runtime/mixing diagnostics; the relaxation to c=2.5 detracts from the “guaranteed fast mixing” story.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with a clear narrative from theory to algorithms to experiments. The dual derivation and notation around the projection could be made more precise; including pseudocode for the projected stochastic gradient algorithm and explicitly stating update schedules (random vs systematic scan) and stopping criteria would aid reproducibility.

Originality
The idea of using the fast-mixing region as a tractable family and projecting onto it is novel, as is the dual projection respecting sparsity and the reverse-KL projected stochastic optimization. The piecewise KL notion is related to prior tree/mixture decompositions but applied in a new context.

Significance
If made practical and better aligned with theoretical guarantees, this approach could become a useful tool: fast, mixable surrogates enabling efficient MCMC with better accuracy than standard variational approximations under fixed budgets. The current experiments suggest promise but are not yet definitive; broader evaluations and tighter mixing criteria would increase impact.

Specific suggestions
- Fix Theorem 6 typo and clarify Theorem 7 notation and constants; include derivation details or supplementary proof.
- Report post-projection spectral norms, observed mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation times, coupling bounds, Gelman-Rubin) to support “fast mixing,” especially when using c>1.
- For Eq. 8, use independent μ̂ or control variates; report sensitivity to step size, sample-pool size, and number of Gibbs updates.
- Explore alternative piecewise objectives (averages or TRW-weighted sums) and compare.
- Provide wall-clock time and account for projection overhead in time-accuracy comparisons; test larger graphs to demonstrate scalability.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and interesting framing; technically nontrivial projection method.
- Practical algorithm that often outperforms standard variational baselines under fixed budgets.
- Clear path to extensions (learning with fast-mixing constraints; other MRFs).

Cons for acceptance
- Guarantees not respected in main experiments; limited empirical scope.
- Some technical and notation issues; gradient estimation bias not fully addressed.
- Limited discussion of stronger mixing criteria and missing runtime diagnostics.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the theory (Theorem 6/7), addressing the unbiasedness/bias issue, and adding mixing/runtime diagnostics. The conceptual contribution is strong and likely to spur follow-up work.
