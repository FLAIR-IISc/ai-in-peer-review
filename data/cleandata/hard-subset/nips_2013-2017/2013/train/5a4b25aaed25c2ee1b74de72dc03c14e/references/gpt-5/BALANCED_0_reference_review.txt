Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes a new “tractable family” for approximate inference: distributions whose parameters guarantee rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs sampling. For Ising models, the sufficient condition is that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix be less than one; the paper uses the bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij| (extending Hayes’ result), so constraining the spectral norm of |β| suffices. Given an arbitrary (possibly slow-mixing) Ising model, the paper projects its parameters onto this fast-mixing set under different divergences: (i) Euclidean distance in parameter space, via an SVD-thresholding-based projection that preserves sparsity through a dual formulation (Theorem 7); (ii) the “inclusive” KL(q||p), of theoretical interest due to the need to sample from the slow-mixing target; (iii) a novel piecewise KL that computes KL on tractable subgraphs; and (iv) the “exclusive” KL(p||q), optimized by stochastic approximation using samples from the fast-mixing model, with gradients expressed via covariance with sufficient statistics. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare these projections to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original parameters, measuring marginal errors against junction-tree ground truth. The authors report improved accuracy over variational baselines and competitive finite-time performance versus Gibbs on the original parameters.

This connects to extensive work on approximate inference using tractable substructures (mean-field, structured MF, GBP, TRW, planar decompositions, piecewise training, mixtures of trees) and to mixing time bounds (Dobrushin, Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum, Hayes). The proposed use of a parameter-space mixing condition as the tractable family and the associated projection machinery appears to be the main novelty.

Strengths:
- Clear and appealing idea: define tractability via rapid mixing rather than low treewidth; this is orthogonal to much of the variational literature and could be impactful for sampling-based inference/learning.
- Solid use of mixing-time theory with a practical sufficient condition for Ising models; the gradient expression for KL(ψ||θ) as Covψ[f,f](ψ−θ) is correct and leverages sampling from the fast-mixing model.
- The dual projection (Theorem 7) addresses two practical issues at once: controlling the spectral norm of the dependency while respecting sparsity; the SVD-thresholding subroutine is efficient and well-founded.
- Experiments cover both grids and random graphs, attractive and mixed couplings, and compare to standard baselines.

Weaknesses:
- The central guarantee is undermined in practice: experiments often use a spectral norm bound 2.5 instead of ≤1.0, with only empirical justification. This weakens the core premise (provably fast mixing) and makes the approach more heuristic.
- Experimental evaluation lacks transparency about computational costs. Time–accuracy comparisons do not account for the (potentially heavy) cost of repeated projections/optimizations, SVDs, and LBFGS-B, making it difficult to assess practical gains versus, say, longer Gibbs runs on the original model.
- The piecewise KL idea is interesting but underdeveloped and reported to perform worse than true KL; design choices for subgraphs and their coverage are not systematically analyzed.
- Stochastic optimization details for KL(ψ||θ): the estimator in Eq. (8) is a biased covariance estimator when reusing the same samples for μ̂ and the product; convergence guarantees of Ergodic Mirror Descent require step-size and number of MCMC updates to scale with iterations, but the experiments fix these hyperparameters without analysis.
- Clarity issues: some notation is terse (triple-dot product, Λ′Z operator), and the derivation/intuition of Theorem 7 is deferred; Section cross-references and symbols (q vs θ, ψ) are occasionally inconsistent.

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria:
- Quality: The theoretical components (mixing-time sufficient condition, Euclidean projection via dual and SVD thresholding, KL(ψ||θ) gradient) are sound. However, key practical choices deviate from the theory (using a looser spectral bound), and convergence/runtime analyses are limited. Experimental methodology would benefit from stronger controls and ablations.
- Clarity: Generally readable, but the projection dual deserves more exposition; experimental setup omits some critical details (e.g., total wall-clock accounting for projection phases, number of Gibbs iterations used for final marginals).
- Originality: Moderate. The idea to project onto a fast-mixing set and the dual to enforce both spectral constraints and sparsity are the main novel elements. Other components (piecewise KL, stochastic approximation for KL(ψ||θ)) build on known techniques.
- Significance: Potentially useful as a new approximation axis, especially for sampling-driven pipelines and learning with MCMC. The current conservativeness of the bound and limited empirical scale/analysis temper impact.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
- Provide experiments with the spectral norm truly ≤1 (and ≤tanh-bound) to validate the promised guarantees; separately, study the empirical fast-mixing regime as c varies, reporting actual mixing diagnostics and total computational cost.
- Tighten the dependency bound by directly constraining ||tanh(|β|)||2 rather than |||β|||2, which could reduce conservativeness.
- Clarify and expand Theorem 7: define the operators precisely (Λ⊙Z vs Λ′Z), state complexity, and show how sparsity is guaranteed in B.
- In stochastic optimization, use an unbiased covariance estimator (factor K/(K−1)) or separate sample sets; provide a stepsize and MCMC update schedule satisfying ergodic mirror descent assumptions, and report sensitivity analyses.
- Report wall-clock comparisons including projection/optimization overhead; add larger graphs; include block Gibbs or Swendsen–Wang baselines.
- For piecewise KL, systematically compare different subgraph families and coverage strategies; discuss when it helps and when it fails.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel tractability notion with a principled projection algorithm that preserves sparsity and controls a meaningful global property.
- Bridges variational and sampling approaches; could enable practical fast-mixing constraints in learning.
- Empirical results suggest improvements over common variational baselines.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Key guarantee not upheld in experiments (c > 1), making the method heuristic in practice.
- Limited and potentially unfair runtime comparisons; unclear practical advantage when accounting for projection cost.
- Some theoretical and methodological details are under-specified; piecewise KL underperforms and is not deeply analyzed.
- Scale of experiments is modest.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful direction, with a technically neat projection method. However, the current version falls short in aligning experiments with the theoretical guarantees, in thorough empirical analysis (including runtime), and in clarity of key derivations. Addressing these issues could make this a strong submission in a future iteration.
