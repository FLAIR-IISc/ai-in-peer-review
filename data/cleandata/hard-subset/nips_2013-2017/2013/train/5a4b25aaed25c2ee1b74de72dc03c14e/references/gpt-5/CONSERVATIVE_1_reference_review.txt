Summary
The paper proposes using “fast-mixing” parameter sets as a tractable family for approximate inference in Ising models. The key idea is to project an arbitrary parameter vector θ onto a set Ψ that guarantees rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs (via a spectral-norm condition on a dependency matrix), and then perform inference by Gibbs on the projected model. Four divergences for projection are considered: (i) Euclidean distance in parameter space with a dual algorithm (Theorem 7) based on SVD thresholding; (ii) the “inclusive” KL(θ||ψ) (zero-avoiding), deemed largely impractical beyond toy cases; (iii) a novel piecewise KL approximated via tractable subgraphs; (iv) the “exclusive” KL(ψ||θ) (zero-forcing), optimized via stochastic approximation using samples from the fast-mixing ψ. Experiments on small grids (8×8) and random graphs (n=10) compare these projections to MF, LBP, TRW and Gibbs on the original parameters; the authors report improved marginal estimates vs variational baselines and favorable time–accuracy tradeoffs versus Gibbs on θ under finite budgets. They also note that in practice a spectral-norm bound of 2.5 (rather than the theoretically sufficient <1) still seems to mix fast and gives better approximations.

Relation to prior work
The mixing-time condition builds on standard results (Dobrushin, spectral norm criteria; Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Hayes). Using Gibbs as a tractable engine is classic, but choosing a parameter-space subset to ensure rapid mixing and projecting onto it is a novel angle. The KL(ψ||θ) gradient matches the known Covψ[f]·(ψ−θ) form for exponential families. The piecewise KL idea relates to piecewise training and tree mixtures, but here is used as a divergence for projection.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: framing tractability via fast mixing and projecting parameters into a guaranteed-mixing set is interesting and could bridge MCMC and variational approximations.
- Reasonable theoretical scaffolding: leverages known bounds; provides a dual formulation (Theorem 7) to enforce graph structure while controlling spectral norm.
- Practical angle: optimizing KL(ψ||θ) via samples from ψ is sensible since ψ is constrained to mix rapidly; avoids intractable expectations under θ.
- The paper is generally clear and situates ideas among variational and sampling methods.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Guarantee vs practice mismatch: The central selling point is a provable mixing guarantee (||R||2<1 via a bound using |β|), yet the experiments use a spectral norm bound of 2.5, explicitly violating the stated sufficient condition. This undermines the main justification for the approach. If tighter empirical constants are required, one should either provide theory supporting them or deliver rigorous empirical evidence of mixing (mixing-time estimates, contraction rates, autocorrelation, or conductance-based diagnostics). As is, the method’s practical success appears to rely on violating its own guarantee.
- Looseness of the bound and projection target: Lemma 5 yields Rij ≤ tanh|βij|; projecting with respect to |||β|||2 rather than ||tanh(|β|)||2 is unnecessarily conservative. The paper does not explore projecting in the tanh-transformed space, which could materially tighten guarantees and reduce distortion.
- Incomplete validation and scale: Experiments are limited to 8×8 grids and n=10 random graphs where exact marginals are available. No larger-scale results, runtime breakdowns, or scalability of the projection (repeated SVDs, LBFGS-B outer loop) are provided. It is unclear whether the dual projection with SVD thresholding remains practical for larger systems and sparse graphs; SVD on dense matrices may become a bottleneck despite the sparsity masking.
- Comparisons: Baselines omit more advanced samplers (blocked Gibbs, Swendsen–Wang, tree/cluster samplers) and modern variational methods that might be competitive in Ising models. Since the premise is “fast mixing,” comparisons against samplers designed to mix rapidly are especially relevant.
- Optimization details for KL(ψ||θ): The stochastic gradient uses a fixed stepsize, a single sample pool of size 500 with one Gibbs sweep per iteration, and reuses the same samples for both terms in the product estimator, introducing bias. While the paper cites ergodic mirror descent, it does not implement the conditions (increasing chain lengths, diminishing stepsizes) needed for convergence guarantees. No sensitivity analysis to these choices is provided.
- Piecewise KL divergence: Defining D = max_T KL(θ(T)||ψ(T)) introduces a nonsmooth objective and depends critically on the chosen subgraph family. No theoretical connection is established between this divergence and the true KL, nor bounds relating performance under projection to approximation error. Empirically it underperforms true KL but remains included without clear guidance.
- Accounting for computational cost: The evaluation explicitly ignores the cost of projection/optimization, which can include tens of thousands of Gibbs iterations and repeated projections. Claims about time–accuracy tradeoffs should incorporate all compute budgets, not only the final sampling phase.
- Exposition gaps: Proofs are deferred, and some notation is terse (e.g., elementwise products, masking). A missing footnote marker suggests minor editorial issues.

Questions for the authors
1. Why not project with respect to the tanh-transformed interactions to exploit the tighter bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij|? Empirically, does this allow adhering to ||R||2<1 while achieving accuracy comparable to the c=2.5 setting?
2. Can you provide empirical mixing-time diagnostics (e.g., contraction coefficients, autocorrelation decay, spectral gaps estimates) demonstrating that the projected models mix rapidly, especially when c>1?
3. What is the computational complexity and wall-clock time of the dual projection routine as n grows? How many SVDs are performed per instance, and are matrices dense after the dual updates?
4. How sensitive is the KL(ψ||θ) optimization to stepsize, pool size, and number of Gibbs updates? Does using independent sample sets for μ̂ and the gradient change the outcome?
5. How does the method compare to blocked Gibbs or Swendsen–Wang under the same compute budget?

Suggestions
- Either adhere to the theoretical constraint and use tighter transformations (tanh), or augment the paper with rigorous empirical mixing diagnostics when relaxing c. Ideally, derive less conservative sufficient conditions for Ising that narrow the theory–practice gap.
- Provide larger-scale experiments, complete compute accounting (including projection), and runtime/accuracy tradeoffs.
- Add baselines with advanced MCMC and structured variational methods.
- Analyze the effect of projection strength (vary c) on both mixing and marginal accuracy.
- Clarify and include proofs; improve notation; fix editorial slips.
- Consider smoothing or averaging variants for the piecewise KL (e.g., weighted sums over subgraphs) and study its relation to the full KL.

Recommendation: Reject (leaning borderline). The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the empirical validation is too limited, the main guarantee is not respected in experiments, the projection target appears overly conservative, and comparisons and compute accounting are insufficient. Strengthening theory–practice alignment, scaling experiments, and broader baselines would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
