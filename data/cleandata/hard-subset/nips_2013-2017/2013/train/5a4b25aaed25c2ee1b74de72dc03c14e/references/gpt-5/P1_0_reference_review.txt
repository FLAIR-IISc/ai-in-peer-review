Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a new notion of tractability for approximate inference: instead of restricting to low-treewidth families (mean-field, structured mean-field, TRW, planar decomposition), restrict to parameter subsets that guarantee fast mixing of univariate Gibbs sampling. For Ising models, a sufficient condition is that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix R be < 1; using the bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|, this reduces to constraining the spectral norm of the absolute pairwise weight matrix. The authors study “projection” of an arbitrary (possibly slow-mixing) Ising model onto this fast-mixing set under several divergences: (a) Euclidean distance in parameter space, for which they derive a dual formulation that preserves sparsity and enforces a spectral norm constraint via iterative SVD-thresholding (Theorem 7); (b) inclusive KL KL(q||p), noted as impractical at scale; (c) a novel piecewise KL that takes a max over tractable subgraphs; and (d) reverse KL KL(p||q), which admits stochastic gradient based on sampling from the fast-mixing model and connects to mean-field as a degenerate case. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare these projections (and Gibbs on the projected model) to MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs on the original parameters.

This line relates to classic sufficient mixing criteria (Dobrushin; Hayes; Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum), variational approximations (MF, structured MF, TRW, GBP), and tractable decompositions (piecewise, mixtures of trees, planar decompositions). The key novelty is to use “fast-mixing” families as the approximation set and to develop computational projections into that set.

Quality
- Theory: The use of ||R||2 < 1 as a sufficient condition is sound, and the Ising-specific bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is standard. The Euclidean projection formulation and dual (Theorem 7) are interesting; it enforces both spectral norm and sparsity via masking and SVD projection Πc. The gradient for reverse KL is correctly given as Covψ[f](ψ−θ), and the stochastic approximation is sensible.
- Caveats: (i) The paper explicitly uses a spectral norm cap of 2.5 in experiments, though the theorem requires <1 for guarantees. This undermines the central “guaranteed fast-mixing” claim empirically; at minimum, please report diagnostics (e.g., autocorrelation/mixing time estimates, spectral gap proxies) verifying rapid mixing under the looser cap. (ii) The optimization for KL(ψ||θ) is non-convex in ψ (KL is Bregman in θ, not ψ), so only local solutions are guaranteed; this is acknowledged but could be emphasized. (iii) The estimator in Eq. (8) is biased when reusing the same samples for both factors; suggest either two independent pools or a control variate to reduce bias/variance. (iv) The piecewise KL “max over subgraphs” is non-smooth; the subgradient rule is stated, but stability/selection bias (which subgraphs dominate) is not analyzed.
- Completeness: Proof details for Theorem 7 are deferred; giving a brief sketch (Lagrangian, sign constraints, projection operator) would help. Complexity per projection step is dominated by repeated SVDs on dense matrices; scalability and sparsity exploitation deserve discussion.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The linkage from mixing-time theory to a concrete projection algorithm is explained cleanly. Minor issues: fix some typos (e.g., missing 1/2 in Eq. (2) formatting), clarify whether α (fields) are constrained (they don’t affect the R bound), and reconcile a small inconsistency in Section 6 about how “marginals are compared” (Gibbs vs exact JT; later you state exact JT is used). Also clarify whether systematic scan is used in experiments and whether the cited bounds apply directly.

Originality
- Using a fast-mixing parameter set as an approximation family and explicitly projecting into it is novel to my knowledge. The dual Euclidean projection that preserves sparsity while controlling || |B| ||2 is a technically neat contribution. The piecewise KL projection is a new twist on piecewise likelihood ideas.
- The reverse-KL projected SGD and its connection to mean-field as the R=0 case is a nice unification.

Significance
- If robust, the approach offers a practical alternative when exact inference is hard and long-run MCMC is too slow: project to a provably fast-mixing model and then sample. This could influence both inference and learning (as the authors note).
- However, significance is tempered by (i) the looseness of the spectral bound in practice (using 2.5 instead of 1.0), (ii) scalability concerns (global SVDs), and (iii) limited empirical scope (small graphs), making it hard to assess impact on large-scale problems.

Empirical evaluation
- Positives: Comparisons to MF/LBP/TRW and to Gibbs on the original parameters are appropriate. The study across attractive/mixed, densities, and strengths is informative.
- Concerns: (i) Time-accuracy comparisons should include projection cost; as written, the projected methods benefit from extra compute not charged in the curves. Consider wall-clock or iteration-normalized plots with and without projection. (ii) Report actual mixing diagnostics of the projected models, especially with c=2.5. (iii) Provide ablations: effect of spectral cap c, of sample pool size/steps, and of subgraph choices for piecewise KL. (iv) For grids, adding comparisons to tree sampling or block Gibbs would be helpful.

Pros
- Novel “fast-mixing projection” viewpoint; clear theoretical grounding.
- Technically interesting dual Euclidean projection with sparsity mask.
- Practical stochastic reverse-KL projection; unifies with MF.
- Sensible empirical comparisons; promising accuracy-time tradeoffs.

Cons
- Guarantees not honored in experiments (c=2.5); no mixing verification.
- Non-convex optimization; limited convergence discussion.
- Scalability of repeated SVDs not addressed; experiments are small.
- Piecewise KL design is heuristic; limited empirical benefit reported.
- Runtime comparisons omit projection cost.

Recommendation: Weak accept (borderline). The conceptual contribution is fresh and relevant to NIPS, and the technical pieces are sound enough to merit discussion, but the empirical section should be strengthened (mixing diagnostics, fair time accounting, scalability commentary). With revisions addressing these points, the paper would make a valuable contribution.
