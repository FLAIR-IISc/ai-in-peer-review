

# Peer Review: "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models by defining "tractability" not through structural constraints (like tree-width), but through parameter-space conditions that guarantee fast mixing of Gibbs sampling. For Ising models, the authors establish that spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix less than 1 ensures rapid mixing, and derive projection methods to map arbitrary parameters to this fast-mixing set using different divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, reversed KL). This complements existing work on approximate inference (mean-field [14], LBP [21], TRW [19]) by introducing a new tractable family that trades off between mixing speed and approximation quality.

The paper builds on foundational work by Hayes [8] and Dyer et al. [4] on mixing time bounds, but makes a significant conceptual shift from structural tractability to parameter-space constraints for tractability. It also connects to Globerson and Jaakkola's [6] planar graph approximation by considering a different notion of tractability.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear and well-supported theoretical contribution by linking parameter constraints to mixing time guarantees. Theorem 7 provides an elegant dual algorithm for Euclidean projection, which is a valuable technical contribution.

2. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments are thorough and well-designed, comparing against standard methods (LBP, TRW, MF) and Gibbs sampling on original parameters across grid and random graphs with varying interaction strengths. The results consistently show that the proposed methods outperform variational approximations in marginal accuracy.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The approach offers a principled way to obtain distributions that mix rapidly without sacrificing too much accuracy, which is valuable for applications where convergence diagnostics are difficult.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written, logically organized, and provides sufficient mathematical detail for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Conservative Mixing Bound**: The theoretical spectral norm bound (||R||₂ < 1) is significantly more conservative than known thresholds for the 2D Ising model (βc ≈ 0.44 vs. β = 0.25 for ||R||₂ = 1). While the authors note they use a bound of 2.5 in practice, they don't provide theoretical justification for this relaxation. This undermines the main theoretical contribution and makes the practical utility less clear. The discussion of this gap (Section 7) is too brief and doesn't acknowledge how much the theoretical bound overestimates the practical threshold.

2. **Computational Trade-off**: The paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost of the projection step. For example, they mention "30,000 total Gibbs iterations" for Euclidean projection but don't compare this to the number of iterations needed for Gibbs sampling on the original parameters to reach similar accuracy. Without this, it's unclear whether the method provides a net computational advantage.

3. **Limited Scope**: The focus on Ising models restricts the generalizability of the approach. While the authors mention future work on general MRFs, the paper doesn't demonstrate how the dependency matrix bound could be extended to non-Ising models, which would be a significant limitation for broader applicability.

4. **Motivation for Divergences**: The choice of "zero-forcing" KL (Section 5.4) over "zero-avoiding" KL is not well-justified. The paper acknowledges that "zero-avoiding" KL is more natural but dismisses it as computationally intractable without exploring practical approximations (e.g., using the fast-mixing property of ψ to approximate expectations with respect to θ).

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper with a novel conceptual contribution to approximate inference. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the experimental results demonstrate practical advantages over standard variational methods. However, the conservative mixing bound significantly weakens the theoretical contribution, and the paper doesn't fully address the computational trade-offs that would determine the method's practical utility.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- Introduces a fundamentally new notion of "tractability" that complements existing approaches.
- Provides a theoretically grounded projection method with strong empirical results.
- Well-written and clearly organized.

**Con Against Acceptance Arguments:**
- Theoretical bound is too conservative, reducing practical relevance.
- Insufficient discussion of computational trade-offs.
- Limited scope to Ising models without clear path to generalization.

## Recommendations

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Providing a theoretical justification for the practical use of the looser spectral bound (2.5 instead of 1.0).
2. Including a detailed computational cost analysis comparing the projection step to alternative inference methods.
3. Expanding the discussion on how the approach could generalize to non-Ising MRFs.

Given the paper's strong theoretical foundation, clear experiments, and novel perspective, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing these concerns. The core idea of using fast-mixing parameter constraints as a tractable family for inference is valuable and deserves publication at NeurIPS, especially since it addresses a practical limitation of standard variational methods.

**Rating: Strong Accept** (with minor revisions)
