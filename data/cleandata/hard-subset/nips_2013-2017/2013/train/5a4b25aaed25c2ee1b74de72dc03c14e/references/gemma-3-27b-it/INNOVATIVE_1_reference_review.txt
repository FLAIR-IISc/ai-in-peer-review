This paper presents a fascinating and bold idea: framing approximate inference as a projection onto a set of "fast-mixing" distributions. The core concept – leveraging the parameter space properties that *guarantee* rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling – is a significant departure from traditional approaches focused on approximating the target distribution directly (e.g., mean-field, belief propagation). The exploration of multiple divergence measures for this projection, and the associated theoretical analysis (particularly Theorem 7), is commendable. The connection to ergodic mirror descent is also a nice touch, framing the stochastic approximation as a well-understood optimization procedure.

The strengths of this work are substantial. The novelty is high; this isn't an incremental improvement, but a fundamentally different way to think about approximate inference. The theoretical grounding, while perhaps not fully exhaustive, is solid, providing a clear criterion (spectral norm of the dependency matrix) for fast mixing. The experimental evaluation, while not deeply extensive, demonstrates promise and provides a reasonable comparison to existing methods. The discussion section honestly acknowledges limitations and suggests avenues for future research. The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.

However, there are weaknesses. The experimental results are not overwhelmingly convincing. While the proposed methods often outperform standard variational approaches, the margin isn't always large. The choice of a spectral norm threshold of 2.5, rather than the theoretically justified 1.0, feels somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from more rigorous justification (beyond the empirical observation that it works better). The piecewise KL divergence, while an interesting idea, doesn’t perform as well as other methods, and the reason isn’t fully explored. The theoretical analysis relies on the dependency matrix, and extending this to more general MRFs, as acknowledged by the authors, is a significant challenge. Furthermore, the computational cost of projection, especially when using stochastic approximation, isn’t fully investigated relative to direct Gibbs sampling.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High novelty:** The idea of projecting onto fast-mixing distributions is a genuinely new and potentially impactful approach.
*   **Strong theoretical foundation:** The connection to mixing time analysis provides a solid basis for the proposed methods.
*   **Promising experimental results:** The methods demonstrate competitive performance compared to existing techniques, hinting at significant potential.
*   **Bold approach:** The paper tackles a difficult problem with a creative and well-motivated solution.
*   **Clear writing and presentation:** The core ideas are articulated effectively.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Modest experimental gains:** The performance improvements aren’t dramatic.
*   **Ad-hoc parameter choice:** The use of a spectral norm of 2.5 lacks strong theoretical support.
*   **Limited scope:** The focus on the Ising model, while providing a concrete example, may limit the generalizability of the approach.
*   **Computational considerations:** The overhead of projection needs further examination.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I lean towards **acceptance**. The novelty and boldness of the approach outweigh the current limitations. This paper introduces a compelling new perspective on approximate inference and opens up exciting avenues for future research. It doesn't present a fully polished solution, but it plants a seed that could grow into something very significant. I believe that the potential impact of this work justifies overlooking the minor flaws and incomplete validation. Further exploration of the theoretical properties and wider applicability of this projection framework is highly encouraged.
