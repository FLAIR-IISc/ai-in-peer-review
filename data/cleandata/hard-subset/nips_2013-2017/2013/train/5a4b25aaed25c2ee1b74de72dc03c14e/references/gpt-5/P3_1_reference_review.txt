Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a new “tractable” approximation family for high-treewidth Ising models: parameter settings for which single-site Gibbs sampling is provably fast-mixing. Building on sufficient mixing conditions framed via the dependency matrix R (Dyer et al. 2009; Hayes 2006), the authors operationalize tractability by constraining the spectral norm of |β|. Given a target Ising model θ that may be slow-mixing, they “project” onto the fast-mixing set Ψ by minimizing different divergences D(θ, ψ): (i) Euclidean distance in parameter space, for which they derive a dual algorithm that preserves sparsity and controls the operator norm (Theorem 7); (ii) KL(θ||ψ) (“zero avoiding”), of theoretical interest due to intractable expectations over θ; (iii) a novel piecewise KL approximation formed by KL on tractable subgraphs; and (iv) KL(ψ||θ) (“zero forcing”), optimized via stochastic projected gradient descent using samples from ψ, which is fast-mixing by construction. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare these projections against LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original parameters, with results suggesting better marginal accuracy than variational baselines and favorable finite-time trade-offs relative to Gibbs.

The idea of using mixing-time conditions as a tractable family is distinct from classical tree- or low-treewidth-based approximations (MF, structured MF, LBP/TRW, planar decompositions), and relates to but is different from guarantees on mixing of special dynamics (e.g., Swendsen–Wang) or sufficient conditions like Dobrushin’s criterion.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: reframing tractability via fast-mixing parameter sets is compelling and orthogonal to graph-structure tractability. It offers a principled way to justify Gibbs-based approximate inference with convergence guarantees.
- Methodological contribution: a dual Euclidean projection that preserves sparsity and enforces an operator-norm constraint on |β| is useful; the reduction from B to D=|B| turns a seemingly nonconvex problem into a convex one over D with a clear interpretation.
- Solid linkage to exponential-family calculus: the gradient identity for KL(ψ||θ) as Covψ[f](ψ−θ) is correct and leads to a practical sampler-based optimizer, akin to persistent/EMD ideas.
- Novel piecewise KL divergence: simple and tractable, extending piecewise likelihood ideas to divergence minimization.
- Empirical evaluation covers grids and random graphs, and compares to standard baselines; exact marginals are used for accuracy assessment.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Core guarantee sidestepped in practice: the central premise is a provable guarantee when ||R||2<1. Yet experiments use a spectral bound of 2.5 “which still preserves rapid mixing.” This weakens the main selling point. At minimum, report empirical diagnostics of mixing (autocorrelation, effective sample size, spectral gap proxies) and the fraction of runs that violated the bound; better, plot accuracy vs bound c and show actual mixing times.
- Theorem 6 likely contains a typo: S′ii=min(Sii, c^2). The usual projection onto the operator-norm ball clips singular values at c, not c^2. Please correct and ensure downstream derivations are consistent.
- Theorem 7 and optimization details: the objective g(Λ, M) uses D(Λ, M)=Πc[R+M−Λ∘Z]. The function is non-smooth at the projection boundary; using LBFGS-B assumes smoothness. Subgradient methods or careful handling of non-differentiability should be discussed. Also, gradients (Eq. 3–4) seem to omit the (D−R) factor one expects from 1/2||D−R||^2 unless this is absorbed via Danskin’s theorem through Πc; more derivation detail would help reproducibility.
- Tightness of the fast-mixing set: you use Rij≤tanh|βij|≤|βij|, then constrain || |β| ||2<1. The tanh-based matrix bound is tighter; even tighter are weighted norms or diagonal scalings (e.g., minimizing ||W R W−1|| to enlarge the feasible region), and Dobrushin-type weighted criteria. Considering such scalings could materially improve accuracy without sacrificing guarantees.
- Experimental methodology and fairness: 
  - The time-accuracy comparison ignores the computational cost of the projection/optimization phase, biasing towards the proposed methods. Either include total wall-clock or show both “training+inference” vs “inference-only” comparisons.
  - Hyperparameters for the stochastic optimizer (step size, pool size, number of scans) are fixed without sensitivity analysis; convergence guarantees of EMD require schedules—report robustness, and show the objective decrease and constraint satisfaction over iterations.
  - The small graph sizes are reasonable, but 8×8 is still modest. A medium-scale experiment (e.g., 16×16) using approximate ground truth (AIS or long runs) would better assess scalability.
- Piecewise KL uses a max over subgraphs, yielding a non-smooth objective and potentially overemphasizing the worst subgraph; alternatives (weighted averages, convex combinations of trees as in TRW) may work better and are worth comparison.
- Clarity/notation issues: 
  - Some inconsistent notation (Z′ vs ∘), missing factors, and minor typos (“KL-divergnce”).
  - Clarify whether α (unary fields) are projected or left unchanged; the constraint applies only to β, but ψ is updated over both α and β when optimizing KL—please state explicitly.
  - Section cross-references (e.g., 3.2) seem off.

Quality
Technically mostly sound, but (i) the operator-norm projection derivation and non-smooth optimization need clearer justification, and (ii) the main theoretical guarantee is not adhered to in experiments, limiting the strength of claims. Empirically, the evaluation is promising but somewhat limited and lacking runtime accounting and mixing diagnostics.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized; however, the dual derivation (Theorem 7) and optimization details need more explicit proofs/subgradient arguments. Fix typos and notational inconsistencies.

Originality
High. Framing a tractable family via fast-mixing constraints and projecting onto it is a novel and interesting departure from structure-based approximations. The piecewise KL divergence is a reasonable innovation.

Significance
Moderate. If strengthened, this line could influence approximate inference by turning mixing-time theory into practical constraints. As presented, the looseness of the bound and experimental shortcuts reduce impact, but the ideas are promising.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Correct Theorem 6 and add a rigorous derivation/proof for Theorem 7, including subgradient handling of Πc and explicit KKT conditions. Provide convergence behavior of the dual solver.
- Report how often the spectral norm constraint is active and satisfied, and include mixing diagnostics to substantiate “fast-mixing” in practice, especially when c>1.
- Explore tighter feasible sets: tanh-based matrices, diagonal reweightings, or block updates, and compare accuracy/feasible region sizes.
- Include total compute time and sensitivity to optimizer hyperparameters; show objective and constraint traces over iterations.
- For the piecewise KL, compare max vs average over subgraphs, and relate to TRW more explicitly.
- Clarify treatment of unary terms during projection.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and potentially impactful framing
- Useful Euclidean projection method that preserves sparsity
- Sound gradient formulation for KL(ψ||θ) and practical optimization approach
- Promising empirical accuracy versus standard variational methods

Cons for acceptance
- Experiments violate the key theoretical constraint (c>1) without rigorous mixing evidence
- Some theoretical derivations/notations need correction and clarification
- Limited scale and incomplete runtime accounting
- Piecewise KL and optimization are non-smooth; convergence and stability not thoroughly assessed

Recommendation: Weak accept if derivations are clarified and experimental evaluation is strengthened as above; otherwise borderline. Confidence: high.
