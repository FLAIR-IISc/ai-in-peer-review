- Proposes projecting intractable Ising model parameters onto a set that guarantees fast Gibbs mixing instead of approximating the distribution directly.  
- Leverages the connection between the dependency matrix spectral norm and mixing time to define the fast-mixing parameter set.  
- Investigates multiple divergence measures for projection: Euclidean (SVD thresholding), KL (theoretical), piecewise KL (low-treewidth approximation), and reversed KL (stochastic approximation).  
- Euclidean projection via SVD thresholding is computationally tractable; KL divergence is theoretically interesting but intractable in practice.  
- Piecewise KL approximates projection on low-treewidth subgraphs but performs worse empirically than other methods.  
- Reversed KL can be optimized with stochastic approximation, offering a practical alternative.  
- Empirical results show the projected parameters often outperform loopy belief propagation and naive mean-field, and provide a trade-off with running Gibbs on original parameters.  
- The work is well-grounded in mixing-time and spectral graph theory literature and cites relevant prior work.  
- The idea of parameter projection to ensure sampler efficiency is novel and insightful.  
- Theoretical results (projection theorems) are clearly stated and provide a solid mathematical basis.  
- Exploration of multiple divergences demonstrates thoroughness and highlights accuracy vs. tractability trade-offs.  
- Experiments are compelling and comparisons to both original and fast-mixing Gibbs are informative.  
- Writing and organization are clear, with mostly consistent notation.  
- The spectral-norm-based mixing bound may be overly conservative, and choice/sensitivity of the norm threshold is unclear.  
- Piecewise KL’s poorer empirical performance and the rationale for chosen subgraphs (horizontal/vertical chains) need more justification.  
- Stochastic approximation for reversed KL uses fixed hyperparameters and needs more systematic tuning and clearer connection to Ergodic Mirror Descent.  
- Scope is limited to the Ising model, leaving questions about generalization to other graphical models.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, strong empirical performance, and thorough divergence exploration.  
- Arguments against acceptance: conservative spectral-norm bound, weak piecewise KL results, and limited scope to Ising models.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions due to originality, theory, and empirical validation.  
- Suggested revisions: investigate tighter spectral-norm bounds, analyze piecewise KL choices and alternatives, systematically tune stochastic approximation, expand connection to Ergodic Mirror Descent, and discuss generalization beyond Ising models.
