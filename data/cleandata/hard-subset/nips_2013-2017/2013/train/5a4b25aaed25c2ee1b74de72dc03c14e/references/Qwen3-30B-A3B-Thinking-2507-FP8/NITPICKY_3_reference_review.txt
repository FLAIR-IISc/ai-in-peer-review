

# Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models by defining "tractable" models as those that are guaranteed to mix rapidly under Gibbs sampling. The authors focus on Ising models and propose projecting arbitrary (slow-mixing) parameters onto a fast-mixing set using various divergences. While the core idea of using fast-mixing as a tractability criterion is novel, the paper contains several critical technical flaws that undermine its contribution.

## Major Technical Issues

The most serious issue concerns the theoretical guarantee for rapid mixing. Theorem 4 claims that if ||R||₂ < 1, the mixing time is bounded by τ(ε) ≤ n/(1-||R||₂) ln(n/ε). However, standard mixing time bounds (e.g., in Levin et al. [10]) state τ(ε) ≤ (1/(1-||R||₂)) ln(1/ε) without the extraneous factor of n. This error makes the mixing time bound significantly looser than claimed and invalidates the theoretical justification for the spectral norm constraint.

The paper further contradicts its own theoretical guarantee in Section 6.1, stating "we find in practice using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of 1.0 can still preserve the rapid-mixing property." This directly violates the ||R||₂ < 1 condition from Theorem 4. The authors provide no experimental evidence that mixing is actually rapid at ||R||₂ = 2.5, nor do they explain how this empirical observation aligns with their theoretical bound. This inconsistency severely undermines the paper's foundation.

Lemma 5 states Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|, but the reference (Hayes [8]) only covers the special case of constant β and zero field. The general case for non-constant β and non-zero fields requires additional justification that the paper omits.

Theorem 8's gradient expression for the reversed KL divergence "d/dψ D(θ,ψ) = ∑_x p(x;ψ)(ψ - θ) · f(x) (f(x)- µ(ψ))" contains confusing notation. The dot product notation is ambiguous, and the expression does not match standard gradient calculations for KL divergence.

## Other Significant Concerns

The paper claims that "removing edges from both θ and ψ" preserves distribution equality (Section 5.3), which is incorrect. Removing edges from both distributions would alter the distributions, and they would not remain equivalent.

The paper states "Gibbs sampling on the rapid-mixing approximation is guaranteed to converge rapidly but will result in less accurate marginals asymptotically." This is misleading - if the projection is accurate, the marginals should be accurate, not "less accurate." The paper fails to clarify this fundamental point.

The dual optimization in Theorem 7 has a critical typo: "S′ ii = min(Sii, c 2)" should be "S′ ii = min(Sii, c)." This error would lead to incorrect implementation.

The experimental comparison with "Gibbs sampling on the original parameters" is misleading because the paper doesn't compare against standard fast-mixing methods like Swendsen-Wang, which would be a more appropriate baseline for their approach.

## Strengths

The paper's core idea of defining tractability based on rapid mixing rather than low treewidth is novel and conceptually interesting. The Euclidean projection method is theoretically sound for the correct spectral norm constraint. The experimental results show that their methods outperform standard variational approaches like mean-field and loopy belief propagation.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a promising conceptual framework but is undermined by multiple critical technical errors that invalidate its theoretical claims. The mixing time bound (Theorem 4) is incorrectly stated, the spectral norm constraint is violated in practice without justification, and key mathematical expressions contain errors.

The paper would be significantly stronger if it addressed these theoretical inconsistencies. As written, the technical flaws are too severe to recommend acceptance. The authors should first correct the theoretical foundation before proceeding with experimental validation.

The paper's significance is limited by the known looseness of the spectral norm bound (as acknowledged in Section 7), which means the proposed approach may not be applicable to many practical problems where the bound is too conservative.

Given the paper's technical shortcomings, I recommend rejection. The authors should address the theoretical errors before resubmitting.
