- Introduces a new notion of tractability for graphical models based on parameter conditions that guarantee fast mixing of Gibbs sampling rather than low treewidth.  
- For Ising models, shows a sufficient condition for fast mixing: the spectral norm of the interaction matrix < 1.  
- Proposes projecting arbitrary Ising parameters onto the fast-mixing set using four divergences: Euclidean, zero-avoiding KL, piecewise KL, and zero-forcing KL.  
- Provides theoretical results for the Euclidean projection and a dual method (Theorem 7) to handle graph-structure constraints.  
- Develops practical algorithms for KL-based projections that use Gibbs sampling to approximate gradients/expectations.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive (grid and random graphs, multiple strengths, 50 trials) and compares against variational baselines and Gibbs on original parameters.  
- Finds zero-forcing KL projection often yields more accurate marginals than mean-field and other variational methods for finite computation budgets.  
- Acknowledges that Gibbs on original parameters can be more accurate given enough time, but projections give better accuracy with limited resources.  
- Strengths: novel idea, strong theoretical foundation, practical implementation, and thorough experiments.  
- Weaknesses: spectral-norm bound is conservative (limits practicality) and not deeply analyzed; piecewise KL underperforms; limited comparison to more recent approximations (e.g., Bethe).  
- Clarity is good overall, but some technical points (dependency matrix to spectral norm connection) need more intuitive explanation and a small illustrative projection example.  
- Review recommends acceptance, citing significant theoretical and practical contributions.  
- Specific suggestions: analyze why the spectral-norm bound is loose, add a small example of the projection process, compare to recent mixing-time or inference methods, and discuss improvements for piecewise KL.
