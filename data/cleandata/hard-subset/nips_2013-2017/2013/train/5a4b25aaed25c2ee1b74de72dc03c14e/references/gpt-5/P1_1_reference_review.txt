Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a new notion of “tractable approximations” for graphical models based not on low-treewidth structure (as in MF, structured MF, LBP, TRW, planar approximations), but on parameter-space conditions that guarantee rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs sampling. For Ising models, using bounds due to Hayes/Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum, the authors leverage a sufficient condition: if the dependency matrix R has spectral norm < 1, Gibbs mixes rapidly; for Ising, Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|, so a sufficient condition is |||β|||2 < 1. Given an intractable model θ, they then “project” to a fast-mixing model ψ by minimizing a divergence subject to this spectral-norm constraint.

They study four divergences:
- Euclidean projection: a dual algorithm is derived that enforces both the spectral-norm constraint on |B| and the original sparsity pattern via a mask Z. The solution uses repeated projection onto a spectral-norm ball via SVD thresholding and LBFGS-B over dual variables (Theorem 7).
- KL(θ||ψ): the “inclusive/zero-avoiding” divergence has gradient μ(ψ) − μ(θ), but is impractical for large, slow-mixing θ; included for completeness/theory.
- A piecewise KL based on tractable subgraphs T (trees or low-treewidth subgraphs): D = max_T KL(θ(T)||ψ(T)).
- KL(ψ||θ): the “exclusive/zero-forcing” divergence has gradient Covψ[f](ψ − θ); the paper gives a Monte Carlo form usable with samples from ψ, and implements a projected stochastic gradient method (with Euclidean projection step) that can be viewed as Ergodic Mirror Descent.

Experiments on 8×8 grids and random graphs with n=10 compare projected models (Euclidean, piecewise, KL(ψ||θ)) to MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs on the original parameters. Accuracy is measured as mean absolute error in marginals (computed by exact junction tree). They also discuss time–accuracy trade-offs.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: proposes “fast-mixing models” as a tractable family for approximate inference, orthogonal to standard low-treewidth approximations. The connection to MF via KL(ψ||θ) restricted to zero spectral norm is insightful.
- Technical contribution: a dual projection algorithm (Theorem 7) that handles spectral-norm constraints on |B| while preserving graph sparsity. The reduction to repeated SVD thresholding is elegant and practically implementable.
- Soundness of KL(ψ||θ) gradient estimator: the covariance-form gradient is correct, and the Monte Carlo estimator is reasonable (with note on unbiasedness if using independent estimates of μ).
- Empirical indication that fast-mixing projections can outperform classic variational methods in marginal accuracy at comparable sampling budgets, and can offer better finite-time performance than running Gibbs on the original slow-mixing model.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification:
- Use of a looser spectral norm bound (2.5) than the theoretical guarantee (<1). This undermines the core selling point (guaranteed fast mixing). If the constraint is relaxed, the approach becomes heuristic; the paper should either (i) present evidence that the dependency norm of R (not just ||β||2) remains <1 in practice, or (ii) provide diagnostics of mixing (e.g., coupling bounds, autocorrelation, multiple-chain Gelman–Rubin, or empirical spectral gap proxies) to support the claim of “preserved” rapid mixing. As written, this is a significant gap.
- Experimental scale and fairness: experiments are limited (8×8 grids, 10-node random graphs). The time–accuracy comparison explicitly excludes the (potentially substantial) cost of repeated Euclidean projections and sampling during optimization, biasing in favor of the projected methods. A more balanced comparison should include total computational effort or wall-clock budgets, or at least sensitivity to projection effort (number of projections/SVDs).
- Theoretical clarity/typos: Theorem 6 states S′ii = min(Sii, c 2). For projection onto a spectral norm ball ||B||2 ≤ c, singular values should be thresholded at c (not c^2 nor c/2). Please correct. Some notation is also inconsistent (e.g., early text says “zero forcing” KL(q||p) but later defines KL(ψ||θ); clarify the ordering consistently).
- Theorem 7 details: the dual objective/gradients and the role of M ≥ 0 and Λ with the sparsity mask Z are interesting, but require clearer derivation and constraints. In particular, explain explicitly how the D = |B| constraint is enforced via M, why Z ⊙ D = 0 is satisfied at the dual optimum, and provide convergence guarantees (or conditions) for the LBFGS-B ascent (e.g., concavity or use of a saddle-point method).
- Piecewise KL: The design choice D(θ,ψ) = max_T KL(θ(T)||ψ(T)) is heuristic and non-convex in ψ; discuss alternatives (e.g., sum or average over T, reweighting like TRW) and the implications for optimization. Report how many subgraphs are used and computational cost.
- Evaluation details: There are conflicting statements about how marginals are obtained (“by running a Gibbs chain” vs “computed via exact junction tree”). Please clarify the evaluation protocol for each method. Report convergence criteria for sampling, number of Gibbs sweeps used for final marginal estimates, and whether systematic/random scan is used consistently across methods.
- Missing baselines/diagnostics: Since the work is motivated by mixing, it would be useful to compare with block Gibbs or tree sampling under the same constraint, and to report mixing diagnostics for ψ vs θ. Sensitivity to hyperparameters (step size, pool size, iterations) and c should be reported.

Quality:
- The analytical pieces (mixing bound, KL gradients) are sound, aside from typos and missing derivation details in the dual projection. The empirical evidence is promising but limited and partially undermined by the relaxed constraint and fairness issues.

Clarity:
- Generally readable and well-structured. However, Section 4–5 needs tighter notation and corrected typos (Theorem 6), and Section 6 needs clarified experimental protocol and time-accounting. Provide a more explicit algorithmic description for the projected stochastic gradient (step sizes, projection frequency).

Originality:
- Novel framing of tractability via fast mixing, and a dual projection algorithm that respects both spectral norm and sparsity, appear original. The projected KL(ψ||θ) approach extends MF in a principled way. The piecewise KL idea is related to piecewise likelihood/mixture-of-trees, but used here in a new projection context.

Significance:
- If strengthened, the approach could influence approximate inference and learning pipelines that rely on MCMC, by providing principled constraints to ensure rapid mixing. The current experimental scope is modest; broader demonstrations and more robust guarantees would increase impact.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Enforce the theoretical mixing condition (||R||2 < 1) in at least a subset of experiments and show corresponding performance; separately study the heuristic relaxed-constraint regime with empirical mixing diagnostics.
- Correct Theorem 6 and expand the derivation of Theorem 7 in the appendix; discuss convexity/duality and convergence.
- Include total computational cost comparisons; optionally provide wall-clock results with and without projection costs.
- Expand experiments (larger grids; stronger couplings), include sensitivity analyses to c and optimizer hyperparameters, and report mixing diagnostics.
- Clarify evaluation protocol and unify notation (θ, ψ; “inclusive/exclusive” KL).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually novel angle on tractable approximations (fast-mixing set).
- Solid technical ideas (dual projection; stochastic projected KL).
- Promising empirical gains over MF/LBP/TRW.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Key guarantee is relaxed in practice without sufficient empirical mixing evidence.
- Limited experimental scale and incomplete time-accounting.
- Some theoretical exposition/typos need fixing; piecewise KL underperforms and is only lightly analyzed.

Overall, this is an interesting and potentially impactful direction; with strengthened guarantees/experiments and clarified exposition, it would be a strong contribution.
