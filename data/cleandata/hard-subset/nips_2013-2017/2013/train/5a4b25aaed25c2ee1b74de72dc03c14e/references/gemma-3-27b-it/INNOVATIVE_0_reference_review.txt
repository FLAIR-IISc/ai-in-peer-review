This paper tackles a fascinating and potentially impactful problem: accelerating MCMC sampling by projecting parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set. The core idea – trading off parameter fidelity for faster convergence – is bold and represents a genuinely novel direction within approximate inference. It cleverly reframes tractability not as a property of the graph structure, but as a property of the parameter space induced by the graph. The exploration of different divergence measures for this projection is also commendable, particularly the introduction of the “piecewise” KL divergence as a pragmatic approximation. While the experimental evaluation is somewhat limited and the theoretical grounding could be strengthened, the novelty and potential significance of this work are substantial.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The central concept of projecting onto a fast-mixing parameter space is a fresh perspective on approximate inference. It moves beyond traditional tractable graph structures and focuses on the dynamics of sampling.
*   **Boldness:** The approach directly addresses a fundamental challenge in MCMC – slow mixing – with a non-trivial, parameter-space-focused solution.
*   **Multiple Divergence Exploration:** Investigating Euclidean distance, KL divergences (inclusive and reversed), and the piecewise KL provides a valuable comparison of different trade-offs between accuracy and computational feasibility.
*   **Connection to Mean-Field:** Recognizing the link between the zero-forcing KL divergence and generalized mean-field inference adds theoretical depth.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of slow mixing and motivates the search for fast-mixing alternatives.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Experimental Validation:** The experiments primarily follow the setup of Hazan & Shashua (2009), which limits the scope of evaluation. Exploring more complex and realistic scenarios (e.g., larger graphs, different interaction structures) would strengthen the findings. The comparison to Gibbs sampling on the *original* parameters feels cursory; more detailed time-accuracy trade-offs are needed.
*   **Theoretical Looseness:** The spectral norm bound for fast mixing (Rij < 1) appears conservative, as noted by the authors. A tighter bound would improve the practicality of the approach and provide stronger theoretical guarantees. The justification for using a bound of 2.5 empirically, without further theoretical investigation, feels ad-hoc.
*   **Piecewise KL Performance:** The piecewise KL divergence doesn’t perform as well as the other methods, raising questions about its practical utility. A deeper analysis of why it underperforms is warranted.
*   **Stochastic Approximation Details:** The stochastic approximation for the reversed KL divergence lacks detailed parameter tuning justification (step size, pool size, iterations). It feels somewhat like a black box, and the sensitivity to these parameters should be explored.
*   **Missing Related Work:** While the paper references key work on mixing times and approximate inference, it could benefit from more extensive discussion of recent advances in variational inference and spectral methods for graphical models.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel idea with the potential to significantly impact the field of approximate inference.
*   The exploration of different divergence measures demonstrates a thoughtful approach to the problem.
*   The connection to mean-field inference provides theoretical grounding and context.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-motivated.
*   The minor theoretical weaknesses do not overshadow the significance of the core idea.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental validation is limited and doesn’t fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach.
*   The theoretical bound for fast mixing appears conservative.
*   The piecewise KL divergence doesn’t perform well in practice.
*   The stochastic approximation algorithm lacks detailed parameter tuning justification.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field, despite its minor flaws. The novelty and boldness of the idea, coupled with the potential for practical impact, outweigh the shortcomings in experimental validation and theoretical tightness. I am inclined to **accept** this paper, with a recommendation that the authors address the weaknesses identified above in a future version. Specifically, expanding the experimental evaluation, investigating a tighter bound for fast mixing, and providing more detailed analysis of the piecewise KL divergence would significantly strengthen the work. The authors should also consider further discussion of related work on variational inference.
