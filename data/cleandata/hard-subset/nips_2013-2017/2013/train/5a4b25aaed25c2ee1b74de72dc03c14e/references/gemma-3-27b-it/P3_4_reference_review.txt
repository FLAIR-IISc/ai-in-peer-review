## Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of performing inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically the Ising model.  The authors propose a novel approach: instead of approximating the intractable distribution with a tractable one (like mean-field), they aim to *project* the intractable parameters onto a set that guarantees fast mixing of Gibbs sampling.  They explore several divergence measures for this projection – Euclidean distance (achieved via SVD thresholding), KL divergence (both directly and approximated via piecewise methods), and a reversed KL divergence – and analyze their computational feasibility and empirical performance.  The core idea is appealing: fast mixing provides a guaranteed convergence rate, and projecting parameters towards this regime could offer a beneficial trade-off between accuracy and computational cost.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper appropriately situates itself within the broader context of approximate inference for graphical models. It acknowledges the limitations of traditional approaches like mean-field [14, 16, 20], loopy belief propagation [21, 19], and planar graph methods [6].  The work builds upon existing research on mixing times in Markov chains [10, 4, 8, 11] and adapts these theoretical results to the practical problem of parameter estimation. The connection to mean-field is nicely highlighted, framing the proposed approach as a generalization that expands the set of approximating distributions beyond fully-factorized forms.  The acknowledgement of related work on piecewise likelihoods [17, 15] demonstrates awareness of relevant approximation techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of directly projecting parameters to guarantee fast mixing is a fresh perspective within the approximate inference landscape.  It moves beyond simply finding a tractable *distribution* to actively manipulating the *parameters* to achieve desirable properties.
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The work is firmly rooted in the theory of Markov chain mixing times, with clear definitions and references to relevant theorems (Definitions 1-3, Theorem 4, Lemma 5).
* **Multiple Divergence Exploration:**  The authors don't settle on a single approach.  Investigating Euclidean distance, KL divergence (and its approximations), and reversed KL divergence demonstrates a thoughtful exploration of the problem space.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper acknowledges the computational challenges associated with each divergence and proposes a stochastic gradient descent approach for the KL divergence, making it more tractable.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental setup, while simplified, provides a reasonable comparison against standard inference algorithms (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs sampling).  The results suggest the projected methods can offer a good trade-off between accuracy and speed.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Looseness of Spectral Norm Bound:** As the authors themselves acknowledge, the spectral norm of 1.0 used as a fast-mixing criterion is conservative.  The known critical value for the 2D Ising model is ~0.44, which undermines the effectiveness of the projection, potentially unnecessarily altering the parameters. This limitation significantly weakens the practical impact of the work.
* **Experimental Scope:** The experiments are limited to relatively small graphs (8x8 grids and random graphs with 10 nodes).  Scalability to larger, more complex networks is a crucial open question and should be addressed.
* **Parameter Sensitivity of Stochastic Gradient Descent:** The authors briefly mention the sensitivity of the zero-forcing KL divergence minimization to parameters (step size, pool size, etc.). More detailed ablation studies exploring the impact of these parameters would be beneficial.
* **Piecewise KL Divergence Implementation:** The choice of subgraphs for the piecewise KL divergence (horizontal/vertical chains for grids) feels somewhat arbitrary.  A more systematic approach to selecting subgraphs, potentially based on treewidth or other structural properties, could improve performance.
* **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Reversed KL:** The authors admit the reversed KL divergence might be inferior to the standard KL. The justification for exploring it feels weak; a stronger argument or a specific scenario where it might excel would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to approximate inference.
* It thoroughly explores multiple divergence measures and proposes practical algorithms for performing the projection.
* The experimental results, while limited, demonstrate the potential of the method to achieve a good trade-off between accuracy and computational cost.
* The work opens up a new direction for research in graphical model inference, focusing on parameter manipulation rather than solely on distribution approximation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The conservative spectral norm bound limits the practical effectiveness of the projection.
* The experiments lack scalability and a comprehensive parameter analysis.
* The justification for exploring the reversed KL divergence is weak.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of approximate inference. While the conservatism of the spectral norm bound and the limited experimental scope are significant weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, the strong theoretical foundation, and the thoughtful exploration of multiple divergences outweigh these concerns. The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it accessible to a broad audience. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors:

1. **Address the Spectral Norm Bound:**  Investigate tighter bounds on the spectral norm that guarantee fast mixing for the Ising model.  A more accurate bound would significantly improve the practicality of the approach.
2. **Expand Experimental Evaluation:**  Include experiments on larger and more complex networks to assess scalability.
3. **Detailed Parameter Tuning:** Conduct a more comprehensive ablation study of the parameters involved in the stochastic gradient descent optimization of the zero-forcing KL divergence.
4. **Clarify Motivation:**  Provide a stronger justification for including the reversed KL divergence in the experimental evaluation.
