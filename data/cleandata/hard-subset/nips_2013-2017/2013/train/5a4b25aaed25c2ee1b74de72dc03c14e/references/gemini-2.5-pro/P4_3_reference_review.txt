This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models. Standard methods, such as mean-field or loopy belief propagation, approximate an intractable distribution by projecting it onto a family of structurally simpler distributions (e.g., fully-factorized or tree-structured). In contrast, this work defines a family of "tractable" distributions based on a different criterion: whether Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to mix rapidly. The authors leverage a known result from the MCMC literature [4, 8] which provides a sufficient condition for fast mixing in Ising models: the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths must be less than one.

The core contribution is to approximate a given (potentially slow-mixing) Ising model by finding the closest set of parameters within this fast-mixing set. The paper explores this projection under four different divergences. First, for the Euclidean distance between parameter vectors, a dual optimization algorithm is derived (Theorem 7) that can perform the projection while preserving the original graph's sparsity. Second, the standard "zero-avoiding" KL-divergence, KL(q||p), is considered, but noted to be intractable as it requires inference on the original, slow-mixing distribution `q`. Third, a novel "piecewise" KL-divergence is proposed as a tractable approximation, where the KL is computed on a set of low-treewidth subgraphs. Finally, the "zero-forcing" KL-divergence, KL(p||q), is explored. This divergence is computationally more feasible because its gradient expectation is taken with respect to the approximating distribution `p`, which is constrained to be fast-mixing and can thus be sampled from efficiently. The optimization is performed using a projected stochastic gradient descent method. Experiments on grid and random graphs show that the proposed methods, particularly the KL(p||q) projection, produce more accurate marginal estimates than standard baselines like loopy belief propagation, tree-reweighted belief propagation, and naive mean-field.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem as a projection onto a constrained parameter set is well-defined. The derivation of the Euclidean projection algorithm via a dual formulation (Theorem 7) is a solid contribution. The use of projected stochastic gradient descent for the KL(p||q) divergence is a principled and appropriate optimization strategy. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against strong and relevant baselines on standard graph structures. Claims of improved marginal accuracy are well-supported by the empirical results.

A notable weakness, which the authors transparently discuss, is the disconnect between the theoretical mixing time bound and the one used in practice. The theory section establishes that a spectral norm less than 1.0 guarantees fast mixing, yet the experiments use a bound of 2.5, noting it "gives better approximation." While this may be true empirically, it is an ad-hoc choice that lacks the theoretical guarantee that is the central premise of the paper. This suggests the underlying theoretical bound is quite loose, which tempers the rigor of the proposed "guarantee."

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the work by contrasting it with existing approximate inference paradigms. The background on mixing times is concise and provides the necessary context. The progression from the simple Euclidean projection to the more complex KL-divergences is logical and easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly. For reproducibility, the description of the stochastic gradient algorithm could benefit from slightly more detail regarding the sample pool management and update schedule, though the citation of Ergodic Mirror Descent [3] provides a theoretical pointer.

**Originality:** The central idea of defining a tractable family of distributions based on MCMC mixing properties, rather than structural properties like treewidth, is highly original. While the mixing time bounds themselves are established results, using them to define a constraint set for a projection-based approximation scheme is a novel and insightful contribution. This reframing of "tractability" for approximate inference is a significant conceptual advance. The proposed "piecewise KL-divergence," while not the best-performing method in the experiments, is also a novel idea for approximating divergences on intractable graphs.

**Significance:** The paper introduces a promising new direction for approximate inference that elegantly connects variational methods with MCMC theory. The results are significant, demonstrating that for a fixed computational budget, sampling from a projected fast-mixing distribution can yield more accurate marginals than sampling from the original slow-mixing one. This has practical implications for practitioners who use MCMC. The work could inspire a new class of inference algorithms that optimize model parameters for both accuracy and sampling efficiency. The primary limitation to its immediate impact is its restriction to Ising models. As the authors note, extending this framework to general Markov random fields presents non-trivial challenges, both in deriving suitable mixing time bounds and in developing corresponding projection algorithms.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   The core concept of defining tractability via fast mixing for approximate inference is highly novel and opens up a new avenue of research.
*   The paper is technically solid, providing a new algorithm for Euclidean projection and a principled stochastic approach for KL projection.
*   The experimental results are strong, showing clear improvements over widely used variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) on standard benchmarks.
*   The work is well-written, clearly motivated, and situates itself effectively within the broader context of approximate inference literature.

### Arguments Against Acceptance
*   There is a significant discrepancy between the theoretical spectral norm bound (< 1.0) that guarantees fast mixing and the ad-hoc bound (2.5) used to achieve good empirical results. This weakens the central claim of projecting onto a *guaranteed* fast-mixing set.
*   The method's applicability is currently limited to Ising models. The path toward generalizing to broader classes of graphical models is not clear and appears challenging, potentially limiting the work's immediate impact.
