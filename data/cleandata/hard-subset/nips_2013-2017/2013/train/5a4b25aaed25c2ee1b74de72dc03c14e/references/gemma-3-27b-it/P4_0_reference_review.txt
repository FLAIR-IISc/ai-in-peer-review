This paper explores the notion of “fast-mixing” graphical models as a tractable approximation for inference in intractable models like high-treewidth Ising models. The core idea is to project parameters onto a set where Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to converge rapidly, leveraging existing theory on mixing times. The authors investigate several projection methods, including Euclidean projection via SVD thresholding and projections based on KL divergence, with varying degrees of computational feasibility. They provide theoretical results for the Euclidean projection and demonstrate empirical performance on small Ising models, comparing their approach to standard inference algorithms like LBP, TRW, and MF.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The framing of “tractability” in terms of fast mixing, rather than specific graph structures, is a fresh perspective. This provides a different lens for approaching approximate inference.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The work is grounded in established theory regarding Markov chain mixing times, specifically utilizing the spectral norm of the dependency matrix. The theorems presented (e.g., Theorem 7) are valuable contributions.
* **Multiple Projection Methods:** Exploring various divergence measures (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL) for projection provides a comprehensive analysis of trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments, while limited to small graphs, demonstrate the potential benefits of the proposed approach and provide a fair comparison with existing methods.  The inclusion of Gibbs sampling with original parameters as a baseline provides useful context.
* **Clear Connection to Mean Field:** The paper effectively positions the zero-forcing KL-divergence approach as a generalization of mean field inference.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability:** The experiments are restricted to small graphs, limiting the assessment of practical applicability. The computational complexity of the KL-divergence based projections, even with approximations, seems prohibitive for larger problem instances.
* **Conservatism of the Spectral Norm Bound:** The discussion acknowledges the potential looseness of the spectral norm bound used to guarantee fast mixing, which impacts the effectiveness of the projection. A tighter bound would significantly improve the approach.
* **Empirical Tuning:**  The discussion of parameter tuning for stochastic gradient descent (step size, pool size, iterations) feels cursory. More rigorous sensitivity analysis could strengthen the empirical results.
* **Piecewise KL-divergence Performance:** The piecewise KL-divergence approach doesn’t perform as well as other methods, and the rationale for its relative weakness isn’t fully explored.  Further investigation into the impact of subgraph selection on performance would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments largely focus on Ising models. While providing a focused evaluation, exploring the applicability to other graphical model families would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound framework for approximate inference.
* It provides valuable theoretical contributions, such as the Euclidean projection theorem.
* The experimental results, while limited, support the potential effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The work addresses a fundamental challenge in graphical model inference: efficiently approximating intractable distributions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The scalability of the proposed methods remains a significant concern.
* The conservatism of the spectral norm bound limits the practical impact.
* The empirical evaluation is restricted to small graphs and a specific model family.
* The piecewise KL-divergence approach appears less promising, and its limitations aren't fully addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a creative and theoretically grounded approach to approximate inference in graphical models. The core idea of leveraging fast-mixing distributions is promising, and the authors provide a solid foundation for future work. However, the scalability limitations and the conservative nature of the spectral norm bound raise concerns about its immediate practical impact. While the empirical results are encouraging, they are limited in scope.

**Recommendation:**

I would recommend **weak accept** with the caveat that the authors address the scalability concerns and investigate tighter bounds on mixing times in a future version. The novelty and theoretical contributions warrant acceptance, but the practical limitations need to be acknowledged and potentially mitigated. The paper represents a valuable contribution to the field, and further research in this direction is likely to be fruitful.
