This paper explores the idea of projecting parameters of an Ising model onto a “fast-mixing” set, aiming to accelerate convergence of Gibbs sampling for inference. The core concept – leveraging fast-mixing Markov chains as a tractable approximation – is a compelling alternative to traditional approaches like mean-field or tree-based approximations. The authors investigate several divergence measures for this projection, including Euclidean distance (via SVD thresholding), KL divergence, piecewise KL divergence, and a “zero-forcing” KL divergence amenable to stochastic approximation. The experimental evaluation on small Ising models demonstrates the potential of this approach, showing improved accuracy compared to standard variational methods, though acknowledging the eventual dominance of sufficiently long Gibbs sampling with the original parameters.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of using fast-mixing as a defining property of a tractable approximation space is interesting and potentially impactful. It shifts the focus from graph structure to parameter space properties.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is well-grounded in the theory of Markov chain mixing times, properly citing relevant work (Dyer et al., Hayes, Levin et al.). The theoretical results (Theorem 6 & 7) for the Euclidean projection are valuable.
*   **Multiple Divergence Measures:** Exploring different divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, zero-forcing KL) provides a nuanced investigation of the trade-offs between accuracy, computational cost, and theoretical properties.
*   **Experimental Validation:**  The experiments provide a reasonable initial evaluation of the proposed methods, demonstrating improvements over LBP, TRW, and naive mean-field.  The comparison with Gibbs sampling provides a crucial benchmark.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with a clear explanation of the motivation, theoretical background, and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scalability:** The experiments are confined to small (8x8 grid, 10-node random graphs) Ising models. This significantly limits the generalizability of the findings.  The computational cost of the projections, particularly for the KL-divergence based methods, is likely to become prohibitive for larger models.
*   **Conservatism of Spectral Norm Bound:** The authors acknowledge that the spectral norm bound for fast mixing may be too conservative, citing the known critical value for the 2D Ising model. This suggests that the projection might be unnecessarily restrictive, potentially sacrificing accuracy.
*   **Experimental Parameter Tuning:** The limited tuning of parameters for the stochastic gradient descent optimization of the zero-forcing KL divergence is acknowledged.  More systematic tuning could potentially improve performance. The choice of a spectral norm bound of 2.5 seems arbitrary without further justification.
*   **Piecewise KL Divergence Implementation:** The choice of subgraphs for the piecewise KL divergence appears somewhat ad-hoc. A more systematic approach to selecting subgraphs could be beneficial.
*   **Lack of Connection to Existing Work:** While the paper cites related work on approximate inference, it could benefit from a more thorough discussion of how this approach relates to other recent advances in variational inference or structured approximations.

**Originality:**

The core idea of leveraging fast-mixing as a tractability criterion is novel. While projections onto constrained parameter spaces have been explored before (e.g., in sparse learning), the specific focus on fast mixing is a distinct contribution. The piecewise KL divergence, while building on existing ideas of piecewise likelihoods, represents a novel application in this context.

**Significance:**

The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in graphical model inference – dealing with intractability.  If the fast-mixing projection can be effectively scaled to larger models, it could provide a valuable alternative to existing approximate inference methods. The concept of fast-mixing as a tractable approximation criterion could potentially extend beyond the Ising model to other Markov random fields. However, the current limited scalability casts doubt on immediate practical impact.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and theoretically grounded approach to approximate inference.
*   The experimental results demonstrate promising performance on small models.
*   The exploration of multiple divergence measures provides valuable insights.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limited scalability of the methods is a significant concern.
*   The conservatism of the spectral norm bound may limit the effectiveness of the projection.
*   The experiments lack thorough parameter tuning and a more systematic evaluation of subgraph selection for the piecewise KL divergence.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **Reject**. While the ideas presented are interesting and the paper is technically sound, the limited scalability and lack of extensive experimentation prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The potential of the approach is evident, but further work is needed to address the scalability issues and demonstrate its effectiveness on larger, more realistic models.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Scalability Investigation:**  Focus on developing more scalable algorithms for the projections, perhaps exploring approximations or efficient optimization techniques.
*   **Tighter Bounds:** Investigate tighter bounds on the spectral norm for fast mixing in Ising models, potentially leveraging more advanced theoretical results.
*   **Systematic Parameter Tuning:** Conduct a more thorough parameter tuning of the stochastic gradient descent algorithm for the zero-forcing KL divergence.
*   **Subgraph Selection:** Develop a more systematic approach to selecting subgraphs for the piecewise KL divergence, perhaps based on graph structure or information-theoretic criteria.
*   **Expand Experiments:** Test the methods on larger, more complex models and compare with state-of-the-art approximate inference algorithms.
*    **Connect to related work:** A deeper discussion of how this work relates to existing variational inference and structured approximation approaches would be beneficial.
