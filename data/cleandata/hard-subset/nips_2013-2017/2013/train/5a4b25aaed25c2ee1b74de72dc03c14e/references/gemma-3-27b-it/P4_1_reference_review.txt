The paper investigates projecting Ising model parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set, aiming to accelerate Gibbs sampling for inference. The core idea is to identify parameter spaces that guarantee rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling, based on the spectral norm of the dependency matrix, and then project parameters from arbitrary distributions onto this space using various divergence measures (Euclidean, KL-divergence, piecewise KL, and a reversed KL). The authors provide theoretical results for Euclidean projection via SVD thresholding and explore practical algorithms for approximating the other divergences. Experiments demonstrate that this projection approach can improve inference speed and accuracy compared to standard variational methods like loopy belief propagation and naive mean field, and often outperforms direct Gibbs sampling given limited computational resources.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The exploration of "fast-mixing" distributions as a tractable approximation is a relatively novel idea. While the concept of fast mixing is well-established, framing it as a target for projection to accelerate inference is a worthwhile contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper builds upon solid theoretical foundations in Markov chain Monte Carlo and graphical models. The derivations of the projection theorems and gradient updates appear correct, and the connection to existing work on spectral norms and mixing times is clearly established.
*   **Completeness:** The authors investigate multiple divergence measures for projection, offering a comprehensive exploration of the idea. They also address practical challenges like approximating intractable divergences and provide a concrete stochastic gradient descent algorithm for the reversed KL-divergence.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments provide convincing evidence that the proposed methods can improve inference accuracy and speed. The comparisons to baseline methods (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs) are well-chosen and demonstrate the benefits of the approach.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical concepts. The organization is logical, progressing from background material to theoretical results and experimental validation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Conservatism of Spectral Norm Bound:** The authors acknowledge that the spectral norm bound used for guaranteeing fast mixing might be too conservative, citing the known critical value for the 2D Ising model. This suggests that the projection might be unnecessarily restrictive, potentially discarding useful information from the original parameters.
*   **Computational Cost of Projection:** While the paper highlights the speedup in inference *after* projection, it doesn't fully address the computational cost of the projection step itself. The experiments report the runtime of Gibbs sampling *after* projection, but a more complete comparison should include the cost of the projection algorithm.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are primarily focused on Ising models with specific graph topologies (grids and random graphs). Expanding the experimental evaluation to other graphical models and application domains would strengthen the generality of the findings.
*   **Lack of Detailed Parameter Tuning:** The authors mention tuning parameters for the stochastic gradient descent algorithm, but the details are sparse. A more thorough discussion of parameter sensitivity and the impact of different settings would be helpful.
*   **Piecewise KL Divergence Implementation:** The performance of the piecewise KL divergence is not compelling. The choice of subgraphs seems critical, and the paper does not offer strong justification for the selected approaches or explore alternative subgraph selection strategies.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a novel and theoretically sound approach to approximate inference in graphical models.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods in improving inference accuracy and speed.
*   The work addresses a challenging problem in probabilistic inference and offers a promising direction for future research.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a comprehensive exploration of the topic.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The spectral norm bound might be overly conservative, limiting the potential benefits of the projection.
*   The computational cost of the projection step is not fully considered in the experimental evaluation.
*   The scope of the experiments is limited, and the generality of the findings is unclear.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of approximate inference. The idea of projecting onto "fast-mixing" distributions is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results are encouraging. While there are some weaknesses, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance to NIPS, but would benefit from addressing the concerns raised regarding the conservatism of the spectral norm bound and the computational cost of the projection.
