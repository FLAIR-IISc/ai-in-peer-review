**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically Ising models. Instead of approximating an intractable distribution with one that has a tractable structure (e.g., low-treewidth or fully factorized), the authors propose approximating it with a distribution that is "fast-mixing." The condition for fast mixing is derived from prior work on MCMC convergence, which guarantees rapid mixing for Gibbs sampling if the spectral norm of the dependency matrix is less than one. For Ising models, this translates to a constraint on the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths. The core contribution is a set of methods for projecting an arbitrary (potentially slow-mixing) set of Ising model parameters onto this fast-mixing set. The paper explores four different projection criteria: Euclidean distance, the inclusive KL-divergence (KL(q||p)), a novel "piecewise KL" approximation, and the exclusive KL-divergence (KL(p||q)).

This work positions itself as an alternative to established variational inference methods like mean-field (MF), structured mean-field, and belief propagation (LBP, TRW). The authors correctly note that minimizing the exclusive KL-divergence (KL(p||q)) with a constraint that the spectral norm is zero is equivalent to naive mean-field, thus framing their method as a direct generalization where the approximating family is expanded from fully-factorized distributions to the larger set of fast-mixing distributions. The piecewise KL-divergence is inspired by work on piecewise likelihoods and tree-based approximations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Conception of Tractability:** The central idea of defining a tractable family of distributions based on MCMC mixing time rather than graph structure is novel and compelling. It opens a new direction for approximate inference that is conceptually different from the dominant structure-based approaches.
2.  **Theoretical Grounding:** The approach is well-grounded in the theory of Markov chain mixing times. The derivation of a projection algorithm for the Euclidean norm (Theorem 7), while complex, appears technically sound.
3.  **Comprehensive Exploration:** The paper explores the projection idea under four different divergences, demonstrating a thorough investigation of the problem. The development of a stochastic projected gradient method for the exclusive KL-divergence is a natural and well-motivated algorithm.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the novel core idea, the paper is riddled with a substantial number of methodological inconsistencies, minor errors, and a lack of rigor that collectively undermine its contribution.

1.  **Fundamental Methodological Flaw in Experiments:** The paper's theoretical motivation rests entirely on Theorem 4, which guarantees fast mixing if the spectral norm of the dependency matrix is less than 1.0. However, in Section 6.1, the authors state: "we find in practice using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of 1.0 can still preserve the rapid-mixing property and gives better approximation". This is a critical flaw. By using a bound of 2.5, there is no longer any theoretical guarantee of fast mixing, which is the entire premise of the paper. This ad-hoc choice of a "better" hyperparameter completely decouples the experiments from the theory. The authors provide no empirical evidence (e.g., convergence diagnostics) to support the claim that a spectral norm of 2.5 still leads to fast mixing. This invalidates the central claim that they are projecting onto a provably fast-mixing set.

2.  **Lack of Rigor in Stochastic Approximation:** In Section 5.4, the authors describe their stochastic gradient estimator for KL(ψ||θ). They correctly note that using the same set of samples to estimate both the mean `µ̂` and the expectation in the gradient (Eq. 8) leads to a biased estimator. Their justification for doing so is that it "makes little difference" in practice. This is an unsubstantiated empirical claim that sidesteps a known statistical issue. A careful paper would either use separate sample sets, or provide a theoretical or empirical analysis of the effect of this bias.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:**
    *   The paper's organization is confusing. Section 4 is dedicated entirely to deriving the Euclidean projection, only for Section 5.1 to re-introduce the Euclidean distance as the first of four divergences. These sections should have been integrated.
    *   The introduction of Ergodic Mirror Descent [3] feels like an afterthought, mentioned in parentheses in two separate sections without a clear explanation of how the proposed algorithm is a specific instance of it or what theoretical guarantees this connection provides.
    *   The footnote in Section 5.3 regarding the motivation for piecewise KL is hand-wavy and lacks formal justification.

4.  **Minor Technical and Notational Issues:**
    *   The "triple dot product" notation in Theorem 7 is non-standard and poorly defined.
    *   Theorem 8 is stated for the gradient of KL(ψ||θ), but the expression appears to be a covariance, not a gradient vector. The derivation should be provided or cited, as the form is not standard.
    *   The piecewise KL divergence uses a `max` over subgraphs, which is unusual. Standard piecewise methods typically use a sum. The authors provide no justification for this choice.
    *   The experiments use a "systematical scan" for Gibbs sampling, while the motivating theory (Theorem 4) is for random updates. While the authors note that similar results exist for systematic scans, citing the specific result would be more rigorous.

5.  **Inadequate Evaluation:** The paper avoids a direct comparison of computational effort. It acknowledges that the projection step has a computational cost but then evaluates performance based only on the sampling time *after* projection. The claim that "more aggressive parameters can lead to this procedure being more accurate than Gibbs in a comparison of total computational effort" is presented without evidence. A truly convincing evaluation would plot accuracy against total wall-clock time, including both projection and sampling.

6.  **Formatting and Presentation:** The reference list is poorly and inconsistently formatted. For example, reference [1] contains trailing commas, [13] is incomplete, and there is no consistent style for titles, journal names, or page numbers. This lack of attention to detail permeates the manuscript.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and interesting idea: defining tractability via fast mixing.
*   The exploration of the idea across multiple divergences is comprehensive.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental methodology contains a fatal flaw: the spectral norm bound used (2.5) is not theoretically justified and undermines the paper's entire premise of guaranteeing fast mixing.
*   The paper suffers from a general lack of rigor, including unsubstantiated claims about biased estimators and hand-wavy justifications for methodological choices.
*   The evaluation is incomplete as it fails to properly account for the computational cost of the projection step.
*   The paper is poorly organized and contains numerous minor notational, presentational, and formatting errors, suggesting a lack of careful preparation.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the central idea of this paper is promising, the execution is deeply flawed. The decision to abandon the theoretically-motivated spectral norm bound of 1.0 for an empirically-chosen value of 2.5 in the experiments is a critical failure that severs the link between the paper's theory and its results. This, combined with a lack of rigor in the stochastic approximation, an incomplete evaluation, and a general sloppiness in presentation and formatting, leads me to recommend rejection. The accumulation of these flaws, both large and small, prevents me from endorsing this work for publication at NIPS.
