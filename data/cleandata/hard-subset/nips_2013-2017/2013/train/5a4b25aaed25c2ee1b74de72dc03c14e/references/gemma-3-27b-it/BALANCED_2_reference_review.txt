This paper explores approximating intractable distributions in high-treewidth graphical models by projecting onto a set of “fast-mixing” distributions, specifically focusing on the Ising model. The core idea is compelling: instead of approximating the intractable distribution directly, project it onto a space where sampling is efficient. The authors investigate several divergence measures for this projection – Euclidean distance (via SVD thresholding), KL divergence (both “zero-avoiding” and reversed versions), and a piecewise KL approximation – and provide theoretical results and empirical evaluations.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles a significant problem in probabilistic inference. The connection to existing work on mean-field inference, loopy belief propagation, and tree-reweighted belief propagation is clearly established. The theoretical contributions, particularly Theorem 7 regarding the Euclidean projection, are valuable and well-presented.  The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing the proposed methods against standard baselines (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs sampling) on relevant datasets. The inclusion of both grid and random graph topologies is commendable.  The discussion section provides honest assessment of limitations and potential future work.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, while the exploration of different divergences is a strength, the practical performance of the “zero-avoiding” KL projection is limited by its computational intractability, rendering it largely theoretical.  The piecewise KL approximation doesn’t perform as well as the other methods in the experiments. This suggests that the choice of tractable subgraphs significantly impacts performance, and more investigation into optimal subgraph selection strategies is needed. 

Second, the experimental results, while showing improvements over some baselines, aren’t overwhelmingly convincing. The gains, especially in the grid graphs, are sometimes modest, and the authors acknowledge the need for parameter tuning.  The comparison with Gibbs sampling is nuanced – while projecting yields faster convergence, the ultimate accuracy is still limited by the approximation. A more detailed analysis of the trade-off between computational cost and accuracy would be beneficial. The claim that the methods presented were more accurate than Gibbs sampling with the same computational effort isn’t fully substantiated by the experimental results.  

Third, the justification for using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 (instead of the theoretically tighter 1.0) feels ad-hoc. The explanation that it “gives better approximation” is insufficient; a more rigorous analysis or ablation study demonstrating the sensitivity of the results to this parameter is required. The authors acknowledge the looseness of this bound in the discussion, suggesting future work to refine it.

Finally, the connection to Ergodic Mirror Descent is mentioned, but not fully elaborated. Expanding on this connection, and discussing its implications for the convergence properties of the algorithm, would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to approximating intractable distributions.
*   Solid theoretical contributions (Theorem 7).
*   Thorough experimental evaluation comparing against relevant baselines.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Addresses a significant problem in probabilistic inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Performance gains are sometimes modest, and require careful parameter tuning.
*   Some of the proposed divergences are computationally intractable or underperform empirically.
*   Justification for the spectral norm bound is ad-hoc.
*   Connection to Ergodic Mirror Descent could be expanded upon.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper that presents a novel and potentially valuable approach to approximate inference. While the performance gains aren’t dramatic, the idea of projecting onto fast-mixing distributions is compelling and deserves further exploration.  I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit, addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly strengthening the experimental validation and providing a more rigorous justification for the empirical choices (e.g., the spectral norm bound). Addressing the computational limitations of the KL divergence methods, and investigating more sophisticated subgraph selection strategies for the piecewise KL approximation, would also be beneficial.
