Summary
This paper proposes to approximate intractable Ising models by projecting parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set: interactions whose dependency matrix has spectral norm below a threshold (drawing on Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum and Hayes). The idea is to define “tractable” models as those for which single-site Gibbs mixes rapidly, even if the graph is high treewidth. The authors study four projections: (i) Euclidean projection via a dual formulation that effectively thresholds singular values while preserving sparsity; (ii) KL(θ||ψ) (zero-avoiding) which is largely theoretical due to expectations under θ; (iii) a “piecewise” KL that computes KL on tractable subgraphs; and (iv) KL(ψ||θ) (zero-forcing) minimized via stochastic approximation using samples from ψ, with Euclidean projections enforcing the spectral constraint. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare these methods to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs, evaluating marginal errors against exact junction-tree baselines.

Strengths
- Clear and appealing framing of “fast mixing” as a tractable family that strictly contains fully factorized models; the KL(ψ||θ) approach can be viewed as a principled generalization of mean-field.
- The dependency-bound argument R_ij ≤ tanh|β_ij| makes the spectral constraint easy to check and optimize against (albeit loose).
- The Euclidean projection with sparsity constraints (Theorem 7) is interesting; the use of SVD thresholding and a dual with LBFGS-B is practical.
- The paper engages with multiple divergence choices and provides a stochastic gradient estimator for KL(ψ||θ) that reduces to an empirical Fisher times (ψ−θ), which is sensible.
- Empirical comparisons on standard synthetic setups (grids; small random graphs) against common baselines.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical soundness and clarity: There are apparent errors/typos in key statements. In Theorem 6, S′ii=min(Sii,c2) appears incorrect; standard spectral-norm projection thresholds singular values at c, not c2. In Theorem 7, g(Λ,M) is written with a reciprocal “1/||D−R||^2F,” which is atypical and likely a typo. Notation around Λ′Z and ⊙ is ambiguous. These issues undermine confidence in the derivation.
- Nonconvexity and duality: The projection problem includes D=|B|, which is nonconvex. The paper claims a dual formulation and then maximizes it with LBFGS-B, but does not justify strong duality or provide guarantees of optimality or convergence. This is a major gap given that the projection operator is central to all proposed methods.
- Using a spectral bound looser than theory: The theory requires ||R||2<1 for mixing guarantees, yet experiments enforce a bound of 2.5 “in practice.” This invalidates the claimed guarantee and blurs the contribution: if the key appeal is a certified fast-mixing family, running outside the proven regime weakens both the motivation and rigor. No empirical mixing diagnostics (e.g., integrated autocorrelation time, coupling bounds, Geweke/Raftery–Lewis), nor sensitivity to the bound, are reported.
- Experimental methodology and fairness: 
  - The evaluation explicitly excludes the substantial computational cost of projection (e.g., 30,000 Gibbs iterations used during optimization), while also comparing to Gibbs on the original parameters. A fair comparison should normalize wall-clock or total sampling budget.
  - Parameter choices for the stochastic optimization (pool size, steps, step size) are fixed without convergence diagnostics, and the setup does not follow conditions required by ergodic mirror descent (e.g., increasing mixing steps, diminishing step sizes).
  - Baselines are limited and not tuned; more robust LBP/TRW variants or modern inference schemes are not considered; no block Gibbs or Swendsen–Wang comparisons, even though these are discussed in the background.
- Limited scope and significance: Results are on small problems (8×8 grids; n=10 random graphs). There are no quantitative tables/plots in the text to assess the claimed accuracy improvements. The piecewise KL idea is underdeveloped (graph selection, weighting, smoothing/max-nonsmoothness) and reported to underperform; the inclusive KL is acknowledged as impractical.
- Clarity: Some sections are hard to follow (e.g., the piecewise KL rationale and footnote reference; ambiguity whether marginal estimates for evaluation are exact or sampled). Notation for masks and elementwise operations could be cleaned up.

Questions for the authors
1. Please correct/clarify Theorems 6 and 7 (threshold level, objective form, notation) and provide a complete, rigorous derivation. Under what conditions does your dual achieve the primal optimum, given D=|B|?
2. Do you actually guarantee sparsity preservation in the projected B? Provide proof or a counterexample.
3. Why choose a spectral bound of 2.5? If guarantees are central, please report results strictly under ||R||2<1, and include empirical mixing diagnostics to validate rapid mixing.
4. Provide time-normalized comparisons that include projection cost. How does your approach compare to running more sophisticated MCMC (e.g., block Gibbs, SW) or tempered approaches for the same compute?
5. For KL(ψ||θ), report convergence diagnostics, sensitivity to pool size/steps/step-size, and the effect of using independent samples for μ̂ and ĝ to reduce bias.
6. For piecewise KL, describe subgraph selection, the number of subgraphs, edge coverage, and whether you tried averaging (instead of max) to reduce variance/nonsmoothness.

Relation to prior work
The fast-mixing sufficient conditions (Dobrushin, spectral norms) and the idea of using them are grounded in prior literature. The novelty lies in casting approximation as a projection onto this set and proposing a dual SVD-thresholding-with-mask routine plus SGD for KL(ψ||θ). This is a reasonable but incremental step; similar spectral projections and constrained variational approximations have appeared in related contexts. The paper should better differentiate from existing mean-field/structured mean-field and tree-based approximations by providing stronger empirical advantages and solid guarantees.

Clarity and reproducibility
Writing is generally understandable but marred by critical typos and missing details. Reproducibility suffers due to incomplete algorithmic specifications, lack of mixing diagnostics, and missing quantitative results and runtime accounting.

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting reframing of “tractability” via fast-mixing constraints; potential to broaden variational families.
- Practical algorithmic components (SVD-based projection; Fisher-like gradient estimator).
- Potentially useful in learning scenarios that require fast MCMC inner loops.

Cons for acceptance
- Key theoretical derivations contain errors/ambiguities; nonconvex duality not justified.
- Experiments do not adhere to the stated guarantees (spectral bound), lack fair compute accounting, and are limited in scope.
- Benefits over established baselines are not convincingly demonstrated with rigorous, quantitative evidence.

Overall rating: 4 (Reject)
Confidence: 0.75 (I am confident in the methodological concerns; would reconsider if the theory and experiments are substantially clarified and corrected.)
