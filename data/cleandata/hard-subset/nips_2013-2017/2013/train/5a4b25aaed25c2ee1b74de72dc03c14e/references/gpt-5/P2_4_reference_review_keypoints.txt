- Proposes projecting intractable Ising parameters onto a "fast-mixing" family constrained by the spectral norm of the dependency matrix R to enable tractable Gibbs sampling.  
- Uses bounds Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij| for Ising models and enforces ||R||2 ≤ c to define the fast-mixing set.  
- Considers four projection objectives: Euclidean (with an SVD-thresholding dual that preserves sparsity), KL(θ||ψ), piecewise KL on tractable subgraphs, and KL(ψ||θ) optimized by stochastic approximation.  
- Euclidean projection via Theorems 6–7 is central, but Theorem 6 likely has a typo (singular-value threshold should be min(Sii,c), not c^2).  
- Theorem 7’s dual objective, gradients, sign conventions, and notation (e.g., Z′D/Hadamard) are unclear and need a careful appendix derivation for reproducibility.  
- The projection couples via D = |B|, making the problem nonconvex; more discussion is needed on optimality (global vs. local) and conditions for correctness.  
- The KL(ψ||θ) gradient estimator is biased because the same samples estimate both terms; separate sample pools or coupling arguments are suggested if unbiasedness is required.  
- Theoretical convergence claims (Ergodic Mirror Descent) do not strictly apply to the implemented algorithm, which uses constant step sizes and single Gibbs sweeps.  
- Key theoretical guarantee (rapid mixing for ||R||2 < 1) is undermined by experiments using c = 2.5; this mismatch requires justification or adherence to proven bounds.  
- Experiments on 8×8 grids and small graphs show projected models often outperform variational baselines and finite-time Gibbs on original models in accuracy–time tradeoffs.  
- Experimental fairness is questionable: projection overhead (Gibbs iterations + repeated Euclidean projections) is excluded from reported wall-clock times.  
- Ground-truth and approximation computation procedures are inconsistently described; clarify that junction tree gives ground truth and Gibbs (with specified burn-in/length) computes approximations.  
- More baselines (GBP, tree sampling, Swendsen–Wang, planar decomposition) and ablations (varying c, sample-pool size, steps, step size) are needed to strengthen empirical claims.  
- Paper is generally well written and motivated but has notational and minor typographical issues that hinder precise understanding.  
- Provide pseudocode/algorithm box for KL(ψ||θ) projection, and give complexity discussion for the dual solver to improve reproducibility.  
- The core idea—using fast-mixing parameter sets as an approximation family and sparsity-preserving Euclidean projection—is novel and technically interesting.  
- Potential significance is high if theoretical bounds, projection algorithms, and fair end-to-end evaluations are solidified; current empirical impact is moderate.  
- Main weaknesses: theory–experiment mismatch on mixing guarantee, theorem typos/notation issues, limited discussion of convexity/optimality and convergence, and incomplete time–accuracy fairness.  
- Suggestions: fix theorem typos and derivations, report performance vs. c (including c ≤ 1) and mixing diagnostics, include full wall-clock costs, add stronger baselines, and use separate sample pools or quantify gradient bias.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject due to technical clarity issues and experiments–theory mismatch, but paper could become a solid contribution after revisions.
