Summary and context
This paper proposes a new notion of tractability for approximate inference: instead of restricting to families that permit exact inference (trees, planar, low-treewidth), restrict to parameter sets that guarantee rapid mixing of (single-site) Gibbs sampling. For Ising models, the authors use sufficient conditions based on the dependency matrix R and the bound ||R||2 < 1; with Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|, they enforce a spectral norm constraint on the absolute interaction matrix. Given an intractable θ, they project onto this “fast-mixing” set Ψ using four divergences: (i) Euclidean distance via a dual formulation leading to iterative singular-value thresholding while preserving graph sparsity (Theorem 7); (ii) KL(θ||ψ), of theoretical interest since it requires expectations under θ; (iii) a novel “piecewise” KL defined via exact KL on tractable subgraphs; (iv) KL(ψ||θ), optimized by stochastic projected gradient using a pool of Gibbs samples from ψ (viewed as Ergodic Mirror Descent). Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare the projected models with LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original parameters.

Relation to prior work is clear: mixing-time bounds (Dyer et al., Hayes), variational methods (MF, TRW, GBP), planar/tractable decompositions (Globerson & Jaakkola; Saul & Jordan), and piecewise training. The main conceptual shift is using fast-mixing parameter sets as the tractable family, thus blending MCMC guarantees with variational-style projections.

Strengths
- Novelty and boldness: Treating fast-mixing parameter regions as a tractable family and projecting onto them is a compelling, under-explored idea. The connection to MF via KL(ψ||θ) with a zero-norm R constraint is elegant and unifying.
- Methodological contributions: 
  - A dual approach (Theorem 7) to project under Euclidean distance while enforcing spectral-norm constraints on |β| and structural sparsity; operationalized through SVD thresholding and LBFGS-B.
  - A new “piecewise” KL that uses exact KL on a cover of tractable subgraphs.
  - A practical stochastic projected scheme for KL(ψ||θ) leveraging that ψ is enforced to be fast-mixing.
- Empirical signal: Projected fast-mixing models often yield more accurate marginals than standard variational baselines at comparable chain lengths, and can outperform running Gibbs on the original parameters under fixed-time budgets.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Correctness/clarity issues:
  - Theorem 6 likely contains a typo: for min ||A−B||F s.t. ||B||2 ≤ c, one thresholds singular values at c (not c^2). Please double-check.
  - In Theorem 7, the dual objective notation appears off: g(Λ,M) = 1/||D−R||^2_F + … is unusual; typically a quadratic with a minus half norm arises in such duals. Also clarify operators: what is Λ′Z, Z′D, and the triple-dot notation—define the Hadamard product explicitly.
  - Several notational inconsistencies (e.g., “Section 3.2” not present, use of |β| instead of tanh|β| vs. dependency R) make it difficult to reproduce.
- Guarantees vs practice: Experiments use a spectral-norm bound 2.5 “which can still preserve rapid-mixing,” but this violates the stated sufficient condition ||R||2 < 1. The paper should be careful not to imply guarantees when they are not met; simultaneously, it would be valuable to report empirical mixing diagnostics to show robustness beyond the conservative theory.
- Experimental methodology:
  - Scale and diversity are limited (8×8 grids, n=10 random graphs). The junction-tree baseline for exact marginals is fine here, but broader graphs and interaction regimes would strengthen claims.
  - Compute accounting is uneven: comparisons exclude the (sometimes substantial) cost of projection/optimization, which weakens time-accuracy narratives. A fair wall-clock analysis would be helpful.
  - Hyperparameters for stochastic projected optimization are set without tuning; include sensitivity analyses (step size, pool size, iterations) and report stability.
  - The piecewise KL is an interesting idea but underexplored; performance is weaker—please analyze why (choice of subgraphs? max vs average over T?).
- Tightness of bounds: The spectral-norm sufficient condition is conservative; using |β| instead of tanh|β| makes it more so. Consider projecting under tanh-transformed weights or comparing alternative sufficient criteria (e.g., Dobrushin’s ||R||1 < 1) to trade tightness vs tractability.
- Missing details: How are fields α treated in projection? Do you project only pairwise terms? Does the dual projection ever densify the graph despite the Z constraints? Provide complexity and convergence guarantees for the dual solver (and for the stochastic projected algorithm).

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix typographical and notation issues in Theorems 6–8; include a self-contained, reproducible derivation (or expanded appendix) and define all operators clearly.
- Report empirical mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation time, effective sample size, coupling heuristic) to substantiate “fast-mixing in practice,” especially when using bounds >1.
- Compare different sufficient conditions (spectral norm, l1/l∞ Dobrushin) and also try projecting with tanh|β|.
- Provide compute-normalized comparisons including projection overhead. Add larger graphs and stronger-coupling regimes if feasible.
- Deepen the analysis of the piecewise KL: choice of subgraphs (trees vs low-treewidth junction trees), weighting schemes (sum/average vs max), and ablations.
- Clarify treatment of α and the exact constraint set Ψ. Discuss potential extension to block Gibbs bounds.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a fresh, actionable notion of tractability (fast mixing) for approximate inference and shows how to project onto it.
- Bridges MCMC and variational inference in a principled way; the KL(ψ||θ) projected approach is practically appealing.
- Contains algorithmic ideas (dual projection, piecewise KL) that others can build on.
- Honest discussion of limitations and future directions.

Cons against acceptance
- Technical presentation has errors/ambiguities that impede reproducibility.
- Theoretical guarantees are not matched by experimental settings (using looser bounds), and empirical mixing is not rigorously assessed.
- Experimental evaluation is modest in scale and does not fully account for computational costs; some methods underexplored.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite imperfections, the core idea—projecting to fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractable family—is novel and promising, with clear potential to influence approximate inference practice. With fixes to the stated technical issues and stronger empirical validation, this work could be impactful.

Confidence: moderate.
