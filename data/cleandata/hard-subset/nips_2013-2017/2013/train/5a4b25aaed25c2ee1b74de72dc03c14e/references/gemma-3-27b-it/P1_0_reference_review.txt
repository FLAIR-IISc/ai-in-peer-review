## Peer Review: Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing

**Summary:**

This paper investigates a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically the Ising model. Instead of approximating the distribution itself (e.g., via mean-field), the authors propose *projecting* the parameters of an arbitrary distribution onto a set of parameters that guarantee fast mixing under Gibbs sampling.  They explore several divergence measures for this projection: Euclidean distance (with a closed-form solution via SVD thresholding), the impractical KL(θ||ψ), a piecewise KL-divergence based on low-treewidth subgraphs, and the more promising KL(ψ||θ) minimized via stochastic approximation.  Experimental results on small Ising models demonstrate that projecting onto the fast-mixing set generally improves marginal accuracy compared to standard variational methods like LBP and mean-field, though not exceeding the accuracy achievable with sufficiently long Gibbs sampling runs on the original parameters.

**Related Work:**

The paper appropriately positions itself within the broader landscape of approximate inference techniques for graphical models. It acknowledges the limitations of exact inference in high-treewidth models and references relevant work on mean-field inference, loopy belief propagation, and tree-reweighted belief propagation. The connection to fast-mixing Markov chains, and especially the use of spectral norms as a sufficient condition for rapid mixing, is well-motivated and linked to existing literature (Dyer et al., Hayes).  The discussion of piecewise likelihood and spanning tree approximations is also relevant.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set is a unique approach to approximate inference, moving away from directly approximating the distribution. This provides a fresh perspective on addressing the intractability of high-treewidth models.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The work is grounded in sound theoretical results regarding mixing times and spectral norms. The derivation of the projection algorithm in the Euclidean norm is particularly strong (Theorem 7).
* **Multiple Divergence Measures:**  Exploring several divergence measures for the projection provides a thorough investigation of the trade-offs between accuracy and computational feasibility.  The zero-forcing KL divergence (KL(ψ||θ)) offers a particularly promising avenue for practical application.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments provide a compelling initial evaluation of the proposed methods, demonstrating improvement over standard variational techniques.  The comparison with Gibbs sampling (both on original and projected parameters) provides a useful benchmark.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear and the key concepts are explained effectively.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conservatism of Spectral Bound:** The authors acknowledge the potential conservatism of the spectral norm bound (||R||₂ < 1) for fast mixing. The observed need to use a larger threshold (2.5) suggests that the theoretical guarantee is often too strict, potentially hindering the effectiveness of the projection.  Further investigation into tighter bounds would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost:**  While the Euclidean projection has a closed-form solution, the stochastic approximation for the zero-forcing KL divergence is computationally expensive, requiring numerous Gibbs sampling steps for gradient estimation.  The experiments don't fully quantify the trade-off between accuracy and computational cost in detail.
* **Limited Scalability:**  The experiments are restricted to small Ising models. Demonstrating the scalability of the proposed methods to larger, more complex graphs would be crucial for assessing their practical applicability.
* **Piecewise Divergence Performance:** The piecewise KL-divergence does not perform as well as the KL(ψ||θ) approach, and its benefits are not clearly articulated. Its inclusion feels less compelling than the other divergence measures.
* **Lack of Theoretical Analysis for Piecewise KL:** The theoretical justifications for using the maximum KL divergence over subgraphs is intuitively appealing but could benefit from more rigorous analysis.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to approximate inference with a strong theoretical foundation.
* The experimental results are encouraging, demonstrating improvement over existing methods.
* The exploration of multiple divergence measures highlights the trade-offs and potential of this approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The conservatism of the spectral norm bound may limit the effectiveness of the method.
* The computational cost of the stochastic approximation for the KL(ψ||θ) divergence is a concern.
* The limited scalability of the experiments raises questions about the practical applicability of the proposed methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant and promising contribution to the field of approximate inference. The idea of projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results are encouraging. While the computational cost and scalability remain concerns, the potential benefits of this approach justify its publication.

**Rating:** Strong Accept (with minor revisions).

**Recommendations:**

* **Investigate Tighter Bounds:** Explore alternative, tighter bounds for fast mixing in Ising models to potentially improve the performance of the projection.
* **Computational Cost Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the stochastic approximation for the zero-forcing KL divergence.  Consider exploring methods for reducing the number of Gibbs sampling steps required for gradient estimation.
* **Scalability Experiments:** Conduct experiments on larger and more complex graphs to assess the scalability of the proposed methods.
* **Clarify Piecewise Divergence:**  Either provide a stronger justification for the piecewise KL-divergence or consider removing it from the paper.
