## NIPS Review: Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing

**Summary:**

This paper investigates a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically Ising models. Instead of approximating the distribution directly (like mean-field), the authors propose *projecting* the parameters of an arbitrary distribution onto a set of parameters that guarantee fast mixing under Gibbs sampling. They explore several divergences for this projection – Euclidean distance (solved via SVD thresholding), KL divergence (theoretical interest only), a piecewise KL divergence based on low-treewidth subgraphs, and the reversed KL divergence (KL(ψ||θ)) – which can be minimized via stochastic approximation leveraging Gibbs sampling on the projected distribution. The paper demonstrates empirically that projecting onto the fast-mixing set generally leads to more accurate marginal estimates than standard variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) and, with comparable computational effort, outperforms Gibbs sampling on the original (slow-mixing) parameters.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds on a rich history of approximation techniques for intractable inference in graphical models. It correctly positions its work within the context of mean-field approaches [14, 16, 20], loopy belief propagation [21, 19], and planar graph inference [6]. The connection to fast mixing times and dependency matrices [4, 8, 11] is well-articulated.  The novelty lies in framing the problem as a projection onto a set of *fast-mixing* parameters, rather than directly approximating the distribution itself. The exploration of piecewise KL divergence builds on related work in piecewise likelihood [17] and tree-based approximations [15].

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Perspective:** The idea of projecting parameters to enforce fast mixing is a compelling and potentially powerful approach to approximate inference. It shifts the focus from approximating the distribution directly to ensuring the efficiency of sampling-based methods.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical grounding, connecting the approach to established results on mixing times and dependency matrices. Theorem 7, providing a dual algorithm for Euclidean projection, is a valuable contribution.
* **Comprehensive Exploration of Divergences:**  The exploration of different divergences for the projection, particularly the reversed KL divergence with its stochastic approximation, showcases a thoughtful approach to the problem.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach compared to strong baselines (LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling). The comparisons on both grid and random graphs strengthen the conclusions.
* **Clear Writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, making it relatively easy to follow the complex concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conservatism of Spectral Norm Bound:** The authors acknowledge that the spectral norm bound of 1.0 for fast mixing may be overly conservative, citing results from the 2D Ising model. While using a larger threshold (2.5) improves performance in practice, this highlights a disconnect between the theoretical guarantee and the empirically observed behavior.  Further investigation into tighter bounds would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost of Stochastic Approximation:** While the reversed KL divergence performs well, the stochastic approximation requires a "pool" of samples and repeated Gibbs updates, adding computational overhead. The paper acknowledges tuning is needed, and the fixed parameters used might not be optimal. A more detailed analysis of the computational cost and sensitivity to hyperparameters would strengthen the results.
* **Piecewise KL Divergence Performance:** The piecewise KL divergence doesn’t perform as well as the reversed KL divergence, despite being easier to evaluate.  The authors don't delve deeply into *why* this is the case – is it the choice of subgraphs, the approximation inherent in the piecewise approach, or something else?
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are focused on the Ising model. While this allows for a thorough evaluation, it's unclear how well the proposed approach would generalize to other Markov random fields.
* **Lack of Discussion of Convergence Guarantees for Stochastic Approximation:**  While the paper mentions Ergodic Mirror Descent, a more detailed explanation of the convergence guarantees for the stochastic approximation used to minimize the reversed KL divergence would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to approximate inference.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the complex concepts.
* The work addresses a significant challenge in graphical model inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The spectral norm bound may be overly conservative, limiting the tightness of the theoretical guarantees.
* The computational cost of the stochastic approximation for the reversed KL divergence warrants further investigation.
* The scope of experiments is limited to the Ising model.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of approximate inference.  The idea of projecting parameters to ensure fast mixing is a clever and promising one, and the empirical results are encouraging. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding the conservatism of the spectral norm bound and the computational cost of the reversed KL divergence, these are not fatal.

**Rating:** **Strong Accept**

I recommend this paper for acceptance to NIPS.  I encourage the authors to address the minor weaknesses raised in this review in a future revision. Specifically, further exploration of tighter bounds on the spectral norm, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the stochastic approximation, and experiments on other graphical model types would strengthen the work further.
