- Proposes "fast-mixing" graphical models by constraining parameters so single-site Gibbs mixes rapidly and projecting intractable θ to a constrained ψ via divergence minimization.  
- For Ising models, uses spectral-norm constraint on absolute coupling matrix (via tanh bound) as a sufficient mixing condition.  
- Considers four projection divergences: Euclidean (with dual algorithm/Theorem 7), inclusive KL (impractical), piecewise KL (max over low-treewidth subgraphs), and reverse KL (stochastic optimization using samples from ψ).  
- Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare projections to MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs, reporting marginal L1 error against exact junction-tree.  
- Strength: novel tractability notion based on mixing-time guarantees rather than graph structure, complementing existing variational and exact approaches.  
- Strength: Theorem 7’s dual Euclidean projection handles sparsity and spectral-norm constraints and is implementable with LBFGS-B plus SVD-thresholding.  
- Strength: Reverse-KL method is well-motivated, connects to mean-field as a limiting case, and has a correct gradient via covariance under ψ.  
- Strength: Piecewise-KL is simple, tractable, and related to tree-based approximations and piecewise training.  
- Honest discussion of trade-offs and limitations in the paper.  
- Major concern: Theoretical guarantee requires ||R||2 < 1, but experiments use a looser bound (2.5) without justification or mixing diagnostics, undermining the main claim.  
- Needs empirical mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, spectral gap, coupling times, or contraction bounds) to substantiate fast-mixing under the relaxed bound.  
- Dual Euclidean projection optimization is non-smooth/non-convex due to SVD thresholding; paper lacks clarity on convexity, uniqueness, and optimality certificates.  
- Masking/sparsity issue: projection may densify couplings, no bounds provided on sparsity deviation or sign distortion and potential loss of computational benefits.  
- Piecewise-KL is ad hoc (max over subgraphs), non-smooth, and lacks theoretical relation/bounds to true KL; alternatives like sums/convex combinations should be considered.  
- Experimental protocol unclear about marginal computation and decoupling of sampling from projection; wall-clock and total compute comparisons are missing.  
- Scalability concern: SVD-based projection may not scale beyond a few hundred nodes; experiments limited to small graphs.  
- Related work: Dobrushin and other contraction criteria (path coupling, block samplers) and stronger comparisons to TRW’s provable bounds are missing.  
- Core derivations are technically sound (dependency matrix bound, reverse-KL gradient), but the Euclidean dual needs clearer conditions and derivation.  
- Clarity: generally well written but has notational inconsistencies, typos, and unclear experimental descriptions.  
- Originality: projection into a fast-mixing set and the Euclidean dual appear novel; reverse-KL generalizes MF; piecewise-KL is new though related to prior tree methods.  
- Significance: approach could be practical if strengthened, but current experiments do not enforce guarantees and scalability is unclear.  
- Suggestions: enforce ||R||2 < 1 or provide mixing diagnostics, analyze optimization landscape and KKT/duality gaps, report wall-clock times and scale experiments, and compare max vs average piecewise-KL and tie to TRW.  
- Acceptance pros: fresh viewpoint, nontrivial projection machinery, promising empirical accuracy vs common variational methods.  
- Acceptance cons: guarantees not honored in experiments, limited scale, incomplete compute accounting, and lack of theoretical bounds for piecewise-KL and dual convergence.  
- Overall score: 6 (weak accept if strengthened; currently borderline).
