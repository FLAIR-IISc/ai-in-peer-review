Summary
The paper proposes using “fast-mixing” parameter sets as a tractable family for approximate inference in Ising models. The key sufficient condition is that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix R (bounded in Ising by |β| entrywise) is less than 1, implying rapid mixing of single-site Gibbs with a mixing-time bound. Given intractable parameters θ, the authors “project” onto the fast-mixing set under four divergences: (i) Euclidean l2 distance (with a dual algorithm relying on spectral-norm projection); (ii) the inclusive KL(θ||ψ) (theoretical only); (iii) a “piecewise” KL built from tractable subgraphs; and (iv) the reverse KL(ψ||θ) optimized via projected stochastic approximation using samples from ψ. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare projected models (Euclidean, piecewise, reverse-KL) against MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs on the original parameters.

Strengths
- Conceptually interesting notion of tractability via guaranteed fast mixing; this bridges variational and MCMC viewpoints and could be useful in practice.
- The reverse-KL projected stochastic approximation is a plausible and scalable method (sampling from ψ is practical if ψ is fast mixing).
- Using the spectral-norm bound on influence is sound in spirit and connects to known Dobrushin-type conditions; the covariance-form gradient for KL(ψ||θ) is correct.
- Empirical evaluation on grids and random graphs is appropriate; exact marginals on 8×8 grids are feasible and provide meaningful accuracy baselines.

Major weaknesses and technical concerns
- Theorem 6 appears incorrect: projecting onto the operator-norm ball requires capping singular values at c, not at c^2. The statement “S′ii = min(Sii, c 2)” should be S′ii = min(Sii, c). This propagates to the dual construction.
- Theorem 7 is difficult to trust as written. The objective g(Λ, M) uses g = 1/||D−R||^2_F + Λ · Z · D (Eq. 2): the “1/” is likely a typo; sign and 1/2 factors typical of Fenchel duals are unclear; notation for Hadamard products vs. triple-dot is inconsistent (Λ′Z vs. Λ · Z · D). The derivation is relegated to the appendix (not provided), so correctness cannot be verified.
- Definition 1 (total variation) is missing the standard factor 1/2. This factor impacts subsequent constants in mixing-time statements.
- The mixing-time bound (Theorem 4) is quoted for random updates, but experiments use systematic scan; while related results exist, the paper should precisely state applicable conditions and constants for the chosen schedule.
- Lemma 5 is cited to Hayes for zero-field uniform β and claimed to generalize “by simple modifications,” but no proof is given. The bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| for general fields/couplings should be justified or referenced properly (e.g., via standard influence bounds).
- The estimator discussion in Sec. 5.4 is flawed. The gradient of KL(ψ||θ) is Covψ[f] (ψ−θ). The proposed estimator ĝ in Eq. (8) uses a sample mean µ̂ from the same samples. The text claims “if two estimators are unbiased and independent, the product is unbiased, thus using separate sets of perfect samples would make ĝ unbiased.” This is incorrect: the target is an expectation of a product minus the product of expectations (a covariance), and using independent samples for the two terms yields zero in expectation, not the covariance. In contrast, using the same sample set yields a slightly biased but asymptotically unbiased estimator with bias factor (K−1)/K; this should be stated accurately.
- The paper repeatedly violates or weakens its own guarantees in experiments by using a spectral-norm bound of 2.5 “which still preserves the rapid-mixing property.” This is not theoretically justified and undermines the main premise of guaranteed rapid mixing. No mixing diagnostics or τ(ε) estimates are reported.

Clarity and presentation issues
- Numerous notation and formatting inconsistencies: Z′D vs. Z · D; undefined “′” operator; triple-dot product defined but not consistently used; ambiguous “!” symbol for ε; typos (“divergnce,” “systematical,” etc.).
- Section references are inconsistent (e.g., “Section 3.2” doesn’t exist in the provided text).
- In Sec. 6, it is stated that marginals are “compared … by running a Gibbs chain on each,” then “with the marginals computed via the exact junction-tree algorithm.” Later, “a single chain … and marginals are estimated from the samples.” These statements conflict; please clarify which is used for evaluation and for what purpose (ground truth vs. approximation).
- The choice of D(θ,ψ) = max_T KL(θ(T)||ψ(T)) introduces non-smoothness; the paper should discuss subgradients, tie-breaking, and why max (vs. sum/average) is chosen.

Experimental concerns
- No runtime, number of SVDs/LBFGS iterations, or computational effort comparisons are reported; yet some methods (reverse-KL projection) incur significant overhead. The text acknowledges this but then omits fair-time analyses.
- Hyperparameters for LBP/TRW/MF (e.g., damping, iterations) are not reported; convergence criteria are unspecified.
- No uncertainty measures (std/quantiles) across 50 trials; no ablations (sample pool size, steps).
- Mixing diagnostics for Gibbs chains (original and projected) are absent; given the core claim, this is critical.

Relation to prior work
- The work relates to Dobrushin-type conditions and matrix-norm influence bounds (Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum). The paper could better situate its spectral-norm criterion among alternative sufficient conditions (e.g., 1-norm, maximum influence, tree uniqueness thresholds) and discuss tightness (it briefly does for grids).
- Connections to structured mean-field/generalized BP are noted but could be sharpened (e.g., covariance-preconditioned updates vs. mirror descent).

Questions for the authors
1. Please correct Theorem 6 and re-derive Theorem 7 with a self-contained proof and unambiguous notation. What guarantees hold for sparsity preservation and convergence?
2. Can you provide a correct unbiased or controlled-bias gradient estimator for KL(ψ||θ), and quantify the bias/variance under your sampling scheme?
3. Why choose max_T in the piecewise KL? How sensitive are results to this choice?
4. Can you provide mixing diagnostics and τ(ε) estimates (or at least coupling-based proxies) to support the “fast mixing” claim in experiments, especially when the spectral norm bound exceeds 1?
5. Please clarify the evaluation protocol: which marginals are exact, which are estimated, and how ground truth is obtained on grids and random graphs?

Typos/minor
- TV distance missing 1/2; Eq. (2) likely has a sign/1/2/cap issue; many small typos; inconsistent symbols for ε and Hadamard products; missing years/venues/pages in references.

Recommendation: Reject
Pros: Interesting and potentially useful idea; plausible algorithmic direction; relevant experiments. Cons: Multiple technical inaccuracies (projection theorem, dual objective), incorrect estimator reasoning, inconsistencies between guarantees and experimental settings, unclear notation/derivations, and insufficiently rigorous/diagnosed experiments. With substantial corrections and clearer theory/experiments, this could be a strong contribution.
