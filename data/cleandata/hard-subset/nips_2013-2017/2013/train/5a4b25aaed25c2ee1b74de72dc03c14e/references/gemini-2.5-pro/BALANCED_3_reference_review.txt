**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models. Traditional methods, such as mean-field or loopy belief propagation, approximate an intractable high-treewidth distribution with a structurally simpler one (e.g., fully-factorized or tree-structured) where exact inference is feasible. In contrast, this work defines a "tractable" model not by its structure, but by its MCMC mixing properties. Specifically, it leverages a known result that an Ising model is guaranteed to have a rapidly mixing Gibbs sampler if the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths is less than one.

The core contribution is to formulate the approximation problem as a projection: given an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing, set of Ising model parameters, the goal is to find the "closest" set of parameters within the fast-mixing set. The paper explores this projection under four different divergences:
1.  **Euclidean Distance**: A novel dual algorithm is derived (Theorem 7) to solve this projection efficiently, capable of handling sparsity constraints.
2.  **"Zero-avoiding" KL(q||p)**: Considered for theoretical completeness, but acknowledged as intractable for slow-mixing target distributions `q`.
3.  **Piecewise KL Divergence**: A novel, tractable approximation to the KL divergence, computed over a collection of low-treewidth subgraphs.
4.  **"Zero-forcing" KL(p||q)**: This divergence is optimized via projected stochastic gradient descent. The gradient requires expectations with respect to the fast-mixing distribution `p`, which can be estimated via Gibbs sampling. The Euclidean projection algorithm is used as a subroutine in each optimization step.

Experimentally, the authors compare their methods against standard variational inference techniques (LBP, TRW, MF) and Gibbs sampling on the original model. On grids and random graphs, the proposed projection methods are shown to produce more accurate marginals than the variational baselines and can outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model in a fixed time budget.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance**: The central idea of redefining "tractability" for approximate inference in terms of MCMC mixing time rather than structural complexity is highly original and significant. It opens up a new and promising direction for research. While variational inference typically restricts the functional form of the approximating distribution, this work restricts its parameters, allowing the preservation of the original high-treewidth graph structure. This is a fundamental conceptual contribution that could inspire a new class of inference algorithms.

2.  **Technical Quality**: The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the dual algorithm for the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a non-trivial contribution that elegantly handles the absolute value within the spectral norm constraint and preserves sparsity. The application of projected stochastic gradient descent to minimize the KL(ψ||θ) divergence is a principled and well-motivated approach to handle the more complex, probabilistically-grounded objective.

3.  **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. It begins with a helpful background on mixing time conditions, builds the core algorithmic idea from a simple Euclidean projection to more sophisticated divergences, and presents a clear experimental validation. The relationship to and distinction from prior work is well-articulated.

4.  **Empirical Soundness**: The experimental evaluation is thorough. The proposed methods are benchmarked against a strong set of established baselines on multiple graph structures and parameter regimes. The results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed approach can outperform standard variational methods and offers a better time-accuracy trade-off than running Gibbs on the original slow-mixing model.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice in the Bound**: The paper's theoretical motivation relies on the `|||β|||_2 < 1` condition for guaranteed rapid mixing. However, the experiments use a spectral norm bound of 2.5, stating it "gives better approximation". This is a significant weakness, creating a disconnect between the theoretical guarantees of the method and its practical implementation.
    *   **Suggestion**: The authors should address this gap more directly. How was the value 2.5 chosen? Is it tuned per problem class? While the theoretical bound is known to be loose, using an ad-hoc larger value undermines the "guarantee" of fast mixing that is central to the paper's premise. The authors could strengthen the paper by providing empirical evidence (e.g., mixing diagnostic plots) that sampling is indeed still fast with the relaxed bound in their experimental settings. A more principled way of selecting this hyperparameter would be a valuable addition.

2.  **Scalability of the Projection Step**: The Euclidean projection algorithm relies on a Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the `n x n` interaction matrix, which has a time complexity of O(n³). This could be a computational bottleneck for very large models (e.g., `n > 1000`). The paper focuses on the accuracy of the resulting model but does not discuss the computational cost or scalability of the projection itself.
    *   **Suggestion**: A brief discussion on the computational complexity and potential scalability limitations would make the paper more complete. Mentioning potential alternatives for large-scale problems, such as iterative methods for computing the top singular values (e.g., Lanczos algorithm), could point towards valuable future work.

3.  **Limited Scope (Ising Models)**: The entire technical development is specific to pairwise Ising models, where a simple bound on the dependency matrix is available (Lemma 5). The paper mentions extending to general MRFs as future work but does not elaborate on the significant challenges involved.
    *   **Suggestion**: While keeping the focus on Ising models is perfectly acceptable, a short paragraph in the discussion outlining the specific hurdles for generalization (e.g., deriving tractable dependency bounds for models with larger cliques or non-binary variables) would provide valuable context and better frame the contribution.

4.  **Clarity on Stochastic Optimization**: The description of the algorithm for minimizing KL(ψ||θ) is somewhat brief.
    *   **Suggestion**: To improve reproducibility, it would be helpful to provide more details on the stochastic approximation scheme. For instance, briefly discussing why using the same set of samples to estimate both `μ̂` and the expectation in Eq. 8 is a reasonable (though biased) choice in practice would be insightful. Clarifying the number of Gibbs steps applied per gradient update would also be useful.

**Summary of Pros and Cons**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and significant conceptual framing for approximate inference based on MCMC mixing times.
*   Provides a solid, non-trivial technical contribution in the form of the Euclidean projection algorithm.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance, outperforming widely used variational methods.
*   The paper is well-written and the ideas are clearly communicated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The practical implementation uses a heuristic spectral norm bound (2.5) that is disconnected from the theoretical guarantee (`< 1`), which is a core part of the motivation.
*   Scalability of the core SVD-based projection algorithm to very large graphs is a potential concern that is not addressed.
*   The contribution of the novel "Piecewise KL" divergence seems minor, as it did not perform particularly well.

**Decision**

This is a strong paper with a genuinely new idea. The conceptual shift from structural tractability to MCMC tractability is a valuable contribution to the NeurIPS community. The technical execution is sound and the empirical results are compelling. While the weakness regarding the looseness of the mixing time bound is notable, the authors are transparent about it, and it does not invalidate the core contribution. This issue is more of a limitation of existing mixing time theory than a flaw in the authors' approach. Overall, the strengths significantly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
