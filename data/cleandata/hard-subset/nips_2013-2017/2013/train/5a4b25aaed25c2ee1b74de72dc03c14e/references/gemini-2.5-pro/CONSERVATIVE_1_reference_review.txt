### Summary

This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models. Instead of using traditional tractable families like low-treewidth or fully factorized distributions, the authors define "tractability" as the set of models for which Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to mix rapidly. They leverage a known sufficient condition for fast mixing in Ising models, namely that the spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix is less than one. The core technical contribution is a set of methods for "projecting" an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing Ising model onto this fast-mixing set. The paper explores four different divergences for this projection: Euclidean distance, the standard KL divergence KL(q||p), a novel piecewise KL approximation, and the reverse KL divergence KL(p||q). For the Euclidean projection, a dual algorithm based on iterative SVD thresholding is derived. For the reverse KL divergence, a stochastic projected gradient descent method is employed. Experiments on grid and random graphs compare these projection methods against standard baselines like Loopy Belief Propagation, TRW, and naive Mean-Field, suggesting the proposed approach yields more accurate marginal estimates.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality of the Core Idea:** The central concept of using a fast-mixing parameter set as the approximating family is novel and insightful. It reframes the problem of approximate inference from a structural perspective (low treewidth) to a dynamic one (fast convergence of sampling), which is a creative and potentially impactful contribution.
2.  **Technical Derivations:** The derivation of the dual algorithm for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a solid technical contribution. The formulation of the reverse KL-divergence minimization as a stochastic projected gradient descent problem is also well-conceived and provides a practical algorithm.
3.  **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper does a good job of situating its approach relative to the extensive literature on variational inference and MCMC mixing times. The observation that minimizing KL(p||q) with a spectral norm constraint of zero recovers mean-field is a nice insight that helps connect this new framework to established methods.

### Weaknesses

The paper presents an interesting new direction, but the work in its current form suffers from several significant weaknesses that undermine its claims of rigor and practical utility.

1.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice:** The entire motivation for the work rests on projecting parameters into a set that *guarantees* fast mixing. The theoretical condition used is that the spectral norm of the absolute weight matrix, `|| |β| ||_2`, is less than 1. However, in the experiments (Section 6.1), the authors state that they use a spectral norm bound of 2.5 because it "gives better approximation". This ad-hoc choice completely severs the connection to the theoretical guarantees that motivate the paper. The method is no longer guaranteed to be fast-mixing, and it becomes a heuristic procedure whose properties are not well understood. The discussion section acknowledges the looseness of the bound but does not justify this significant deviation in the experimental protocol. This is a major flaw in methodological soundness.

2.  **Scalability Concerns:** The proposed projection algorithms rely on computing a Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the `n x n` interaction matrix. This has a computational complexity of O(n³). This cost is prohibitive for the very large-scale graphical models where approximate inference is most critical. For the stochastic gradient method, this O(n³) projection must be performed at every iteration, making the overall procedure extremely expensive. The paper fails to address this critical limitation, and the experiments on small 64-node grids do not demonstrate the method's feasibility for larger, more realistic problems.

3.  **Unfair Experimental Comparison:** The experiments compare the accuracy of marginals obtained by running Gibbs sampling for a fixed number of iterations. However, the paper explicitly states, "this does not take into account the computational effort deployed during projection." This is a critical omission. The projection phase, especially for the stochastic KL method, can be extremely computationally intensive (many iterations, each involving an SVD). A fair comparison would measure accuracy against total wall-clock time, including both the projection and subsequent sampling. Without this, the claim that the proposed method "generally gives better results" in finite time compared to Gibbs on the original parameters is unsubstantiated.

4.  **Limited Scope and Generalizability:** The technical development is restricted entirely to Ising models with pairwise interactions. While this is a common starting point, the extension to general discrete MRFs is presented as a significant future challenge. The dependency matrix bound in Lemma 5 is specific to the binary case. This substantially limits the immediate applicability of the work compared to more general methods like belief propagation or mean-field.

### Recommendation: Reject

While the core idea of using fast-mixing sets for approximate inference is creative and thought-provoking, the paper's execution and evaluation fall short of the standards expected for NIPS. The abandonment of the theoretical guarantees in the experimental section for a heuristic choice of the spectral norm bound is a primary concern. Furthermore, the unaddressed issues of scalability (due to the O(n³) SVD step) and the unfairness of the experimental time comparisons make the practical utility of the proposed methods questionable. The work feels preliminary and requires a more rigorous and realistic evaluation, including a justification for the parameter choices and a scalable projection algorithm, before it can be considered a significant contribution.
