This paper explores approximating intractable distributions with “fast-mixing” models, specifically within the context of the Ising model. The core idea – projecting parameters onto a set guaranteeing rapid Gibbs sampling convergence – is interesting and potentially useful. The authors investigate several divergence measures for this projection, including Euclidean distance, KL-divergence (in various approximations), and a “zero-forcing” KL-divergence. While the concept is promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, ranging from imprecise terminology and incomplete justification of choices to inconsistencies in notation and experimental setup. These, cumulatively, significantly detract from the paper's overall quality.

The introduction provides adequate motivation but lacks sufficient context regarding existing approximation techniques. The comparison to mean-field, loopy belief propagation, etc., feels cursory and doesn’t fully establish the novelty of the proposed approach. The notion of “tractable” being redefined as “fast-mixing” is a reasonable framing, but its implications need to be more clearly articulated.

The background section on Markov chain Monte Carlo is standard, but the presentation of total variation distance and the dependency matrix feels somewhat disjointed. Definition 1 uses "X" where "x" would be more appropriate for a random variable. The explanation of the dependency matrix (Definition 2) could benefit from a concrete example to illustrate its meaning. Furthermore, while Theorem 4 is stated correctly, the connection to the central problem is not immediately obvious, requiring the reader to mentally reconstruct the link between spectral norm and mixing time.

Section 3, detailing mixing time bounds, correctly presents the relevant theory. However, the notation is inconsistent: sometimes 'β' is used for interaction strength, while other times 'βij.' This ambiguity is particularly problematic in Lemma 5, where it’s unclear if 'βij' refers to a single interaction or the entire matrix. The statement that "simple modifications" to Hayes’ proof yield the result in Lemma 5 lacks detail and justification. 

The projection section (Section 4) introduces Theorem 6 and 7. Theorem 7’s derivation is not fully explained; the jump from Eq. (1) to the objective function in Eq. (2) lacks sufficient clarity. The mention of LBFGS-B without specifying the constraints imposed on M and Λ is a minor oversight. The use of the "triple dot product" notation is unnecessarily verbose and doesn't contribute to clarity.

Section 5, concerning divergences, is the most problematic. The rationale for choosing the specific divergences is not always convincing. The claim that the “zero-avoiding” KL divergence is only of “theoretical interest” is a harsh dismissal without substantial justification. The piecewise KL divergence is presented as an attempt to balance accuracy and computational convenience, but the experimental results (Section 6) suggest it's significantly less accurate than other methods. The discussion of the "zero-forcing" divergence and its connection to mean-field is insightful, but the caveat about potential non-convexity is buried within the text. The connection to Ergodic Mirror Descent is mentioned but not elaborated.

The experimental section (Section 6) is inadequately described. The selection of graph topologies and parameter ranges feels arbitrary and lacks a clear justification. The justification for using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of the theoretically tighter value of 1.0 is weak and relies on empirical observation. The lack of detailed ablation studies regarding the optimization parameters for the zero-forcing KL divergence is a significant omission. Comparing to TRW and LBP is fine, but it would have been preferable to also compare against a standard variational method such as structured mean-field.

The discussion section (Section 7) acknowledges the looseness of the spectral norm bound but offers no concrete suggestions for improvement. The conclusion is generic and doesn't highlight the key contributions or limitations of the work.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   Numerous minor flaws throughout the paper (notation, terminology, justification).
*   Incomplete explanation of key derivations (Theorem 7).
*   Insufficient experimental detail and justification of choices.
*   Weak motivation for certain divergence measures.
*   Lack of comparison to relevant baselines.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The central idea of projecting onto fast-mixing distributions is novel.
*   The investigation of multiple divergence measures is commendable.
*   The theoretical framework is generally sound.

**Overall:**

Despite the novelty of the central idea, the numerous flaws in presentation, justification, and experimental detail outweigh the paper's strengths. The paper feels unfinished and lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS submission. I recommend **rejection**. Significant revisions addressing the issues outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration. Specifically, the paper needs a thorough proofreading for consistency, clearer explanations of key derivations, a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, and a stronger justification for the chosen methods and parameters.
