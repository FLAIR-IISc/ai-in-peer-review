This paper proposes a novel approach to accelerating Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling for Ising models by projecting parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set. The core idea – trading off parameter fidelity for guaranteed mixing properties – is interesting and potentially impactful. However, the paper suffers from a significant number of technical shortcomings, inconsistencies, and a lack of thorough experimental evaluation that, despite the novelty of the approach, lead me to recommend rejection.

The introduction establishes the motivation well, framing the work within the broader context of approximate inference and highlighting the limitations of existing methods. The background section on mixing times is adequate, though somewhat cursory. However, the presentation quickly deteriorates in subsequent sections. 

The formulation of the projection problem in Section 4 is marred by several issues. The claim that Theorem 6 directly translates to the Ising model parameter space is misleading. Theorem 6 constrains the spectral norm of the *projected matrix* itself, whereas the paper correctly identifies that it's the spectral norm of the *absolute value of interactions* (R matrix) that dictates mixing. The introduced "dual method" appears unnecessarily complex and its connection to a practically implementable algorithm is unclear. The LBFGS-B optimization mentioned lacks any details about the constraints applied beyond a vague “bound constraints”.

Section 5 introduces multiple divergence measures, each with its own set of problems. The discussion of the KL-divergence (Section 5.2) correctly identifies the computational difficulty, but the leap to the piecewise KL-divergence (Section 5.3) feels ad hoc. The choice of subgraphs (horizontal/vertical chains for grids, random spanning trees) is not justified and likely impacts performance significantly. The “zero-forcing” KL-divergence (Section 5.4), while theoretically appealing, relies on accurate gradient estimation via Gibbs sampling, which introduces bias and requires careful tuning.  The assertion that estimating the gradient using the same samples twice "makes little difference" is unsubstantiated and questionable.

The experimental section (Section 6) is severely lacking. The descriptions of the configurations are brief, and the choice of parameters (e.g., dn=1.0, de values) feels arbitrary. The comparison with LBP, TRW, and naive mean-field is valuable, but the justification for selecting the spectral norm bound of 2.5 (instead of the theoretically derived 1.0) is weak. The claim that more aggressive parameter tuning during optimization can lead to better accuracy but hinders interpretability is a concerning admission.  Furthermore, the reported results offer little quantitative detail; a simple statement that "the proposed methods are more accurate" is insufficient. A detailed table of results showing marginal error across different configurations and methods is essential. The phrase "roughly, for β < βc, mixing is known to occur quickly" is imprecise and should be replaced with specific references and quantitative bounds. The discussion of the discrepancy between the spectral norm bound and the known critical value for the 2D Ising model is important but briefly brushed aside.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel idea of projecting onto a fast-mixing parameter space.
*   Potential for accelerating MCMC sampling.
*   Exploration of multiple divergence measures.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Numerous technical inconsistencies and misleading claims.
*   Lack of thorough theoretical justification for the proposed algorithms.
*   Insufficient experimental detail and quantitative results.
*   Ad hoc choices in the piecewise divergence approach.
*   Weak justification for parameter settings and the relaxed spectral norm bound.
*   Unsubstantiated claims regarding the impact of gradient estimation bias.
*   Poorly written and organized in places.



In conclusion, while the core idea of this paper is intriguing, the numerous flaws in its technical execution, the lack of rigorous experimental evaluation, and the overall sloppiness of the presentation preclude its acceptance at NIPS. The paper requires substantial revision and a much more thorough validation before it can be considered a meaningful contribution to the field. A more focused and rigorous analysis of a single divergence measure, coupled with comprehensive experimental results, would be a significant improvement.
