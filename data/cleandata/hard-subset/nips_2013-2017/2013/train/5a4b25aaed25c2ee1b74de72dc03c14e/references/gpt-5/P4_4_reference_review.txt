Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes using “fast-mixing” parameter sets as a tractable family for approximate inference in Ising models: instead of enforcing low treewidth for exact inference, it enforces parameter-space conditions that guarantee rapid convergence of univariate Gibbs sampling. For Ising models with binary variables, a sufficient condition is that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix is < 1; using the bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|, a conservative sufficient condition is to constrain the spectral norm of |β|. The paper studies projecting an arbitrary parameter vector θ to a fast-mixing parameter vector ψ under four discrepancies: (i) Euclidean distance via a dual formulation that yields an iterative singular-value thresholding respecting sparsity (Theorem 7), (ii) inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ) (theoretically appealing but impractical when θ is slow-mixing), (iii) a novel “piecewise” KL formed by computing KL on low-treewidth subgraphs and taking the maximum across subgraphs, and (iv) reverse KL KL(ψ||θ), which allows stochastic approximation using samples from ψ because ψ is constrained to be fast-mixing. The optimization for non-Euclidean divergences uses projected (stochastic) gradient descent with Euclidean projection onto the fast-mixing set.

This connects to classic variational families (mean-field, structured mean-field, LBP/TRW), but proposes a new tractability notion. The work leverages mixing-time bounds from Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum and Hayes, and relates to piecewise likelihoods and mixtures-of-trees approximations. The stochastic optimization perspective references Ergodic Mirror Descent. Empirically, on 8×8 grids and small random graphs, the proposed projections yield more accurate marginals than MF/LBP/TRW, and for finite-time budgets can outperform Gibbs on the original parameters because of guaranteed rapid mixing on ψ.

Quality
- Theory: The use of ||R||2 < 1 as a sufficient condition for rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs is standard and correctly referenced. The bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is standard; replacing tanh|β| by |β| yields a conservative sufficient condition. The Euclidean projection onto a spectral-norm ball via singular-value thresholding is correct; the dual formulation (Theorem 7) appears to address sparsity and the absolute-value coupling, but some details are terse and there is at least one apparent typographical inconsistency in the dual objective (missing constant and formatting in Eq. 2). Proofs are in an appendix (not provided here), so full verification is not possible from the main text alone.
- Algorithms: Projected (stochastic) gradient descent is a reasonable approach. The reverse-KL gradient estimator is derived and a practical estimator is proposed; unbiasedness is discussed under idealized independent sampling.
- Empirics: The setups are standard and controlled. However, comparisons do not normalize for total compute: the cost of projection (including substantial Gibbs sampling within optimization) is excluded when comparing to “Gibbs on original parameters,” which biases time-accuracy comparisons. The experiments are limited to small graphs; there is no direct measurement of mixing times, spectral norms of the resulting dependency matrices, or how often the fast-mixing guarantee actually holds in practice.

Clarity
Exposition is generally clear with useful preliminaries. Noted issues:
- Typos/notation: The dual objective (Eq. 2) appears malformed; constants in the objective are unclear. There are minor section cross-reference inconsistencies (e.g., referencing Section 3.2). Some notation (e.g., Z and its role) could be clarified further in the main text.
- Practical choices (e.g., spectral bound c = 2.5 > 1.0) are stated but not justified or analyzed.

Originality
The notion of “fast-mixing as a tractable family” and projecting parameters onto this set appears novel. The dual Euclidean projection respecting sparsity is technically interesting. The “piecewise KL” divergence is a fresh approximation idea. The reverse-KL projection with stochastic approximation generalizes MF to a larger family.

Significance
The approach offers a new axis of approximation that may be attractive whenever Gibbs sampling is the desired inference engine. If robustly validated and extended, this could influence approximate inference and learning (e.g., constraining parameters during MCMC-based learning). Currently, significance is limited by conservative bounds, limited empirical scope, and lack of runtime-normalized comparisons.

Strengths
- Clear, principled definition of a fast-mixing tractable family with theoretical guarantees.
- Technically interesting Euclidean projection method that preserves sparsity while enforcing a spectral norm constraint.
- Stochastic optimization for reverse KL leverages fast mixing for sampling, generalizing mean-field.
- Empirical indications of improved marginal accuracy over MF/LBP/TRW on tested instances.

Weaknesses
- Practical deviation from guarantees: using spectral norm bound c = 2.5 > 1.0 undermines the stated mixing guarantee; no analysis of when this remains safe.
- Dual derivation details and constants are not fully transparent in the main paper; Eq. 2 likely contains a typo.
- Limited empirical scope (small graphs) and incomplete compute-normalized comparisons; projection costs are omitted in time-accuracy plots.
- No direct measurement of mixing times, spectral gaps, or acceptance-rejection statistics to validate “fast mixing” empirically.
- Piecewise KL underperforms; rationale and design choices (max vs sum, choice of subgraphs) are not fully explored.
- Convergence guarantees for the practical stochastic schedule (constant step size, one Gibbs sweep) are not provided.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a complete, self-contained derivation of the dual, check constants, and correct typos; clarify constraints and the role of Z, M, and Λ.
- Enforce the theoretical bound based on Rij ≤ tanh|βij| (e.g., constrain ||tanh(|B|)||2 < 1) or justify/characterize safe regimes for looser c via empirical mixing diagnostics (autocorrelations, coupling times, or spectral norm of R).
- Include runtime-normalized comparisons that account for projection cost; report wall-clock and iteration counts.
- Measure and report actual mixing behavior (e.g., effective sample size per second, convergence diagnostics) for both original and projected models.
- Expand experiments to larger graphs and stronger couplings; add ablations on c, pool size, Gibbs updates, and subgraph selection for piecewise KL.
- Explore alternative sufficient conditions (e.g., Dobrushin l1 bound) and block-Gibbs variants to enlarge the tractable family.

Arguments for acceptance
- Introduces a novel and principled tractable family with a concrete projection framework.
- Provides new algorithms (dual Euclidean projection; stochastic reverse-KL projection) with promising empirical accuracy gains over standard variational baselines.
- Potentially impactful idea that bridges MCMC mixing theory and variational approximations.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees are not adhered to in practice (c > 1), and empirical fast mixing is not validated.
- Dual method lacks fully clear presentation in the main text; some equations appear incorrect.
- Experimental scope and compute-normalized comparisons are insufficient for a NeurIPS-level claim.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The core idea is compelling and methodologically interesting, but the paper would benefit from corrected and clarified derivations, adherence to or principled relaxation of the mixing guarantees, and stronger, compute-normalized empirical validation.
