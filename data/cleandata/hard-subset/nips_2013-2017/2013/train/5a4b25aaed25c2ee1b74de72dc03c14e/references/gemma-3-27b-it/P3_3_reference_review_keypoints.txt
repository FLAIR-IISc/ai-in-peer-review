- Novel approach: project arbitrary Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing parameter set to enable efficient Gibbs sampling rather than approximating the distribution directly.  
- Divergence methods: evaluates Euclidean (SVD thresholding), forward KL (theoretical), piecewise KL on low-treewidth subgraphs, and reversed KL minimized via stochastic approximation.  
- Theoretical contribution: connects to dependency-matrix mixing results and provides a dual algorithm (Theorem 7) for Euclidean projection.  
- Empirical results: projected fast-mixing parameters yield more accurate marginals than LBP, TRW, MF and outperform Gibbs on original slow-mixing parameters.  
- Experiments cover grid and random graphs, strengthening empirical claims within Ising models.  
- Clear writing and organization makes complex concepts accessible.  
- Spectral norm bound conservatism: the theoretical 1.0 bound is likely too conservative; practical performance improves with larger thresholds (e.g., 2.5).  
- Computational cost: stochastic approximation for reversed KL needs sample pools and repeated Gibbs updates, adding overhead and hyperparameter sensitivity.  
- Piecewise KL underperformance: piecewise KL is easier to evaluate but performs worse than reversed KL; reasons (subgraph choice vs approximation) are not analyzed.  
- Limited generality: experiments are restricted to Ising models, leaving generalization to other MRFs unclear.  
- Convergence guarantees: insufficient discussion/detail on convergence guarantees for the stochastic approximation (Ergodic Mirror Descent referenced but not fully explained).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded, strong empirical results, and clear presentation addressing a significant inference challenge.  
- Arguments against acceptance: conservative spectral bound, computational overhead of stochastic approximation, and limited experimental scope.  
- Overall assessment: reviewer recommends strong accept, urging future work on tighter spectral bounds, deeper computational analysis, and experiments on other graphical models.
