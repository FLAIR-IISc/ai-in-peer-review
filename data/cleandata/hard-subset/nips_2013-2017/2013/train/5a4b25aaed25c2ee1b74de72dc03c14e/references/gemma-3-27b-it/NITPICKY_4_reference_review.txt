This paper explores approximating intractable distributions with “fast mixing” models, specifically focusing on the Ising model. The core idea – projecting parameters onto a set guaranteeing rapid Gibbs sampling convergence – is interesting and potentially useful, particularly given the challenges in scaling traditional variational inference methods. The authors investigate several divergence measures for this projection, including Euclidean distance, KL-divergence (both tractable and approximated forms), and a “zero-forcing” KL-divergence amenable to stochastic approximation.

However, despite a promising premise, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, both in technical details, experimental evaluation, and presentation, leading to a significantly weakened impact. While the concepts are intriguing, the execution lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS submission.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of using fast-mixing as a criterion for approximating distributions is a fresh perspective.
*   **Multiple Divergence Exploration:** Investigating various divergences offers a comprehensive look at the projection problem.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The connection to spectral norms and Gibbs sampling theory provides a sound theoretical basis.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, although limited, demonstrate the potential of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Technical Imprecision:** Lemma 5 states R<sub>ij</sub> ≤ tanh|β<sub>ij</sub>| ≤ |β<sub>ij</sub>|. This is correct, but the claim that Hayes [8] *proves* this for the general case is misleading. Hayes proves it for constant β and zero-field. A simple modification is mentioned, but not detailed – it needs elaboration or citation of a more general result.
*   **Theorem 7’s Implementation:** The algorithm derived from Theorem 7 appears overly complex. The use of LBFGS-B with bound constraints feels ad-hoc, and the computational cost is not discussed. The statement about the dual method "fixing" the issues of density and graph structure is overstated; the projected matrix B will still likely be dense.
*   **Piecewise KL Divergence Issues:** The selection of subgraphs T for the piecewise KL divergence is not sufficiently justified. Using only horizontal and vertical chains for the grid graph seems arbitrary and potentially suboptimal. Furthermore, the results of this divergence are not competitive with the other methods.
*   **Zero-Forcing KL Divergence:** The claim that minimizing this divergence is "related to" naive mean-field is vague. The connection requires a much clearer explanation. The explanation of the stochastic gradient estimation process is insufficient; a clearer description of the dependence between samples is required.
*   **Experimental Design Concerns:** The choice of a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of the theoretically derived 1.0 is presented as an empirical observation *after* the experiments, with limited justification. The authors admit it’s looser and improves approximation, suggesting the theoretical basis is flawed or incomplete. The comparison against other inference algorithms lacks detail - the hyperparameter settings and implementation choices aren’t sufficiently described. The description of the graph configurations is not completely clear (e.g., “mixed interactions” requires further specification).
*   **Writing and Clarity:** The paper suffers from repetitive phrasing and occasionally convoluted sentences. The flow between sections feels disjointed. Section 6.1’s use of "dn" and "de" is confusing without prior definition within the section.
*   **Missing Citations:** The discussion of related work feels incomplete. For example, the connection to structured mean-field inference (mentioned in the introduction) isn’t further explored. The use of Gibbs sampling as an approximation within the zero-forcing KL divergence deserves a more thorough comparison to existing MCMC-based variational methods.



**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea of fast-mixing parameter projection is novel and could stimulate further research.
*   The paper tackles a significant problem in graphical model inference.
*   The theoretical foundation provides a solid starting point.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The technical details are often imprecise or lacking in justification.
*   The experimental evaluation is insufficient and lacks a thorough comparison to relevant baselines.
*   The writing quality is subpar, hindering clarity and understanding.
*   The looseness of the spectral norm bound undermines the theoretical grounding.
*   The overall presentation feels incomplete and rushed.




**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting idea, the numerous flaws in technical details, experimental design, and presentation significantly detract from its quality. The paper feels like a preliminary exploration of a promising idea rather than a polished, complete research contribution. I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors should address the technical imprecisions, provide a more rigorous experimental evaluation, and significantly improve the clarity of the writing before resubmission. A deeper investigation of the theoretical underpinnings and a more convincing justification for the empirically derived parameters are also essential.
