This paper introduces a novel and compelling framework for approximate inference in graphical models, specifically the Ising model. The central idea is to shift the notion of a "tractable" approximating family. Instead of relying on models with low treewidth (e.g., trees or fully factorized distributions, as in mean-field), the authors propose using the family of "fast-mixing" distributions—models whose parameters guarantee rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling to the stationary distribution. For the Ising model, they leverage a known sufficient condition for fast mixing: the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths, `|||β|||_2`, must be less than 1. The paper's main contribution is to develop methods for "projecting" an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing, Ising model onto this fast-mixing set.

The authors explore four different notions of "closeness" or divergence for this projection:
1.  **Euclidean Distance:** They derive a dual algorithm based on iterative SVD thresholding (Theorem 7) to find the closest fast-mixing parameter set in the L2 norm, while preserving the graph's sparsity structure.
2.  **"Zero-avoiding" KL(θ||ψ):** This is the standard KL divergence used in variational inference. However, it is intractable for this problem as it requires computing expectations over the original slow-mixing distribution `θ`.
3.  **Piecewise KL:** A novel and tractable approximation to the KL divergence, computed over a set of low-treewidth subgraphs of the original model.
4.  **"Zero-forcing" KL(ψ||θ):** This reversed KL divergence requires expectations over the approximating fast-mixing distribution `ψ`. The authors propose a practical stochastic projected gradient descent algorithm to minimize this, where gradients are estimated via Gibbs sampling from `ψ`, and each step involves a Euclidean projection. This method is elegantly framed as a generalization of mean-field inference.

Experimentally, the proposed methods (particularly the KL(ψ||θ) projection) are shown to outperform standard variational methods like Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted BP (TRW), and Naive Mean Field (MF) in estimating marginals on grid and random graphs. They also demonstrate that for a fixed computational budget, sampling from the projected fast-mixing model can yield more accurate marginals than sampling from the original slow-mixing model.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core concept of using fast-mixing parameter sets as the approximating family is highly original and significant. It opens up a new avenue for approximate inference that is conceptually different from the dominant structure-based approaches. This is a valuable contribution to the field that could inspire follow-up work on other models and MCMC algorithms.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The paper is technically solid. The identification of the appropriate fast-mixing condition from the literature is correct, and the development of the Euclidean projection algorithm (Theorem 7) is a non-trivial technical result. Building upon this to create a stochastic optimization procedure for the KL(ψ||θ) divergence is a well-conceived and practical approach. The connection drawn to mean-field (as the special case where the spectral norm bound is zero) is insightful.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, explains the necessary background on mixing times, details the proposed algorithms, and presents a convincing experimental evaluation. The progression from the simple Euclidean case to the more complex KL divergences is logical and easy to follow.

4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The method consistently outperforms widely-used variational baselines and offers a clear advantage over direct Gibbs sampling on the original model when computation time is limited.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Looseness of the Theoretical Bound:** The primary weakness, which the authors commendably acknowledge in the discussion, is the conservative nature of the theoretical fast-mixing condition (`|||β|||_2 < 1`). In the experiments, a heuristic bound of 2.5 is used because it yields better results. While this is a reasonable practical choice, it weakens the "guarantee" of fast mixing that is central to the paper's motivation. The paper would be strengthened by a more direct empirical investigation of this gap. For instance, a plot showing the trade-off between the chosen spectral norm bound, the final marginal accuracy, and the empirical mixing time of the resulting chain would provide valuable insight.

2.  **Scalability of the Projection Step:** The Euclidean projection algorithm relies on computing a Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the `n x n` interaction matrix. This has a computational complexity of O(n^3), which could be prohibitive for very large graphs (e.g., n > 1000). In contrast, methods like LBP have a complexity that is linear in the number of edges. A discussion of the computational complexity and potential scalability limitations of the proposed projection method should be included in the main text.

3.  **Evaluation of the Piecewise KL Divergence:** The paper introduces the "piecewise KL" as a novel idea but states in the introduction that "Experimentally, this does not perform as well as the true KL-divergence" and omits it from the main experimental results. This is a missed opportunity. Including the results for this method, even if they are not the best, would complete the evaluation of the paper's contributions and allow readers to better assess the utility of this new divergence.

4.  **Clarity on Optimization Details:** The stochastic gradient descent procedure for minimizing KL(ψ||θ) is described as an instance of Ergodic Mirror Descent, but the implementation uses a constant step size and a fixed number of MCMC updates per iteration. While this is a pragmatic choice, the link to the theoretical convergence guarantees of the cited work could be made more explicit, or the authors could simply frame it as a standard stochastic projected gradient method without invoking the more specific framework if the theoretical conditions are not met.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and significant conceptual shift in approximate inference, defining tractability by fast mixing rather than graph structure.
*   Develops a technically sound and practical new algorithm for inference in Ising models.
*   Provides strong empirical evidence that the proposed method outperforms several standard and widely-used baselines.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and honest about its own limitations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The practical implementation deviates from the theory by using a heuristic spectral norm bound, softening the central "fast-mixing guarantee".
*   The scalability of the core projection step (SVD) to very large graphs is a potential limitation that is not discussed.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is an excellent paper that introduces a creative and promising new direction for research in approximate inference. The strengths, particularly the originality and significance of the core idea and the strong empirical results, far outweigh the weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance. The work is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community and to inspire future research exploring this new class of approximations.
