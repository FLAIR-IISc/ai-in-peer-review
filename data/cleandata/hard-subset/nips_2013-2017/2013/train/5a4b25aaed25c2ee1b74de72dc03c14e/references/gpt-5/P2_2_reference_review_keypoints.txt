- Proposes "fast-mixing" parameter sets: constrain parameters so single-site Gibbs provably mixes rapidly; for Ising a sufficient condition is ||R||2 < 1 with R bounded by tanh|βij|.
- Studies projecting arbitrary parameters θ into the fast-mixing set Ψ under multiple divergences: Euclidean, KL(θ||ψ), a novel piecewise KL, and reverse KL KL(ψ||θ).
- Gives a dual algorithm (Theorem 7) for Euclidean projection enforcing a spectral-norm constraint and preserving sparsity via bounds on |B|.
- Derives reverse-KL gradient as Covψ[f](ψ−θ) and minimizes it by stochastic projected gradient using Gibbs samples from ψ.
- Experiments on 8×8 grids and 10-node random graphs show projected fast-mixing approximations give more accurate marginals than standard variational methods and can beat Gibbs on original model for fixed sampling budgets.
- Authors acknowledge looseness of the spectral bound and practically use a larger threshold (c=2.5) than the theoretical c=1.
- Situates work relative to mixing-time theory and distinguishes it from mean-field/TRW by defining tractability via dynamical mixing conditions.
- Notes the Euclidean SVD thresholding is standard; Theorem 7's dual construction enforcing |B| and sparsity is novel.
- Piecewise KL idea relates to piecewise likelihood/tree mixtures but used here as a divergence proxy for projection.
- Theoretical claims (mixing-time condition, dependency bound, reverse-KL gradient) are sound where stated; projected SGD method is appropriate.
- Theorem 7 is practical, offering a concrete optimization recipe (e.g., LBFGS-B with bounds).
- Experimental setup covers multiple graph families, interaction regimes, and baselines.
- Main guarantee is weakened in practice by using c=2.5; no diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation, spectral gap) are provided to confirm fast mixing in experiments.
- Evaluation clarity issues: unclear what serves as ground truth, inconsistent statements about whether marginals are computed by Gibbs or exact junction-tree, and whether estimators are consistent across methods.
- Computational budget fairness is unclear: projection (especially reverse-KL) is expensive and its cost is not accounted for in accuracy comparisons; no wall-clock or matched-budget comparisons.
- Scalability and complexity are not analyzed; dual projection still needs repeated SVDs and constrained solves, and impact on post-projection density/sparsity is not quantified.
- Piecewise KL underperforms and is under-analyzed (choice of subgraphs, aggregation method sensitivity not explored).
- Minor technical issues: probable typo in Theorem 6 (S′ii bound), wrong section cross-reference, and small wording/spelling items.
- Clarity is generally good, but the paper should sketch Theorem 7 intuition, clarify update scheme (random vs systematic scan), treatment of fields α, and how mask Z and |B| constraints interact with coupling signs.
- Originality: novel idea to use fast-mixing parameter sets and nontrivial dual projection enforcing |B| and sparsity; reverse-KL projected SGD generalizes mean-field.
- Significance: new axis for approximate inference useful for MCMC-focused practitioners, but practical impact depends on tighter mixing conditions and clearer, fairer empirical evidence.
- Pros for acceptance: novel perspective and algorithms, solid theoretical framing, correct gradients, and empirical improvements over standard variational methods.
- Cons for acceptance: mixing guarantee relaxed without diagnostics, ambiguous evaluation protocol, unclear compute fairness, and limited analysis of scalability and failure modes.
- Requests for authors: report effective sample sizes/autocorrelations for c=2.5; clarify evaluation pipeline and ground truth; provide computational complexity and runtime comparisons; analyze sensitivity to spectral bound c and optimization hyperparameters; explain any bias introduced by |B| constraint on coupling signs.
- Overall recommendation: borderline/weak accept — promising and principled but needs stronger empirical validation of mixing guarantees and clearer, fairer evaluation.
