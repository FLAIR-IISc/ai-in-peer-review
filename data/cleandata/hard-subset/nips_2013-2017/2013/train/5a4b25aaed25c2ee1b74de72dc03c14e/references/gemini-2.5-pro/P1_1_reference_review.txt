### **Summary**

This paper proposes a novel approach for approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically Ising models. Instead of approximating an intractable model with one that has a tractable structure (e.g., a tree or fully-factorized graph), the authors propose approximating it with a model that is "fast-mixing," meaning Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to converge quickly. The condition for fast mixing is derived from the literature and relies on the spectral norm of the interaction matrix being less than a constant.

The core contribution is to frame this approximation as a projection problem: given the parameters of a slow-mixing model, find the closest set of parameters in the fast-mixing set. The paper explores this projection under four different divergences:
1.  **Euclidean distance:** The authors derive a novel dual optimization algorithm (Theorem 7) to perform this projection efficiently while preserving the original graph's sparsity.
2.  **KL(q||p) (zero-avoiding):** This is presented as a theoretical ideal, but is intractable as it requires inference on the original slow-mixing distribution q.
3.  **Piecewise KL:** A new, tractable approximation to the KL divergence, computed over low-treewidth subgraphs of the model.
4.  **KL(p||q) (zero-forcing):** This is tractable because expectations are taken with respect to the fast-mixing distribution p. The authors present a stochastic gradient descent method to minimize this divergence, where gradients are estimated via MCMC sampling and the projection step uses the Euclidean algorithm. This approach is insightfully framed as a generalization of mean-field inference.

Experiments on grid and random graphs show that the proposed methods, particularly the zero-forcing KL projection, outperform standard variational methods like LBP, TRW, and Mean-Field in terms of marginal accuracy. Furthermore, they demonstrate that for a fixed computational budget, running Gibbs sampling on the projected (fast-mixing) parameters can yield more accurate marginals than running it on the original (slow-mixing) parameters.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Idea:** The central idea of defining a tractable family of models based on fast-mixing dynamics rather than graph structure is highly original and thought-provoking. It opens up a new avenue for approximate inference that is conceptually different from the dominant structure-based approaches (mean-field, structured-mean-field, LBP). This is a significant contribution to the field.

2.  **Solid Technical Contributions:** The paper provides concrete algorithms to realize its core idea. The derivation of the dual algorithm for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) that handles both the spectral norm constraint and sparsity is non-trivial and technically sound. The formulation of the KL(p||q) minimization as a stochastic projected gradient descent is elegant and practical.

3.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is thorough. The proposed methods are compared against strong and relevant baselines on standard graph topologies. The results clearly demonstrate the practical utility of the approach, showing superior accuracy over established variational methods. The inclusion of a time-vs-accuracy comparison against standard Gibbs sampling is particularly compelling, as it directly addresses the practical trade-off that motivates the work.

4.  **Clarity and Honesty:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The authors clearly explain the motivation, background, and their proposed methods. Crucially, they are transparent about the limitations of their work, such as the known looseness of the spectral norm bound for fast mixing (Section 7), which strengthens the credibility of the paper.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The most significant weakness is the discrepancy between the theoretical condition for fast mixing (`|| |B| ||_2 < 1`) and the bound used in experiments that yields the best results (`c=2.5`). While the authors acknowledge this, the paper would be stronger with more investigation into this gap. For instance, an empirical study of the mixing times of the projected models (e.g., using standard MCMC convergence diagnostics) for different values of `c` could help justify the choice of `c=2.5` and provide insight into how conservative the theoretical bound really is in these settings.

2.  **Scalability of the Projection:** The Euclidean projection algorithm relies on an SVD. While the optimization is clever, the scalability of this core step to very large graphs (e.g., n > 1000s) is a concern. A discussion of the computational complexity of the projection step (Theorem 7) and its practical limitations would be a valuable addition.

3.  **Details for Reproducibility:** The description of the stochastic optimization for the KL(p||q) projection is somewhat brief. The authors state they use a fixed step size and sample pool size that "performed reasonably well." Providing more detail on the sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters would improve the paper's reproducibility and practical value for readers wishing to implement the method.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   In Section 6.1, the paper states that the total computational effort for the KL(ψ||θ) projection can be around 30,000 Gibbs iterations. This is a substantial upfront cost. A plot showing "total time" (projection time + sampling time) vs. accuracy would be very informative to better understand the practical trade-off compared to simply running a long Gibbs chain on the original parameters.
*   The performance of the proposed "piecewise KL" divergence is mentioned in the introduction as not performing as well as the true KL divergence, but its performance relative to the other proposed methods and baselines is not explicitly detailed in the experiments. Clarifying its standing would be helpful.
*   The extension to general MRFs is mentioned as future work. Could the authors briefly comment on the main obstacles? Is the primary challenge in deriving a similarly simple bound on the dependency matrix for non-binary variables, or in the projection algorithm itself?

### **Overall Evaluation**

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and significant contribution to the field of approximate inference.

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims are supported by proofs (one deferred to the appendix) and the empirical claims are well-supported by a solid experimental setup.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The motivation is well-established and the connection to prior work is well-articulated.
*   **Originality:** The core idea of using fast-mixing as the criterion for a tractable approximation family is, to my knowledge, novel and represents a significant departure from standard approaches.
*   **Significance:** The results are important. The proposed method provides a new, effective tool for approximate inference that outperforms widely used variational methods. The conceptual shift it introduces is likely to inspire follow-up research.

Despite the identified weaknesses, primarily the theory-practice gap regarding the spectral norm bound, the strengths of the paper are overwhelming. The novelty of the core idea, the quality of the technical execution, and the convincing empirical results make this a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
