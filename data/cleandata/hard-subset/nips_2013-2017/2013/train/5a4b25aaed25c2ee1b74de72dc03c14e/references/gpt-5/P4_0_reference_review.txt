Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “fast-mixing” models as a tractable family for approximate inference: instead of requiring low treewidth, the parameters are constrained so that single-site Gibbs sampling mixes rapidly. For Ising models x ∈ {−1,+1}, a sufficient condition is that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix R be less than 1; using the bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij| (building on Dyer et al. [4] and Hayes [8]), this reduces to a constraint on the spectral norm of the absolute pairwise interaction matrix. Given an intractable parameter vector θ, the paper “projects” to ψ in the fast-mixing set Ψ by minimizing various divergences D(θ,ψ).

Four projections are explored:
- Euclidean projection: minimize ||θ−ψ||2 subject to ||R(ψ)||2 ≤ c and graph sparsity. A dual method (Theorem 7) is derived that uses repeated SVD singular-value thresholding (Theorem 6) combined with masking to preserve sparsity.
- Inclusive KL: KL(θ||ψ), “zero avoiding,” but needs expectations under θ and is thus impractical beyond low-treewidth settings.
- Piecewise KL: approximate KL via exact KL on tractable subgraphs T (e.g., trees or low-treewidth subgraphs), akin to piecewise training [17] and spanning-tree approximations [15], selecting ψ by minimizing maxT KL(θ(T)||ψ(T)).
- Reverse KL: KL(ψ||θ), “zero forcing,” which permits stochastic approximation using samples from ψ because ψ is forced to be fast-mixing; this is positioned as a generalization of mean-field (MF corresponds to constraining R to zero).

Experiments on 8×8 grids and random graphs with varying edge densities compare Euclidean projection, piecewise KL, and reverse KL against LBP, TRW [19], MF [20], and Gibbs on the original model. Reverse KL uses an Ergodic Mirror Descent-style scheme [3] with a sample pool and periodic Euclidean projection. Marginal accuracy is reported by comparing to exact junction-tree marginals.

Quality
- Theory: The use of spectral-norm-based sufficient conditions for fast mixing in single-site Gibbs is grounded in [4, 8]. Lemma 5 (Rij ≤ tanh|βij|) is standard. The SVD-thresholding result (Theorem 6) is standard. However:
  - The constraint ||R||2 < 1 is sufficient; experiments use a looser bound (c=2.5) that exceeds the theoretical guarantee. The paper observes it “can still preserve” mixing empirically, but then the guarantee no longer applies. A quantitative diagnostic of mixing times or spectral gaps under c=2.5 would help, or at least empirical trace plots/autocorrelation to support the claim.
  - The optimization in Eq. (1) has a nonconvex coupling via D=|B|. The paper states a dual (Theorem 7) and solves it with L-BFGS-B, but does not discuss convexity/duality gap, global optimality conditions, or convergence guarantees. Clarity on whether the dual is tight and under what conditions would strengthen the theoretical soundness.
  - The expression for g(Λ,M) in Eq. (2) includes 1/||D−R||^2F, which seems dimensionally odd and nonstandard; likely a typographical error (perhaps a negative squared norm is intended). Clarifying the correct dual objective and its derivation is important.
  - The gradient for reverse KL (Theorem 8) is plausible, and the proposed estimator (Eq. 8) is reasonable if independent samples are used to estimate μ(ψ) and the outer expectation. Using the same samples introduces bias; this is acknowledged, but a brief variance/bias discussion or control variates would be useful.
- Empirics: The setup is standard and compares against relevant baselines (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs). However:
  - The description of how marginals are computed is ambiguous (“running a Gibbs chain on each” vs “marginals computed via exact junction tree”). Please clarify whether Gibbs is used for estimating ψ’s marginals, only for optimization of ψ, or both, and whether the reported marginal errors are against exact JT marginals for the target model.
  - Time-accuracy comparisons exclude the projection cost, which can be substantial (e.g., repeated SVDs and Gibbs steps during reverse-KL optimization). For a fair comparison, include wall-clock or iteration-normalized curves that account for the projection overhead.
  - No quantitative results are presented here (numbers/plots); it is therefore hard to assess effect sizes, robustness, or statistical significance. Reporting means/variances and mixing diagnostics would help.

Clarity
The paper’s motivation and high-level flow are clear. The algorithmic sections would benefit from:
- A precise, correct statement of the dual objective in Theorem 7 with derivation details and conditions for equivalence to Eq. (1).
- Notational consistency: early “zero-avoiding” is KL(q||p), later defined as KL(θ||ψ); ensure consistent symbols. Section references (e.g., “Section 3.2”) and a footnote referenced in Section 5.3 appear mismatched/missing.
- Experimental protocol clarifications as noted above.

Originality
- The central idea—approximating an intractable model by projecting into a parameter set with provable fast-mixing for Gibbs—is novel compared to classic low-treewidth approximations (MF, TRW, GBP [21], planar [6], piecewise [17]). The dual Euclidean projection with sparsity preservation is a technical contribution, as is using reverse KL with a fast-mixing constraint viewed as a relaxation of MF.
- The piecewise KL leverages known ideas but applies them in a new way to define a projection objective over subgraphs.

Significance
- If robust, this provides a principled alternative to structure-based tractable families, with a clear MCMC-based inference path and potential learning applications (e.g., constraining parameters during MLE to ensure fast-mixing gradients).
- Practical impact depends on: (i) tightness of the mixing-time condition (the spectral-norm bound is conservative, acknowledged by the authors), (ii) scalability of the projection (SVDs and dual optimization on large graphs), and (iii) empirical gains vs. strong baselines when accounting for total compute. Current evidence is promising but incomplete.

Pros
- Clear, interesting problem formulation; connects mixing-time theory to approximate inference.
- Novel projection framework; dual approach to preserve sparsity.
- Reverse-KL fast-mixing approximation generalizes MF in a principled way.
- Broad comparisons to standard methods; discussion of time-accuracy trade-offs.

Cons
- Theoretical guarantees are undermined in practice by relaxing the spectral-norm bound to 2.5; no empirical mixing diagnostics to compensate.
- Dual optimization lacks convexity/duality-gap analysis; Eq. (2) likely contains a typo.
- Experimental methodology needs clarification; projection overhead not accounted for; no quantitative results provided here.
- Piecewise KL design choices (selection of T) and their effect on accuracy/computation are underexplored.

Recommendations for authors
- Provide a corrected, detailed dual derivation with conditions for optimality and a discussion of convexity.
- Either enforce ||R||2 < 1 in experiments or provide mixing diagnostics supporting the relaxed bound (e.g., autocorrelation, spectral gap estimates, coupling times).
- Clarify marginal computation and include end-to-end time-accuracy plots including projection.
- Explore tighter influence bounds (e.g., using tanh|βij| rather than |βij| throughout) and alternative sufficient conditions, and assess impact on projection quality.
- Report scalability (matrix sizes, sparsity, SVD costs) and sensitivity to hyperparameters in stochastic optimization.

Overall assessment
A conceptually novel and potentially impactful direction with a sound motivation and a mix of theoretical and algorithmic ideas. However, theoretical clarity (dual objective/guarantees), adherence to or justification for relaxed mixing constraints, and more rigorous empirical evaluation are needed to support acceptance.
