### Summary

This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in graphical models, specifically Ising models. The central idea is to redefine the notion of a "tractable" model. Instead of relying on structural properties like low treewidth, the authors define tractability in terms of MCMC efficiency, specifically, whether Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to mix rapidly. They leverage a known condition for fast mixing in Ising models: the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths must be less than one.

The main contribution is a set of methods to "project" the parameters of an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing Ising model onto this set of "fast-mixing" parameters. The paper explores four different notions of closeness for this projection:
1.  **Euclidean Distance:** A dual algorithm is derived to perform this projection efficiently while preserving the graph's sparsity structure.
2.  **"Zero-avoiding" KL-divergence (KL(q||p)):** This is the most principled probabilistic measure but is intractable to compute for the target distribution q.
3.  **Piecewise KL-divergence:** A novel and tractable approximation to the KL-divergence, computed over a collection of low-treewidth subgraphs of the original model.
4.  **"Zero-forcing" KL-divergence (KL(p||q)):** This divergence can be optimized via stochastic gradient descent, as expectations are taken with respect to the fast-mixing distribution p, for which samples can be generated efficiently. This approach is elegantly framed as a generalization of mean-field inference, where the approximating family is expanded from fully-factorized distributions to the much richer set of fast-mixing distributions.

Experiments on grid and random graphs show that sampling from the projected fast-mixing models provides more accurate marginal estimates than standard variational methods like LBP and Mean-Field, and can outperform direct Gibbs sampling on the original model within a finite time budget.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a solid contribution, and the stochastic approximation framework for the reversed KL-divergence is well-reasoned. The authors are commendably honest about the limitations, particularly the looseness of the theoretical spectral norm bound, which they directly address in the discussion and experiments. This self-awareness strengthens the paper. The work is a complete piece, presenting a new idea, the theory to realize it, and an empirical validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins by clearly situating the proposed idea within the broader context of approximate inference, making the motivation immediately apparent. The technical sections are easy to follow, and the conceptual connections—for example, to mean-field—are insightful and clearly explained.

**Originality:** The originality of this work is its greatest strength. The core concept of defining tractability via MCMC mixing time and projecting parameters into this space is a fundamental and bold departure from the dominant paradigm of structural approximation in variational inference. While the mixing time bounds themselves are not new, their use as constraints for an optimization-based approximation scheme is, to my knowledge, entirely novel. The piecewise KL-divergence is also a creative and clever idea. This work has the potential to open up a new and fruitful subfield of research.

**Significance:** The significance is very high. This paper doesn't just offer an incremental improvement on an existing method; it proposes a new way of thinking about approximate inference. The idea of trading asymptotic accuracy for guaranteed rapid convergence of MCMC is a powerful and practical one. Researchers are likely to build on these ideas by seeking tighter mixing-time bounds, extending the framework to more general MRFs, and exploring applications in learning where MCMC-based gradient estimates are required. The insight that this generalizes mean-field is particularly profound and connects this new direction to a classic and well-understood technique.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Contribution:** The central idea of projecting onto a "fast-mixing" set is a paradigm shift for approximate inference. It is precisely the kind of bold, high-impact idea that NIPS should champion.
2.  **Strong Potential for Impact:** This work could inspire a significant new line of research focused on MCMC-based tractable families, tighter mixing bounds as constraints, and applications in probabilistic learning.
3.  **Solid Technical Execution:** The paper provides concrete algorithms and a sound theoretical basis (e.g., Theorem 7) for its main ideas.
4.  **Insightful Framing:** The connection drawn between this method and the generalization of mean-field provides a valuable conceptual anchor and highlights the significance of the contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Looseness of the Theoretical Bound:** The primary weakness, acknowledged by the authors, is that the spectral norm condition for fast mixing is quite conservative. The empirical finding that a bound of 2.5 works better than the theoretically-justified 1.0 turns the bound into a hyperparameter, weakening the "guarantee" of fast mixing. However, this points to an interesting direction for future work rather than a fatal flaw.
2.  **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small models. The scalability and performance on larger, real-world problems are not fully established. While the results are promising, a more extensive validation would have been beneficial.

### Conclusion

This paper is an outstanding example of creative and foundational research. It challenges the conventional definition of a "tractable" family in approximate inference and proposes a compelling new alternative based on the dynamics of MCMC sampling. While the practical implementation relies on theoretical bounds that are known to be loose, and the empirical validation is not exhaustive, these are minor weaknesses compared to the sheer novelty and potential impact of the core idea. The work is clear, technically sound, and thought-provoking. It is a perfect fit for NIPS, as it is likely to stimulate discussion and inspire significant follow-up research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
