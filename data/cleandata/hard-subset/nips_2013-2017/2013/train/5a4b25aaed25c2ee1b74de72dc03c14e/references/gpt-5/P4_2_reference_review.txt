Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes using “fast-mixing” parameter sets as a tractable family for approximate inference in Ising models. The key idea is to take arbitrary (possibly slow-mixing) parameters θ and project them to ψ within a set Ψ that guarantees rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs sampling via a sufficient spectral-norm condition on the dependency matrix: for Ising models, a sufficient condition is ||R||2 < 1 with Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|. The authors study four projection objectives: (1) Euclidean distance in parameter space, for which they derive a dual algorithm that preserves graph sparsity while constraining the spectral norm of |B| (Theorem 7), leveraging singular value thresholding (Theorem 6); (2) inclusive KL(θ||ψ), which is theoretically appealing but impractical on high-treewidth models due to expectations under θ; (3) a novel piecewise KL defined over tractable subgraphs T by KL(θ(T)||ψ(T)), computable via exact inference on low-treewidth subgraphs; and (4) reverse KL(ψ||θ) optimized by stochastic approximation using Gibbs samples from ψ, with a gradient estimator (Eq. 8). The approach is positioned as a generalization of mean-field (MF corresponds to zero spectral norm), conceptually related to variational methods (MF, TRW, LBP) and to decompositions into tractable substructures (piecewise training, tree mixtures, planar decompositions).

Experiments evaluate on 8×8 grids and random graphs with n=10 and various edge probabilities and coupling strengths, comparing Euclidean projection, piecewise KL, and reverse KL to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original model. The main empirical claim is that fast-mixing projections produce more accurate marginals than these variational baselines, and can be preferable to Gibbs on the original model at finite computation budgets, though the theoretical mixing guarantee (||R||2<1) is relaxed in practice (a bound of 2.5 is used).

Quality
- Technical soundness: The use of dependency matrix bounds and ||R||2<1 as a sufficient condition is standard, and the inequality Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is appropriate. The Euclidean projection via singular value thresholding is classical; the dual formulation that enforces sparsity on |B| while constraining ||·||2 is technically interesting. The reverse-KL stochastic approximation is plausible, and the connection to Ergodic Mirror Descent is reasonable.
- Caveats: (a) Theorems/formulas contain apparent typos: in Theorem 6, S′ii should be min(Sii, c), not “c 2”; in Theorem 7, g(Λ,M) includes 1/||D−R||^2F, which seems dimensionally inconsistent and likely intended to be a negative quadratic term (e.g., −(1/2)||D−R||^2F). (b) The theoretical guarantee requires random-scan Gibbs and ||R||2<1; experiments use systematic scan and a relaxed bound of 2.5, so guarantees do not strictly apply. (c) The gradient estimator for KL(ψ||θ) uses the same samples for both terms and single-step MCMC updates with a fixed step size; the stated convergence claims (via EMD) typically require increasing MCMC steps and diminishing step sizes. (d) The projection step may require repeated SVDs; computational complexity and convergence of the dual LBFGS-B routine are not analyzed, and scalability beyond n≈64 is not assessed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and structured. The background on mixing, dependency matrices, and the relation to Dobrushin is clear. However:
- Fix the noted typos in Theorems 6 and 7 and verify all dual expressions and gradients.
- Clarify whether the accuracy metric uses exact marginals (junction tree) or estimates from Gibbs; Section 6 contains conflicting statements.
- Specify update schemes (random vs systematic) where mixing bounds are invoked, and cite the exact bound for systematic scan if used.
- Provide algorithmic details: complexity per projection step, number of SVDs per iteration, handling of the absolute-value constraint D=|B|, and how sparsity is enforced numerically.

Originality
Using the fast-mixing region as a tractable family and projecting onto it appears novel and conceptually appealing, distinct from tree-based or planar decompositions. The dual projection that preserves sparsity under a spectral-norm constraint on |B| adds a nontrivial technical contribution. The piecewise-KL objective is related to piecewise training and tree-mixture approximations but applied here for parameter projection; its novelty is moderate. The reverse-KL projection with fast-mixing sampling generalizes MF in a principled way.

Significance
If made robust and scalable, the approach could offer a practical alternative when exact inference is intractable and standard variational methods are inaccurate. However, current experiments are limited in scale (8×8 grids, n=10 random graphs) and use a relaxed spectral threshold; no wall-clock/runtime comparisons, scaling studies, or ablations are provided. The potential impact is promising but not yet demonstrated convincingly.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated new notion of tractable family (fast-mixing) with principled sufficient conditions.
- Technically interesting projection method that preserves sparsity under spectral-norm constraints.
- Multiple divergences explored; reverse-KL algorithm is practical and connects to MF.
- Empirical improvements over common variational baselines on tested setups.

Cons for acceptance
- Gaps between theory and practice: relaxed threshold (2.5) violates the stated guarantee; systematic vs random scan mismatch.
- Apparent typos/inconsistencies in core theorems; dual objective/formulas need correction and clarity.
- Limited experimental scale and lack of runtime/scalability analysis; unclear benefits on larger models.
- Inconsistency in evaluation protocol (exact vs sampled marginals); limited ablations/sensitivity analyses (e.g., effect of c, sample pool size, iterations).
- No theoretical guarantees on approximation quality post-projection (e.g., bounds on KL or marginal errors).

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct and verify all theoretical statements (Theorems 6 and 7) and provide a complete derivation of the dual; include complexity analysis and convergence properties of the projection routine.
- Align experiments with guarantees (use random-scan, bound ≤1), and separately report performance under relaxed bounds with empirical mixing diagnostics.
- Expand experiments to larger grids and sparse real-world graphs; include wall-clock and scalability metrics; report SVD counts and per-iteration costs.
- Clarify the evaluation protocol; if exact marginals are available, separate accuracy and time–accuracy plots; otherwise, use consistent sampling-based estimates with effective sample size diagnostics.
- Provide ablations across divergences and subgraph choices for piecewise KL; analyze sensitivity to the spectral bound c and sampling hyperparameters.
- Discuss alternative sufficient conditions (e.g., weighted Dobrushin, block Gibbs) and whether the projection framework can accommodate them.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The core idea and some techniques are interesting and potentially impactful, but the current version has theoretical inconsistencies and limited empirical validation. With corrected theory and stronger, scalable experiments aligned with the guarantees, this work could be competitive.
