- Introduces projecting Ising model parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set to guarantee rapid Gibbs sampling convergence.
- Derives Euclidean projection via singular value thresholding and investigates three divergence-based projections: zero-avoiding KL (theoretical), piecewise KL, and zero-forcing KL with stochastic approximation.
- Theoretical results are strong and well-supported (Theorems 4–8), establishing a clear connection between spectral norm bounds and mixing time.
- Provides a practical algorithm for Euclidean projection and detailed derivations/proofs.
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive (2D grids and random graphs), comparing to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs; methods yield better marginal accuracy while ensuring fast mixing.
- Authors acknowledge limitations: spectral norm bound is conservative; piecewise KL performs worse than true KL; zero-avoiding KL is computationally intractable for large graphs.
- Paper is well-written and organized, with clear definitions, sufficient proof detail, and reproducible experimental/SGD implementation descriptions.
- Original contribution: shifts tractability focus from graph structure to parameter-space fast-mixing conditions; projection and stochastic-approximation methods are novel.
- Significant practical impact: provides a principled trade-off between approximation quality and guaranteed mixing, with potential extensions to other MRFs and learning.
- Strengths summarized: novel perspective, solid theory, comprehensive experiments, practical implementation, clear baseline comparisons.
- Weaknesses summarized: conservative spectral bound, unexplained empirical choice of bound 2.5 over 1.0, limited analysis of piecewise KL, missing discussion of projection computational complexity, superficial connection to other fast-mixing methods (e.g., Swendsen–Wang).
- Recommendation: Strong accept — significant, well-executed contribution likely of interest to researchers and practitioners.
