- Paper proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a set that guarantees fast-mixing Gibbs sampling instead of approximating the distribution directly.
- Explores multiple divergence measures for projection: Euclidean (SVD thresholding), KL-divergence (theoretically and piecewise approximations), and reversed KL minimized via stochastic approximation.
- Provides theoretical results for the Euclidean projection using matrix perturbation theory and mixing-time bounds.
- Empirically compares projection methods to baselines (LBP, TRW, naive mean field) on grid and random graphs, including time–accuracy comparisons.
- Positions work relative to mean-field, loopy BP, TRW, and prior mixing-time/ Dobrushin spectral norm results, citing relevant literature.
- Novelty: shifting focus to parameter projection for fast mixing is a fresh and potentially impactful idea.
- Theoretical grounding: solid justification for Euclidean projection and correct discussion of dependency matrices and mixing bounds.
- Multiple divergences: comparative analysis highlights trade-offs between computational complexity and approximation quality; piecewise KL seen as pragmatic compromise.
- Empirical evaluation: experiments are reasonable and results show improved accuracy over standard variational methods.
- Presentation is clear, well-organized, and notation is consistent.
- Spectral norm bound used appears overly conservative (e.g., cited 2.5 vs. known critical values), limiting practical applicability.
- Computational cost, especially for stochastic approximation of reversed KL, is not thoroughly analyzed or scaled with graph size.
- Piecewise KL-divergence underperforms in experiments and lacks deeper investigation or alternative subgraph selection strategies.
- Reversed KL optimization depends heavily on SGD tuning (step size, pool size, iterations) without sensitivity analysis or justification.
- Scalability is unclear; experiments are limited (graphs with ~10 nodes) and larger-scale feasibility is not addressed.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded approach with empirical gains and useful comparative analysis.
- Arguments against acceptance: conservative spectral bound, incomplete computational assessment, weak piecewise KL performance, and unclear scalability.
- Overall assessment: solid contribution with weaknesses outweighed by strengths.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
- Requested revisions: refine/justify spectral norm bound, provide detailed computational-cost analysis, explore alternative subgraph selection for piecewise KL, include SGD sensitivity analysis, and discuss scalability/future directions.
