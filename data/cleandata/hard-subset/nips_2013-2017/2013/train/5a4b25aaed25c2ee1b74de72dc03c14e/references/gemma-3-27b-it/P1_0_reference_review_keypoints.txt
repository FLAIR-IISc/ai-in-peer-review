- Novel idea: project Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set for Gibbs sampling instead of approximating the distribution directly.  
- Explores multiple divergences for projection: Euclidean (closed-form via SVD thresholding), KL(θ||ψ) (impractical), piecewise KL over subgraphs, and KL(ψ||θ) (promising via stochastic approximation).  
- Euclidean projection has a strong theoretical derivation (Theorem 7) and closed-form solution.  
- KL(ψ||θ) (zero-forcing KL) performs best in practice but requires costly stochastic gradient estimation using Gibbs sampling.  
- Piecewise KL-divergence performs worse than KL(ψ||θ) and lacks strong theoretical justification.  
- Experimental results on small Ising models show projected parameters improve marginal accuracy over LBP and mean-field.  
- Projected models still do not surpass accuracy achievable by sufficiently long Gibbs sampling on the original parameters.  
- The spectral-norm condition for fast mixing (||R||₂ < 1) appears conservative; authors used a larger threshold (≈2.5) in practice.  
- Conservatism of spectral bound may limit projection effectiveness; tighter mixing bounds are needed.  
- Computational cost of stochastic approximation is high and the experiments do not fully quantify accuracy vs. cost trade-offs.  
- Experiments are limited to small graphs, raising scalability and practical applicability concerns.  
- Related work is well-situated: connects to mean-field, LBP, tree-reweighted methods, and literature on fast-mixing via spectral norms.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly presented, and mathematically clear.  
- Recommendation to accept: novel, theoretically grounded, encouraging experimental results, and clear exposition.  
- Concerns against acceptance: conservative spectral bound, high computational cost for KL(ψ||θ), and limited scalability of experiments.  
- Suggested revisions: investigate tighter mixing bounds, analyze computational cost of stochastic approximation, run larger-scale experiments, and clarify or drop piecewise KL.
