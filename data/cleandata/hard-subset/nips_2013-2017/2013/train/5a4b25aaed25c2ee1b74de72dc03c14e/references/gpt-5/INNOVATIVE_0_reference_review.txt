Summary
This paper proposes a new approximation paradigm for high-treewidth graphical models: rather than approximating with a low-treewidth family, approximate with a “fast-mixing” family—parameter settings that guarantee rapid convergence of univariate Gibbs. For Ising models, the sufficient condition is that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix (bounded by the spectral norm of the absolute pairwise interaction matrix) is less than one. The main algorithmic idea is to project a given (possibly slow-mixing) parameter vector onto this fast-mixing set under different divergences:

- Euclidean projection: a dual approach built on spectral-norm projection via SVD thresholding, while enforcing sparsity via a mask (Theorem 7).
- KL(q||p): conceptually appealing but impractical at scale due to expectations under q.
- Piecewise KL: a tractable surrogate using low-treewidth subgraphs.
- KL(p||q): “zero-forcing” KL optimized by stochastic approximation leveraging that p (the approximation) is enforced to be fast-mixing; this generalizes MF by enlarging the tractable family from fully-factorized to fast-mixing.

Experiments on grids and small random graphs compare against MF/LBP/TRW and Gibbs on the original parameters. The proposed fast-mixing projections yield more accurate marginals than variational baselines and can outperform raw Gibbs under finite time budgets.

Relation to prior work
The approach connects mixing-time sufficient conditions (Dobrushin, spectral norm criteria; Hayes; Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum) with approximate inference, offering a distinct alternative to mean-field, structured mean-field, LBP/TRW, and planar approximations. The piecewise divergence echoes piecewise training and tree mixtures. The stochastic optimization angle leverages ideas akin to Ergodic Mirror Descent. To my knowledge, using the fast-mixing region as the approximation family and explicitly projecting onto it is a novel and interesting direction.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual shift: “tractable = fast-mixing” as a modeling family, with concrete projection procedures. This is a bold and potentially influential idea.
- Methodological contributions: a dual Euclidean projection that handles absolute-value constraints and sparsity; use of KL(ψ||θ) with sampling-friendly gradients for constrained stochastic optimization; a simple piecewise KL surrogate.
- Empirical signal: consistent gains over MF/LBP/TRW on the tested settings; pragmatic time–accuracy comparisons highlighting the utility of guaranteed rapid mixing.
- Clear connections: positions MF as a degenerate fast-mixing projection (zero spectral norm), which is insightful.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Guarantees vs practice: The central selling point is guarantees of rapid mixing under ||R||2<1, yet experiments frequently use a relaxed bound (2.5), which invalidates the guarantee. This needs either empirical validation of mixing (e.g., coupling bounds, autocorrelation, spectral gap proxies) or a principled rationale/alternative bound (e.g., uniqueness region conditions).
- Technical clarity/correctness: Theorem 7’s dual objective appears suspicious (g containing 1/||D−R||F^2 would be unusual and ill-behaved). Please check the exact form (I suspect a missing minus sign and factor 1/2 with a squared norm). Also discuss convexity: the constraint D=|B| makes the Euclidean projection problem nonconvex; what guarantees exist for the proposed dual/L-BFGS-B procedure?
- Evaluation details: 
  - Ground-truth marginals: the text alternates between Gibbs-based estimation and exact junction-tree; exact JT is infeasible on 8×8 grids. Please clarify precisely how ground truth was obtained.
  - Time accounting: comparisons exclude the projection cost, which is substantial for KL(ψ||θ). Include wall-clock comparisons that account for training + sampling time to support the finite-budget claim.
  - Sensitivity and ablations: show performance vs spectral-norm bound c; sample-pool size and step size; number of Gibbs updates between gradient steps; and the effect of using separate samples for µ̂ and ĝ.
- Piecewise KL: While elegant and tractable, it underperforms; more analysis on why (choice of subgraphs, variance/bias trade-offs) would help.
- Scope: The work is focused on Ising; the Discussion rightly points to general MRFs but stops short of giving even a candidate dependency bound for non-binary or higher-order factors.

Quality
Overall technically sound and honest about limitations, but Theorem 7’s form and the nonconvexity deserve explicit treatment. Experimental methodology needs clearer ground-truth and time-accounting.

Clarity
Generally readable and well-organized. Some notation is terse (e.g., Λ′Z), typos likely in Theorem 7, and the description of ground-truth estimation is inconsistent. The empirical section would benefit from more precise reporting.

Originality
High. Framing “fast-mixing” as the tractable family and developing projection operators under multiple divergences is fresh and non-incremental.

Significance
Promising. If refined, this could become a standard tool where one wants sampling-based approximations with guaranteed mixing, and it suggests new constrained-learning strategies.

Questions for authors
- Please correct/clarify Theorem 7’s objective and discuss convexity and convergence guarantees of the dual solver.
- How do you select the spectral-norm bound c? Can you relate it to uniqueness thresholds (e.g., Weitz tree) or provide adaptive tuning with measured mixing diagnostics?
- How were “true” marginals obtained on 8×8 grids? If approximated, how accurate are they?
- Can you report empirical mixing metrics for ψ vs θ to validate the “fast-mixing” claim when c>1?

Pros
- Conceptually novel and potentially impactful direction.
- Concrete algorithms with practical optimization recipes.
- Empirical improvements over common variational baselines.

Cons
- Theoretical guarantees not matched by experimental settings (c>1); missing mixing validation.
- Possible errors/ambiguities in Theorem 7 and lack of convexity discussion.
- Evaluation methodology needs clearer ground-truth and fair time accounting.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning). Despite imperfections, the core idea is bold and novel, and the paper provides concrete algorithms and promising evidence. I encourage the authors to fix the Theorem 7 issue, align experiments with guarantees (or rigorously justify relaxed bounds), and clarify evaluation details. My confidence is moderate.
