### NIPS Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel conceptual framework for approximate inference in graphical models, specifically the Ising model. Instead of defining the family of tractable distributions based on graph structure (e.g., trees, fully-factorized distributions), the authors propose using "fast-mixing" distributions as the tractable set. A distribution is considered fast-mixing if its parameters satisfy certain conditions that guarantee rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling. For the Ising model, the paper leverages a known sufficient condition: the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths, `|||β|||_2`, must be less than one.

The core contribution is a method to "project" an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing, Ising model onto this fast-mixing set. The paper explores four different divergences to measure the distance for this projection:
1.  **Euclidean Distance:** The authors derive a dual optimization algorithm based on iterative SVD thresholding that efficiently computes this projection while preserving the original graph's sparsity.
2.  **KL(q||p) (zero-avoiding):** Where `q` is the original distribution and `p` is the fast-mixing approximation. This is presented as theoretically interesting but computationally intractable for the target problems.
3.  **Piecewise KL-Divergence:** A novel tractable approximation to the KL divergence, computed by maximizing the KL divergence over a set of low-treewidth subgraphs of the original model.
4.  **KL(p||q) (zero-forcing):** This divergence is tractable because it requires expectations with respect to the fast-mixing distribution `p`, which can be estimated via MCMC. The authors propose a stochastic projected gradient descent algorithm to minimize this divergence.

Experimentally, the proposed methods are evaluated on grid and random graphs. The results show that projecting onto the fast-mixing set, particularly using the KL(p||q) divergence, yields more accurate marginal estimates than standard variational methods like Mean Field, Loopy Belief Propagation, and Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation. The method also provides a better time-accuracy trade-off than running Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing model for a finite number of steps.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound for the most part. The derivation of the Euclidean projection that preserves sparsity (Theorem 7) is a solid technical contribution. The application of stochastic projected gradient descent for the KL(p||q) case is well-motivated and leverages the core idea of the paper effectively (i.e., using the fast-mixing property of the iterate `ψ` to estimate gradients). The experimental methodology is rigorous, using exact inference for ground truth marginals and comparing against a strong set of relevant baselines.

However, there is a significant disconnect between the theory presented and the experiments conducted. The entire motivation rests on the theoretical bound `||R||_2 < 1` for guaranteed fast mixing. Yet, the experiments use a spectral norm bound of 2.5, which is empirically chosen and lacks the theoretical guarantees that form the paper's foundation. The authors acknowledge this in the discussion, but it remains a major weakness. The work would be much stronger if it included an empirical analysis of mixing times for the projected models to demonstrate that they are, in fact, "fast-mixing" even with this relaxed constraint, or if it provided some justification for the choice of 2.5.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, and the progression from background theory to the proposed algorithms is logical and easy to follow. The distinction between the different divergences and their computational trade-offs is explained well. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly.

**Originality:** The core idea of using fast-mixing as a criterion for defining a tractable family of distributions for approximate inference is highly original. This represents a significant conceptual departure from the vast body of work on variational inference that relies on structural properties of the graph. While the underlying MCMC theory is not new, its use in this optimization context is novel and creative. The proposed "piecewise KL" divergence is also a novel idea, even if its empirical performance was not top-tier.

**Significance:** This work has the potential to be very significant. It opens up a new and promising direction for designing approximate inference algorithms. If the approach can be tightened and generalized, it could offer a powerful alternative to traditional variational methods, especially for models where Gibbs sampling is a natural fit but suffers from slow mixing. Researchers are likely to build upon this idea of "variational inference on a dynamic property" rather than a static one.

However, the practical significance is currently limited by a few factors. First, the looseness of the bound, as discussed, is a key issue. Second, the computational cost of the projection, which involves an SVD at each step of an iterative optimization, is not discussed. For large graphs, this could be prohibitively expensive compared to the message-passing updates of LBP or TRW. A complexity analysis is needed to position the method's practicality. Third, the work is limited to Ising models; its significance would be greatly amplified if a path to extending it to more general discrete or continuous MRFs were outlined.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a highly novel and creative core idea: using fast-mixing as the basis for a tractable family in approximate inference.
*   The technical contribution for Euclidean projection is solid and solves the important problem of preserving sparsity.
*   The stochastic gradient approach for KL(p||q) is elegant and well-motivated within the paper's framework.
*   Strong empirical results show state-of-the-art performance against established and widely used baselines on the tested problems.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly communicates a new research direction that could inspire follow-up work.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The major theoretical premise (`||R||_2 < 1`) is abandoned in the experiments in favor of an empirical, unjustified constant (2.5), creating a significant gap between theory and practice.
*   The computational cost of the projection algorithm is not analyzed, leaving its scalability and practicality for large-scale problems in question.
*   The scope is limited to Ising models, and the path to generalization is not explored in depth.

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is a very interesting and thought-provoking paper that introduces a genuinely new idea to the field of approximate inference. The originality and the strength of the empirical results are major selling points. The main weakness is the disconnect between the theoretical motivation and the experimental implementation regarding the spectral norm bound. While this is a serious concern, the novelty of the overall framework and its demonstrated potential outweigh this flaw, which can be addressed in future work. The paper clearly advances the state of the art and is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
