### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth Ising models. Traditional methods approximate an intractable distribution with one from a structurally simple family (e.g., fully-factorized for mean-field, or low-treewidth for structured mean-field) where exact inference is efficient. The key idea here is to define a different notion of tractability: "fast-mixing" models, where Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to converge rapidly to the stationary distribution. The authors leverage a known result from the MCMC literature [8] which provides a sufficient condition for fast mixing in Ising models: the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths, `|||β|||_2`, must be less than 1.

The core of the paper is to formulate the approximation problem as finding a projection from a given (potentially slow-mixing) set of Ising parameters `θ` onto the set of fast-mixing parameters `Ψ`. The authors explore this projection under four different divergences:
1.  **Euclidean Distance**: A non-probabilistic measure of closeness between parameter vectors. The paper derives a novel and non-trivial dual algorithm (Theorem 7) to compute this projection, which correctly handles graph sparsity and the absolute value within the spectral norm constraint. This projection method, based on iterative SVD thresholding, serves as a building block for the other divergences.
2.  **KL(θ||ψ) ("zero-avoiding")**: A natural probabilistic divergence, but intractable to optimize as it requires computing expectations with respect to the original, slow-mixing distribution `θ`. It is discussed for theoretical completeness.
3.  **Piecewise KL**: A novel, tractable approximation to the KL divergence. It is defined as the maximum KL divergence over a collection of low-treewidth subgraphs of the original model.
4.  **KL(ψ||θ) ("zero-forcing")**: This reversed KL-divergence is tractable to optimize. Its gradient requires expectations with respect to the approximating distribution `ψ`. Since `ψ` is constrained to be fast-mixing, these expectations can be efficiently estimated via Gibbs sampling. This optimization is performed using a projected stochastic gradient descent algorithm, where each gradient step is followed by a Euclidean projection onto the fast-mixing set. The authors note this can be seen as a generalization of mean-field inference.

Experiments on grid and random graphs compare the proposed methods against loopy belief propagation (LBP), tree-reweighted belief propagation (TRW), naive mean-field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters. The results show that the proposed projection methods, particularly the one based on KL(ψ||θ), yield more accurate marginals than the other variational methods and outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model given a finite time budget.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the dual algorithm for the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) appears correct and is a solid contribution, providing a practical tool for the proposed framework. The application of projected stochastic gradient descent for the KL(ψ||θ) divergence is a standard and appropriate optimization strategy. The claims are well-supported by experiments on standard graph structures (grids, random graphs) and compared against relevant baselines. The authors are also transparent about a key weakness: the looseness of the theoretical bound. They empirically find that a spectral norm bound of 2.5 works better than the theoretically justified 1.0, which, while pragmatic, creates a disconnect between the theoretical guarantee of fast mixing and the practical implementation.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation for introducing a new class of tractable models is well-argued in the introduction. The background on mixing times and the specific theoretical results used are explained sufficiently for the reader to understand the basis of the work. The progression from the simple Euclidean projection to the more complex KL-divergence-based projections is logical. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly. The description of the projected stochastic gradient descent algorithm is slightly brief but understandable.

**Originality:**
The originality of this work is high. The central idea of defining the tractable family based on sampling efficiency (fast mixing) rather than structural simplicity (low treewidth) is novel and insightful. This represents a significant conceptual departure from mainstream variational inference techniques like mean-field or LBP. While it builds upon existing work in MCMC theory [4, 8] and optimization, the combination of these elements to create a new approximate inference framework is a creative and original contribution. The proposed "piecewise KL" divergence is also a novel idea, even if its empirical performance was not the strongest.

**Significance:**
The potential significance of this work is substantial. It opens up a new avenue for research in approximate inference by fundamentally rethinking what constitutes a "tractable" model. Researchers and practitioners could build on this idea by identifying other, potentially tighter, conditions for fast mixing or by extending the framework to other graphical models beyond the Ising model. The KL(ψ||θ) projection method, in particular, provides a principled way to trade off model fidelity for guaranteed sampling performance, which is a highly relevant problem in practice. The demonstration that this approach can outperform standard variational methods suggests it could become a valuable tool in the practitioner's toolkit.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Novelty**: The core concept of using "fast-mixing" as the basis for a tractable approximation family is highly original and could inspire significant follow-up work.
*   **Technical Contribution**: The paper provides a concrete and non-trivial algorithm (Theorem 7) for projecting onto the set of fast-mixing Ising models, which is a valuable contribution in its own right.
*   **Strong Empirical Results**: The proposed methods, especially the KL(ψ||θ) projection, demonstrate superior performance over established and widely used approximate inference algorithms like LBP and MF on the tested benchmarks.
*   **Clarity and Completeness**: The paper is well-written, self-contained, and presents a complete piece of work from theory and algorithm design to empirical validation.

### Arguments for Rejection:
*   **Gap between Theory and Practice**: The use of a spectral norm bound of 2.5 in experiments, rather than the theoretically justified bound of 1.0, weakens the central claim of *guaranteeing* fast mixing. This warrants a more thorough discussion or justification.
*   **Computational Cost**: The computational cost of the projection, which involves iterative SVDs and MCMC sampling for the KL-based methods, is not explicitly analyzed or compared against the baselines. A discussion of the trade-off between the upfront projection cost and the subsequent sampling efficiency would strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Scope**: The work is currently restricted to Ising models. While the authors suggest extensions are possible, the specific technical machinery developed is model-dependent, and the challenges for generalization seem significant.
