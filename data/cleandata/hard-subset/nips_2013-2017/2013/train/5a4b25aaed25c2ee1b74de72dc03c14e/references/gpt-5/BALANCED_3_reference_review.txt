Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a new notion of “tractable” approximations for inference in high-treewidth Ising models: instead of restricting to low-treewidth structures (trees, planar graphs) or fully/structured mean-field families, the authors propose projecting parameters onto a set that guarantees rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs sampling. The sufficient condition used is that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix be < 1 (via [8,4]), which, for Ising models, can be upper-bounded by the spectral norm of the absolute pairwise interaction matrix using Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|. The paper develops algorithms to project a given parameter vector θ to a fast-mixing ψ using four divergences: (i) Euclidean (with a dual SVD-thresholding-based projection that enforces sparsity/graph constraints), (ii) KL(θ||ψ) (zero-avoiding; impractical in general because it requires expectations under θ), (iii) a novel “piecewise” KL using tractable subgraphs, and (iv) KL(ψ||θ) (zero-forcing), optimized via stochastic approximation using samples from ψ (fast-mixing). This is framed as a generalization of mean-field (which corresponds to constraining the dependency norm to zero). Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare against MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs on the original parameters, showing improved marginal accuracy over variational baselines and favorable time-accuracy tradeoffs relative to Gibbs when computation is limited.

Strengths
- Conceptually novel angle: defines tractability via provable mixing-time conditions rather than exact inference, bridging variational approximations and MCMC under a unifying projection perspective.
- Clear connection to classical mixing-time criteria (Dobrushin vs spectral norm, [4,8]); for Ising, the bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is standard and well-motivated.
- Practical algorithmic contributions: a dual formulation for Euclidean projection that maintains sparsity and a stochastic approximation procedure for minimizing KL(ψ||θ) with projection steps (with a neat link to Ergodic Mirror Descent [3]).
- The “piecewise” KL idea is simple and potentially useful when low-treewidth substructures cover the graph.
- Empirical study compares to standard baselines and discusses time-accuracy tradeoffs in a realistic way.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical clarity/rigor: The statement of Theorem 6 appears to have a typographical error (S′ii = min(Sii, c2) should be c). The dual objective in Theorem 7, g(Λ,M) = 1/||D−R||^2F + Λ·Z·D, is dimensionally unusual and likely mis-typed (a standard Lagrangian would yield a quadratic form, not an inverse norm). Without a clear, correct derivation (appendix not provided), it is hard to assess the validity of the dual method.
- Guarantee vs practice mismatch: The core selling point is a guarantee of rapid mixing when ||R||2 < 1, but the experiments adopt a spectral bound of 2.5 “which still preserves rapid-mixing in practice.” This undercuts the main premise unless accompanied by empirical mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation times, effective sample size, coupling bounds) or an improved bound. The contrast with the known 2D Ising critical threshold (βc ≈ 0.4407) is acknowledged, but no quantitative mixing evidence is provided.
- Experimental clarity: Section 6 states that marginals are compared using Gibbs chains and also that “marginals [are] computed via the exact junction-tree algorithm,” which is infeasible on 8×8 grids. This needs correction and clarification (ground truth vs estimates, and for which settings exact JT is used).
- Fairness of time comparisons: The reported time-accuracy comparisons do not account for the time spent on projection/optimization; while this is noted, it makes it difficult to draw conclusions about practical efficiency. A wall-clock accounting or budgeted evaluation would be more informative.
- Piecewise KL: The max-over-subgraphs definition and use of only the maximizing subgraph’s gradient may be unstable; more discussion and ablations on the choice and number of subgraphs (trees vs width-2 strips) and the computational budget are needed.
- Scope of evaluation: Only small random graphs and modest grids are tested; no comparisons on larger or denser instances, nor on strongly mixed-sign interactions where mixing is most problematic.
- Notation/typos: Several notational rough edges (e.g., triple dot product, Theorem statements, systematic vs random-scan assumptions) reduce readability.

Quality
- The high-level idea and the KL(ψ||θ) gradient estimator are sound (∇ψ KL(ψ||θ) = Covψ[f]·(ψ−θ); the estimator in Eq. 8 is consistent). However, the derivations for the Euclidean projection dual (Theorem 7) need correction/clarification. The experimental methodology has inconsistencies (JT vs Gibbs for marginals), and the use of a bound above 1.0 without mixing diagnostics weakens the central guarantee.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and well-situated, but the noted typos and contradictory statements in the experiment section should be fixed, and the missing/unclear derivations provided. Explain computational complexity and convergence behavior of the LBFGS-B dual routine.

Originality
- The projection-to-fast-mixing idea and the piecewise-KL divergence are fresh. The connection of KL(ψ||θ) minimization under mixing constraints to mean-field is insightful.

Significance
- Potentially impactful direction for approximate inference, especially if extended to general MRFs with better mixing criteria. Current empirical evidence is promising but limited; stronger validation and guarantees would increase significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct Theorems 6 and 7 and include full derivations; validate the dual algorithm on synthetic cases where the solution is known.
- Either adhere to ||R||2 < 1 in experiments or provide strong empirical mixing diagnostics if using looser bounds (ESS, ACT, Gelman–Rubin with multiple chains, coupling-based estimates).
- Clarify how marginals are computed (ground-truth vs sampled) and provide wall-clock comparisons that include projection time.
- Provide ablations on: spectral bound c, sample pool size, step sizes, number of iterations, and subgraph choices for piecewise KL.
- Report sparsity preservation and computational cost per projection step; discuss scalability.
- Explore block Gibbs or Swendsen–Wang under analogous fast-mixing conditions, and assess sensitivity to attractive vs mixed-sign models.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and potentially useful framework.
- Solid link between mixing-time theory and approximate inference practice.
- Practical algorithms with encouraging empirical results against common baselines.

Arguments against acceptance
- Key theoretical derivations appear incorrect/unclear as written.
- Experiments do not substantiate the claimed guarantees (use of c=2.5 without diagnostics) and have methodological inconsistencies.
- Limited empirical scope and no end-to-end efficiency accounting.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper proposes an interesting and promising idea with initial algorithms and results, but the current version has theoretical inaccuracies/typos and experimental gaps that should be addressed. With corrected derivations, proper mixing diagnostics, clarified evaluation, and broader experiments, this work could make a strong submission.
