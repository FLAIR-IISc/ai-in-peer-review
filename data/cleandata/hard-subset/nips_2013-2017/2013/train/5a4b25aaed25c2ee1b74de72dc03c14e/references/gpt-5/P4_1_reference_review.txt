Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a new notion of tractable approximation for Ising models: instead of constraining graph structure (trees, planar, low treewidth), it constrains parameter sets to those that guarantee rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs sampling. Using standard mixing-time theory, the paper adopts a sufficient condition for fast mixing based on the spectral norm of a dependency matrix R: for Ising models with pairwise couplings βij, Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|, and if ||R||2 < 1 one obtains a polynomial mixing-time bound. The paper’s core idea is to “project” arbitrary (possibly slow-mixing) parameters θ onto a fast-mixing set Ψ by minimizing a divergence D(θ, ψ) subject to a spectral-norm constraint.

Four projections are considered: (i) Euclidean projection under Frobenius norm, for which a dual method based on iterative singular value thresholding is derived (Theorem 7), enforcing both a spectral-norm constraint and sparsity pattern; (ii) inclusive KL(θ||ψ), theoretically appealing but intractable at scale because it requires expectations under θ; (iii) a “piecewise” KL computed exactly on a collection of tractable subgraphs T and aggregated as maxT KL(θ(T)||ψ(T)); and (iv) reverse KL(ψ||θ), which admits stochastic approximation using samples from ψ (enforced to be fast-mixing), yielding a projected stochastic gradient procedure related to ergodic mirror descent and generalizing mean-field (the special case where R=0).

Experiments on 8×8 grids and n=10 random graphs compare marginal accuracy (mean absolute error vs exact junction-tree marginals) for Euclidean, piecewise, and reverse-KL projections, against LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original parameters. The paper reports that fast-mixing projections are more accurate than variational baselines, and that, for fixed sampling budgets, Gibbs on the projected model can outperform Gibbs on the original model. The discussion acknowledges that the spectral-norm bound is loose (e.g., for 2D ferromagnets), and that a tighter condition would be valuable.

Quality
- Positive: The formulation of projection onto a fast-mixing set is well-motivated; the use of spectral-norm conditions tied to established mixing-time results is appropriate; the stochastic projected optimization for KL(ψ||θ) is conceptually sound; the connection to mean-field as R=0 is clear.
- Concerns:
  1) Gradient for reverse KL: In exponential families, KL(ψ||θ) = A(ψ) − A(θ) − (ψ−θ)·μ(ψ) and ∇ψ KL(ψ||θ) = Σψ (θ−ψ), where Σψ is the Fisher information/covariance of sufficient statistics. Theorem 8 and estimator (Eq. 8) correspond to Σψ(ψ−θ), which has the opposite sign. If used directly in a standard descent step, this would ascend the objective. This appears to be a sign error that affects correctness of the main scalable projection method.
  2) Guarantees vs practice: The experiments commonly use a spectral-norm bound c=2.5 (rather than <1), which voids the theoretical rapid-mixing guarantee. No empirical diagnostics of mixing (e.g., effective sample sizes, autocorrelation, coupling bounds) are provided to substantiate rapid mixing under this relaxed bound.
  3) Dual Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) lacks sufficient detail for verification: the exact dual objective and constants are unclear (e.g., g(Λ, M) contains an unusual “1/||·||^2” term in the provided text), and convexity/optimality guarantees are not discussed. Computational complexity and convergence properties of the dual ascent with repeated SVD thresholding are not analyzed.
  4) Evaluation methodology: Accuracy is reported w.r.t. exact junction-tree marginals but the paper also states marginals are “compared by running a Gibbs chain”; the evaluation protocol needs clarification. Runtime comparisons exclude the substantial projection effort (e.g., ~30k Gibbs updates plus repeated Euclidean projections), making time–accuracy trade-offs hard to interpret. Graphs are small; scalability is not demonstrated.
  5) Piecewise KL: The max-over-subgraphs surrogate is simple but yields a non-smooth objective and, by the authors’ admission, underperforms true KL; motivation and design choices (max vs average, subgraph selection) could be better justified.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-situated within prior work (MF, structured MF, BP/TRW, planar decompositions, mixing-time criteria). However, there are several notational and typographical issues (potentially including constants in Theorem 6, the dual objective in Theorem 7, and the sign in Theorem 8), and some ambiguity in the experimental protocol. Including full derivations (or a polished supplement), explicit algorithm pseudocode, and clarifying the evaluation would improve clarity.

Originality
The central idea—approximating with a fast-mixing family defined by parameter-space constraints and projecting onto it—is novel relative to standard structure-based tractability (trees/planar/low-treewidth) and to classical variational methods. The Euclidean projection with sparsity and spectral-norm control, and the stochastic projected reverse-KL approach, are interesting contributions. The piecewise KL surrogate is a modest extension of known piecewise/mixture-of-trees ideas.

Significance
If technically correct and practically realizable, the approach could provide a principled way to obtain models amenable to reliable MCMC with quantifiable mixing guarantees, offering a different trade-off than variational approximations. However, the looseness of the sufficient condition and the reliance on bounds >1 in experiments weaken the practical impact. The potential sign error in the main gradient would also undermine the main empirical claims if not corrected.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel approximation family (fast-mixing) with clear theoretical grounding.
- Concrete projection algorithms; connection to mean-field and ergodic mirror descent.
- Empirical evidence suggesting improved marginal accuracy over common variational baselines.

Cons for acceptance
- Apparent sign error in the reverse-KL gradient (Theorem 8 / Eq. 8).
- Use of spectral-norm bounds that forgo guarantees; no empirical mixing diagnostics.
- Insufficiently specified dual derivation and optimization properties for Euclidean projection.
- Limited scalability and runtime accounting; small graphs only.
- Piecewise KL surrogate underperforms and is weakly motivated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct and verify the reverse-KL gradient; provide a clear derivation and unit test on small models.
- Either enforce the proven mixing condition in experiments or provide diagnostics showing rapid mixing under relaxed bounds; study performance vs bound c.
- Present full dual derivation, clarify constants, discuss convexity/duality gap, and analyze computational complexity.
- Provide end-to-end runtime including projection; add larger-scale experiments.
- Clarify evaluation (how exact marginals are obtained), and consider smoother aggregations for piecewise KL (e.g., average) with rationale.

Overall recommendation
Given the technical concerns (notably the reverse-KL gradient) and the gap between the theoretical guarantee and the experimental settings, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. The idea is promising and could become significant with corrected theory, stronger guarantees or diagnostics, and clearer/expanded empirical validation.
