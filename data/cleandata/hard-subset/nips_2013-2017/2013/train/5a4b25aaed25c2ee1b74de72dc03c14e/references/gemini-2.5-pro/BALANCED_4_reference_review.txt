### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel approach for approximate inference in graphical models, specifically Ising models. Instead of approximating an intractable high-treewidth model with a tractable low-treewidth one (as in structured mean-field or tree-reweighted belief propagation), the authors propose approximating it with a "fast-mixing" model. A fast-mixing model is one whose parameters satisfy certain conditions that guarantee rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling to the stationary distribution. The paper leverages a known result that for an Ising model, if the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths is less than one, the mixing time is polynomially bounded.

The core contribution is to frame the approximation problem as a projection: given an arbitrary (potentially slow-mixing) set of parameters θ, find the closest set of parameters ψ within the fast-mixing set. The paper explores four different notions of "closeness" or divergence:
1.  **Euclidean Distance:** The authors derive a dual algorithm based on iterative singular value thresholding to solve for the L2 projection while respecting graph sparsity.
2.  **"Zero-avoiding" KL-divergence (KL(θ||ψ)):** This is the standard KL divergence, but is computationally intractable for slow-mixing θ, making it primarily of theoretical interest.
3.  **Piecewise KL-divergence:** A novel, tractable approximation to KL(θ||ψ) defined as the maximum KL divergence over a collection of low-treewidth subgraphs of the original model.
4.  **"Zero-forcing" KL-divergence (KL(ψ||θ)):** This divergence requires expectations with respect to the approximating distribution ψ. Since ψ is constrained to be fast-mixing, these expectations can be estimated efficiently via sampling. The authors propose a stochastic projected gradient descent algorithm to minimize this divergence.

Experiments on 2D grids and random graphs show that the proposed methods, particularly the one based on KL(ψ||θ), outperform standard variational methods like loopy belief propagation, tree-reweighted BP, and naive mean-field in terms of marginal accuracy. They also show that running Gibbs sampling on the projected (fast-mixing) parameters yields more accurate marginals in a finite time budget than running it on the original, slow-mixing parameters.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The central idea of using the set of fast-mixing distributions as the approximating family is highly original and conceptually significant. It presents a new paradigm for approximate inference that departs from the conventional focus on structural tractability (e.g., low treewidth). This could inspire a new line of research into algorithmically-defined tractable families.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It provides a non-trivial optimization algorithm (Theorem 7) for the Euclidean projection and correctly frames the more complex KL(ψ||θ) projection as a stochastic optimization problem solvable with projected gradient methods. The connection to prior work is clear, and the generalization of naive mean-field (which corresponds to a spectral norm of zero) is insightful.
3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured. The background on mixing times and the derivation of the projection methods are easy to follow.
4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The proposed methods demonstrate superior accuracy over several widely-used baselines for approximate inference. The comparison effectively highlights the practical utility of the proposed framework.
5.  **Scientific Honesty:** The authors are transparent about the limitations of their work, particularly regarding the looseness of the theoretical spectral norm bound, which is a commendable quality.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The primary weakness is the discrepancy between the theoretical guarantee and the practical implementation. The theory relies on a spectral norm bound of 1.0 for guaranteed fast mixing, but the experiments find that a bound of 2.5 works better. While the authors openly discuss this, it weakens the core claim of "projecting onto a *guaranteed* fast-mixing set." The method appears more like a principled way to regularize interaction strengths to encourage faster mixing, rather than a strict projection.
2.  **Scalability Concerns:** Experiments are conducted on relatively small graphs (10- and 64-node models) where exact marginals can be computed for evaluation. While this is necessary for rigorous comparison, it leaves open questions about how the method scales to larger, more realistic problems where it would be most useful. The computational cost of the SVD-based projection step, in particular, may become a bottleneck for very large graphs.
3.  **Details for Reproducibility:** The description of the stochastic gradient descent algorithm for minimizing KL(ψ||θ) could be more detailed. Specifics on how the sample pool is managed, how gradients are estimated from the same samples used for marginals, and how the projection is interleaved with gradient steps would be beneficial for reproducibility. While the authors cite Ergodic Mirror Descent, a more explicit algorithm box would improve clarity.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   The paper introduces a novel and compelling conceptual framework for approximate inference.
    *   The proposed methods are technically sound and well-motivated.
    *   Empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear advantage over established baselines.
    *   The paper is well-written and is likely to be impactful and inspire follow-up work.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The practical implementation deviates from the theoretical guarantees (using a spectral norm of 2.5 vs. 1.0), which may confuse readers about the method's claims.
    *   The experiments are limited to small-scale models, and the scalability of the approach is not fully explored.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

Overall, this is a strong paper with a high-quality contribution. My recommendation is to accept it. The weaknesses are addressable and do not detract from the importance of the core ideas. I offer the following suggestions to further strengthen the manuscript:

1.  **Reframing the "Guarantee":** I suggest slightly reframing the discussion around the spectral norm bound. Instead of presenting it as a hard guarantee that is then relaxed, it might be more accurate to describe the method as projecting onto a family of distributions parameterized by a spectral norm bound `c`. The theory provides a value of `c=1` for a provable worst-case guarantee, while empirically, a larger `c` provides a better trade-off between accuracy and mixing speed for the problems under study. This would more accurately reflect the experimental findings without undermining the theoretical motivation.
2.  **Adding a Scalability Experiment:** To address concerns about scalability, it would be valuable to include an experiment on a larger graph (e.g., a 20x20 grid). Since exact marginals are unavailable, evaluation could be done by empirically measuring mixing properties. For instance, a plot of the autocorrelation of samples from the Gibbs chain on the original vs. projected parameters would provide direct evidence of improved mixing. This would powerfully complement the existing accuracy experiments.
3.  **Time-Accuracy Plot:** The text mentions time-accuracy comparisons, but no corresponding figure is present. A plot of marginal error versus total wall-clock time (including the time for projection) for your methods versus Gibbs on the original parameters would be extremely compelling and would make the practical benefit of the approach immediately obvious.
4.  **Algorithm Details:** Including a concise algorithm box summarizing the stochastic projected gradient descent procedure for the KL(ψ||θ) case would significantly improve the clarity and reproducibility of the experimental setup.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel, well-executed, and important contribution to the field of approximate inference. I believe it will be of great interest to the NeurIPS community.
