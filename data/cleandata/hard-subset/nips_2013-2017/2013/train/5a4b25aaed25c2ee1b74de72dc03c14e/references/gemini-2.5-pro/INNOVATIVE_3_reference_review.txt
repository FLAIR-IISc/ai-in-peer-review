This paper introduces a novel and thought-provoking approach to approximate inference in graphical models. The central idea is to redefine the notion of a "tractable" approximating family. Instead of relying on distributions with simple graphical structures (e.g., fully-factorized for mean-field or tree-structured for TRW-BP), the authors propose using the family of distributions for which Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing." For the Ising model, this property can be characterized by a constraint on the spectral norm of the matrix of interaction strengths. The paper frames approximate inference as a projection problem: given a set of parameters for a potentially slow-mixing model, find the "closest" set of parameters within the fast-mixing set. This projection is explored under four different divergences: the standard Euclidean distance, the inclusive KL divergence (KL(θ||ψ)), a novel piecewise KL divergence, and the reversed KL divergence (KL(ψ||θ)).

The authors provide concrete algorithms for these projections. For the Euclidean distance, they derive an elegant dual optimization algorithm based on iterative SVD thresholding. For the more principled but computationally challenging KL divergences, they discuss the trade-offs. The inclusive KL requires inference on the original, intractable model, rendering it mostly of theoretical interest. The reversed KL, however, only requires expectations over the fast-mixing approximation, which can be estimated via Gibbs sampling. The authors propose a practical stochastic projected gradient descent algorithm for this case. This method can be seen as a direct generalization of naive mean-field inference, which corresponds to the extreme case of projecting onto the set of models with zero interaction strength.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Significance:** The paper's core contribution is a fundamental conceptual shift in how we think about approximate inference. Defining tractability through dynamic properties (fast mixing) rather than static structural ones (low treewidth) is a bold and exciting idea. It opens up a new and potentially rich research direction that could connect the variational inference and MCMC communities more closely. This is not an incremental improvement but a foundational proposal for a new class of variational methods.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Framework:** The approach is well-grounded in the established theory of Markov chain mixing times. The formulation of the problem as a projection is clean and powerful. The derivation of the dual algorithm for the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a solid and non-trivial technical contribution that provides a practical tool for implementing the simplest version of their idea.

3.  **Unifying Perspective:** The connection drawn between their reversed KL projection and naive mean-field is insightful. It elegantly positions their method as a strict generalization, moving from a constraint of zero spectral norm (fully factorized) to a more relaxed constraint of a small spectral norm, thereby expanding the expressiveness of the approximating family while retaining a form of tractability (fast sampling).

4.  **Practicality:** The paper goes beyond a purely conceptual proposal by developing and experimenting with concrete algorithms. The stochastic optimization scheme for the reversed KL divergence, while complex, provides a clear path to applying this method in practice.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The primary weakness is the acknowledged looseness of the spectral norm bound used to guarantee fast mixing. The theory relies on a spectral norm of less than 1, but the experiments achieve better results using a heuristic value of 2.5. While the authors are transparent about this, it does create a disconnect between the theoretical motivation and the practical implementation, leaving the choice of this crucial hyperparameter largely an empirical question.

2.  **Scalability and Validation:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small-scale models (8x8 grids, 10-node graphs). While sufficient for a proof of concept, it remains unclear how the computational cost of the projection step (especially the iterative SVD) and the performance of the approximation will scale to larger, more realistic problems.

3.  **Algorithmic Complexity:** The stochastic projected gradient descent method for minimizing KL(ψ||θ) introduces several hyperparameters (step size, sample pool size, MCMC steps per iteration) that can be difficult to tune. The paper notes this but provides a fixed setting, which may not be optimal and could obscure the true potential or difficulty of applying the method.

**Recommendation:**

Despite the weaknesses, which are understandable for an initial exploration of such a novel concept, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The sheer originality and potential impact of the core idea—defining a variational family based on fast mixing—far outweigh the current limitations. This is exactly the kind of bold, high-risk, high-reward work that NIPS should champion. It challenges established conventions and opens up an entirely new avenue for research in approximate inference. The weaknesses, particularly the looseness of the mixing time bounds, are not failures of the authors' methodology but rather reflect known challenges in the underlying theory, and they present exciting open questions for future work. This paper is likely to be highly influential and to stimulate a great deal of follow-up research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a fundamentally new and significant concept for approximate inference.
*   Provides a strong theoretical basis and novel algorithms for realizing the concept.
*   Offers a unifying view that connects their method to established techniques like mean-field.
*   Experimental results, though limited, successfully demonstrate the promise of the approach over standard variational methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The practical implementation relies on a heuristic bound that is not justified by the presented theory.
*   The experimental validation is limited to small-scale problems.
