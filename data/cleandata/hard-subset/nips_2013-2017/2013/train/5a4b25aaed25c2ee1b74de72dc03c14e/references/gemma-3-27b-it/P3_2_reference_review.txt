## Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents an interesting and technically sound approach to accelerating Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling for Ising models by projecting parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set. The core idea – leveraging the well-understood conditions for rapid mixing to improve sampling efficiency – is novel and has potential practical benefits. The exploration of different divergence measures for the projection is thorough, and the experimental evaluation is well-designed. However, the practical impact of the proposed methods, particularly beyond the simpler Euclidean projection, feels somewhat limited given the computational costs and the relatively modest gains observed in the experiments. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns outlined below.

**Summary of the Paper:**

The paper addresses the challenge of slow mixing in MCMC sampling for Ising models. Instead of directly tackling the intractable inference problem, the authors propose projecting the parameters of a given Ising model onto a subset where Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to mix rapidly. This "fast-mixing" set is defined by a constraint on the spectral norm of the dependency matrix. The paper explores four projection methods based on different divergences: Euclidean norm (via SVD thresholding), KL(q||p), a piecewise KL divergence based on low-treewidth subgraphs, and KL(p||q) approximated via stochastic approximation.  The authors provide theoretical results for the Euclidean projection and demonstrate the effectiveness of these approaches experimentally, comparing them against standard inference methods like loopy belief propagation, tree-reweighted belief propagation, and naive mean-field, as well as Gibbs sampling on the original parameters.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of explicitly projecting parameters to enforce fast mixing is a fresh perspective on improving MCMC efficiency.  It's a departure from traditional approximation schemes that focus on approximating the distribution itself.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundation is well-developed, with clear definitions and a solid connection to existing results on mixing times (Dyer et al., Hayes).  The derivation of the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is particularly strong.
* **Thorough Exploration:** The investigation of multiple divergence measures provides a nuanced understanding of the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The background on mixing times is helpful for readers unfamiliar with the area.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are comprehensive, covering different graph topologies and interaction strengths, and include comparisons to relevant baseline methods.
* **Connection to Mean Field:** The authors correctly position the KL(p||q) divergence as a generalization of mean-field inference.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Impact:** While the proposed methods generally outperform standard variational methods, the improvements over Gibbs sampling on the original parameters are often modest. The computational cost of the more sophisticated divergences (piecewise KL, KL(p||q)) raises concerns about their scalability.
* **Conservatism of Spectral Norm Bound:** The authors acknowledge that the spectral norm bound of 1.0 is conservative, potentially limiting the effectiveness of the projection.  The use of 2.5 in the experiments feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks a rigorous justification. A more adaptive or tighter bound would be beneficial.
* **KL(q||p) Limited Applicability:** The theoretical interest of the KL(q||p) projection is noted, but its practical intractability is a significant limitation.  This section feels somewhat detached from the rest of the paper.
* **Piecewise KL Divergence Performance:** The piecewise KL divergence consistently underperforms the other methods, despite its conceptual appeal. A deeper investigation into the reasons for this (e.g., choice of subgraphs, approximation error) is warranted.
* **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis of the projections.  How much information is lost during the projection process? What are the conditions under which the approximation is likely to be accurate?

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to improving MCMC efficiency.
* The exploration of different divergence measures provides valuable insights.
* The experimental results demonstrate the potential of the proposed methods, particularly the Euclidean projection.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The practical impact of the proposed methods, beyond the Euclidean projection, is limited by computational cost and modest gains.
* The conservative spectral norm bound may hinder the effectiveness of the projection.
* The piecewise KL divergence performs poorly, raising questions about its utility.



**Specific Suggestions/Questions for the Authors:**

* **Justification for Spectral Norm:** Provide a more rigorous justification for the use of a spectral norm bound of 2.5 in the experiments. Can this value be determined adaptively based on the specific Ising model?
* **Piecewise KL Divergence Analysis:** Investigate the reasons for the poor performance of the piecewise KL divergence. Experiment with different subgraph selection strategies or explore alternative approximations of the KL divergence.
* **Error Analysis:**  Provide an error analysis of the projections.  Can you bound the difference between the original and projected distributions?
* **Scalability:** Discuss the scalability of the proposed methods to larger Ising models.  Are there any optimizations that can be made to reduce the computational cost of the more complex divergences?
* **Comparison to other Fast-Mixing techniques:** Is this approach orthogonal or complementary to established fast-mixing techniques like block Gibbs sampling or tree sampling?

**Overall Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly concerning the justification of the spectral norm bound and the analysis of the piecewise KL divergence, would significantly strengthen the paper. I believe this work represents a valuable contribution to the field of approximate inference and has the potential to inspire further research in this area.
