### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically the Ising model. Instead of approximating an intractable distribution with one from a structurally simple family (e.g., fully factorized for mean-field or low-treewidth for structured mean-field), the authors define the set of "tractable" distributions as those that are guaranteed to be "fast-mixing" for Gibbs sampling. For the Ising model, they use a known sufficient condition for rapid mixing: the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths, `|| |β| ||_2`, must be less than 1.

The core contribution is to "project" an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing Ising model onto this set of fast-mixing models. The paper explores four different projection methods based on different divergences:
1.  **Euclidean Distance:** The authors derive a novel dual optimization algorithm (Theorem 7) to find the closest parameters in the L2 sense, which cleverly handles both the spectral norm constraint and graph sparsity by iteratively thresholding a singular value decomposition.
2.  **"Zero-avoiding" KL-Divergence (KL(q||p)):** This is presented as a theoretically sound but practically intractable method, as it requires computing expectations with respect to the original slow-mixing distribution `q`.
3.  **Piecewise KL-Divergence:** A novel, tractable approximation to the KL divergence is proposed, where the KL is calculated over low-treewidth subgraphs (e.g., trees or chains) of the original graph.
4.  **"Zero-forcing" KL-Divergence (KL(p||q)):** This is a practical method that can be optimized via stochastic gradient descent. Since expectations are taken with respect to the fast-mixing distribution `p`, they can be efficiently approximated via Gibbs sampling. This method is framed as a generalization of mean-field inference.

Experiments on grid and random graphs show that the proposed methods, particularly the KL(p||q) projection, produce more accurate marginals than standard variational methods like Loopy BP, TRW, and Mean-Field. Furthermore, they demonstrate that Gibbs sampling on the projected (fast-mixing) parameters can yield better estimates than running Gibbs for the same amount of time on the original (slow-mixing) parameters.

### Review

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   The theoretical contributions are sound. Theorem 7, which provides a method for the Euclidean projection while preserving sparsity, is a non-trivial and valuable result. The derivation of the gradient for the reversed KL-divergence (Theorem 8) is also correct.
*   The claims are well-supported by experimental results. The comparisons are made against strong and relevant baselines (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs). The evaluation setup is clear, and the chosen metric (mean absolute distance of marginals) is appropriate.
*   The authors are commendably honest about the weaknesses of their work. They explicitly discuss the looseness of the spectral norm bound in Section 7, noting that it is conservative compared to known phase transition thresholds for specific models. They also acknowledge that using a bound of 2.5 instead of 1.0 in their experiments is an empirical choice, which deviates from their theoretical guarantee. This transparency is a significant strength. The work is a complete piece, presenting theory, algorithms, and empirical validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.
*   The motivation is established effectively in the introduction, relating the work to a rich history of approximate inference methods. The background section provides the necessary context on mixing times and the dependency matrix in an accessible manner.
*   The paper flows logically from the core theoretical tool (Theorem 4), to the development of the projection algorithms, to the empirical validation.
*   The description of the stochastic gradient descent algorithm for the KL(ψ||θ) projection is clear and provides enough detail for a reader to understand the implementation (e.g., using a "pool" of samples and Ergodic Mirror Descent).
*   A minor point for improvement could be a more thorough motivation for the "piecewise" KL divergence. The justification that identical distributions remain identical on subgraphs is clear, but the leap to using `max_T KL(θ(T)||ψ(T))` as the objective could be explained more fully. Why the maximum, and not, for example, a sum?

**Originality:** The work is highly original.
*   The central idea of defining a tractable family based on dynamic properties (fast mixing) rather than static structural properties (low treewidth) is novel and insightful. It opens up a new perspective on what constitutes a "good" approximation for inference.
*   While using fast-mixing conditions is known in the MCMC literature, applying it as a constraint for a projection-based approximate inference scheme is, to my knowledge, a new contribution.
*   The proposed algorithms, particularly the dual method for Euclidean projection and the stochastic optimization for KL projection, are novel applications of existing optimization techniques to this new problem formulation.
*   The work is clearly distinguished from prior work. It correctly positions itself as a generalization of mean-field (which corresponds to a spectral norm of zero) but distinct from other belief propagation and tree-based methods.

**Significance:** The paper has the potential for significant impact.
*   It introduces a new class of approximation algorithms that offer a different trade-off between accuracy and computational cost. The idea of trading asymptotic accuracy for guaranteed rapid convergence is very practical for real-world applications where finite computational budgets are a key constraint.
*   The results are compelling. The method outperforms several widely used variational inference techniques in the experiments. The time-accuracy comparison against standard Gibbs sampling is particularly powerful, demonstrating a clear practical benefit.
*   The framework is potentially generalizable. As the authors suggest in their discussion, the core idea could be extended to other graphical models beyond the Ising model and to other MCMC algorithms with their own fast-mixing conditions. This could inspire a new line of research into "dynamically-tractable" inference.

### Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):
*   **Novel Conceptual Contribution:** Redefining "tractability" in terms of fast-mixing is a creative and powerful idea.
*   **Strong Technical Results:** The derivation of the Euclidean projection algorithm (Theorem 7) is a solid technical contribution.
*   **Practical Algorithm:** The KL(p||q) projection provides a concrete, well-motivated algorithm that performs very well empirically.
*   **Thorough and Honest Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, the baselines are appropriate, and the authors are transparent about the limitations of their theoretical bounds.

### Arguments for Rejection (Cons):
*   **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The most significant weakness is the disconnect between the theoretical guarantee (spectral norm < 1.0) and the empirically used value (2.5). While the authors are upfront about this, it does weaken the claim of "guaranteed" fast mixing in the experiments and suggests the theoretical tool is not as sharp as one might hope. A more detailed discussion on why this larger bound still works well in practice would strengthen the paper.
*   **Computational Cost of Projection:** The paper does not analyze the computational complexity of the Euclidean projection step itself, which relies on an iterative SVD-based optimization. For large graphs, this could be a significant computational cost that needs to be factored into the overall efficiency comparison.

In conclusion, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The originality and significance of the core idea, combined with strong technical execution and compelling empirical results, make this a clear candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
