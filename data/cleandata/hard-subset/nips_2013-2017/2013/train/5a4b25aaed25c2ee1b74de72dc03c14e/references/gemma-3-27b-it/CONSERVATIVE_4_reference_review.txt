This paper explores approximating intractable distributions with “fast-mixing” distributions, specifically within the context of the Ising model. The core idea – projecting parameters onto a set guaranteeing rapid Gibbs sampling convergence – is interesting, and builds on established connections between spectral properties of the dependency matrix and mixing times. The authors explore several divergence measures for this projection, including Euclidean distance, piecewise KL, and a reversed KL divergence amenable to stochastic approximation.

However, despite the potentially valuable premise, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence and a somewhat superficial exploration of the proposed methods. My primary concern is the experimental evaluation. While comparisons are made to standard inference algorithms (LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling), the presented results don’t convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage of the proposed projection methods *over* established baselines, especially considering the computational cost of the projection step itself. The claim that the methods are “more accurate than variational approximations” (specifically MF) requires stronger justification, particularly given the relative simplicity and widespread use of MF. The results feel incremental at best, and the justification for choosing a spectral norm bound of 2.5 (rather than the theoretically tighter 1.0) feels ad-hoc and lacking in robust analysis.

The theoretical development, while generally sound, feels somewhat disconnected from the experimental evaluation. The derivation of the projection algorithm based on singular value thresholding (Theorem 7) is mathematically correct, but its practical benefit is unclear. The piecewise KL divergence, while conceptually intriguing, doesn’t perform well empirically, and the justification for choosing specific subgraphs (horizontal/vertical chains for grids) isn’t sufficiently motivated. The discussion of the reversed KL divergence and its stochastic approximation is well-presented, but its practical advantages over other methods are not convincingly demonstrated. The paper states that the stochastic gradient descent requires intensive tuning, and that the presented parameters were chosen for reasonable performance, rather than optimal performance. This is concerning, since it suggests that the reported results could improve significantly with further tuning.

The connection to existing work is reasonable, but could be more thorough. While the paper cites relevant literature on mixing times and approximate inference, it doesn't fully situate itself within the broader landscape of variational inference methods, or discuss potential limitations relative to more recent advances in that area. The discussion of related work feels cursory.

**Strengths:**

*   The core idea of leveraging fast-mixing properties for approximate inference is novel and potentially valuable.
*   The theoretical analysis is generally correct and provides a mathematical foundation for the proposed methods.
*   The exploration of different divergence measures offers some flexibility in the projection process.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The experimental results are not compelling and do not demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The justification for the chosen spectral norm bound is weak.
*   The piecewise KL divergence performs poorly in practice.
*   The paper lacks a thorough comparison to recent advances in variational inference.
*   The discussion of computational cost is insufficient; it’s unclear if the improved accuracy (where observed) justifies the added computational burden of the projection step.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel idea with a solid theoretical foundation.
*   The exploration of different divergence measures is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental results are not convincing.
*   The paper lacks a clear demonstration of practical significance.
*   The theoretical analysis feels somewhat disconnected from the experimental evaluation.
*   The limitations of the proposed methods are not adequately addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the idea is interesting, the lack of strong experimental evidence and the incremental nature of the contributions do not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. The paper feels more like a preliminary investigation than a complete and convincing scientific contribution. The authors need to significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation, provide a more rigorous justification for their choices, and clearly demonstrate the practical benefits of their approach. Additional comparisons with state-of-the-art inference algorithms are crucial. A more thorough discussion of the computational cost and scalability of the proposed methods is also needed.
