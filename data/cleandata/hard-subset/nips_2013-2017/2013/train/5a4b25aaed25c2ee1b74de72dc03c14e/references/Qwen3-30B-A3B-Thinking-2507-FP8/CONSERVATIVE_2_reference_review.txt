

**Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"**

This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models by projecting arbitrary (slow-mixing) parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set that guarantees rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling. The authors define fast-mixing as parameter configurations where the spectral norm of the dependency matrix is less than one, and develop four projection methods using different divergences (Euclidean norm, "zero-avoiding" KL, piecewise KL, and "zero-forcing" KL). The main technical contribution is a dual algorithm for Euclidean projection based on singular value thresholding.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with careful mathematical derivations.
- The theoretical foundation for fast-mixing conditions is sound, with appropriate references to existing literature on mixing times.
- The experimental setup is reasonable, comparing with standard variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) and Gibbs sampling on original parameters.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Insufficient empirical validation:** The experiments are limited to small graphs (8×8 grids and 10-node random graphs) and fail to demonstrate the approach's effectiveness on larger, more challenging problems. The paper claims "projecting onto the fast-mixing set generally gives better results" but provides inadequate evidence. The comparison with Gibbs sampling on original parameters is particularly weak—the paper acknowledges that with infinite time, Gibbs sampling would be more accurate, but doesn't properly analyze the time-accuracy trade-off central to the paper's motivation.

2. **Overly conservative theoretical bound:** The paper uses a spectral norm bound of 1.0 to guarantee fast mixing, but admits in practice they use 2.5 (Section 7). This suggests the theoretical bound is too conservative by a factor of ~2.5, undermining the practical relevance of their approach. The paper doesn't address why this bound is so loose or how to tighten it, which is a significant limitation given the paper's central claim about fast mixing.

3. **Limited comparison with established methods:** The paper compares with only four standard methods, omitting more recent or sophisticated approaches like mean-field with higher-order dependencies or methods specifically designed for fast-mixing. The authors fail to address whether their projection method would work with other MCMC techniques beyond Gibbs sampling.

4. **Questionable novelty:** The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing work on fast-mixing models. For instance, the paper cites [11] for the Ising model mixing time threshold but doesn't meaningfully connect their approach to this literature. The projection methods themselves appear to be minor extensions of established techniques rather than significant innovations.

5. **Superficial justification for divergence choices:** The paper presents four different divergences but provides weak theoretical arguments for why "zero-forcing" KL (which they claim works well in practice) is preferable to "zero-avoiding" KL. The explanation for the poor performance of the "piecewise" KL is superficial and doesn't address why it fails to match the true KL divergence.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a theoretically interesting idea, it lacks sufficient empirical validation and fails to convincingly demonstrate significant advantages over established methods. The conservative theoretical bound and limited experimental scope make the practical relevance of the approach unclear. The paper would need to substantially strengthen its empirical evaluation, including larger-scale experiments, more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and a thorough analysis of the time-accuracy trade-offs.

The paper's main contribution appears to be a minor technical extension of existing projection methods rather than a significant advance in the field. The authors would need to provide much stronger evidence that their approach offers meaningful advantages over current state-of-the-art methods before it would be suitable for NIPS.

**Pro/Con Summary:**
*Pro:*
- Well-structured theoretical foundation
- Clear presentation of the projection methods
- Reasonable experimental setup for small graphs

*Con:*
- Inadequate empirical validation on meaningful problem sizes
- Overly conservative theoretical bound (2.5x looser than needed)
- Limited comparison with state-of-the-art methods
- Unclear practical advantages over existing approaches
- Superficial explanation for divergence choices
