## NIPS Review: Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing

**Summary:**

This paper explores a novel approach to approximating inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically the Ising model. The core idea is to project parameters from an arbitrary distribution onto a set guaranteed to induce fast mixing in Gibbs sampling. The authors investigate several divergence measures for this projection: Euclidean distance (using SVD thresholding), KL divergence (both theoretically and via piecewise approximations), and a reversed KL divergence minimized via stochastic approximation. They demonstrate through experiments that projecting parameters can improve inference accuracy compared to standard variational methods like LBP and mean field, and often achieve competitive results with Gibbs sampling on the original (slow-mixing) parameters, but with significantly faster convergence.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The work builds upon the extensive literature on approximate inference in graphical models. It connects to mean-field inference [14], structured mean-field approaches [16, 20], and belief propagation variants [19, 21]. However, instead of approximating the distribution directly, this paper focuses on manipulating the parameters to guarantee a computationally efficient sampling procedure. The use of spectral norms to characterize mixing times builds on [4, 8, 11], and the stochastic approximation approach draws connections to Ergodic Mirror Descent [3]. The paper also appropriately situates itself within the broader landscape of variational methods [9] and relates to work on exploiting tractable substructures [15, 17].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of projecting parameters to guarantee fast mixing is a fresh perspective on approximate inference, offering an alternative to direct distribution approximation.
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The paper is firmly rooted in the theory of Markov chain mixing times, providing a clear connection between parameter properties and computational efficiency. Theorem 7 provides a concrete algorithm for Euclidean projection.
* **Multiple Divergence Measures:** Exploring several divergence measures provides a comprehensive analysis of the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost. The piecewise KL divergence is a particularly clever attempt to balance tractability with approximation quality.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, showing improvements over standard variational inference techniques and, in some cases, achieving comparable accuracy to Gibbs sampling with significantly faster convergence.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The technical concepts are explained clearly, and the notation is consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conservatism of Spectral Norm Bound:** The authors acknowledge the looseness of the spectral norm bound (1.0) compared to known critical values for specific Ising models (e.g., 0.4407 for the 2D case). Using a tighter bound could potentially improve the accuracy of the projection.
* **Computational Cost of KL Divergence Projections:** While the theoretical exploration of KL divergence projection is valuable, its practical implementation is limited by the need for intractable expectations. The stochastic approximation approach (zero-forcing KL) is a good attempt to address this, but its performance relies on careful parameter tuning (step size, pool size, etc.).
* **Limited Scalability of Piecewise KL:** The piecewise KL divergence requires selecting appropriate subgraphs, and its effectiveness likely depends heavily on this choice. The current implementation with spanning trees or simple chains might not scale well to more complex graphs.
* **Lack of Detailed Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares several methods, more detailed ablation studies could help to understand the contribution of individual components (e.g., the effect of different step sizes in the stochastic approximation).



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to approximate inference with strong theoretical foundations.
* It demonstrates significant practical benefits compared to established methods.
* It provides a thorough exploration of different divergence measures and their trade-offs.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The spectral norm bound is conservative, potentially limiting the accuracy of the projection.
* The computational cost of some of the proposed methods (e.g., KL divergence projections) can be substantial.
* Scalability to very large graphs remains a concern.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of approximate inference. The idea of projecting parameters to guarantee fast mixing is both novel and theoretically sound, and the experimental results are encouraging. While there are some weaknesses, they are outweighed by the strengths of the work. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. 

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Investigate tighter bounds on the spectral norm:** Explore alternative criteria for fast mixing that might lead to a more accurate and less conservative projection.
* **Provide more detailed computational analysis:**  Include a table summarizing the computational cost (e.g., running time, memory usage) of each projection method for different graph sizes.
* **Explore different subgraph selection strategies for the piecewise KL divergence:** Investigate more sophisticated methods for choosing subgraphs that balance tractability and accuracy.
* **Conduct ablation studies:** Analyze the sensitivity of the stochastic approximation approach to different parameter settings.
* **Clarify the connection between the spectral norm bound of 2.5 and its impact on rapid mixing.**
