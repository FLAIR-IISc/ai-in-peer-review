## Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

**Summary:**

This paper explores approximating intractable Ising model parameters by projecting them onto a set of “fast-mixing” parameters – those guaranteeing rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling. The core idea is that even if exact inference is impossible, a nearby fast-mixing model can provide a good approximation. The authors investigate several projection methods based on different divergences: Euclidean distance (using SVD thresholding), KL-divergence (theoretically interesting but intractable), a piecewise KL approximation using low-treewidth subgraphs, and a reversed KL-divergence minimized via stochastic approximation. They demonstrate experimentally that these projections, particularly those based on divergences, generally outperform standard inference methods like loopy belief propagation and mean-field in terms of marginal accuracy, albeit at a computational cost.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The idea of explicitly targeting fast mixing as a criterion for approximation is a refreshing departure from typical tractable model approximations (mean-field, tree-belief propagation etc.). It frames tractability in terms of algorithm performance (Gibbs sampling convergence) rather than structural properties of the graph.
* **Multiple Projection Methods:** The exploration of different divergence measures for projection provides a useful comparative analysis. The Euclidean projection offers a computationally efficient baseline, while the KL-divergence-based approaches aim for more accurate approximations. The piecewise KL offers a trade-off.
* **Strong Experimental Evaluation:** The paper conducts a thorough experimental evaluation on both grid and random graphs, comparing the proposed methods to several baselines, and evaluates accuracy in terms of marginal estimation error.  The inclusion of computational time as a comparison metric is a significant strength.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid grounding in Markov chain mixing times and lays out the necessary definitions and theorems.
* **Clear Writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically structured.  The motivation and key ideas are clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Looseness of Spectral Norm Bound:** The authors acknowledge this in the discussion, but the practical use of a relaxed spectral norm bound (2.5 instead of the theoretical 1.0) raises questions about the strictness of the “fast-mixing” guarantee.  A more thorough investigation of the trade-off between bound tightness and approximation accuracy would be valuable.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors demonstrate accuracy gains, the computational cost of the projection step, particularly for the KL-divergence-based methods, isn’t fully addressed. A more detailed analysis of the computational scaling is needed. The parameter tuning involved in stochastic gradient descent for the reversed KL-divergence is also acknowledged as being non-trivial.
* **Limited Scope:** The work is currently focused on the Ising model.  While the concept of fast-mixing parameters could be applicable to other graphical models, the paper doesn’t explicitly explore this generalization, limiting its broader impact.
* **Piecewise KL Performance:** The piecewise KL-divergence performs worse than the other approaches despite its attempt at balancing tractability and accuracy. Further investigation into why this is the case, and potential improvements, would be helpful.
* **Missing Related Work:** While the authors cite relevant papers, the connection to recent work on spectral learning and parameter regularization techniques for graphical models could be stronger.



**Assessment of Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theorems are correctly stated and the experimental results seem robust. However, the looseness of the spectral norm bound and the computational complexity concerns slightly diminish the overall quality. (Score: 4/5)
* **Clarity:**  The paper is well-written and the concepts are explained clearly. The mathematical notation is generally accessible. (Score: 5/5)
* **Originality:** The approach of explicitly targeting fast-mixing parameters is novel and represents a worthwhile contribution.  The specific projection methods and their comparison are also original. (Score: 4/5)
* **Significance:** The potential to improve inference accuracy in intractable graphical models is significant. The results suggest that the proposed methods could be useful in practice. However, the computational cost and limited scope need to be addressed for broader adoption. (Score: 3.5/5)

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Novel and well-motivated approach to approximate inference.
* Strong experimental results demonstrating accuracy gains.
* Solid theoretical foundation in Markov chain mixing times.
* Clear and well-written presentation.

**Against:**

* Looseness of the spectral norm bound.
*  Computational complexity of projection methods.
* Limited scope (Ising model only).
* Piecewise KL divergence's underwhelming performance.




**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **Accept with Minor Revisions**. This paper presents a novel and promising approach to approximate inference in intractable graphical models. The experimental results are encouraging, and the paper is well-written and theoretically sound. Addressing the weaknesses related to the spectral norm bound, computational complexity, and scope would significantly strengthen the contribution.  Specifically, I recommend the authors:

1.  Investigate the trade-off between the spectral norm bound and the accuracy of the approximation.
2.  Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the projection methods.
3.  Discuss potential extensions of the approach to other graphical models.
4.  Offer a deeper analysis of why the piecewise KL divergence performs poorly.
