Summary
The paper proposes using “fast-mixing” Ising models as a tractable family for approximate inference. The key idea is to project an arbitrary (possibly slow-mixing) parameter vector θ onto a set Ψ of parameters that satisfy sufficient conditions for rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs sampling, specifically a bound on the spectral norm of a dependency matrix R. For Ising models, Lemma 5 bounds Rij by tanh|βij|, leading to the sufficient condition ||R||2<1. The paper develops: (i) an Euclidean projection procedure framed via a dual (Theorem 7) and implemented with LBFGS-B, (ii) a KL(θ||ψ) projection (impractical at scale due to needing expectations under θ), (iii) a “piecewise” KL built from tractable subgraphs, and (iv) a KL(ψ||θ) projection optimized by stochastic approximation with Gibbs samples under ψ, coupled with Euclidean projection to enforce the mixing constraint. Experiments on 8×8 grids and 10-node random graphs compare to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original parameters.

Strengths
- Interesting and timely framing: replacing exact-inference-tractable families by fast-mixing families grounded in mixing-time theory. This connects MCMC theory with variational-style projections.
- The use of spectral-norm constraints on |β| to guarantee rapid mixing for univariate Gibbs is well-motivated and leverages known results (Dyer et al., Hayes).
- The KL(ψ||θ) projected stochastic optimization with sample reuse is a reasonable practical instantiation; the gradient derivation reduces to Covψ[f](ψ−θ).
- The “piecewise KL” idea is simple to compute and aligns with tree/low-treewidth approximations explored in prior work.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and rigor
- Theorem 6: S′ii = min(Sii, c2) is incorrect; the standard projection onto an operator-norm ball uses S′ii = min(Sii, c). This is a serious mathematical error.
- Definition 1 (total variation) appears incorrect; TV commonly uses 1/2 Σx|p−q|. The text shows “1” on a separate line, which is likely a typesetting error but currently reads incorrectly.
- Theorem 7 and Eq. (2): The dual objective is suspicious. The “1/||D−R||2F + Λ·Z·D” form (i) lacks a clear derivation, (ii) seems to invert a norm rather than use a standard quadratic penalty, and (iii) would be unbounded above in Λ unless additional constraints are present. The gradients (Eqs. 3–4) and notation “Λ′Z” are unclear (Hadamard product vs matrix product). Overall, the dual is not convincing as written.
- Convexity/duality: The primal includes the nonconvex constraint D=|B|. No argument is made that the problem is convex, nor that strong duality holds, yet a dual is proposed and optimized. This undermines the soundness of Theorem 7 and the projection algorithm.
- Mixing-time guarantee vs practice: The theory requires ||R||2<1 for random-scan Gibbs. The experiments use systematic scan and even relax the bound to 2.5 “in practice,” voiding the formal guarantee. If the guarantee is central to the contribution, the methodology should either adhere to it or provide new theory for the chosen setting.
- KL(ψ||θ) gradient: While the covariance-form gradient is correct, the text’s claim that “product of two unbiased estimators will also be unbiased” is generally false; and even with independence, the proposed estimator uses shared samples, which introduces bias. The text acknowledges dependence but downplays it; a more careful, unbiased (or controlled-bias) estimator and variance analysis are needed.
- Sectioning and references: The experiments cite “Section 3.2” which does not exist. References are incomplete (missing years/venues), and some notation is inconsistent (e.g., random vs systematic scan assumptions).

Methodological and experimental issues
- Evaluation inconsistency: The paper states marginals are “compared against those of the target distributions by running a Gibbs chain on each” and also that “marginals [are] computed via the exact junction-tree algorithm.” These statements conflict. Please clarify the ground truth and the estimation procedure consistently.
- Fairness of comparisons: Time–accuracy comparisons explicitly ignore the computational cost of the projection (e.g., 30k Gibbs updates for Euclidean projection), which can be substantial. This biases against baselines and does not reflect equal-compute comparisons.
- Limited scope: The experiments are on small graphs (8×8, 10 nodes) where exact inference is possible, yet the main motivation is intractable high-treewidth models. There is no assessment on larger graphs or real datasets.
- No mixing diagnostics: The core selling point is fast mixing, yet there is no empirical measurement of mixing behavior (autocorrelation, effective sample size, spectral gap proxies) or verification that the ψ chains actually mix faster than θ under the various constraints, especially when the bound is relaxed to 2.5.
- Piecewise KL uses a max over subgraphs, yielding a nonsmooth objective; no discussion of subgradients or stability. The choice of T and its coverage is ad hoc; weighting schemes or averaging could be better behaved.

Clarity and presentation
- Notation issues (e.g., “Λ′Z”) and several typographical/formatting errors impede readability.
- Important derivations (Lemma 5 extension to nonzero field, Theorem 7 dual) are relegated to an appendix or hand-waved; readers cannot verify correctness from the main text.
- The relationship to prior related work on Dobrushin conditions, Weitz/tree-uniqueness regimes, and other spectral/contractive conditions is referenced but not contrasted experimentally (e.g., how does an ℓ1-based projection compare to the spectral one?).

Pros
- Novel and appealing idea of fast-mixing projection as an alternative tractable family.
- Practical algorithmic instantiation via projected stochastic gradients for KL(ψ||θ).
- Potentially useful in MCMC-based learning/inference pipelines.

Cons
- Multiple mathematical inaccuracies and missing proofs (notably Theorem 6, Theorem 7).
- Theoretical guarantees not respected in experiments (bound relaxed; systematic scan).
- Ambiguities and inconsistencies in evaluation methodology.
- Limited empirical scope, no mixing diagnostics, and unfair compute accounting.
- Presentation/notation/refs need significant polishing.

Recommendation
Reject. The conceptual direction is interesting and could be impactful, but the paper in its current form has too many technical and methodological issues to meet the NeurIPS bar. I encourage the authors to:
- Fix the projection theorem statements (Theorem 6) and provide a correct, rigorous derivation (and convexity/duality justification) for Theorem 7, or propose an alternative sound projection method.
- Align experiments with the guarantees (random scan; enforce ||R||2<1) or provide new theory supporting the practical choices.
- Provide unbiased or well-characterized estimators for KL(ψ||θ) gradients, with variance control.
- Clarify evaluation, include mixing diagnostics, and perform compute-fair comparisons on larger graphs.
- Clean up notation, sectioning, and references.
