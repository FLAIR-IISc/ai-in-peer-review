Summary
This paper proposes using “fast-mixing” parameter sets as a tractable family for approximate inference in Ising models. The key sufficient condition is that the spectral norm of a dependency matrix is below 1 (via Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Hayes bound gives Rij ≤ tanh|βij|). Given intractable parameters θ, the authors project to a fast-mixing ψ under several divergences: (1) Euclidean projection via an SVD-thresholding dual (Theorem 7), while preserving sparsity; (2) KL(θ||ψ) (“zero-avoiding”)—theoretically appealing but impractical because expectations under θ are intractable; (3) a novel piecewise KL formed by projecting onto tractable subgraphs and taking the max over them; (4) KL(ψ||θ) (“zero-forcing”), optimized with stochastic approximation using Gibbs on ψ (fast-mixing), with a gradient that involves the Fisher information. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare Euclidean, piecewise, and KL(ψ||θ) projections against LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original parameters; the claim is that the proposed methods yield more accurate marginals than variational baselines, and for finite time can outperform Gibbs on original parameters.

Strengths
- Interesting and well-motivated perspective: using fast-mixing regions as the tractable set, rather than low treewidth.
- The dual Euclidean projection that aims to respect sparsity is potentially useful; the idea to project under ||·||2 constraints on |B| as a proxy for fast mixing is natural.
- The connection between KL(ψ||θ) minimization and mean-field (as an extreme case with zero spectral norm) is insightful; the gradient expression using Covψ[f] (ψ−θ) is correct and useful.
- The piecewise KL construction is simple and leverages tractable substructures.
- The overall approach relates to and slightly generalizes several familiar ideas (mean-field, tree approximations, planar decompositions) in a fresh way.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness/clarity
- Theorem 6: For projection onto the spectral norm ball, the singular values should be thresholded at c, i.e., S′ii = min(Sii, c). The manuscript states “c 2”, which appears incorrect (and dimensionally inconsistent).
- Theorem 7 and Eq. (1): The optimization introduces D = |B| and enforces ||D||2 ≤ c and sparsity via Z⊙D = 0, but it is unclear how the signs of B are handled when minimizing ||A−B||F given the constraint on D=|B|. Is there a provably optimal way to recover signs (e.g., sign(Bij)=sign(Aij))? This is not specified. The dual notation uses operators like Λ'Z, Z ' D, triple dot product, Πc[·] without cleanly defining ’ (Hadamard or transpose?), and Πc on nonnegative matrices. Overall, the construction is under-specified and hard to reproduce without the appendix.
- Convexity: It is not discussed whether {B: || |B| ||2 ≤ c, Z⊙B=0} is convex in B. Projection by SVD thresholding of |B| may not be a projection onto a convex set in B; convergence guarantees for projected gradient descent then do not apply.
- Section references/typos: “Section 3.2” is referenced but the paper has no 3.2; several typos (“KL-divergnce”, “makes makes”, “systematical scan”), inconsistent terminology (“zero forcing” vs. “zero-forcing”), and notational ambiguities (e.g., Λ'Z). A footnote marker “1” appears in Section 5.3 with no footnote. These reduce clarity.
- Reversed KL gradient: While Theorem 8 matches the known form ∇ψ KL(ψ||θ) = Covψ[f](ψ−θ), the justification for unbiasedness is misstated: “product of two unbiased estimators is unbiased” is generally false. The estimator in Eq. (8) can be unbiased only if one uses independent samples to estimate μ and the covariance term; using the same pool introduces bias. The text acknowledges this but the theoretical claim is imprecise.
- Mixing regime mismatch: The guarantees require ||R||2 < 1 (random scan). Experiments use systematic scan and, more importantly, choose a spectral norm bound 2.5 “which still preserves rapid-mixing” without theoretical support or empirical diagnostics. This undermines the core “guaranteed fast mixing” premise.

Experimental design/reporting
- The experimental section is under-specified and lacks quantitative results (no tables/plots, no error bars, no runtime accounting). Claims like “more accurate than variational approximations” are unverifiable from the writeup.
- The comparison to Gibbs on original parameters does not normalize for computational effort; projection steps themselves can be very expensive (30k Gibbs iterations plus repeated Euclidean projections), yet this overhead is not counted in time-accuracy plots.
- No measurement of mixing time or effective sample size is provided to support “fast mixing” in practice, especially with the relaxed spectral bound c=2.5. Without d(t) curves or autocorrelation analyses, it is unclear whether the approximations really achieve rapid mixing.
- Hyperparameters for stochastic approximation (step size, pool size, number of iterations) are chosen ad hoc “without intensive tuning”; no sensitivity analysis or convergence diagnostics are reported.
- The piecewise KL setup: choice of subgraphs is plausible, but there is no ablation on which families (trees vs width-2) matter, nor an analysis of the non-smooth max over subgraphs and its optimization stability.
- Reversed KL optimization is nonconvex; no convergence guarantees or empirical convergence behavior are discussed.

Related work and positioning
- The relationship to Dobrushin/Weiss bounds and other sufficient conditions is mentioned, but there is limited discussion of how tight the spectral-norm-of-|β| condition is in general graphs (beyond the 2D Ising critique), or whether alternative norms could improve tightness without sacrificing tractability.
- Connections to structured mean-field/convex free energy approximations (e.g., TRW, CCCP/CVM) are only briefly touched; it would help to contrast the proposed family with those, both theoretically and empirically.

Formatting/references
- References are incomplete (missing years/pages/venues). Several citations are not precisely mapped to statements (e.g., [13] is about “extra updates delay mixing,” which does not justify “fast mixing of univariate Gibbs is sufficient for some other methods”).
- Numerous minor formatting and notation inconsistencies.

Pros
- Novel perspective: projection into fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractable family.
- Potentially useful dual projection idea for Euclidean distance with sparsity.
- Correct and useful gradient identity for KL(ψ||θ).

Cons
- Many technical imprecisions and unclear derivations; important projection details are missing.
- Experimental evidence is sparse, lacks quantitative rigor, and does not support the “guaranteed fast mixing” claim (especially with c=2.5).
- Notational/formatting issues and incorrect statements (Theorem 6 threshold, unbiasedness claim) reduce confidence.

Recommendation: Reject
While the core idea is promising and could be impactful with a solid theoretical and empirical treatment, the current manuscript has too many technical gaps, unclear notation/derivations, and insufficient experimental evidence. I encourage the authors to: (1) fix Theorem 6 and fully detail/prove Theorem 7 with precise notation and a reproducible algorithm, including how to recover B from D; (2) clarify convexity and projection properties; (3) either respect the theoretical mixing bound in experiments or provide empirical mixing diagnostics justifying relaxed bounds; (4) report comprehensive quantitative results with runtime normalization and sensitivity analyses; (5) correct the estimator bias discussion; and (6) clean up formatting, references, and section cross-references.

Confidence: High.
