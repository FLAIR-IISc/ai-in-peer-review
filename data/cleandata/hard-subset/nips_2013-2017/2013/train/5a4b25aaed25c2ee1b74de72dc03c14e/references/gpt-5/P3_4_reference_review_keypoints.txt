- Proposes fast-mixing Ising models as a tractable approximation family and studies projections under four divergences (Euclidean, inclusive KL, piecewise KL, reverse KL).  
- Uses a spectral-norm bound on the dependency matrix R (with R_ij ≤ tanh(|β_ij|)) and ||R||2 < 1 as the sufficient condition for rapid Gibbs mixing.  
- Main technical contribution: Euclidean projection via a dual formulation that enforces a spectral-norm constraint on |B| while preserving sparsity using SVD thresholding.  
- Inclusive KL(θ||ψ) is theoretically natural but impractical because it requires expectations under the original (possibly slow-mixing) θ.  
- Piecewise KL computes exact KL on low-treewidth subgraphs, extending piecewise likelihood ideas to divergences, but subgraph-choice effects are underexplored.  
- Reverse KL(ψ||θ) optimized via stochastic approximation using samples from ψ plus Euclidean projected gradient, viewed as expanding mean-field to a larger fast-mixing family.  
- Theory is grounded in standard mixing-time results but contains unclear/typo-prone details (e.g., Theorems 6–7) and missing derivation steps that impede reproducibility.  
- The Euclidean projection dual is interesting and practical, but notation and some formula signs/constants appear incorrect or undefined in the text.  
- Optimization: gradient for reverse KL is correct (Cov_ψ(f,(ψ−θ)·f)) and the persistent-sample scheme is reasonable, but experiments do not follow the theoretical step-size/chain-length schedules.  
- Experiments compare to LBP, TRW, naive mean-field, and Gibbs on small graphs (8×8 grids and small random graphs) using exact junction-tree marginals for evaluation.  
- Experimental issues: the spectral constraint is relaxed to 2.5 (invalidating guarantees), no empirical mixing-time diagnostics are reported, and the relaxation rationale is not fully justified.  
- Comparisons are not normalized by total compute or wall-clock time, and convergence-vs-time curves across methods are not sufficiently reported.  
- Piecewise KL evaluation is limited; richer subgraph families (e.g., bounded-width junction trees) and systematic study of coverage effects are missing.  
- Results are limited to small graphs, so practical impact and scalability remain untested.  
- Clarity: paper is generally well-written with good literature placement, but projection dual notation, cross-references, and scan-type (random vs systematic) distinctions need fixing.  
- Originality: the fast-mixing projection family and the dual/stochastic projection methods are novel and a valuable new axis for approximation beyond low-treewidth approaches.  
- Significance: conceptually promising, but practical impact is tempered by conservative theoretical conditions, lack of guarantees when relaxed, and limited empirical validation.  
- Strengths: introduces a fresh approximation family, provides a nontrivial sparsity-preserving projection algorithm, and connects mixing-time theory to practical inference methods.  
- Weaknesses/suggestions: report empirical mixing diagnostics, consider projecting in influence (tanh(|β|)) space to reduce conservativeness, align optimization schedules with theory, normalize comparisons by compute, explore richer subgraphs, and fix theorem typos/complexity details.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel concept with technical depth and promising empirical hints; potential utility beyond this work.  
- Cons for acceptance: theory–practice mismatch in experiments, reproducibility/presentation issues, and limited/unfair experimental evaluation.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject; encourage revision addressing theory–practice alignment, clarity, and stronger empirical evaluation.
