**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel framework for approximate inference in graphical models, specifically focusing on the Ising model. The core idea is to redefine the notion of a "tractable" approximating distribution. Instead of using structurally simple models (e.g., fully factorized for mean-field, or low-treewidth for structured mean-field), the authors propose using "fast-mixing" models. These are models whose parameters satisfy conditions that guarantee rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling to the stationary distribution. For the Ising model, the paper leverages the known result that if the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths is less than one, the model is fast-mixing.

The main contribution is a method to "project" the parameters of a given, potentially slow-mixing, Ising model onto this set of fast-mixing models. The authors explore this projection under four different divergences:
1.  **Euclidean Distance:** They derive a novel and efficient dual algorithm (Theorem 7) to find the closest fast-mixing parameter set in the Frobenius norm, while preserving the graph's sparsity structure. This involves iteratively thresholding singular values.
2.  **KL(q||p) ("zero-avoiding"):** This is presented as theoretically ideal but computationally intractable for general graphs, as it requires computing expectations over the original slow-mixing distribution `q`.
3.  **Piecewise KL:** A novel, tractable approximation to KL(q||p), where the KL divergence is calculated over a set of low-treewidth subgraphs (e.g., spanning trees or chains).
4.  **KL(p||q) ("zero-forcing"):** This divergence is more practical, as it requires expectations over the fast-mixing distribution `p`, which can be approximated via Gibbs sampling. The authors propose minimizing this using a projected stochastic gradient descent method, where the projection step utilizes their Euclidean projection algorithm. This is elegantly framed as a generalization of mean-field inference.

Experimentally, the proposed methods (particularly the KL(p||q) projection) are shown to produce more accurate marginal estimates than standard variational methods like Loopy Belief Propagation, Tree-Reweighted BP, and Naive Mean-Field on grid and random graphs.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The central idea of using fast-mixing dynamics, rather than structural simplicity, to define the family of tractable distributions is highly original and conceptually significant. It opens up a new and promising avenue for approximate inference, moving beyond the traditional trade-offs of variational methods. This is a fresh perspective that could inspire considerable follow-up work.

2.  **Solid Technical Contribution:** The derivation of the Euclidean projection algorithm (Theorem 7) is a strong technical result. It provides an efficient way to solve a non-trivial constrained optimization problem that is the core building block for the more advanced KL-based projections.

3.  **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper does an excellent job of positioning its work relative to the existing literature. The framing of the KL(p||q) projection as a generalization of mean-field (where the spectral norm constraint is relaxed from 0 to <1) is particularly insightful and helps ground the new idea in a familiar context.

4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate that the proposed approach, especially the KL(p||q) projection, consistently outperforms widely-used variational baselines in terms of marginal accuracy. This provides compelling evidence for the practical utility of the method.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice:** The most significant weakness is the discrepancy between the theoretical guarantee and the experimental implementation. The theory guarantees fast mixing for a spectral norm `|| |β| ||_2 < 1`, but the experiments are run with a bound of `c = 2.5`. While the authors acknowledge this in the discussion, it is a critical point that undermines the paper's central premise. If the chosen model is not provably fast-mixing, the method loses its primary theoretical justification. The paper would be much stronger if it either used `c < 1` or provided an empirical/theoretical justification for why `c=2.5` still leads to rapid convergence in practice (e.g., by showing empirical mixing plots).

2.  **Limited Scope (Ising Models):** The entire technical development is specific to the Ising model, where a simple bound on the dependency matrix in terms of `tanh|β_ij|` exists. The paper mentions extending to general MRFs as future work but does not discuss the substantial challenges involved. This makes the current contribution somewhat narrow.

3.  **Performance of Piecewise KL:** The "piecewise KL" is introduced as a novel divergence, but it is summarily noted that it "does not perform as well." The experiments seem to confirm this. While it is good that the authors are honest about this, it weakens this part of the contribution. More analysis on *why* this approximation is not effective would be valuable.

4.  **Optimization Details:** The stochastic gradient descent for KL(p||q) involves several hyperparameters (step size, sample pool size, Gibbs steps per iteration) that can significantly impact performance. The paper states these were chosen "without intensive tuning," which makes the results less reproducible and raises questions about the sensitivity of the method to these choices.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea of approximating with fast-mixing models is highly novel and has the potential to be very influential.
*   The paper provides a strong technical contribution with the Euclidean projection algorithm.
*   The experimental results are strong, showing clear improvements over established baselines.
*   The work is well-written, well-motivated, and clearly situates itself within the broader field of approximate inference.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The use of a spectral norm bound of 2.5 in experiments, which violates the theoretical condition for guaranteed fast mixing, is a major flaw that needs better justification.
*   The contribution is currently limited to Ising models, and its generalizability is unclear.

**Recommendation:**

Despite the weaknesses, particularly the disconnect between theory and experiments, the novelty and potential impact of the core idea are very high. This is the kind of creative, thought-provoking work that NIPS should encourage. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the issue of the spectral norm bound in the final version.

**Detailed Comments for the Authors:**

*   Regarding the spectral norm bound `c=2.5`: This is the most critical point. Could you please add an experiment showing how marginal accuracy and empirical mixing time vary as a function of `c`? This would help justify your choice and show whether the theoretical bound is simply too loose or if you are operating outside the "guaranteed" fast-mixing regime.
*   The Piecewise KL is an interesting idea. Could you provide more intuition on why it might be underperforming? Is it because the `max` operator makes the objective too unstable, or is the approximation fundamentally flawed?
*   In Section 5.4, you mention that using the same samples to estimate both `µ̂` and the gradient makes little difference. While this is often true in practice, it would be good to add a brief justification or citation to support this, as it technically introduces bias into the gradient estimate.
*   The discussion on the looseness of the spectral norm bound for the 2D Ising model is excellent and shows a good understanding of the literature. This self-awareness strengthens the paper.
