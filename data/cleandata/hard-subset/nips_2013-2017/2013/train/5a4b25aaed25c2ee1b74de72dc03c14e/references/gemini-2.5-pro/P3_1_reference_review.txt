This paper introduces a novel and interesting approach to approximate inference in Ising models by defining a new class of "tractable" distributions. Instead of relying on structural properties like low treewidth, the authors define tractability based on dynamic properties, specifically whether the model is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing" for single-site Gibbs sampling. The core idea is to take an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing, Ising model and project its parameters onto the set of fast-mixing models. The paper grounds the notion of fast-mixing in established MCMC theory, using the condition that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix (bounded by the absolute value of the interaction matrix, `|β|`) is less than a constant (`|| |β| ||_2 < 1`).

The main contributions are threefold:
1.  A novel algorithm (Theorem 7) to project an Ising parameter matrix onto the set of sparse matrices with a bounded spectral norm under the Euclidean norm. This is a non-trivial technical result that forms the building block for other methods.
2.  The extension of this projection framework to more probabilistically meaningful divergences, most notably the "zero-forcing" KL-divergence `KL(ψ||θ)`. This is optimized using projected stochastic gradient descent, where the gradient is estimated via sampling from the fast-mixing approximation `ψ`, and the projection step uses the algorithm from (1).
3.  An empirical demonstration on grid and random graphs that this projection approach, particularly with the `KL(ψ||θ)` divergence, outperforms standard variational methods like Mean Field, LBP, and TRW, and provides a better time-accuracy trade-off than running Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing model.

### Strengths

*   **Originality & Significance:** The central idea of using "fast-mixing" as the criterion for a tractable approximate family is highly original and significant. It reframes the problem of approximate inference from a static, structural perspective to a dynamic, algorithmic one. This could open up a new line of research in variational inference where the choice of the approximating family `Q` is motivated by the efficiency of sampling algorithms, which is a very practical and relevant concern. The connection drawn to mean-field (as a projection onto the zero spectral norm set) is insightful and elegantly situates the work within the existing literature.

*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the dual optimization for the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a solid contribution. The use of projected stochastic gradient descent for the KL divergence is a principled and appropriate optimization strategy. The experimental setup is well-designed, comparing against strong and relevant baselines on standard graph structures. The results convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. It provides sufficient background on mixing times and dependency matrices to make the work self-contained. The motivation for each of the four divergences is clearly explained, along with their respective computational trade-offs.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

1.  **Looseness of the Mixing Time Bound:** The primary weakness, which the authors commendably acknowledge in the discussion, is the conservativeness of the `||R||_2 < 1` bound. For the 2D Ising grid, this condition is famously loose. The authors' empirical choice to use a bound of 2.5 is a pragmatic solution that yields better results, but it undermines the theoretical "guarantee" of fast mixing that is central to the paper's motivation. It would strengthen the paper to provide more discussion or empirical evidence on this point. For instance, for the chosen bound of 2.5, can the authors show empirical mixing plots (e.g., potential scale reduction factor or trace plots) to demonstrate that the resulting models are indeed fast-mixing in practice, even if not strictly guaranteed by Theorem 4? This would help justify the choice of 2.5 and bridge the gap between the theory and practice.

2.  **Computational Complexity of the Projection Step:** The core projection algorithm (Theorem 7) is solved via L-BFGS-B, where each step involves computing `D(Λ,M)`, which in turn requires a singular value decomposition (SVD) of an `n x n` matrix. An SVD has a complexity of `O(n^3)`. This projection is performed at every iteration of the outer stochastic gradient descent loop. This suggests the overall method may not scale well to large graphs with many variables. A discussion of the computational complexity and potential scalability limitations would be a valuable addition. For larger problems, would it be feasible to use randomized SVD or other approximation techniques for the projection step?

3.  **Details of Stochastic Optimization:** The performance of the `KL(ψ||θ)` minimization can be sensitive to the hyperparameters of the stochastic optimization (step size, sample pool size, number of Gibbs updates per iteration). While the authors report the parameters used, the paper would be more complete with a brief discussion of their sensitivity. For example, how does the final accuracy change with a smaller sample pool or fewer iterations? This would give readers a better sense of the algorithm's robustness. The reference to Ergodic Mirror Descent is good, but the practical implementation seems to be a simpler variant; clarifying the connection or any deviation would be helpful.

4.  **Piecewise KL Divergence:** The formulation of the piecewise KL divergence using a `max` over subgraphs is interesting but not fully motivated. It implies that the gradient is determined entirely by the single subgraph with the highest KL divergence. An alternative, perhaps more common in related literature (e.g., composite likelihood), would be to use a sum or average of the KL divergences over the subgraphs. Is there a specific reason (theoretical or practical) for choosing `max` over summation?

### Summary and Recommendation

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Proposes a genuinely novel and compelling framework for approximate inference based on fast-mixing dynamics.
*   Provides a solid, non-trivial technical algorithm for the core projection problem.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance against established and relevant baselines.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and opens up interesting avenues for future research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The theoretical guarantee of fast mixing is weakened by the practical need to use a looser, empirically chosen bound.
*   Potential scalability issues due to the reliance on SVD are not addressed.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The conceptual contribution is significant and thought-provoking, and it is backed by sound technical work and convincing experiments. The weaknesses primarily represent opportunities for future work rather than fatal flaws. I recommend **acceptance** for this paper. It is a high-quality contribution that will be of great interest to the NIPS community working on graphical models and approximate inference.
