This paper introduces a novel and compelling perspective on approximate inference in graphical models. The central idea is to shift the definition of a "tractable" model from one with a low-treewidth structure (e.g., trees, junction trees) to one whose parameters guarantee fast mixing of Gibbs sampling, regardless of its treewidth. For a given intractable Ising model, the authors propose projecting its parameters onto this set of fast-mixing models. The paper makes several key contributions: (1) It formalizes the "fast-mixing" set for Ising models using a known spectral norm condition on the interaction matrix. (2) It derives a non-trivial dual algorithm for projecting parameters onto this set under the Euclidean norm, which cleverly handles sparsity constraints and the absolute value in the condition. (3) It extends this projection framework to more probabilistically meaningful divergences (a novel piecewise KL and the standard reversed KL), using the Euclidean projection as a subroutine in a projected stochastic gradient descent scheme. (4) It provides a thorough experimental comparison against standard variational methods and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters, demonstrating the practical utility of the approach.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance):**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The core idea of using dynamic properties (fast mixing) rather than static structural properties (low treewidth) to define the approximating family is a significant conceptual contribution. It opens up a new avenue for research in approximate inference that is fundamentally different from the dominant variational paradigms. This fresh perspective is precisely the kind of work that NIPS should champion. The results are likely to inspire follow-up work on applying this principle to other models, learning algorithms, and MCMC methods.

2.  **Strong Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the Euclidean projection in Theorem 7 is a solid piece of optimization work. The formulation of a dual problem to handle the challenging constraints (`|||B|||₂ ≤ c` and sparsity) is elegant. Furthermore, the way this projection is leveraged within a projected stochastic gradient descent framework to handle KL-divergences is methodologically sound and well-explained. The connection drawn to mean-field inference as a special case (where the spectral norm is constrained to zero) is insightful and helps position the work within the existing literature.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation and background, carefully builds the theoretical foundation for the fast-mixing set, presents its core technical contribution (the Euclidean projection), and then logically extends this to other divergences. The experimental section is well-designed, with appropriate baselines and clear metrics for evaluation. The authors are honest and clear about the limitations of their approach, particularly in the discussion section.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive. The proposed methods are compared against strong and relevant baselines (LBP, TRW, MF). The inclusion of "Gibbs on original parameters" is crucial, as it allows for a direct comparison of the trade-off being made: sacrificing asymptotic accuracy for guaranteed rapid convergence. The experiments on both grid and random graph structures with varying interaction strengths demonstrate the robustness of the findings. The results compellingly show that for a finite computational budget, sampling from the projected fast-mixing distribution yields more accurate marginals than sampling from the original slow-mixing one.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Looseness of the Fast-Mixing Bound:** A key issue, which the authors commendably acknowledge, is the potential looseness of the spectral norm bound (`|||β|||₂ < 1`). In their experiments, they find that a much looser bound (`c = 2.5`) yields better results while still appearing to mix quickly. This raises a critical question for the practitioner: how should one choose the projection threshold `c`? The "guarantee" of fast mixing is tied to the theoretical value, but the best practical performance is achieved with an empirically chosen value that lacks this guarantee. This weakens the central premise of *guaranteed* fast mixing, making the choice of `c` a sensitive hyperparameter. More discussion on the sensitivity to `c` or a more principled way to select it would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Scalability of the Projection:** The core projection algorithm (Theorem 7) relies on an iterative SVD, which has a complexity of O(n³) for an n-node graph. While solved in the dual with L-BFGS-B, the per-iteration cost is still dominated by the SVD. This may limit the applicability of the method to very large-scale problems where `n` is in the many thousands or more. A brief discussion of the computational complexity and its practical limitations would be a valuable addition.

3.  **Scope Limited to Ising Models:** The entire technical development is specific to the pairwise binary Ising model. While this is an important case study, the paper's title and introduction suggest a more general principle. Generalizing to general discrete MRFs would require deriving new bounds on the dependency matrix and a new projection algorithm, which the authors note is a non-trivial challenge. While this does not detract from the quality of the current contribution, it does limit its immediate applicability.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a highly original and significant contribution to the field of approximate inference. The central idea is creative, the technical execution is solid, and the experimental results are convincing. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the practical application and extension of the method rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed approach. The paper is well-written, honest about its limitations, and is likely to stimulate new research. It clearly meets the high standards for publication at NIPS.
