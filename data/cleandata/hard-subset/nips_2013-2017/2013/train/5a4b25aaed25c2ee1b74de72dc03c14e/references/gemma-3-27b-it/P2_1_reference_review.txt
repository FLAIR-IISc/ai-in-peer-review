## Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

**Summary:**

This paper explores a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically the Ising model. The core idea is to project parameters of an intractable distribution onto a set of “fast-mixing” distributions – those guaranteed to converge quickly under Gibbs sampling. The authors investigate several divergences for performing this projection, including Euclidean distance (with a computationally efficient iterative thresholding algorithm), KL divergence (both theoretically and via piecewise approximations), and a reversed KL divergence amenable to stochastic approximation.  They demonstrate experimentally that projecting parameters can lead to improved marginal accuracy compared to standard variational methods and, in some cases, offer a time-accuracy trade-off advantage over direct Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing parameters.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of explicitly projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set is a fresh perspective on approximate inference. It moves beyond simply approximating the distribution itself and focuses on ensuring efficient sampling.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The paper is well-grounded in the theory of Markov chain mixing times and provides rigorous analysis for the Euclidean projection via singular value decomposition.  The discussion of dependency matrices and spectral norms is clear and connects to established results.
* **Multiple Divergence Exploration:**  Investigating a variety of divergences – Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL – provides a comprehensive view of the trade-offs between computational cost, accuracy, and theoretical properties. The stochastic approximation approach for the reversed KL divergence is particularly clever.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments, while relatively small-scale, demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed methods compared to established alternatives like LBP, TRW, and naive mean-field. The comparison with Gibbs sampling, both on original and projected parameters, is insightful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making the core ideas accessible.  The notation is consistent and the mathematical derivations are presented clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conservatism of Spectral Norm Bound:** As the authors themselves acknowledge, the spectral norm bound of 1.0 for guaranteeing fast mixing appears overly conservative. The empirically observed performance with a higher bound (2.5) suggests room for improvement in the theoretical understanding. This limits the applicability of the method if more aggressive bounds aren’t explored.
* **Computational Complexity:** The stochastic approximation approach for the reversed KL divergence, while promising, involves significant tuning of hyperparameters (step size, pool size, Gibbs updates). The authors admit limited tuning in their experiments.  A more thorough exploration of parameter sensitivity and scalability is needed.
* **Limited Scalability & Graph Size:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small graphs (8x8 grids and 10-node random graphs). It’s unclear how well the proposed methods will scale to larger, more complex graphical models.  The computational cost of the SVD-based Euclidean projection may become prohibitive for very large parameter matrices.
* **Piecewise KL Divergence Performance:** The piecewise KL divergence, despite its potential for tractability, doesn't perform as well as the other methods in the experiments. This suggests that the choice of subgraphs (horizontal and vertical chains in the grid case) significantly impacts performance and requires careful consideration.
* **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons to standard variational methods are provided, a comparison to more recent and advanced inference techniques (e.g., those leveraging deep learning) would strengthen the evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable and original contribution to the field of approximate inference. The idea of projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set is conceptually appealing and theoretically sound.  The exploration of multiple divergences and the development of computationally efficient algorithms (especially the SVD-based Euclidean projection and the stochastic approximation for the reversed KL divergence) are significant achievements.  While the experimental evaluation is limited in scale, it provides encouraging evidence of the benefits of the proposed approach.  

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty and theoretical grounding.**
* **Potential to improve accuracy and efficiency of inference.**
* **Well-written and clearly presented.**
* **Addresses a significant problem in probabilistic modeling.**

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* **Conservatism of spectral norm bound.**
* **Limited scalability and computational complexity.**
* **Piecewise KL divergence does not perform well.**
* **Comparison to state-of-the-art is lacking.**

**Rating:**

**Strong Accept (7/10).**  The paper presents a strong contribution with clear novelty and theoretical support.  The identified weaknesses are not fundamental flaws and could be addressed in future work. The potential impact of this research is substantial, and it merits inclusion in NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily focusing on a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability limitations, and exploration of tighter bounds for the spectral norm.  Addressing the points made in the "Discussion" section of the paper (i.e. investigating tighter bounds and the use of more sophisticated inference techniques for comparison) would significantly strengthen the contribution.
