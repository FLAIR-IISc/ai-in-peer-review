**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a new approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth Ising models. Instead of approximating an intractable distribution with a structurally simple one (e.g., fully-factorized or tree-structured), the authors propose approximating it with a "fast-mixing" distribution. A distribution is defined as fast-mixing if its Gibbs sampler is guaranteed to converge rapidly to the stationary distribution. For Ising models, this property is linked to a sufficient condition: the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths, `|| |β| ||₂`, must be less than one.

The core contribution is a set of methods to "project" a given slow-mixing Ising model onto this fast-mixing set. The authors explore four divergences for this projection:
1.  **Euclidean distance:** They derive a dual algorithm based on iterative singular value thresholding to find the closest parameter set in the fast-mixing set, while preserving sparsity.
2.  **"Zero-avoiding" KL(θ||ψ):** This is acknowledged as being of theoretical interest only, as it requires intractable expectations over the original slow-mixing distribution.
3.  **Piecewise KL:** A novel, heuristic approximation to the KL-divergence computed over a set of tractable subgraphs (e.g., trees or low-treewidth chains).
4.  **"Zero-forcing" KL(ψ||θ):** This is the main practical probabilistic method. Since it involves expectations over the fast-mixing distribution ψ, it can be minimized via stochastic gradient descent, where each step involves a Euclidean projection onto the fast-mixing set.

Experiments on small grid and random graphs show that the proposed methods, particularly the reverse KL projection, can yield more accurate marginals than standard baselines like Loopy Belief Propagation, Tree-Reweighted BP, and Naive Mean Field, and can outperform Gibbs sampling on the original parameters given a limited time budget.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The idea of defining a tractable family based on dynamic properties (fast mixing) rather than static structural properties (low treewidth) is original and thought-provoking. It opens a new perspective on approximate inference.
2.  **Technical Contribution:** The derivation of the Euclidean projection algorithm (Theorem 7) that handles sparsity constraints appears to be a solid technical contribution. This algorithm serves as a key building block for the more sophisticated divergence-based methods.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It clearly presents the background, motivates the problem, derives the methods, and outlines the experimental setup.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Significant Disconnect between Theory and Practice:** The paper's entire theoretical foundation rests on the guarantee of rapid mixing when the spectral norm of the dependency matrix is less than one (`||R||₂ < 1`). However, in the experiments (Section 6.1), the authors state: "we find in practice using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of 1.0 can still preserve the rapid-mixing property and gives better approximation". This is a major issue. By using a bound of 2.5, the central theoretical guarantee of the paper is abandoned. The method is no longer projecting onto a *provably* fast-mixing set, but rather performing a form of spectral norm regularization. The authors must either provide theoretical justification for the new bound or reframe the paper as a study on spectral norm regularization for improving MCMC, rather than a principled projection onto a guaranteed tractable class.

2.  **Limited and Potentially Misleading Empirical Evaluation:**
    *   The experiments are conducted on very small graphs (8x8 grids and 10-node random graphs). It is unclear how the method's performance and computational cost scale to larger, more realistic problems.
    *   The cost of the projection itself, which involves repeated SVDs within an L-BFGS or SGD loop, is not thoroughly analyzed or compared against the runtime of the baselines. The paper notes that the projection time is not included in the Gibbs sampling comparison, which makes it difficult to assess the true computational trade-offs.
    *   The stochastic optimization for KL(ψ||θ) has several hyperparameters (step size, sample pool size, iterations) that were admittedly not tuned. This makes the reported results potentially fragile and hard to reproduce or build upon. High-quality empirical work at NIPS requires more robust parameter sensitivity analysis.

3.  **Weakness of the Theoretical Bound:** As the authors honestly discuss in Section 7, the `||R||₂ < 1` condition is known to be very conservative. For the 2D Ising grid, the true phase transition occurs at `βc ≈ 0.44`, while the spectral norm bound requires `β < 0.25`. This raises a significant concern about the utility of the approach: if the guarantee of fast mixing only applies to models with very weak interactions where inference is already relatively easy, the practical impact may be limited. The fact that the experiments required relaxing this bound to 2.5 reinforces this weakness.

4.  **Limited Novelty of Proposed Divergences:** While the framework is new, the components are familiar. The reverse KL projection (Section 5.4) is methodologically very similar to variational inference, with the primary novelty being the definition of the constraint set `Ψ`. The proposed "piecewise KL divergence" is a heuristic that the paper admits "does not perform as well as the true KL-divergence" and lacks strong theoretical justification for its quality as an approximation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The core idea of using fast-mixing sets as a basis for approximation is novel and could inspire future work.
*   The derivation for the Euclidean projection is technically sound and could be a useful tool in other contexts.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The critical disconnect between the theoretical guarantee (`||R||₂ < 1`) and the experimental implementation (`||R||₂ < 2.5`) undermines the paper's central claim of projecting onto a *provably* fast-mixing set.
*   The empirical evaluation is performed on small-scale problems and lacks a rigorous analysis of computational costs and hyperparameter sensitivity, making the results less convincing.
*   The underlying theoretical bound is acknowledged to be loose, questioning the practical applicability of the approach in regimes where approximate inference is most needed.

**Conclusion**

While the paper presents a conceptually novel and interesting idea, the execution and validation fall short of the high standards expected for NIPS. The fundamental contradiction between the theoretical motivation and the experimental implementation is a critical flaw. Without this guaranteed link to fast mixing, the method becomes a heuristic for spectral norm regularization, which is less novel and requires a much more extensive empirical study to justify its benefits over established techniques. The limited scale of the experiments and the lack of a proper accounting for the projection's computational cost further weaken the paper's contribution. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
