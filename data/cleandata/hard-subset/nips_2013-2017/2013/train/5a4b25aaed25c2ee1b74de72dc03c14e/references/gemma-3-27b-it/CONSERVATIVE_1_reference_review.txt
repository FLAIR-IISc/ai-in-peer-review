This paper explores approximating intractable distributions with “fast mixing” distributions, specifically within the context of the Ising model. The core idea – projecting parameters onto a set guaranteeing rapid Gibbs sampling convergence – is interesting, but the execution and justification fall short of NIPS standards. The authors investigate several divergence measures for this projection, including Euclidean distance and variations of KL-divergence. 

The introduction adequately positions the work within the broader landscape of approximate inference, referencing relevant techniques like mean-field and loopy belief propagation. However, the motivation for focusing on “fast mixing” as a notion of tractability feels somewhat arbitrary. While rapid mixing is desirable, the paper doesn’t convincingly argue *why* this specific constraint should be prioritized over existing approaches. It feels like re-framing a convergence issue as a tractability issue without substantial gain.

The technical development (Sections 3 & 4) is largely sound, relying on established results regarding mixing times and spectral norms. Theorem 7, providing an algorithm for Euclidean projection, is a positive contribution. However, the practical implications of this theorem are limited; the resulting matrix is dense, negating any potential sparsity benefits. The exploration of different divergences is a good attempt to broaden the applicability, but the theoretical justification for some choices (e.g., the “zero-avoiding” KL) is weak, acknowledging its intractability. The piecewise KL divergence is a potentially promising direction, but its performance lags behind other methods in the experiments. The zero-forcing KL divergence, framed as a generalization of mean-field, offers a reasonable approach, but the stochastic approximation method relies heavily on parameter tuning, which isn’t thoroughly addressed.

The experimental section (Section 6) is a major weakness. The configurations tested (8x8 grids and small random graphs) are relatively simple. More importantly, the comparison to established inference algorithms (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs sampling) lacks sufficient detail. While the authors state their methods outperform variational approaches, the margin of improvement isn’t clearly demonstrated. The tuning of the stochastic approximation algorithm for the zero-forcing KL divergence isn’t justified and feels ad-hoc, hindering a fair comparison. The assertion that the spectral norm bound of 2.5 is sufficient for rapid mixing, despite being looser than the established theoretical threshold (βc ≈ 0.4407), undermines the rigor. The reasoning provided is unconvincing – simply observing better empirical performance doesn't justify loosening a theoretically derived bound. The performance comparisons lack statistical significance testing, making it difficult to definitively assess whether observed differences are meaningful.

**Strengths:**

*   The idea of leveraging fast-mixing properties for approximate inference is conceptually interesting.
*   Theorem 7 provides a concrete algorithm for Euclidean projection.
*   The exploration of different divergence measures demonstrates a breadth of investigation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The motivation for focusing on fast mixing as a notion of tractability is weak.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope and lacks statistical rigor.
*   The loosening of the spectral norm bound is unjustified and undermines the theoretical foundation.
*   Parameter tuning for the stochastic approximation method is not adequately addressed.
*   The practical benefits of the projection algorithms are not convincingly demonstrated.




**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The paper lacks a compelling justification for the proposed approach. It doesn't clearly articulate *why* fast mixing is a superior constraint compared to existing methods.
*   The experimental results are not conclusive and don’t demonstrate a significant improvement over established inference algorithms.
*   The theoretical justification is weakened by the unjustified loosening of the spectral norm bound.
*   The paper feels incomplete – further investigation into the piecewise KL divergence and a more rigorous experimental evaluation are needed.

**Arguments for acceptance (weak):**

*   The idea of projecting onto fast-mixing distributions is novel and could potentially lead to future research.
*   Theorem 7 is a potentially useful technical result, although its practical applicability is limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents some interesting ideas, its weaknesses significantly outweigh its strengths. The lack of a compelling motivation, limited experimental validation, and questionable theoretical justification render it unsuitable for NIPS. The paper would benefit from a more focused scope, a more rigorous experimental evaluation, and a stronger theoretical foundation.
