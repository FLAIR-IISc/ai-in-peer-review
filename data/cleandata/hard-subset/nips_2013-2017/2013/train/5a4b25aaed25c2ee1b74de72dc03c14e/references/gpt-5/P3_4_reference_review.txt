Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a new “tractable family” for approximate inference: the set of fast-mixing Ising models, defined via sufficient parameter-space conditions guaranteeing rapid convergence of univariate Gibbs sampling. The central sufficient condition used is a spectral-norm bound on the dependency matrix R, with R_ij ≤ tanh(|β_ij|) for Ising models. The authors study projections of arbitrary (possibly slow-mixing) Ising parameters θ onto the fast-mixing set Ψ under four divergences: (i) Euclidean (l2) distance on parameters, solved via a dual formulation that leverages SVD thresholding while preserving sparsity; (ii) inclusive KL(θ||ψ), noted as theoretically natural but impractical since it requires expectations under θ; (iii) a piecewise KL based on low-treewidth subgraphs where exact KL can be computed; and (iv) reverse KL(ψ||θ), optimized with stochastic approximation using samples from ψ combined with Euclidean projected gradient steps, viewing this as an expansion of mean-field (spectral norm zero) to a larger fast-mixing family.

Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare these projections to LBP, TRW, naive mean-field, and Gibbs on the original model, reporting marginal errors versus exact junction-tree marginals. The authors also discuss time–accuracy trade-offs and acknowledge that the spectral norm condition used in theory is conservative; they empirically relax the bound (to 2.5) to obtain better approximations while still observing fast mixing.

Quality:
- Theory: The mixing-time condition via ||R||2 < 1 is standard (Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum; Hayes) and the Ising-specific bound R_ij ≤ tanh(|β_ij|) is appropriate. The Euclidean projection algorithm is the most technical contribution: the dual construction that enforces a spectral-norm constraint on |B| while respecting sparsity is interesting and practically relevant. However, several details are unclear or possibly incorrect/typo-prone: in Theorem 6, S′_ii = min(S_ii, c 2) looks like a typo and should be min(S_ii, c). In Theorem 7, notation for Λ ' Z (Hadamard product?) is not explicitly defined, the sign/constant factors in g(Λ,M) seem odd (1/||D−R||^2 rather than −(1/2)||D−R||^2), and the transition from primal (Eq. 1) to the dual lacks detail in the main text (relegated to appendix). These issues impede reproducibility.
- Optimization for KL(ψ||θ): The gradient expression equals Cov_ψ(f, (ψ−θ)·f), which is correct. The stochastic approximation that uses a persistent “pool” of samples and one Gibbs sweep per iteration is reasonable, and the link to ergodic mirror descent is appropriate. However, the algorithmic guarantees cited (step-size and chain-length schedules) are not matched in the experiments (constant step, fixed pool, one sweep).
- Experiments: The setups are standard; evaluation via exact JT is sound. But fairness and completeness are limited: (a) the relaxed spectral constraint (2.5) breaks the fast-mixing guarantee; no empirical mixing-time diagnostics or acceptance criteria are reported; (b) comparisons do not normalize by total compute; (c) the impact of subgraph choices for the piecewise KL is only lightly explored; (d) results are on small graphs.

Clarity:
Overall well-written with a clear narrative and good placement within the literature (mean-field, structured MF, TRW, planar/zero-field planar exact inference). Notation around the projection dual could be clarified; some cross-references appear off (e.g., “Section 3.2” is not present). The mixing condition is presented plainly, but the paper sometimes switches between random scan (theoretical bound) and systematic scan (experiments) without quantifying the gap.

Originality:
The core idea—to use fast-mixing distributions as an approximation family and to project parameters onto a set defined by mixing conditions—is novel and interesting. The dual Euclidean projection with sparsity constraints and the stochastic projection framework for reverse KL are new combinations of known tools. The piecewise KL idea extends earlier piecewise likelihood ideas to divergences, although it is less compelling empirically.

Significance:
Conceptually, the paper opens a new axis for approximation: contractive dynamics rather than low treewidth. This is a useful addition to the toolkit and could affect both inference and learning (e.g., MLE with MCMC gradients constrained to fast-mixing sets). However, the practical impact is tempered by (i) conservative conditions leading to potentially poor approximations unless relaxed; (ii) lack of guarantees once relaxed; and (iii) limited experimental scope.

Strengths:
- Introduces “fast-mixing projection” as a tractable approximation family distinct from tree-structured approximations.
- A concrete dual algorithm for Euclidean projection that maintains sparsity and spectral constraints.
- Stochastic projected optimization for reverse KL that generalizes mean-field.
- Solid grounding in mixing-time theory; appropriate citations.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Theory–practice mismatch: using ||R||2 bound of 2.5 invalidates the guarantee. Suggest reporting empirical mixing diagnostics (e.g., spectral gap estimates, coupling times, auto-correlation decay) and the observed spectral norms to characterize when relaxation is safe.
- Tightness: you bound R_ij by |β_ij| though tanh(|β_ij|) is tighter; could you project directly in the “influence” space (i.e., enforce constraint on tanh(|β|) or use a monotone mapping/majorization) to reduce conservativeness?
- Update scheme: Theoretical convergence of ergodic mirror descent requires increasing chain lengths or decreasing steps; provide sensitivity analyses, or adopt a schedule with at least modest theoretical backing.
- Fair comparisons: normalize by total compute or report wall-clock/GPU hours; include convergence curves for marginal error vs time for all methods, including “Gibbs on original.”
- Piecewise KL: explore richer subgraph families (e.g., junction-tree with bounded width via min-fill), and report how coverage affects accuracy; compare to TRW’s tree mixtures more carefully.
- Clarity issues: fix typos in Theorems 6–7 and define all operators; provide a complexity analysis of the dual LBFGS-B loop and SVD calls on sparse matrices; clarify systematic vs random scan in theory/experiments.
- Related work: briefly discuss stronger uniqueness/mixing regions (e.g., Weitz self-avoiding-tree bounds) and block Gibbs mixing conditions; comment on whether block-wise constraints could yield less conservative projections.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually fresh approximation family with clear theoretical basis.
- Technically nontrivial projection method with potential utility beyond this paper.
- Empirical indications that fast-mixing projections can outperform standard variational baselines at fixed time.

Cons for acceptance:
- Theoretical guarantees are not maintained in experiments; no empirical mixing validation.
- Presentation issues and possible typos in key theorems hinder reproducibility.
- Limited experimental scope and fairness; modest gains and no large-scale validation.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising and could have impact, but the paper needs cleaner theory-to-practice alignment, clearer algorithms, and stronger empirical evaluation. I encourage a revision addressing the above points. Confidence: high.
