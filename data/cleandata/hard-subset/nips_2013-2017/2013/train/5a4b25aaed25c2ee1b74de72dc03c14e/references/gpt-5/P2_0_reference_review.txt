Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a new “tractable family” for approximate inference: distributions that satisfy parameter-space sufficient conditions for rapid mixing of single-site Gibbs sampling. In the Ising case, the sufficient condition is that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix (upper-bounded by the spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix) is less than one. Given an intractable model, the authors “project” its parameters onto this fast-mixing set under several divergences: (a) Euclidean distance, solved via a dual approach combining singular value thresholding with sparsity constraints; (b) inclusive KL KL(q||p), only feasible on small graphs; (c) a novel “piecewise” KL based on exact KL on tractable subgraphs; and (d) reverse KL KL(p||q), optimized by stochastic approximation using samples from the fast-mixing model (framed as Ergodic Mirror Descent). Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare the projected models to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original parameters, reporting improved marginal accuracy over variational baselines and time–accuracy trade-offs vs Gibbs.

This sits alongside classic tractable families (mean-field, structured mean-field, tree-based/TRW, planar zero-field approximations) and MCMC-based approximations. It is conceptually close to expanding the mean-field family (zero spectral norm) to a larger set with guaranteed rapid mixing, and connects to work on mixing-time bounds (Dobrushin, spectral norm sufficient conditions).

Quality
- Theory: The use of the spectral norm bound for rapid mixing is standard and appropriate. The derivation of Euclidean projection via a dual with singular value thresholding is a concrete technical contribution aimed at preserving sparsity while enforcing an operator-norm constraint on |β|. However, there are several issues that need correction/clarification:
  - Theorem 6 appears to have a typographical error: projecting onto the operator-norm ball should threshold singular values at c, not at c^2. Please clarify.
  - In Theorem 7, the objective g has formatting inconsistencies (e.g., “1 / ||D−R||^2_F” appears to be 1/2||D−R||_F^2; gradients use a “′” operator that is not defined; the notation Λ'Z vs Λ·Z·D is ambiguous). These hinder verification and reproducibility.
  - The dependency bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is reasonable, but the translation from constraints on R to constraints on |β| is only sufficient; this is acknowledged, but it limits how tight the method can be.
- Algorithms: The projected gradient scheme for other divergences and the stochastic estimator for reverse KL are sensible, and the connection to Ergodic Mirror Descent is appropriate. That said, convergence guarantees under approximate gradients and projections are not established (beyond citation), and the choice of step size, sample pool size, and number of Gibbs steps is ad hoc.
- Experiments: The experimental section shows promise but contains inconsistencies that need to be addressed for the results to be convincing:
  - The text first states that marginals are computed via exact junction-tree, yet later says marginals are estimated from a single Gibbs chain; exact JTA is infeasible on 8×8 grids. This contradiction must be resolved.
  - The projection stage can be computationally expensive (e.g., “30,000 total Gibbs iterations” plus repeated projections), yet computational effort during projection is not accounted for when comparing to LBP/TRW/MF. This biases time–accuracy comparisons.
  - The authors enforce a spectral norm bound of 2.5 in practice, while the theory requires <1 for a guarantee. This undermines the claimed guarantees; if looser thresholds are used, empirical diagnostics of mixing and failure cases should be reported.

Clarity
The paper is generally well motivated and the big picture is clear. However, notation glitches (Theorem 6/7), undefined operators, and the contradictory description of how marginals are computed significantly reduce clarity. More explicit algorithms (pseudocode), clear statement of projection inputs/outputs, and a precise definition of Z, Λ, M and the operators would help. Details of experimental setup (numbers of sweeps, burn-in, seeds, stopping criteria, and whether projection cost is counted) should be spelled out.

Originality
- Conceptually novel: using “fast mixing” as a tractability notion, and projecting parameters onto that set, is a fresh angle distinct from standard variational families.
- Technical novelty: the dual Euclidean projection preserving sparsity under an operator-norm constraint on |β| is a neat contribution; the “piecewise KL” is a plausible, if not fully successful, new surrogate; framing reverse-KL projection onto fast-mixing families as a generalization of mean-field is insightful.

Significance
The idea could influence approximate inference by offering a family that trades structural tractability for dynamical tractability. If made robust and scalable, this could be useful in practice: guaranteed-rapid mixing samplers with better fidelity than MF, and a principled way to tune parameters toward that set. However, significance is limited by:
- The looseness of the spectral-norm sufficient condition, which the authors acknowledge; using a threshold >1 to improve accuracy removes guarantees. Quantifying how often the method is truly fast-mixing at different thresholds would be valuable.
- Small-scale experiments and unclear runtime accounting; it remains uncertain how this compares to modern baselines at realistic scales.
- The piecewise KL approximation underperforms, and inclusive KL is largely impractical—so the main practical path is reverse KL with sampling.

Pros
- Clear, interesting new notion of tractability; good motivation.
- Concrete algorithmic contribution for Euclidean projection with sparsity.
- Reverse-KL projection via stochastic approximation is practical and connects to MF.
- Empirical results suggest improved marginal accuracy over standard variational methods in tested settings.

Cons
- Theoretical guarantees hinge on a conservative condition; experiments relax it, voiding guarantees.
- Typos/notation errors in key theorems; unclear derivations.
- Experimental methodology has inconsistencies (JTA vs Gibbs), small scale, and fairness/runtimes not carefully controlled.
- No empirical mixing diagnostics to validate “fast mixing” after projection.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix Theorem 6/7 statements and provide complete, unambiguous derivations (or move full proofs to appendix with precise notation).
- Report empirical spectral norms and actual mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, coupling time proxies) across thresholds; study the trade-off between norm bound and both accuracy and mixing.
- Make time–accuracy plots that include projection cost; compare to well-tuned LBP/TRW/MF and, if possible, structured mean-field and planar decompositions.
- Clarify the evaluation protocol (exactly how marginals are computed on each graph type) and ensure reproducibility.
- Explore tighter sufficient conditions (e.g., Dobrushin with 1-norm, block updates) or adaptive local bounds to reduce conservatism.

Overall assessment
Interesting and potentially impactful idea with a solid starting point, but the current presentation has theoretical and experimental clarity issues, and the guarantees are not borne out in practice as implemented. I would rate this as borderline: promising but in need of revision to meet NeurIPS standards for rigor and reproducibility.
