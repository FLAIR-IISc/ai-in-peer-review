- Novel approach: project Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set to guarantee rapid Gibbs sampling instead of restricting graph structure.
- Key theoretical condition: for Ising models, a sufficient rapid-mixing condition is that the spectral norm of the interaction matrix is < 1.
- Core contribution: algorithms to project parameters onto the fast-mixing set using four divergences, with zero-forcing KL (KL(ψ||θ)) as the most practical via stochastic approximation.
- Relation to prior work: complements tractable-subgraph approaches (Hazan & Shashua, Globerson & Jaakkola) and applies mixing-time theory to inference approximation in a novel way.
- Theoretical novelty: Lemma 5 and Theorem 4 provide a clear foundation for parameter-space tractability.
- Practical algorithms: elegant dual for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) and a workable stochastic method for zero-forcing KL.
- Empirical evaluation: comprehensive comparisons (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs) across grid and random graphs using marginal accuracy; shows projection often outperforms variational methods for limited computation.
- Trade-off clarity: demonstrates projected fast-mixing parameters give better finite-budget results, while original Gibbs would be better asymptotically.
- Conservative mixing bound: spectral-norm condition is much stricter than known 2D Ising critical thresholds (e.g., βc ≈ 0.44 vs. spectral bound β = 0.25).
- Unjustified practical choice: use of a spectral bound of 2.5 in experiments lacks theoretical motivation and undermines consistency.
- Piecewise KL limitation: piecewise approximation is easy to evaluate but underperforms true KL without sufficient explanation or analysis.
- Scalability concerns: experiments limited to small graphs; SVD-based dual projection is O(n^3) and may not scale to realistic sizes.
- Incomplete related work: should connect better to recent mixing-time results (e.g., Lubetzky & Sly) and fast-mixing conditions for other MRFs/Potts models.
- Recommendation and revisions: accept with minor revisions — justify the 2.5 spectral bound, tighten mixing conditions for specific graphs, test larger graphs and analyze piecewise KL limitations, and clarify relation to other mixing criteria (e.g., Dobrushin).
- Final assessment: Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 7/10; recommended for NIPS acceptance with minor revisions.
