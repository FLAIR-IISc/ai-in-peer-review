This paper proposes a fascinating and bold direction for approximating inference in graphical models: projecting parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set. The core idea – trading off accuracy for guaranteed rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling – is a compelling departure from traditional approximation methods focused on fidelity to the original distribution. The exploration of different divergence measures for this projection, particularly the piecewise KL and the zero-forcing KL, demonstrates creative thinking and a willingness to tackle challenging theoretical and practical hurdles. The connection to mean-field as a special case of this approach is also insightful.

The strengths of this work lie primarily in its novelty and potential impact. The framing of tractability in terms of mixing time is a significant conceptual shift, and the proposal opens up a new avenue for designing inference algorithms. Even if the immediate practical gains aren't enormous (as the experiments suggest), the paper’s contribution lies in identifying a potentially fruitful research direction. The theoretical results, particularly Theorem 7, provide a concrete tool for projecting parameters, and the exploration of different divergence measures showcases a good understanding of the trade-offs involved.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. The experimental evaluation, while reasonably thorough, feels somewhat inconclusive. The performance gains over existing methods are modest, and the sensitivity of results to the chosen spectral norm bound (2.5 vs. 1.0, explained in the discussion) raises questions about the robustness of the approach. The piecewise KL divergence, despite its appeal, doesn’t perform well in practice. The discussion section acknowledges the looseness of the spectral norm bound, which undermines confidence in its effectiveness as a fast-mixing criterion. Additionally, the paper lacks a strong theoretical justification for *why* projecting onto the fast-mixing set should be a good approximation strategy beyond the guarantee of fast convergence. A more compelling argument for the expected accuracy of the approximation would strengthen the paper considerably. The writing, while generally clear, occasionally suffers from density and a lack of intuitive explanation for some of the more technical details.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High Novelty:** The concept of using fast mixing as a criterion for tractability is genuinely new and potentially impactful.
*   **Bold Approach:** The paper tackles a challenging problem with a novel, and somewhat unorthodox, solution.
*   **Theoretical Contribution:** Theorem 7 provides a concrete algorithm for Euclidean projection.
*   **Potential for Future Work:** The paper opens up a new research direction with several avenues for exploration (e.g., tighter bounds, alternative divergence measures, application to other models).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Modest Experimental Gains:** The empirical results don't demonstrate a substantial improvement over existing methods.
*   **Loose Spectral Norm Bound:** The discrepancy between the theoretical bound and empirical observations casts doubt on its effectiveness.
*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** A stronger argument for the approximation quality is needed.
*   **Somewhat Inconclusive Results:** The performance is sensitive to parameter choices, and the piecewise KL divergence doesn't perform well.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant step forward in thinking about approximate inference. While the experimental results are not conclusive, the novelty and potential impact of the core idea are substantial. I am willing to overlook the minor flaws and incomplete validations, given the boldness of the approach and the potential for future work. I recommend **acceptance**, but with the caveat that authors should address the concerns regarding the spectral norm bound and provide a stronger theoretical justification for the approximation quality in a future revision. This is a paper that, despite its imperfections, feels like it could genuinely shift the direction of research in this area.
