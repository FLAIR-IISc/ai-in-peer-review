- Summary: Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set (interaction spectral norm < 1) and performing inference using Euclidean, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences, leveraging mixing-time theory instead of low-treewidth structure.

- Originality (strength): Introduces a novel tractability notion—fast-mixing parameter space—distinct from prior low-treewidth or mean-field approaches.

- Technical quality (strength): Strong theoretical foundations (Theorems 4–8), an elegant Euclidean projection via dual optimization, and a comprehensive experimental setup across graphs and methods.

- Significance (strength): Shows practical gains—better marginals under finite compute than standard variational methods and improved performance over Gibbs sampling on slow-mixing parameters.

- Clarity (strength): Well-organized, consistent notation, clear background on mixing times, and sufficiently detailed experiments for reproducibility.

- Conservative bound (weakness): The spectral-norm condition (||R||₂ < 1) is overly conservative compared to known model-specific thresholds (e.g., 2D Ising), limiting tightness.

- Computational complexity (weakness): Piecewise KL needs tractable subgraph selection and reversed KL requires careful stochastic-gradient tuning; overall computational costs are not fully analyzed.

- Limited scope (weakness): Focused solely on Ising models with only brief remarks on general MRF extensions and no developed theory for broader classes.

- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, well-motivated idea with sound theory, comprehensive experiments, and a meaningful link between mixing-time theory and practical inference.

- Arguments against acceptance: Practical applicability limited by conservative bound, Ising-only focus, and incomplete analysis of computational overhead.

- Recommendation: Recommend acceptance, with a request for more discussion of the conservative spectral-norm bound (e.g., 2D Ising threshold) and a more detailed analysis of computational costs.
