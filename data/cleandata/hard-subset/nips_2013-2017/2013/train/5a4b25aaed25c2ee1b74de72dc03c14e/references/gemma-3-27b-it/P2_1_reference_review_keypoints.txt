- Proposes projecting intractable Ising model parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set to enable efficient Gibbs sampling.  
- Explores multiple divergences for projection: Euclidean (with SVD-based algorithm), KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL (stochastic approximation).  
- Theoretical foundation connects dependency matrices, spectral norms, and mixing-time guarantees; provides rigorous analysis for Euclidean projection.  
- Novel idea: focus on modifying parameters for fast mixing rather than directly approximating the distribution.  
- Empirical results on small graphs show improved marginal accuracy over LBP, TRW, and mean-field, and favorable time-accuracy trade-offs versus Gibbs on original parameters.  
- Writing and organization are clear; notation and derivations are well presented.  
- Spectral norm bound of 1.0 for guaranteed fast mixing is likely overly conservative; empirical success with higher bounds suggests need for tighter theory.  
- Stochastic approximation for reversed KL requires substantial hyperparameter tuning (step size, pool size, Gibbs updates) and was only lightly tuned in experiments.  
- Experiments are small-scale (8x8 grids, 10-node graphs), leaving scalability and performance on larger models unclear.  
- SVD-based Euclidean projection may be computationally prohibitive for very large parameter matrices.  
- Piecewise KL performed poorly in experiments, indicating sensitivity to subgraph choices and limited practical utility as implemented.  
- Lacks comparison to more recent state-of-the-art inference methods (e.g., deep-learning-based approaches).  
- Overall assessment: original, theoretically grounded contribution with promising algorithms (SVD projection and stochastic reversed KL) but limited experimental scale.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, strong theoretical grounding, potential accuracy/efficiency gains, clear presentation, and relevance to probabilistic modeling.  
- Arguments against acceptance: conservative spectral bound, computational complexity and scalability concerns, weak piecewise KL results, and missing comparisons to advanced methods.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept (7/10) with minor revisions to address complexity/scalability discussion and to explore tighter spectral bounds.
