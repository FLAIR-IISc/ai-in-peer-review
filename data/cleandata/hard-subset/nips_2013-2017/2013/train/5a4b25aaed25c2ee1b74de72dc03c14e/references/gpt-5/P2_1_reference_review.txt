Summary
The paper proposes a new “tractable family” for approximate inference in Ising models: the set of parameter vectors that guarantee fast mixing of (single-site) Gibbs sampling. For Ising models with pairwise weights β and fields α, they use a sufficient condition for rapid mixing based on the dependency matrix R and the spectral norm bound ||R||2 < 1, with a simple bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|. They then study projections of an arbitrary, possibly slow-mixing parameter vector θ onto the fast-mixing set Ψ under several divergences: (i) Euclidean distance, for which they derive a dual solution (Theorem 7) that enforces both a spectral-norm constraint and sparsity via SVD thresholding (Theorem 6) and an LBFGS-B outer loop; (ii) inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ), mainly of theoretical interest; (iii) a piecewise KL that computes exact KL on tractable subgraphs; and (iv) reverse KL KL(ψ||θ), optimized via stochastic approximation with samples from ψ, combined with Euclidean projection steps—interpretable as a generalization of mean-field (MF) from fully-factorized families to fast-mixing ones. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare these projections against LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on original parameters, reporting marginal errors (using exact JT for ground truth) and some time–accuracy trade-offs.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: framing “tractability” via fast-mixing parameter regimes and projecting onto that set is a fresh perspective that bridges MCMC mixing theory with variational-style projection.
- Technical contribution: a concrete dual algorithm (Theorem 7) to project onto a spectral-norm-bounded, sparsity-constrained set for Ising interactions, using SVD thresholding in a principled way; and a stochastic projection approach for reverse KL minimizing over a nonconvex set.
- Clear connections to established methods: shows mean-field as a degenerate special case (R=0), relates to Dobrushin and spectral norm criteria, and to piecewise/structured approximations.
- Empirical scope: evaluates multiple divergences (Euclidean, piecewise, reverse KL) against standard baselines on both grids and random graphs, with exact JT ground truth for these sizes.

Weaknesses
- Guarantees vs practice mismatch: although the theoretical selling point is guaranteed rapid mixing under ||R||2<1, the experiments explicitly use a much larger bound (2.5 rather than 1.0) “which still preserves rapid-mixing in practice.” This undermines the main guarantee and weakens the narrative. If guarantees are relaxed, this becomes another heuristic projection without theoretical mixing assurances.
- Conservativeness of the bound: the spectral-norm condition is known to be very conservative for grids (e.g., threshold β<0.25 vs the critical ~0.44), acknowledged in the discussion. The paper would benefit from tighter, structure-aware bounds or experimental evidence that projections under tighter norms still yield useful approximations.
- Algorithmic clarity/complexity: Theorem 7’s dual and the use of Πc via SVD thresholding are elegant, but practical complexity is not analyzed. Repeated SVDs on dense matrices may become costly; sparsity is enforced via constraints, yet Πc itself densifies. No runtime/scale characterization is provided.
- Experimental clarity: The description of how marginals are obtained is inconsistent: at one point it mentions running Gibbs on each model; elsewhere it says marginals are computed via exact junction tree. Please clarify what is used for evaluation (JT ground truth is feasible for 8×8 grids; Gibbs should be used only for approximate optimization).
- Piecewise KL: The “max over subgraphs” choice is plausible but non-smooth; it is unclear why max (vs, e.g., average or weighted sum) is preferred, whether it upper/lower bounds the true KL, and how sensitive results are to the chosen set of subgraphs.
- Limited baselines/ablations: Natural comparisons missing include projection under Dobrushin’s l1 bound (which is cheaper to enforce), and ablations on the spectral norm threshold c (accuracy vs mixing time and whether chains actually mix fast across settings).

Quality
- The theoretical parts build on known mixing-time results and standard matrix norm facts; the bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is reasonable but proof details are deferred. The dual in Theorem 7 is interesting; however, differentiability/subgradient issues of Πc and convergence guarantees of the outer optimization are not discussed. The reverse-KL gradient estimator acknowledges bias/variance trade-offs; citing Ergodic Mirror Descent is appropriate, but concrete convergence or sample complexity guidance is absent.
- Empirical claims are plausible (projected models outperform MF/LBP/TRW in some regimes; reverse-KL > Euclidean > piecewise), but quantitative results, sensitivity analyses, and runtime/mixing diagnostics are missing in this summary. Using c=2.5 contradicts the guarantee-oriented framing.

Clarity
- Overall well-written and well-organized, with useful background on mixing times. Some notation (e.g., the use of ' for Hadamard products, triple-dot product) could be defined more explicitly in the main text. The evaluation section needs to resolve the Gibbs-vs-JT inconsistency and provide clearer details on subgraph choices and parameter schedules.

Originality
- The high-level idea—projection onto a fast-mixing set as the tractable family—is novel and interesting. The dual projection for spectral-norm-and-sparsity constraints appears new in this context. The piecewise KL idea echoes prior piecewise likelihood/training, but its use for projection into fast-mixing models is a new angle.

Significance
- Potentially impactful as a new lens for approximate inference, especially if tighter fast-mixing criteria or better projections can be developed. As presented, significance is moderate: the guarantees are weakened in practice; scalability and robustness remain to be demonstrated; yet the idea can spur follow-up work and may be useful in learning scenarios constrained to fast-mixing regions.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Report actual mixing diagnostics for ψ (autocorrelation, spectral gaps proxy, or total variation bounds where possible), especially when using c>1. Without this, the “fast-mixing” premise is not substantiated.
- Compare against projection under the Dobrushin l1-norm bound and report accuracy/runtime trade-offs.
- Provide complexity and runtime for the Euclidean projection (number/size of SVDs, LBFGS-B iterations), and discuss scalability beyond n≈100.
- Clarify evaluation: are ground-truth marginals computed by JT for all settings? When are Gibbs estimates used?
- Justify the choice of max over subgraphs in piecewise KL and assess sensitivity to T (trees vs low-treewidth graphs).
- Include ablations on c and show how accuracy and mixing change.
- Minor: define Hadamard product notation clearly; ensure references are complete.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and timely perspective on tractability via fast mixing.
- Technical contribution (dual spectral-norm projection with sparsity) is interesting.
- Empirical indications that projections can outperform standard variational methods.

Arguments against acceptance
- Central guarantee is not upheld in experiments (c>1), diluting the contribution.
- Limited analysis of computational complexity and scalability.
- Experimental methodology needs clarification and stronger diagnostics.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising, and with stronger empirical validation of mixing guarantees, tighter bounds, and clearer experiments, this could be a solid contribution. As is, the mismatch between theory and practice and limited analysis reduce confidence.
