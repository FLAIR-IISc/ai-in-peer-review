This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models. Instead of approximating an intractable distribution with one from a structurally simple family (e.g., fully-factorized for mean-field or tree-structured for loopy belief propagation), the authors propose using a family of "fast-mixing" distributions. The key idea is to define a set of tractable models as those for which Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to converge rapidly. Based on prior work, the authors use a sufficient condition for fast mixing in Ising models: the spectral norm of the absolute value of the interaction matrix must be less than one. The main contribution is a set of algorithms for "projecting" a given, potentially slow-mixing, set of Ising parameters onto this fast-mixing set using different divergence measures.

The paper is well-written and the core idea is original. However, the work suffers from several significant weaknesses in its methodological rigor and experimental validation, which call into question its practical significance and soundness compared to established techniques.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality:** The conceptual framing of "tractability" in terms of MCMC mixing time, rather than structural properties like treewidth, is a genuinely novel and interesting perspective in the context of variational inference.
2.  **Technical Contribution:** The derivation of an algorithm for projecting parameters onto the fast-mixing set under the Euclidean norm (Theorem 7) appears to be technically sound and is a non-trivial contribution.
3.  **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper does a good job of situating the work relative to existing literature, and the observation that minimizing the reverse KL-divergence KL(ψ||θ) can be seen as a generalization of mean-field is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Abandonment of Theoretical Guarantees:** The most critical weakness of the paper lies in the disconnect between its theoretical motivation and its practical implementation. The entire framework is built upon the promise of projecting onto a set of parameters *guaranteed* to be fast-mixing, defined by the condition `|| |B| ||_2 < 1`. However, in Section 6.1, the authors state: "we find in practice using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of 1.0 can still preserve the rapid-mixing property and gives better approximation". This decision, while perhaps pragmatically motivated, fundamentally undermines the paper's central claim. The method is no longer a principled projection onto a provably fast-mixing set, but rather a heuristic regularization of the parameter space. Without the theoretical guarantee, the approach loses much of its appeal and it becomes unclear what properties the resulting approximation truly holds. The discussion in Section 7 acknowledging the looseness of the bound does not sufficiently justify abandoning it entirely in the experiments.

2.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is not comprehensive enough to convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed methods over well-established baselines.
    *   **Scale:** The experiments are conducted on small 8x8 grids and 10-node random graphs. These are settings where exact inference is tractable and the challenges of high-treewidth models are not fully represented. The performance on larger, more realistic problems remains an open question.
    *   **Unfair Comparison:** The authors explicitly state, "Note that this does not take into account the computational effort deployed during projection." This is a major flaw in the evaluation. The primary motivation for this work is computational—to enable fast inference via sampling. To ignore the potentially substantial pre-computation cost of the projection itself renders the time-accuracy comparison with Gibbs sampling on the original parameters incomplete and potentially misleading. A fair comparison must measure accuracy against total wall-clock time, including both projection and sampling.
    *   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The stochastic gradient algorithm for minimizing KL(ψ||θ) involves several hyperparameters (step size, sample pool size, iterations). The authors state these were chosen "without intensive tuning." This raises concerns about the robustness of the results. Established methods like LBP are largely parameter-free and their performance is more predictable.

3.  **Unaddressed Computational Cost:** The paper fails to analyze the computational complexity of the proposed projection algorithms. The Euclidean projection method (Theorem 7) involves solving a dual optimization problem with L-BFGS that requires, at each step, a Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to compute `D(Λ,M)`. For a graph with `n` nodes, this is computationally expensive (`O(n^3)`) and likely intractable for the very large-scale problems where approximate inference is most critical. This cost may far exceed that of running many iterations of standard message-passing algorithms. Without this analysis, the practical viability of the approach is difficult to assess.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   Presents a novel and intellectually stimulating idea of using MCMC mixing time to define a tractable family for approximation.
    *   Provides a sound technical derivation for the Euclidean projection algorithm.

*   **Arguments for rejection:**
    *   The experimental implementation discards the theoretical guarantee that forms the foundation of the paper, reducing the method to a heuristic.
    *   The experimental evaluation is performed on small-scale problems and uses an unfair comparison methodology that ignores the cost of the projection step.
    *   The computational complexity of the proposed projection algorithms is not analyzed and is likely to be prohibitive for large graphs, limiting the method's practical significance.
    *   The robustness of the stochastic optimization procedure is not established.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an intriguing new idea, it is more of a position paper than a complete piece of work. The gap between the theoretical motivation and the empirical execution is too large, and the validation provided is insufficient to demonstrate a clear and practical advantage over established, well-understood approximate inference methods. The core premise is undermined by the ad-hoc choice of the spectral norm bound in experiments, and the lack of a rigorous computational cost analysis and fair empirical comparison prevents a clear assessment of its utility. Therefore, I cannot recommend this paper for acceptance in its current form.
