This paper explores projecting Ising model parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set to accelerate Gibbs sampling. The core idea – leveraging parameter constraints to improve MCMC convergence – is interesting and potentially valuable. The authors investigate several divergence measures for this projection, including Euclidean distance, KL divergences (both standard and “zero-avoiding”), and a piecewise approximation. While the theoretical framework is reasonably well-developed, particularly regarding the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7), and the experiments demonstrate some benefit, the paper suffers from a proliferation of minor, yet persistent, flaws that ultimately detract from its overall impact.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of targeting a “fast-mixing” parameter space as a means of accelerating inference is a worthwhile investigation.  The piecewise KL-divergence offers a pragmatic approach to approximating intractable divergences.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The derivation of the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is mathematically sound and builds on established results regarding spectral norm constraints.  The connection to existing work on mixing times (Dyer et al., Hayes) is clearly established.
* **Experimental Evaluation:** The authors compare their methods to a reasonable set of baselines (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs) on standard Ising model configurations.
* **Clear Motivation:** The introduction provides a solid contextualization of the problem, outlining the limitations of existing approaches to inference in high-treewidth graphical models.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Overly Detailed Focus on Minor Issues:** The paper is riddled with minor inconsistencies and stylistic issues that, while individually small, collectively create a sense of carelessness. For example, inconsistent notation (e.g., use of both θ and β to represent parameters) and minor grammatical errors abound. The discussion of the LBFGS-B algorithm in the proof of Theorem 7 feels tacked on and insufficiently motivated.
* **Looseness of Spectral Norm Bound:** The authors acknowledge that their empirically chosen spectral norm threshold of 2.5 is significantly more permissive than the known critical value for the 2D Ising model (~0.44). This undermines the claim of “guaranteed fast mixing” and raises concerns about the effectiveness of the projection.  The justification for using a larger bound (“can still preserve the rapid-mixing property”) is weak.
* **Insufficient Justification for Parameter Choices:** The choice of step size, sample pool size, and number of iterations in the stochastic gradient descent algorithm for the zero-forcing KL-divergence is largely arbitrary. While the authors mention "intensive tuning," no details of this tuning process are provided.
* **Lack of Theoretical Analysis for Piecewise KL:** The theoretical properties of the piecewise KL-divergence are not thoroughly investigated.  The impact of the choice of subgraphs (trees, low-treewidth graphs) on the quality of the approximation is not adequately addressed.
* **Repetitive Discussion of KL-Divergence Challenges:** The difficulties in computing the standard KL-divergence are reiterated multiple times without offering substantial new insights.
* **Formatting and Presentation:**  Several equations are not properly aligned, and the reference list could be formatted more consistently.  The use of abbreviations (e.g., “MRFs”) without prior definition is unhelpful.
* **Missing Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions the computational effort involved in projection, a more detailed analysis comparing the cost of projection versus the cost of long Gibbs chains would be valuable.

**Specific Concerns:**

* **Definition 1 (Total Variation Distance):** The summation should be over all possible values of x, not just a generic 'x'.
* **Theorem 4:** The condition ||R||2 < 1 is sufficient, but not necessary, for fast mixing. The authors should acknowledge this.
* **Section 5.2:** The statement that minimizing KL(θ||ψ) leads to exact marginal estimates is misleading. It leads to *better* estimates, but not necessarily exact ones, especially with finite samples.
* **Section 6.1:** The phrasing "attractive interactions" for uniformly drawn parameters from unif(0, de) is unconventional and potentially confusing.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The core idea of projecting onto a fast-mixing set is novel and could lead to improvements in MCMC-based inference.
* The theoretical results for the Euclidean projection are sound.
* The experiments demonstrate some benefit compared to standard variational methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is plagued by numerous minor flaws that detract from its overall quality.
* The looseness of the spectral norm bound undermines the guarantee of fast mixing.
* The parameter choices in the stochastic gradient descent algorithm are poorly justified.
* The theoretical analysis of the piecewise KL-divergence is insufficient.



**Overall Assessment:**

Reject. While the paper presents an interesting idea and some solid theoretical work, the pervasive minor flaws, combined with the questionable choice of the spectral norm threshold and lack of detailed justification for key parameter settings, outweigh the strengths.  The paper feels insufficiently polished for NIPS.  I recommend that the authors thoroughly revise the manuscript, addressing the points raised above, and resubmit to a future conference. A more rigorous analysis of the spectral norm bound and a more careful treatment of the experimental setup are essential.
