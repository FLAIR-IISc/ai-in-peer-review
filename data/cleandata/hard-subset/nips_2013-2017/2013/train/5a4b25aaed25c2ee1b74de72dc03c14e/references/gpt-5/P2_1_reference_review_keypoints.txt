- Proposes projecting Ising parameters onto a fast-mixing set (spectral-norm bound ||R||2 < 1) to ensure fast-mixing Gibbs sampling.
- Uses bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij| and spectral-norm criterion to define the fast-mixing family.
- Studies projections under multiple divergences: Euclidean, inclusive KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL.
- Derives a dual solution for Euclidean projection enforcing spectral-norm and sparsity via SVD thresholding (Theorems 6–7).
- Optimizes reverse KL via stochastic approximation using samples from the projected model, generalizing mean-field to fast-mixing families.
- Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare projections to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs, using JT as ground truth for these sizes.
- Strength: novel framing of tractability through fast-mixing parameter regimes linking MCMC mixing theory with variational projections.
- Strength: concrete technical contribution—dual spectral-norm projection with principled SVD thresholding and a stochastic projection for nonconvex reverse KL.
- Strength: clear connections to mean-field (R=0), Dobrushin and spectral criteria, and piecewise/structured approximations.
- Strength: empirical scope includes multiple divergences and baselines with exact JT ground truth for tested sizes.
- Weakness: experiments use c=2.5 (≫1), contradicting the theoretical ||R||2<1 guarantee and weakening the main claim.
- Weakness: spectral-norm condition is conservative for grids (β threshold << critical), so bound may be too loose to be practically useful.
- Weakness: algorithmic complexity unclear—repeated SVDs may be costly, Πc can densify matrices, and no runtime/scale analysis is provided.
- Weakness: inconsistency in evaluation description—unclear when marginals come from Gibbs vs junction tree; needs clarification.
- Weakness: piecewise KL uses max over subgraphs without justification; non-smooth choice, unclear bounds, and sensitivity not analyzed.
- Weakness: missing baselines and ablations, e.g., projection using Dobrushin l1 bound and sensitivity analyses on spectral threshold c.
- Quality: theory builds on known mixing results; proof details deferred and subgradient/convergence of outer optimization not fully addressed.
- Quality: reverse-KL gradient estimator discussion lacks concrete convergence or sample-complexity guidance.
- Clarity: generally well-written with good background, but some notation and evaluation details should be defined more explicitly.
- Originality: high-level idea is novel; the dual projection for spectral-norm-and-sparsity constraints appears new in this context.
- Significance: potentially impactful as a new lens for approximate inference, but weakened guarantees, scalability, and robustness limit current significance.
- Suggestion: report mixing diagnostics for projected models (autocorrelation, spectral gap proxies, TV bounds), especially when using c>1.
- Suggestion: compare to Dobrushin l1-norm projection and report accuracy/runtime trade-offs.
- Suggestion: provide complexity/runtime details for Euclidean projection (number/size of SVDs, LBFGS-B iterations) and scalability beyond n≈100.
- Suggestion: clarify evaluation protocol (when JT vs Gibbs used), justify max over subgraphs in piecewise KL, and include ablations on c.
- Acceptance argument: conceptually novel, interesting technical contribution, and empirical indications that projections can outperform standard variational methods.
- Rejection argument: central mixing guarantee not upheld in experiments, limited complexity analysis, and experimental methodology needs stronger validation.
- Recommendation: weak reject—promising idea but requires stronger empirical validation of mixing guarantees, tighter bounds, and clearer experiments.
