Summary
The paper proposes using “fast-mixing” parameter sets as a tractable family for approximate inference in Ising models. The key sufficient condition is a spectral-norm bound on the dependency matrix (via |β| and Lemma 5), which ensures rapid mixing of single-site Gibbs (Theorem 4). Given intractable parameters θ, the authors project to a fast-mixing ψ under several divergences: (i) Euclidean distance with an SVD-thresholding-based dual that preserves sparsity/absolute-value constraints (Theorem 7); (ii) KL(θ||ψ) (inclusive), theoretically appealing but requiring expectations under θ; (iii) a “piecewise” KL defined over tractable subgraphs; and (iv) KL(ψ||θ) (reverse), optimized by stochastic projected gradient using samples from ψ (fast mixing). Experiments on small random graphs and 8×8 grids compare these projections against MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs on original parameters.

Strengths
- Interesting and timely perspective: treating fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractable family bridges MCMC mixing guarantees with variational approximations.
- Clear high-level framing of sufficient mixing conditions via spectral norm and their connection to Dobrushin’s criterion.
- The Euclidean projection with structural constraints is potentially useful; the dual formulation and use of LBFGS-B is practical.
- The stochastic projected gradient scheme for KL(ψ||θ) is a natural way to leverage fast-mixing constraints.
- The paper is reasonably well written, situating the work among mean-field, TRW, piecewise training, and tree mixtures.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness/soundness
- Theorem 6 appears incorrect as written: for min ||A−B||_F s.t. ||B||_2 ≤ c, the standard solution thresholds singular values at c (S′ii = min(Sii, c)), not “c 2” (unclear if this is a typesetting error). Please clarify.
- The dual objective in Theorem 7 has unusual signs/scaling (e.g., g contains “1/||D−R||_F^2”), which is atypical for a Lagrangian-derived dual; the presentation raises questions about derivation and convexity. Full derivation and verification are needed.
- The gradient for KL(ψ||θ) (Theorem 8) is unconventional. Typically, ∇ψ KL(ψ||θ) can be expressed via expectations and (co)variances under ψ; the provided expression and unbiasedness discussion (Eq. 8) rely on a product of two estimated terms and independence assumptions that are not met when reusing the same MCMC pool. A precise, correct derivation and an estimator with controlled bias/variance are needed.
- The paper occasionally conflates the dependency bound R ≤ tanh|β| with |β|; while the inequality holds, using |||β|||_2 < 1 is stronger than necessary. This is fine for sufficiency but should be consistently reasoned through when enforcing constraints.

Mismatch between guarantees and experiments
- The “guaranteed rapid mixing” narrative is undermined by using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 in experiments (Section 6.1), explicitly exceeding the <1 sufficient condition. Claims of guaranteed convergence thus do not apply to the reported results. If the method is used heuristically beyond the guarantee, that should be clearly separated, and mixing should be empirically verified.
- No mixing diagnostics (e.g., integrated autocorrelation times, effective sample size, Geweke/R-hat variants, coupling bounds) are presented to substantiate “rapid mixing” in practice, especially under the relaxed bound. This is crucial given the paper’s premise.

Experimental design and clarity
- There are contradictions about how “ground-truth” marginals are obtained. Section 6 says “compared against those of the target distributions by running a Gibbs chain,” then states “marginals computed via the exact junction-tree algorithm,” and later says marginals are estimated from Gibbs samples. Exact JT on 8×8 grids is not feasible. Please clarify the gold-standard and ensure it is truly reliable (e.g., very long AIS or carefully diagnosed long runs).
- Computational accounting is inconsistent: comparisons “do not take into account” the substantial cost of projection (e.g., 30,000 Gibbs iterations plus repeated projections). For a fair time–accuracy trade-off, total wall-clock or iteration budgets should be matched across methods.
- Hyperparameters for the stochastic optimization (step size, pool size, iterations) are ad hoc; no sensitivity analysis, schedule, or convergence diagnostics are provided. The ergodic mirror descent conditions mentioned are not satisfied.
- Baselines are limited and underspecified (LBP/TRW variants, damping, convergence/stopping). More robust baselines (e.g., TRW-S, GBP) and stronger MCMC schemes (block Gibbs, Swendsen–Wang) would make the empirical case stronger.
- The “piecewise KL” is introduced as novel, but is very close in spirit to piecewise training and tree mixtures; the specific choice “max over T” yields a non-smooth objective with no theory, and empirically underperforms. More justification or alternative aggregations (e.g., averages with weights) would help.

Presentation
- Several notation and typographical issues (e.g., Theorem 6, Theorem 7 expressions, missing constants) reduce confidence.
- References are incomplete and in places insufficient for reproducibility.

Quality: The core idea is appealing, but the theoretical presentation contains apparent errors/omissions, and the empirical evidence does not support the key guarantee (due to using an invalid bound and lacking mixing diagnostics). As it stands, this is not yet methodologically solid.

Clarity: Generally readable, but with important inconsistencies (evaluation protocol), missing derivation details, and typos in key theorems.

Originality: Moderately original in framing projections to fast-mixing sets and combining with reverse-KL stochastic projection; connections to piecewise/tree methods are known.

Significance: Potentially impactful if delivered with correct theory and convincing experiments; current evidence is insufficient.

Questions for the authors
- Please correct/clarify Theorem 6 and Theorem 7 (full derivations), and provide the correct gradient for KL(ψ||θ) with a justified estimator.
- How are “ground-truth” marginals computed on 8×8 grids? If via sampling, what diagnostics and run lengths?
- What is the spectral norm of |β| after projection in experiments? How often is it <1? Any empirical mixing-time estimates?
- How sensitive are results to the projection bound and to SGD hyperparameters? Any learning-rate schedules?
- What is the computational cost of the dual projection relative to inference, and how does this affect time–accuracy trade-offs?

Pros
- Interesting and potentially useful modeling primitive (fast-mixing family).
- Practical projection machinery and a plausible stochastic projected gradient scheme.
- Clear motivation and ties to existing variational methods.

Cons
- Apparent technical errors/typos in core theorems and gradients.
- Guarantees not respected in experiments; no mixing diagnostics.
- Contradictory evaluation protocol; small-scale experiments; limited baselines.
- Sparse theoretical support for piecewise KL and optimization properties.

Recommendation: Reject. The idea is promising, but the paper needs corrected theory, coherent and guaranteed experimental settings (or clear separation of heuristic regimes), rigorous mixing/evaluation diagnostics, and stronger, fair empirical comparisons.
