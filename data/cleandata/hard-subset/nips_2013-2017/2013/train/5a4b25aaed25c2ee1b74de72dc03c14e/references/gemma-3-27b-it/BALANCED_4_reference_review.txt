This paper explores the idea of projecting parameters of an Ising model onto a “fast-mixing” set, aiming for faster convergence in Gibbs sampling. The core idea—approximating intractable distributions with tractable ones based on mixing properties—is a sensible direction, building on established work in approximate inference (mean-field, tree-reweighted BP, etc.). The authors correctly identify the importance of spectral norm bounds for guaranteeing fast mixing and propose several methods for projection, including Euclidean projection via SVD thresholding, and divergences based on KL. The exploration of different divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, zero-forcing KL) and the stochastic approximation approach for the zero-forcing KL are commendable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Approach:** Framing tractability in terms of mixing time is a valuable perspective and offers a potentially fruitful alternative to traditional approaches based on graph structure (e.g., tree-width).
*   **Theoretical Foundations:** The paper is well-grounded in the theory of Markov chain mixing times and leverages relevant results (Dyer et al., Hayes, Levin et al.). Theorem 7 provides a concrete algorithm for Euclidean projection.
*   **Exploration of Divergences:** The investigation of different divergence measures—and the trade-offs between computational cost and accuracy—is a strength. The piecewise KL divergence, though not performing as well empirically, represents a creative attempt to balance tractability and accuracy.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments, while relatively basic, provide a reasonable comparison against standard inference algorithms (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs) and demonstrate the potential benefits of the proposed approach.
*   **Clear Connections to Existing Work:** The paper clearly situates itself within the broader landscape of approximate inference methods and references relevant literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Gains:** The experimental results do not demonstrate a substantial improvement over existing methods. While the proposed methods often perform better than naive mean-field and LBP, the gains are not consistently large, and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters remains superior given sufficient time.
*   **Conservativeness of Spectral Norm Bound:** The authors acknowledge that the spectral norm bound used (2.5) is likely too conservative. This significantly impacts the effectiveness of the projection, as a tighter bound would allow for a closer approximation of the original distribution. This issue undermines the practical value of the approach.
*   **Computational Cost:**  The stochastic approximation approach for the zero-forcing KL-divergence, while theoretically sound, appears computationally expensive and sensitive to parameter tuning (step size, pool size, iterations). The paper acknowledges this, but a more thorough analysis of computational complexity is needed.
*   **Limited Scope:** The experiments are restricted to Ising models.  The generalizability of the proposed approach to other graphical model families remains unclear. Addressing this through theoretical discussion or limited empirical evaluation would be beneficial.
*   **Clarity in Notation:** The notation related to divergences and projections (e.g., D(θ,ψ), Πc[A]) could be made more consistent and clearly defined.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel perspective on approximate inference by focusing on mixing time.
*   The theoretical results (Theorem 7) are valuable and provide a concrete algorithm for Euclidean projection.
*   The exploration of different divergences adds to the body of knowledge in approximate inference.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical gains are limited and do not justify the added complexity of the proposed approach.
*   The conservative spectral norm bound significantly limits the effectiveness of the method.
*   The computational cost and parameter sensitivity of the stochastic approximation approach are concerning.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and theoretically sound approach to approximate inference. However, the limited empirical gains, conservative spectral norm bound, and computational complexity concerns prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. The work is promising, but requires significant refinement before it can be considered a major contribution.

**Recommendations:**

*   **Investigate tighter bounds on the spectral norm for fast mixing.** This is the most critical improvement.
*   **Conduct a more thorough analysis of the computational complexity and parameter sensitivity of the zero-forcing KL divergence approach.** Explore alternative optimization strategies.
*   **Expand the experimental evaluation to other graphical model families.**  Demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.
*   **Refine the notation and improve clarity in the presentation.**
*   **Consider a more in-depth comparison to state-of-the-art inference algorithms.**



**Rating:** Reject (with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the major weaknesses).
