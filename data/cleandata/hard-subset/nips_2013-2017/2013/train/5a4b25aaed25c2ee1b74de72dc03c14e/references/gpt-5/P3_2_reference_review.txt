Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “fast-mixing” graphical models as a tractable family: parameters are constrained so that single-site Gibbs mixes rapidly. For Ising models, the sufficient condition is that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix R is < 1; using Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|, the authors enforce a spectral-norm constraint on the absolute coupling matrix to guarantee mixing. Given an intractable parameter vector θ, they “project” to a fast-mixing ψ by minimizing a divergence subject to this constraint.

Four divergences are considered: (i) Euclidean distance in parameter space, with a dual algorithm (Theorem 7) that enforces both sparsity (graph structure) and a constraint on |||B|||2; (ii) inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ), theoretically appealing but impractical because it requires expectations under θ; (iii) a novel piecewise KL that evaluates KL on low-treewidth subgraphs obtained by dropping edges from both θ and ψ and taking a max over subgraphs; and (iv) reverse KL KL(ψ||θ), optimized via stochastic approximation using samples from ψ (which is fast-mixing), leveraging a pooled-sample scheme related to Ergodic Mirror Descent.

Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare these projections (Euclidean, piecewise-KL, reverse-KL) against MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs on original parameters, reporting marginal L1 errors (with ground truth from exact junction-tree). The approach often outperforms standard variational baselines at comparable sampling time; Gibbs on original parameters can be more accurate if run long enough, but the authors argue mixing may be prohibitively slow.

Strengths
- A clear, conceptually novel angle: defining tractability via mixing-time guarantees rather than graph structure. This is complementary to MF/structured MF, TRW, and planar/low-treewidth exact inference approximations.
- For Euclidean projection, Theorem 7 is a useful contribution: a dual formulation that accounts for sparsity, absolute values, and a spectral-norm constraint, implementable with off-the-shelf LBFGS-B and a repeated SVD-thresholding operator.
- The reverse-KL optimization with sampling under ψ is well-motivated and connects to MF as a limiting case (zero-norm constraint), with a correct gradient expression via covariance under ψ.
- The piecewise-KL idea is simple and computationally tractable, tying to tree-based approximations (TRW, mixtures of trees) and piecewise training.
- The paper is honest about the trade-offs (e.g., inclusive KL impractical; looseness of the spectral bound; scheduling issues).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Guarantees vs practice: the theoretical guarantee requires ||R||2 < 1, yet experiments use a looser spectral bound of 2.5 “which still preserves rapid mixing” without justification or diagnostics. This undermines the central selling point (guaranteed fast mixing). At minimum, empirical mixing diagnostics (autocorrelations, spectral gap estimates, coupling times, or the Levin–Peres d(t) bound via contraction) are needed to substantiate that the chains are indeed fast-mixing under the relaxed bound.
- The dual Euclidean projection: the optimization is a non-smooth, non-convex max over Λ, M because Πc involves an SVD thresholding operator; LBFGS-B may converge to local optima without guarantees. The paper should clarify convexity/duality status, existence/uniqueness, and provide sensitivity or optimality certificates (e.g., KKT residuals, duality gap).
- Masking and sparsity: constraining D = |B| with Z · D = 0 enforces B_ij = 0 off-graph, but Πc will generally densify; the dual attempts to counteract that, but no bounds on deviation from the original sparsity or on the distortion of signs are provided. In practice, do projections lead to dense couplings that negate computational benefits (e.g., for block Gibbs or other samplers)?
- Piecewise KL: taking a max over subgraphs is ad hoc and non-smooth; no theoretical relation to true KL or bounds are provided (contrast with TRW’s convex upper bounds on A(θ)). The motivation “if θ and ψ define the same distribution, their subgraphs match” is true but does not justify max-T objective. A sum or convex combination of subgraph KLs (with coverage constraints) would be closer to existing upper bounds and might be smoother and better behaved.
- Experimental protocol: there is confusion about how marginals are produced for evaluation (“run a Gibbs chain” vs “exact junction-tree”). If exact is available for all test cases, all methods should be evaluated against exact marginals, and sampling-based estimates should be decoupled from projection optimization. Moreover, wall-clock or total compute comparisons are omitted; the projection steps (tens of thousands of Gibbs iterations plus repeated SVDs) are nontrivial, so time-accuracy comparisons to Gibbs on original parameters are incomplete.
- Scale: tests are restricted to small graphs; it is unclear whether the SVD-based projection scales beyond a few hundred nodes.
- Related work positioning: connections to Dobrushin’s condition are noted, but the paper does not discuss other high-temperature contraction criteria (e.g., path coupling), or alternative mixing-time–based parameter regions for block Gibbs/Swendsen–Wang. TRW and planar decompositions are cited; a stronger comparison to TRW’s provable bounds would help.

Quality
Technically sound in the core derivations (dependency matrix bound, reverse-KL gradient via covariance). The Euclidean projection dual is interesting but needs a clearer derivation and a statement of conditions under which it is correct and useful. The experimental evidence is suggestive but not conclusive due to the loosened norm bound, small scale, and incomplete compute accounting.

Clarity
Generally well written, with helpful background. There are some notational inconsistencies/typos (e.g., missing 1/2 in Eq. (2), clarity on Z and Hadamard products, and whether updates use random vs systematic scan). The experimental description should be tightened to avoid confusion about how marginals are computed and what compute is counted.

Originality
The notion of projection into a fast-mixing set and the dual formulation for Euclidean projection appear novel. The reverse-KL-with-fast-mixing-sampler perspective is a clean generalization of MF. Piecewise-KL is a new variant, though closely related to prior tree/mixture decompositions.

Significance
If strengthened, the approach could become a practical alternative when exact inference is intractable and naive Gibbs is slow. However, current guarantees are not enforced in practice, and scalability remains unclear.

Suggestions for improvement
- Either enforce ||R||2 < 1 in all experiments, or include mixing diagnostics showing that ψ actually mixes rapidly under the relaxed bound, and study accuracy vs bound c trade-offs.
- Provide theoretical discussion on the optimization landscape of Theorem 7’s dual, with KKT conditions and empirical checks (duality gap, stationarity).
- Report wall-clock and iteration counts for all methods, including projection overhead, and evaluate on larger graphs.
- For piecewise-KL, compare max vs average-over-subgraphs and relate to TRW bounds; provide empirical ablation.
- Clarify evaluation (exact vs sampled marginals), scanning scheme, and hyperparameter tuning for baselines.

Arguments for acceptance
- Fresh, principled viewpoint on tractability via mixing guarantees.
- Technically nontrivial projection machinery; clear connection to MF via reverse-KL.
- Promising empirical accuracy relative to common variational methods.

Arguments against acceptance
- Guarantees are not honored in experiments (loosened bound), undercutting the core premise.
- Limited scale and incomplete compute accounting.
- Lacks theoretical bounds for piecewise-KL and convergence/optimality guarantees for the dual projection.
