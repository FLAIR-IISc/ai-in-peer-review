### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel and thought-provoking approach to approximate inference in graphical models. The central idea is to redefine the notion of a "tractable" family of distributions. Instead of relying on structural properties like low treewidth, the authors propose using a family of distributions for which Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing." For the Ising model, they leverage a known sufficient condition for rapid mixing: the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths must be less than one.

The core of the work is to approximate an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing Ising model by projecting its parameters onto this fast-mixing set. The authors explore four different projection methods based on different divergences:
1.  **Euclidean Distance:** A direct projection of the parameter vectors, for which the authors derive a novel and efficient dual algorithm based on singular value thresholding that can preserve sparsity.
2.  **Inclusive KL-Divergence (KL(q||p)):** A theoretically natural but computationally intractable projection, as it requires inference on the original slow-mixing model.
3.  **Piecewise KL-Divergence:** A novel, tractable approximation to the KL divergence, computed by decomposing the graph into low-treewidth subgraphs.
4.  **Exclusive KL-Divergence (KL(p||q)):** A practical approach where the divergence can be minimized via stochastic projected gradient descent, since expectations are taken with respect to the fast-mixing distribution and can be estimated via sampling.

Experiments on grid and random Ising models show that the proposed methods, particularly the one based on minimizing KL(p||q), outperform standard variational methods like loopy belief propagation and mean-field. Furthermore, sampling from the projected fast-mixing model yields more accurate marginals in finite time than sampling from the original slow-mixing model.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its conceptual contribution. The idea of defining a tractable family based on dynamical properties (fast mixing) rather than static structural properties is a bold and significant departure from the mainstream of approximate inference research. It elegantly connects the theory of MCMC mixing times with the practice of variational inference, opening up a genuinely new and exciting research direction. This is precisely the kind of high-risk, high-reward idea that can push the field forward.

2.  **Strong Technical Contributions:** Beyond the core concept, the paper provides solid technical results. The derivation of the dual algorithm for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) that handles sparsity constraints is non-trivial and useful in its own right. The formulation of the KL(p||q) minimization using stochastic projected gradient descent is practical and well-motivated.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. It provides clear background on mixing time theory, articulates the core idea persuasively, and presents the different projection methods in a logical and easy-to-follow manner. The connection to mean-field as an extreme case of their framework (zero spectral norm) is particularly insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Looseness of the Theoretical Bound:** The main weakness, which the authors commendably address, is the conservative nature of the spectral norm bound for guaranteeing rapid mixing. In the experiments, a bound of 2.5 is used instead of the theoretically justified 1.0, as it yields better approximations. This creates a disconnect between the theoretical motivation (a guarantee of fast mixing) and the empirical implementation. While this is a valid concern, for a paper introducing a new paradigm, this gap is acceptable. The core idea is to project onto *some* region where mixing is believed to be fast; future work can focus on deriving and using tighter, more practical bounds.

2.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are performed on relatively small-scale models (e.g., 8x8 grids). While sufficient for a proof of concept, the real benefit of fast mixing (polynomial vs. exponential complexity) becomes most apparent on larger, more challenging problems. Demonstrating the method's scalability would strengthen the paper's claims.

3.  **Dependence on Single-Site Gibbs:** The entire framework is built upon the fast-mixing conditions for single-site Gibbs sampling. While this is a reasonable starting point, many other MCMC algorithms exist with different mixing properties. The true potential of this framework will be realized by extending it to other samplers.

### Recommendation and Justification

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a fundamentally new and highly significant idea for approximate inference. The shift in perspective from static structure to dynamic properties is a major conceptual leap.
*   It is a rare paper that successfully builds a practical bridge between two important but often separate fields: MCMC theory and variational inference.
*   The proposed KL(p||q) method is practical, principled, and demonstrates strong empirical performance against established baselines.
*   The paper is well-executed, with solid technical derivations and clear exposition.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The empirical work relies on an ad-hoc relaxation of the theoretical bound that guarantees fast mixing.
*   The experimental validation is limited to small-scale problems.

In my view, the arguments for acceptance are far more compelling. This work is not an incremental improvement on an existing method; it is a creative and bold proposal that has the potential to inspire a new subfield of research. While the methodology is not yet perfect—the gap between the theoretical bound and the empirically useful one being the most notable issue—the core idea is powerful enough to overlook these limitations. This paper prioritizes a groundbreaking concept over methodological perfection, and for that, it should be celebrated and shared with the NIPS community. It is a quintessential NIPS paper that is likely to be influential and spark much follow-up work.
