### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically Ising models. Instead of relying on structurally simple approximations (like trees or fully-factorized distributions), the authors define a "tractable" family of models as those that are "fast-mixing," meaning Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to converge rapidly. The primary condition for fast mixing is derived from prior work, requiring the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths, `|| |β| ||_2`, to be less than 1.

The core contribution is a set of methods to project an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing, Ising model onto this fast-mixing set. The authors explore four divergences for this projection:
1.  **Euclidean distance:** A non-probabilistic projection for which they derive a dual optimization algorithm based on iterative SVD thresholding that respects graph sparsity.
2.  **KL(q||p) ("zero-avoiding"):** Considered ideal but computationally intractable for the target slow-mixing distribution `q`.
3.  **Piecewise KL:** A novel, tractable approximation to the KL divergence based on low-treewidth subgraphs.
4.  **KL(p||q) ("zero-forcing"):** This divergence is optimized using stochastic projected gradient descent, where expectations are estimated via sampling from the fast-mixing approximation `p`. This is framed as a generalization of mean-field inference.

Experiments on grid and random graphs compare these projection methods to standard approximate inference techniques like LBP, TRW, and mean-field, as well as to Gibbs sampling on the original parameters. The results suggest that the proposed methods can yield more accurate marginals than standard variational methods.

### Strengths
1.  **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The core idea of defining tractability through mixing time rather than structural simplicity is creative and compelling. It opens up a new avenue for designing approximate inference algorithms that could potentially capture more complex dependencies than traditional methods.
2.  **Technical Contribution in Projection:** The derivation of a projection algorithm for the Euclidean norm (Theorem 7) that handles both the absolute value constraint and graph sparsity is a non-trivial technical contribution.
3.  **Insightful Connection to Mean-Field:** Framing the KL(ψ||θ) projection as a generalization of mean-field (where the spectral norm is constrained to be < 1 instead of 0) provides a clear and useful conceptual link to established literature.

### Weaknesses
The paper, while containing promising ideas, suffers from a significant number of flaws ranging from a major disconnect between theory and practice to numerous minor issues in clarity, methodology, and presentation.

1.  **Major Disconnect Between Theory and Experiments:** The entire theoretical motivation rests on Theorem 4, which guarantees rapid mixing if `||R||_2 < 1`. For an Ising model, this translates to `|| |β| ||_2 < 1`. However, the experiments are conducted with a spectral norm bound of 2.5. This choice is theoretically unsupported and fundamentally undermines the paper's central claim of projecting onto a *guaranteed* fast-mixing set. The authors acknowledge in the discussion that the theoretical bound is conservative, but this does not justify using an arbitrary, much larger value without any empirical or theoretical validation that this new bound still ensures rapid mixing. The paper lacks any experiments (e.g., convergence plots of the total variation distance) to support the claim that the resulting distributions are, in fact, "rapid-mixing."

2.  **Lack of Experimental Rigor:**
    *   The computational cost of the projection step is omitted from the time-accuracy comparisons. The authors' justification for this is convoluted and unconvincing. For a fair comparison, especially against Gibbs sampling on the original model, the total wall-clock time, including projection, must be considered.
    *   The optimization of KL(ψ||θ) uses a fixed step size and untuned hyperparameters. The paper cites Ergodic Mirror Descent, which typically requires a carefully chosen (e.g., decaying) step-size schedule for convergence guarantees. The lack of tuning raises concerns about the optimality of the reported results.
    *   In the gradient estimator for KL(ψ||θ) (Eq. 8), the same set of samples is used to estimate both the marginals `µ̂` and the main expectation. The authors dismiss the resulting bias by stating it "makes little difference," a claim that is presented without evidence.
    *   The novel "piecewise KL" divergence is introduced but then summarily dismissed as not performing well, without presenting the corresponding negative results or an analysis of its failure modes. As presented, it is an incomplete contribution.

3.  **Clarity and Notational Issues:**
    *   Several notational choices are non-standard and ambiguous. The "triple dot product" in Theorem 7 is undefined. The use of `'` for transpose in matrix expressions like `Z'D` is confusing; element-wise product `Z .* D` should be used if that is the intent.
    *   Theorem 6 appears to contain a typo: `S'_ii = min(Sii, c 2)`. The `2` is extraneous; it should likely be `min(Sii, c)`.
    *   Lemma 5 is a key result linking the dependency matrix to the Ising parameters, but its proof is omitted, with only a vague reference to modifying a proof from [8]. Given its importance, a sketch of this modification should have been included.
    *   The footnote in Section 5.3 regarding edge removal is confusingly worded and its logical purpose is unclear.

4.  **Inconsistent and Incomplete Referencing:** The bibliography is poorly formatted. Several references are incomplete (e.g., [1] Byrd et al., [13] Peres et al.), while others have inconsistent formatting (e.g., presence/absence of journal names, page numbers). This lack of attention to detail detracts from the paper's overall quality.

### Recommendation
**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The paper proposes a genuinely novel and interesting perspective on approximate inference.
*   The Euclidean projection algorithm is a solid technical contribution.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The experimental validation is critically flawed due to the use of a spectral norm bound (2.5) that is completely disconnected from the paper's theoretical foundation (`< 1`).
*   The experimental methodology lacks rigor in its hyperparameter selection, gradient estimation, and computational cost comparison.
*   The paper is marred by numerous issues of clarity, notational ambiguity, typos, and sloppy referencing.
*   A key proposed method (piecewise KL) is presented without a proper evaluation.

While the core idea is promising, the execution and evaluation are not up to the standards of a NIPS publication. The gulf between the theoretical motivation and the experimental implementation is a fatal flaw. The sheer number of minor errors further suggests a rushed and unpolished submission. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors should be encouraged to build upon their interesting idea, but this requires a much more careful and theoretically grounded experimental validation, as well as a thorough revision of the manuscript's presentation.
