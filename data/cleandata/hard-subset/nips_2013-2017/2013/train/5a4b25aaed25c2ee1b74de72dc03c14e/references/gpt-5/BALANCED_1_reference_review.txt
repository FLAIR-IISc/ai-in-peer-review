Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a new “tractable family” for approximate inference: distributions that are guaranteed to be fast-mixing under univariate Gibbs updates. For Ising models with variables in {−1,+1}, the authors leverage a sufficient condition for rapid mixing based on the spectral norm of the dependency matrix R, using the bound Rij ≤ tanh |βij|, and the mixing-time result that ||R||2 < 1 implies polynomial mixing time (Hayes; Dyer et al.). Given a potentially slow-mixing Ising model θ, the paper studies projections to a fast-mixing parameter set Ψ under four divergences:

1) Euclidean projection in parameter space: they derive a dual formulation that enforces both a spectral-norm constraint on |β| and sparsity respecting the original graph. This reduces to repeated singular value thresholding (Theorem 7) embedded in an LBFGS-B optimization over dual variables that impose zero-pattern constraints.

2) Inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ): theoretically appealing (“zero-avoiding”), with gradient μ(ψ) − μ(θ), but intractable when θ is slow-mixing.

3) A novel “piecewise” KL that drops edges to obtain tractable subgraphs T and uses maxT KL(θ(T)||ψ(T)); gradients come from the active subgraph.

4) Reverse KL KL(ψ||θ): amenable to stochastic approximation because expectations are under ψ, which is constrained to be fast-mixing. They use a sample pool with incremental Gibbs updates and project after gradient steps, relating to Ergodic Mirror Descent.

Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare these projections against LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original parameters, evaluating mean absolute marginal errors (ground truth by junction-tree). The fast-mixing projections often outperform standard variational methods for a fixed sampling budget; Gibbs on the original model can be more accurate with unlimited time but may mix slowly. The authors also note they sometimes relax the spectral-norm cap (use 2.5 rather than 1.0) for better approximations, claiming it still tends to mix fast in practice.

Strengths
- Clear and interesting re-framing: tractability via provably fast mixing (for a sampler) rather than via low treewidth or convex relaxations.
- Concrete algorithmic contribution: dual Euclidean projection that enforces both spectral-norm and sparsity constraints, operationalized via SVD thresholding and LBFGS-B.
- Practical optimization procedure for reverse KL using sampling, with a thoughtful connection to stochastic approximation and Ergodic Mirror Descent.
- Novel “piecewise KL” idea that leverages tractable subgraphs to define a proxy divergence, easy to compute on chains/low-treewidth subgraphs.
- Empirical comparisons to common baselines (LBP, TRW, MF) on multiple settings, with 50-trial averages.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The central theoretical guarantee requires ||R||2 < 1, yet in experiments the authors sometimes set the cap to 2.5 to improve approximation quality. This undermines the advertised guarantee and should be handled more carefully (e.g., separate ablations with and without guarantees, and empirical diagnostics of mixing when the bound is violated).
- Limited scale and scope: 8×8 grids and 10-node random graphs are small; conclusions about practicality and accuracy may not generalize. The projection step itself involves repeated SVDs and LBFGS-B; runtime and scaling behavior are not reported.
- Experimental protocol is somewhat confusing: at points it suggests both Gibbs and exact junction-tree are used for marginals; please clearly separate ground-truth computation from approximate marginal estimation. Also, time-accuracy plots should include the cost of projection/optimization for a fair compute budget comparison.
- The piecewise KL is ad hoc (max over subgraphs) without theoretical bounds linking it to the true KL, nor guidance on choosing T beyond simple covers. It would be helpful to connect to TRW-style weightings or provide bounds/consistency properties.
- Optimization details for KL(ψ||θ): convergence behavior and variance control are not fully explored; using the same samples to estimate both μ and product terms introduces bias, contrary to the independence argument mentioned.
- Clarity gaps: Theorem 7 requires careful exposition. It is not fully clear how one recovers a signed sparse β from D = |B| after projection, and how sparsity and sign constraints interact. Pseudocode would help.

Quality
Technically sound in its use of known mixing-time conditions and matrix norm projections; the dual Euclidean projection is interesting. However, the relaxation of the spectral norm cap in experiments, lack of mixing diagnostics, and limited theoretical development for piecewise KL and reverse-KL optimization weaken the overall rigor.

Clarity
Generally well-written, with a solid background section. The experimental description needs to disentangle ground-truth computation and evaluation protocols. Provide concrete algorithmic pseudocode and complexity discussion for the dual projection and the stochastic projected-gradient procedure.

Originality
The idea of projecting to a fast-mixing set as an approximate inference primitive is novel and clever. The dual projection with sparsity constraints and the piecewise KL are original. Using reverse KL with sampling under a constrained fast-mixing family is a natural but worthwhile extension of mean-field.

Significance
Potentially useful as an alternative to standard variational approximations, especially when one wants sampling-based estimates with mixing guarantees. However, the conservative nature of the sufficient condition and the small-scale experiments limit practical impact at this stage.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel framing and methods (projection to fast-mixing set).
- Concrete algorithms with connections to mixing-time theory and convex-analytic tools.
- Promising empirical improvements over common variational baselines.

Cons for acceptance
- Undermining of the main guarantee in practice (using cap=2.5) without careful treatment.
- Limited empirical scale and incomplete compute-budget accounting.
- Lacks theoretical guarantees for the piecewise divergence and the stochastic reverse-KL optimization.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations with strict guarantees (||R||2 ≤ 1) versus relaxed caps, and report empirical mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation times, effective sample sizes, Gelman–Rubin where applicable).
- Include runtime and scaling analyses: number of SVDs, LBFGS-B iterations, cost per gradient step, and end-to-end wall-clock comparisons at equal compute.
- Clarify Theorem 7: how sparsity and signs are enforced to reconstruct β from D = |B|; supply pseudocode and complexity bounds.
- For the piecewise KL, explore weighted combinations of subgraphs (analogous to TRW) and attempt to derive bounds or consistency properties. Compare different T selections systematically.
- For KL(ψ||θ), reduce estimator variance (e.g., control variates, Polyak/Ruppert averaging) and separate sample sets to remove bias when feasible; discuss convergence diagnostics.
- Evaluate on larger grids (e.g., 20×20) and higher-degree random graphs; include strong-attraction regimes near/above criticality to stress-test the method.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a fresh and promising direction with solid ideas, but the experimental methodology and the handling of the core guarantee need strengthening. With clearer protocols, stricter evaluation, and more thorough analysis, this work could become impactful.
