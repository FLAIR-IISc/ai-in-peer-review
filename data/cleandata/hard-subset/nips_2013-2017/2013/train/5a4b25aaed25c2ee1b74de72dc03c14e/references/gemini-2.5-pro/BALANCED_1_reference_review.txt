### Summary

This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in graphical models, specifically Ising models. The central idea is to define a tractable family of distributions not by their graphical structure (e.g., low treewidth), but by their "fast-mixing" properties, which guarantee that Gibbs sampling converges quickly to the stationary distribution. The authors leverage a known result from the MCMC literature which states that an Ising model is fast-mixing if the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths is less than one.

The main contribution is a framework for projecting an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing, Ising model onto this set of fast-mixing models. The paper explores four different divergences for this projection:
1.  **Euclidean distance:** A method is derived to solve this projection using an iterative, dual optimization approach based on thresholding a singular value decomposition (Theorem 7).
2.  **Zero-avoiding KL-divergence (KL(q||p)):** Acknowledged as computationally intractable for the target problem, as it requires inference on the original slow-mixing distribution `q`.
3.  **Piecewise KL-divergence:** A novel, tractable approximation to KL(q||p) based on maximizing the KL divergence over a collection of low-treewidth subgraphs.
4.  **Zero-forcing KL-divergence (KL(p||q)):** This is minimized using projected stochastic gradient descent, where the gradient is estimated by sampling from the fast-mixing approximation `p`, and the projection step uses the Euclidean projection algorithm. This is framed as a generalization of mean-field inference.

Experiments on 2D grids and random graphs show that the proposed methods, particularly the KL(p||q) projection, outperform standard variational methods like loopy belief propagation and mean-field in terms of marginal accuracy. Furthermore, sampling from the projected distribution can yield more accurate marginals than running Gibbs sampling on the original distribution for a limited time.

### Quality and Clarity

The paper is technically sound and well-written. The motivation is clear, the background on mixing times is presented concisely, and the proposed algorithms are described in sufficient detail. The derivation of the Euclidean projection via a dual formulation (Theorem 7) is a solid technical contribution. The connection between minimizing KL(p||q) and mean-field theory is insightful and correctly positions the work within the broader context of variational inference.

The experimental setup is reasonable, comparing against appropriate and widely-used baselines. The results support the central claims of the paper. The authors are also commendably transparent about the limitations of their work, particularly regarding the looseness of the theoretical bounds, which is discussed in the conclusion.

### Originality and Significance

The primary contribution of this work—defining a tractable family for approximate inference based on dynamic properties (fast mixing) rather than static structural properties (low treewidth)—is highly original and significant. It opens up a new and promising direction for research in approximate inference. While the MCMC theory it builds upon is established, its application as a constraint in a projection-based inference framework is novel. The piecewise KL-divergence is also a new idea, even if its empirical performance in this paper was not state-of-the-art. This work could inspire further research into other parameter-space conditions that guarantee efficient inference and how they can be used to construct new variational families.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The core idea of using fast-mixing as the criterion for tractability is a creative and powerful shift in perspective from traditional structure-based approximate inference methods.
2.  **Strong Technical Contributions:** The paper provides a non-trivial algorithm for projecting onto the set of fast-mixing parameters under the Euclidean norm. The stochastic optimization procedure for the KL(p||q) projection is also well-founded.
3.  **Promising Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates superior accuracy over established variational baselines like LBP, TRW, and MF, suggesting it is a competitive new approach for approximate inference in Ising models.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, making a potentially complex set of ideas accessible.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The most significant weakness is the discrepancy between the theoretical motivation and the practical implementation. The theory guarantees fast mixing for a spectral norm bound of 1.0, but the experiments achieve the best results with a bound of 2.5, which lacks theoretical justification. This suggests that the spectral norm itself might be a good regularizer, but the specific threshold from the bound is too conservative.
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper would be substantially strengthened by addressing this gap. Could the authors provide more intuition or empirical analysis for why a bound of 2.5 works well? For instance, showing a plot of marginal error vs. the spectral norm bound would be very informative. A more thorough discussion on the sensitivity to this hyperparameter is needed. Alternatively, exploring tighter, more modern mixing time bounds from the literature could bridge this gap.

2.  **Computational Cost of Projection:** The projection algorithms themselves have a non-negligible computational cost. The Euclidean projection requires an iterative optimization involving SVDs, and the KL(p||q) approach involves running MCMC within a stochastic gradient descent loop. The experiments compare the accuracy of sampling from the *final* projected model for a fixed time but do not appear to factor in the "offline" cost of finding the projection.
    *   **Suggestion:** For a fairer comparison, especially against "Gibbs on original parameters," the total computational budget should be considered. How does the accuracy of the proposed method look when the time taken for projection is included in the total runtime? This is a crucial point for assessing the practical utility of the method.

3.  **Limited Scope:** The entire framework is developed and evaluated only for Ising models. The dependency matrix bound in Lemma 5 is specific to this case.
    *   **Suggestion:** While extending to general MRFs is rightly identified as future work, a short discussion of the key challenges would be beneficial. For general discrete MRFs, bounding the dependency matrix `R` in terms of model parameters is much harder. Acknowledging this would give the reader a better sense of the difficulty in generalizing the approach.

4.  **Underwhelming Piecewise KL Results:** The novel piecewise KL-divergence is an interesting idea, but the paper states it "does not perform as well as the true KL-divergence."
    *   **Suggestion:** It would be helpful to include these results in the main plots (even if they are worse) and offer some analysis. Why might this approach be suboptimal? Is it the choice of subgraphs (chains/trees)? This could turn a negative result into a more insightful lesson for future work in this area.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and thought-provoking idea. The concept of using fast-mixing properties to define tractable model families for approximate inference is a significant contribution that could open new research avenues. The technical execution is solid, and the empirical results are promising. While there is a notable gap between the motivating theory and the practical implementation, and questions remain about the overall computational trade-off, the originality and potential impact of the core idea are high. The strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and it would be a valuable contribution to the NeurIPS community.
