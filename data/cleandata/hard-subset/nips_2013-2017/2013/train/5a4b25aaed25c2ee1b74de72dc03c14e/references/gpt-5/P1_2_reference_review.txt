Summary
The paper proposes a new notion of “tractable” approximations for high-treewidth Ising models: instead of restricting to low-treewidth structures, it restricts to parameter sets that guarantee rapid mixing of single-site Gibbs sampling. The key sufficient condition used is a spectral-norm bound on the dependency matrix (Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Hayes), with a simple bound for Ising models Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij| implying that ensuring || |β| ||2 < 1 guarantees fast mixing. Given a potentially slow-mixing model θ, the paper studies projections ψ onto a “fast-mixing set” Ψ under several divergences:

- Euclidean projection in parameter space: a dual formulation (Theorem 7) yields an algorithm that preserves sparsity and enforces the spectral-norm constraint on |ψ| via iterative SVD thresholding within an LBFGS-B scheme.
- KL(θ||ψ) (“inclusive”): theoretically attractive but impractical at scale because expectations under θ are intractable.
- A novel piecewise KL based on tractable subgraphs (trees or low-treewidth subgraphs), taking a max over subgraph KLs.
- KL(ψ||θ) (“exclusive”): optimized stochastically using samples from ψ (which is constrained to be fast-mixing) with a covariance-based gradient (Theorem 8), implemented via a “sample pool” and projected gradient.

Experiments on 8×8 grids and 10-node random graphs compare the proposed projections (Euclidean, piecewise, exclusive KL) against MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs on the original parameters. The authors report improved marginal accuracy compared to variational baselines and better time–accuracy tradeoffs than Gibbs on the original model at finite budgets.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The use of spectral-norm conditions for rapid mixing is sound and well-supported by prior work. Lemma 5 (Rij ≤ tanh|βij|) is plausible (common in the literature) but only sketched; a complete proof or precise citation for the general (non-zero field) case would be helpful. The Euclidean projection is reduced to a convex problem over D=|B| with convex constraints; the dual derivation (Theorem 7) seems reasonable, though details are deferred. Theorem 6 (projection via SVD thresholding) is standard.
- The gradient for KL(ψ||θ) is correctly derived: ∇ψ KL(ψ||θ)=Covψ[f](ψ−θ), which matches the given estimator form Eψ[((ψ−θ)·f)(f−µ)]. The discussion about unbiasedness when using separate sample sets is correct; using the same pool introduces bias (as acknowledged).
- A major caveat is that, while the central promise is “guaranteed fast mixing,” the experiments relax the spectral bound to 2.5 (vs. the proven requirement <1), undermining the guarantee. This needs either a principled justification (e.g., stronger model-specific bounds, empirical checks of ||R||2, or diagnostics of mixing) or a reframed claim.
- Experimental methodology is mostly reasonable for small graphs. However, the description of how marginals are computed is confusing: at times it says “estimated from samples,” elsewhere “computed via exact junction tree.” Please clarify what is used as ground truth and how approximate marginals are obtained for each method. Also, the computational cost of repeated SVDs in the projection loop vs. baselines should be quantified when discussing time–accuracy tradeoffs.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written, with a clear motivation and connections to mean-field, TRW, and planar decompositions. Still, several notational points are opaque:
  - The constraint D=|B| and the role of Z in Eq. (1) would benefit from a short explanation of how the sign of B is handled in the minimization, and why the problem reduces to minimizing ||R−D||F with R=|A|.
  - The notation Λ′Z and the triple dot product should be defined precisely; state shapes of Λ, Z, and M and whether Λ′Z denotes a Hadamard product or a contraction.
  - Clarify if the result in Theorem 7 preserves sparsity of B or only constrains D to be zero off-graph.
- The experimental section should clearly state: (1) what is used as ground truth; (2) whether “Gibbs on original parameters” is run to convergence or to a fixed budget; (3) whether the projection cost is included in time plots.

Originality
- The central idea—projecting parameters into a provably fast-mixing set—is novel and conceptually distinct from tree-based approximations, mean-field, or planar decompositions. The dual Euclidean projection that respects sparsity while constraining the spectral norm of |β| is a nice technical contribution.
- The piecewise-KL divergence and the projection via KL(ψ||θ) constrained to fast mixing extend variational ideas to a new approximating family; viewing MF as the degenerate case (zero spectral norm) is insightful.

Significance
- If robust and scalable, “fast-mixing projections” could be a useful alternative approximating family with favorable sampling properties and straightforward stochastic optimization. The idea may influence learning with MCMC-based gradients by constraining parameters to fast-mixing regions.
- Current empirical validation is limited to small graphs. The SVD-based projection likely scales as O(n^3) per iteration, and the paper does not address scalability to larger graphs. The looseness of the spectral-norm criterion (especially on grids near criticality) limits the tightness of the approximation; acknowledging and discussing this is good, but the experimental relaxation to 2.5 weakens the central guarantee.

Pros
- Clear, principled connection between mixing-time theory and approximate inference.
- A concrete optimization method (dual Euclidean projection) that enforces a nontrivial, global constraint.
- A practical stochastic routine for KL(ψ||θ) using samples from ψ.
- Empirical gains over MF/LBP/TRW on the tested settings.

Cons
- The chief selling point (“guaranteed fast mixing”) is not realized in experiments due to using a spectral bound >1; no empirical verification of ||R||2<1 or mixing diagnostics are provided.
- Scalability and computational cost not fully addressed; repeated SVDs may be a bottleneck.
- Piecewise KL is somewhat ad hoc (max over subgraphs), potentially nonsmooth; alternatives (weighted sums, log-sum-exp) or a discussion of bounds would strengthen the case.
- Some clarity issues and missing proofs/citations (Lemma 5 general case, Theorem 7 details).

Suggestions
- Either (a) adhere to || |β| ||2<1 in experiments to uphold the guarantee; or (b) provide mixing diagnostics (e.g., spectral gap estimates, coupling bounds, or rigorous upper bounds via Dobrushin) to justify using 2.5, and rephrase claims accordingly.
- Clarify the evaluation protocol and report wall-clock time including projection cost vs. baselines.
- Provide ablations on the choice of subgraphs for piecewise KL and consider a smooth aggregate instead of max.
- Add details or an appendix sketch for Lemma 5 in the non-zero-field case and a clearer derivation/convexity argument for Theorem 7.
- Discuss scalability (approximate SVDs, power iterations, sparsity-exploiting methods) and alternative norm constraints (e.g., Dobrushin L1) that may be cheaper.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising and original, but the main guarantee is not upheld in the experiments, scalability is unclear, and some technical details need tightening. With stronger empirical validation under the stated guarantees (or justified relaxations) and clearer exposition, this could be a solid contribution.
