## Peer Review for "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

**Summary:**

This paper explores a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically Ising models. Instead of approximating the distribution itself, the authors propose projecting the parameters of an intractable distribution onto a set of parameters guaranteed to induce fast mixing in Gibbs sampling. They investigate several divergence measures for this projection – Euclidean distance (via SVD thresholding), KL-divergence (theoretically, and via piecewise approximations), and a reversed KL-divergence minimized via stochastic approximation.  The paper provides theoretical results for Euclidean projection and demonstrates empirical results comparing these projection methods to standard inference techniques like LBP, TRW, and naive mean field.  The core idea – leveraging fast mixing as a tractable approximation – is interesting and potentially impactful.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper positions itself within the broader context of approximate inference for intractable graphical models. It correctly acknowledges related work in mean-field inference, loopy belief propagation, and tree-reweighted belief propagation. The contribution distinguishes itself from these approaches by focusing on *parameter* projection to achieve fast mixing, rather than directly approximating the distribution. The connection to Dobrushin's criterion and the use of spectral norm bounds for mixing times are well-established, and the paper appropriately cites relevant literature (e.g., Levin, Peres, and Wilmer; Hayes). The piecewise KL-divergence draws inspiration from related work on piecewise likelihood and mixtures of trees.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of projecting parameters to enforce fast mixing is a fresh perspective on approximate inference. It shifts the focus from distribution approximation to ensuring efficient sampling.
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The paper provides solid theoretical justification for the Euclidean projection, leveraging results from matrix perturbation theory. The discussion of mixing time bounds and dependency matrices is accurate and relevant.
* **Multiple Divergence Measures:** Exploring different divergences (Euclidean, KL, reversed KL) provides a comparative analysis and highlights the trade-offs between computational complexity and approximation quality. The piecewise KL-divergence offers a pragmatic compromise.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup is reasonable, comparing the proposed methods to established baselines on both grid and random graph topologies. The inclusion of time-accuracy comparisons is valuable.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.  Definitions are clearly stated, and the notation is consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conservatism of Spectral Norm Bound:** The authors acknowledge that the used spectral norm bound of 2.5 appears overly conservative compared to the known critical value for the 2D Ising model. This limits the practical applicability of the method and raises questions about the tightness of the fast-mixing guarantee.  A discussion of how to refine this bound would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:**  While achieving better accuracy than other variational methods, the computational cost of the proposed methods, particularly the stochastic approximation for the reversed KL-divergence, is not thoroughly explored.  A more detailed analysis of the scaling with graph size and parameter dimensionality would be helpful.
* **Limited Exploration of Piecewise KL:** The piecewise KL-divergence, while conceptually promising, doesn't perform as well as the other methods in the experiments.  The paper doesn't delve deeply into why this is the case or explore alternative subgraph selection strategies that might improve performance.  A more thorough exploration of this approach is warranted.
* **Dependence on Tuning:** The stochastic gradient descent for the reversed KL-divergence requires significant parameter tuning (step size, pool size, iterations).  The chosen parameters are not fully justified, and a sensitivity analysis to these parameters would strengthen the results.
* **Lack of Scalability Discussion:** While experiments are conducted on graphs with 10 nodes, the paper does not explicitly address the scalability of these methods to larger, more realistic graphical models.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to approximate inference.
* The empirical results demonstrate that the proposed methods can outperform standard variational techniques in terms of accuracy.
* The exploration of multiple divergence measures offers valuable insights into the trade-offs involved in parameter projection.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The conservative spectral norm bound limits the practical applicability of the method.
* The computational cost of some of the methods is not fully assessed.
* The piecewise KL-divergence doesn't perform as well as expected and requires further investigation.
* The scalability of the methods to larger graphs remains unclear.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of approximate inference. The core idea of projecting parameters to enforce fast mixing is innovative and well-motivated. While there are some weaknesses, particularly concerning the conservatism of the spectral norm bound and computational cost, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

*   Discuss potential refinements to the spectral norm bound to reduce its conservatism.
*   Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed methods.
*   Explore alternative subgraph selection strategies for the piecewise KL-divergence.
*   Include a sensitivity analysis of the key parameters used in the stochastic gradient descent algorithm.
*   Briefly discuss the potential scalability challenges and future directions for addressing them.
