**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel concept for approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically Ising models. Instead of approximating an intractable distribution with one that is structurally simple (e.g., fully-factorized or tree-structured), the authors propose approximating it with a distribution from the set of "fast-mixing" models. These are models whose parameters satisfy conditions that guarantee rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling. The primary condition explored is that the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths, `|| |β| ||_2`, is less than 1. The main contribution is a set of algorithms for "projecting" an arbitrary (slow-mixing) set of Ising parameters onto this fast-mixing set. The authors derive a method for projection under the Euclidean norm and then extend this to handle KL-divergences using a projected stochastic gradient descent framework. Experiments on grid and random graphs show that sampling from the projected model can yield more accurate marginals in a fixed amount of time compared to standard variational methods or Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing model.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Conceptual Contribution:** The core idea of defining tractability by MCMC mixing time rather than by graphical structure (like treewidth) is novel and interesting. It offers a new perspective on approximation families and could inspire further work in this direction.
2.  **Technical Soundness of Euclidean Projection:** The derivation of a dual method (Theorem 7) to perform the Euclidean projection while preserving graph sparsity is a solid technical contribution.
3.  **Insightful Connection to Mean-Field:** The paper correctly frames naive mean-field as a degenerate case of its proposed KL(ψ||θ) projection, where the spectral norm constraint is tightened to zero. This provides a nice unifying view.
4.  **Promising Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method, particularly the zero-forcing KL projection, outperforms standard baselines like LBP, TRW, and MF, and can be more efficient than running Gibbs on the original model.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is appealing, the paper is marred by a significant number of methodological inconsistencies, unclear explanations, and a general lack of rigor and polish.

1.  **Abandonment of Theoretical Guarantees in Practice:** The most significant flaw is the disconnect between the theoretical motivation and the experimental implementation. The entire premise rests on projecting onto a set of parameters *guaranteed* to be fast-mixing, defined by the condition `|| |β| ||_2 < 1`. However, in Section 6.1, the authors state they use a spectral norm bound of 2.5 because it "gives better approximation". This completely undermines the paper's central claim. The method is no longer a projection onto a provably fast-mixing set but a heuristic regularization with an empirically tuned, unprincipled threshold. This choice is not justified and raises serious questions about the method's theoretical grounding. Is `c=2.5` universally applicable? How sensitive are the results to this choice?

2.  **Weak Motivation for Piecewise KL:** The "piecewise KL-divergence" is presented as a novel contribution, but its motivation is tenuous. The justification provided is trivial, and the choice of the `max` operator over subgraphs instead of, for instance, a sum, is not explained. The authors themselves admit it "does not perform as well as the true KL-divergence," which diminishes its significance as a contribution.

3.  **Lack of Rigor in Stochastic Approximation:** The gradient estimation for the zero-forcing KL divergence (Eq. 8) reuses the same samples to estimate both the mean `µ̂` and the expectation. The authors dismiss the resulting bias by stating it "makes little difference," which is not a sufficiently rigorous argument for a NIPS paper. Furthermore, the hyperparameters for the stochastic optimization (step size, sample pool, iterations) are chosen "without intensive tuning." The performance of the main proposed algorithm hinges on these choices, and their casual selection suggests the experimental results may not be robust.

4.  **Clarity and Notational Issues:** The manuscript suffers from numerous clarity issues.
    *   The use of `KL(θ||ψ)` and `KL(ψ||θ)` is ambiguous; `KL(p(x; θ) || p(x; ψ))` would be standard and clearer.
    *   The relationship between the matrices `A`, `B`, `R`, and the parameters `β` in Section 4 is confusingly presented.
    *   The statement in Section 6 that minimizing KL(θ||ψ) "doesn’t provide a useful measure of marginal accuracy" is poorly phrased. The point is that this objective enforces marginal matching by definition, making it unsuitable for *evaluating* marginal accuracy, not that it is a useless objective in general.
    *   A footnote is referenced in Section 5.3 ("distribution1") that does not appear to exist in the paper.
    *   The proof for Lemma 5 is omitted, with the assertion that "simple modifications to the proof can give this result." This should be included in an appendix for completeness.

5.  **Formatting and Presentation:** The paper exhibits a notable lack of care in its preparation. The bibliography is riddled with errors (e.g., `[1] SIAM J. Sci. Comput.,, , ,`). Multiple references have extra commas, inconsistent journal abbreviations, and missing page or volume numbers. Such sloppiness throughout the bibliography suggests the paper was prepared in a rush and not thoroughly proofread.

**Recommendation**

While the paper presents a genuinely interesting and novel idea, the execution is too flawed for acceptance at NIPS in its current form. The contradiction between the theoretical motivation (projecting onto a provably fast-mixing set) and the experimental practice (using an ad-hoc, larger bound) is a major conceptual issue. This, combined with the lack of rigor in the stochastic optimization, the weak motivation for one of the proposed divergences, and the overall poor state of the manuscript's presentation and formatting, leads me to recommend rejection. The core idea is strong enough that a substantially revised version that addresses these critical issues could be a valuable contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The central idea of using fast-mixing models as an approximation family is novel and could stimulate new research.
*   The zero-forcing KL projection method shows strong empirical performance against established baselines.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Major Flaw:** The theoretical justification for the method is abandoned in the experiments by using an arbitrary spectral norm bound (`c=2.5`) instead of the theoretically derived one (`c=1.0`).
*   The manuscript suffers from a lack of rigor concerning the motivation for the piecewise KL divergence and the analysis of the stochastic gradient estimator.
*   The paper is poorly written and formatted, with confusing notation, unclear statements, and numerous errors in the bibliography, indicating a lack of care unbecoming of a top-tier publication.
