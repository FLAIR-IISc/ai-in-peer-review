This paper introduces a novel and thought-provoking approach to approximate inference in Ising models. The central idea is to re-define the notion of a "tractable" model family. Instead of relying on structural properties like low treewidth that permit exact inference, the authors propose using a family of models for which Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing." Approximate inference is then framed as projecting an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing, set of Ising model parameters onto this fast-mixing set. This projection is explored under several different divergences.

The paper's key contributions are:
1.  The conceptual proposal of "fast-mixing" distributions as a tractable family for approximation. This is based on the condition that the spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix is less than a constant (`|| |β| ||_2 < 1`).
2.  An algorithm for projecting parameters onto this set under the Euclidean norm. This is achieved by deriving a dual optimization problem that is solved via iterative SVD thresholding (Theorem 7). This is a non-trivial technical result.
3.  The extension of this projection framework to other divergences, including a novel "piecewise KL-divergence" that approximates the true KL by computing it on tractable subgraphs, and the standard "zero-forcing" KL-divergence (`KL(ψ||θ)`). The latter is minimized using a projected stochastic gradient descent method, leveraging the fact that sampling from the fast-mixing approximation `ψ` is efficient.
4.  An experimental comparison on grid and random graphs, showing that the proposed methods outperform standard variational approaches like LBP, TRW, and Mean Field, and can provide more accurate marginals than running Gibbs sampling on the original slow-mixing model within a finite time budget.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The most compelling aspect of this paper is its originality. The idea of defining tractability in terms of MCMC mixing time rather than exact inference complexity is a fundamentally new perspective in the landscape of approximate inference. This is a bold conceptual leap that moves beyond the incremental improvements often seen in this area. It elegantly connects the literature on approximate variational inference with the rich theory of MCMC mixing times, potentially opening up a new and fruitful direction of research.
*   **Strong Conceptual Framework:** The paper frames the problem cleanly as a projection onto a constrained set. This is an appealing and generalizable framework. The exploration of different divergences (Euclidean, Piecewise KL, Reversed KL) within this framework is thorough and provides a nice trade-off between computational ease and probabilistic interpretation. The connection drawn between their reversed-KL projection and mean-field inference (as the degenerate case of zero spectral norm) is particularly insightful.
*   **Novel Technical Contributions:** Beyond the high-level concept, the paper delivers concrete technical contributions. The derivation of the dual algorithm for the sparse Euclidean projection is clever and useful. The proposed "piecewise KL-divergence" is also a creative and pragmatic idea for creating tractable approximations to an intractable objective, inspired by related work in structured prediction.
*   **Promising Empirical Validation:** While not exhaustive, the experiments provide a strong proof-of-concept. The proposed methods consistently outperform widely used baselines, demonstrating the practical viability of the core idea. The comparison with finite-time Gibbs sampling on the original parameters is particularly relevant, as it directly addresses the practical motivation for this work.

**Weaknesses / Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Looseness of the Theoretical Bound:** A primary weakness, which the authors openly acknowledge, is the conservative nature of the spectral norm bound for fast mixing. In the experiments, a heuristically chosen bound of 2.5 is used instead of the theoretically justified 1.0, because it yields better results while seemingly preserving the fast-mixing property. This disconnect between theory and practice somewhat undermines the "guarantee" of rapid mixing that is central to the paper's motivation. While the idea remains compelling, the specific set being projected onto is not as rigorously defined as one might hope.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small-scale problems (8x8 grids, 10-node random graphs). While sufficient for a proof-of-concept, the scalability of the projection algorithms, particularly the iterative SVD and the stochastic gradient methods, to much larger graphs is not demonstrated.
*   **Ad-hoc Nature of Stochastic Optimization:** The projected gradient descent for the reversed KL-divergence relies on hyperparameters (step size, sample pool size) that were chosen without extensive tuning. A more thorough analysis of the optimization procedure's sensitivity and convergence would strengthen the paper.

**Decision:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The weaknesses, while valid, are largely related to the completeness of the validation and the tightness of the underlying theory. They do not detract from the novelty and significance of the core conceptual contribution. This is precisely the kind of work NIPS should encourage: it presents a bold, new way of thinking about a fundamental problem. The idea of using fast-mixing as a criterion for tractability is elegant and powerful. The paper provides a solid theoretical and empirical foundation for this idea, and its limitations (like the loose bound) are themselves interesting and important avenues for the future work this paper will surely inspire. This work has the potential to be highly influential and to shift the conversation in the field of approximate inference.
