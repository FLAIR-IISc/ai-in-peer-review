Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes using “fast-mixing” parameter sets as a tractable family for approximate inference in Ising models. Instead of constraining graphical structure to be low-treewidth (as in mean-field [14], structured mean-field [16, 20], loopy BP [21], TRW [19], planar decompositions [6], or tree mixtures [15]), the authors constrain parameters so that univariate Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to mix rapidly. The guarantee is based on a sufficient condition from mixing-time theory: if the spectral norm of the dependency matrix R is less than 1, Gibbs mixes rapidly [4, 8, 10]. For Ising models, they bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij| (extending Hayes [8] beyond zero field), so a conservative sufficient condition is that the spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix is < 1.

Given an arbitrary parameter vector θ (possibly slow-mixing), the core idea is to project onto the fast-mixing set Ψ under various divergences D(θ, ψ): (i) Euclidean distance, with a dual algorithm that enforces sparsity and a spectral norm constraint via iterative singular-value thresholding (Theorem 7); (ii) the inclusive KL(θ||ψ), which is only practical on small graphs due to expectations under θ; (iii) a novel “piecewise” KL defined over tractable subgraphs; and (iv) the reverse KL(ψ||θ), which allows stochastic approximation because expectations are under ψ, and ψ is enforced to be fast-mixing. They interpret the reverse-KL projection as a generalization of mean-field (MF corresponds to the degenerate case where ||R||2 = 0).

Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare Euclidean projection, piecewise KL, and reverse-KL projection to MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs sampling on original parameters. Accuracy is assessed via mean absolute error of node marginals computed by exact junction-tree on these small instances. The authors report that fast-mixing projections yield more accurate marginals than MF/LBP/TRW, and for finite budgets can outperform Gibbs on the original parameters due to faster mixing. They also note using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 (rather than 1.0) in practice to improve accuracy, acknowledging the looseness of the theoretical bound.

Quality (technical soundness):
- Foundations: The use of the spectral norm of the dependency matrix for mixing guarantees is well-grounded [4, 8]. The Ising-specific bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is plausible (and commonly used); the paper claims a simple extension of [8] to nonzero fields.
- Optimization for Euclidean projection: The dual construction (Theorem 7) to enforce both the spectral norm constraint on |B| and sparsity via a mask Z is technically interesting. The computational strategy uses LBFGS-B with projection Πc[·] implemented via singular value thresholding (Theorem 6). There appear to be minor typographical issues (e.g., S′ii = min(Sii, c) rather than “c 2”), but the approach is standard and sound for spectral norm projections.
- Reverse KL gradient: The estimator for ∇ψ KL(ψ||θ) using Eψ[( (ψ−θ)·f(x) )(f(x)−μ(ψ))] is consistent with the score-function identity, and the stochastic approximation scheme is reasonable. The reliance on dependent MCMC samples is justified via Ergodic Mirror Descent [3], although detailed convergence rates or tuning guidelines are not provided.
- Piecewise KL: The proposed “max over subgraphs T of KL(θ(T)||ψ(T))” is tractable and differentiable but lacks theoretical guarantees relating it to the full KL; no bounds or consistency results are given.
- Guarantees vs practice: In experiments the authors often use a spectral norm bound (2.5) above the proven threshold (< 1), so fast mixing is not guaranteed, weakening the theoretical guarantee in empirical evaluation.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clearly written, with useful preliminaries on mixing times and concise derivations. Some notation switches between β and θ could be streamlined. A few typographical errors (e.g., missing 1/2 in the total variation definition, the threshold in Theorem 6) should be corrected. The dual derivation for Theorem 7 is said to be in the appendix; ensuring that proof is complete would strengthen clarity.

Originality:
The central idea of projecting to a fast-mixing set as a tractable family is novel relative to structure-based approximations (MF, structured MF, LBP, TRW, tree mixtures, planar). The dual Euclidean projection with sparsity and spectral constraints, and the use of reverse-KL with stochastic approximation under a fast-mixing constraint, are interesting contributions. The piecewise KL formulation is also a new variant, albeit heuristic.

Significance:
Conceptually, expanding the tractable family to fast-mixing parameter sets is appealing and may influence approximate inference and learning methods (e.g., constraining MCMC-based learning to a fast-mixing region). The empirical scope is limited to small graphs, and no real-world tasks are included. The looseness of the spectral norm bound for grids (noted by the authors) limits practical guarantees and may reduce immediate impact, but the direction is promising.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled formulation of a fast-mixing tractable family grounded in mixing-time theory [4, 8].
- Concrete algorithms for projection: dual Euclidean projection that preserves sparsity and enforces spectral norm constraints; stochastic approximation for reverse-KL.
- Novel “piecewise KL” idea leveraging tractable subgraphs.
- Empirical evidence (on small graphs) that fast-mixing projections can outperform common variational baselines for marginal estimation and offer favorable time-accuracy trade-offs.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Theoretical guarantees vs empirical settings: using a spectral norm bound above 1.0 undermines the fast-mixing guarantee in practice. Reporting empirical mixing diagnostics, or bounding norms ex post, would help.
- Bound looseness: the spectral criterion is conservative on grids (βc ≈ 0.4407 vs spectral bound ≈ 0.25). Exploring tighter sufficient conditions (e.g., Dobrushin, Weitz-tree uniqueness) or block-Gibbs conditions could improve utility.
- Scope of experiments: limited to 8×8 and 10-node graphs; include larger graphs and real datasets if possible. Provide quantitative plots/tables of errors, convergence, and wall-clock cost including projection time.
- Piecewise KL lacks theory: provide relationships to full KL (bounds or consistency), and study sensitivity to choice of subgraphs T.
- Optimization details: characterize computational complexity of the dual Euclidean projection (SVD frequency, scaling), and provide guidance on hyperparameters for the reverse-KL stochastic approximation; consider variance reduction (e.g., separate chains for μ̂ and ĝ).
- Minor clarity issues: fix TV definition factor, Theorem 6 threshold, and ensure the appendix includes complete proofs.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel angle on tractability via fast-mixing parameter sets, with solid theoretical underpinning.
- Practical algorithms with demonstrated gains over standard variational baselines on benchmark Ising models.
- Potential to influence both inference and learning constrained to fast-mixing regions.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is small-scale and excludes real-world tasks; runtime comparisons omit projection cost.
- The guarantees are not strictly upheld in experiments (using spectral bounds > 1), limiting the claimed “guaranteed fast mixing.”
- Piecewise KL is heuristic with limited theoretical justification.

Overall: The paper offers a fresh and technically sound perspective with promising empirical indications, but would benefit from stronger experimental validation and tighter alignment between guarantees and practice.
