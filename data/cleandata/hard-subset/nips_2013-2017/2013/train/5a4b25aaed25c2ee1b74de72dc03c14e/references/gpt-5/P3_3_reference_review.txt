Summary
The paper proposes a new tractable approximation family for inference in high-treewidth Ising models: the set of fast-mixing models under single-site Gibbs sampling. The key sufficient condition used is ||R||2 < 1, where R is the dependency matrix; for Ising models with pairwise couplings β, this is upper-bounded by |||β|||2 < 1 via Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|. Given an intractable θ, the authors study projections ψ ∈ Ψ (the fast-mixing set) minimizing different divergences: (i) Euclidean (Frobenius) distance on parameters, for which they derive a dual algorithm that enforces sparsity and a spectral-norm constraint by iterative SVD thresholding (Theorem 7); (ii) inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ) (mainly theoretical due to the need for expectations under θ); (iii) a piecewise KL that computes KL on tractable subgraphs and takes the max over a family of trees/low-treewidth subgraphs; (iv) exclusive KL KL(ψ||θ), optimized by stochastic approximation using samples from ψ, with a projected gradient step onto Ψ via the Euclidean projection. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare against MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs on the original parameters; the proposed projections often reduce marginal error versus variational baselines and offer better time-accuracy tradeoffs than running Gibbs on the original slow-mixing model for a fixed sampling budget.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on Dobrushin-type contraction criteria and more recent spectral-norm sufficient conditions for fast mixing (Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum; Hayes), specialized here to Ising models via Rij ≤ tanh|βij|. It situates itself relative to variational families (MF, structured MF, TRW, planar decompositions) by proposing fast-mixing families as an alternative “tractable” class. The reversed-KL objective leads to a covariance-preconditioned gradient equal to Covψ[f] (ψ−θ), which is consistent with exponential-family theory; the sampling-based optimization is related to Ergodic Mirror Descent.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: reframing tractability in terms of rapid mixing parameter regions and projecting onto this set. This is a refreshing alternative to graph-structural tractability.
- Technical contribution: a principled Euclidean projection under sparsity and spectral-norm constraints via a dual with SVD thresholding; the projection under |||β|||2 ≤ c is useful in its own right.
- Methodological breadth: compares several divergences (Euclidean, inclusive/reversed KL, piecewise KL). The reversed KL formulation connects to MF as the c=0 extreme.
- Empirical signals: on small-to-moderate graphs, projections often outperform standard variational approximations on marginal error; the time-accuracy comparison to Gibbs on original parameters is practically relevant.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Guarantees vs practice: The key selling point is a guaranteed fast-mixing family. Yet in experiments the authors use a spectral bound c=2.5, exceeding the sufficient condition c<1, thus voiding the guarantee. While this may still be fast-mixing empirically, it undercuts the paper’s core premise. At minimum, report the achieved |||β|||2 and empirical mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation times, Gelman-Rubin, coupling-based bounds, or drift/minorization estimates) to substantiate “rapid mixing” beyond anecdotes. A principled way to choose c (e.g., line search trading error vs mixing-time proxy) would strengthen the contribution.
- Tightness of bounds and choice of constraint: The dependency bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij| suggests a natural constraint ||tanh|β|||2 < 1, which is strictly weaker than |||β|||2 < 1. The paper acknowledges looseness but does not attempt to exploit tanh for a tighter feasible set (e.g., constraining ||tanh|β|||2 ≤ c); this could significantly enlarge Ψ and reduce approximation error while retaining guarantees.
- The Euclidean projection formulation: The problem minB,D ||A−B||F s.t. ||D||2 ≤ c, Z⊙D=0, D=|B| is nonconvex because of D=|B|. The paper replaces A with R=|A| and appears to optimize over magnitudes, implicitly assuming sign(Bij)=sign(Aij). This assumption is plausible (the Frobenius objective decouples across entries given fixed D), but it should be stated and proved. Otherwise, Theorem 7’s “equivalence” is unclear. Moreover, details of the dual derivation and the exact meaning of Λ′Z (Hadamard product?) should be clarified; currently the notation in Eq. 2–4 is terse and easy to misread.
- Possible typo in Theorem 6: For projection onto the spectral-norm ball, singular values are clipped at c, not “c 2”. Please check S′ii = min(Sii, c).
- Piecewise KL choice: Defining D(θ,ψ)=maxT KL(θ(T)||ψ(T)) yields a non-smooth objective and a worst-case subgraph focus that may be overly conservative and not obviously correlated with the full-graph KL or marginal error. A convex combination (weights over T) or a composite likelihood connection might be better motivated and smoother to optimize. Also, it would help to discuss whether this quantity is a divergence (e.g., when can maxT KL(·) be zero yet θ≠ψ?).
- Optimization and convergence: The reversed-KL objective is typically nonconvex; the projected gradient with biased stochastic gradients (same samples used twice) and a fixed step size lacks convergence guarantees. While Ergodic Mirror Descent is cited, the conditions (increasing MCMC steps, decreasing step sizes) are not used. Empirical stability is useful, but some ablation (pool size, steps per iteration) and sensitivity analysis would help.
- Scalability: The dual projection repeatedly calls an SVD on dense matrices and solves an LBFGS-B problem. Complexity and memory growth with n should be discussed. The experiments are limited to n≤64; a runtime plot and analysis of number of SVDs per projection would help assess practical utility.
- Experimental methodology: 
  - The comparison to Gibbs on original parameters does not control for total compute (projection can be costly). A fair-time comparison would be more compelling (e.g., allocate the same wall-clock budget to projection+sampling vs long-run Gibbs).
  - The evaluation uses exact marginals for ground truth, which is great; however, plots/tables showing error vs coupling strength, and the achieved spectral norms post-projection, would make the story clearer. 
  - It would be informative to separate attractive vs mixed regimes, and to include stronger baselines such as generalized BP or planar decomposition (when applicable).
- Clarity and notation: Some notation is heavy (triple dot product) and insufficiently defined (Λ′Z). The dependency R bound (Lemma 5) is stated without proof; a short proof sketch would be helpful.

Questions for the authors
- Can you formalize the sign argument that reduces the Euclidean projection with D=|B| to an optimization over magnitudes with signs matching A? Is Theorem 7 exact under this assumption?
- Why not constrain ||tanh|β|||2 ≤ c to tighten Ψ while preserving guarantees? Any computational obstacles?
- Can you report empirical mixing diagnostics and the achieved spectral norms to justify c=2.5 still being fast mixing in your settings?
- How many SVDs does the dual solver require per projection on average, and how does runtime scale with n?

Pros
- Novel perspective: tractability via fast-mixing parameter regions.
- Useful projection operator under spectral norms with sparsity.
- Clear connection of reversed KL gradient to exponential-family covariance.
- Promising empirical gains over standard variational baselines on small graphs.

Cons
- Theoretical guarantees are not honored in experiments (c>1) and no mixing diagnostics are provided.
- Nonconvexity and missing details in the Euclidean projection equivalence/sign handling.
- Piecewise KL is weakly justified and non-smooth.
- Limited scale and compute-uncontrolled comparisons.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, and the Euclidean projection machinery is a solid contribution. However, the central “fast-mixing” guarantee is not convincingly demonstrated in practice, key optimization details need clarification, and the empirical study is limited in scale and fairness. With tighter constraints (or better bounds), stronger diagnostics, and clearer theory, this work could be a strong contribution.
