- Addresses approximate inference in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a set guaranteed to be fast-mixing instead of simplifying graph structure.  
- Leverages MCMC theory: Gibbs sampling mixes rapidly if the spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix < 1, and uses this as the tractability criterion.  
- Develops algorithms to find the closest fast-mixing parameters under four divergences: Euclidean, forward KL (KL(q||p)), piecewise KL, and reverse KL (KL(p||q)).  
- Derives a dual optimization algorithm for Euclidean projection that handles sparsity constraints (Theorem 7).  
- Proposes a practical stochastic gradient descent method to minimize the reverse KL using expectations under the fast-mixing distribution.  
- Introduces a novel piecewise KL-divergence that approximates full KL on tractable subgraphs.  
- Experiments on grid and random graphs show Gibbs sampling on projected parameters yields more accurate marginals than LBP, TRW, MF and outperforms Gibbs on original parameters under time constraints.  
- Evaluates accuracy against exact marginals via junction-tree, providing a fair and reliable benchmark.  
- Paper is technically sound, clearly written, and logically organized; connects reversed KL projection to mean-field as a special case.  
- Empirical section should better justify the experimental spectral-norm bound of 2.5 (theory uses 1.0) and analyze sensitivity or tuning of this value.  
- The piecewise KL method is introduced but its empirical results are not presented or discussed, leaving an experimental gap.  
- The contribution is highly original, combining MCMC mixing theory and variational ideas in a new way; piecewise KL is a novel element.  
- Significance: opens a new research direction, with practical impact for time-limited inference and potential extensions to learning and other models.  
- A notable missing evaluation: the computational overhead of the projection step—total time (projection + sampling) versus standard Gibbs is not compared.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel tractability notion, solid theory and algorithms, strong empirical gains, and clear writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear motivation for experimental spectral-norm choice and lack of accounting for projection computational cost.
