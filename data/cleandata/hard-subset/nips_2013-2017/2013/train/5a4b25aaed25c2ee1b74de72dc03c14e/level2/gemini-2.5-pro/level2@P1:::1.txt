This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models. The central idea is to define a family of "tractable" distributions not by their graphical structure (e.g., low treewidth), but by their mixing properties. Specifically, the authors define a set of models that are guaranteed to be "fast mixing" for Gibbs sampling, based on a sufficient condition from the MCMC literature: the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths must be less than one. The main contribution is a set of algorithms for projecting an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing Ising model onto this fast-mixing set.

The authors explore projections under four different divergences:
1.  The standard Euclidean distance between parameter vectors, for which they derive an efficient dual optimization algorithm based on iterative SVD thresholding.
2.  The inclusive KL divergence KL(q||p), which is theoretically motivated but computationally intractable for the target distributions (q) of interest.
3.  A novel "piecewise" KL divergence, which approximates the full KL divergence by maximizing over the exact KL divergences on a set of tractable subgraphs.
4.  The exclusive KL divergence KL(p||q), which is tractable because expectations are taken with respect to the fast-mixing distribution (p). This is minimized using a projected stochastic gradient method. The authors nicely frame this as a generalization of mean-field inference.

Experiments on grid and random graphs demonstrate that Gibbs sampling on the projected parameters yields more accurate marginal estimates than standard variational methods like LBP, TRW, and Mean Field. Furthermore, for a fixed computational budget, their method is shown to be more accurate than running Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing parameters.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core concept of defining tractability via mixing time and using projection as an inference mechanism is highly original and significant. It presents a new paradigm for approximate inference that elegantly connects the fields of variational methods and MCMC theory. This approach could inspire a new line of research into approximation families defined by dynamics rather than structure.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The paper is well-grounded in established MCMC theory. The derivation of the Euclidean projection algorithm via a dual formulation is a solid technical contribution. The use of projected stochastic gradient descent for the KL(p||q) projection is a practical and principled way to handle the intractable optimization problem.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. It provides the necessary background on mixing time bounds, systematically presents each projection method, and explains the experimental setup and results effectively. The connection drawn between the KL(p||q) projection and mean-field inference is particularly insightful.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the proposed method's effectiveness. The comparison against a strong set of baselines (LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original model) convincingly demonstrates that for time-limited inference scenarios, running a fast chain on an approximate model is superior to running a slow chain on the exact one.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice in the Mixing Bound:** A central premise of the paper is the "guarantee" of fast mixing provided by the spectral norm bound of 1.0. However, the experiments use a bound of 2.5, stating that this works better in practice. This raises an important question: is the theoretical bound too conservative to be useful for creating accurate approximations? While the authors are commendably transparent about this in the discussion, it weakens the paper's main claim. The work would be strengthened by a more thorough investigation of this gap. For instance, an ablation study showing performance as the spectral norm bound is varied from 1.0 to 2.5 would be very informative.

2.  **Computational Cost of Projection:** The projection step itself, particularly for the KL divergence, involves a non-trivial iterative optimization procedure. The paper's experiments compare the runtime of Gibbs sampling *after* the projection is complete. While this is a fair comparison of sampling efficiency, it omits the potentially significant upfront "training" cost of finding the projection. It would be valuable to discuss this cost in more detail and perhaps include a comparison that incorporates the projection time, to give a fuller picture of the total computational effort required.

3.  **Performance of Piecewise KL:** The "piecewise KL" is introduced as a novel contribution. However, its experimental performance is not clearly distinguished from the other methods in the results section. Clarifying how this specific method performed relative to the Euclidean and reversed-KL projections would help in assessing the value of this particular contribution.

**Summary of pros and cons for acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** Highly novel and significant conceptual contribution, introducing a new way to think about approximate inference.
*   **Pro:** The approach is technically sound and well-grounded in established theory.
*   **Pro:** Experimental results are strong and demonstrate clear practical benefits over widely used methods in time-limited settings.
*   **Con:** There is a significant disconnect between the theoretical spectral norm bound that "guarantees" fast mixing and the relaxed bound used in practice, which needs further justification.
*   **Con:** The non-trivial computational cost of the projection step is not fully accounted for in the experimental comparisons.

Overall, this is a very strong paper that introduces a creative and promising new direction for research in approximate inference. The strengths, particularly the novelty and significance of the core idea, substantially outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is well-executed and the results are compelling. It is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community and to inspire future work.
