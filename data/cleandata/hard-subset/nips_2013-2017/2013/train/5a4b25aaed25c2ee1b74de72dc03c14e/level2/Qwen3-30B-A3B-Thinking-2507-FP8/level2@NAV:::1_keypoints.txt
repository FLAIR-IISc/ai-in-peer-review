- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set as an alternative to low-treewidth approximations.
- Claims projections under Euclidean, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences yield distributions that mix fast and can be efficiently sampled with Gibbs.
- Main benefit: projected distributions give better accuracy than original parameters when interactions are strong and sampling time is limited.
- Theorem 7: provides an efficient Euclidean projection algorithm via singular value thresholding.
- Experiments show the method outperforms standard variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) and beats Gibbs on original parameters under constrained compute.
- Authors acknowledge their spectral norm bound is conservative (≈1.76× looser than the 2D Ising threshold).
- Paper is well-written; theoretical development is sound and experiments are thorough across graph topologies and interaction strengths.
- Notable insight: mean-field inference appears as a degenerate case of their framework (Section 5.4).
- Weakness: paper does not explain why the spectral norm bound is conservative or how to tighten it.
- Weakness: piecewise KL divergence is described but not thoroughly evaluated against theoretically grounded reversed KL.
- Weakness: lacks a detailed discussion of projection time vs. sampling time trade-offs to guide practical use.
- Overall: strong, practically useful contribution to approximate inference for high-treewidth models; minor limitations do not significantly reduce impact.
