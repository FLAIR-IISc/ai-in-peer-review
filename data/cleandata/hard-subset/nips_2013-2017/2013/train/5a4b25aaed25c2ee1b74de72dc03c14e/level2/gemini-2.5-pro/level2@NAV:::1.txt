This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models where exact inference is intractable. Instead of approximating the model with a structurally simple one (e.g., low-treewidth), the authors define a "tractable" model as one that is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing" for Gibbs sampling. The core contribution is an algorithm to project the parameters of an arbitrary Ising model onto this set of fast-mixing models, where the fast-mixing property is guaranteed by a bound on the spectral norm of the interaction matrix. The paper explores this projection under several divergences: Euclidean distance, a novel "piecewise" KL divergence, and the forward and reverse KL-divergences. This work presents a compelling alternative to established variational methods like mean-field and belief propagation, which rely on structural rather than dynamic notions of tractability.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of defining tractability via fast mixing is novel and significant. It opens a new and promising direction for approximate inference, shifting the focus from the static structure of a model to the dynamic behavior of sampling algorithms on it.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of a dual algorithm to perform the Euclidean projection while preserving graph sparsity is a solid contribution. The application of stochastic projected gradient descent to minimize the KL divergence is well-motivated and appropriate.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It clearly explains the theoretical background on mixing times, develops the projection algorithms, and presents a thorough experimental evaluation.
*   **Experimental Support:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the method's utility. For a fixed computational budget, sampling from the projected parameters yields more accurate marginals than both standard variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) and Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing parameters, supporting the paper's main claims.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The primary weakness, which the authors commendably acknowledge, is the gap between the theoretical spectral norm bound for fast mixing (<1) and the empirically optimal one (~2.5). This suggests the theoretical condition is quite loose for the models tested. While this does not invalidate the approach, a deeper discussion or preliminary analysis of this gap would strengthen the paper.
*   The proposed "piecewise KL" divergence is an interesting idea but is reported to perform poorly. The paper might be more focused if this section were condensed or moved to an appendix to allow more space for analysis of the more successful methods.

In summary, this is a strong and well-executed paper that introduces a novel and practically useful framework for approximate inference. The identified weaknesses are minor and represent avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws. I recommend acceptance.
