Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes using “fast-mixing” distributions as a tractable family for approximate inference in Ising models. Rather than ensuring exact tractable structure (e.g., trees or low treewidth), the approach constrains parameters to a set for which single-site Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to mix rapidly, via a sufficient condition ||R||2 < 1 on the spectral norm of the dependency matrix R. For Ising models, R can be bounded in terms of pairwise interactions, giving a concrete spectral-norm constraint on |β|. Given (possibly slow-mixing) parameters θ, the paper studies projections onto the fast-mixing set under several divergences: (i) Euclidean distance in parameter space, enabled by a dual formulation with iterative SVD thresholding that preserves a graph sparsity mask; (ii) inclusive KL(θ||ψ), mainly of theoretical interest due to requiring expectations under θ; (iii) a piecewise KL that computes exact KL on a set of tractable subgraphs and takes the max; (iv) reverse KL(ψ||θ), optimized with stochastic approximation using samples from ψ, which is constrained to be fast-mixing. The reverse-KL approach is positioned as a generalization of mean-field, replacing the fully-factorized family with the fast-mixing family. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare these projections to LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original parameters, showing that projections often yield better marginal estimates when sampling time is limited.

Quality:
- Theory: The use of the spectral norm of the dependency matrix (Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Hayes) as a sufficient mixing condition is standard and correctly cited. For Ising, the bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is plausible and widely used. The dual projection (Theorem 7) is a key technical piece that addresses both spectral norm control and sparsity preservation via a mask; the optimization via LBFGS-B with simple bound constraints seems appropriate. The gradient for reverse KL (Theorem 8) matches the Fisher-information form ∇ψ KL(ψ||θ) = Covψ[f]·(ψ−θ), expressed as Eψ[((ψ−θ)·f(x))(f(x)−μ(ψ))], which is correct and enables a straightforward Monte Carlo estimator.
- Algorithms: The projected gradient scheme for general divergences is reasonable, with Euclidean projections as a subroutine. The stochastic approximation for reverse KL, reusing a “pool” of samples updated by one Gibbs sweep per iteration, is a pragmatic instantiation of ergodic mirror descent.
- Experiments: The setup (grids, small random graphs, varying strength and sign of interactions) is sensible. Evaluating marginals via exact junction tree provides a strong ground truth at the tested scales. Comparisons to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on original parameters are relevant. The time–accuracy discussion is candid; however, the wall-clock accounting excludes the cost of projection for the proposed methods.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with appropriate references to mixing-time literature and variational inference. The role of R and the spectral constraint is well motivated. Some derivations are deferred to the appendix (e.g., Theorem 7 dual), which is fine, but a brief discussion of convexity/tightness would help. A minor clarification: the definition of total variation distance typically includes a 1/2 factor.

Originality:
The central idea—defining a tractable family via fast-mixing conditions and projecting onto it—appears novel and distinct from the more common tree/planar/low-treewidth or convex free energy families. The piecewise KL construction and the reverse-KL-within-fast-mixing-family as a generalization of mean-field are interesting contributions. The dual projection that enforces a spectral constraint on |β| while preserving sparsity via a mask is also technically novel.

Significance:
The approach offers a new angle for approximate inference: rather than relaxing model structure, relax the parameter regime to enable rapid sampling. In regimes where exact inference is impossible and mixing is otherwise prohibitively slow, this can be impactful. Practitioners who rely on MCMC with hard time budgets may benefit from such projections. The main limitations are the conservativeness of the spectral-norm condition and the computational overhead of repeated projections/SVDs. Still, the idea should inspire further work on tighter mixing conditions, extensions beyond Ising, and integration with learning.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled use of mixing-time theory to define a tractable approximation family.
- Technically interesting projection method (Theorem 7) that addresses spectral constraints and sparsity.
- Reverse-KL optimization with provably estimable gradients from fast-mixing ψ; connects to and extends mean-field.
- Sensible experimental comparisons showing advantages under limited sampling time.
- Honest discussion of the conservativeness of the spectral criterion and practical deviations (using a larger threshold).

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Theoretical/practical gap: experiments often relax the spectral threshold beyond 1.0 (e.g., 2.5), voiding the fast-mixing guarantee. Please quantify how often the relaxed models still mix rapidly (e.g., empirical spectral gap/autocorrelation times) and provide failure cases.
- Runtime accounting: include total compute (projection + sampling) in time–accuracy plots. Also report SVD/dual optimization cost and convergence behavior.
- The piecewise KL variant underperforms; more analysis of why (choice of T, “max over T” vs weighted average, subgradient instability) would be useful. Consider a smoothed max or TRW-like convex combination over subgraphs.
- Dual projection details: clarify whether the projection solves the original nonconvex problem with D=|B| exactly or a relaxation; discuss optimality conditions and uniqueness.
- Sensitivity analyses: step-size scheduling for reverse-KL, sample-pool size, number of Gibbs updates per iteration, and impact of using distinct samples for μ̂ and gradient.
- Scope: extend discussion to other sufficient conditions (e.g., Dobrushin’s L1 criterion), block Gibbs, or Swendsen–Wang; could alternative constraints yield less conservative sets or better approximations?
- Notation: explicitly state symmetry of β and handling of self-potentials; reaffirm that α does not affect the mixing bound and whether α is left unchanged in projection.

Questions for the authors:
1) How do you select the spectral threshold c in practice, and can you provide a heuristic that adapts c to target a desired mixing time?  
2) Do projected parameters remain sparse in large graphs, or does the dual solution tend to densify within the mask? Please report average degree post-projection.  
3) Can you provide empirical mixing-time (or integrated autocorrelation time) measurements to validate the guarantee (or its relaxation) on the tested instances?  
4) How sensitive is reverse-KL optimization to initialization (e.g., θ projected in Euclidean norm vs zero)?  
5) Would a convex combination of subgraphs in the piecewise KL (akin to TRW) improve stability and performance?

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and well-motivated problem formulation (fast-mixing family) with concrete algorithms.  
- Potentially useful in practice for time-limited MCMC.  
- Solid theoretical grounding and connections to existing variational methods.  
- Empirical evidence of improved accuracy over standard approximations under realistic constraints.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Guarantees are not adhered to in main empirical setting (relaxed threshold), and practical mixing is not rigorously measured.  
- Computational overhead of projection not fully accounted for; scalability unclear.  
- Piecewise KL lacks strong empirical benefits and needs deeper analysis.

Overall, this is an interesting and thoughtful contribution that opens a promising line of work. With additional empirical validation of mixing under relaxed thresholds and clearer runtime/scalability reporting, the paper would be even stronger.
