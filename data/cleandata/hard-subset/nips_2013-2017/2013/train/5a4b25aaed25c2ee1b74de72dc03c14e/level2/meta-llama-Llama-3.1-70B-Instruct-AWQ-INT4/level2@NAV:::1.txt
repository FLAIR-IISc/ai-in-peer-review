This paper introduces a novel approach to inference in high-treewidth graphical models by projecting the original model parameters onto a set that guarantees fast mixing of Gibbs sampling. The authors propose a new notion of "tractability" that focuses on parameter-space conditions for rapid mixing rather than exact inference. They derive a dual algorithm to solve the projection problem under several divergences, including Euclidean distance, piecewise KL-divergence, and zero-forcing KL-divergence.

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the mixing time bounds for Ising models. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the proposed methods to Gibbs sampling on the original parameters and several standard variational methods. The results show that the proposed methods are more accurate than variational approximations and can provide better results than Gibbs sampling with finite time.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the technical details. The organization is logical, and the paper is easy to follow. The references are comprehensive, and the authors have done a good job of situating their work within the broader context of graphical models and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods.

The originality of the paper lies in its novel approach to inference in high-treewidth graphical models. The authors' focus on parameter-space conditions for rapid mixing is a new direction that has the potential to lead to more efficient and accurate inference algorithms. The piecewise KL-divergence and zero-forcing KL-divergence are also novel contributions that can be useful in other contexts.

The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a fundamental problem in graphical models and provides a new approach to inference that can be widely applicable. The results have implications for a range of applications, including computer vision, natural language processing, and statistical physics.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The spectral norm bound used to guarantee rapid mixing is conservative, and the authors acknowledge that a tighter bound would be more informative. Additionally, the paper focuses on Ising models, and it is unclear how the approach will generalize to more complex models.

In terms of future work, the authors suggest extending the approach to general Markov random fields, which will require finding a bound on the dependency matrix and developing an algorithm to project onto the fast-mixing set. They also suggest using fast-mixing distributions for learning, which could be a promising direction.

Overall, the paper is a significant contribution to the field of graphical models and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods. It introduces a novel approach to inference that has the potential to lead to more efficient and accurate algorithms, and it provides a thorough analysis of the technical details.
