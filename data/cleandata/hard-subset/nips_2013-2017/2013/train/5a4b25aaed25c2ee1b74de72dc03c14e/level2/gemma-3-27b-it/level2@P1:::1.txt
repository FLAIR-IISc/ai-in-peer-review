This paper explores the idea of projecting Ising model parameters onto a set guaranteed to yield faster mixing in Gibbs sampling. The core idea is compelling: instead of directly inferring with potentially slow-mixing parameters, transform them to a space where convergence is faster, potentially trading off some accuracy for speed. The authors investigate several divergence measures for this projection, including Euclidean distance (via SVD thresholding), KL divergence (both theoretically and via piecewise approximations), and a reversed KL divergence amenable to stochastic approximation. The experiments demonstrate that projecting parameters can lead to more accurate results than using the original parameters when time is limited, and compare favorably against standard variational inference techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of explicitly projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set, rather than focusing solely on inference algorithms, is a fresh perspective.  It frames tractability as a property of the parameter space itself.
* **Thoroughness:** The authors consider multiple divergence measures for the projection, analyzing their trade-offs in terms of computational cost and theoretical properties.  The inclusion of both KL(θ||ψ) and KL(ψ||θ) is a strength.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The paper provides sufficient background on mixing times and dependency matrices, linking the work to existing theory.  Theorem 7, providing a dual method for the Euclidean projection, is a valuable contribution.
* **Empirical validation:** The experimental setup is reasonable, and the comparisons to other inference algorithms (LBP, TRW, MF, and original Gibbs) provide a good benchmark.  The results demonstrate practical benefits of the projection approach.
* **Clear writing:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the complex concepts accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Loose Spectral Norm Bound:** The discussion in Section 7 correctly points out the potential conservatism of the spectral norm bound (||R|| < 1).  Using a bound of 2.5 seems ad-hoc.  Further investigation into tighter bounds (e.g., connecting to known critical values for specific Ising models) would strengthen the theoretical foundations.  The justification for using 2.5 feels purely empirical, and doesn't offer insight.
* **Piecewise KL Divergence Performance:** The piecewise KL divergence doesn’t perform as well as the true KL divergence (as expected, given the approximation). However, the paper doesn't fully explore the impact of different subgraph choices (e.g., tree structures vs. low-treewidth graphs) on its performance.
* **Stochastic Approximation Tuning:** The parameters for the stochastic gradient descent used to minimize the zero-forcing KL divergence (step size, pool size, iterations) appear somewhat arbitrary. While the authors acknowledge this, a more systematic sensitivity analysis or justification of these choices would be beneficial.  A claim of “reasonable performance” doesn’t fully justify the parameter setting.
* **Limited Scope:** While the focus on Ising models allows for a detailed analysis, the paper doesn't address the generalizability of the approach to other Markov Random Fields.  Acknowledging the challenges and potential extensions to other models would be valuable.
* **Missing Related Work:** While references are present, the paper could benefit from citing more recent work on spectral graph theory and its application to Markov chain mixing times.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful idea for improving inference in graphical models.
* The theoretical analysis is sound and provides a solid foundation for the proposed approach.
* The experimental results demonstrate practical benefits, particularly in scenarios with limited computational resources.
* The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The spectral norm bound appears somewhat loose and could be improved.
* The stochastic approximation parameters are not well-justified.
* The scope is limited to Ising models.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. The core idea is innovative, the technical work is solid, and the experimental results are promising. While the weaknesses noted above are valid, they do not fundamentally undermine the contribution of the paper. The authors have identified a potentially valuable direction for research, and the paper provides a strong starting point for future work. Addressing the loose spectral norm bound in future work would be particularly beneficial.
