Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes approximating intractable Ising models by projecting their parameters onto a set guaranteed to be fast-mixing for single-site Gibbs sampling. The guarantee leverages bounds on the dependency matrix and the sufficient condition ||R||2 < 1 (Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum; Hayes). The authors (i) derive a Euclidean projection that preserves sparsity via a dual formulation and SVD thresholding (Theorem 7), (ii) explore several divergences for projection (Euclidean, inclusive KL via tractable subgraphs, reversed KL optimized by stochastic approximation/Ergodic Mirror Descent), and (iii) empirically show that Gibbs sampling on the projected model achieves better finite-time marginal accuracy than sampling on the original model when interactions are strong. The work connects to variational methods (MF, TRW, LBP), planar/tractable substructures (Globerson & Jaakkola; mixtures of trees; piecewise training), and mixing-time theory.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled framing of “tractability” via fast mixing, distinct from treewidth-based approximations.
- A practical Euclidean projection algorithm that respects sparsity and graph structure.
- A stochastic optimization scheme for KL(ψ||θ) that generalizes MF beyond fully factorized families.
- Empirical evidence that finite-time accuracy can improve by projecting to fast mixing, with comparisons to LBP/TRW/MF.

Weaknesses:
- Guarantees vs practice: experiments use a spectral-norm bound of 2.5 (vs 1.0), undermining provable rapid mixing; no reporting of observed ||R||2 or mixing times.
- Limited scale (8×8 grids, n=10 random graphs), no real-world datasets, and no statistical significance/error bars.
- Evaluation does not account for projection cost, complicating time-accuracy tradeoffs; no comparison to block Gibbs or Swendsen–Wang.
- The piecewise KL construction is under-motivated empirically and underperforms; design choices for subgraphs are heuristic.
- Some clarity issues: consistency between random vs systematic-scan assumptions; ambiguity around how ground-truth marginals and estimates are obtained.

Quality: Sound core theory (standard mixing bounds; dual for Euclidean projection), but experimental methodology could be tighter and better justified.

Clarity: Generally well written; Theorem statements and algorithms are clear. Add precise evaluation protocol and report norms/mixing diagnostics.

Originality: Moderate. The notion of projecting onto a fast-mixing family and the dual projection are novel; the reversals of KL connect to known MF perspectives.

Significance: Promising direction for finite-time inference and potentially for learning with MCMC-based gradients.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, principled approach; clear potential impact on practical MCMC.
- Solid integration of mixing-time theory and optimization; constructive empirical trends.

Cons for acceptance:
- Weak empirical breadth/rigor; guarantees not adhered to in practice.
- Missing runtime/mixing diagnostics and stronger baselines.

Constructive suggestions:
- Report ||R||2 before/after projection and empirical mixing-time estimates; include time-to-accuracy curves including projection overhead.
- Evaluate on larger grids and compare to block Gibbs/Swendsen–Wang.
- Ablate divergences and sensitivity to step sizes, pool size, and chain length; provide error bars.
- Either enforce the provable bound in experiments or systematically study performance as the bound is relaxed.
- Release code and subgraph selection details for reproducibility.
