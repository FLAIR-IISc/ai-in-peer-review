- Novel idea: define tractable distributions by mixing properties (fast mixing) rather than graphical structure.
- Use a spectral-norm condition (matrix of absolute interactions < 1) as a sufficient fast-mixing guarantee and project arbitrary Ising models onto this set.
- Main contribution: algorithms to compute projections under four different divergences.
- Euclidean projection: efficient dual optimization using iterative SVD thresholding.
- Inclusive KL (KL(q||p)): theoretically motivated but intractable for the target distributions q.
- Piecewise KL: novel approximation maximizing exact KLs on tractable subgraphs, but its empirical benefit is unclear.
- Exclusive KL (KL(p||q)): tractable because expectations are under the fast-mixing p; optimized with projected stochastic gradient; related to mean-field.
- Experiments: Gibbs sampling on projected parameters yields more accurate marginals than LBP, TRW, and Mean Field.
- For a fixed computational budget, projected-model Gibbs outperforms Gibbs on the original slow-mixing model.
- Strength: highly original and significant conceptual contribution linking variational methods and MCMC theory.
- Strength: technically sound derivations and practical optimization methods.
- Strength: clear writing and insightful connection between KL(p||q) projection and mean-field inference.
- Strength: strong experimental validation against solid baselines in time-limited settings.
- Weakness: theoretical mixing bound (spectral norm < 1) is conservative; experiments use relaxed bound (2.5), creating a theory-practice gap that needs further study (e.g., ablation over bounds).
- Weakness: projection optimization (especially for KL) can be computationally expensive and projection time is not fully accounted for in runtime comparisons.
- Weakness: empirical performance of the piecewise KL is not clearly distinguished from other methods in results.
- Overall: strong and promising paper; novelty and results outweigh weaknesses, but needs more justification for the relaxed bound and accounting of projection cost.
- Pros for acceptance: novel concept, solid theory, and convincing experiments.
- Cons for acceptance: disconnect between theoretical guarantee and practical settings; unaccounted projection computational cost.
