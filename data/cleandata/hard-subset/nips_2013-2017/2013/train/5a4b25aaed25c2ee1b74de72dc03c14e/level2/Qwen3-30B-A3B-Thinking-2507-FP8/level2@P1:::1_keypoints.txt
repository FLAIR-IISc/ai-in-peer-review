- Introduces projecting Ising-model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set (||R||₂ < 1) so Gibbs sampling converges rapidly, shifting tractability from graph structure to interaction strengths.  
- Derives projection algorithms under multiple divergences: Euclidean (via singular value thresholding), piecewise KL, and reversed KL (optimized by stochastic approximation).  
- Experiments on grids and random graphs show projected-parameter Gibbs outperforms variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) in marginal accuracy under time constraints; original-parameter Gibbs converges to higher accuracy only with exponentially more time.  
- Strength — Originality: Defining tractability by mixing-time guarantees is novel and broadens beyond structure-based approximations, with thorough related-work comparison.  
- Strength — Technical quality: Sound theoretical foundations (Theorems 4,5,7), clean Euclidean projection derivation, and practical stochastic optimization for reversed KL.  
- Strength — Significance: Tackles slow Gibbs convergence in strong-interaction regimes and demonstrates valuable time-accuracy tradeoffs; potential extension to general MRFs.  
- Weakness — Spectral norm bound is conservative (e.g., 2D Ising threshold mismatch); needs more comparison with tighter bounds and discussion of empirical permissiveness.  
- Weakness — Piecewise KL lacks justification for subgraph choices (e.g., treewidth-2 chains, spanning trees) and missing computational cost vs. accuracy discussion.  
- Weakness — Algorithm clarity: Dual optimization (Theorem 7) is dense; a high-level pseudocode (especially for Euclidean projection) would aid reproducibility.  
- Weakness — Experimental scope limited to small graphs (8×8 grids, 10-node random graphs); should discuss scalability and complexity (e.g., Euclidean projection O(n³) vs. stochastic KL scalability).  
- Overall assessment: Strong, novel, and empirically validated contribution that meaningfully improves approximate inference under time constraints.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
