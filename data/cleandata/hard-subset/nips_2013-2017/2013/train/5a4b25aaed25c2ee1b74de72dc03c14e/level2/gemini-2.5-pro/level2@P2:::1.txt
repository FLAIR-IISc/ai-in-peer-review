This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models, particularly for models with strong interactions where MCMC methods like Gibbs sampling mix slowly. Instead of approximating the graphical model structure (as in mean-field or loopy belief propagation), the authors propose to approximate the model's parameters. The core idea is to project an intractable (slow-mixing) set of parameters onto the set of parameters that are guaranteed to be "fast mixing." This notion of tractability is defined by a sufficient condition from prior work, which states that if the spectral norm of the dependency matrix is less than one, Gibbs sampling converges rapidly. For Ising models, this can be related to the spectral norm of the absolute interaction strengths being less than a constant.

The paper develops algorithms for this projection under several different divergences. First, for the Euclidean distance between parameter vectors, a dual algorithm is derived (Theorem 7) that can perform the projection efficiently via iterative SVD thresholding while preserving the graph's sparsity structure. This Euclidean projection then serves as a key subroutine for minimizing more probabilistically motivated divergences. The authors consider the standard KL-divergence KL(q||p), a novel "piecewise" KL-divergence approximation based on tractable subgraphs, and the reversed KL-divergence KL(p||q). The reversed KL projection is solved using a stochastic projected gradient descent scheme, where expectations are estimated via Gibbs sampling from the (fast-mixing) projected distribution.

Experiments on 2D grids and random graphs demonstrate that this approach, particularly the reversed KL projection, achieves higher accuracy in marginal estimation compared to standard variational methods like Mean Field, Loopy BP, and TRW. Furthermore, a key result shows that for a limited computational budget, running Gibbs sampling on the projected fast-mixing parameters yields more accurate marginals than running it on the original slow-mixing parameters, directly validating the paper's core motivation.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of defining a tractable family of distributions based on fast-mixing properties, rather than structural simplicity, is a significant and novel contribution. It provides a new perspective on approximate inference that directly addresses the practical problem of MCMC convergence time, which is a major hurdle for many applications. This could open up a new line of research in approximate inference and learning.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the dual algorithm for the sparse Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a valuable contribution in its own right. The framework for minimizing the reversed KL-divergence using stochastic projected gradient descent is principled and well-motivated. The connections drawn to existing methods, such as framing naive mean-field as an extreme case of this projection, are insightful.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the relevant background on mixing times is presented concisely, and the proposed methods are developed in a logical sequence. The experimental setup and results are also presented clearly and effectively support the paper's claims.

4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The proposed methods are compared against strong and relevant baselines. The finding that Gibbs sampling on the projected parameters is more accurate than on the original parameters under a fixed time budget is a powerful demonstration of the practical utility of the proposed approach, especially for models with strong couplings.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Looseness of the Fast-Mixing Bound:** The authors correctly note in the discussion that the spectral norm bound for guaranteeing fast mixing is often conservative. A key concern is that the experiments use a spectral norm bound of 2.5, which violates the theoretical condition (||R||_2 < 1) that provides the "guarantee" of fast mixing. While this pragmatic choice yields better empirical results, it undermines the theoretical foundation of the method. The paper would be strengthened by addressing this more directly in the main body. For instance, the authors could provide more intuition or empirical analysis on how the choice of this threshold `c` affects the trade-off between approximation accuracy and mixing speed. Is there a principled way to choose `c` in practice?

2.  **Computational Cost of Projection:** The projection itself, especially for the reversed KL-divergence, requires a significant number of Gibbs sampling steps (mentioned as 30,000 total iterations in the experimental setup). The current evaluation compares the accuracy of sampling from the final projected parameters versus other methods. However, for a practitioner, the total computational cost, including the projection phase, is critical. The paper would be more comprehensive if it included a comparison based on total runtime (projection + sampling) versus accuracy. While the authors note this makes results harder to interpret, even a small-scale experiment or a more detailed discussion on this trade-off would be very valuable.

3.  **Performance of Piecewise KL:** The "piecewise KL-divergence" is presented as one of the paper's novel contributions. However, its performance is only briefly mentioned as not being as good as the "true" KL-divergence. The experimental results section does not appear to show quantitative results for this method. To properly evaluate this contribution, it would be helpful to include its performance in the main result figures or tables. This would allow the reader to assess its practical utility as a tractable alternative to other divergences.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel and promising direction for approximate inference. The core concept is original, the technical contributions are solid, and the experimental results are compelling. The main weakness lies in the gap between the theory (which requires a strict bound for a guarantee) and the practice (which uses a looser bound for better performance). Despite this, the demonstrated empirical success and the novelty of the approach make this a strong contribution to the field. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and is likely to inspire future work.
