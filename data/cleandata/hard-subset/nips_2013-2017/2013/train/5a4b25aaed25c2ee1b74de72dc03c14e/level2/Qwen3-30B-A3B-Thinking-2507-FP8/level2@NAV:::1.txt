

This paper introduces an interesting approach to improving inference in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set, rather than relying on traditional low-treewidth approximations. The main claim is that projecting parameters under various divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, and reversed KL) yields distributions that can be sampled efficiently via Gibbs sampling, providing better accuracy than original parameters when interaction strengths are strong and sampling time is limited.

The paper makes several strong theoretical contributions, including Theorem 7 which provides an efficient algorithm for Euclidean projection onto the fast-mixing set via singular value thresholding. The experimental section demonstrates that the proposed method outperforms standard variational approaches (LBP, TRW, MF) and provides a more accurate approximation than Gibbs sampling on original parameters when computational resources are constrained. The authors correctly acknowledge the conservative nature of their spectral norm bound (Section 7), noting it's approximately 1.76 times looser than the known threshold for 2D Ising models.

The paper is well-written, with clear technical contributions that extend the literature on approximate inference. The theoretical development is sound, and the experimental validation is thorough across multiple graph topologies and interaction strengths. The connection to mean-field inference as a degenerate case of their approach (Section 5.4) is particularly insightful.

However, the paper could be strengthened by addressing the conservative nature of their spectral norm bound more directly. While they note the looseness in Section 7, they don't provide a compelling explanation for why this occurs or how it might be improved. The piecewise KL divergence approach (Section 5.3) is described but not thoroughly evaluated against the more theoretically grounded reversed KL approach. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational trade-offs between projection time and sampling time, which would help practitioners understand when this approach is most beneficial.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in approximate inference for high-treewidth models. The theoretical foundation is solid, the experimental results are compelling, and the approach has clear practical utility for applications where rapid inference is needed with strong interactions. The minor limitations noted above don't detract significantly from the paper's overall quality and contribution.
