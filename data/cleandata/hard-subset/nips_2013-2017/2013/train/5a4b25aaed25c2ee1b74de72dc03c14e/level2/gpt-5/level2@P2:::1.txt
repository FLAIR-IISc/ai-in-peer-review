Summary and contributions
The paper proposes to approximate general Ising models by projecting their parameters onto a set guaranteed to be “fast mixing” for single-site Gibbs sampling. The fast-mixing set is characterized via a sufficient condition on the dependency matrix R: ||R||2 < 1, together with a bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij| for Ising models, implying a spectral norm constraint on the matrix of absolute pairwise interactions. The authors consider several projection objectives:
- Euclidean projection: a dual formulation (Theorem 7) yields a practical algorithm that uses iterative SVD thresholding while preserving sparsity via a masking constraint.
- KL(q||p) (“zero-avoiding”): theoretically attractive but impractical unless exact inference in q is feasible.
- A novel piecewise KL based on tractable subgraphs (trees or low treewidth subgraphs), using a max over subgraphs.
- KL(p||q) (“zero-forcing”): amenable to stochastic approximation since expectations are w.r.t. the fast-mixing model, with a gradient expression (Theorem 8) showing the covariance structure Covψ[f]·(ψ−θ).

Empirically, on 8×8 grids and small random graphs, the projected models used with Gibbs sampling yield more accurate marginals than LBP, TRW, and MF in regimes of strong interactions and limited sampling time, and outperform direct Gibbs on the original parameters when time is limited. The paper positions the approach as complementary to standard variational approximations, with a different notion of tractability (fast mixing vs low treewidth). It relates to Dobrushin-type conditions and mixing time theory (e.g., Hayes, Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum).

Quality
- Theory: The derivations of the sufficient mixing condition and the link to spectral norm are standard; specializing to Ising via the tanh bound is appropriate. Theorem 8 is consistent with the known identity ∇ψ KL(ψ||θ) = Covψ[f]·(ψ−θ). The dual Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is interesting and practically useful in preserving sparsity; however, more detail on convexity/duality gap and uniqueness would help. The reliance on the spectral norm of |β| is conservative but clear.
- Algorithms: The stochastic optimization for KL(ψ||θ) is plausible, but the gradient estimator (Eq. 8) uses the same sample pool to estimate both μ and the product term and uses dependent MCMC samples. The text partly acknowledges bias/variance issues and mentions ergodic mirror descent, but the prescribed schedule (single Gibbs sweep per iteration, fixed step size, fixed pool) does not satisfy known conditions for convergence guarantees.
- Experiments: The setup is reasonable, but important details are missing or inconsistent. At one point the text states that true marginals are computed via junction tree to assess accuracy; elsewhere, it says marginals are estimated from sampling on each model. This needs clarification. The comparison does not equalize total computational effort, and the authors explicitly do not account for the cost of projection (which can be substantial for the stochastic KL method), making time-accuracy claims hard to interpret.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The mask-and-dual approach for Euclidean projection is clearly motivated. However:
- The experimental description conflicts regarding whether true marginals are computed exactly or estimated by sampling.
- The piecewise KL objective uses a max over subgraphs; subgradient handling and the rationale for max vs average deserve more explanation.
- A footnote marker (“1”) appears without the corresponding note.

Originality
The main novelty is the shift from low-treewidth approximations to a fast-mixing constrained family, coupled with concrete projection algorithms, including a sparsity-preserving Euclidean projection and a practical stochastic scheme for KL(ψ||θ). The piecewise KL approximation is a new variant inspired by piecewise likelihood and tree mixtures. The conceptual link to mean-field as the degenerate zero-spectral-norm case is insightful.

Significance
The idea of constraining to fast-mixing models is appealing for practitioners who rely on MCMC, offering a principled way to trade bias for faster convergence. If made robust, this could influence approximate inference and even learning (as the authors note). That said, the conservativeness of the spectral norm condition and the lack of strong empirical validation of mixing speed/quality limit immediate impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a clear and practical framework for approximating intractable Ising models by fast-mixing projections.
- Solid and useful algorithmic contribution for Euclidean projection that preserves sparsity.
- Sound gradient characterization for KL(ψ||θ) and a practical stochastic optimization scheme.
- Empirical indications that projected fast-mixing models can outperform common variational methods and direct Gibbs under time constraints.
- Connections drawn to classical mixing time results and to mean-field.

Cons for acceptance
- The “guarantee” is undermined in experiments by using a spectral bound of 2.5 (>1), leaving mixing-time guarantees unsubstantiated in practice.
- No direct empirical assessment of mixing (e.g., autocorrelation, effective sample size, coupling diagnostics) to validate the central claim of fast mixing.
- Experimental reporting is unclear/inconsistent about use of exact versus sampled marginals; fairness in compute is not controlled.
- The stochastic gradient estimator is biased as implemented; convergence guarantees are not met by the chosen schedule.
- The piecewise KL approximation is heuristic; motivation and comparative benefit are not convincingly demonstrated.

Constructive suggestions
- Use the theoretically justified bound (||R||2 < 1) in at least one set of experiments to verify the promised fast mixing; if looser bounds are used, include empirical diagnostics (ESS per second, integrated autocorrelation time, Geweke/Raftery–Lewis diagnostics) and acceptance of loss of guarantee.
- Clarify the evaluation protocol: specify whether ground-truth marginals come from exact junction-tree and ensure all reported errors compare estimates to those ground truths. If sampling-based estimates are used anywhere, report variance over multiple chains/seeds.
- Equalize or normalize by total computational budget when comparing against direct Gibbs and variational baselines; alternatively, provide time-accuracy curves that include projection time.
- For the stochastic KL(ψ||θ) algorithm, reduce bias by using independent samples to estimate μ and the product term, or employ control variates; consider increasing the number of MCMC updates per iteration or using a diminishing step-size schedule to align with ergodic mirror descent conditions.
- Provide more detail on the optimization landscape of the Euclidean dual (concavity of g, duality gap, convergence criteria) and computational cost scaling.
- Justify the choice of max in the piecewise KL; compare to averaging over subgraphs or convex combinations (e.g., TRW-style weights), and report ablations.
- Report sensitivity to the spectral norm bound c, including post-projection ||R||2 values and resulting accuracy vs mixing speed.

Minor
- Fix the missing footnote marker and notational slips.
- State explicitly the update scheme (random vs systematic scan) used in experiments and reconcile it with the theorems (cite the appropriate bound for systematic scan).

Overall
An interesting and potentially impactful direction with solid algorithmic ideas, but the experimental validation and practical realization of the “fast mixing” guarantee need tightening. With clarified and strengthened experiments and a more careful stochastic optimization procedure, this work would make a strong contribution.
