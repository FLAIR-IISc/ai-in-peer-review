- Main idea: Project intractable high-treewidth distributions onto a "fast-mixing" set to guarantee rapid Gibbs sampling convergence.
- Tractability notion: Defines tractability via parameter-space conditions instead of exact inference algorithms.
- Algorithmic contribution: Derives a dual algorithm to project distributions onto the fast-mixing set under multiple divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, zero-forcing KL).
- Theoretical result: Provides a bound on the dependency matrix for Ising models to support the fast-mixing criterion.
- Experimental setup: Compares projected-methods to Gibbs on original parameters and several standard variational methods.
- Strength — novelty: Introduces a new, distinct approach to approximate inference different from traditional variational methods.
- Strength — theory: Supplies solid theoretical foundations, including the dependency bound and projection duality.
- Strength — experiments: Offers thorough experimental evaluation and comparisons.
- Strength — insight: Gives new insights into Gibbs mixing times for original and projected distributions.
- Weakness — spectral bound: The spectral norm bound used is acknowledged as too conservative, limiting applicability.
- Weakness — compute cost: Method requires repeated Gibbs sampling and projections, potentially computationally expensive.
- Weakness — scope: Paper focuses on Ising models; extension to general MRFs is unclear.
- Originality — tractability notion: Proposes a novel parameter-space-based notion of tractability.
- Originality — projection algorithm: Presents a novel dual algorithm for projection onto the fast-mixing set.
- Originality — evaluation: Provides new empirical insights on approximate inference performance.
- Significance — advancement: Advances the state of the art in approximate inference for high-treewidth models.
- Significance — insights: Offers valuable insights into Gibbs mixing behavior and inference performance.
- Significance — impact potential: Approach could affect applications in vision, NLP, and computational biology.
- Quality — soundness: Paper is technically sound with solid theory and experiments.
- Quality — clarity: Well-written and easy to follow with clear assumptions and descriptions.
- Quality — completeness: Delivers a complete treatment of the method, algorithm, and experiments.
- Recommendation: Recommend acceptance with minor revisions to tighten the spectral bound analysis, clarify computational complexity, and consider extensions to general MRFs.
