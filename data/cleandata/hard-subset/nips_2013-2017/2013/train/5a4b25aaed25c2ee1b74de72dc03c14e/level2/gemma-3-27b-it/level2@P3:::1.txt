The paper proposes a method for improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model parameters onto a set guaranteed to have fast mixing properties. The core idea is compelling: rather than directly attempting to infer in intractable models, transform the parameters to a tractable (fast-mixing) space while minimizing divergence from the original parameters. The work explores several divergence measures (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL) for this projection, with a focus on practical algorithms for performing the projection. The experimental results demonstrate that projecting parameters can lead to more accurate results than directly sampling from the original parameters when limited sampling time is available, and compare favorably to existing inference techniques.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses a practical challenge in applying MCMC methods to Ising models. The connection to existing work on tractable approximations (mean-field, tree-reweighted belief propagation) is clearly articulated. The theoretical contributions, namely Theorems 6 and 7 regarding the Euclidean projection, are solid and presented rigorously. The exploration of different divergence measures, and the associated computational tradeoffs, is valuable. The experiments are well-designed, with comparisons to relevant baselines, and the results convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach. 

However, several areas could be strengthened. While the paper provides a clear overview of the theoretical background on mixing times, the connection between the spectral norm of the dependency matrix and practical mixing behavior could be further elaborated. The discussion acknowledges a looseness in the spectral norm bound compared to known critical values for specific Ising models (e.g., 2D Ising), but doesn’t fully explore why this discrepancy exists or potential refinements to the bound. The piecewise KL divergence, while theoretically interesting, performs less well in experiments. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of *why* it doesn’t perform as expected, and whether modifications to the subgraph selection strategy could improve its performance. The experimental section, while comprehensive, lacks error bars or statistical significance testing.  Reporting these would strengthen the conclusions drawn from the results. The explanation of the stochastic gradient descent algorithm used to minimize the zero-forcing KL-divergence is concise; more details regarding parameter tuning and convergence criteria would be helpful for reproducibility. 

Arguments for acceptance:
* Addresses a relevant and practically important problem in graphical model inference.
* Presents a novel approach based on parameter projection, offering a new perspective on tractability.
* Provides solid theoretical foundations for the Euclidean projection.
* Demonstrates empirical improvements over existing inference methods in relevant scenarios.

Arguments against acceptance:
* The spectral norm bound for fast mixing appears conservative, and a deeper investigation is warranted.
* The performance of the piecewise KL divergence is underwhelming, and its limitations are not fully explored.
* Lack of error bars or statistical significance testing in the experimental results.
* Limited details provided about parameter tuning of the stochastic gradient descent algorithm.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. The weaknesses are relatively minor and could be addressed with revisions. The paper offers a valuable addition to the literature on approximate inference in graphical models, and its acceptance is recommended contingent on addressing the concerns outlined above. Specifically, providing statistical significance testing in the experimental section and a more thorough discussion of the spectral norm bound would significantly strengthen the paper.
