- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set to make Gibbs sampling converge reliably when original parameters mix slowly.  
- Frames tractability by rapid mixing (dependency/mixing-time constraints) instead of low treewidth, offering a novel inference perspective.  
- Provides theoretical foundation using Markov chain mixing-time results and gives theorems for Euclidean-norm projection.  
- Derives Euclidean projection solved via SVD thresholding.  
- Investigates multiple divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, zero-forcing KL) and trade-offs between accuracy and computation.  
- Develops a stochastic approximation method for the zero-forcing KL divergence.  
- Empirically compares projected models against LBP, TRW, and mean-field, showing benefits especially for strong interactions and varied graph topologies.  
- Situates the work clearly within existing approximate inference and mixing-time literature.  
- Notes the spectral-norm bound of 1.0 is overly conservative in practice, leading to suboptimal projections and a gap between theory and empirical best values.  
- Highlights the potentially high computational cost of the projection step, particularly for zero-forcing KL, and calls for a detailed cost breakdown.  
- Observes poor experimental performance of the piecewise KL divergence without fully explaining the reasons.  
- Limits scope to the Ising model, leaving generalization to broader MRFs under-specified.  
- Provides sparse details on hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity for the stochastic-gradient method used.  
- Argues for acceptance based on novelty, theoretical grounding, empirical improvements from projection, and the stochastic KL contribution.  
- Argues against acceptance due to the conservative spectral bound, possible prohibitive projection cost, and limited generalizability beyond Ising models.  
- Overall assessment: strong, well-written paper with solid theory and experiments; recommends acceptance while asking authors to address the spectral-norm issue and provide more detail on computational cost and hyperparameter tuning.
