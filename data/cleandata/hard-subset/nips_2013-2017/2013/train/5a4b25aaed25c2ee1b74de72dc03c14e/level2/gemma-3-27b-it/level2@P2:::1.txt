This paper explores projecting Ising model parameters onto a set guaranteed to be fast-mixing, aiming to improve Gibbs sampling efficiency when original parameters lead to slow convergence. The core idea – finding a “tractable” model space defined by rapid mixing rather than low treewidth – is a valuable contribution. The authors investigate several divergences for this projection, including Euclidean distance (solved via SVD thresholding), KL divergence (approximated piecewise and via stochastic approximation), and explore theoretical connections to existing work on mixing times.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:** The concept of focusing on fast-mixing parameter spaces as a form of tractability is a fresh perspective compared to traditional approaches like tree-based approximations or mean-field methods. This framing offers a potentially powerful alternative for tackling inference in intractable models.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a sound theoretical basis for the approach, drawing on results from Markov chain theory (specifically mixing times and dependency matrices) and presenting theorems for projecting parameters in the Euclidean norm.
* **Multiple Divergence Exploration:** Investigating various divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, zero-forcing KL) and their trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost demonstrates a thorough exploration of the idea. The stochastic approximation approach for the zero-forcing KL divergence is particularly interesting.
* **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation, comparing the proposed methods to established techniques like LBP, TRW, and MF, provides empirical evidence of the benefits of projection, especially in scenarios with strong interactions. The experiments are well-designed and test on various graph topologies and interaction strengths.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively situates its work within the existing literature on approximate inference, citing relevant papers on mean-field methods, loopy belief propagation, and mixing times.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conservatism of Spectral Norm Bound:** The discussion in Section 7 points out a significant weakness: the spectral norm bound of 1.0 seems overly conservative in practice, leading to potentially suboptimal projections. While acknowledged, this impacts the practical effectiveness of the approach. A tighter bound would significantly improve the method’s performance. The fact that a bound of 2.5 is empirically better suggests a substantial gap in the theory.
* **Computational Cost of Projections:** The paper acknowledges the computational burden of the projection step, particularly for the zero-forcing KL divergence. The experiments compare *time-accuracy*, but a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost of the projection *itself* relative to the benefits gained would be helpful. Is the projection step, in some cases, prohibitively expensive?
* **Piecewise KL Divergence Performance:** The piecewise KL divergence doesn’t perform well in the experiments, and the rationale for its poor performance is not fully explored. While it's stated to be easy to evaluate, its limited accuracy makes it less valuable.
* **Limited Scope (Ising Model):** While focusing on the Ising model allows for a detailed analysis, it limits the immediate generalizability of the approach. The paper briefly mentions extending the work to general MRFs but doesn’t provide specific details on how this would be achieved.
* **Lack of Detailed Hyperparameter Tuning Discussion:** The authors mention hyperparameter tuning for the stochastic gradient descent algorithm used for the zero-forcing KL-divergence, but the details are sparse. A more comprehensive discussion of how parameters were chosen and their sensitivity analysis would strengthen the results.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to approximate inference.
* The experimental results demonstrate that projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set can improve Gibbs sampling accuracy, particularly when the original parameters lead to slow convergence.
* The exploration of different divergences and the development of a stochastic approximation method for the KL divergence are significant contributions.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the concepts and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The conservative spectral norm bound limits the practical effectiveness of the method.
* The computational cost of the projection step could be prohibitive in some cases.
* The paper's scope is limited to the Ising model, and the generalizability to other MRFs is not fully addressed.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to approximate inference. The theoretical foundation is solid, the experimental evaluation is thorough, and the writing is clear. While the conservative spectral norm bound and the computational cost of the projection are legitimate concerns, the overall contributions outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the issue of the spectral norm bound in future work.  A minor revision addressing the computational cost breakdown and providing more details on hyperparameter tuning would further strengthen the paper.
