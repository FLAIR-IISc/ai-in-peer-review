- Contribution: project Ising-model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set (spectral-norm-constrained) and minimize divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, reversed KL) so Gibbs sampling converges rapidly.  
- Theoretical strength: solid connections to mixing-time bounds and dependency matrices; Euclidean projection algorithm (Theorem 7) is rigorous.  
- Experimental strength: thorough comparisons (LBP, TRW, MF) on grids and random graphs showing projected parameters give better marginal accuracy under time constraints.  
- Practical insight: projection yields improved accuracy within limited sampling time, supported by empirical results.  
- Major weakness 1: theoretical bound looseness—spectral norm <1 guarantee is far stricter than known 2D Ising threshold (β_c≈0.44); authors use norm≤2.5 without justification, undermining guarantees.  
- Major weakness 2: experiments are small-scale (8×8 grids, 10-node graphs), gains are modest and lack statistical significance testing, so scalability and significance are unclear.  
- Major weakness 3: KL-divergence justification is inconsistent—reversed KL empirically outperforms zero-avoiding KL despite theoretical expectations, with no explanation.  
- Minor issues: unclear rationale for piecewise KL using subgraphs, confusing "zero-forcing" terminology, and inconsistency between practical norm choice (2.5) and theoretical norm <1.  
- Recommendation: technically competent but not acceptable for NIPS in current form due to the unresolved theoretical gap and insufficient scaling evidence.  
- Suggested revisions: compare spectral bound to known Ising thresholds, use tighter bounds if possible, run larger experiments with significance tests, reconcile KL discrepancy, and justify the pragmatic norm choice.
