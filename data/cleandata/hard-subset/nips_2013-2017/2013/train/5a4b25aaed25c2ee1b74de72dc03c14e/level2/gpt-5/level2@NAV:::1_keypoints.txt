- Paper proposes projecting intractable Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing family using dependency-matrix bounds (||R||2<1) and studies several projection/divergence methods.  
- Derives a sparsity-preserving Euclidean projection via a dual formulation and SVD thresholding (Theorem 7).  
- Explores projections under multiple divergences: Euclidean, inclusive KL via tractable subgraphs, and reversed KL optimized by stochastic approximation.  
- Empirically shows Gibbs sampling on projected models can yield better finite-time marginal accuracy than on original models when interactions are strong.  
- Clear, principled framing of tractability via fast mixing (distinct from treewidth-based approaches).  
- Provides a practical Euclidean projection algorithm that respects sparsity and graph structure.  
- Introduces a stochastic optimization scheme for KL(ψ||θ) that generalizes mean-field beyond fully factorized families.  
- Empirical comparisons to LBP/TRW/MF indicate constructive improvements in finite-time inference.  
- Experiments violate the provable bound (use spectral-norm 2.5 vs 1.0) and do not report ||R||2 or observed mixing times, weakening theoretical guarantees.  
- Limited experimental scale (8×8 grids, n=10 random graphs), no real-world datasets, and no error bars or statistical significance reporting.  
- Evaluation omits projection cost and time-to-accuracy tradeoffs, and lacks comparisons to block Gibbs or Swendsen–Wang.  
- Piecewise KL construction appears under-motivated and empirically underperforms; subgraph selection is heuristic.  
- Some clarity issues: inconsistent assumptions about random vs systematic scan and ambiguity in how ground-truth marginals are obtained.  
- Theoretical core is sound, but experimental methodology and justification need strengthening.  
- Generally well written; recommend adding precise evaluation protocol and reporting of norms/mixing diagnostics.  
- Originality is moderate: projecting to a fast-mixing family and the dual projection are novel, while reversed KL links to known MF perspectives.  
- Significance: promising direction for improving finite-time inference and learning with MCMC-based gradients.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel principled approach, solid integration of mixing-time theory and optimization, and positive empirical trends.  
- Cons for acceptance: weak empirical breadth/rigor, unmet guarantees in practice, and missing runtime/mixing diagnostics and stronger baselines.  
- Suggested improvements: report ||R||2 before/after and empirical mixing times; include projection overhead in time-accuracy curves; evaluate larger grids and block-swapping samplers; ablate divergences and hyperparameters; enforce or systematically relax provable bounds; release code and subgraph selection details.
