1. Novel approach: project parameters onto a fast-mixing set to ensure rapid Gibbs sampling in high-treewidth Ising models.  
2. Multiple projection methods proposed: Euclidean, zero-avoiding KL, piecewise KL, and zero-forcing KL.  
3. Main empirical finding: Gibbs sampling with projected parameters yields higher marginal accuracy under strong interactions and limited sampling time.  
4. Evaluation: experiments on grid and random graphs comparing against variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) and Gibbs on original parameters show promising results.  
5. Strength — Novelty: introduces a new tractability notion based on parameter-space fast-mixing rather than structural approximations.  
6. Strength — Theory: solid theoretical foundation using spectral norm constraints, dual optimization, and SGD for KL minimization.  
7. Strength — Experiments: extensive empirical validation across topologies and baselines.  
8. Strength — Practical relevance: useful when exact inference is infeasible and original Gibbs mixes too slowly.  
9. Weakness — Spectral norm bound is overly conservative and looser than known Ising thresholds.  
10. Weakness — Computational overhead of projection (singular value thresholding, SGD) is significant and under-accounted in comparisons.  
11. Weakness — Limited to Ising models; extension to general MRFs and dependency matrix projections remains open.  
12. Weakness — Evaluation focuses on marginal accuracy and omits runtime-vs-accuracy and robustness metrics.  
13. Suggestion: investigate tighter spectral norm bounds to improve theoretical guarantees and practical utility.  
14. Suggestion: provide detailed analysis of computational trade-offs between projection cost and sampling efficiency.  
15. Suggestion: extend methods to general MRFs and explore applicability to learning tasks.  
16. Suggestion: include additional evaluation metrics such as runtime comparisons and robustness analysis.  
17. Recommendation: accept, provided authors address spectral bound looseness and discuss computational trade-offs.  
18. For acceptance: novel tractability notion, strong theory, experimental validation, practical relevance under time constraints.  
19. Against acceptance: conservative bounds, high projection cost, restricted to Ising models.  
20. Overall: valuable, technically sound contribution that aligns with conference scope.
