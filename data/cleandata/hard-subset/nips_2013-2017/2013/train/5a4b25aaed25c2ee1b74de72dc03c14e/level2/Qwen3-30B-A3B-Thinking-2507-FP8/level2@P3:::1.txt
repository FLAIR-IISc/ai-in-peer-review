

The paper introduces a novel framework for approximate inference in high-treewidth Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set, where Gibbs sampling converges rapidly. This contrasts with traditional tractability notions based on graph structure (e.g., mean-field, LBP) and instead focuses on parameter-space constraints guaranteeing rapid mixing via spectral norm bounds on the dependency matrix. The core contribution is a projection algorithm minimizing four divergences: Euclidean distance (using SVD thresholding), piecewise KL, and two variants of KL divergence (zero-avoiding and zero-forcing). The work extends prior mixing-time theory (e.g., Hayes [8], Dyer et al. [4]) to a practical inference strategy.

**Strengths:**  
The theoretical foundation is sound, with Theorem 7 providing an efficient dual algorithm for Euclidean projection. The experiments comprehensively evaluate the approach against standard variational methods (LBP, TRW, mean-field) and Gibbs sampling on original parameters. Key findings—projected parameters yield more accurate marginals than variational methods under finite sampling time, and outperform original-Gibbs when time is constrained—address a critical practical challenge in MCMC. The piecewise KL divergence and zero-forcing KL approaches are well-motivated, with the latter offering a scalable stochastic optimization strategy. The analysis of spectral norm conservatism (Section 7) demonstrates self-awareness of limitations.

**Weaknesses:**  
The spectral norm bound (||R||₂ < 1) is overly conservative for Ising models, as the known critical threshold βc ≈ 0.44 (for 2D grids) is significantly higher than the bound’s β = 0.25. While the paper justifies using ||R||₂ ≤ 2.5 empirically, this lacks theoretical justification and weakens the "guaranteed fast-mixing" claim. The piecewise KL divergence underperforms in experiments without clear explanation—e.g., why spanning trees fail to capture structure better than the zero-forcing KL approach. The comparison to modern MCMC methods (e.g., Hamiltonian Monte Carlo, Swendsen-Wang) is absent, leaving open whether the projection approach is competitive with specialized samplers for strong interactions.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**  
1. **Clarify spectral bound rationale:** Explicitly discuss why ||R||₂ ≤ 2.5 preserves fast mixing (e.g., via empirical mixing-time curves) and whether tighter bounds exist for Ising models. Cite recent work (e.g., Lubetzky & Sly [11] on 2D Ising mixing) to contextualize the looseness.  
2. **Re-evaluate piecewise KL:** Test alternative subgraph sets (e.g., low-treewidth graphs from junction trees) instead of random spanning trees, and analyze *why* piecewise KL underperforms relative to zero-forcing KL.  
3. **Expand experimental comparisons:** Include Hamiltonian Monte Carlo or Swendsen-Wang as baselines to assess if the projection approach offers advantages over state-of-the-art MCMC for strong interactions.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper presents a theoretically grounded, practical solution to a well-motivated problem in approximate inference. The core idea—redefining tractability via parameter constraints—is original and significant, with clear experimental validation of its utility in time-constrained settings. While the spectral bound conservatism and piecewise KL limitations require addressing, these are fixable through minor revisions. The work advances the state of the art by bridging mixing-time theory with scalable inference, offering a valuable alternative to structure-based approximations. It aligns with NIPS’s focus on rigorous, impactful methods for probabilistic modeling.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The contributions are novel, well-supported, and address a meaningful gap in inference for high-treewidth models. The suggested revisions are reasonable and would strengthen the paper without requiring major rework.
