- Main idea: Project Ising model parameters onto a “fast-mixing” family (spectral-norm constraint ||R||2<1) to enable rapid Gibbs sampling for approximate inference.  
- Methods: Study projections under Euclidean distance, inclusive KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL (stochastic optimization using samples from constrained ψ).  
- Key theory: Uses standard spectral-norm mixing condition (Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Hayes) and bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| for Ising; dual projection (Theorem 7) enforces spectral constraint while preserving sparsity.  
- Reverse-KL gradient: Derivation matches Fisher-information form with a Monte Carlo–estimable expression (Theorem 8).  
- Algorithms: Projected-gradient scheme with Euclidean projections and a sample-pool stochastic approximation (one Gibbs sweep per iteration) is reasonable and pragmatic.  
- Experiments: Tested on 8×8 grids and small random graphs, comparing to LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs; marginals evaluated via exact junction tree.  
- Empirical caveat: Wall-clock comparisons omit projection cost; projections often use relaxed thresholds >1, voiding theoretical mixing guarantees.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally well organized; some derivations in appendix; suggest brief discussion of convexity/tightness and correct total-variation definition.  
- Originality: Novel framing of tractable family via fast-mixing constraints and technical dual projection that preserves sparsity.  
- Significance: Offers a new practical angle for time-limited MCMC users, though conservativeness of spectral condition and projection overhead limit immediate impact.  
- Strength: Principled use of mixing-time theory to define approximation family linked to sampling guarantees.  
- Strength: Technically interesting spectral-norm projection that retains sparsity via mask.  
- Strength: Reverse-KL approach generalizes mean-field and provides estimable gradients using fast-mixing ψ.  
- Strength: Experiments show improved marginals under limited sampling budgets.  
- Weakness: Main experiments relax spectral threshold (e.g., to 2.5), undermining guarantees; need empirical mixing-time validation.  
- Weakness: Runtime accounting incomplete — must include projection (SVD/dual optimization) cost and convergence behavior.  
- Weakness: Piecewise KL underperforms; requires analysis of T choice, max vs weighted combination, and optimization stability.  
- Weakness: Need clarity on whether dual projection solves original nonconvex problem exactly or a relaxation, and on optimality/uniqueness.  
- Weakness: Sensitivity to optimization hyperparameters (step-size, pool size, Gibbs updates per iter, sample reuse) not analyzed.  
- Weakness: Scope limited to this spectral condition; consider other sufficient conditions (Dobrushin L1, block/cluster schemes, non-local MCMC) for less conservative sets.  
- Weakness: Notation should explicitly state β symmetry, handling of self-potentials α, and whether α is left unchanged in projection.  
- Questions for authors: How to choose/adapt spectral threshold c in practice to target mixing time?  
- Questions: Do projections densify parameters within the mask or preserve sparsity; report average degree post-projection?  
- Questions: Provide empirical mixing-time or integrated-autocorrelation measurements for tested instances?  
- Questions: How sensitive is reverse-KL to initialization (Euclidean-projected θ vs zero)?  
- Questions: Would a convex combination over subgraphs (rather than max) improve piecewise-KL stability and performance?  
- Pro-accept: Novel, well-motivated formulation with solid theory and promising practical benefits under time-limited sampling.  
- Against-accept: Empirical adherence to guarantees is weak, projection cost/scalability unclear, and some methods (piecewise KL) lack clear benefit.
