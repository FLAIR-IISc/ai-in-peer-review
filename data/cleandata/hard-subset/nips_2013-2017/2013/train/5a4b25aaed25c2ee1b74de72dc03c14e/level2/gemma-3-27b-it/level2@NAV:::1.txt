## Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

This paper addresses the challenge of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for Ising models, particularly when interaction strengths are high. The authors propose projecting Ising model parameters onto a set guaranteed to be fast mixing, thereby accelerating convergence. The core idea – trading off parameter accuracy for faster sampling – is a compelling one, and builds on the well-established line of work aiming to find tractable approximations to intractable distributions (e.g., mean field, tree-reweighted BP). The paper clearly positions its contribution within this landscape, referencing key works like [6, 14, 16, 21].

The paper’s strength lies in the exploration of different divergence measures for the projection: Euclidean distance (with a novel iterative SVD-based algorithm – Theorem 7), KL divergence (both theoretical and piecewise approximations), and a reversed KL divergence approximated via stochastic gradient descent.  The Euclidean projection offers a computationally efficient solution, while the exploration of KL divergences acknowledges the desire for probabilistic guarantees. The connection drawn between the zero-forcing KL divergence and mean-field approximation is also insightful.  Experimental evaluation on both grid and random graphs demonstrates that projecting parameters indeed leads to faster convergence than sampling from original parameters, and competitive results compared to LBP, TRW, and MF. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While Theorem 7 provides a mathematically elegant solution for Euclidean projection, the practical benefit of destroying sparsity is not thoroughly discussed. The piecewise KL approximation doesn't perform as well as other methods, raising questions about its utility. More importantly, the choice of a spectral norm threshold of 2.5 (instead of the theoretically derived 1.0) feels *ad hoc* and requires stronger justification – the authors acknowledge a looseness in the bound but don’t fully investigate its impact. The experiments, while comprehensive, lack a detailed analysis of the computational cost of projection versus the speedup gained in sampling; a more thorough time-accuracy trade-off analysis would strengthen the conclusions.  Finally, the description of the stochastic gradient descent algorithm is somewhat terse, making replication challenging.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practical problem (slow mixing) with a novel approach.
*   Explores multiple divergence measures for projection.
*   Demonstrates empirically improved convergence speed compared to standard methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The justification for the relaxed spectral norm bound is weak.
*   The trade-off between projection cost and sampling speedup isn't sufficiently analyzed.
*   Algorithm description lacks detail for reproducibility.



**Overall:** This paper presents an interesting and potentially useful contribution. Despite the weaknesses, the core idea of parameter projection is promising.  I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, specifically requesting a more rigorous justification of the spectral norm threshold, a detailed cost-benefit analysis, and a clearer description of the stochastic gradient descent algorithm.
