This paper proposes an alternative notion of a "tractable" model, focusing on "fast mixing" models that guarantee rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling to the stationary distribution. The authors develop an algorithm to project Ising model parameters onto a parameter set that is guaranteed to be fast mixing under several divergences. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is clear and concise.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a projection algorithm that can be used to obtain a fast-mixing approximation of an intractable Ising model. The authors consider four different divergences, including the Euclidean norm, the "zero-avoiding" KL-divergence, a novel "piecewise" KL-divergence, and the "zero-forcing" KL-divergence. The paper also provides experimental results comparing the proposed methods with Gibbs sampling on the original parameters and several standard variational methods.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem of inference in high-treewidth graphical models and the concept of fast mixing.
* The development of the projection algorithm is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the technical challenges involved.
* The experimental results are well-presented and provide a clear comparison of the proposed methods with existing approaches.

The weaknesses of the paper include:

* The paper assumes a relatively high level of background knowledge in graphical models and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods. While the authors provide some background information, the paper may be difficult to follow for readers without a strong background in these areas.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach. In particular, the authors note that the spectral norm bound is "too conservative" in some cases, but do not provide a clear explanation of the implications of this limitation.
* The experimental results are limited to a relatively small set of configurations, and it is unclear how well the proposed methods will generalize to more complex models.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical models and Markov chain Monte Carlo methods. The proposed approach has the potential to improve the accuracy and efficiency of inference in high-treewidth graphical models, and the experimental results provide a clear demonstration of the effectiveness of the approach.
