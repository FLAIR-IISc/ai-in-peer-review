

This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set, ensuring Gibbs sampling converges rapidly. The core idea—using parameter-space conditions (specifically, ||R||₂ < 1) to guarantee fast mixing—shifts the focus from graph structure to interaction strengths, differing from traditional tractable approximations like mean-field or tree-based methods. The authors derive projection algorithms under multiple divergences: Euclidean (via singular value thresholding), piecewise KL, and reversed KL (KL(ψ||θ)), with the latter enabling practical optimization via stochastic approximation. Experiments on grids and random graphs demonstrate that their method outperforms variational techniques (LBP, TRW, MF) in marginal accuracy under time constraints, while Gibbs sampling on original parameters (slow-mixing) eventually converges to higher accuracy but requires exponentially more time.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Originality and Conceptual Contribution:** The paper’s core idea—defining "tractability" via mixing time guarantees rather than graph structure—is fresh and well-motivated. It extends beyond standard variational inference (e.g., mean-field as a degenerate case of reversed KL projection) and addresses a critical bottleneck in MCMC for high-treewidth models. The comparison to related work (e.g., Globerson & Jaakkola 2006, Hazan & Shashua 2008) is thorough.  
2. **Technical Quality:** The theoretical foundation (Theorems 4, 5, 7) is sound, with clear connections to mixing time literature (Dyer et al. 2004, Hayes 2006). The Euclidean projection algorithm is elegantly derived, and the stochastic approximation for reversed KL is practical. Experiments are well-designed, comparing against relevant baselines (LBP, TRW, MF) and rigorously measuring marginal accuracy via junction-tree marginals.  
3. **Significance:** The method directly tackles a real-world problem: the slow convergence of Gibbs sampling in strong-interaction regimes. The time-accuracy tradeoff analysis (e.g., Figure 1) is compelling, showing that projected parameters yield better results *within limited sampling time*—a crucial consideration for practitioners. The potential for extension to general MRFs (Section 7) further elevates its impact.  

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**  
1. **Spectral Norm Bound Looseness:** The authors acknowledge that ||R||₂ < 1 is conservative (e.g., for 2D Ising, β_c ≈ 0.44 vs. β=0.25 for ||R||₂=1). While Section 7 discusses this, a more detailed comparison with tighter bounds (e.g., from [11, 7]) would strengthen the argument. *Suggestion:* Add a sentence in Section 3 contrasting the spectral bound with known thresholds, and clarify why a looser bound (e.g., ||R||₂ ≤ 2.5) still preserves fast mixing empirically.  
2. **Piecewise KL Implementation Details:** The piecewise KL section (Section 5.3) lacks justification for subgraph selection (e.g., why chains of treewidth 2 for grids? Why spanning trees for random graphs?). *Suggestion:* Briefly justify the subgraph choices (e.g., "Spanning trees ensure coverage of all edges while enabling exact inference via junction tree") and add a sentence on computational cost vs. accuracy tradeoffs.  
3. **Clarity of Algorithm Description:** Section 4’s dual optimization (Theorem 7) is mathematically dense. *Suggestion:* Include a high-level pseudocode for the Euclidean projection step (e.g., "Iterate: (1) Compute SVD of R; (2) Threshold singular values; (3) Update D") to aid reproducibility.  
4. **Experimental Scope:** The experiments focus on small graphs (8×8 grids, 10-node random graphs). *Suggestion:* Briefly discuss scalability limits (e.g., "The Euclidean projection scales as O(n³) for n variables, but the stochastic KL method handles larger graphs via Gibbs sampling") to contextualize applicability.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a strong contribution with clear novelty, solid theory, and compelling experiments. The work advances the state of the art in approximate inference by offering a principled alternative to structure-based approximations, with immediate practical utility. The weaknesses noted are minor and addressable in revision; they do not undermine the core idea. The paper aligns well with NIPS’s focus on rigorous, impactful methods for probabilistic modeling.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The significance of the problem addressed, the technical soundness, and the empirical validation make this a valuable addition to the literature. The authors have clearly demonstrated a meaningful improvement over existing approaches under realistic time constraints.
