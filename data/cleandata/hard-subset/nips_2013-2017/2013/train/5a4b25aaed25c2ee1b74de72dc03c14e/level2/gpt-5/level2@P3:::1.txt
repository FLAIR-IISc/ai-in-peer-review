Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes projecting arbitrary Ising model parameters onto a set that guarantees fast mixing of single-site Gibbs sampling. The guarantee leverages a sufficient condition for rapid mixing based on the spectral norm of the dependency matrix R (Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum 2009; Hayes 2006), with a simple bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij| for Ising couplings. The contributions are: (1) a Euclidean projection scheme that enforces a spectral-norm bound on |β| while respecting sparsity via a dual formulation and iterative SVD thresholding (Theorem 7); (2) exploration of several divergences for projection—Euclidean, inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ) (of theoretical interest only), a novel piecewise KL computed on tractable subgraphs, and the reverse KL KL(ψ||θ) optimized by stochastic approximation using a sample pool and Euclidean re-projection; and (3) empirical evaluations on 8×8 grids and small random graphs, comparing to MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs on the original parameters. The work is positioned as an alternative to classical tractable-family approximations (MF, structured MF, LBP/TRW, planar graph decomposition) by instead insisting on parameter regimes ensuring rapid mixing, potentially yielding better anytime accuracy under limited sampling budgets.

Quality:
- Theory: The use of ||R||2 < 1 to guarantee mixing is standard, and the bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is appropriate for translating couplings to dependency. The Euclidean projection problem with a spectral-norm constraint on |β| under sparsity is nontrivial; the dual approach with LBFGS-B and SVD thresholding is interesting and appears sound at a high level. However, the set Ψ = {β: || |β| ||2 ≤ c, β respects sparsity} is nonconvex due to the absolute value, and the paper does not make explicit whether the dual optimization is globally correct or an efficient heuristic; providing convexity/optimality statements or clarifying assumptions would strengthen the contribution. The SGD procedure for KL(ψ||θ) with sample reuse connects to Ergodic Mirror Descent (Duchi et al.), but convergence conditions (step-size schedules, number of MCMC steps per iteration) are not made explicit beyond a fixed step size. The gradient estimator uses a product of two Monte Carlo estimates; unbiasedness requires independence, which is discussed, but variance implications and the effect of reusing the same pool warrant further analysis.
- Experiments: The evaluation protocol is reasonable and the baselines appropriate. The results, as described, suggest improved marginal accuracy over MF/LBP/TRW in strong-coupling regimes and better finite-time accuracy than running Gibbs on the original parameters. However, projection costs are excluded from the time budget, so the anytime advantage is not fully quantified. The experiments also use a spectral norm threshold 2.5, exceeding the theoretical guarantee (<1), which undermines the claimed guarantee; while the paper acknowledges looseness of the bound, actual mixing diagnostics (e.g., autocorrelation times, multiple-chain convergence diagnostics) would be valuable to justify this choice.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful background on mixing times and a logical progression from Euclidean projection to probabilistic divergences. Some derivations (Theorem 7) are dense; clarifying the exact objective (missing constants/1/2 factors), assumptions (symmetry of β, treatment of fields α), and computational complexity per iteration (SVD cost, number of LBFGS iterations) would aid reproducibility. The description of piecewise KL could be expanded to explain the choice of max over subgraphs vs. averaging or reweighting.

Originality:
The central idea—projecting to a fast-mixing set and using that as the tractable family for approximate inference—appears novel relative to mean-field/structured mean-field, LBP/TRW, mixtures-of-trees (Pletscher et al.), and planar decompositions (Globerson–Jaakkola). The dual projection algorithm and the use of KL(ψ||θ) with enforced fast mixing as a generalization of MF are also fresh.

Significance:
The approach offers a new lever for practical inference: when couplings are strong and time is limited, replacing the model by a nearby fast-mixing one and sampling from it can outperform standard variational methods and finite-time Gibbs on the original model. If robust and scalable, this could influence both inference and learning workflows. Current experiments are on modest sizes; demonstrating scalability and clearer guarantees would increase impact.

Pros:
- Conceptually novel framing of “tractable” as “fast-mixing,” distinct from low-treewidth approximations.
- Concrete algorithmic contributions, including a structured Euclidean projection via dual SVD thresholding.
- Practical stochastic optimization procedure for KL(ψ||θ), connecting to mirror descent with MCMC.
- Empirical gains over strong baselines in challenging regimes; good discussion of limitations.

Cons:
- Theoretical guarantee is not enforced in experiments (spectral norm bound set to 2.5); no mixing diagnostics are reported.
- Nonconvexity from |β| makes projection optimality unclear; convergence and complexity guarantees are limited.
- Time-accuracy comparisons exclude projection cost; anytime claims would benefit from matched wall-clock budgets.
- Piecewise KL design (max over subgraphs) seems pessimistic and empirically underperforms; alternatives are not explored.
- Scalability beyond small graphs is not assessed.

Suggestions for improvement (prioritized):
Essential:
- Report post-projection norms (|| |β| ||2 and Dobrushin ||·||1), and include mixing diagnostics (e.g., integrated autocorrelation times, multiple-chain R̂) to justify using thresholds >1.
- Provide wall-clock comparisons that include projection/training time under matched compute budgets to substantiate anytime advantages.
- Clarify the projection problem’s convexity/optimality status; state conditions under which Theorem 7 yields a global solution and discuss computational complexity.

Important but optional:
- Include sensitivity analyses for the SGD hyperparameters (step size, pool size, number of Gibbs updates) and show robustness.
- Consider average or reweighted piecewise KL (e.g., TRW-style weights) instead of max over subgraphs; evaluate on the same setups.
- Clarify symmetry enforcement (βij=βji) and how univariate fields α are handled during projection with respect to mixing conditions.
- Compare systematic vs random scan empirically to align with the theoretical bound’s assumptions.

Recommendation:
The paper presents a novel and promising direction with solid initial algorithms and encouraging results, but the gap between guarantees and practice, and the lack of compute-matched comparisons, limit confidence. With the essential clarifications and diagnostics, the contribution would be strong. As it stands, this leans toward weak accept.
