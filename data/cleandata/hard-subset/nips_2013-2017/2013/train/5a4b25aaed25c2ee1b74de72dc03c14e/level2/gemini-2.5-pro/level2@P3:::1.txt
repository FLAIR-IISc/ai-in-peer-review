**Summary**
This paper addresses the problem of approximate inference in Ising models, particularly in regimes where high treewidth or strong interactions make standard methods like Gibbs sampling intractable due to slow mixing. The central and novel idea is to redefine the notion of a "tractable" model. Instead of approximating the model's graphical structure (as in mean-field or tree-reweighted belief propagation), this work proposes to approximate the model's parameters by projecting them onto a set that is guaranteed to be "fast mixing".

The work leverages a known condition from the MCMC literature which guarantees rapid mixing for Gibbs sampling if the spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix is less than one. Based on this condition, the paper develops algorithms to find the closest fast-mixing parameters to a given (potentially slow-mixing) parameter set. Four different projection methods are explored, corresponding to four different divergences:
1.  Euclidean distance between parameter vectors, for which a dual optimization algorithm is derived.
2.  The forward KL-divergence (KL(q||p)), noted as being primarily of theoretical interest due to the difficulty of computing expectations with respect to the original slow-mixing distribution q.
3.  A novel "piecewise KL-divergence" that approximates the full KL by computing it on tractable subgraphs.
4.  The reversed KL-divergence (KL(p||q)), which is minimized using a practical stochastic gradient descent method, as expectations are taken with respect to the fast-mixing distribution p.

Experiments on grid and random graphs demonstrate that Gibbs sampling on the projected parameters yields more accurate marginals than standard variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) and, crucially, also outperforms Gibbs sampling on the original parameters when the sampling time is limited.

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound and presents a well-executed piece of research. The theoretical foundation is solid, building upon established results on MCMC mixing times. The derivation of the dual algorithm for Euclidean projection in Theorem 7 is a valuable contribution that handles sparsity constraints effectively. The proposed stochastic optimization procedure for the reversed KL-divergence is practical and well-motivated.

The experimental evaluation is thorough. The comparison against a strong set of baselines, including both variational methods and standard Gibbs sampling, convincingly demonstrates the utility of the proposed approach. The choice to measure accuracy against exact marginals calculated via the junction-tree algorithm ensures a fair and reliable evaluation.

One point that could be clarified concerns the experimental setup. The theory is based on a spectral norm bound of 1.0, but the experiments use a bound of 2.5. While the discussion section correctly points out the looseness of the theoretical bound, it would be beneficial to provide more justification for this specific choice within the experimental section itself. Was this value tuned, and how sensitive are the results to it? A brief analysis of this trade-off would strengthen the paper's practical guidance.

**Clarity**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation for shifting the focus from structural tractability to fast-mixing tractability is articulated well in the introduction. The progression from the theoretical background to the different projection methods is logical and easy for the reader to follow. The connection drawn between the reversed KL projection and mean-field (as a special case with a zero spectral norm constraint) is insightful and helps position the work within the existing literature.

The description of the experimental setup and the interpretation of the results are also clear. However, while the piecewise KL-divergence is introduced as one of the main methods, its empirical results are not explicitly presented or discussed in Section 6. Including these results, even if they are not superior to the other methods, would make the experimental section more complete and provide valuable insights into the performance of this novel divergence.

**Originality**
The main contribution of this paper is highly original. The idea of projecting parameters to guarantee fast mixing, rather than simplifying the graph structure, presents a new and fundamentally different approach to approximate inference. While the individual components (MCMC mixing theory, KL-divergences, SVD thresholding) are known, their combination to create this new class of inference algorithms is novel and creative. The proposed piecewise KL-divergence is also a novel contribution. The work is clearly differentiated from prior art, which has largely focused on variational methods operating on structural approximations.

**Significance**
The results presented are significant and likely to be influential. This work opens a new research direction for approximate inference that bridges MCMC theory and variational methods. The finding that sampling from a "close" fast-mixing distribution can be more accurate in practice than attempting to sample from the true, slow-mixing one has important practical implications, especially for applications where inference must be performed under a strict time budget. Researchers and practitioners working with MCMC methods for graphical models are likely to find these ideas useful and build upon them. The proposed framework could also be extended to other types of models or used within learning algorithms, as suggested in the discussion.

One aspect that could enhance the assessment of significance is a more detailed analysis of the computational overhead of the projection step. The current experiments focus on the accuracy of sampling *after* the projection is complete. A comparison of total computation time (projection + sampling) versus standard Gibbs sampling would provide a more complete picture of the method's practical efficiency. This is not essential for acceptance but would be a valuable addition.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a highly novel and significant conceptual contribution: defining tractability via mixing time for approximate inference.
*   Technically sound, providing both a novel algorithm for Euclidean projection and a practical stochastic method for KL projection.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating clear benefits over established variational methods and standard Gibbs sampling in time-limited scenarios.
*   The paper is well-written and opens up several interesting avenues for future research.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The choice of the spectral norm bound in the experiments (2.5 vs. the theoretical 1.0) could be better motivated.
*   The computational cost of the projection phase is not fully accounted for in the experimental time-accuracy comparisons.

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a novel, well-executed, and important contribution to the field of approximate inference in graphical models.
