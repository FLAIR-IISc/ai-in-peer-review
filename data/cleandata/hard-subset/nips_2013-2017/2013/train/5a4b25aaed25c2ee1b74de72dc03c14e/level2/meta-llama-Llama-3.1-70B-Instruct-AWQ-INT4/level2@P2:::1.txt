This paper presents a novel approach to inference in high-treewidth graphical models by projecting the model parameters onto a set that guarantees fast mixing of Gibbs sampling. The authors propose a new notion of "tractability" that focuses on parameter-space conditions for rapid mixing rather than exact inference. They develop algorithms to project the model parameters onto this set using various divergences, including Euclidean distance, piecewise KL-divergence, and reversed KL-divergence.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and methodology. The introduction provides a thorough background on the challenges of inference in high-treewidth graphical models and the limitations of existing approximations. The authors then present their approach, which is based on a simple condition for rapid mixing in Ising models.

The technical contributions of the paper are significant, particularly the development of the dual algorithm for projecting onto the fast-mixing set using the Euclidean distance. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the mixing time bounds and the dependency matrix for Ising models.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the proposed methods to several standard variational methods and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters. The results show that the proposed methods are more accurate than variational approximations and can provide better results than Gibbs sampling with finite time.

The paper's strengths include:

* A novel approach to inference in high-treewidth graphical models that focuses on parameter-space conditions for rapid mixing.
* Significant technical contributions, including the development of the dual algorithm for projecting onto the fast-mixing set.
* A thorough analysis of the mixing time bounds and the dependency matrix for Ising models.
* A comprehensive experimental evaluation that compares the proposed methods to several standard variational methods and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters.

The paper's weaknesses include:

* The apparent looseness of the spectral norm bound, which may be too conservative in some cases.
* The need for further research to extend the approach to general Markov random fields, which will require finding a bound on the dependency matrix and developing an algorithm to project onto the fast-mixing set.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of inference in high-treewidth graphical models.
* The technical contributions are substantial, and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-executed.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and methodology.
