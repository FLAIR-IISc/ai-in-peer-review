- Proposes projecting intractable Ising-model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" parameter set (based on a spectral-norm condition) instead of simplifying graph structure.  
- Uses prior sufficient condition: Gibbs mixes rapidly if the dependency matrix spectral norm is below a threshold (||R||_2 < 1), applied to Ising interaction strengths.  
- Derives an efficient dual algorithm (Theorem 7) for Euclidean projection using iterative SVD thresholding while preserving graph sparsity.  
- Treats the Euclidean projection as a subroutine to minimize probabilistic divergences for closer approximations.  
- Considers three divergences: forward KL (KL(q||p)), a novel piecewise KL based on tractable subgraphs, and reverse KL (KL(p||q)).  
- Solves reverse KL projection with stochastic projected gradient descent, estimating expectations via Gibbs sampling from the projected (fast-mixing) distribution.  
- Experiments on 2D grids and random graphs show the method—especially reverse KL—yields more accurate marginal estimates than Mean Field, Loopy BP, and TRW.  
- Key empirical claim: for a fixed computation budget, Gibbs sampling on projected fast-mixing parameters gives more accurate marginals than sampling the original slow-mixing parameters.  
- Strength: original and significant idea—defining tractable families by mixing properties offers a new direction for approximate inference and learning.  
- Strength: strong technical contributions—the sparse Euclidean projection algorithm and principled reverse KL optimization are well-derived and insightful.  
- Strength: paper is clear, well-organized, and experimentally thorough against relevant baselines.  
- Weakness: the spectral-norm fast-mixing bound is conservative and experiments use a looser threshold (c = 2.5) that violates the theoretical guarantee, weakening theoretical-practical alignment.  
- Weakness: projection (especially reverse KL) is computationally expensive (e.g., many Gibbs steps); paper lacks end-to-end runtime vs. accuracy comparisons including projection cost.  
- Weakness: the piecewise KL contribution is under-evaluated—quantitative results for its performance are missing or limited.
