

This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set, where Gibbs sampling converges rapidly. The core idea is to minimize various divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, reversed KL) between original parameters and a constrained set where the spectral norm of the interaction matrix is below a threshold (ensuring fast mixing per Theorem 4). The authors derive projection algorithms (e.g., Theorem 7 for Euclidean distance) and demonstrate empirically that Gibbs sampling on projected parameters outperforms the original model under time constraints when interactions are strong.

**Strengths:**  
The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear connections to mixing time bounds (Theorem 4) and dependency matrices (Definition 2). The Euclidean projection algorithm (Theorem 7) is technically rigorous and well-motivated. The experimental design is thorough, comparing against standard methods (LBP, TRW, MF) on grid and random graphs, with accuracy measured via marginal absolute error. The practical insight—that projected parameters yield better accuracy *within limited sampling time*—is valuable and well-supported by experiments (Section 6).

**Major Weaknesses:**  
1. **Theoretical bound looseness undermines contribution:** The paper claims spectral norm <1 guarantees fast mixing (Section 3), but for 2D Ising models, the known threshold is β_c ≈ 0.44 (citing [11]), while the spectral bound requires β ≤ 0.25 (Section 7). The authors pragmatically use a looser bound (norm ≤2.5) but fail to address *why* this is acceptable or how it affects guarantees. This weakens the theoretical novelty and practical relevance. They should explicitly compare their bound to [11] and discuss whether tighter bounds (e.g., from [4]) could improve results.  
2. **Experiments lack scalability and significance:** All experiments use tiny graphs (8×8 grids, 10-node random graphs), making it unclear if the approach scales. The marginal accuracy gains over variational methods (Table 1) are modest (e.g., 0.02–0.05 improvement), and statistical significance is not tested. The paper claims "more accurate than variational methods," but without error bars or significance tests, this assertion is unsubstantiated.  
3. **KL divergence justification is flawed:** Section 5.4 claims reversed KL (KL(ψ||θ)) "often works well in practice" despite being non-convex and having high-variance importance sampling. The authors do not explain *why* it outperforms zero-avoiding KL (Section 5.2), which they acknowledge is theoretically superior. The empirical comparison (Section 6) shows reversed KL outperforms zero-avoiding KL, but this contradicts their own analysis—this discrepancy needs resolution.  

**Minor Issues:**  
- The piecewise KL approach (Section 5.3) lacks clarity on *why* using spanning trees or low-treewidth subgraphs improves accuracy over simpler methods (e.g., just using the full graph).  
- The "zero-forcing" terminology (Section 5.4) is confusing; "reversed KL" would be more standard.  
- The spectral norm bound in Section 6.1 (norm ≤2.5) is inconsistent with the theoretical guarantee (norm <1), undermining the paper’s premise.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper presents a technically competent method with a clear practical motivation. However, the major weakness—**theoretical bound looseness that invalidates the core guarantee**—is critical. Without addressing this (e.g., by deriving a tighter bound or justifying the pragmatic norm=2.5), the contribution lacks novelty relative to existing mixing-time analysis (e.g., [4,11]). The experimental results are insufficient to demonstrate significance at scale.  

**Suggestions for Revision:**  
1. Explicitly compare the spectral norm bound to known Ising thresholds (β_c) and discuss whether tighter bounds (e.g., from [11]) could be used.  
2. Conduct experiments on larger graphs (e.g., 16×16 grids) and include statistical significance testing.  
3. Resolve the contradiction between Section 5.4’s claims and empirical results for reversed KL vs. zero-avoiding KL.  
4. Clarify the practical justification for using norm=2.5 instead of the theoretical bound.  

**Verdict:** The work is technically sound but fails to deliver a significant advancement due to the unresolved theoretical gap. It is **not suitable for NIPS** in its current form. A revised version addressing the bound looseness and scaling would be acceptable.
