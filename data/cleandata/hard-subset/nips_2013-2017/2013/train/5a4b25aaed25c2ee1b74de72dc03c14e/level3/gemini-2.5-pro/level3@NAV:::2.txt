This paper proposes a novel approach for approximate inference in Ising models where Gibbs sampling mixes slowly due to strong interactions. The core idea is to project the model's original parameters onto a nearby set of parameters that are guaranteed to be "fast-mixing" by satisfying the Dobrushin criterion (specifically, a spectral norm bound on the interaction matrix). This projection is framed as a constrained optimization problem, and the authors explore solutions under several divergences, including Euclidean distance and KL-divergences. This work connects the areas of MCMC and variational inference by proposing a hybrid approach where the target distribution is modified to make sampling, rather than exact inference, tractable.

The primary strength of the paper is its originality. Defining a "tractable" family of distributions based on mixing time rather than graphical structure (e.g., low treewidth) is a creative and promising direction. The combination of deterministic optimization for the projection and stochastic sampling for inference is an interesting and relatively under-explored paradigm that could inspire future work.

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in clarity and experimental rigor. The methodological description is ambiguous in several key areas. The derivation and description of the dual problem for Euclidean projection are difficult to follow. It is also unclear how the `ZijBij = 0` constraints, intended to preserve graph sparsity, guarantee that the projected parameters can span the same space as the original; they might unduly restrict the solution space. Most importantly, the overall procedure, particularly for the KL-divergence projection, is unclear. An algorithm box is essential to clarify whether the projection is fully optimized before sampling begins or if projection updates and sampling steps are interleaved. The convergence claim for the projected gradient descent strategy also needs stronger justification.

The experimental evaluation is unconvincing. Comparing methods based on a fixed number of Gibbs iterations (30,000) is arbitrary and presents an unfair comparison against deterministic methods like LBP. Crucially, the experiments fail to report wall-clock times, ignoring the computational cost of the projection itself and leaving the practical utility of the method in question. Furthermore, the plots lack error bars, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the claimed improvements. The paper also omits comparisons to highly relevant baselines designed to improve mixing, such as blocked Gibbs sampling or Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs Sampler.

In summary, while the paper introduces a promising and original idea, the execution in its current form is incomplete. The lack of methodological clarity and a weak, unfair experimental evaluation prevent a recommendation for acceptance. The authors are encouraged to clarify their method and conduct a more rigorous and comprehensive empirical study.
