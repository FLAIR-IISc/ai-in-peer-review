- Proposes projecting Ising-model parameters onto a fast-mixing set Ψ (based on dependency matrix R and spectral norm) so Gibbs sampling is provably fast-mixing.  
- For Ising models uses Rij ≤ tanh(|βij|) and fast mixing if ||R||2 < 1, building on prior mixing-time results.

- Introduces Euclidean projection onto Ψ via a dual that enforces a spectral-norm constraint and sparsity mask to avoid graph densification.  
- Considers three KL-based projections: KL(θ||ψ) (impractical due to expectations under θ), piecewise KL on tractable subgraphs, and KL(ψ||θ) optimized by stochastic approximation.

- Presents a practical KL(ψ||θ) method using projected stochastic gradient descent with samples from ψ and a sample pool; connects to mean-field as a c → 0 limit.  
- Empirical claim: for strong interactions and limited sampling time, Gibbs on projected ψ outperforms Gibbs on θ and common variational baselines on grids and small graphs.

- Technical idea is sound and grounded in mixing-time theory, and the Euclidean dual projection could be useful.  
- Derivation of the Euclidean dual (recovering signed B from D = |B|) and relation of D-based feasible set to the true R constraint are under-specified and may be conservative.

- Steps from the primal to the dual (objective and gradients) are hard to follow; need fuller derivation and discussion of convexity/duality gap.  
- The KL(ψ||θ) algorithmic procedure is described narratively and ambiguous (when/how projections are applied, interplay of sampling, gradient estimation, and projection).

- Convergence justification cites Ergodic Mirror Descent, but the experimental settings (fixed step size, single Gibbs pass per iteration) do not meet standard convergence conditions; guarantees are unclear.  
- Experiments use an empirically chosen spectral bound c = 2.5 (violating the theoretical ||R||2 < 1 guarantee) without mixing diagnostics or justification.

- Clarity issues: need explicit description of projection mapping (handling signs, preserving sparsity) and whether interactions can migrate to non-edges.  
- Clarity issues: require an explicit algorithm box for KL(ψ||θ) with inputs/outputs, projection frequency, step-size policy, and stopping criteria.

- Originality: framing fast-mixing distributions as a tractable family and projection under divergences is novel; piecewise KL is a reasonable adaptation of existing ideas.  
- Significance: could be practically useful for strong-coupling regimes if validated, with an appealing connection to mean-field.

- Empirical evaluation concerns: comparisons by iteration count are not wall-clock fair and exclude projection time; need wall-clock or flop-normalized budgets.  
- Empirical evaluation concerns: missing stronger MCMC baselines for slow-mixing regimes (blocked Gibbs, Swendsen–Wang, lifted sampling) under matched budgets.

- Empirical evaluation concerns: lacks mixing diagnostics (ESS, autocorrelation, spectral-gap proxies), error bars, and details on number of runs/seeds.  
- Empirical ablations needed: sensitivity to spectral norm bound c, pool size, Gibbs updates per iteration, step size, and comparisons among projection types under matched budgets.

- Pros: novel tractable family with grounding in theory; practical projected SGD for KL(ψ||θ) linking to mean-field; dual Euclidean projection aiming to preserve sparsity.  
- Cons: dual derivation and mapping back to signed parameters under-specified; overall algorithm ambiguous and lacks reproducible procedure and convergence guarantees.

- Reviewer questions for authors: How is signed B recovered from D = |B|? Is Z ∘ D = 0 and ||D||2 ≤ c equivalent to the desired R constraint or conservative? Are there mixing diagnostics for c = 2.5? Can you provide a clear algorithm box for KL(ψ||θ)?

- Recommendation: Borderline reject — promising and relevant but needs clearer derivations, explicit algorithms, stronger/ fairer experiments, error bars, and mixing diagnostics before acceptance.
