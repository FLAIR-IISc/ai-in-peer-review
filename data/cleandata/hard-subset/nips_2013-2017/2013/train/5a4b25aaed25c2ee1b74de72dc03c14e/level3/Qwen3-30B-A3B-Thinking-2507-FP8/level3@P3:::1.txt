

**Review: Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing**

This paper proposes a novel approach to accelerate Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set satisfying spectral bounds. Building on prior rapid-mixing results [4,8], the authors develop projection methods that find parameters close to the original but with weaker interactions ensuring rapid mixing. The key insight is that for strong interactions, Gibbs sampling may take exponential time to converge, but projecting onto a nearby fast-mixing model can yield better marginal estimates within fixed computational time.

**Strengths:**
1. **Originality:** The paper's core contribution—projecting onto the nearest rapidly-mixing model as an inference tool—is novel, extending prior mixing-time results [4,8] to a practical inference framework.
2. **Technical Soundness:** The Euclidean projection method (Theorem 7) is well-developed, and the dual algorithm for spectral norm constraints is technically rigorous.
3. **Experimental Evidence:** On small Ising models (8×8 grids, 10-node graphs), projected models outperform variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) and original-model Gibbs within fixed computation time when interactions are strong.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Scalability Concerns:** The most significant limitation is the lack of experiments on larger models. Mixing-time comparisons on small systems (8×8 grids, 10 nodes) may not generalize, as the paper acknowledges but fails to address. The spectral norm bound (||R||₂ < 1) appears overly conservative (Section 7), with a gap of ~1.76 from the known threshold βc ≈ 0.44 for 2D Ising models.
2. **Experimental Flaws:** The projection step's high computational cost (e.g., 30,000 Gibbs iterations for Euclidean projection) is omitted from timing comparisons in Figure 2, artificially favoring the proposed method. The authors should include projection time or clarify its omission.
3. **Clarity Issues:** Key results lack proofs (Theorem 6, Lemma 5), and Section 4's notation (g, M, Lambda) is confusingly introduced. The dependency matrix R is not adequately discussed, and its maximization makes tractability unclear. Section 1's KL(q||p) usage flips conventional p/q roles.
4. **Limited Applicability:** The approach is restricted to binary Ising models with unclear generalizability to non-binary variables (where convergence bounds are harder to establish). The paper misses exploring KL(θ||ψ) projection as a "best case" (using junction trees on toy models) and fails to explain the large error gap between reverse-KL projection and mean-field.
5. **Experimental Scope:** Experiments lack larger/realistic models; the authors should include symmetric models with tractable marginals or use long-chain sampling as ground truth.

**Quality & Significance:** The theoretical development is sound but lacks sufficient experimental validation for broader applicability. While the idea is elegant and leverages recent mixing bounds, the experimental comparisons do not convincingly demonstrate scalability or practical advantages beyond toy models. The significance is limited by the narrow scope of experiments and unresolved scalability concerns.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting theoretical idea but falls short of the experimental rigor expected at NIPS. I recommend rejection due to the unaddressed scalability concerns, omitted projection costs in timing comparisons, and insufficient exploration of the method's limitations. The authors should address these issues by conducting experiments on larger models, including projection time in all timing comparisons, providing complete proofs for key theorems, and discussing generalizability to non-binary variables before reconsideration.
