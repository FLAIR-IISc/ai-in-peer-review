- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set (spectral-norm constrained) so univariate Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to mix quickly.  
- Uses spectral-norm bound (||R||2 < 1) with Rij ≤ tanh|βij| to relate Ising couplings to mixing guarantees, following Dyer et al. and Hayes.  
- Formulates projections under multiple divergences: Euclidean, KL(θ||ψ), piecewise KL, and KL(ψ||θ).  
- Presents an exact Euclidean projection preserving sparsity via a dual formulation and singular-value thresholding within L-BFGS-B (Theorem 7).  
- Handles other divergences with projected (stochastic) gradient methods and Euclidean projection onto the fast-mixing set.  
- Empirically on 8×8 grids and small random graphs, projected fast-mixing models produce more accurate marginals than MF, LBP, and TRW.  
- Under finite time budgets, projected models often outperform Gibbs on the original slow-mixing parameters.  
- KL(ψ||θ) performs best empirically; KL(θ||ψ) is impractical except for tiny graphs; piecewise KL is intermediate but easy to evaluate.  
- Mixing guarantee and Euclidean projection derivation are technically sound and well-motivated; stochastic estimator (Eq. 8) is carefully discussed regarding bias/variance.  
- Caveat: experiments sometimes use spectral norm bound c = 2.5 (violating theoretical <1), forfeiting guarantees—need quantification of how often models fail to be fast mixing and sensitivity of accuracy/runtime to c.  
- Caveat: Theorem 4 is for random-scan Gibbs but experiments use systematic scan—clarify conditions and whether bounds hold for the update schedule used.  
- Clarity issues: specify L-BFGS-B stopping criteria, tolerances, and warm-starting strategy for the inner Euclidean projection.  
- Figure 2: clarify x-axis “number of samples” vs end-to-end budgets; report absolute run times (seconds) on fixed hardware and include LBP/TRW/MF runtimes.  
- Minor edits and notation fixes requested: text corrections (lines 222, 226), define Z and Hadamard product earlier, and briefly remind/define the triple dot product before Theorem 7.  
- Originality: novel idea to project onto a fast-mixing spectral-norm-constrained family while preserving sparsity; dual Euclidean projection is technically original.  
- Significance: provides a new inference tool for taming slow-mixing models with promising empirical gains, but impact depends on larger-scale demonstrations and detailed cost–accuracy tradeoffs.  
- Strengths: principled fast-mixing family definition, elegant sparsity-preserving Euclidean projection, workable stochastic projected-gradient scheme, and consistent empirical improvements.  
- Weaknesses / requests: report empirical mixing diagnostics, sensitivity plots over c ∈ {0.8,1.0,1.5,2.5}, L-BFGS-B optimization details, total projection time and memory, and absolute runtime comparisons with error bars.  
- Additional request: quantify estimator bias from using the same sample pool for µ̂ and ĝ (ablation with independent pools or larger pools).  
- Additional request: clarify implications of systematic vs random Gibbs scan for guarantees and practice.  
- Scalability concern: include larger graph experiments to demonstrate optimization and sampling scaling.  
- Pro accept: novel, well-motivated, technically solid with empirical gains and conceptual generalization of mean-field.  
- Con accept: experiments violate guarantees in key cases (c=2.5), uncertain scalability and total cost once projection overhead is included, and some clarity gaps remain.  
- Overall: interesting and timely contribution leaning positive, contingent on clarifications about runtime, optimization stopping criteria, sensitivity to spectral bound, and ideally one larger-scale experiment.
