This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models by projecting the model parameters onto a set that guarantees fast mixing of Gibbs sampling. The authors explore various divergences for the projection, including Euclidean distance, KL-divergence, and piecewise KL-divergence. The main idea is to find a parameter vector ψ that is close to the original vector θ but guaranteed to be fast mixing.

The paper relates to previous work on approximate inference in graphical models, such as mean-field inference, structured mean-field, loopy belief propagation, and tree-reweighted belief propagation. The authors also draw on the literature on mixing times in Markov chains, citing a recent textbook and several papers on the topic.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a constrained optimization algorithm for projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set. The authors provide a dual formulation of the projection problem and derive a projected gradient descent strategy for computing the projection under various divergences. They also propose a novel piecewise KL-divergence based on computing the exact KL-divergence on several low-treewidth subgraphs.

The paper's strengths include its originality, technical soundness, and potential significance. The idea of projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set is novel and has the potential to improve the accuracy of approximate inference in graphical models. The paper's technical contributions, including the dual formulation of the projection problem and the projected gradient descent strategy, are sound and well-executed.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The description of the dual of the projection problem is unclear, and it is not obvious how the zij * dij = 0 constraints guarantee that the new parameterization spans the same space. The overall procedure is also ambiguous, and it is not clear whether the authors fully optimize the projection and then sample or interleave projection updates with sampling. An algorithm box would be helpful to clarify the procedure.

Additionally, the experiments are not entirely convincing. The choice of 30K Gibbs iterations is arbitrary, and iteration-based comparisons are unfair versus deterministic methods. There are no timing comparisons, so the practical benefit of the proposed approach is unclear. The plots lack error bars, so the statistical significance of the results is unknown. Finally, there are no comparisons to blocked Gibbs or Gogate's Lifted Gibbs Sampler, which would be useful benchmarks.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and original approach to approximate inference in Ising models.
* The technical contributions, including the dual formulation of the projection problem and the projected gradient descent strategy, are sound and well-executed.
* The paper has the potential to improve the accuracy of approximate inference in graphical models.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The description of the dual of the projection problem is unclear.
* The overall procedure is ambiguous, and it is not clear whether the authors fully optimize the projection and then sample or interleave projection updates with sampling.
* The experiments are not entirely convincing, and there are no timing comparisons or comparisons to relevant benchmarks.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, but with revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above. The authors should clarify the description of the dual of the projection problem, provide an algorithm box to clarify the procedure, and strengthen the experimental evaluation by including timing comparisons and comparisons to relevant benchmarks.
