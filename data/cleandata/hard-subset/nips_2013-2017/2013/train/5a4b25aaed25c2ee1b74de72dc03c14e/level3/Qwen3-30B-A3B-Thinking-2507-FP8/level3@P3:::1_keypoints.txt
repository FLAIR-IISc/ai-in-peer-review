- Novelty: Proposes a new inference approach—projecting parameters onto the nearest rapidly-mixing model—extending prior mixing-time results to practice.  
- Technical soundness: Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) and the dual algorithm for spectral-norm constraints are well-developed and rigorous.  
- Experimental evidence: On small Ising models (8×8 grids, 10-node graphs), projected models outperform variational methods and original-model Gibbs within fixed compute time for strong interactions.  
- Scalability concerns: No experiments on larger models; small-system results may not generalize, and the spectral-norm bound (||R||₂<1) appears overly conservative (≈1.76 gap from βc≈0.44).  
- Experimental flaws: Projection’s high computational cost (e.g., 30,000 Gibbs iterations) is omitted from timing comparisons, biasing results in favor of the method.  
- Clarity issues: Key proofs are missing (Theorem 6, Lemma 5); Section 4 introduces confusing notation (g, M, Λ); dependency matrix R is not adequately discussed and its maximization/tractability is unclear; KL(q||p) usage flips conventional roles.  
- Limited applicability: Approach is restricted to binary Ising models with unclear generalization to non-binary variables; authors don’t explore KL(θ||ψ) projection or explain the large error gap between reverse-KL projection and mean-field.  
- Experimental scope lacking: Should include larger/realistic models, symmetric models with tractable marginals, or long-chain sampling as ground truth to validate practical usefulness.  
- Quality & significance: Theoretical contributions are elegant and sound, but significance is limited by narrow experiments and unresolved scalability/implementation concerns.  
- Recommendation: Reject in current form; require experiments on larger models, inclusion of projection time in timings, complete proofs, and discussion of generalizability before reconsideration.
