

This paper proposes an interesting approach to improve inference in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set that guarantees rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling. The authors leverage spectral bounds on the dependency matrix to define a tractable parameter space subset, then develop projection methods under various divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL) to find parameters close to the original model while satisfying mixing conditions. The core technical contribution is the dual method for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7), which appears novel in applying spectral norm constraints to Ising models.

The paper's main strengths lie in its elegant theoretical connection between spectral bounds and mixing times (building on Dyer et al. [4] and Hayes [8]), and the clear demonstration that for small models, projected Gibbs sampling outperforms standard variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) within fixed computational budgets. The idea of using fast-mixing models as a tractable family for inference is conceptually fresh and well-motivated.

However, significant concerns undermine the paper's contribution. First, the experimental evaluation omits the computational cost of the projection step (30,000+ Gibbs iterations for Euclidean projection), biasing results in favor of the proposed method. Second, the paper fails to demonstrate scaling to larger models, which is critical for practical impact—the limited experiments on small graphs (10 nodes, 8x8 grids) cannot address whether gains persist with increasing model size. Third, the paper lacks clarity in several key areas: Theorem 6 and Lemma 5 lack proofs, Section 4's notation (g, M, Lambda) is confusing, and the KL divergence convention is reversed in Section 1. Fourth, the paper doesn't address the spectral norm bound's looseness (conservative by ~1.76x for 2D Ising models) nor explore the potential "best case" of KL(θ||ψ) projection.

While the core idea is promising, the experimental evaluation and presentation do not adequately establish practical advantages over existing approaches. The paper would benefit from larger-scale experiments, clearer exposition of technical results, and a more thorough discussion of the projection's computational cost. The significance of the work is limited by these experimental flaws and lack of generalizability beyond binary Ising models.
