- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a space guaranteeing fast mixing for Gibbs sampling to avoid slow convergence or intractability.  
- Fast-mixing models are defined via the spectral norm of the dependency matrix.  
- Considers projection under multiple divergences: Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL.  
- Distinguishes itself by targeting sampling convergence properties rather than direct marginal approximation.  
- Novelty: the fast-mixing projection idea is a fresh contribution to graphical-model inference.  
- Thorough empirical exploration of divergence choices elucidates accuracy vs. computational-cost trade-offs.  
- Empirically outperforms loopy BP, tree-reweighted BP, and mean-field in presented experiments, and matches or beats Gibbs on original parameters within limited runtime.  
- Provides theoretical grounding (connection to ergodic mirror descent) and clear exposition of mixing-time theory.  
- Major weakness: projection computational cost is high, particularly for reversed KL which requires iterative Gibbs sampling inside the projection.  
- Experiments are limited to small models; scalability to larger, complex graphs is unresolved.  
- Concern that the spectral norm bound may be overly conservative; tighter bounds need investigation.  
- Methodological detail lacking: stopping criteria for the inner L-BFGS-B loop in projected gradient descent is unclear.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on clarifying stopping criteria, reporting absolute running times and runtime comparisons (LBP, TRW, MF), and discussing scalability and future work to reduce computational cost.
