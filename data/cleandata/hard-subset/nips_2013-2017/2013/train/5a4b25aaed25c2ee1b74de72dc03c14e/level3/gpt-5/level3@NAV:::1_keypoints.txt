- Paper proposes projecting strongly-coupled Ising parameters θ to nearby ψ that satisfy spectral bounds for rapid mixing, then running Gibbs on ψ as a tractable surrogate for inference.

- Develops several projection methods: Euclidean (spectral-norm constraints), piecewise KL on low-treewidth subgraphs, and reverse KL (KL(ψ||θ)) optimized with stochastic approximation.

- Builds on rapid-mixing spectral results (Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum, Hayes) to justify the approach and connects mixing-time theory with variational/MCMC practice.

- Experiments on small grids and random graphs show regimes where projected models give more accurate marginals within a fixed sampling budget than LBP/TRW/MF and Gibbs on the original model.

- Novelty: projection-onto-nearest provably fast-mixing model and piecewise-KL surrogate appear to be new contributions.

- Technical rigor issues: several “standard results” (e.g., SVD projection in Theorem 6, Ising dependency bound in Lemma 5) lack citations or proof sketches and may be misstated.

- The derivation and tractability of the projection dual (Theorem 7) and the role/definition of the dependency matrix R need clearer explanation.

- Optimization over M, Λ (maximizing g) using LBFGS-B may be nontrivial; convergence properties and practical tractability are not analyzed.

- In practice the spectral guarantee is relaxed (e.g., using 2.5 instead of 1), which undermines the claimed mixing-time guarantees without justification or diagnostics.

- Experimental timing omits the potentially large projection cost, biasing comparisons and giving incomplete end-to-end runtime results.

- Clarity problems: Section 4 is hard to follow with abrupt notation (g, M, Λ, Z) and theorems appearing without intuition or a running example; dependency matrix definition and its maximization are not discussed explicitly.

- Notational inconsistency/confusion in KL directions (flip between KL(q||p) and KL(p||q)) and several results lack references.

- Significance limited by scope: experiments are restricted to small Ising models, so scalability and real-world impact are unclear.

- Generalization to multi-state MRFs is unclear due to missing dependency bounds beyond binary Ising models.

- Pros: elegant principled idea, multiple projection objectives explored, and reverse-KL with stochastic approximation appears practical; empirical gains in certain regimes.

- Cons: questionable scalability, missing end-to-end timing, relaxed guarantees in practice, clarity gaps, unexplained gap between MF and reverse-KL projection, and limited exploration of KL(θ||ψ).

- Suggestions: include projection time in timing plots, add larger/symmetric/tractable models or long chains for ground truth, analyze scaling of mixing/accuracy with n, explore KL(θ||ψ) on toy problems, explain MF vs reverse-KL behavior, fix KL notation, provide references/proof sketches, and discuss feasibility of optimizing over R and extensions to non-binary MRFs.
