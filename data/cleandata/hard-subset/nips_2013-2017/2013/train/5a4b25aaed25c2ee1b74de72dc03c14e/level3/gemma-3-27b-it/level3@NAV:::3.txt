## Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

This paper introduces a novel approach to inference in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a space guaranteeing fast mixing of Gibbs sampling. The core idea—leveraging parameter transformations to improve sampling efficiency—is a valuable contribution, addressing a key bottleneck in applying MCMC methods to complex models. The authors rigorously explore various projection methods based on different divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL), offering both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation. 

The work builds upon established techniques in graphical model inference (mean-field, loopy BP, TRW) and mixing time analysis, positioning itself as an alternative that explicitly targets sampling convergence.  The connection to existing work on fast mixing conditions, particularly the spectral norm of the dependency matrix, is well-articulated.  The exploration of diverse divergences and the development of corresponding projection algorithms (especially the stochastic approximation approach for reversed KL) demonstrates a significant effort and ingenuity.

The paper’s strengths lie in its strong theoretical grounding, the novelty of the approach, and compelling experimental results. The empirical evaluation consistently demonstrates that the proposed methods outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy and often achieve comparable or superior performance to Gibbs sampling on the original model, *given reasonable runtimes*.  The reversed KL projection consistently yielded the best performance, further strengthening the contribution. 

However, the practical utility of the method remains somewhat uncertain due to runtime concerns. The experiments are limited to relatively small models, and the reported results lack absolute runtime figures in seconds, making a direct comparison to other methods difficult.  The description of the projected-gradient algorithm in Sec. 5.1 is unclear regarding the stopping criteria for the nested L-BFGS-B iterations; specifying these criteria is crucial for reproducibility and understanding computational cost.  Expanding the experimental evaluation to larger, more realistic models, perhaps utilizing tractable random field models like those proposed by Flach, would be highly beneficial to assess scalability. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound approach to improving MCMC efficiency.
*   Strong empirical results on small models demonstrating superior accuracy.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the methodology and experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited scalability demonstrated by experiments on small models only.
*   Lack of detailed runtime analysis hinders practical assessment.
*   Unclear stopping criteria for nested optimization within the projected-gradient algorithm.



**Minor Edits:** As suggested, replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T” (line 222) and use “subgradient” instead of “derivative” (line 226).

**Overall:**  This is an interesting and promising paper with convincing empirical results. However, addressing the concerns regarding runtime and scalability in a rebuttal is crucial to demonstrate its potential for broader impact. I lean towards acceptance, contingent upon a satisfactory response to the reviewer questions.
