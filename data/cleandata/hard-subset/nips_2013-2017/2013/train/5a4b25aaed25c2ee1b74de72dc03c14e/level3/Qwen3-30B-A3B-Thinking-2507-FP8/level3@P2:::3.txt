

# Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth Ising models by projecting parameters onto a set of "fast-mixing" models, defined by spectral norm constraints on the interaction matrix. The core idea is to transform an intractable (slow-mixing) Ising model into a fast-mixing one through parameter projection, enabling efficient Gibbs sampling. The authors explore four different divergences for this projection: Euclidean distance, zero-avoiding KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL. They provide theoretical guarantees for the Euclidean projection using a dual optimization approach, and develop practical algorithms for the other divergences that leverage Gibbs sampling within the projection procedure. The work relates to previous NIPS contributions on variational inference (e.g., mean-field, TRW, loopy BP) but introduces a fundamentally different notion of tractability based on mixing time rather than graph structure.

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with well-supported theoretical results. Theorem 7 provides a novel dual optimization approach for Euclidean projection, and the experimental design is rigorous, comparing against multiple standard inference methods (LBP, TRW, mean-field, Gibbs on original parameters) across various graph topologies and interaction strengths. The empirical results consistently show that the proposed methods outperform standard variational approaches in marginal accuracy. However, the spectral norm bound appears conservative (factor of ~1.76 compared to known Ising threshold), and the authors should clarify how this affects practical implementation. The paper is technically sound, but the high computational cost of the proposed methods (particularly reversed KL projection) needs more thorough analysis.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The background on mixing times is concise and well-motivated. The experimental section clearly describes the setup, but two aspects require clarification: (1) The nested optimization in Section 5.1 (L-BFGS-B within projected gradient descent) lacks stopping criteria for inner iterations; (2) Figure 2's x-axis labeled "number of samples" is misleading since sampling is only used for reversed KL; the authors should report absolute runtime in seconds for fair comparison with LBP, TRW, and MF. The minor typo "onto the tree" (line 222) should be corrected to "onto graph T," and "derivative" should be replaced with "subgradient" (line 226).

**Originality**: The paper makes a significant contribution by introducing the concept of "fast-mixing" as a new criterion for tractable models, distinct from previous work that focused on graph structure (mean-field, TRW) or approximation quality (LBP). While spectral norm bounds for mixing times have been studied (e.g., Hayes [8]), the authors' use of these bounds to define a tractable parameter set for inference is novel. The piecewise KL divergence approach is particularly innovative, extending the concept of tractable subgraphs to divergence minimization. This work clearly differs from recent NIPS papers on inference (e.g., Hazan & Shashua [9], Globerson & Jaakkola [6]).

**Significance**: The results demonstrate that fast-mixing projections can provide more accurate marginal estimates than standard variational methods and outperform Gibbs sampling on original parameters for reasonable computational budgets. This addresses a practical problem in graphical models where exact inference is intractable and traditional variational methods may be inaccurate. The approach could be particularly valuable for applications requiring fast inference with reasonable accuracy. However, the current computational overhead may limit practical utility, and the authors should address scalability concerns (e.g., testing on larger models as suggested in key points).

**Pro-acceptance arguments**: 
1. Novel theoretical framework for "fast-mixing" inference
2. Strong empirical results showing consistent improvements over multiple baselines
3. Well-justified theoretical foundation with clear connections to mixing time literature
4. Practical approach that could benefit many applications requiring approximate inference

**Con-acceptance arguments**: 
1. Computational cost of the proposed methods is high (needs clearer runtime analysis)
2. Spectral norm bound appears conservative (factor of ~1.76)
3. Limited scalability (only tested on small graphs)
4. Some algorithmic details require clarification (nested optimization, figure labeling)

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes an important contribution to the field of approximate inference in graphical models. The theoretical insights are novel and well-supported, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed approach. While the computational cost is a concern that needs addressing in the final version, the results are promising and the work advances the state of the art in a meaningful way. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the algorithmic details and computational analysis. The paper aligns well with NIPS's focus on rigorous scientific contributions that advance the field.
