

This paper proposes an interesting approach to improving inference in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set defined by spectral norm constraints. The authors develop a framework for projecting parameters under various divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL) while preserving the original graph structure, with the goal of enabling faster Gibbs sampling. The key technical contribution is the dual method for Euclidean projection and the adaptation of projected gradient descent for other divergences, particularly the reversed KL which requires Gibbs sampling during the projection process.

The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on small graphs, showing that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, mean-field, and loopy BP in accuracy, and often beat Gibbs sampling on the original model within reasonable time limits. The reversed KL projection emerges as the most effective approach. This work builds on previous notions of tractable approximations (e.g., mean-field, tree-reweighted methods) but introduces a novel perspective by focusing on parameter-space conditions guaranteeing rapid mixing rather than structural constraints.

Strengths include the clear theoretical foundation relating spectral norms to mixing times, well-executed experiments comparing to multiple baselines, and the practical insight that fast-mixing approximations can yield better results than exact Gibbs sampling with limited runtime. The paper is well-written with clear mathematical formulations, though minor edits are needed (e.g., "subgradient" instead of "derivative").

Weaknesses include the limited scalability (only small graphs tested), unclear runtime reporting in Figure 2 (labeled "number of samples" but sampling is only used for reversed KL), and insufficient discussion of the high computational cost of the reversed KL method. The request for clarification on the nested optimization stopping criteria is valid, as this affects reproducibility. The paper would benefit from larger-scale experiments (e.g., using Boris Flach's tractable random-field models) to better assess practical utility.

Overall, this is a technically sound contribution with clear empirical validation, though the practical limitations require further investigation. The work advances the state of the art in approximate inference by introducing a new notion of "tractability" based on mixing time guarantees, making it suitable for NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarifications requested.
