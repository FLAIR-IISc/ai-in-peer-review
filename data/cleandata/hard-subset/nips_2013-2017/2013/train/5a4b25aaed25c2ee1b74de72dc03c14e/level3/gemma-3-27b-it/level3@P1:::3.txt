This paper explores a novel approach to inference in Ising models by projecting model parameters onto a space guaranteed to facilitate fast mixing in Gibbs sampling. The core idea is to address the limitations of traditional inference methods – such as intractability due to high treewidth or slow convergence with strong interactions – by transforming the problem into one where Gibbs sampling is demonstrably efficient. The authors define fast-mixing models based on the spectral norm of the dependency matrix and investigate several divergence measures to achieve this projection, including Euclidean distance, KL-divergence, piecewise KL-divergence, and a reversed KL-divergence. The work builds upon a rich history of approximations in graphical models, referencing techniques like mean-field inference [14, 20], loopy belief propagation [21], and tree-reweighted belief propagation [19]. It distinguishes itself by focusing on the convergence properties of the sampling process itself, rather than directly approximating the marginals.

The strengths of this paper lie in its innovative approach and compelling empirical validation. The concept of leveraging fast-mixing parameter spaces is a valuable contribution. The thorough exploration of different divergence measures for projection provides a nuanced understanding of the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed methods outperform established techniques like loopy belief propagation, tree-reweighted belief propagation, and mean field, particularly when computational resources are limited.  Furthermore, achieving comparable or superior accuracy to Gibbs sampling on the original parameters within a restricted runtime is a significant result.  The connection drawn to ergodic mirror descent [3] provides a theoretical grounding for the stochastic approximation approach. The paper also offers a clear and well-structured presentation of the underlying theory related to mixing times [4, 8, 10, 11], making it accessible to a broader audience.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The computational cost associated with the projection process, especially the reversed KL divergence which requires iterative Gibbs sampling *within* the projection algorithm, remains a concern. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed analysis of runtime scaling and absolute execution times is needed. The experiments are currently limited to relatively small models. Scaling the proposed methods to larger, more complex graphs is a critical open question.  The discussion of the spectral norm bound's tightness (Section 7) raises a valid point; further investigation into tighter bounds could significantly improve the practical applicability of the approach.  The description of the projected gradient descent algorithm in Section 5.1 lacks clarity regarding the stopping criteria for the inner L-BFGS-B optimization loop. A more detailed explanation of this would strengthen the methodological rigor.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing space is a fresh perspective on inference in graphical models.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The proposed methods consistently outperform existing techniques in the presented experiments.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The work is grounded in established theory regarding Markov chain Monte Carlo and mixing times.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making the core concepts accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Computational Cost:** The runtime of the projection process could be prohibitive for large-scale applications.
*   **Scalability:** The experiments are limited to small models, leaving open the question of scalability.
*   **Tightness of Bounds:** The current spectral norm bound may be overly conservative.
*   **Lack of Detail:** Some aspects of the algorithm, such as the stopping criteria for the inner L-BFGS-B loop, require further clarification.



Overall, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed approach is innovative, the empirical results are promising, and the theoretical foundation is solid. However, addressing the concerns regarding computational cost, scalability, and algorithmic details would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors providing clarification on the points raised above in a rebuttal. Specifically, the authors should: 1) clarify the stopping criteria for the inner L-BFGS-B iterations in Section 5.1, 2) provide absolute running times in seconds for all methods and include a runtime comparison for LBP, TRW, and MF, and 3) discuss the potential for scaling to larger models and future work to address the computational cost.
