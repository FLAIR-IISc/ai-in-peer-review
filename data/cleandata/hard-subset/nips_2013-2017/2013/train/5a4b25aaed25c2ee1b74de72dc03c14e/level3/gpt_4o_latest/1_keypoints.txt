- Novel idea: Projecting model parameters onto the nearest rapidly-mixing model is original and shifts focus from structural tractability to mixing-time guarantees.
- Theoretical strength: Rigorous derivations for projections under multiple divergences and spectral-norm-based rapid-mixing guarantees; elegant dual formulation for Euclidean projection.
- Empirical strength: Proposed methods outperform standard variational techniques (mean-field, loopy BP) in marginal accuracy under finite sampling time on tested models.
- Projection methods: Introduces Euclidean, piecewise KL, and reverse KL projection approaches for enforcing fast mixing.
- Scalability concern: Unclear scalability—projection step (especially SGD with repeated Gibbs sampling) is computationally expensive and its cost is not reported.
- Limited experiments: Evaluation is restricted to small toy models (e.g., 8×8 grids, 10-node random graphs), limiting generalizability.
- Generalization gap: No clear extension or discussion for general MRFs or non-binary state spaces and how spectral-norm bounds adapt.
- Clarity and rigor issues: Contains unproven claims (e.g., looseness of spectral norm bound), missing references, and inconsistent KL notation.
- Reverse KL gap: Large performance gap between mean-field error and reverse KL projection error is observed but not theoretically explained.
- Argument for acceptance: Innovative, theoretically solid approach that could inspire future mixing-time-aware inference research.
- Argument against acceptance: Practicality concerns due to scalability, omitted projection costs, and narrow experimental scope reduce impact.
- Recommendation: Recommend rejection in current form, advising experiments on larger models, accounting for projection costs, and exploring extensions to general MRFs.
