Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a new tractable family for approximate inference: fast-mixing Ising models, defined by a sufficient condition that the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute pairwise couplings is below a threshold (building on mixing-time bounds from Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum and Hayes). Given a potentially slow-mixing Ising model, the authors “project” its parameters onto this fast-mixing set while preserving the original graph structure (enforced via a masking matrix) and then perform inference by running Gibbs sampling on the projected model, which is guaranteed (under the bound) to mix rapidly. Four projections are studied: Euclidean distance, inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ), a piecewise KL computed on tractable subgraphs, and the reverse KL KL(ψ||θ). The Euclidean projection is solved via a dual formulation that reduces to repeated singular value thresholding with sparsity constraints and is optimized with L-BFGS-B; the other divergences are handled by projected gradient, where each gradient step is followed by Euclidean projection back into the fast-mixing set. For the reverse KL, the gradient requires expectations under ψ, so Gibbs sampling is run inside the projection procedure, leveraging fast mixing.

Experiments on small random graphs (n=10) and 8×8 grids compare estimated marginals to exact marginals (via junction-tree) and trade off runtime vs accuracy. Baselines include LBP, TRW, naive mean-field, and (outer) Gibbs on the original model. The proposed projections consistently outperform LBP/TRW/MF in marginal accuracy; for reasonable time budgets they also outperform Gibbs on the original parameters. Among the proposed divergences, reverse KL performs best.

The work connects to classical variational methods (mean-field can be seen as reverse-KL projection onto the zero-spectral-norm set), to structured approximations (tree/planar/clustered), and to mixing-time criteria (Dobrushin vs spectral). Using fast-mixing constraints as the tractable family is a novel and appealing shift from structure-based tractability.

Quality
- The technical development is sound: the dual for Euclidean projection with a structural mask is clearly derived; the projected-gradient framework is appropriate; the reverse-KL gradient estimator is carefully discussed (including its relation to Ergodic Mirror Descent).
- The empirical claims (accuracy improvements over LBP/TRW/MF; reverse KL best among projections) are supported on the tested instances.
- However, the guarantees hinge on the spectral norm bound. In practice the paper relaxes the threshold (uses 2.5 instead of 1.0) to improve approximation quality, which undermines the fast-mixing guarantee; this requires clearer justification and diagnostics.
- Runtime and convergence details for the nested optimization (outer projected gradient, inner Euclidean projection via L-BFGS-B, and inner-inner sampling for reverse KL) need to be made precise for reproducibility and fair comparison.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, with good motivation and references. The algorithmic sections are dense but readable. A few details and figures require clarification (see questions below).
- The piecewise KL construction and choice of subgraphs are explained and easy to reproduce.

Originality
- The core idea—projecting onto a fast-mixing parameter set defined by spectral constraints while preserving the original graph—is new to my knowledge and distinct from prior NIPS work on trees, TRW, planar approximations, and structured mean-field.
- The dual Euclidean projection with sparsity mask is a nice technical contribution.

Significance
- As a concept, the fast-mixing family provides a promising middle ground between exact but structurally limited approaches and purely heuristic MCMC. If broadly scalable, this could influence practical inference under tight time budgets.
- Current experiments are small; scalability and end-to-end efficiency remain open. The looseness of the spectral bound may limit practical utility unless better bounds or adaptive thresholds are developed.

Strengths
- Clear and novel framing of “tractability” via fast mixing, with a concrete spectral-norm sufficient condition.
- Projection preserves the original graph structure.
- Solid algorithmic toolkit: dual Euclidean projection (L-BFGS-B), projected-gradient for other divergences, and stochastic reverse-KL with sampling under ψ.
- Consistent empirical gains over LBP/TRW/MF; reverse-KL projection is particularly strong.
- Honest discussion of bound looseness and practical tradeoffs.

Weaknesses and questions for the authors
- Stopping criteria: Section 5.1’s projected-gradient has nested loops where each projection step uses L-BFGS-B. Please specify (a) the stopping criteria and tolerances for the inner L-BFGS-B, (b) max iterations per projection, and (c) how inaccuracies in the inner solve affect convergence guarantees of the outer loop.
- Runtime reporting: Figure 2’s x-axis labeled “number of samples” is unclear, since sampling is used only inside the reverse-KL projection. It likely reflects wall-clock runtime; please relabel and report absolute times (seconds) including projection cost. For fairness, also report runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF.
- Mixing guarantees vs practice: You use a spectral-norm bound of 2.5, which voids the theoretical fast-mixing guarantee. How often did ψ fail to mix rapidly in practice (e.g., via autocorrelation or coupling diagnostics)? Can you show accuracy/runtime as a function of the bound c, including c≤1.0?
- Scalability: What is the computational complexity per outer iteration (SVDs, mask operations, gradient evaluations, sampling), and empirical scaling with n and edge density? Please include results on larger models to assess practicality.
- Reverse-KL gradient estimation: Did you try using independent sample pools for the two terms in Eq. 8 to reduce bias/variance? Any effect on performance?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments on larger instances, e.g., tractable random-field benchmarks such as those released by Boris Flach, to stress-test scalability and accuracy/runtime trade-offs.
- Include end-to-end wall-clock comparisons at matched accuracy targets between: (a) Gibbs on original model, (b) reverse-KL projection + Gibbs under ψ, and (c) LBP/TRW/MF.
- Explore adaptive spectral thresholds or tighter mixing conditions to reduce conservativeness while preserving guarantees where possible.

Minor comments
- Line 222: replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T.”
- Line 226: use “subgradient” instead of “derivative.”
- Clarify whether the projection preserves the sign of couplings (you constrain |B| via D=|B|); a brief note would help.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel and well-motivated idea; technically solid core method.
- Stronger accuracy than standard variational baselines.
- Reverse-KL projection is practical and effective; preserves graph structure.

Cons:
- Limited scalability evidence; experiments mostly small.
- Runtime accounting and stopping criteria unclear; fairness of comparisons needs tightening.
- Reliance on loosened spectral bound undermines formal guarantees.

Overall assessment
An interesting, well-executed paper with convincing small-scale empirical results and a fresh perspective on tractability via mixing-time constraints. Practical impact hinges on runtime/scalability and clearer accounting of guarantees when relaxing the bound. With clarifications and stronger runtime evaluation in the rebuttal, I lean toward a weak accept.
