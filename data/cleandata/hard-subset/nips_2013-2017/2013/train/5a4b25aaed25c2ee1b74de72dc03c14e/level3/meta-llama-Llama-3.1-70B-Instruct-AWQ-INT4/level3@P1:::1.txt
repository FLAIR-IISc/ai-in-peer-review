This paper proposes an innovative approach to speed up Gibbs mixing in strongly coupled pairwise Ising models by projecting parameters onto a nearby "fast-mixing" model that satisfies spectral bounds. The authors develop several projection methods that find parameters close to the original but weak enough to ensure rapid mixing. The experimental results on small Ising models show regimes where projected models give better marginal estimates within fixed computation time than variational methods and original-model Gibbs.

The technical development in this paper builds heavily on prior rapid-mixing results [4,8], though projecting onto the nearest rapidly-mixing model as an inference tool appears novel. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling and the need for approximations. They also provide a thorough review of related work in the field, including mean-field inference, structured mean-field, loopy belief propagation, and tree-reweighted belief propagation.

The main contribution of this paper is the development of a new notion of "tractability" that insists not that a graph has a fast algorithm for exact inference, but only that it obeys parameter-space conditions ensuring that Gibbs sampling will converge rapidly to the stationary distribution. The authors show that for Ising models, a sufficient condition for rapid-mixing is the spectral norm of pairwise weight matrix is less than 1.0.

The authors propose four different projection methods: Euclidean distance, zero-avoiding KL-divergence, piecewise KL-divergence, and zero-forcing KL-divergence. They provide a detailed derivation of the projection algorithms and show that they can be used to find parameters close to the original but weak enough to ensure rapid mixing.

The experimental results show that the proposed methods are more accurate than variational approximations and can provide better marginal estimates within fixed computation time. However, there are some concerns about the scalability of the approach to larger models, as the mixing-time comparisons may not generalize with problem size. Additionally, the computational cost of the projection step appears high and is not accounted for in timing comparisons, which may bias the results in favor of the proposed method.

One of the main concerns about this paper is the unclear generalizability beyond binary states, as convergence bounds are harder for non-binary variables. The authors should discuss this limitation and provide some insights into how the approach can be extended to more general cases.

The paper has some clarity issues, such as the use of "standard results" without references or proof sketches. For example, Theorem 6 and Lemma 5 are stated without proof or reference. The notation in Section 4 is also unclear, and the introduction of Theorem 4 and the notation (g, M, Lambda) requires reorganization and explanation.

The dependency matrix R needs more discussion, as its maximization makes tractability unclear. The authors should state and address this issue.

The experimental flaws, such as the omission of projection computation from Figure 2 timings, should be clarified. The results must include projection time or clarify this point.

The experiments lack larger/realistic models, and the authors should consider alternative models, such as symmetric models with tractable marginals or using long-chain sampling as ground truth.

The paper is missing an exploration of KL(θ||ψ) projection as a potential "best case," which could be used on toy models.

The mean-field is noted as a reverse-KL special case, but the observed large error gap versus reverse-KL projection is unexplained and needs discussion.

Overall, this paper proposes an elegant idea leveraging recent mixing bounds, but the experimental comparisons and limited applicability raise significant concerns.
