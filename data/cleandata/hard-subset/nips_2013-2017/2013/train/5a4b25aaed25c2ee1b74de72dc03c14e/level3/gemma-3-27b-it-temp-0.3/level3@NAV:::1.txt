## Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

This paper proposes a novel approach to accelerate Gibbs sampling in strongly coupled pairwise Ising models by projecting model parameters onto a nearby “fast-mixing” model, guided by spectral bounds on the dependency matrix. The core idea – leveraging recent rapid-mixing results to improve inference – is elegant and potentially impactful. The authors explore several projection methods, including Euclidean projection and KL-divergence based approaches, and demonstrate through experiments on small Ising models that these projected models can yield more accurate marginal estimates within a fixed computational budget compared to standard Gibbs sampling and variational methods.

The work builds heavily on established results in Markov chain mixing times [4, 8], and the concept of projecting onto a rapidly-mixing model appears to be a genuinely novel contribution. The exploration of different divergence measures for projection is also a strength, providing a nuanced investigation of the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost. The experimental setup, while limited to small models, provides a reasonable initial validation of the proposed methods.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The most significant concern is the scalability of the proposed approach. The experiments are performed on relatively small models, and it remains unclear whether the observed gains will generalize to larger, more realistic scenarios. The computational cost of the projection step itself is not accounted for in the timing comparisons, which biases the results in favor of the proposed method. This omission is critical and needs to be addressed. 

Furthermore, the generalizability beyond binary states is questionable, as convergence bounds are more challenging to establish for non-binary variables. Clarity issues also detract from the paper's impact. Several “standard results” are stated without sufficient references or even proof sketches (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5), hindering reproducibility and understanding. The dependency matrix *R* and its maximization require further discussion regarding tractability. Section 4, in particular, is poorly organized and difficult to follow, with unclear notation (g, M, Lambda) and a poorly explained Theorem 4. A notational inconsistency in Section 1 regarding KL-divergence usage should also be corrected.

Finally, the experiments lack exploration of larger/realistic models, and the potential of KL(θ||ψ) projection is not fully investigated. The large error gap observed between mean-field and reverse-KL projection requires a more thorough explanation.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel idea leveraging recent mixing bounds, potential for accelerating inference in challenging models, and a well-defined problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Scalability concerns, missing computational cost analysis, clarity issues, limited experimental validation, and questionable generalizability.

**Overall:** While the paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable idea, the significant concerns regarding scalability, computational cost, and clarity prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address the identified weaknesses and resubmit in the future.
