This paper proposes a novel approach to speed up Gibbs mixing in strongly coupled pairwise Ising models by projecting parameters onto a nearby "fast-mixing" model that satisfies spectral bounds. The authors introduce several projection methods that find parameters close to the original but weak enough to ensure rapid mixing. The experimental results on small Ising models show regimes where projected models give better marginal estimates within fixed computation time than variational methods and original-model Gibbs.

The paper builds heavily on prior rapid-mixing results [4,8], though projecting onto the nearest rapidly-mixing model as an inference tool appears novel. The technical development is sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the projection methods and their relationship to existing work. However, there are some concerns regarding the clarity and completeness of the presentation.

One major concern is the unclear generalizability of the approach to larger models. While the experimental results are promising, it is unclear whether the gains in marginal estimate accuracy will scale to larger models. The mixing-time comparisons may not generalize with problem size, and the authors should provide more discussion on this point.

Another concern is the computational cost of the projection step, which appears high and is not accounted for in the timing comparisons. This biases the results in favor of the proposed method, and the authors should provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the projection step.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from more discussion on the generalizability of the approach beyond binary states. The convergence bounds are harder for non-binary variables, and the authors should provide more explanation on how the approach can be extended to these cases.

There are also some clarity issues with the presentation. Several "standard results" are stated without references or proof sketches (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5 missing/partial proof). The notation in Section 4 is unclear, and the introduction of g, M, and Lambda requires reorganization and explanation.

Furthermore, the dependency matrix R needs more discussion. Its maximization makes tractability unclear, and the paper should state and address this point.

The experimental evaluation is limited to small Ising models, and the authors should consider including larger or more realistic models. Suggested alternatives include symmetric models with tractable marginals or using long-chain sampling as ground truth.

Finally, the paper lacks an exploration of KL(θ||ψ) projection as a potential "best case" (junction tree could be used on toy models). The mean-field is noted as a reverse-KL special case, but the observed large error gap versus reverse-KL projection is unexplained and needs discussion.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to speed up Gibbs mixing in strongly coupled pairwise Ising models. The idea of projecting parameters onto a nearby "fast-mixing" model that satisfies spectral bounds is elegant and leverages recent mixing bounds.

In terms of significance, the paper has the potential to contribute to the development of more efficient inference algorithms for graphical models. However, the limited experimental evaluation and unclear generalizability to larger models reduce the significance of the paper.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written, and the technical development is sound. However, the clarity issues and lack of discussion on some important points reduce the overall quality of the paper.

Arguments for acceptance:

* Novel approach to speed up Gibbs mixing in strongly coupled pairwise Ising models
* Sound technical development
* Potential to contribute to the development of more efficient inference algorithms for graphical models

Arguments against acceptance:

* Unclear generalizability to larger models
* Computational cost of the projection step is not accounted for in the timing comparisons
* Limited experimental evaluation
* Clarity issues with the presentation
* Lack of discussion on some important points

Recommendation: The paper has some interesting ideas, but it needs more work to address the concerns mentioned above. I would recommend a major revision to address these issues before considering acceptance.
