- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set (interaction matrix spectral norm < 1) to enable rapid Gibbs convergence.  
- Develops projection methods under four divergences: Euclidean, inclusive KL (KL(θ||ψ)), piecewise KL on tractable subgraphs, and exclusive KL (KL(ψ||θ)).  
- Derives a dual optimization algorithm for the Euclidean projection.  
- Uses projected stochastic gradient descent for KL-based projections.  
- Empirically compares marginals from Gibbs on projected parameters to Gibbs on original parameters and variational methods (Mean Field, LBP, TRW).  
- Strength: Highly original idea—defining tractability by mixing properties rather than graph structure.  
- Strength: Solid grounding in MCMC mixing-time theory and sound technical development of projection algorithms.  
- Weakness: Exposition is unclear and hampers reproducibility, especially the dual algorithm (Theorem 7) and its dual variables.  
- Weakness: Unclear interaction between the constraint ZijBij = 0, the projection D = |B|, and whether the parameter space is preserved or shrunk.  
- Weakness: Ambiguous description of the KL minimization procedure (whether projection is fully optimized per step or interleaved), and missing algorithm pseudocode.  
- Weakness: Convergence claims for projected stochastic gradient descent are hand-wavy with insufficient connection to cited ergodic mirror descent theory.  
- Experimental weakness: Comparisons use marginal accuracy after fixed Gibbs iterations but ignore projection computational cost, making evaluations unfair.  
- Experimental weakness: Missing important MCMC baselines that improve mixing (e.g., block Gibbs, Swendsen–Wang, lifted samplers).  
- Experimental weakness: Results averaged over 50 trials lack error bars, preventing assessment of statistical significance.  
- Experimental weakness: Practical experiments use spectral norm bound 2.5 instead of the theoretically justified 1.0, creating a theory–practice gap that is unexplained.  
- Argument for acceptance: Novel, conceptually appealing approach that could open new research directions by combining deterministic projection and stochastic sampling.  
- Argument for rejection: Significant clarity and experimental rigor issues (poorly explained methods, missing baselines, no timing or error bars, and unexplained deviation from theoretical bounds).
