Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a new notion of tractable approximation for inference in high-treewidth Ising models: instead of projecting onto distributions that admit exact inference (e.g., trees, low-treewidth graphs), it projects the original parameters onto a set guaranteed to be fast mixing for univariate Gibbs sampling. The sufficient condition used is that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix is < 1; for Ising, this is bounded by the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute edge strengths |β| (via tanh bounds on Rij), following mixing-time results of Dyer et al. and Hayes. This contrasts with classic tractable approximations such as mean-field [Jordan et al., Xing et al.], structured mean-field, LBP, TRW [Wainwright et al.], and planar/low-treewidth decompositions [Globerson & Jaakkola; Saul & Jordan; Sutton & McCallum]. The authors formulate “projection” under several divergences: Euclidean distance on parameters; KL(θ||ψ) (“inclusive”, zero-avoiding); a piecewise KL formed by exact KL computed on a set of tractable subgraphs; and KL(ψ||θ) (“exclusive”, zero-forcing). The Euclidean projection is solved exactly while preserving the original graph sparsity via a dual formulation (Theorem 7) that repeatedly thresholds singular values with L-BFGS-B. Other divergences are handled by projected (stochastic) gradient steps with Euclidean projection onto the fast-mixing set.

Empirically, on 8×8 grids and small random graphs, the projected fast-mixing models yield more accurate marginals than MF, LBP, and TRW, and—under finite time budgets—often outperform Gibbs on the original (potentially slow-mixing) parameters. Among the proposed divergences, minimizing KL(ψ||θ) performs best; KL(θ||ψ) is impractical except on tiny graphs. The piecewise KL performs in between but is easy to evaluate.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The mixing guarantee relies on ||R||2 < 1 and the bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij| for Ising, which is standard. The Euclidean projection onto the fast-mixing set with sparsity constraints is a solid contribution: the dual derivation and use of singular value thresholding within an L-BFGS-B loop are well motivated, and the algorithm preserves graph structure. The projected-gradient approach for other divergences is reasonable, and the stochastic approximation for KL(ψ||θ) is carefully described, including the estimator in Eq. 8 and discussion of bias/variance.
- Caveat: the experiments sometimes use a spectral norm bound of 2.5 (vs. the theoretical < 1), which forfeits the mixing guarantee; please quantify how often the resulting models fail to be fast mixing and how sensitive accuracy/runtime are to this bound.
- Another caveat: Theorem 4 is stated for random-scan Gibbs, but experiments use systematic scan. While you note similar results exist, please clarify the conditions and whether the chosen bound remains valid for your update schedule.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized. The relationship to prior inference methods is clear, and the algorithmic components are described with sufficient detail to reproduce the Euclidean projection. A few clarity issues:
- Section 5.1 projected-gradient has an inner Euclidean projection solved by L-BFGS-B. Please specify stopping criteria, tolerances, and how you warm-start across iterations.
- Figure 2: the x-axis label “number of samples” is confusing because sampling is only used during optimization for KL(ψ||θ). If the figure reflects end-to-end computational budgets, please report absolute running times (in seconds) on a fixed hardware setup and include LBP/TRW/MF run times for a fair comparison.
- Minor edits: line 222 “onto the tree” → “onto graph T”; line 226 “derivative” → “subgradient” when nonsmooth max over T is involved.
- Notation: explicitly define Z and the Hadamard product early in Section 4; define the “triple dot product” before Theorem 7 (you do, but a brief reminder linking to Hadamard products would help).

Originality
Projecting onto a fast-mixing class defined by spectral norm constraints and preserving sparsity is a novel and interesting idea, distinct from classical variational approximations that enforce tractable exact inference. The dual Euclidean projection that preserves the graph structure is technically original and useful. Using KL(ψ||θ) under a fast-mixing constraint can be seen as a principled generalization of mean-field (R = 0) to a much larger family with sampling-based gradients.

Significance
The approach provides a new tool in the inference toolbox: when exact inference is impossible and plain Gibbs is slow, one can “tame” the model to be fast mixing and then sample accurately within a time budget. The empirical gains over MF/LBP/TRW are promising. The potential impact would be strengthened by demonstrations on larger graphs and by more detailed cost–accuracy tradeoffs that include the (sometimes substantial) optimization overhead during projection. The idea may also influence learning methods that rely on MCMC, as noted by the authors.

Strengths
- Clear, principled definition of a fast-mixing tractable family; rigorous sufficient conditions anchored in mixing-time theory.
- Practical and elegant Euclidean projection that enforces graph sparsity and spectral constraints via a dual formulation.
- Stochastic projected-gradient scheme for KL(ψ||θ) with a simple reusable sample pool; connections to Ergodic Mirror Descent.
- Consistent empirical improvements over MF/LBP/TRW; compelling finite-time advantage over Gibbs on original parameters.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Mixing bound looseness: you often use a spectral norm bound of 2.5. Please:
  - Report empirical mixing diagnostics (e.g., spectral gap proxies, autocorrelation times) to show chains remain fast mixing.
  - Provide sensitivity plots vs the bound c ∈ {0.8, 1.0, 1.5, 2.5}.
- Optimization details: specify L-BFGS-B stopping criteria, maximum iterations, and convergence behavior; report total projection time and memory.
- Runtime reporting: provide absolute run times (optimization + sampling) with error bars, and include LBP/TRW/MF times on the same hardware.
- Estimator bias: Eq. 8 uses the same pool both to estimate µ̂ and ĝ. Please quantify the effect (e.g., ablation using independent pools or larger pool sizes).
- Update schedule: clarify implications of using systematic scan vs random scan for the guarantees and in practice.
- Scalability: include experiments on larger graphs (e.g., larger grids or standard tractable random-field benchmarks) to demonstrate scaling of both optimization and sampling.

Pro acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated formulation; technically solid Euclidean projection.
- Demonstrated accuracy gains over standard variational baselines.
- Generalization of mean-field via fast-mixing constraints is conceptually appealing.

Con acceptance
- Guarantees not strictly respected in key experiments (c = 2.5); practical impact depends on runtime and mixing behavior that are not fully quantified.
- Limited scale of experiments; unclear scalability and total cost compared to strong baselines when accounting for projection overhead.
- Some clarity gaps in optimization details and runtime reporting.

Overall assessment
An interesting and timely contribution that brings mixing-time theory into approximate inference by projection. The empirical results are convincing on small to medium graphs, and the methodology is potentially impactful. I lean positive, contingent on clarifications about runtime, stopping criteria, and sensitivity to the spectral bound, and with a request to report absolute times and, if possible, add a larger-scale experiment.
