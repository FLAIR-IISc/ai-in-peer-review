This paper proposes to approximate intractable Ising models by projecting their parameters onto a subset that guarantees rapid mixing of single-site Gibbs sampling. The fast-mixing set is defined via a sufficient condition on the dependency matrix: if the spectral norm of the absolute pairwise interaction matrix is < 1 (building on Hayes; Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum), then single-site Gibbs mixes rapidly. The authors study four projection objectives: Euclidean distance in parameter space; “inclusive” KL(q||p); a tractable piecewise KL built from low-treewidth subgraphs; and the “exclusive” KL(p||q) with p the fast-mixing model, optimized by stochastic approximation with a sample pool. They derive a dual formulation that enables Euclidean projection while preserving sparsity and enforcing the spectral-norm constraint (via iterative singular-value thresholding in a constrained form). Experiments on grids and random graphs suggest that sampling from the projected, guaranteed-fast-mixing approximations yields more accurate marginals within a fixed (limited) sampling budget than sampling from the original parameters; and that KL(p||q) projections outperform piecewise KL and Euclidean projections.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself among tractable-family approximations (mean field, structured mean field, LBP/TRW, planar decomposition) but replaces structural tractability with dynamical tractability (rapid mixing). The mixing-time criterion relates to Dobrushin-type conditions, but uses a tighter spectral norm bound (||R||2 < 1) rather than ||R||1. It also connects to mean-field as a limiting case (zero dependency norm), and to learning with MCMC-based gradients via Ergodic Mirror Descent.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The use of spectral-norm-based sufficient conditions for fast mixing is well-grounded. The Euclidean projection via a dual using LBFGS-B appears reasonable, and the stochastic approximation for KL(p||q) follows standard practice. However, several methodological details are unclear:
  - The derivation and interpretation of the dual for Eq. (1) are hard to follow. In particular, the roles of M and Λ, the projection Πc[·], and how sparsity is preserved could use a clearer, step-by-step explanation and algorithmic summary.
  - The constraint Z ∘ D = 0 is intended to enforce graph sparsity. Since D = |B|, this indeed forces B_ij = 0 on non-edges. Please clarify explicitly that this preserves the original edge set and does not shrink the parameter space beyond enforcing sparsity and the spectral-norm bound.
  - The overall optimization procedure is ambiguous. For KL(p||q), do you interleave gradient steps (using samples) with Euclidean projections at every iteration, or fully solve the Euclidean projection subproblem only occasionally? A concise algorithm box with inputs, steps, and stopping criteria would greatly help.
  - Convergence guarantees: You cite Ergodic Mirror Descent to argue convergence of the stochastic procedure. Please specify the assumptions (step-size, number of MCMC steps per iteration, boundedness) under which your projected scheme converges, and whether the projection step preserves the conditions needed by the EMD analysis.
  - Use of a spectral-norm bound of 2.5 in experiments contradicts the stated sufficient condition (< 1). This removes the formal mixing guarantee. Either use a bound within the theorem, or empirically substantiate mixing (e.g., by effective sample sizes/autocorrelations, coupling times, or bounds on R-hat) and analyze sensitivity to the bound.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written, but key pieces are underspecified. The dual derivation (Theorem 7) should be expanded with intuition, constraints, and a brief proof sketch in the main text. Define all notation locally (e.g., triple dot product) and dimensions of variables.
- Provide an explicit algorithmic description for each projection objective, including the sampling/projection interleaving schedule, sample-pool maintenance, and stopping criteria.
- Discuss computational complexity per iteration and overall.

Originality:
- The framing of “fast mixing” as a tractable family to project onto, and the use of spectral-norm constraints with Euclidean/KL projections, is novel and interesting. The piecewise KL is a pragmatic idea, though related to earlier piecewise likelihood and tree mixtures.

Significance:
- If validated, the approach could provide a practical bridge between MCMC and variational approximations: when exact mixing is slow, replace the model by a nearby fast-mixing one and sample efficiently. However, the current empirical evidence is not yet convincing enough to assess practical impact, due to evaluation design and missing baselines.

Experimental evaluation:
- Comparisons are iteration-based (e.g., “30,000 Gibbs iterations”) and do not account for projection overhead, which can be substantial (e.g., repeated SVDs and LBFGS-B solves; 30k Gibbs steps used during optimization). Provide wall-clock time or flop-normalized budgets and report total time to a target error.
- Add error bars/confidence intervals across 50 trials and conduct statistical tests; current plots without uncertainty hinder interpretation.
- Provide mixing diagnostics for the projected models and for sampling on original parameters (ESS per second, integrated autocorrelation times, R-hat).
- The choice of 30k iterations is arbitrary; include time–accuracy curves and budget sweeps.
- Include stronger baselines: blocked Gibbs, Swendsen–Wang, and, where applicable, lifted Gibbs (e.g., Gogate) or tree sampling. Even if your guarantees are for single-site Gibbs, practitioners might use block/cluster updates; comparisons would contextualize the practical value.
- Analyze sensitivity to sample-pool size, step size, number of iterations, and the spectral-norm bound c. Report ablations for attractive vs mixed interactions and for grid vs random graphs.
- Clarify whether node fields are projected or left unconstrained, and their effect on accuracy.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually appealing and novel use of fast-mixing conditions as a tractable family.
- Solid theoretical underpinnings for the spectral-norm condition; interesting dual projection method preserving sparsity.
- A practical stochastic optimization approach for KL(p||q), connecting to mean-field in a principled way.

Cons for acceptance:
- Methodological ambiguities (dual derivation, algorithmic procedure, convergence claims).
- Empirical evaluation lacks fair time accounting, uncertainty quantification, and relevant MCMC baselines; reliance on a spectral-norm threshold that breaks guarantees.
- Limited analysis of when piecewise KL helps; unclear practical guidance for choosing divergences and parameters.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Provide an algorithm box for each projection objective, including initialization, sampling/projection interleaving, stopping, and complexity.
- Tighten and clarify the dual derivation; add intuition and constraints mapping (how M and Λ enforce |B| sparsity and spectral norm).
- Either respect the theoretical bound (< 1) in experiments or justify larger c with mixing diagnostics and error/mixing trade-offs.
- Add runtime-based comparisons and error bars; include blocked/cluster Gibbs and tree sampling baselines.
- Discuss how to extend to general MRFs: dependency bounds, projection algorithms, and whether similar duals exist.
- Explore adaptive schedules for step sizes and number of MCMC steps, aligned with EMD conditions, and report convergence behavior.

Overall: promising idea with potential impact, but the paper needs clearer method development and substantially stronger, fairer empirical evidence to support its practical value.
