This paper proposes a novel approach to speeding up Gibbs mixing in strongly coupled pairwise Ising models by projecting parameters onto a nearby "fast-mixing" model that satisfies spectral bounds. The authors develop several projection methods that find parameters close to the original but weak enough to ensure rapid mixing. The technical development builds heavily on prior rapid-mixing results, although projecting onto the nearest rapidly-mixing model as an inference tool appears novel.

The main idea of the paper is to use the dependency matrix R, which measures how much the conditional distribution of a variable can vary when the values of its neighbors are fixed. The authors show that if the spectral norm of R is less than one, the mixing time of Gibbs sampling is bounded. They then propose several projection methods to find a set of parameters that is close to the original but has a spectral norm less than one.

The authors evaluate their methods on small Ising models and show that they can give better marginal estimates within a fixed computation time than variational methods and original-model Gibbs. However, the experiments are limited to small models, and it is unclear whether the gains will scale to larger models. Moreover, the computational cost of the projection step appears high and is not accounted for in the timing comparisons, which biases the results in favor of the proposed method.

The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. However, the authors could be more careful in evaluating the strengths and weaknesses of their work. For example, they mention that the spectral norm bound is too conservative but do not provide a clear explanation of why this is the case.

The paper is clearly written, and the organization is good. However, some sections, such as Section 4, are hard to follow, and the notation could be improved. The authors use the "triple dot product" notation, which is not commonly used in the literature. Additionally, the dependency matrix R needs more discussion, and its maximization makes tractability unclear.

The originality of the paper is high, as it proposes a novel approach to speeding up Gibbs mixing. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their work differs from previous contributions and adequately reference related work.

The significance of the paper is also high, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The results are important, and other researchers are likely to use these ideas or build on them. However, the authors could provide more discussion on the implications of their results and how they can be applied in practice.

In terms of quality, the paper is good, but there are some issues that need to be addressed. For example, the authors mention that the piecewise KL-divergence does not perform as well as the true KL-divergence but do not provide a clear explanation of why this is the case. Additionally, the mean-field is noted as a reverse-KL special case, but the observed large error gap versus reverse-KL projection is unexplained and needs discussion.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the ideas are novel and significant. However, there are some issues that need to be addressed, such as the scalability of the method, the computational cost of the projection step, and the clarity of some sections.
