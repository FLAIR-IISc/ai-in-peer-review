- Project idea: project an arbitrary Ising model onto the set of fast-mixing models (spectral norm of absolute interaction matrix below a threshold) while preserving graph sparsity.  
- Divergences: evaluate four projection divergences—Euclidean, KL(θ||ψ), piecewise KL over tractable subgraphs, and reversed KL(ψ||θ).  
- Optimization: Euclidean solved via a dual formulation with L-BFGS-B; other divergences via projected-gradient where Euclidean projection is used in each step.  
- Reversed KL: requires estimating gradients by Gibbs sampling on the projected (fast-mixing) model, making stochastic optimization feasible.  
- Empirical result: reversed KL projection yields the most accurate marginals and projected-model Gibbs often outperforms original-model Gibbs under fixed time budgets on small graphs/grids.  
- Strength: highly original framing—using fast-mixing properties rather than structural simplicity to define a tractable approximating family.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance across baselines (LBP, TRW, MF) and graph types tested.  
- Strength: technically solid contributions (dual Euclidean projection, principled stochastic formulation for reversed KL).  
- Weakness/Question: projected-gradient description lacks detail on inner L-BFGS-B stopping criteria—are inner solves run to convergence or for fixed iterations?  
- Weakness/Question: Figure 2’s x-axis labeled "number of samples" is ambiguous—clarify what it denotes for non-sampling methods like Euclidean projection, LBP, and TRW.  
- Weakness/Request: report absolute wall-clock times (including projection optimization and baseline convergence times) to enable fair efficiency-accuracy comparisons.  
- Weakness/Scalability: experiments are small (10-node, 8×8 grids); SVDs and iterative L-BFGS-B may not scale—ask for experiments on larger/benchmark models to assess scalability.  
- Minor edit: change "projection θ(T) onto the tree" to "projection θ(T) onto the graph T" for clarity.  
- Minor edit: piecewise KL objective is a max over functions and non-differentiable—use "subgradient" instead of "derivative."  
- Overall: paper is well-written, original, and empirically convincing but needs clarification of runtime/algorithmic details and stronger evidence of scalability; recommend acceptance contingent on satisfactory author response.
