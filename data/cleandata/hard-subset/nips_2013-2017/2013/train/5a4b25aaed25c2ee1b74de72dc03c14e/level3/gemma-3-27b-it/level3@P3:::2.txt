This paper proposes a novel approach to accelerating Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting model parameters onto a space guaranteed to satisfy the Dobrushin criterion for fast mixing. The core idea – combining stochastic and deterministic inference to improve sampling efficiency – is interesting and addresses a significant challenge in probabilistic modeling. The authors explore several divergence measures for this projection, including Euclidean distance, piecewise KL divergence, and a reversed KL divergence minimized via stochastic approximation.

The paper builds upon existing work in approximating intractable distributions, drawing parallels to mean-field inference and tree-based approximations. It correctly identifies the limitations of standard Gibbs sampling with strong interactions and seeks a principled way to overcome them. The connection to existing results on mixing times, particularly Theorem 4, provides a solid theoretical foundation.

However, despite the promise of the idea, several weaknesses hinder the paper's overall impact. Firstly, the description of the dual problem derived in Theorem 7 is unclear. The motivation behind introducing matrices *M* and *Λ* is not sufficiently explained, and the connection to the original minimization problem feels obscured rather than clarified. 

A critical concern is the ambiguity regarding whether the projected parameter space is indeed equivalent to the original. The authors constrain *z<sub>ij</sub>d<sub>ij</sub> = 0*, aiming to preserve the graph structure, but it's not convincingly demonstrated that this constraint doesn't inadvertently reduce the space of accessible parameters. Could this constraint lead to a suboptimal solution compared to unconstrained optimization? This point requires further clarification.

Furthermore, the overall procedure lacks clarity. Is the projection fully optimized before sampling begins, or are projection updates interleaved with Gibbs sampling iterations? This detail is crucial for understanding the practical implementation and computational cost. An algorithm box outlining the complete procedure, including initialization, projection steps, and sampling details, is essential.

The convergence of the projected gradient descent strategy is also not addressed. While the authors mention Ergodic Mirror Descent, a formal justification for convergence, given the specific projection and divergence measures, is missing.

The experimental evaluation is particularly unconvincing. The choice of 30,000 Gibbs iterations as a benchmark appears arbitrary. Comparing iteration counts directly against deterministic methods (LBP, TRW, MF) is unfair, as these methods have vastly different computational costs per iteration. A more appropriate comparison would involve wall-clock time or overall computational effort.

Critically, the paper lacks timing comparisons. The runtime of the projection step is completely unaccounted for, rendering it impossible to assess the practical benefit of the proposed approach. A significant projection time could negate any gains from faster Gibbs mixing.

The plots presented lack error bars, making it impossible to determine the statistical significance of the reported results. Differences in accuracy might be due to random fluctuations rather than genuine improvements.

Finally, the paper fails to compare against established Gibbs sampling techniques like blocked Gibbs sampling or Gogate's Lifted Gibbs Sampler, which are often more efficient than naive univariate Gibbs sampling.

In summary, this paper presents a promising idea for accelerating Gibbs sampling, but suffers from a lack of clarity, incomplete theoretical justification, and inadequate experimental validation. The proposed method needs clearer development and a more rigorous and fairer experimental evaluation to demonstrate its practical value.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel idea for accelerating Gibbs sampling.
*   Connection to well-established results on mixing times.
*   Exploration of different divergence measures.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Unclear dual problem description.
*   Ambiguity regarding parameter space equivalence.
*   Lack of clarity in the overall procedure.
*   Missing convergence guarantees for projected gradient descent.
*   Unconvincing and incomplete experimental evaluation (arbitrary iteration count, missing timing, lack of error bars, missing baselines).



**Overall Assessment:** Reject with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The core idea has merit, but the paper requires substantial improvements in clarity, theoretical rigor, and experimental validation before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field.
