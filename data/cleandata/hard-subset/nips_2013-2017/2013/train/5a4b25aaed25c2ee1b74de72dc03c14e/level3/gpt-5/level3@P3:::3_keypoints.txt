- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set (spectral-norm bound) that preserves graph structure, then running Gibbs on the projected model for guaranteed rapid mixing (under the bound).
- Studies four projection objectives: Euclidean, inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ), piecewise KL on tractable subgraphs, and reverse KL KL(ψ||θ), with reverse KL performing best empirically.
- Euclidean projection solved via a dual formulation with repeated singular value thresholding and L-BFGS-B; other divergences optimized by projected gradient with Euclidean projection steps.
- Reverse-KL requires sampling under ψ inside the projection loop, leveraging the fast-mixing property for gradient estimation.
- Experiments on small graphs (n=10 and 8×8 grids) compare marginals to exact junction-tree results and baseline methods (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs); projections consistently outperform LBP/TRW/MF and often beat Gibbs on original parameters for reasonable time budgets.
- Technical development is sound: clear dual derivation, appropriate projected-gradient framework, and careful discussion of the reverse-KL gradient estimator.
- Empirical claims are supported on tested instances, with reverse-KL projection being particularly strong.
- Theoretical guarantees depend on the spectral-norm bound, but the paper relaxes this bound (uses 2.5 instead of 1.0) which undermines the fast-mixing guarantee and needs justification.
- Nested optimization and runtime details (outer projected gradient, inner L-BFGS-B, inner-inner sampling) are insufficiently specified for reproducibility and fair comparison.
- Paper is generally well written and motivated; algorithmic sections are dense but readable; piecewise KL construction is clear and reproducible.
- The core idea of using fast-mixing spectral constraints as a tractable family is novel and distinct from tree/planar/structured mean-field approaches.
- The dual Euclidean projection with sparsity mask is a notable technical contribution.
- Conceptually significant as a middle ground between structurally-limited exact methods and heuristic MCMC if scalable.
- Current experiments are small; scalability and end-to-end efficiency remain open concerns, and the loosened spectral bound may limit practical utility.
- Strengths: novel fast-mixing framing, graph-structure preservation, solid algorithmic toolkit, consistent empirical gains, and honest discussion of bound looseness.
- Key weaknesses/questions: unspecified stopping criteria and tolerances for inner L-BFGS-B, max iterations per projection, and effect of inner-solve inaccuracies on outer-loop convergence.
- Key weaknesses/questions: unclear runtime reporting (Figure 2 x-axis), need absolute wall-clock times including projection cost and comparable runtimes for baselines.
- Key weaknesses/questions: use of spectral-norm 2.5 undermines mixing guarantees—provide diagnostics on ψ mixing behavior and accuracy/runtime vs bound c (including c≤1.0).
- Key weaknesses/questions: scalability—provide computational complexity per outer iteration (SVDs, masking, gradients, sampling) and empirical scaling with n and edge density plus larger-model experiments.
- Key weaknesses/questions: reverse-KL gradient estimation—did authors try independent sample pools for variance/bias reduction and what was the effect?
- Suggested improvements: add larger-instance experiments (e.g., public random-field benchmarks), end-to-end wall-clock comparisons at matched accuracy targets, and explore adaptive or tighter mixing thresholds.
- Minor comments: wording edits (Line 222, Line 226) and clarify whether projection preserves coupling signs when constraining |B| via D=|B|.
- Pros for acceptance: novel, well-motivated, technically solid, empirically stronger than standard variational baselines, and preserves graph structure.
- Cons for acceptance: limited scalability evidence, unclear runtime accounting and stopping criteria, and reliance on a loosened spectral bound that weakens formal guarantees.
- Overall assessment: interesting, well-executed paper with convincing small-scale results and a fresh perspective; practical impact depends on clearer runtime/scalability evaluation and justification when relaxing the spectral bound—leaning toward weak accept.
