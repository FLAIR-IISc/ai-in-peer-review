- Introduces projection of Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set defined by spectral norm constraints to enable efficient Gibbs sampling.
- Explores four divergences for projection: Euclidean, zero-avoiding KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL.
- Provides a novel dual optimization result (Theorem 7) for Euclidean projection with theoretical guarantees.
- Develops practical algorithms for non-Euclidean divergences that use Gibbs sampling inside the projection loop.
- Experimental design is rigorous, comparing to LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs across graph types and interaction strengths.
- Empirical results show consistent improvements in marginal accuracy over standard variational methods and original Gibbs for given budgets.
- Spectral norm bound used is conservative (≈1.76 factor vs known Ising threshold) and its practical impact needs clarification.
- Computational cost—especially for reversed KL projection—is high and lacks sufficient runtime analysis.
- Clarity is generally good, with concise background on mixing times and clear experiment descriptions.
- Algorithmic details need clarification: stopping criteria for inner L-BFGS-B within projected gradient descent are not specified.
- Figure 2's x-axis label "number of samples" is misleading; absolute runtimes in seconds should be reported for fair comparison.
- Minor corrections needed: fix "onto the tree" → "onto graph T" and replace "derivative" with "subgradient" (line 226).
- Originality: novel notion of tractability based on mixing time rather than graph structure; piecewise KL approach is particularly innovative.
- Significance: promising practical gains for approximate inference, but scalability and computational overhead limit immediate applicability.
- Recommendation: strong accept with minor revisions to clarify algorithmic details and provide better computational/runtime analysis.
