- Paper introduces approximating slow-mixing Ising models by projecting parameters onto a set guaranteed to be fast-mixing (spectral norm of absolute interactions < 1).  
- Inference is then performed by running Gibbs sampling on the projected fast-mixing model, bridging variational inference and MCMC.  
- Four projection objectives are evaluated: Euclidean, KL(θ||ψ) ("zero-avoiding"), a novel piecewise KL (based on subgraphs), and reversed KL(ψ||θ) ("zero-forcing").  
- Key technical contribution: an efficient dual algorithm for the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7).  
- Another technical result: gradient derivation for the reversed KL projection and use in projected gradient descent (Theorem 8).  
- The Euclidean dual algorithm is used as a subroutine inside projected gradient steps for complex divergences.  
- Experiments compare methods against strong baselines (LBP, TRW, MF) and Gibbs on original parameters.  
- Empirical finding: reversed KL projection often yields the best marginal accuracy and favorable accuracy/runtime trade-offs for limited budgets.  
- Authors transparently acknowledge looseness of the spectral-norm bound for guaranteeing fast mixing.  
- Clarity is generally good, and the motivation for using fast-mixing as tractability is compelling.  
- Important optimization details are unclear: inner-loop stopping criteria for the Euclidean projection solved with L-BFGS-B are not specified.  
- Experimental x-axis labeled "number of samples" is ambiguous for non-sampling methods; wall-clock time comparisons are recommended.  
- Primary weakness: scalability is untested — experiments are limited to small 8x8 grids and 10-node random graphs where exact inference is feasible.  
- Concern that projection costs (especially for KL(ψ||θ), which needs iterative sampling) may negate gains from fast-mixing in practice.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel conceptual framework, strong empirical evidence on tested models, and potential to inspire MCMC/variational hybrid research.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear/unstated computational costs of projection and limited experimental scale leave practical utility in question.  
- Suggested improvements: report separate wall-clock times for projection and final sampling, include runtimes for baselines, and add experiments on larger models where exact inference is infeasible.  
- Minor textual fixes: correct "projection ... onto the tree" to "onto the graph T" and refer to the piecewise KL gradient as a "subgradient" rather than "derivative."
