

**Review for "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"**

This paper introduces a novel approach to inference in high-treewidth Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set that guarantees rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling. The authors propose constrained optimization under various divergences to find parameters satisfying the Dobrushin criterion (||R||₂ < 1), shifting the focus from graph structure to parameter constraints. This complements existing variational methods (e.g., mean-field, loopy belief propagation) by offering a different tractability condition—fast mixing rather than low treewidth.

**Strengths:**
- The core idea of "fast-mixing" parameters as a tractability condition is conceptually fresh and well-motivated, extending previous work on mixing times (Dyer et al. 2000, Hayes 2006) and connecting to mean-field as a degenerate case.
- The exploration of multiple divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, zero-forcing KL) provides flexibility and insight into trade-offs between approximation quality and computational feasibility.
- The experimental design reasonably compares against standard methods (LBP, TRW, naive mean-field) and demonstrates that projected parameters yield more accurate marginals than variational methods under limited sampling time.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Clarity:** The dual formulation in Theorem 7 is poorly explained. The constraint zijDij = 0 is ambiguous—it's unclear how it preserves the original parameter space (it could reduce dimensionality) and whether the projected parameters span the same space as the original. An algorithm box is essential to clarify the procedure (e.g., whether projection is done before sampling or interleaved with sampling).
- **Quality:** The stochastic optimization approach lacks convergence guarantees. The arbitrary choice of 30,000 Gibbs iterations for projection makes the comparison with Gibbs sampling on original parameters unfair (projection runtime isn't accounted for in timing comparisons). The spectral norm bound (||R||₂ < 1) is overly conservative for 2D Ising models (as noted in Section 7), with the known threshold βc ≈ 0.4407 versus the paper's β = 0.25.
- **Significance:** Experiments lack error bars, making statistical significance impossible to assess. Comparisons omit advanced methods like blocked Gibbs or Gogate's Lifted Gibbs Sampler, which would provide more meaningful benchmarks. The practical benefit is unclear without timing data.
- **Originality:** While the core concept is novel, the experimental evaluation doesn't convincingly demonstrate superiority over existing methods, limiting the paper's impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The "fast-mixing" perspective offers a valuable complement to existing tractability notions.
- The method provides a principled way to trade off mixing time and approximation quality, which could benefit practitioners.
- The connection to mean-field as a degenerate case is insightful and well-grounded.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Methodological ambiguities (dual formulation, constraint handling) prevent rigorous evaluation.
- The experimental evaluation is unconvincing due to arbitrary parameter choices, lack of runtime comparisons, and insufficient benchmarking.
- The paper doesn't address the looseness of the spectral bound, which weakens the practical relevance.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper presents a promising idea with conceptual novelty, but the current version has significant weaknesses in clarity and experimental validation. The core contribution is interesting and potentially impactful, but the lack of methodological clarity and fair experimental comparison undermines its scientific contribution. With major revisions—clarifying the dual formulation, providing a clear algorithm, establishing convergence guarantees, and conducting a rigorous experimental evaluation including timing comparisons—the paper could be a strong contribution to NIPS.

**Rating:** 4 (Weak Accept) - The paper has a promising idea but requires substantial revisions to address clarity and experimental weaknesses.

**Final Recommendation:** Major Revision
