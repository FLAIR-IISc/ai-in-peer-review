This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models. The central idea is to approximate a given intractable (slow-mixing) distribution by projecting its parameters onto a set of parameters that are guaranteed to be "fast-mixing." The set of fast-mixing models is defined by a sufficient condition from the MCMC literature: the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths must be less than a constant (here, 1). After projecting the original parameters onto this set, inference (e.g., estimating marginals) is performed by running Gibbs sampling on the resulting fast-mixing model. This work bridges ideas from variational inference, which approximates with a structurally simple model family, and MCMC methods.

The authors propose and evaluate four different projection methods, based on minimizing different divergences between the original and projected models: (1) Euclidean distance between parameter vectors, (2) the "zero-avoiding" KL divergence KL(θ||ψ), (3) a novel tractable "piecewise KL" divergence based on subgraphs, and (4) the "zero-forcing" reversed KL divergence KL(ψ||θ). A key technical contribution is an efficient dual algorithm for the Euclidean projection, which is then used as a subroutine within a projected gradient descent framework to handle the other, more complex divergences.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem is clear, and the derivations for the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) and the gradient for the reversed KL divergence (Theorem 8) appear correct and are non-trivial contributions. The experimental evaluation is well-designed, comparing the proposed methods against standard and relevant baselines like Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), Naive Mean Field (MF), and standard Gibbs sampling on the original parameters. The authors are also commendably transparent about a key limitation: the looseness of the spectral norm bound for guaranteeing fast mixing (Section 7).

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for using "fast mixing" as a new notion of tractability is compelling and well-explained. However, several important details regarding the optimization and experimental setup are unclear, which could hinder reproducibility.
*   The projected gradient algorithm described in Section 5.1 involves an inner loop for the Euclidean projection (solved with L-BFGS-B) within each gradient step. The stopping criteria for these inner-loop iterations are not specified. How much computational effort is spent on each projection?
*   In the experimental results (Figure 2), the x-axis is labeled "number of samples," but this seems to apply only to the Gibbs-based methods. For methods like LBP, TRW, and MF, which are not sampling-based, this axis is ambiguous. It likely represents runtime, but this should be clarified. A comparison using wall-clock time in seconds for *all* methods would be much more informative for assessing the practical trade-offs.

**Originality:** The main idea of defining a tractable family of distributions based on MCMC mixing time properties, rather than graphical structure (e.g., low treewidth), is highly original. While related to variational inference (the authors correctly note that mean field is a degenerate case where the spectral norm is constrained to zero), this perspective is new and refreshing. It opens up a new avenue for designing approximate inference algorithms that blend optimization and sampling in an interesting way. The "piecewise KL" divergence is also a novel, though seemingly less effective, contribution.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant. The proposed methods, particularly the one based on the reversed KL divergence, consistently outperform strong variational baselines (LBP, TRW, MF) in terms of marginal accuracy on the tested models. Furthermore, for a limited computational budget, the proposed approach provides more accurate marginals than running Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing model. This suggests the method could be very useful in practical scenarios where inference must be completed within a fixed time budget.

The primary weakness and main question regarding the paper's significance is scalability. The experiments are conducted on small 8x8 grids and 10-node random graphs, where exact inference is still feasible. The practical utility of this method hinges on its performance and computational cost on larger, more realistic problems where both exact inference and standard MCMC are intractable. The projection step itself, especially for KL(ψ||θ) which requires iterative sampling, could be very computationally expensive, potentially negating the benefits of fast mixing in the final inference stage.

### Summary of Arguments
**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  Introduces a novel and principled conceptual framework for approximate inference based on "fast-mixing" distributions.
2.  Provides strong empirical evidence on small- to medium-sized models that the proposed methods outperform widely used variational baselines.
3.  The reversed KL projection method demonstrates a compelling trade-off between accuracy and runtime compared to standard Gibbs sampling.
4.  The work could inspire new research at the intersection of variational methods and MCMC.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  The computational cost of the projection procedure is not clearly reported or analyzed, making it difficult to assess the method's practical utility.
2.  The experimental evaluation is limited to small models, leaving the scalability of the approach as a major open question.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions
*   To strengthen the paper, I strongly recommend clarifying the experimental runtimes. Please report the absolute wall-clock time (in seconds) for the projection phase and the final sampling phase separately. It would also be valuable to include the runtimes for the baseline methods (LBP, TRW, MF) for a complete and fair comparison.
*   To address the scalability concern, it would be highly beneficial to include experiments on at least one larger model instance where exact inference is not possible. For example, using larger grid graphs or established benchmarks like the tractable random-field models from Flach et al. would significantly bolster the paper's claims.
*   Minor textual suggestions:
    *   Line 222: "projection ... onto the tree" should probably be "projection ... onto the graph T".
    *   Line 226: Since the objective in the piecewise KL is a maximum over functions, its gradient is technically a subgradient. Using the term "subgradient" instead of "derivative" would be more precise.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a very interesting and original core idea. The empirical results are convincing, though limited in scale. If the authors can address the concerns about computational cost and scalability in their rebuttal, I would be inclined to recommend acceptance.
