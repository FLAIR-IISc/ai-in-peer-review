### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models. Instead of approximating an intractable distribution with one that is structurally simple (e.g., a tree or a fully factorized distribution), the authors propose approximating it with a distribution from the set of "fast-mixing" models. A model is defined as fast-mixing if the spectral norm of the matrix of its absolute edge interaction strengths is less than a given constant (e.g., 1), a condition which guarantees that Gibbs sampling converges rapidly.

The core of the method is to take an arbitrary (potentially slow-mixing) Ising model and project its parameters onto this set of fast-mixing models. This projection is constrained to preserve the original graph's sparsity. The authors explore four different divergences to define the projection: Euclidean distance between parameter vectors, the standard KL-divergence (KL(θ||ψ)), a novel piecewise KL-divergence based on tractable subgraphs, and the reversed KL-divergence (KL(ψ||θ)).

The optimization procedures are tailored to the divergence. The Euclidean projection is solved efficiently using a dual formulation with L-BFGS-B. The other divergences are minimized using a projected-gradient algorithm, where each projection step leverages the Euclidean projection solver. Notably, minimizing the reversed KL-divergence requires estimating the gradient via Gibbs sampling on the (fast-mixing) projected model, which is feasible due to the fast-mixing guarantee.

Experiments on small random graphs and grids show that the proposed methods, particularly the one based on reversed KL-divergence, consistently produce more accurate marginals than standard baselines like loopy belief propagation (LBP), tree-reweighted belief propagation (TRW), and mean-field (MF). For a limited time budget, Gibbs sampling on the projected model also outperforms Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing model.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of defining a tractable family of models based on fast-mixing properties rather than structural simplicity (like low treewidth) is highly original and offers a new perspective on approximate inference. It cleverly connects the theory of MCMC mixing times to practical variational inference. This approach could inspire new classes of approximation algorithms.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are convincing. The proposed projection methods, especially the reversed KL variant, consistently outperform widely-used approximate inference techniques like LBP, TRW, and MF in terms of marginal accuracy across various graph structures and interaction strengths. The comparison showing that sampling from the projected model can be more accurate than sampling from the original model under a fixed time budget is a strong argument for the method's practical utility.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the dual algorithm for the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is elegant and practical. The formulation of the reversed KL projection as a stochastic optimization problem that leverages the fast-mixing property of the approximating distribution is a key insight and can be viewed as a principled generalization of mean-field inference to a much richer approximating family.

### Weaknesses and Questions for Authors

1.  **Clarity on a Key Algorithm:** The projected-gradient algorithm described in Section 5.1 is a crucial component for three of the four proposed methods. However, its description is somewhat high-level. The algorithm involves an outer loop for the gradient steps and an inner loop where L-BFGS-B is used to solve the Euclidean projection. The computational cost and behavior of this procedure depend heavily on the inner loop's termination.
    *   **Question:** What are the stopping criteria for the inner L-BFGS-B iterations within the projected-gradient algorithm? Are they run to convergence at each step, or for a fixed number of iterations? This detail is essential for understanding the overall runtime complexity and for reproducibility.

2.  **Ambiguity in Experimental Evaluation and Reporting:** The experimental results are promising, but the reporting makes a fair comparison of the runtimes difficult.
    *   **Question:** The x-axis in Figure 2 is labeled "number of samples." This is confusing as sampling is primarily used to evaluate the final marginals and within the reversed KL optimization. Could the authors clarify what this axis represents, especially for methods like Euclidean projection, LBP, or TRW that do not inherently rely on sampling for inference?
    *   **Request:** For a more transparent comparison of the efficiency-accuracy trade-off, it would be highly beneficial to report the absolute wall-clock times (in seconds). This should include the time taken for the projection optimization for the proposed methods and the inference/convergence time for the baselines (LBP, TRW, MF).

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The experiments are performed on relatively small models (10-node graphs and 8x8 grids). The practicality of the proposed method for larger-scale problems is unclear. The projection step involves singular value decompositions and an iterative L-BFGS-B optimization, which may not scale well to graphs with thousands of nodes.
    *   **Suggestion:** To better demonstrate the method's potential, it would be valuable to include experiments on larger, more structured models. For instance, testing on some of the benchmark tractable random-field models (e.g., from Boris Flach's work) could provide valuable insights into the scalability and performance on problems with known ground truth.

### Minor Edits

-   Line 222: "projection θ(T) onto the tree" is slightly ambiguous when T is a general tractable subgraph. Suggest changing to "projection θ(T) onto the graph T".
-   Line 226: The objective for the piecewise KL divergence is a maximum over several functions, making it non-differentiable. "subgradient" would be more precise than "derivative".

### Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that introduces a novel and interesting idea for approximate inference. The conceptual shift from structural tractability to fast-mixing tractability is a significant contribution. The empirical results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority over several standard baselines on the tested problems.

The main weakness lies in the potential for high computational cost and the limited scale of the experiments, which makes it difficult to judge the method's practical utility for larger problems. The lack of clarity on runtimes and algorithmic details for the projection step is a key concern that the authors should address.

Overall, the paper presents a high-quality, original contribution with convincing initial results. If the authors can clarify the runtime aspects and provide a more compelling case for its scalability, this work could have a notable impact on the field of approximate inference. I recommend acceptance, contingent on a satisfactory author response to the questions about runtime and algorithmic clarity.
