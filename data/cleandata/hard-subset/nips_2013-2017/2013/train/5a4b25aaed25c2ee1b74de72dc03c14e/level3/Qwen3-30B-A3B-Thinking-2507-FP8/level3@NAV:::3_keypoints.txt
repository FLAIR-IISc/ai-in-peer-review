- Proposes projecting Ising-model parameters onto a fast-mixing set defined by spectral-norm constraints to enable faster Gibbs sampling.
- Develops a projection framework for multiple divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL) while preserving graph structure.
- Key technical contribution: dual method for Euclidean projection and adaptation of projected gradient descent for other divergences.
- Reversed KL projection requires running Gibbs sampling inside the projection (nested optimization).
- Empirical results on small graphs show consistent improvements over TRW, mean-field, and loopy BP, often beating Gibbs on the original model within time limits.
- Reversed KL projection performs best empirically.
- Novel perspective: focuses on parameter-space conditions guaranteeing rapid mixing rather than structural tractability constraints.
- Strengths: clear theoretical link between spectral norms and mixing, well-executed comparisons, useful practical insight about fast-mixing approximations.
- Paper is well-written with clear math, though minor wording edits are needed (e.g., "subgradient").
- Weakness: scalability limited—experiments only on small graphs.
- Weakness: runtime reporting unclear (Figure 2 labeled "number of samples" despite sampling used only for reversed KL).
- Weakness: high computational cost of reversed KL method not sufficiently discussed.
- Needs clarification on nested optimization stopping criteria for reproducibility.
- Recommends larger-scale experiments (e.g., Boris Flach's tractable random-field models) to assess practical utility.
- Overall: technically sound and empirically validated; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing clarifications.
