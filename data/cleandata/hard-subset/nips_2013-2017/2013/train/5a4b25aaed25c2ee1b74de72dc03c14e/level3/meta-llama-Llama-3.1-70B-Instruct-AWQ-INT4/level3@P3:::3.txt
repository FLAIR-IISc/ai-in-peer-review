The paper "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing" proposes an algorithm to project Ising model parameters onto a parameter set that is guaranteed to be fast mixing, under several divergences. The main idea is to take an arbitrary (slow-mixing) set of parameters and project onto the fast-mixing set, using four different divergences: Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL. The projection is constrained to preserve the original graph structure.

The paper starts by discussing the difficulties of inference in high-treewidth graphical models, which has motivated several classes of approximations based on tractable families. The authors propose a new notion of "tractability", insisting not that a graph has a fast algorithm for exact inference, but only that it obeys parameter-space conditions ensuring that Gibbs sampling will converge rapidly to the stationary distribution. For the case of Ising models, they use a simple condition that can guarantee rapid mixing, namely that the spectral norm of the matrix of interaction strengths is less than one.

The authors then derive a projection in the Euclidean norm, based on an iterative thresholding of the singular value decomposition. They extend this to more probabilistic divergences, including a novel "piecewise" divergence, based on computing the exact KL-divergence on several low-treewidth subgraphs. They also consider projecting onto the KL-divergence, which requires a stochastic approximation approach where one repeatedly generates samples from the model, and projects in the Euclidean norm after taking a gradient step.

The paper presents experimental results comparing the proposed methods to Gibbs sampling on the original parameters, along with several standard variational methods. The proposed methods are more accurate than variational approximations. Given enough time, Gibbs sampling using the original parameters will always be more accurate, but with finite time, projecting onto the fast-mixing set generally gives better results.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a thorough discussion of the background and related work, and the experimental results are convincing. The idea of projecting onto the fast-mixing set is novel and interesting, and the authors provide a good motivation for why this approach can be useful.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors note that the spectral norm bound is too conservative, and a tighter bound on when rapid mixing will occur would be more informative. Additionally, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of the computational complexity of the proposed methods, which makes it difficult to evaluate their practicality.

Overall, the paper is a good contribution to the field of approximate inference in graphical models. The authors provide a novel approach to projecting onto the fast-mixing set, and the experimental results are convincing. However, the paper could be improved by providing a clearer explanation of the computational complexity of the proposed methods, and by exploring ways to tighten the spectral norm bound.
