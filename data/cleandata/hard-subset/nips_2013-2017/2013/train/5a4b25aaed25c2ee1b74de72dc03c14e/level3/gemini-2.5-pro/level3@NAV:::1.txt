This paper introduces a novel approach for approximate inference in strongly coupled Ising models. The core idea is to project the original model parameters onto a nearby set of parameters guaranteed to be "fast-mixing" for Gibbs sampling, based on spectral bounds established in prior work [4, 8]. This reframes the notion of a "tractable" approximation from one of structural simplicity (e.g., mean-field) to one of rapid MCMC convergence. The authors propose several projection methods based on different divergences and demonstrate their effectiveness on small-scale models.

The primary strength of this work is its originality and elegance. The proposal to use projection onto a fast-mixing set as an inference tool is a clever idea that bridges theoretical MCMC analysis with practical approximate inference. The experimental results on small Ising models are promising, suggesting that in certain regimes, sampling from the projected model can yield more accurate marginal estimates than standard Gibbs sampling or variational methods within a fixed computational budget.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that undermine its conclusions. The most critical issue lies in the quality of the experimental evaluation. The computational cost of the projection step itself, which appears substantial, is not accounted for in the timing comparisons. This omission fundamentally biases the results in favor of the proposed method and makes the central efficiency claims questionable. Secondly, the work's significance is limited by a lack of evidence for scalability; experiments are confined to small models, and it is unclear if the performance gains will generalize to larger, more realistic problems. The approach is also restricted to binary variables, as the required convergence bounds are harder to obtain for general MRFs.

Finally, the paper suffers from major clarity issues. Section 4 is poorly organized and introduces notation without adequate explanation, making it difficult to follow. Several key results (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5) are stated without sufficient proof or citation, and the notation for KL-divergence is used inconsistently in the introduction.

In summary, while the paper presents a conceptually appealing idea, the flawed experimental comparison, limited scope, and significant clarity problems prevent a recommendation for acceptance. The work could represent a valuable contribution if the authors conduct a more rigorous and fair experimental evaluation—crucially, one that includes the projection overhead in all timing results—and substantially revise the manuscript for clarity and technical completeness.
