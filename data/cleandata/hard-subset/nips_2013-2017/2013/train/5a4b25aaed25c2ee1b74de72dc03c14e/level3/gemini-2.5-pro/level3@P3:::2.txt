This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models by defining a new class of "tractable" distributions. Instead of tractability being defined by graphical structure (e.g., low treewidth), the authors define it by parameter-space conditions that guarantee fast mixing of Gibbs sampling. Specifically, they leverage the Dobrushin criterion (and a tighter spectral norm variant) which bounds interaction strengths to ensure rapid convergence. The core contribution is a method to project the parameters of a given, potentially slow-mixing, Ising model onto this fast-mixing set. This projection is formulated as a constrained optimization problem, and the authors explore solutions under the Euclidean distance as well as several KL-based divergences. The work relates to the broad literature on variational inference but re-frames the problem: rather than approximating with a structurally simple family like mean-field, it approximates with a dynamically simple (i.e., fast-sampling) family.

The main strength of this paper lies in its originality and the conceptual novelty of its approach. The idea of using fast-mixing properties to define a tractable approximation family is a creative and promising departure from standard structure-based methods. This could open up a new direction for approximate inference algorithms. Furthermore, the combination of deterministic optimization (for the projection) and stochastic methods (for sampling and gradient estimation) is an interesting and under-explored synthesis that could prove powerful. The paper provides a good theoretical setup for the problem, including derivations for the Euclidean projection and gradients for the KL divergences.

Despite the promising core idea, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in its clarity and experimental evaluation that undermine its contributions.

**Clarity and Methodological Details:**
The presentation of the core projection algorithm is difficult to follow. Theorem 7 presents the dual of the Euclidean projection problem, but its derivation is relegated to the appendix and the intuition behind the formulation is not explained. It is also unclear how the `Zij * Dij = 0` constraints, intended to preserve graph sparsity, guarantee that the projected parameters span the same space and not a potentially more constrained one.

More critically, the overall procedure is ambiguous. For the KL(ψ||θ) projection, which uses stochastic gradient descent, it is not specified whether the projection is fully optimized once before sampling begins, or if projection updates are interleaved with sampling. The text mentions a "pool" of samples being updated by a single Gibbs step after each parameter update, suggesting an online or interleaved process. A clear algorithm box is essential to disambiguate the procedure and would greatly improve the paper's reproducibility and clarity.

**Technical Soundness:**
The paper proposes a projected gradient descent strategy for the KL-based projections and cites Ergodic Mirror Descent to guarantee convergence. However, the theoretical conditions for convergence (e.g., decreasing step sizes) are not followed in the experiments, where a constant step size is used. This disconnect between the cited theory and the practical implementation weakens the theoretical grounding of the algorithm used in the experiments. The convergence properties of the proposed practical algorithm are therefore not established.

**Experimental Evaluation:**
The experimental validation is not convincing and contains several flaws that make it difficult to assess the practical benefit of the proposed method.

1.  **Unfair Comparisons:** The primary comparison is based on the number of Gibbs iterations *after* the projection is complete. This ignores the substantial computational cost of the projection itself, which for the KL(ψ||θ) method involves 30,000 total Gibbs iterations. Comparing this against deterministic methods like Loopy BP or Mean Field based on "sampling iterations" is misleading, as those methods have no such phase.
2.  **Missing Timing Results:** The central claim is that the method is superior when "limited time is available." However, no wall-clock timing comparisons are provided. Without knowing the runtime of the projection step, it is impossible to verify if the total time (projection + sampling) is actually less than simply running a longer Gibbs chain on the original parameters for equivalent accuracy.
3.  **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The plots do not include error bars, making it impossible to determine if the observed differences between methods are statistically significant or simply due to random variation across the 50 trials.
4.  **Incomplete Baselines:** The paper compares against standard Gibbs sampling but omits comparisons to more advanced samplers known to improve mixing, such as block Gibbs sampling or lifted Gibbs samplers (Gogate & Domingos, UAI 2011), which would be more relevant competitors.
5.  **Arbitrary Parameters:** The choice of 30,000 Gibbs iterations for the projection phase seems arbitrary. Similarly, using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of the theoretically justified 1.0 is done without rigorous justification, other than that it "gives better approximation." This suggests the bound itself might be too loose to be practical, a key weakness which is only briefly mentioned in the discussion.

In summary, this paper introduces a highly original and interesting idea for approximate inference. However, the work in its current form feels preliminary. The methodological description is unclear, and the experimental evaluation is not rigorous enough to convincingly demonstrate the practical advantages of the proposed approach. The core idea is significant, but it requires a much clearer algorithmic description and a stronger, fairer experimental evaluation to constitute a complete contribution.
