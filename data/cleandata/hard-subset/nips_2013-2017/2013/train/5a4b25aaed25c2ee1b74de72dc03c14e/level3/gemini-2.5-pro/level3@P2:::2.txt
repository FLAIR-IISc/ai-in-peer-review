This paper introduces a novel approach for approximate inference in Ising models where Gibbs sampling mixes slowly due to strong interactions. The central idea is to project the original model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set, defined as the set of parameters whose interaction matrix has a spectral norm less than one—a condition that guarantees rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling. The authors develop methods for performing this projection under four different divergences: the Euclidean distance between parameter vectors, an inclusive "zero-avoiding" KL-divergence (KL(θ||ψ)), a novel "piecewise" KL-divergence computed on tractable subgraphs, and an exclusive "zero-forcing" KL-divergence (KL(ψ||θ)). For the Euclidean projection, a dual optimization algorithm is derived. For the KL-divergences, a projected stochastic gradient descent method is employed. The paper experimentally evaluates the accuracy of marginals obtained from Gibbs sampling on the projected parameters and compares them to Gibbs on the original parameters and standard variational methods like Mean Field, LBP, and TRW.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's main contribution is conceptual: it proposes defining a "tractable" family of distributions based on their mixing properties rather than their graphical structure (e.g., low treewidth). This is a creative and potentially impactful idea that offers a new perspective on approximate inference. By combining deterministic optimization (projection) with stochastic sampling, the work explores an interesting and under-explored synthesis of different inference paradigms. This could open up new avenues for research in approximate inference and learning for graphical models.

*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is grounded in solid MCMC theory, using established results on mixing times to define the target set for projection. The development of projection algorithms for different divergences, particularly the dual formulation for the Euclidean case and the stochastic gradient approach for the KL-divergence, represents a sound technical contribution.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Clarity:** The paper's exposition of the methodology is a significant weakness and makes the work difficult to fully understand and reproduce.
    *   The derivation and description of the dual algorithm for the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) are particularly opaque. The intuition behind the dual variables M and Λ is not explained, and the structure of the objective function `g(Λ,M)` is not motivated, making the result hard to interpret.
    *   The constraint `ZijBij = 0` is used to preserve the graph structure. However, it is not clear how this interacts with the projection `D = |B|`. Does this guarantee that the parameterization still spans the same space, or could it inadvertently shrink it?
    *   The overall procedure for minimizing the KL-divergence is ambiguous. The paper describes a projected gradient descent strategy, but it is unclear whether the projection is fully optimized at each step or if the sampling and projection updates are interleaved. The lack of a clear algorithm box summarizing the procedure is a major omission.
    *   The claim of convergence for the projected stochastic gradient descent method is hand-wavy. While "Ergodic Mirror Descent" is cited, the connection is not detailed, and the conditions under which the proposed algorithm converges are not discussed.

*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments, as presented, are not sufficient to convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed method.
    *   **Unfair Comparisons:** The evaluation is based on marginal accuracy after a fixed number of Gibbs iterations. This is an arbitrary metric that completely ignores the computational cost of the projection step itself, which can be substantial (e.g., involving 30,000 Gibbs iterations for the KL(ψ||θ) projection). A much fairer and more informative evaluation would compare methods based on total computation time (projection time + sampling time) vs. accuracy. Without this, it is impossible to assess if the proposed method offers a real-world advantage.
    *   **Missing Baselines:** The paper fails to compare against other MCMC methods designed to improve mixing, such as block Gibbs sampling or the Swendsen-Wang algorithm, which are highly relevant baselines for Ising models. A comparison to more recent MCMC advances like lifted samplers would also strengthen the evaluation.
    *   **Lack of Statistical Rigor:** Results are averaged over 50 trials, yet the plots lack error bars. This makes it impossible to judge the statistical significance of the reported performance differences and weakens the paper's empirical claims.
    *   **Theory-Practice Gap:** The experiments use a spectral norm bound of 2.5, which empirically yields better results than the theoretically justified bound of 1.0. While honest, this discrepancy undermines the theoretical foundation of the work. This choice requires more rigorous justification or analysis, as it suggests the theoretical bound is overly conservative to the point of being impractical.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Introduces a highly novel and conceptually appealing idea of defining tractability via mixing time.
*   The approach of projecting parameters to enable fast MCMC is a new tool that could be significant for the field.
*   The work provides a solid foundation for future research exploring this direction.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The methodological description is unclear, hindering reproducibility and full understanding of the proposed algorithms.
*   The experimental evaluation is flawed due to unfair comparisons (no timing results), missing key baselines, and a lack of statistical rigor (no error bars).
*   The gap between the theory (spectral norm < 1) and the parameters used in practice (spectral norm < 2.5) is not adequately explained or justified.

In summary, the paper presents a promising and original idea. However, the weaknesses in clarity and the experimental evaluation are significant. To be acceptable for NIPS, the paper would need to be substantially revised to include a clearer, more detailed description of the algorithms (ideally with pseudocode), and a much more rigorous and fair experimental comparison that includes timing results, error bars, and more relevant baselines.
