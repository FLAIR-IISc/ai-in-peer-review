- Proposal: Project Ising-model parameters onto a fast-mixing set (spectral norm of dependency matrix < 1) and run single-site Gibbs on the projected model.  
- Tractable family: Uses a sufficient spectral-norm condition on the dependency matrix R (via bounds R_ij ≤ tanh|β_ij| ≤ |β_ij|) to guarantee rapid mixing.  
- Core idea: Preserve graph sparsity while shrinking interaction strengths so |||β|||_2 (on |β|) meets the mixing criterion.  
- Four projection divergences: Euclidean, inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ), piecewise KL (exact on low-treewidth subgraphs), and reversed KL KL(ψ||θ).  
- Euclidean projection: Dual formulation that preserves sparsity and enforces the spectral constraint by masked SVD-thresholding.  
- Inclusive KL: Theoretically attractive but impractical because it requires expectations under the intractable target θ.  
- Piecewise KL: Tractable by operating on low-treewidth subgraphs but gives underwhelming empirical gains.  
- Reversed KL: Practical via stochastic optimization since expectations are under fast-mixing ψ; empirically the most effective.  
- Optimization: Projected-gradient methods using the Euclidean projection as a prox step, with L-BFGS-B used in the dual.  
- Experiments: 8×8 grids and small random graphs show projected models (especially reversed KL) yield more accurate marginals than LBP/TRW/MF and beat original Gibbs for limited compute.  
- Relation to prior work: Situates well among variational and decomposition methods; novelty is defining tractability by mixing-time guarantees rather than graph structure.  
- Theoretical connections: Links to Dobrushin-type criteria and spectral bounds; reversed-KL generalizes mean-field to the fast-mixing set.  
- Technical quality: Development is sound; Euclidean projection and masked SVD-thresholding are practical contributions; stochastic projected algorithm is appropriate.  
- Missing/unclear details: Inner L-BFGS-B stopping criteria and outer-loop convergence diagnostics need clarification.  
- Mixing-scan discrepancy: Paper’s bounds are for random-scan Gibbs but experiments use systematic scan; authors should state whether guarantees extend or rely on empirical mixing.  
- Spectral bound relaxation: Authors use c=2.5 in practice despite 1.0 being sufficient; need evidence on mixing failures and diagnostics under c=2.5.  
- Clarity suggestions: Add pseudocode/algorithm box for the projected-gradient loop, clarify figures (absolute runtimes, axes), and distinguish θ/ψ and their R matrices.  
- Scalability/significance concerns: Approach is promising but experiments are small-scale and projection overhead (esp. for KL(ψ||θ)) may limit practical impact.  
- Suggested additional experiments: Larger models, wall-clock time vs accuracy plots for all baselines, sensitivity analyses for KL(ψ||θ) hyperparameters, and parameter-distortion histograms.  
- Pros: Novel reframing of tractability via fast-mixing, solid theoretical basis, practical Euclidean projection, and strong empirical gains vs standard variational baselines.  
- Cons: Modest experimental scale, reliance on a conservative sufficient condition with ad hoc relaxation, and limited utility of inclusive/piecewise KL in non-toy settings.  
- Recommendation: Lean toward acceptance conditional on clarifying runtime methodology, stopping criteria, mixing guarantees, and strengthening empirical evaluation and runtime reporting.
