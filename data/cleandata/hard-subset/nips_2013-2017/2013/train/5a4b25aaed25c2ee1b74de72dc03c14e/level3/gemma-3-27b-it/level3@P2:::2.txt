This paper proposes a novel approach to accelerating Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model parameters onto a set guaranteed to satisfy the Dobrushin criterion for rapid mixing. The core idea – combining stochastic (Gibbs) and deterministic (projection) inference – is interesting and represents an under-explored area. The authors explore various divergence measures for the projection, including Euclidean distance, piecewise KL-divergence, and a reversed KL-divergence minimized via stochastic approximation.

The paper is well-motivated, situating the work within the broader context of approximate inference in graphical models, particularly highlighting the limitations of tree-based approximations and the challenges of slow mixing in high-treewidth models. The connection to existing work on mixing times and the Dobrushin criterion is clearly established, and the authors appropriately cite relevant literature (e.g., [6, 10, 11]). However, some aspects of the technical development and experimental evaluation require further clarification and strengthening.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of projecting parameters to enforce fast mixing is a fresh perspective on accelerating MCMC sampling.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The work builds upon established results from Markov chain theory, specifically the connection between spectral norm of the dependency matrix and mixing time.
*   **Exploration of Divergences:** Investigating different divergence measures provides valuable insight into the trade-offs between computational cost and approximation accuracy.
*   **Potential Significance:** If successfully developed, this approach could offer significant speedups for inference in complex Ising models, potentially impacting applications in diverse fields.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Method:** The description of the projection procedure, particularly regarding the dual formulation in Theorem 7, is somewhat unclear. The explanation of how the *z<sub>ij</sub>d<sub>ij</sub> = 0* constraints maintain the representational capacity of the parameter space is insufficient. It’s not immediately obvious that the projected parameterization doesn’t unnecessarily restrict the model.
*   **Ambiguous Procedure:** The interaction between projection and sampling is not fully elucidated. Is the projection fully optimized before sampling, or are projection updates interleaved with Gibbs sampling steps? An algorithm box would greatly improve clarity.
*   **Convergence Guarantees:** The paper doesn’t explicitly address the convergence properties of the projected gradient descent strategy used to minimize the reversed KL-divergence. While mentioning Ergodic Mirror Descent [3], a more detailed discussion of the convergence conditions would be beneficial.
*   **Experimental Weaknesses:** The experimental evaluation is unconvincing. The choice of 30,000 Gibbs iterations for evaluation is arbitrary and doesn’t provide a fair comparison with deterministic inference methods. Iteration-based comparisons are misleading without considering runtime. Crucially, the paper lacks any timing comparisons, making it impossible to assess the practical benefit of the proposed projection methods.
*   **Lack of Statistical Significance:** Plots are presented without error bars, preventing an assessment of the statistical significance of the reported results.
*   **Missing Baselines:**  The comparison lacks important baselines such as blocked Gibbs sampling or Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs Sampler, which are often more efficient than standard Gibbs sampling.
*   **Looseness of Bound:** The discussion acknowledges that the spectral norm bound of 1.0 is conservative compared to known critical values for certain Ising models (e.g., the 2D Ising model [11]). A tighter bound would enhance the practical utility of the method.

**Quality:** The paper’s technical soundness is reasonable, although the clarity of the projection algorithm and the lack of convergence analysis raise concerns. The experimental setup is flawed and weakens the support for the claims.

**Clarity:** While the introduction and background sections are well-written, the technical details of the projection algorithm are difficult to follow. The lack of an algorithm box and a clear description of the overall procedure hinders understanding.

**Originality:** The idea of parameter projection for fast mixing is novel. However, the implementation details and experimental results need substantial refinement to demonstrate its true potential.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is significant if the proposed methods can reliably accelerate inference in complex Ising models. However, the current experimental evidence is insufficient to establish this.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to accelerating MCMC.
*   Solid theoretical foundation in Markov chain theory.
*   Exploration of multiple divergence measures.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Unclear method description and lack of convergence analysis.
*   Flawed experimental evaluation with arbitrary parameter choices and missing runtime comparisons.
*   Lack of statistical significance in experimental results.
*   Missing comparisons to relevant baselines.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising idea with the potential to advance the field of approximate inference. However, the current presentation lacks clarity, and the experimental evaluation is insufficient to support the claims. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the identified weaknesses. The authors should focus on clarifying the projection algorithm, providing convergence guarantees, conducting a more rigorous and comprehensive experimental evaluation with timing comparisons and statistical analysis, and including relevant baselines. A clear algorithm box would also significantly improve the paper's accessibility.
