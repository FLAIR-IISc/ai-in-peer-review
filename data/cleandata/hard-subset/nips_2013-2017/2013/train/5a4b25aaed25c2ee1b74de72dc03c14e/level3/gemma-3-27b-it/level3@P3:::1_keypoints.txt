- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set using a spectral bound on the dependency matrix to speed Gibbs sampling.
- Novelty: framing tractability in terms of Gibbs mixing-time bounds is a fresh contribution compared to mean-field and belief propagation approaches.
- Builds on solid theoretical foundations relating Gibbs mixing times and spectral bounds.
- Explores multiple projection methods (Euclidean, piecewise KL, reverse KL) with varied computational trade-offs.
- Experimental results suggest the approach can improve sampling in regimes where Gibbs mixing is slow.
- Main scalability concern: experiments are limited to small Ising models and may not generalize to larger, realistic models.
- Major computational-cost issue: projection time is not included in timing comparisons, biasing results and questioning Figure 2’s conclusions.
- Limited generalizability: convergence bounds and methods are tied to binary variables and are not addressed for non-binary models.
- Clarity problems: several standard results (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5) lack references or proof sketches, hindering reproducibility.
- The modification to Lemma 5’s proof is mentioned but not detailed, leaving a gap in verification.
- Determining the dependency matrix R’s spectral norm involves an implicit maximization that is not sufficiently discussed for practical tractability.
- Section 4 is poorly organized and introduces notation (g, M, Lambda) unclearly; Theorem 4 lacks context and explanation.
- Notational inconsistency: KL(q||p) usage in Section 1 flips conventional argument order and should be corrected.
- The reverse-KL projection is only briefly mentioned; KL(θ||ψ) projection (potential “best case”) is not fully explored and could be informative.
- Unexplained empirical gap: the large performance difference between mean-field and reverse-KL projections needs investigation.
- Arguments for acceptance: the idea is novel, theoretically grounded, and could stimulate further research; experiments show potential benefits.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing scalability analysis, unaccounted projection cost, clarity issues, limited generalizability, and biased experimental setup.
- Overall assessment: interesting and elegant approach but insufficient practical validation and clarity to justify acceptance in current form.
- Recommendation: reject with encouragement to address scalability, include projection time in comparisons, clarify proofs/notation, and explore generalizations to strengthen the paper.
