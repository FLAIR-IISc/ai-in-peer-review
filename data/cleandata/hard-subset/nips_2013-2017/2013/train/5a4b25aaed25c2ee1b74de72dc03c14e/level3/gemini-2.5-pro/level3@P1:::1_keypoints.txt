- Novel idea: define tractable distributions by guaranteed fast-mixing Gibbs sampling rather than low treewidth.
- Core method: project Ising model parameters onto the nearest parameter set with spectral-norm-bounded dependency matrix (||R||2 < 1).
- Proposed projection criteria: Euclidean distance, a novel piecewise KL, and forward/reverse KL divergences.
- Theoretical contribution: constrained optimization formulation and a dual method for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7).
- Solid grounding in MCMC mixing-time theory, leveraging prior convergence results.
- Empirical claim: reverse-KL projection yields more accurate marginals than MF, LBP, TRW and original Gibbs on tested small problems.
- Time-accuracy plots suggest benefit under time constraints, but this claim is undermined by a critical flaw.
- Critical experimental flaw: projection computation time (notably expensive reverse-KL SGD with 30k Gibbs iterations) is omitted from runtime comparisons.
- Experiments are limited to very small models (8x8 grids, 10-node graphs), leaving scalability and generalization unclear.
- Missing strong baseline: exact "zero-avoiding" KL(θ||ψ) projection via junction-tree/exact marginals was not evaluated.
- Clarity issues: key sections (e.g., Section 4) are opaque; variables and objective in Theorem 7 lack motivation and explanation.
- Missing citations or proofs for central results (Theorem 6, Lemma 5); promised derivations (e.g., Hayes modification) should be included in appendix.
- Notational inconsistency: KL-divergence convention differs from standard mean-field literature and causes confusion.
- Practical gap: dependency matrix R involves an intractable maximization; the paper does not explain how this is computed or bounded tractably.
- Limited applicability: framework developed only for binary Ising models; extension to general MRFs is nontrivial and not addressed.
- Overall assessment: original and potentially significant idea but manuscript is not ready—needs corrected timing comparisons, clearer exposition, stronger baselines, and larger-scale experiments.
