Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes to approximate an intractable Ising model by projecting its parameters onto a subset guaranteed to be fast-mixing for single-site Gibbs. The fast-mixing set is defined by a norm-based dependency bound (Rij ≤ tanh|βij|) and a sufficient criterion (e.g., Dobrushin’s ||R||1 < 1 or the spectral norm bound ||R||2 < 1 from Hayes/Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum) that ensures rapid convergence. The authors (i) give a sparsity-preserving Euclidean projection using a dual formulation with masked spectral-norm constraints, (ii) consider several divergences for projection (Euclidean, KL(θ||ψ), a piecewise KL based on tractable subgraphs, and KL(ψ||θ)), and (iii) for KL(ψ||θ) propose a stochastic projected gradient method that alternates sampling from the current fast-mixing ψ with Euclidean projections. Empirically, they compare projected models plus Gibbs against standard variational methods (MF, LBP, TRW) and Gibbs on the original parameters.

The work sits at an interesting intersection of variational approximations (MF, TRW, structured MF), mixing-time analysis (Dobrushin-type and spectral-norm sufficient conditions), and stochastic optimization with MCMC-based gradients (e.g., Ergodic Mirror Descent). It is also related to algorithmic alternatives that accelerate mixing without parameter projection, such as blocked Gibbs, tree sampling, and Swendsen–Wang.

Strengths
- Clear and timely motivation: biasing parameters toward a provably fast-mixing set to trade a controlled approximation for faster MCMC convergence.
- Novel projection viewpoint: casting “fast mixing” as a tractable family and projecting under different divergences; the connection that MF is a degenerate case (zero dependency) is insightful.
- The sparsity-preserving Euclidean projection via a dual that enforces a masked spectral-norm constraint is technically interesting.
- The stochastic projected-gradient idea for KL(ψ||θ) that interleaves sampling and projection is a promising hybrid of deterministic and stochastic inference.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Dual of the projection: The description of Theorem 7 is difficult to follow. The definition of g(Λ, M), the role of D = Πc[R + M − Λ ∘ Z], and the gradients need to be derived or at least sketched in the main text. As written, signs and scaling (e.g., the “1/||D−R||^2_F” term) look atypical for a Lagrangian dual of a squared-error projection. Please:
  - Provide a clear primal, Lagrangian, KKT conditions, and derivation showing why Z ∘ D = 0 plus D = |B| implies Z ∘ B = 0 and equivalence to the original constrained problem.
  - Explain how sparsity is preserved in B even though the spectral projection is applied to R + M − Λ ∘ Z.
  - State existence/uniqueness conditions and complexity of solving the dual with L-BFGS-B.
- On the constraint Z ∘ D = 0 and the “same space”: Because D = |B|, Z ∘ D = 0 implies Bij = 0 for all non-edges (since |Bij| = 0 ⇒ Bij = 0), so the sparsity pattern is preserved. However, the feasible set is strictly smaller than “all sparse Ising models” because of the additional spectral-norm constraint on |B|. Please clarify this point in the text and discuss whether the projection can inadvertently eliminate sign structure (e.g., shrink contrasting signs differently) and whether any sparse B satisfying || |B| ||2 ≤ c is reachable by your dual parameterization.
- Overall algorithm is ambiguous: Do you (a) fully solve the projection under a chosen divergence and then run Gibbs, or (b) interleave gradient steps, Euclidean projections, and brief Gibbs updates? Section 6 mixes both descriptions. An algorithm box is needed that lists inputs, divergence choice, initialization, the per-iteration sequence (sampling, gradient estimate, step size, Euclidean projection, stopping criteria), and the final sampling procedure.
- Convergence guarantees: The projected-gradient procedure cites Ergodic Mirror Descent for asymptotic guarantees, but the experiments use fixed step sizes, a fixed pool, and one Gibbs sweep per iteration. Under these choices, convergence is not guaranteed. Please either (i) give a theorem with conditions you enforce (diminishing steps, increasing MCMC updates, bounded variance) or (ii) frame the method as a heuristic and report stability diagnostics (objective traces, gradient norms, sensitivity to hyperparameters).
- Use of a spectral bound c = 2.5: This contradicts the stated sufficient condition ||R||2 < 1. If you deliberately relax the bound, please quantify observed mixing (autocorrelation times, effective sample sizes, or coupling diagnostics) to support the claim that chains still mix rapidly. Otherwise the fast-mixing guarantee is void.
- Experimental design and reporting:
  - Iteration-based comparisons are unfair: comparing 30K Gibbs iterations to deterministic methods by iteration count biases against deterministics and ignores the (potentially heavy) cost of repeated projections. Please report wall-clock time, and include projection time in budgets.
  - No timing comparisons: Without runtimes, it is unclear whether the projection+Gibbs pipeline is practically advantageous over well-tuned Gibbs (or alternative samplers).
  - Error bars and statistical tests are missing: With 50 trials, show means ± standard errors and report whether differences are significant.
  - Missing baselines: Include blocked Gibbs, tree sampling, Swendsen–Wang, and at least a representative lifted sampler (e.g., Venugopal & Gogate, AAAI’12), even if the latter applies only to certain symmetries, to contextualize speed/accuracy gains against stronger MCMC baselines.
  - Additional diagnostics: On small graphs where KL(θ||ψ) is tractable, compare your stochastic KL(ψ||θ) projection to the exact KL(θ||ψ) projection to quantify approximation quality; provide ablations for divergence choices; and show iteration traces (objective, marginal error).
- Reproducibility details: Provide precise stopping criteria, step-size schedules, initialization, and the spectral-norm projection tolerance; release code if possible.

Quality
Technically promising, but the dual derivation and convergence story need to be tightened, and the empirical evaluation currently does not substantiate practical gains due to missing runtimes, baselines, and error bars.

Clarity
Writing is generally readable, but key parts (dual, algorithmic workflow) are unclear. An algorithm box and a clearer explanation of constraints would help considerably.

Originality
The projection-to-fast-mixing idea and its coupling with stochastic gradients under KL(ψ||θ) are original and under-explored relative to prior NIPS work on MF/TRW/BP and mixing-time analyses.

Significance
Potentially significant if shown to deliver better accuracy-time tradeoffs than strong MCMC baselines and if the method scales with clear guarantees. As presented, significance is limited by experimental evidence.

Arguments for acceptance
- Interesting, novel angle: “fast-mixing” as a tractable family; hybrid deterministic–stochastic inference.
- Useful projection machinery with sparsity preservation; potential impact on MCMC-based learning/inference.

Arguments against acceptance
- Ambiguous method description; unclear dual derivation.
- No convergence guarantees under used schedules; relaxed spectral bound undermines guarantees.
- Empirical evaluation lacks timing, error bars, and strong MCMC baselines; 30K-iteration comparisons are not compelling.

Overall assessment
Promising idea and direction, but the paper needs clearer method development (derivations, algorithm specification, convergence) and stronger, fairer experiments (timings, error bars, stronger baselines). I lean toward rejection in its current form, but would be positive on a revision addressing the above.
