This paper introduces a novel framework for approximate inference in Ising models by defining a tractable set of distributions based on MCMC mixing time rather than graph structure. The core idea is to approximate a given slow-mixing Ising model by projecting its parameters onto the set of "fast-mixing" models. A model is deemed fast-mixing if the spectral norm of the matrix of its absolute interaction strengths is below a certain threshold (here, less than 1), which provides a sufficient condition for rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling. The authors develop methods to perform this projection while preserving the original graph's sparsity.

The paper explores four different divergence measures to define the projection:
1.  **Euclidean distance:** A dual algorithm based on L-BFGS-B is derived to find the closest fast-mixing parameter set in the L2 sense.
2.  **"Zero-avoiding" KL divergence (KL(θ||ψ)):** This is of theoretical interest but intractable for slow-mixing models.
3.  **Piecewise KL divergence:** A novel, tractable approximation to the KL divergence based on tractable subgraphs.
4.  **"Zero-forcing" KL divergence (KL(ψ||θ)):** This is minimized using a projected stochastic gradient descent approach, where gradients are estimated by running Gibbs sampling on the (fast-mixing) projected model.

The proposed methods are evaluated on 8x8 grids and small random graphs. They are compared against standard approximate inference algorithms like Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), Naive Mean Field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original, unmodified parameters.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel conceptual contribution. Defining tractability in terms of fast-mixing parameters, rather than structural properties like low treewidth, is a fresh and compelling perspective. It opens up a new avenue for research in approximate inference that connects MCMC theory with variational methods.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the dual algorithm for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a solid contribution. The use of a projected stochastic gradient framework to handle KL divergences is well-motivated and appears to be implemented correctly.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing on the tested models. The proposed projection methods, especially the one minimizing the reversed KL divergence, consistently outperform strong baselines like LBP, TRW, and MF in terms of marginal accuracy. Crucially, the experiments demonstrate a clear trade-off: for a limited time budget, running Gibbs on the projected fast-mixing model yields more accurate marginals than running it on the original slow-mixing model. This highlights the practical utility of the approach in time-constrained settings.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Clarification:**

*   **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The main weakness concerns the practical utility for large-scale problems. The projection step itself can be computationally expensive. The reversed KL projection, which performs best, requires an iterative optimization procedure that runs MCMC sampling in its inner loop. The experimental results, while strong, are on relatively small graphs (10 nodes and 64 nodes). It is unclear how the method's runtime and approximation quality scale to larger, more complex models. The reporting of computational effort in the paper is also unclear, making it difficult to assess the overall efficiency.
*   **Looseness of the Bound:** As the authors commendably acknowledge, the spectral norm bound used to guarantee fast mixing is known to be conservative. This looseness may unnecessarily restrict the space of approximating distributions, potentially limiting the accuracy of the final projection. While the authors experiment with a looser bound in practice, this highlights a gap between the theory and the best practical implementation.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Algorithmic Clarity:** In Section 5.1, the projected gradient descent method is described as iterating between a gradient step and a Euclidean projection. Since the Euclidean projection itself is solved via an iterative L-BFGS-B algorithm (Theorem 7), this implies a nested loop structure. Could you please clarify the stopping criteria used for the inner L-BFGS-B optimization within each step of the outer projected gradient descent?

2.  **Experimental Clarity:** The x-axis in Figure 2 is labeled "number of samples," which is confusing since sampling is only explicitly part of the optimization for the KL(ψ||θ) method. This axis appears to represent runtime or computational effort. To allow for a fair comparison of the total computational budget, could you please clarify the meaning of this axis and provide absolute running times (in seconds) for:
    a) The projection phase of your proposed methods.
    b) The inference runtime for the baselines (LBP, TRW, MF).

3.  **Scalability Experiments:** To better demonstrate the significance of this work, I would strongly encourage you to test its scalability on larger models. For example, evaluating on some of the tractable but high-treewidth grid or random-field models used in challenges like the PASCAL Inference Challenge (e.g., models from Flach et al.) would provide a much stronger case for the practical applicability of your approach.

4.  **Minor Textual Suggestions:**
    *   Line 222: "projection onto the tree" might be clearer as "projection onto the graph T" since `T` is not necessarily a tree.
    *   Line 226: Since the piecewise KL divergence is a `max` over several KL divergences, the objective is not necessarily differentiable. "Subgradient" would be a more precise term than "derivative" in this context.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and significant idea for approximate inference. The technical work is solid, and the empirical results are convincing for the problems tested. The main open question is regarding the computational overhead and scalability of the projection step, which I hope the authors can address in their rebuttal. Despite this concern, the originality of the "tractability via fast mixing" concept and the quality of the results make this a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
