This paper proposes a novel approach to inference in Ising models by projecting model parameters onto a set guaranteed to be fast-mixing under Gibbs sampling. The core idea is compelling: rather than attempting exact inference in intractable high-treewidth models, or relying on potentially inaccurate variational approximations, the authors seek a parameter space where MCMC converges rapidly, even if at the cost of some accuracy. This is achieved through projection using various divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL). The experimental results demonstrate that this approach consistently outperforms standard variational methods and, for limited computational budgets, surpasses Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing parameters.

The paper is well-motivated, building on existing work in approximate inference and the theory of mixing times in Markov chains. The authors correctly identify the limitations of approaches like mean-field and loopy belief propagation and position their work as an alternative that leverages the strengths of MCMC while mitigating its drawbacks. The connection to previous work on fast-mixing conditions, particularly the spectral norm of the dependency matrix, is clearly articulated. The derivation of the Euclidean projection algorithm using a dual formulation and L-BFGS-B is technically sound. The exploration of multiple divergence measures provides a valuable comparative analysis.

A significant strength of the work lies in its empirical validation. The experimental setup, inspired by Hazan and Shashua (2012), is well-defined and provides a fair comparison between the proposed methods and existing alternatives. The consistent outperformance of the projected models, especially the reversed KL divergence approach, is a convincing demonstration of the technique’s effectiveness. The authors also acknowledge the trade-offs involved, recognizing that Gibbs sampling on the original parameters will ultimately be more accurate given sufficient time.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the theoretical foundation is solid, the practical implications of the proposed approach are somewhat unclear. The experiments are conducted on relatively small models (8x8 grids and 10-node random graphs). Scaling this approach to larger, more realistic problems remains an open question. The computational cost of the projection step, especially with the reversed KL divergence, is a concern. The paper mentions "intensive tuning" of parameters for the stochastic gradient descent in section 6.1 but doesn’t fully detail the parameters chosen.

Further, the description of the projected-gradient algorithm in Section 5.1 lacks clarity. Specifically, the nested loops involving the inner L-BFGS-B optimization require a clearer specification of the stopping criteria. What conditions trigger the termination of the inner L-BFGS-B loop? This detail is crucial for understanding the computational complexity and overall efficiency of the algorithm.

The labeling of the x-axis in Figure 2 as "number of samples" is misleading, as sampling is primarily used in the reversed KL divergence calculation. It is likely that the x-axis represents runtime, but this should be explicitly stated. Providing absolute running times in seconds for all algorithms (including LBP, TRW, and MF) would allow for a more comprehensive and fair comparison.  Including a discussion of the computational cost of each method would be valuable.

I recommend the following minor edits: Replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T" on line 222 for clarity, and use "subgradient" instead of "derivative" on line 226 as the algorithm generally uses a subgradient.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to approximate inference.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods.
*   Sound theoretical foundation based on the theory of fast-mixing Markov chains.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Scalability to larger models is uncertain.
*   Computational cost of the projection step, particularly for the reversed KL divergence, is a concern.
*   Lack of clarity regarding the stopping criteria for the inner L-BFGS-B loop in Section 5.1.
*   The x-axis labeling in Figure 2 is misleading.



**Overall assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of approximate inference. The proposed approach is innovative, well-supported by theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, and addresses a challenging problem in probabilistic modeling. While some concerns remain regarding scalability and computational cost, the potential benefits of this technique warrant its acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns raised regarding the computational details and scalability in a rebuttal. Specifically, clarification on the stopping criteria for the inner loop and reporting of absolute runtimes are crucial. I also encourage the authors to address the scalability concerns, perhaps by including experiments on larger models or discussing potential optimizations.
