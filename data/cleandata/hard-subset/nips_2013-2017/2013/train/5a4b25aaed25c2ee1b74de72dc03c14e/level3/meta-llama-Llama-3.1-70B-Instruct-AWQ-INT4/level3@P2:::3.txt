This paper proposes a novel approach to inference in high-treewidth graphical models by projecting the model onto a set of fast-mixing models, which are guaranteed to have rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling. The authors define "fast-mixing" models as those with parameter-space conditions that ensure rapid mixing, specifically that the spectral norm of the matrix of interaction strengths is less than one. This approach is distinct from traditional approximations based on tractable families, such as mean-field or structured mean-field.

The authors provide a thorough background on mixing times in Markov chains and the dependency matrix, which is used to define the fast-mixing condition. They then derive a projection algorithm for the Ising model, which is the primary focus of the paper. The algorithm is based on a dual method that enforces the graph structure and ensures that the projected matrix is sparse. The authors also consider various divergences, including Euclidean distance, KL-divergence, piecewise KL-divergence, and reversed KL-divergence.

The experimental evaluation compares the proposed methods to several standard variational methods, including loopy belief propagation, tree-reweighted belief propagation, naive mean-field, and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters. The results show that the proposed methods consistently outperform the variational methods in terms of accuracy, and for reasonable runtimes, they also beat Gibbs sampling on the original model. The reversed KL-divergence performs best among the proposed methods.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and the underlying theory. The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the results are convincing. However, there are some minor issues that need to be addressed. Specifically, the authors should clarify the stopping criteria for the inner iterations of the projected-gradient algorithm in Section 5.1. Additionally, the x-axis label in Figure 2 should be changed to "runtime" instead of "number of samples," and the authors should provide absolute running times in seconds for a fair comparison with other methods.

One potential limitation of the proposed approach is its practical utility, which may be limited by high runtime. The authors should provide more information on the computational complexity of their methods and how they scale to larger models. Including experiments on larger models, such as tractable random-field models, would be beneficial in assessing the scalability of the approach.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel approach to inference in high-treewidth graphical models, which is distinct from traditional approximations. The use of fast-mixing models as a projection target is a new idea, and the authors provide a thorough theoretical foundation for their approach.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve the accuracy and efficiency of inference in high-treewidth graphical models. The proposed approach could be useful in a variety of applications, including computer vision, natural language processing, and computational biology.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the results are convincing. With some minor revisions to address the issues mentioned above, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
