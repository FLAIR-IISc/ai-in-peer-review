- Proposes projecting high-treewidth graphical models onto a set of fast-mixing models, defining fast-mixing via rapid Gibbs convergence and deriving a condition for Ising models.  
- Introduces several projection methods using different divergences between Gibbs distributions.  
- Paper is well-written with a clear explanation of the approach.  
- Thorough literature review linking the dependency matrix spectral norm to mixing time.  
- Experimental results are convincing and show accuracy improvements over LBP and TRW.  
- Methods can be more efficient than Gibbs on the original model when interactions are strong.  
- Main strength: provides a new perspective on approximate inference with empirical performance gains.  
- No clear stopping criterion for inner iterations of the projected-gradient algorithm (Section 5.1).  
- Figure 2 x-axis labeled "number of samples" is unclear, since sampling is only used for reversed KL projection.  
- Should report absolute running times (seconds) and compare runtimes with LBP, TRW, and MF.  
- Practical utility may be limited by high runtime; paper lacks a clear estimate of computational cost.  
- Scalability to larger models is unclear; needs experiments on larger/tractable random-field models.  
- Minor edits suggested: replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T" and use "subgradient" instead of "derivative."  
- Overall: technically sound, original, and potentially significant, but practical runtime/scalability concerns need addressing.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, solid theory review, motivated projection methods, and convincing empirical gains.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear stopping rule, figure labeling issues, high runtime, missing cost estimate, and unclear scalability.
