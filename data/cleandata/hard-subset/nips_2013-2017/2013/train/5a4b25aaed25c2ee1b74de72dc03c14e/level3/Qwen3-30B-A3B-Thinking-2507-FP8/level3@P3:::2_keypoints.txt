- Introduces projecting Ising model parameters onto a set that guarantees fast Gibbs mixing via the Dobrushin criterion.  
- Proposes optimizing projections under multiple divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL).  
- Extends tractable approximation classes to parameter sets ensuring rapid Gibbs convergence, beyond tree-structured/low-treewidth methods.  
- Theoretical foundations linking parameter constraints to mixing times are solid and build on prior work.  
- The Euclidean projection algorithm (Theorem 7) is well-motivated but the dual formulation is confusing and under-explained.  
- Combining stochastic (Gibbs sampling) and deterministic inference for reversed KL under a fast-mixing constraint is conceptually interesting and novel.  
- Strength: explores multiple divergence measures showing different optimization approaches for the same problem.  
- Strength: core idea of fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractable approximation class is novel and potentially impactful.  
- Major clarity issues undermine the contribution—critical technical points are unclearly explained.  
- The constraint zij * dij = 0 is not justified or explained in terms of preserving the parameter space.  
- Unclear whether projection is fully optimized before sampling or interleaved with sampling; algorithmic procedure is missing.  
- Requires an algorithm box to make the procedure reproducible.  
- Experimental choices (30,000 Gibbs iterations for projection) appear arbitrary and bias comparisons against deterministic methods.  
- No timing comparisons reported, so practical benefit versus computational cost is unclear.  
- Plots lack error bars, preventing assessment of statistical significance.  
- Fails to compare with state-of-the-art sampling methods (e.g., blocked Gibbs, Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs), leaving practical value unproven.  
- No theoretical convergence guarantees provided for the projected gradient descent strategy.  
- The spectral norm bound used is conservative (≈1.76 worse than known thresholds for 2D Ising), and this limitation is not adequately addressed.  
- Overall: sound theory but unconvincing experiments and unclear presentation; needs clearer algorithms, fairer/complete experiments, and addressing of theoretical limits.  
- Pro for acceptance: promising, theoretically grounded idea combining stochastic and deterministic inference.  
- Con for acceptance: weak experimental rigor, missing comparisons and timing, and critical technical details left unexplained.
