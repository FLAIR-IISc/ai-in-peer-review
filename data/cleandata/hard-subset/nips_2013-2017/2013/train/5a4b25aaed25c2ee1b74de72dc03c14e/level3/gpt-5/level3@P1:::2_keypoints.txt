- Proposal: project intractable Ising parameters onto a fast-mixing subset to guarantee rapid single-site Gibbs sampling via a spectral-norm dependency bound.
- Fast-mixing criterion: use sufficient condition ||R||2 < 1 (spectral norm of absolute pairwise interaction matrix) to ensure rapid mixing.
- Projection objectives: study Euclidean, inclusive KL (q||p), piecewise KL on low-treewidth subgraphs, and exclusive KL (p||q) optimized by stochastic approximation.
- Euclidean projection method: derive a dual formulation that preserves sparsity and enforces the spectral-norm constraint via iterative singular-value thresholding.
- Experiments: projected fast-mixing models yield more accurate marginals under limited sampling budgets; KL(p||q) projections outperform piecewise KL and Euclidean.
- Relation to prior work: reframes tractable-family approximations from structural to dynamical tractability; connects to Dobrushin-type conditions, mean-field, and MCMC-based learning methods.
- Technical soundness: spectral-norm condition and projection approaches are well-grounded, and stochastic approximation follows standard practice.
- Unclear dual derivation: Theorem 7 and roles of M and Λ, projection Πc[·], and sparsity preservation need clearer, stepwise explanation and algorithmic summary.
- Sparsity constraint clarification: explicitly state that Z ∘ D = 0 preserves the original edge set (forces B_ij = 0 on non-edges) and does not further shrink the parameter space.
- Ambiguous optimization procedure: unclear whether gradient steps and Euclidean projections are interleaved every iteration or solved occasionally—needs an explicit algorithm box with inputs and stopping criteria.
- Convergence guarantees: must state assumptions (step-size, MCMC steps per iteration, boundedness) under which the projected stochastic scheme converges and whether projection preserves EMD conditions.
- Spectral-norm inconsistency: using c = 2.5 in experiments contradicts the <1 sufficient condition and removes formal guarantees; experiments must either use valid bounds or empirically validate mixing.
- Clarity issues: expand dual derivation with intuition and proof sketch, define all notation locally (e.g., triple dot product) and variable dimensions.
- Algorithmic details missing: provide explicit algorithms for each projection objective, sampling/projection schedules, sample-pool maintenance, and stopping criteria.
- Complexity reporting: discuss computational complexity per iteration and overall, including costs of repeated SVDs and LBFGS-B.
- Originality: novel framing of fast-mixing as a tractable family and use of spectral-norm constraints with projection-based approximations.
- Significance: could bridge MCMC and variational methods if validated, but current empirical support is insufficient to judge practical impact.
- Experimental shortcomings: comparisons ignore projection overhead and lack wall-clock or flop-normalized budgets; projection costs can be substantial.
- Statistical reporting: add error bars/confidence intervals and statistical tests across trials; current plots lack uncertainty measures.
- Mixing diagnostics missing: report ESS/sec, integrated autocorrelation times, R-hat, and other diagnostics for both projected and original models.
- Budget sensitivity: choice of 30k iterations is arbitrary—include time–accuracy curves and budget sweeps instead of fixed iteration counts.
- Missing baselines: include stronger MCMC baselines (blocked Gibbs, Swendsen–Wang, lifted/tree samplers) to contextualize single-site Gibbs guarantees.
- Sensitivity analyses needed: study effects of sample-pool size, step size, number of iterations, spectral-norm bound c, attractive vs mixed interactions, and graph types.
- Node fields: clarify whether node fields are projected or unconstrained and analyze their effect on accuracy.
- Pros for acceptance: conceptually appealing, solid theoretical grounding for spectral-norm condition, novel dual projection preserving sparsity, and a principled stochastic optimization for KL(p||q).
- Cons for acceptance: methodological ambiguities, weak empirical evaluation (time accounting, uncertainty, baselines), and use of spectral bounds that break guarantees.
- Suggested fixes: provide algorithm boxes, clarify and tighten dual derivation, respect theoretical bounds or justify larger c with diagnostics, add runtime comparisons and stronger baselines, and extend discussion to general MRFs.
- Overall: promising and novel idea but requires clearer method exposition and substantially stronger, fairer empirical evidence to demonstrate practical value.
