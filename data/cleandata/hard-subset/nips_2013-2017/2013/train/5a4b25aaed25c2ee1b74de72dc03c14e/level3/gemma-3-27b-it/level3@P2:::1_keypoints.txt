- Novelty: Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set to accelerate Gibbs sampling, a compelling and seemingly novel preprocessing idea.
- Theoretical foundation: Builds on established mixing-time and spectral-norm results to justify the projection approach.
- Experimental validation: Small-model experiments show the projected-parameter methods can outperform standard variational inference and original-parameter Gibbs sampling under limited compute.
- Multiple divergences: Evaluates Euclidean, piecewise KL, and reversed KL projections, highlighting trade-offs between accuracy and computational feasibility.
- Scalability concerns: Experiments are limited to small models, leaving unclear whether benefits and projection costs scale to realistic problem sizes.
- Projection cost omission: Experimental timings appear to exclude the projection computation, biasing results and undermining fairness of comparisons.
- Limited generalizability: Analysis and bounds focus on binary variables; extension to non-binary states is nontrivial and not addressed.
- Clarity and technical detail gaps: Several "standard results" lack references or proofs; the dependency matrix maximization and tractability are insufficiently explained.
- Difficult projection algorithm presentation: Section 4 is hard to follow due to unclear notation and lack of intuitive explanation.
- Notational inconsistency: KL(q||p) is used in a nonstandard order; notation should be corrected for consistency.
- Experimental design limitations: Evaluation lacks larger/realistic models and stronger ground-truth baselines (e.g., symmetric models or long-chain sampling).
- Missing comparisons: The potential of minimizing KL(θ||ψ) using exact inference on toy models (e.g., via junction tree) is not explored.
- Unexplained error gaps: Large performance gap between mean-field (reverse KL) and reverse-KL projection is reported but not analyzed.
- Argument for acceptance: Introduces a theoretically motivated, novel approach with promising preliminary empirical results.
- Argument for acceptance: Exploration of multiple divergence measures shows thoughtful consideration of practical trade-offs.
- Argument for acceptance: The idea could be valuable if scalability and evaluation issues are resolved.
- Argument against acceptance: Scalability and practical cost of the projection step are questionable and not adequately addressed.
- Argument against acceptance: Experimental evaluation is incomplete and potentially biased due to omitted projection runtimes.
- Argument against acceptance: Paper needs clearer technical exposition, proofs, and tractable treatment of dependency maximization.
- Overall assessment: Interesting and well-motivated work but with serious concerns on scalability, evaluation completeness, and clarity; recommend rejection pending major revisions.
