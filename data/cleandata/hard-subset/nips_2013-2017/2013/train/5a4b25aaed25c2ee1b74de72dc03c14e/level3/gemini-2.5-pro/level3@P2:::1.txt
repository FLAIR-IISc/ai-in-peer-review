This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models where Gibbs sampling mixes slowly due to strong interactions. The central idea is to project the parameters of a given slow-mixing model onto a nearby set of parameters that are guaranteed to be "fast-mixing." The condition for fast mixing is derived from prior work [4, 8], which bounds the MCMC mixing time based on the spectral norm of the dependency matrix, which in turn is bounded by the interaction strength matrix. The authors present methods for this projection under four different divergences: Euclidean distance, the "zero-avoiding" KL-divergence (KL(θ||ψ)), a novel tractable "piecewise" KL-divergence, and the "zero-forcing" KL-divergence (KL(ψ||θ)). The latter is optimized using a stochastic gradient method. Experiments on small grid and random graphs show that, for a fixed number of sampling iterations, Gibbs sampling on the projected parameters can yield more accurate marginal estimates than both standard variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) and Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing parameters.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core concept of defining a tractable family of distributions based on fast MCMC mixing, rather than graphical structure (e.g., low treewidth), is a creative and potentially significant contribution to the field of approximate inference. It addresses the fundamental challenge of MCMC convergence and reframes it as an approximation problem. If this approach can be scaled, it offers a principled way to trade asymptotic accuracy for guaranteed rapid convergence.

2.  **Technical Contributions:** The paper develops specific algorithms to perform the proposed projection. The derivation of a dual method for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) and the application of stochastic projected gradient descent for the reverse KL-divergence are solid technical contributions that make the conceptual idea concrete.

3.  **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the problem and framing its solution within the broader context of approximate inference methods like mean-field and belief propagation.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental validation has several critical weaknesses that undermine the paper's conclusions.
    *   **Unaccounted Computational Cost:** The primary claim is that the proposed method is more accurate for a given computational budget. However, the experiments (e.g., Figure 2) appear to only measure the time for Gibbs sampling *after* the projection is complete. The projection itself, especially for the KL-divergence methods which require thousands of Gibbs iterations within a stochastic optimization loop, is computationally expensive. Failing to include this significant cost in the timing comparisons presents a biased and potentially misleading picture of the method's efficiency.
    *   **Scalability Concerns:** The experiments are conducted on very small models (10-node random graphs and 8x8 grids). The performance benefits observed may not generalize to larger, more realistic problems. The relationship between model size, interaction strength, and the mixing time gap is complex, and the small scale of the experiments does not provide sufficient evidence of the method's utility on problems where approximate inference is truly necessary.
    *   **Missing Baselines and Analysis:** The authors could have strengthened their case by including an analysis of the "zero-avoiding" KL(θ||ψ) projection on these small models, where exact marginals can be computed with the junction tree algorithm. This would serve as a useful "best-case" benchmark for projection-based methods. Furthermore, the paper notes that mean-field is a special case of the reverse-KL projection (with zero spectral norm), yet the large performance gap between the two is not discussed or explained.

2.  **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper suffers from several clarity issues that make it difficult to fully assess the technical details.
    *   Section 4 is particularly difficult to follow. The notation (g, M, Λ) is introduced abruptly, and the connection between the primal and dual formulations in Theorem 7 is not well-explained.
    *   Several key results are presented as "standard" without adequate citation or proof. For instance, Theorem 6 (projection onto the spectral norm ball) and Lemma 5 (bound on the dependency matrix for Ising models) should be accompanied by a proper reference or a proof sketch in the appendix.
    *   The use of `KL(q||p)` in the introduction seems to reverse the conventional roles of the true distribution (q) and the approximation (p), which could confuse readers.

3.  **Limited Generalizability:** The presented fast-mixing bounds and projection algorithms are specific to pairwise binary Ising models. The authors acknowledge that extending this to general discrete Markov random fields is an open challenge. This limitation currently narrows the practical impact of the work.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Presents a highly novel and elegant idea for approximate inference by connecting it to MCMC mixing times.
*   The conceptual reframing of "tractability" is thought-provoking and could inspire future research.
*   The paper includes sound technical derivations for the proposed projection algorithms.

### Arguments for Rejection:
*   The experimental evaluation is critically flawed due to the omission of the projection cost, which is central to the paper's claims about efficiency.
*   Experiments are limited to small-scale models, and the scalability of the approach is not established.
*   Significant clarity issues in key technical sections (especially Section 4) and missing proofs/citations for important theorems detract from the paper's quality.

### Summary and Recommendation

This paper introduces a fascinating and original idea: performing approximate inference by projecting a slow-mixing model onto a nearby fast-mixing one. However, the execution falls short of the standard required for publication at NIPS. The experimental evaluation, which is crucial for a paper making claims about computational trade-offs, is fundamentally flawed by not accounting for the cost of the projection step itself. Combined with significant clarity issues in the technical sections and a lack of evidence for scalability, the paper in its current form is not convincing. While the core idea is excellent, the weaknesses in its presentation and validation are too substantial to overlook. I would encourage the authors to thoroughly revise their experimental comparisons and improve the clarity of the manuscript for a future submission.
