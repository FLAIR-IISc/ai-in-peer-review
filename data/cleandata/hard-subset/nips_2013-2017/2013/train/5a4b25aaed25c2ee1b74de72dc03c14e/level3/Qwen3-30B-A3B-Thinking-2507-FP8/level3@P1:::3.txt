

This paper introduces a novel approach to inference in high-treewidth Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set defined by spectral norm constraints, rather than using traditional variational approximations. The authors define fast-mixing as satisfying ||R||₂ < 1, where R is the dependency matrix, which for Ising models translates to the spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix being less than 1. This represents a meaningful shift from the standard "tractable" definition (tree-structured or low-treewidth), as the authors argue that some high-treewidth models can still be efficiently sampled from if parameter constraints guarantee rapid mixing.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it develops projection algorithms under multiple divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL), extending beyond the standard variational inference framework. The Euclidean projection is solved via a dual optimization approach using L-BFGS-B, while the reversed KL divergence requires a projected gradient descent algorithm with Gibbs sampling inside the projection loop. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the mixing time bound, relating to existing work on rapid mixing (e.g., Hayes [8], Dyer et al. [4]).

The experimental section is well-designed, comparing the proposed methods against standard inference techniques (LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on original parameters) on small grid and random graphs. The results consistently show that the proposed methods outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in marginal accuracy, and for reasonable runtimes, they also beat Gibbs sampling on the original model. Notably, the reversed KL divergence approach performs best among the proposed methods, which aligns with the authors' theoretical arguments. The paper also provides valuable insight into the trade-off between mixing time and approximation accuracy.

Strengths of the paper include:
- Clear theoretical foundation for the fast-mixing condition
- Novel projection framework that extends beyond traditional variational methods
- Comprehensive experimental comparison that demonstrates practical utility
- Well-motivated approach that addresses a real problem in inference for Ising models

However, several weaknesses require attention. The spectral norm bound appears quite conservative (e.g., for 2D Ising models, the bound is ~1.76x looser than the known critical threshold), which limits the practical applicability of the method. While the authors note they use a bound of 2.5 in practice, this should be better justified in the paper. The computational cost of the reversed KL projection is significant, as it requires running Gibbs sampling during the projection procedure, and the paper doesn't provide absolute runtime measurements for fair comparison with other methods. The x-axis in Figure 2 labeled "number of samples" is confusing, as sampling is only used in the reversed KL method; the authors should clarify whether this represents runtime or samples, and provide absolute running times for all methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with good technical detail. However, some minor revisions would improve clarity: replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T" (line 222), and use "subgradient" instead of "derivative" (line 226). The authors should also clarify the stopping criteria for the nested L-BFGS-B optimization in the projected-gradient algorithm.

In terms of originality, this work distinguishes itself from previous NIPS papers on approximate inference (e.g., [14, 16, 19, 20, 21]) by focusing on parameter-space constraints rather than structural approximations. While the core idea of using mixing time bounds for inference has been explored (e.g., [6]), this paper provides a systematic framework for projecting onto fast-mixing sets under multiple divergences.

The significance is strong: the authors demonstrate a practical approach to inference in models where traditional Gibbs sampling would be prohibitively slow. The method provides a useful alternative to variational methods, particularly when finite-time inference is required. However, the current experimental scope is limited to small graphs (max 10 nodes), and the paper would benefit from scalability experiments on larger models (e.g., using the tractable random-field models from Flach et al. [22]).

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with interesting theoretical insights and convincing empirical results. The method addresses a meaningful problem in graphical models, and the results suggest practical utility for inference when time is limited. The main concern is the conservative spectral bound and the lack of absolute runtime comparisons. If these issues are addressed in the revision, this paper would make a valuable contribution to NIPS. The authors should clarify the computational cost of their approach relative to standard methods, as this will be crucial for adoption in practice. The paper's contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance, but the practical utility would be strengthened by addressing the runtime concerns.
