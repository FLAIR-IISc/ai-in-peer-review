This paper proposes using “fast-mixing” Ising models as the tractable family for approximate inference: given slow-mixing parameters θ, project to nearby parameters ψ that satisfy a sufficient spectral-norm condition for rapid mixing of single-site Gibbs. The key technical ingredients are: (i) bounding the dependency matrix R for Ising by Rij ≤ tanh|βij|, yielding the sufficient condition ||R||2 < 1; (ii) algorithms to compute projections under several divergences D(θ,ψ): Euclidean distance in parameter space via a dual that combines singular-value clipping with a sparsity mask; piecewise KL based on exact KL over tractable subgraphs; inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ) (theoretical only); and reverse KL KL(ψ||θ) optimized by stochastic approximation using samples from ψ, with Euclidean projection steps onto the fast-mixing set. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare projected models (sampled with Gibbs) to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original parameters. In regimes with strong couplings and limited sampling time, projection to fast-mixing parameters often yields more accurate marginals than the baselines. The approach connects to classical mixing-time bounds [4,8], variational inference (MF/TRW/BP), and planar exact inference [6].

Quality
- Technical soundness: The use of the Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum/ Hayes conditions and the bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is appropriate for Ising, and the spectral-norm criterion ||R||2 < 1 is a valid sufficient condition for rapid mixing. The Euclidean projection is reduced to singular-value clipping with sparsity constraints via a dual solved with L-BFGS-B; the reverse-KL algorithm is a stochastic projected method reminiscent of Ergodic Mirror Descent [3]. However:
  - Theoretical guarantees are undermined in practice by using a spectral bound of 2.5, far above the proven threshold (<1). This weakens the central “guaranteed fast-mixing” claim and should be explicitly framed as heuristic.
  - Theorem 6 (projection onto a spectral-norm ball by singular value thresholding) is treated as “standard” but needs a precise citation (e.g., Golub & Van Loan; convex projection onto operator-norm ball). Lemma 5 is asserted with only a pointer; a proof sketch or citation specific to general fields (not only zero-field) is needed.
  - The dependency matrix R is defined via a max over boundary configurations. In practice the paper operates on the upper bound R ≤ |β| (tanh(|β|)), but the distinction and computational tractability should be clearly stated up front.
  - The gradient estimator for KL(ψ||θ) (Eq. 8) relies on sampling from ψ; using the same samples to estimate μ and the outer expectation introduces bias. This should be acknowledged with either a correction (independent batches) or empirical evidence that the bias is negligible.

Clarity
- Section 4 is difficult to follow. The optimization in Eq. (1), the role of Z as a sparsity mask, and the dual variables (Λ, M) merit a clearer derivation, intuitive explanation, and consistent notation. A short algorithm box (inputs, outputs, complexity per iteration) would help.
- KL notation flips p and q roles at points in the intro; please enforce a consistent convention throughout.
- Several “standard results” are used without adequate referencing or proof sketches. At minimum, add (i) a clear reference for the operator-norm projection; (ii) a proof sketch or appendix for Lemma 5 in the presence of fields; and (iii) a brief recap of the mixing-time bound for systematic vs random scan [4,8].
- The paper should state whether β is constrained to be symmetric, with zero diagonal, and how symmetry is enforced by the projection.

Originality
- The central idea—projecting to the nearest member of a fast-mixing family for approximate inference—appears novel and complements prior tractable-family approximations (MF, structured MF [20], LBP/TRW [19,21], planar exact [6]). The reverse-KL version can be viewed as a principled generalization of mean field (MF is the degenerate case ||R||2=0).
- The piecewise KL construction extends “piecewise likelihood” ideas [17] from training to projection; this is an interesting, if empirically weaker, alternative.

Significance
- If made reliable and scalable, the approach could provide a practical finite-time alternative to running Gibbs on the original model, and may have impact in learning where MCMC-based likelihood gradients are required.
- Current experiments are limited to small graphs (8×8 grids; 10-node random graphs), and timing comparisons exclude the projection cost, which can be substantial. As presented, significance is promising but not yet demonstrated at scale.

Experimental evaluation
- Finite-time results indicate that projected models can beat LBP/TRW/MF and short-run Gibbs on strong couplings, which supports the thesis.
- However:
  - Projection time is excluded from timing comparisons, biasing in favor of the proposed method. Please report wall-clock budgets including projection, or compare at equal total compute.
  - The decision to use a spectral bound of 2.5 contradicts the theoretical guarantee; include diagnostics that the resulting chains are indeed fast-mixing (e.g., integrated autocorrelation times, Gelman–Rubin, or coupling diagnostics), and ablate performance vs the bound value.
  - Include larger or more realistic instances (e.g., larger grids, random graphs with n≥50), or models with tractable ground truth (planar zero-field Ising using [6]; junction tree on small graphs; very long runs with convergence diagnostics).
  - Explore inclusive KL (θ||ψ) on toy graphs via exact marginals to quantify the potential “best case,” and discuss why reverse-KL projection substantially outperforms MF despite both optimizing KL(ψ||θ).
  - Compare to block Gibbs or tree/block samplers; fast mixing of single-site Gibbs is sufficient but not necessary for these.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, conceptually elegant idea linking mixing-time theory to approximate inference.
- Novel projection formulations (Euclidean dual with sparsity; reverse-KL projected stochastic descent).
- Empirical gains in challenging regimes where standard variational methods struggle.
- Potentially useful for learning with MCMC-in-the-loop under mixing constraints.

Cons for acceptance
- Reliance on a loose sufficient condition; empirical use of bounds that violate the guarantee.
- Clarity and notation issues in the core projection section; missing citations/proofs for “standard” steps.
- Scalability and fairness of comparisons: small graphs; projection cost excluded; limited baselines for samplers.
- Unclear generalization beyond binary Ising; no path sketched for multinomial variables where influence bounds are trickier.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper presents an appealing and potentially impactful direction, but requires stronger empirical validation (including total runtime and larger models), adherence to or transparent relaxation of theoretical guarantees, and improved exposition of the projection algorithm and assumptions. Addressing these issues would make the contribution suitable for NIPS.
