- Introduces projection-based inference for Ising models by projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing model space using a spectral-norm bound while preserving graph structure.  
- Defines fast-mixing models via a spectral norm bound on the Ising edge strength matrix.  
- Proposes projection methods using KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL, and Euclidean distance, implemented with LBFGS-B and projected gradient descent.  
- Reversed KL projection requires running Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model to compute the projection.  
- Experiments on small random models (8×8 grids, 10-node graphs) show the proposed methods outperform TRW, MF, LBP, and Gibbs in accuracy; reversed KL performs best.  
- Methods achieve favorable accuracy–runtime trade-offs compared to Gibbs sampling on the original model.  
- Strength: Novel tractability notion focused on fast-mixing models, offering a fresh approach beyond tree-based approximations.  
- Strength: Theoretical grounding via spectral-norm-based fast-mixing guarantees and dual optimization for Euclidean projection.  
- Strength: Empirical validation convincingly shows improved accuracy on tested cases.  
- Strength: Practical relevance in addressing slow Gibbs convergence for strong interactions.  
- Weakness: High computational cost of projection (e.g., tens of thousands of Gibbs iterations) raises scalability and practical utility concerns.  
- Weakness: Experiments restricted to small graphs; larger-scale evaluation is needed to support broader claims.  
- Weakness: Unclear stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B loop, hindering reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Figure 2’s horizontal axis is ambiguous and absolute runtimes for baseline algorithms are not reported, reducing transparency.  
- Weakness: Spectral norm bound appears overly conservative compared to known Ising thresholds; tighter bounds are needed.  
- Suggestion: Clarify LBFGS-B stopping criteria and provide SGD parameter-tuning details.  
- Suggestion: Include experiments on larger models to demonstrate scalability.  
- Suggestion: Report absolute runtimes for all algorithms and clarify Figure 2’s axis (runtime vs. number of samples).  
- Suggestion: Investigate tighter spectral-norm bounds to improve theoretical and practical applicability.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions if authors address runtime reporting, algorithmic clarity, and expand experimental scope.
