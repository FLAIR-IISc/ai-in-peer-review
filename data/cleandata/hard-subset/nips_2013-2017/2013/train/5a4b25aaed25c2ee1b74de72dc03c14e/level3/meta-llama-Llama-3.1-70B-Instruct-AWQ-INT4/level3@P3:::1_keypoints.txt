- Proposes projecting Ising-model parameters onto a nearby "fast-mixing" model satisfying spectral bounds to speed up Gibbs mixing.
- Uses the dependency matrix R and shows that ||R||2 < 1 implies bounded Gibbs mixing time.
- Introduces several projection methods to find parameters close to the original but with spectral norm < 1.
- Builds heavily on prior rapid-mixing theory while presenting the novel idea of projection as an inference tool.
- Empirical results on small Ising models show better marginal estimates within fixed compute time than variational methods and original-model Gibbs.
- Experiments are limited to small models, leaving scalability to larger models unclear.
- The projection step appears computationally expensive and its cost is not included in timing comparisons, biasing results.
- Theoretical development and claims are sound and supported by analysis and experiments.
- The paper understates weaknesses: the spectral-norm bound is noted as conservative but lacks clear explanation why.
- Writing and organization are generally good, but Section 4 is hard to follow and the notation (e.g., triple dot product) is nonstandard.
- The dependency matrix R needs more discussion; maximizing it raises tractability concerns.
- Originality and significance are high; the approach could influence future work.
- Some empirical phenomena are unexplained, e.g., why piecewise KL underperforms true KL and the large error gap vs reverse-KL in the mean-field case.
- Recommendations: discuss practical implications, address scalability and projection cost, clarify Section 4 and notation, and explain KL/mean-field discrepancies.
- Acceptance arguments: novel approach, strong technical development, and significant potential impact; rejection arguments: limited experiments, unaccounted computational cost, and clarity/notation issues.
