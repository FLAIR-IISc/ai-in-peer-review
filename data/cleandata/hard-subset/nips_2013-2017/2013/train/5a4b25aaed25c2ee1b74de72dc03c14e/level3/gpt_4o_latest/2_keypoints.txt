- Introduces projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set (Dobrushin criterion) to speed up Gibbs sampling via constrained optimization.
- Explores multiple divergences for projection: Euclidean, piecewise KL, and reversed KL, while keeping parameters in the original space.
- Targets high-treewidth graphical models where exact inference is intractable and Gibbs sampling mixes slowly due to strong interactions.
- Novelty: combines stochastic and deterministic inference principles and uses spectral-norm constraints to guarantee rapid mixing.
- Theoretical rigor: provides a dual formulation for Euclidean projection and motivates iterative SVD thresholding for parameter projection.
- Experimental setup: compares proposed method against variational methods (loopy BP, mean-field) and standard Gibbs sampling, showing improved accuracy under limited computation.
- Clarity issue: dual formulation and certain constraints (e.g., z_{ij}d_{ij}=0) are not clearly explained, harming interpretability and reproducibility.
- Algorithm description is ambiguous; interleaving projected gradient updates with Gibbs sampling lacks a clear pseudocode or detailed procedure.
- No formal convergence guarantee is provided for the projected gradient descent strategy, weakening theoretical completeness.
- Experimental concerns: choice of 30K Gibbs iterations is arbitrary and not justified.
- Evaluation metric issue: comparisons should be reported in computational time rather than iterations for practical relevance.
- Missing error bars in results make statistical significance of improvements unclear.
- Projection overhead is not included in comparisons, potentially misrepresenting practicality for larger models.
- Missing relevant baselines, such as blocked Gibbs samplers and Gogate’s "Lifted Gibbs Sampler," limits empirical evaluation.
- Overall: promising and theoretically grounded idea but requires clearer exposition, convergence analysis, and more rigorous experimental validation.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel hybrid approach, theoretical contributions to fast-mixing projection, and promising empirical gains over variational methods.
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear formulations and algorithmic details, missing convergence guarantees, and incomplete experimental evaluation.
- Recommendation: Weak Accept, contingent on addressing clarity, theoretical gaps, and experimental shortcomings.
