## Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

This paper presents a novel approach to inference in Ising models by projecting model parameters onto a space guaranteed to induce fast mixing Markov chains. The core idea – leveraging fast mixing as a proxy for tractability – is interesting and offers a different perspective compared to traditional methods like mean-field or loopy belief propagation. The authors explore several divergence measures (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL) for this projection and demonstrate empirically that their proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and can even surpass Gibbs sampling given limited runtime, with the reversed KL divergence performing best. 

The work builds upon established concepts in Markov chain Monte Carlo and graphical model approximation, referencing relevant literature on mixing times (Dyer et al., Levin & Peres) and inference algorithms (Globerson & Jaakkola, Yedidia et al.). The theoretical foundation, particularly Theorem 7 for the Euclidean projection, is well-developed, and the connection to spectral norm bounds for fast mixing is clearly articulated. The experimental setup, comparing against a range of established inference techniques, is thorough and provides convincing evidence of the method’s effectiveness on small, randomly generated models. 

However, the practical utility of this approach is potentially limited by computational cost. While the paper demonstrates accuracy gains, the runtime required for the projection step, especially with the reversed KL divergence involving Gibbs sampling *within* the projection, is a significant concern. The paper lacks sufficient detail regarding absolute runtimes, making a fair comparison difficult. The x-axis in Figure 2, labeled “number of samples”, is misleading as it pertains to sampling *within* the reversed KL projection, not the overall runtime. 

Several points require clarification in rebuttal. First, the nested loop structure within the projected-gradient algorithm (Sec. 5.1) needs a clearly defined stopping criterion for the inner L-BFGS-B iterations. Second, providing absolute running times in seconds for all methods, including LBP, TRW, and MF, is crucial for a comprehensive evaluation. Finally, the authors should address the potential scalability of their approach. Including experiments on larger, more complex models – perhaps leveraging tractable random field models from Flach – would significantly strengthen the paper. Minor edits, as suggested (line 222, line 226), should be incorporated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to inference based on fast mixing guarantees.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating improved accuracy over existing methods.
*   Solid theoretical foundation and clear connection to established concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Potential scalability issues and high runtime.
*   Lack of clarity regarding absolute runtimes and stopping criteria.
*   Limited evaluation on larger, more complex models.

**Overall:** This is a promising paper with interesting results. However, addressing the concerns regarding runtime and scalability is essential for demonstrating the practical relevance of this work. I lean towards acceptance, contingent on a strong rebuttal addressing these points.
