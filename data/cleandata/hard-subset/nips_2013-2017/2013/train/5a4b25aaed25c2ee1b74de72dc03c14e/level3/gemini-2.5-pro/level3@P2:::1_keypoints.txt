- Proposes projecting slow-mixing Ising model parameters onto a nearby set guaranteed to be fast-mixing using spectral-norm-based bounds on dependencies.  
- Considers four projection divergences: Euclidean, zero-avoiding KL (KL(θ||ψ)), a novel tractable piecewise KL, and zero-forcing reverse KL (KL(ψ||θ)) optimized with stochastic gradient.  
- Develops concrete algorithms: a dual method for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) and stochastic projected gradient descent for reverse KL.  
- Cleanly motivates reframing tractability in terms of MCMC mixing rather than graphical structure.  
- Experiments (small grids and random graphs) show Gibbs on projected parameters can produce more accurate marginals for a fixed number of sampling iterations.  
- Major experimental flaw: timing comparisons ignore the computational cost of the projection step, biasing claims of efficiency.  
- Scalability concerns: evaluations are limited to very small models (10-node graphs, 8x8 grids), so generalization to realistic sizes is unclear.  
- Missing baselines/analysis: did not evaluate zero-avoiding KL projection against exact marginals on small models; relationship to mean-field not explained.  
- Clarity issues: Section 4 is hard to follow with abrupt notation (g, M, Λ) and weak exposition of primal-dual connections.  
- Incomplete references/proofs: Theorem 6 and Lemma 5 are stated as standard but lack citations or proof sketches.  
- Confusing notation: use of KL(q||p) in the introduction reverses conventional roles and may mislead readers.  
- Limited applicability: methods and fast-mixing bounds are specific to pairwise binary Ising models; extension to general discrete MRFs is unresolved.  
- Arguments for acceptance: highly novel reframing, sound technical derivations, and potential to inspire follow-up work.  
- Arguments for rejection: flawed/biased experimental validation, limited-scale tests, and significant clarity and completeness problems.  
- Recommendation: interesting and promising idea but not ready for acceptance—needs corrected timing experiments, scalability evidence, and clearer technical exposition.
