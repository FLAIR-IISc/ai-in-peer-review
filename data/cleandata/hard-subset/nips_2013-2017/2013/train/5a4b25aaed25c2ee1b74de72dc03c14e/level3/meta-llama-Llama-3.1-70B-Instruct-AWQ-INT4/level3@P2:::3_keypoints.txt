- Proposes projecting high-treewidth graphical models onto fast-mixing models (spectral norm of interaction matrix < 1) to guarantee rapid Gibbs mixing.  
- Distinguishes this projection approach from traditional tractable-family approximations like mean-field.  
- Provides thorough background on mixing times and the dependency matrix to motivate the fast-mixing condition.  
- Derives a projection algorithm for the Ising model using a dual method that enforces graph structure and sparsity.  
- Considers multiple divergences for projection (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL).  
- Experimental comparison includes loopy/TRW BP, naive mean-field, and Gibbs on original parameters.  
- Results show proposed methods consistently outperform variational methods; reversed KL performs best among proposed variants.  
- For reasonable runtimes, projected models can also beat Gibbs sampling on the original model.  
- Paper is well-written and explanations/theory are clear.  
- Minor issues: unclear stopping criteria for inner iterations of the projected-gradient algorithm (Sec. 5.1).  
- Minor issues: Figure 2 x-axis should be "runtime" and authors should report absolute running times in seconds.  
- Practical limitation: potential high runtime and unclear computational complexity and scalability to larger models.  
- Recommendation: include complexity analysis and experiments on larger/tractable random-field models to assess scalability.  
- Scores: Quality 8/10, Clarity 9/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 8/10.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel approach with convincing empirical gains; rejection arguments: limited practical utility due to runtime and missing scalability/complexity details plus minor notation/labeling issues.
