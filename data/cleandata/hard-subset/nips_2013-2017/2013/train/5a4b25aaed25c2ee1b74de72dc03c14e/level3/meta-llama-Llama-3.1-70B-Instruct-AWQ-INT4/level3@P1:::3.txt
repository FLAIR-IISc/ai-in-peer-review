This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models by projecting the model onto a set of fast-mixing models. The authors define a "fast-mixing" model as one where Gibbs sampling converges rapidly to the stationary distribution, and derive a condition for this to occur in the case of Ising models. They then propose several methods for projecting an arbitrary Ising model onto this set, using different divergences between Gibbs distributions.

The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed approach. The authors provide a thorough review of the literature on mixing times in Markov chains and establish a connection between the spectral norm of the dependency matrix and the mixing time. The proposed projection methods are well-motivated and the experimental results are convincing.

The main strength of the paper is its ability to provide a new perspective on approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models. The authors demonstrate that their approach can outperform existing methods, such as loopy belief propagation and tree-reweighted belief propagation, in terms of accuracy. The experimental results also show that the proposed methods can be more efficient than Gibbs sampling on the original model, especially when the interaction strengths are strong.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors do not provide a clear stopping criterion for the inner iterations of the projected-gradient algorithm in Section 5.1. Additionally, the x-axis in Figure 2 is labeled as "number of samples", but it is unclear what this refers to, as sampling is only used for the reversed KL projection. It would be more informative to report absolute running times in seconds and provide runtime comparisons with other methods, such as LBP, TRW, and MF.

Furthermore, the practical utility of the proposed approach may be limited by its high runtime. The authors do not provide a clear estimate of the computational cost of their method, and it is unclear how it would scale to larger models. It would be beneficial to include experiments on larger models, such as tractable random-field models, to test the scalability of the proposed approach.

Minor edits are also suggested, such as replacing "onto the tree" with "onto graph T" and using "subgradient" instead of "derivative".

Overall, the paper provides an interesting and convincing empirical results, but the practical utility of the proposed approach needs to be clarified in the rebuttal.
