This paper proposes approximate inference in general Ising models by projecting parameters onto a set that guarantees fast mixing for single-site Gibbs sampling. The tractable family is defined by a sufficient condition on the dependency matrix R (spectral norm below 1) derived from bounds R_ij ≤ tanh|β_ij| ≤ |β_ij| and the mixing-time guarantee of Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum/Hayes [4,8]. The key idea is to keep the original graph structure while shrinking interaction strengths so that |||β|||_2 (applied to |β|) satisfies the mixing criterion, then run Gibbs on the projected model. The authors instantiate this with four divergences between the target θ and projected ψ: Euclidean distance (solved via a dual projection that preserves sparsity and enforces the spectral constraint by SVD-thresholding with masking), inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ) (theoretically attractive but impractical since it requires expectations under θ), a tractable “piecewise” KL formed by exact KL on low-treewidth subgraphs, and the reversed KL KL(ψ||θ), which admits stochastic approximation since expectations are under fast-mixing ψ. Optimization for the divergences proceeds by projected gradient methods using the Euclidean projection as a prox step (with L-BFGS-B for the dual). Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare marginal errors and time–accuracy tradeoffs versus LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original parameters; the projected models, especially with reversed KL, yield more accurate marginals than variational baselines and, for limited computation, outperform Gibbs on the original parameters.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates well among variational approximations (MF/structured MF [14,20], LBP/TRW [21,19]) and decomposition approaches (planar [6], tree mixtures [15], piecewise [17]). The novelty is to define tractability by mixing-time guarantees rather than graph structure, connecting to Dobrushin-type criteria and spectral bounds [4,8]. The reversed-KL projection can be viewed as a generalization of mean-field (zero spectral norm) to the broader fast-mixing set, and the use of Ergodic Mirror Descent [3] for stochastic projected optimization is appropriate.

Quality: The technical development is mostly sound. The Euclidean projection under a spectral-norm constraint on |β| while preserving sparsity is a nice contribution; the dual formulation and use of masked SVD-thresholding are plausible and practical. The stochastic projected algorithm for KL(ψ||θ) is standard and well motivated. Claims are supported by experiments, and the authors openly discuss looseness of the spectral bound and practical choices (e.g., using a bound of 2.5). Some aspects need clarification: the stopping criteria and convergence diagnostics for the inner L-BFGS-B loop in the Euclidean projection; the theoretical implications of using systematic scan in experiments while the main bound is stated for random updates (the paper cites that similar results hold [4,8], but it would help to be explicit); and the safety of exceeding the sufficient bound (c=2.5) while still claiming fast mixing in practice.

Clarity: Generally clear and well organized. The derivations for the dual projection (Thm 7) are helpful; pseudocode or a short algorithm box for the projected-gradient loop (including sample-pool updates) would further aid reproducibility. Figures should report absolute runtimes and clarify axes. Minor edits: line 222 “onto the tree” → “onto graph T”; line 226 “derivative” → “subgradient”. Please also distinguish clearly between θ, ψ, and their corresponding R matrices in the text and figures.

Originality: The projection notion over a fast-mixing family is, to my knowledge, new for Ising models, as is the dual Euclidean projection that enforces a spectral norm bound on |β| while preserving sparsity. The piecewise KL adaptation and the reversed-KL projected method are interesting combinations of known ingredients in a new setting.

Significance: If scalable, the approach offers a practical alternative when exact inference is intractable and MCMC on the target is slow: project once, then sample quickly. The empirical gains over LBP/TRW/MF are promising. However, current experiments are small scale, and the computational overhead of projection (notably for KL(ψ||θ)) may limit utility without further engineering. A more thorough runtime analysis would clarify impact.

Requests for clarification:
- Inner-loop stopping criteria: In the projected-gradient methods, how are L-BFGS-B tolerances and maximum iterations set for the Euclidean projection step? What is the outer-loop stopping rule?
- Figure 2 x-axis: “number of samples” seems specific to KL(ψ||θ); for other methods, does this axis represent wall-clock runtime? Please report absolute runtimes (seconds) for all methods, including LBP/TRW/MF, to enable fair comparison at equal compute.
- Mixing update schedule: bounds and projections are derived for random-scan Gibbs; experiments use systematic scan. Do your guarantees extend, or do you rely on empirical rapid mixing?
- Spectral bound choice: You state that c=2.5 works well though 1.0 is the proven sufficient threshold. How often did chains fail to mix rapidly under c=2.5, and how was mixing diagnosed?

Suggestions:
- Include larger models (e.g., larger grids, or other tractable random-field benchmarks) to assess scalability and the cost/benefit of projection versus longer Gibbs runs.
- Report sensitivity to hyperparameters in KL(ψ||θ): step sizes, pool size, number of iterations, and number of Gibbs updates per iteration.
- Add time–accuracy plots with wall-clock time for all baselines; include block Gibbs or tree sampling for completeness.
- Analyze parameter distortion: histogram of |β| shrinkage, effect on fields α, and how distance to the spectral boundary correlates with marginal error.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually novel reframing of tractability via fast mixing, with a concrete algorithmic instantiation.
- Solid theoretical underpinnings and a practically implementable Euclidean projection preserving sparsity.
- Empirically stronger than standard variational baselines; reversed-KL approach is effective.

Cons against acceptance:
- Experimental scale is modest; computational overhead and runtime reporting are insufficiently detailed.
- Reliance on a conservative sufficient condition, with ad hoc relaxation (c=2.5) in practice.
- Piecewise KL results are underwhelming; inclusive KL is impractical beyond toy cases.

Overall: An interesting and well-motivated contribution with convincing initial results; I lean toward acceptance provided the authors clarify runtime methodology, stopping criteria, and mixing guarantees, and strengthen the empirical evaluation with clearer, fair runtime comparisons.
