This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models. Instead of approximating the model with a structurally simple one (e.g., a tree or a fully-factorized distribution) as is common in variational methods, the authors propose projecting the model's parameters onto the set of "fast-mixing" models. A model is defined as fast-mixing if the spectral norm of the matrix of its absolute interaction strengths is bounded. This condition guarantees that Gibbs sampling will converge rapidly. The paper develops projection algorithms for several divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL) and presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation.

The main strength of this work is its original and interesting conceptualization of a "tractable" model class, shifting the focus from structural simplicity to guaranteed rapid convergence of MCMC. The empirical results are convincing, demonstrating that for a fixed time budget, sampling from the projected model can produce more accurate marginals than both standard variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) and Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing model. The reversed KL projection, in particular, shows consistently strong performance. This is a significant finding that could open up a new direction for approximate inference.

However, the paper's claims about practical utility are not yet fully supported, as the computational cost of the projection itself is a major concern. Several clarifications are needed:
1.  The projected-gradient algorithm in Section 5.1 involves nested loops. The authors should specify the stopping criteria for the inner L-BFGS-B optimization to make the algorithm fully reproducible and its complexity understandable.
2.  The x-axis in Figure 2, labeled "number of samples," is unclear, as sampling is only used in one of the methods. This axis should likely be runtime. For a fair comparison, the authors should report absolute running times in seconds for all methods, critically including the projection time for their proposed methods and the inference time for the baselines (LBP, TRW, MF).

To strengthen the paper and better assess its scalability, I suggest including experiments on larger, more structured models, such as the tractable random-field models from Boris Flach's UAI 2012 challenge. Minor textual improvements include replacing "onto the tree" with "onto graph T" (line 222) and using "subgradient" instead of "derivative" where appropriate (line 226).

In summary, this paper introduces a promising new technique for approximate inference with strong initial results. Its acceptance hinges on the authors' ability to clarify the true computational cost and demonstrate the method's value beyond small-scale models in their rebuttal.
