- Introduces projecting Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set by constraining the spectral norm of absolute edge strengths to <1.  
- Proposes four projection methods: Euclidean, KL zero-avoiding, KL zero-forcing (reversed KL), and piecewise KL approximation.  
- Preserves original graph structure while guaranteeing rapid Gibbs sampling convergence for faster approximate inference.  
- Strong theoretical foundation linking spectral norm constraints to mixing times (Theorems 4–7).  
- Comprehensive empirical comparison across graph topologies and interaction strengths.  
- Reversed KL projection consistently outperforms variational baselines in accuracy.  
- For reasonable runtimes, proposed methods can beat Gibbs on the original model, according to reported results.  
- Authors acknowledge conservative spectral norm bound and discuss future work.  
- Missing specification of stopping criteria for inner L-BFGS-B iterations, harming reproducibility.  
- Figure 2’s x-axis labeled "number of samples" is misleading; runtime should be reported in absolute seconds.  
- Runtime data for baselines (LBP, TRW, MF) are not provided, preventing fair comparisons.  
- Experiments are limited to small models; scalability to larger or tractable random-field models is not demonstrated.  
- Minor issues: inconsistent terminology ("onto the tree" vs "onto graph T") and misuse of "derivative" vs "subgradient."  
- Runtime claim that methods "beat Gibbs" lacks concrete support without absolute time measurements.  
- Overall: novel and technically sound approach with convincing empirical gains (especially reversed KL), but practical utility is tempered by runtime and scalability concerns.
