

This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set satisfying the Dobrushin criterion (spectral norm of interaction matrix <1), enabling rapid Gibbs convergence. The work extends beyond traditional tractable approximations (e.g., mean-field, loopy BP) by focusing on parameter-space constraints rather than structural simplification. It derives a dual optimization method for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7), explores KL divergence-based projections (zero-avoiding, piecewise, zero-forcing), and tests the approach against Gibbs sampling on original parameters, LBP, TRW, and mean-field. The core contribution aligns with recent NIPS work on mixing times (e.g., Hayes [8], Dyer et al. [4]) but reframes tractability through parameter constraints.

**Strengths:**  
The paper’s central idea—using parameter projection to guarantee fast mixing—is conceptually fresh and addresses a genuine limitation in MCMC inference for strong interactions. The dual optimization approach (Theorem 7) is technically sound for Euclidean projection, and the zero-forcing KL divergence (Section 5.4) provides a practical, implementable strategy that generalizes mean-field. The experimental motivation is clear: for limited sampling time, projected parameters yield more accurate marginals than slow-mixing originals or variational methods. This bridges a gap between stochastic and deterministic inference, an under-explored area in NIPS literature.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Methodological Ambiguity:** The constraint $Z_{ij}D_{ij} = 0$ (Section 4) lacks justification for preserving the parameter space. It may restrict the space (e.g., forcing zero edges not in $Z$), but the paper does not clarify whether the projection spans the same space as the original model. The dual optimization’s derivation is opaque, and the absence of an algorithm box impedes reproducibility.  
2. **Convergence Uncertainty:** The projected gradient descent strategy (Section 5.1) claims convergence via Ergodic Mirror Descent [3], but no proof or conditions for convergence under the specific constraints are provided. This is critical for practical use.  
3. **Experimental Flaws:**  
   - The 30,000 Gibbs iterations for projection (Section 6.1) are arbitrary and unfair—this computational cost is ignored in comparisons, making "time-accuracy" claims misleading.  
   - No wall-clock timing comparisons are included, obscuring the practical trade-off between projection cost and sampling speed.  
   - Results lack error bars (Section 6), so statistical significance is unverified.  
   - Baselines miss key methods: blocked Gibbs (which can accelerate convergence) and Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs Sampler (a state-of-the-art approach for structured models).  
4. **Overstated Claims:** The paper asserts "Gibbs sampling using projected parameters is more accurate when limited time is available," but experiments compare projected Gibbs (after 30K iterations) to original Gibbs *without* projection. This conflates projection cost with sampling time, rendering the comparison invalid.  

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel perspective on tractability via parameter constraints; promising theoretical foundation; practical zero-forcing KL approach.  
*Con:* Critical methodological gaps (constraint interpretation, convergence); experimentally biased comparisons; missing baselines and error analysis.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper’s core idea has merit but requires significant revision. The methodological ambiguities and experimental flaws undermine the claimed advantages. To strengthen the submission:  
1. Clarify the constraint $Z_{ij}D_{ij}=0$’s role in preserving parameter space and provide an algorithmic pseudocode.  
2. Include wall-clock timing (projection + sampling) and error bars in experiments.  
3. Compare against blocked Gibbs and Gogate’s sampler, and justify the 30K iteration choice.  
4. Prove convergence of the projected gradient descent under the specific constraints.  

Without these fixes, the paper risks overclaiming its contribution. As a foundational idea, it deserves attention, but the current implementation lacks the rigor expected at NIPS. The experimental section particularly needs overhaul to fairly evaluate the method’s practical utility. The paper is *not* ready for acceptance in its current form, but could be a strong candidate after addressing these concerns.
