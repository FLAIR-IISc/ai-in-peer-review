- Introduces a novel framework for approximate inference by projecting slow-mixing Ising models onto a tractable set of fast-mixing models defined via MCMC mixing-time (spectral norm) rather than graph structure.  
- Fast-mixing criterion: spectral norm of absolute interaction matrix < 1 gives a sufficient condition for rapid Gibbs convergence.  
- Projection methods preserve original graph sparsity while enforcing the fast-mixing constraint.  
- Four divergence-based projection objectives explored: Euclidean (L2), zero-avoiding KL (KL(θ||ψ)), piecewise KL (tractable subgraph approximation), and zero-forcing KL (KL(ψ||θ)).  
- Euclidean projection solved via a dual algorithm using L-BFGS-B (Theorem 7).  
- KL(θ||ψ) is theoretically interesting but intractable for slow-mixing target models.  
- Piecewise KL is a novel, tractable approximation based on tractable subgraphs but may be nondifferentiable (max over KLs).  
- KL(ψ||θ) minimized with projected stochastic gradient descent, estimating gradients by running Gibbs on the projected model.  
- Empirical evaluation on 8x8 grids and small random graphs shows projection methods—especially reversed KL—outperform LBP, TRW, MF in marginal accuracy.  
- Demonstrates practical trade-off: for fixed time budgets, Gibbs on projected fast-mixing model yields more accurate marginals than Gibbs on original slow-mixing model.  
- Main weakness: computational cost and scalability—projection (especially reversed KL) is expensive and experiments are limited to small graphs, with unclear runtime reporting.  
- The spectral-norm fast-mixing bound is conservative, potentially restricting approximating distributions and limiting accuracy; authors use looser bounds in practice.  
- Requests clarification on algorithmic details: stopping criteria for inner L-BFGS-B when used inside outer projected gradient descent.  
- Requests experimental clarity: define the x-axis labeled "number of samples," and report absolute running times for projection phase and baseline inference.  
- Suggests scalability experiments on larger, high-treewidth benchmarks (e.g., PASCAL Inference Challenge models) to demonstrate practical applicability.  
- Minor textual suggestions: clarify "projection onto the tree" to "projection onto the graph T," and use "subgradient" instead of "derivative" for the piecewise KL objective.  
- Overall assessment: technically solid, original, and empirically convincing for tested problems; main concern is projection overhead and scalability; recommends acceptance.
