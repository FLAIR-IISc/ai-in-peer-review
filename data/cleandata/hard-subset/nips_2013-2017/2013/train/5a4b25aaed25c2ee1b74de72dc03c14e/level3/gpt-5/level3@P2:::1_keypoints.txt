- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a set guaranteeing rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs by constraining the dependency matrix spectral norm.  
- Uses bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| and enforces ||tanh|β|||2 < 1 (practically relaxed to constraints on |β|) to ensure fast mixing.  
- Develops multiple projection methods: Euclidean dual projection with spectral-norm and sparsity constraints (Theorem 7).  
- Also studies projections minimizing KL(θ||ψ) (zero-avoiding), piecewise KL via tractable subgraphs, and KL(ψ||θ) (zero-forcing) via stochastic approximation plus repeated Euclidean projections.  
- Experiments on small grids and random graphs show projected models often give more accurate marginals within fixed Gibbs iterations and beat MF/LBP/TRW on average.  
- Conceptually relates to mean-field (as degenerate reverse-KL) and piecewise/mixture-of-trees methods; using fast-mixing constraints as an inference family appears novel.  
- Theoretical use of ||R||2 < 1 is standard; need reference/proof sketch for general-field case (Hayes covers zero field).  
- Theorem 6 (singular value thresholding) is standard; cite Eckart–Young–Mirsky or provide short derivation.  
- Theorem 7 (dual projection) is interesting but Section 4 is hard to parse and contains notation and typographical issues (Λ, M, Z, D, missing 1/2, triple-dot product unclear).  
- Experiments often use spectral bound c = 2.5, violating the ||R||2 < 1 guarantee and undermining theoretical claims.  
- If using c > 1, must report empirical mixing diagnostics (autocorrelations, spectral gap proxies) and state guarantees do not apply.  
- The paper uses systematic-scan Gibbs in experiments but the theory assumes random-scan; cite the appropriate condition or explain the transfer.  
- Reverse-KL stochastic gradient reuses samples for both product terms, which can bias estimates; recommend independent mini-batches or analyze/empirically check bias.  
- Connect the stochastic procedure to ergodic mirror descent requirements (step sizes, MCMC steps per iteration).  
- Time-vs-accuracy comparisons omit projection cost (repeated SVDs and LBFGS-B), biasing results; include total wall-clock or Pareto curves including projection.  
- Notation inconsistencies (KL directions used ambiguously); standardize notation KL(θ||ψ) vs KL(ψ||θ).  
- Explicitly state that R is not computed directly but upper-bounded by tanh|β|, and that constraints are applied to this tractable bound.  
- Add citations for standard matrix results and provide proof sketches for Lemma 5 and Theorem 7 in the appendix.  
- Projecting to a fast-mixing family is novel and the Euclidean dual projection preserving sparsity is a strong technical contribution.  
- Promising idea: uses mixing-time theory to define a tractable family broader than trees and mean-field, helpful when sampling time is limited.  
- Current experiments are small (8×8, n=10 graphs) and lack projection cost accounting, so scalability and fairness are unclear.  
- Scope limited to binary Ising models; extension to general discrete MRFs is open.  
- Experimental positives: sensible baselines, exploration of variants, and focus on strong-coupling regimes.  
- Missing experiments: larger instances (32×32, 64×64), planar cases with exact algorithms, and comparisons to block Gibbs, Swendsen–Wang, or tree sampling.  
- Recommend ablations varying spectral bound c (including c<1), experiments on KL(θ||ψ) with exact inference, and analysis of why reverse-KL outperforms MF.  
- Pros: clear idea, novel projection machinery, and empirical regimes where projections help under limited sampling.  
- Cons: experiments violate guarantees, omit projection cost, limited scale/baselines, and clarity/notation issues.  
- Suggested improvements: enforce/report c<1 experiments, include projection time in budgets, add larger-scale and additional sampler baselines, clean notation, and analyze stochastic-gradient settings.  
- Overall: appealing and potentially impactful but currently oversteps guarantees, under-accounting compute, and needs clearer exposition—reviewer leans to weak reject pending revisions.
