- Novel idea: project an intractable model onto a “fast-mixing” family defined by spectral-norm conditions rather than graph structure, connecting approximate inference and MCMC theory.

- Reverse-KL projection is framed as a generalization of mean-field by expanding the tractable family from fully-factorized to fast-mixing.

- Promising initial empirical results on small models: sampling from the projected model can produce more accurate marginals than MF, LBP, TRW, and short-run Gibbs on the original model.

- Critical experimental flaw: projection pre-computation time (often large) is omitted from wall-clock comparisons, biasing results in favor of the proposed method.

- Scalability concern: projection cost may be prohibitive and observed benefits on 8x8 grids may not scale to larger, realistic problems.

- Applicability limitation: method relies on binary Ising mixing bounds and extending to non-binary discrete MRFs is non-trivial, limiting generalizability.

- Missing technical detail: key results (e.g., Lemma 5, Theorem 6) are stated without proofs/sketches or adequate citations.

- Poor exposition in Section 4: notation and dual formulation (g, M, Λ) are introduced abruptly and the Euclidean projection algorithm is hard to follow; reorganization needed.

- Dependency matrix R issues: paper does not discuss the intractability of computing R’s entries (maximization over configurations) and does not acknowledge approximations/limitations.

- Notational inconsistency: KL divergence written as KL(q||p) in a nonstandard way, risking confusion and should be corrected.

- Experimental gaps: evaluations are limited to small grids/random graphs; need tests on models with known solutions or long-run MCMC ground truth for larger problems.

- Missing baselines: for toy models where exact inference is possible, the best-case KL(θ||ψ) projection (using exact marginals) should be computed to establish an upper bound.

- Unexplained empirical behavior: the paper does not analyze why relaxing the spectral-norm constraint from zero (mean-field) yields large performance improvements.

- Overall verdict: elegant and original concept but manuscript requires major revision to fix experimental bias, clarify technical details, and address scalability before publication.
