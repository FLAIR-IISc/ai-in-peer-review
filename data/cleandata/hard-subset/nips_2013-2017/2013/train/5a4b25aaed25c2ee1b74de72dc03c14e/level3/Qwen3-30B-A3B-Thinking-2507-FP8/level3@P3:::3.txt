

# Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a set of "fast-mixing" models, defined by constraining the spectral norm of the absolute edge strengths matrix to be less than 1. The authors propose four different projection methods based on Euclidean distance, KL divergence (both zero-avoiding and zero-forcing variants), and a piecewise KL approximation. The key insight is that while exact inference in high-treewidth models is intractable, parameter constraints can guarantee rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling, enabling more efficient approximate inference.

The work builds on previous NIPS contributions in approximate inference (e.g., Globerson & Jaakkola, 2007 on planar graphs; Wainwright et al., 2003 on tree-reweighted methods) but offers a distinct perspective by focusing on parameter constraints rather than structural approximations. Unlike mean-field or tree-reweighted methods that impose structural restrictions (fully factorized or tree-structured distributions), this approach preserves the original graph structure while ensuring fast mixing.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes several important contributions. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear connections between spectral norm constraints and mixing times (Theorems 4-7). The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the proposed methods against standard baselines (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs on original model) across multiple graph topologies and interaction strengths. The results consistently show that the proposed methods (particularly reversed KL projection) outperform variational methods in accuracy, and for reasonable runtimes, also surpass Gibbs sampling on the original model. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the conservative spectral norm bound (Section 7), and provide a thoughtful discussion of future directions.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper would benefit from several clarifications and extensions. First, the projected-gradient algorithm in Section 5.1 features nested loops (inner L-BFGS-B), but the stopping criteria for inner iterations are not specified—this is critical for reproducibility. Second, Figure 2's x-axis labeled "number of samples" is misleading, as sampling is only used for reversed KL projection. The authors should report absolute running times in seconds and include runtime data for LBP, TRW, and MF to enable fair comparison. Third, the paper lacks experiments on larger models; including tractable random-field models (e.g., from Boris Flach) would better demonstrate scalability.

Minor issues include inconsistent terminology ("onto the tree" should be "onto graph T" on line 222) and the use of "derivative" instead of "subgradient" on line 226. The runtime analysis also needs improvement—the paper claims the proposed methods "beat Gibbs on the original model" for reasonable runtimes, but without absolute time measurements, this claim lacks concrete support.

**Overall assessment:**
The paper presents an interesting and technically sound approach to approximate inference with convincing empirical results. The core idea—projecting parameters to guarantee fast mixing—is novel and well-motivated. The reversed KL projection method consistently outperforms baselines in accuracy, making it a valuable addition to the inference toolbox. However, the practical utility may be limited by high runtime (particularly for reversed KL), which requires clarification in the rebuttal.

**Pro/Con acceptance arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel perspective on "tractable" models based on fast mixing rather than structural restrictions
- Theoretical results are well-supported and clearly presented
- Comprehensive experimental comparison demonstrates consistent accuracy improvements
- Well-written and properly contextualized within existing literature

*Con:*
- Incomplete runtime analysis (lacks absolute times for all methods)
- Missing details on inner loop stopping criteria
- Limited scalability evaluation (only small random models)
- Spectral norm bound appears conservative (factor of ~1.76 too tight for 2D Ising)

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of approximate inference, advancing the state of the art in a demonstrable way. While the runtime concerns warrant clarification, the method is promising for applications where strong interactions make standard Gibbs sampling impractical. With minor revisions addressing the requested clarifications, this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
