- Novel idea: project Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set (spectral norm of absolute interaction matrix < 1) to guarantee rapid Gibbs mixing instead of simplifying graph structure.
- Conceptual contribution: reframes approximation from static (treewidth) to dynamic (mixing speed), offering a new perspective on tractable families.
- Methodological novelty: combines deterministic projection optimization with stochastic sampling, including a dual algorithm for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7).
- Technical contribution: derives projection methods under four divergences—Euclidean, KL(q||p), a novel piecewise KL, and KL(p||q)—and uses projected gradient descent for KL cases.
- Clarity issue: Theorem 7 and the dual Euclidean projection are opaque; roles of auxiliary matrices and objective g(Λ,M) lack intuitive explanation.
- Sparsity concern: It’s unclear how the constraint ZijBij = 0 on D = |B| ensures the projected parameters preserve the original graph’s sparsity pattern.
- Algorithmic ambiguity: The KL-projection procedure (gradient steps, Euclidean projection, and sample pool updates) is not specified step-by-step, hindering reproducibility.
- Convergence gap: No formal convergence guarantees are provided for the stochastic gradient procedure using correlated MCMC samples, despite citing Ergodic Mirror Descent.
- Experimental fairness: Comparisons are misleading because they ignore the substantial pre-computation cost of projection (e.g., 30k Gibbs iterations), and no wall-clock timing is reported.
- Missing baselines: Only single-site Gibbs is used as an MCMC comparator; stronger samplers (blocked Gibbs, lifted methods) are omitted.
- Statistical rigor: Results report means over 50 trials without error bars or significance measures, preventing assessment of result reliability.
- Hyperparameter choice concern: Using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of the theoretical 1.0 is arbitrary and undermines the theoretical motivation without ablation or justification.
- Overall assessment: Idea is original and potentially impactful but the paper needs clearer exposition, formal guarantees, fairer and more rigorous experiments, and better justification of practical choices.
