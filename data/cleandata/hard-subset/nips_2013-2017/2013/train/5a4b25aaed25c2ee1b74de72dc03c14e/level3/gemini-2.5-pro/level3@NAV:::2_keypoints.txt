Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set via the Dobrushin/spectral-norm criterion to enable faster Gibbs sampling.

Frames the projection as a constrained optimization and evaluates solutions under multiple divergences (Euclidean, KL).

Connects MCMC and variational inference by modifying the target distribution to make sampling tractable.

Strength: original idea of defining tractable families by mixing time rather than graphical structure.

Strength: interesting hybrid paradigm combining deterministic projection with stochastic sampling.

Weakness: methodological description is ambiguous; derivation of the dual for Euclidean projection is hard to follow.

Weakness: unclear whether ZijBij = 0 constraints preserve sparsity without overly restricting the solution space.

Weakness: KL-divergence projection procedure is unclear and lacks algorithmic detail (an algorithm box is needed).

Unclear whether projection is fully optimized before sampling or interleaved; convergence claim for projected gradient descent requires stronger justification.

Experimental evaluation is unconvincing—using a fixed 30,000 Gibbs iterations is arbitrary and unfair versus deterministic methods.

Experiments omit wall-clock times and projection computational cost, leaving practical utility uncertain.

Plots lack error bars, so statistical significance of improvements is unclear.

Missing comparisons to relevant mixing-improvement baselines (e.g., blocked Gibbs, Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs Sampler).

Overall: promising and original but incomplete; methodological clarity and a more rigorous, comprehensive empirical study are required before recommendation.
