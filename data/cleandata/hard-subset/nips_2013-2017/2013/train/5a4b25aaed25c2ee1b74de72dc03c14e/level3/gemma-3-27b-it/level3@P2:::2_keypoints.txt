- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters to satisfy the Dobrushin criterion to accelerate Gibbs sampling, combining stochastic sampling and deterministic projection.  
- Novel idea exploring an under-explored hybrid of projection and MCMC with potential practical impact.  
- Solid theoretical grounding linking dependency matrix spectral norm to rapid mixing and citing relevant literature.  
- Investigates multiple projection divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, reversed KL via stochastic approximation) to trade off speed and accuracy.  
- Description of the projection procedure and dual formulation (Theorem 7) is unclear, especially how z_ij d_ij = 0 constraints preserve representational capacity.  
- Interaction between projection and Gibbs sampling is ambiguous—unclear if projection is pre-optimized or interleaved; no algorithm box.  
- Lacks explicit convergence guarantees for the projected gradient/descent method minimizing reversed KL; references Ergodic Mirror Descent but provides insufficient analysis.  
- Experimental evaluation is weak: uses an arbitrary 30,000 Gibbs iterations without runtime comparisons, making iteration-based comparisons misleading.  
- No timing or runtime comparisons, so practical speedups from projection cannot be assessed.  
- Results lack error bars or statistical analysis, preventing assessment of significance.  
- Important baselines (e.g., blocked Gibbs, Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs) are missing from comparisons.  
- Spectral norm bound of 1.0 is conservative/loose compared to known critical values (e.g., 2D Ising), reducing practical tightness.  
- Technical soundness is reasonable but undermined by unclear algorithmic details and missing convergence analysis.  
- Clarity is mixed: background is good, but technical projection details and overall procedure are hard to follow.  
- Originality: idea is novel but implementation and experiments need substantial refinement.  
- Significance: approach could be impactful if developed and validated, but current evidence is insufficient.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel MCMC acceleration approach, solid theoretical basis, exploration of multiple divergences.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear method, missing convergence proofs, flawed/insufficient experiments, lack of statistical rigor and baselines.  
- Overall recommendation: reject now but encourage revision to clarify projection algorithm, provide convergence analysis, add timing and statistical evaluations, include relevant baselines, and present a clear algorithm box.
