This paper proposes using fast-mixing Ising models as a tractable surrogate for inference: given strongly coupled parameters θ, project to nearby parameters ψ whose dependency matrix satisfies spectral bounds ensuring rapid mixing, then run Gibbs on ψ. The paper develops several projections—Euclidean (via spectral-norm constraints), piecewise KL on low-treewidth subgraphs, and reverse KL (KL(ψ||θ)) optimized with stochastic approximation—building on spectral mixing results of Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum and Hayes. Experiments on small grids and random graphs show regimes where projected models yield more accurate marginals within a fixed sampling budget than LBP/TRW/MF and Gibbs on the original model. The use of projection onto the nearest provably fast-mixing model as an inference tool appears novel and conceptually elegant, connecting mixing-time theory with variational/MCMC practice.

Quality: The approach is technically motivated, but several issues weaken rigor. Key “standard results” (e.g., SVD projection in Theorem 6, the Ising dependency bound in Lemma 5) lack citations or proof sketches; the SVD capping formula appears misstated. The projection dual (Theorem 7) and the role of the dependency matrix R require clearer derivation and discussion of tractability; maximizing g over M, Λ with LBFGS-B may be nontrivial and convergence properties are not analyzed. Critically, the spectral guarantee is sometimes relaxed in practice (using 2.5 rather than 1), undermining the claimed mixing-time guarantees. Experimental timing omits the (potentially large) projection cost, biasing comparisons.

Clarity: Section 4 is hard to follow: notation (g, M, Λ, Z) and Theorem 4/7 appear abruptly and should be reorganized with intuition and a small running example. The dependency matrix definition and its maximization should be discussed explicitly. There is a notational flip in KL(q||p)/KL(p||q) in Sec. 1. Several results need references.

Originality: While built on known rapid-mixing bounds, the projection-for-inference idea and the piecewise-KL surrogate are new to my knowledge.

Significance: Potentially impactful if it scales, but current evidence is limited to small Ising models. Generalization to multi-state MRFs is unclear due to missing dependency bounds.

Pros:
- Elegant, principled idea linking mixing-time guarantees with approximate inference.
- Multiple projection objectives explored; reverse-KL with stochastic approximation is practical.
- Empirical regimes where the method beats common variational baselines at fixed time.

Cons:
- Questionable scalability; no large or realistic models, no size-vs-accuracy analysis.
- Omitted projection time in comparisons; results should reflect end-to-end compute.
- Guarantees relaxed in practice; needs justification and diagnostics.
- Clarity gaps (missing references/proofs; confusing Section 4/notation).
- Unexplained gap between MF (reverse KL special case) and reverse-KL projection.
- Missing exploration of KL(θ||ψ) “best case” on tiny graphs with junction tree.

Suggestions: Include projection time in all timing plots; add larger grids or symmetric models with tractable marginals, or long-chain ground truth; analyze scaling of mixing/accuracy with n; explore KL(θ||ψ) projections on toys; explain MF vs reverse-KL behavior; fix KL notation; provide references/proof sketches; discuss the feasibility of optimizing over R and prospects for non-binary extensions. Overall, promising but not yet ready without stronger empirical and methodological clarity.
