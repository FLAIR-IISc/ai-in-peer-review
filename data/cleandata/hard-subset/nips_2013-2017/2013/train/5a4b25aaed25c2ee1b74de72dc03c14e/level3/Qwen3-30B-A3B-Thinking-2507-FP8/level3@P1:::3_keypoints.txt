- Novel approach: project Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set defined by spectral norm constraint (||R||₂ < 1), i.e., spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix < 1.

- Conceptual shift: redefines "tractable" from low-treewidth structure to parameter constraints that guarantee rapid mixing and efficient sampling.

- Main contributions: projection algorithms for multiple divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, reverse KL).

- Euclidean method: solved via dual optimization using L-BFGS-B.

- Reverse KL method: uses projected gradient descent with Gibbs sampling inside the projection loop.

- Theory: provides mixing-time bounds and connects to existing rapid-mixing results (e.g., Hayes, Dyer et al.).

- Experiments: compares proposed methods to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on original parameters on small grid and random graphs.

- Empirical results: proposed methods outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in marginal accuracy; reverse KL performs best among proposed approaches.

- Practical trade-off: analyzes the balance between enforced mixing-time constraints and approximation accuracy.

- Strengths: solid theoretical foundation, novel parameter-space projection framework beyond standard variational methods, comprehensive experiments, and clear motivation.

- Major weakness: spectral-norm bound is conservative (e.g., ~1.76× looser than known 2D Ising critical threshold); choice of practical bound (2.5) needs better justification.

- Major weakness: reverse KL projection is computationally expensive due to nested Gibbs sampling; paper lacks absolute runtime comparisons for fair evaluation.

- Presentation issues: Figure 2 x-axis labeled "number of samples" is confusing—needs clarification whether it means samples or runtime, and absolute run times should be reported.

- Minor revisions: wording fixes ("onto graph T" instead of "onto the tree", "subgradient" instead of "derivative") and clarify stopping criteria for nested L-BFGS-B in projected-gradient.

- Originality: distinguishes from prior NIPS work by focusing on parameter-space constraints for fast mixing; extends prior mixing-time ideas into a systematic projection framework.

- Significance and limitations: promising practical alternative to variational methods for finite-time inference, but current experiments are small-scale (max 10 nodes); scalability and runtime clarity needed to strengthen practical impact.

- Overall: high-quality, significant contribution worthy of acceptance if conservative spectral bounds and runtime/complexity concerns are adequately addressed.
