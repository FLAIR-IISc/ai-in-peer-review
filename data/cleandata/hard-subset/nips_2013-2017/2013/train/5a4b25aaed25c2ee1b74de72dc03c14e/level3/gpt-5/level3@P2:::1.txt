Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes to approximate an intractable Ising model by projecting its parameters onto a set that guarantees rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs sampling. The key idea is to use sufficient conditions based on the dependency matrix and its spectral norm: for an Ising model with interactions β, a sufficient condition for rapid mixing is that the spectral norm of |β| be less than one (via bounds Rij ≤ tanh|βij| and ||R||2 < 1 implying rapid mixing). The paper develops several ways to project to the “fast-mixing” set: (i) Euclidean projection using a dual formulation that enforces both a spectral-norm constraint on |β| and graph sparsity (Theorem 7), (ii) projection minimizing KL(θ||ψ) (zero-avoiding) for toy cases, (iii) a piecewise KL approximation via tractable subgraphs, and (iv) projection minimizing KL(ψ||θ) (zero-forcing) using stochastic approximation with sampling from ψ, coupled with repeated Euclidean projections.

Experimentally, on small grids and random graphs with strong couplings, the projected models often yield more accurate marginals within a fixed number of Gibbs iterations than running Gibbs on the original parameters, and outperform MF/LBP/TRW on average. The approach is conceptually related to mean-field (viewed here as a degenerate reverse-KL projection with zero spectral norm) and to piecewise training/mixtures of trees, but the specific use of “fast-mixing” constraints as an inference family appears novel. The mixing-time tools draw on Dobrushin-type bounds and more recent spectral-norm criteria [Dyer et al., Hayes]; the experimental baselines include LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs.

Quality
- Theory: The use of ||R||2 < 1 as a sufficient condition is standard; the bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is sensible (and ≤ |βij|), but a precise reference or proof sketch for the general-field case would help (Hayes covers zero field). Theorem 6 (singular value thresholding for Frobenius projection under spectral norm) is a standard result; please cite an appropriate reference or give a short derivation. The dual projection (Theorem 7) is interesting and practically important for preserving sparsity and constraining |β|, but Section 4 is hard to parse: notation (Λ, M, Z, D, ·′) needs to be introduced cleanly; Eq. (2) seems to miss a 1/2 factor; and the role of Z and the “triple dot” product should be clarified with consistent symbols and dimensions.
- Assumptions vs practice: The guarantees require ||R||2 < 1 (and are derived for random-scan Gibbs), yet the experiments frequently use a spectral bound of 2.5 because it “still preserves rapid mixing in practice.” This undermines the main guarantee. If you rely on looser bounds, please (a) justify empirically by reporting mixing diagnostics of ψ (autocorrelation times, coupling estimates, spectral gap proxies) and (b) state clearly that guarantees do not apply in those settings. Also, you run systematic scan Gibbs; cite and state the corresponding sufficient condition (or explain the transfer from random-scan).
- Reverse-KL optimization: The stochastic gradient estimator (Eq. 8) reuses the same sample pool to estimate both factors of a product, which can introduce bias; the text notes this qualitatively. Please either use independent mini-batches or provide an analysis/empirical check that the bias is negligible, and connect your procedure concretely to ergodic mirror descent requirements (step sizes, MCMC steps per iteration).
- Computational accounting: The “time vs accuracy” comparisons appear to exclude the often-substantial cost of projection (repeated SVD thresholding and LBFGS-B). This biases results toward the proposed method. Please include total wall-clock budgets (projection + sampling) or show Pareto curves with/without projection cost.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, but several places need careful revision:
  - Notation inconsistencies: KL(q||p) is used for both “zero-avoiding” and “zero-forcing” at different points; standardize to KL(θ||ψ) vs KL(ψ||θ).
  - Section 4: define Z, Λ, M, D(Λ,M) before use; fix typos (e.g., ’Z, missing 1/2 in quadratic).
  - Dependency matrix: state explicitly that you do not compute R by definition; instead you upper-bound R with tanh|β| and constrain ||tanh|β||2 ≤ c (which is further relaxed to |||β|||2 ≤ c). This addresses tractability concerns and should be highlighted.
  - Cite standard results (Eckart–Young–Mirsky for spectral projection; a matrix analysis text for singular value thresholding).
- Proof sketches for Lemma 5 and Theorem 7 (appendix) would strengthen the presentation.

Originality
- Projecting onto a fast-mixing family as an inference tool is, to my knowledge, novel. The Euclidean dual projection under an absolute-value spectral constraint with sparsity is a nice technical contribution. The piecewise KL adaptation connects to piecewise training and mixtures of trees, but the use as a projection objective is new.

Significance
- The idea is appealing: it uses algorithmic mixing-time theory to define a tractable family broader than trees and mean-field, and it can empirically help when only limited sampling time is available. However, the current experiments are small (8×8 grids; n=10 random graphs), and the projection cost is not fully accounted for. It is unclear whether the gains persist at scale or under fair time budgets. The scope is limited to binary Ising models; extending guarantees and projections to general discrete MRFs remains open.

Experimental evaluation
- Positives: sensible baselines (MF/LBP/TRW/Gibbs), exploration of piecewise and KL variants, and regimes with strong couplings.
- Missing/unclear:
  - Larger instances (e.g., 32×32, 64×64) or structured models with known ground truth (planar zero-field cases where exact algorithms exist) to test scalability.
  - Comparisons to block Gibbs, Swendsen–Wang, or tree sampling, especially in strong-coupling regimes.
  - Fair time accounting including projection.
  - Analysis of how the spectral bound c trades off bias vs mixing speed; ablations varying c (including c<1) would be instructive.
  - Experiments on KL(θ||ψ) for small graphs (junction tree) to show “best case” behavior of the projection objective.
  - Discussion of why reverse-KL projection outperforms MF so markedly despite MF being a reverse-KL method; is the benefit primarily from allowing nonzero interactions constrained by c?

Pros
- Clear, elegant idea: use fast-mixing constraints as a tractable family for approximate inference.
- Novel projection machinery (dual Euclidean projection preserving sparsity; reverse-KL + projection).
- Empirical regimes where projected models beat MF/LBP/TRW and original-parameter Gibbs under limited sampling.
- Connects mixing-time theory with practical inference.

Cons
- Guarantees are not respected in key experiments (spectral bound > 1), weakening the main premise.
- Projection cost is omitted from time comparisons; results may not hold under fair budgets.
- Limited scale and baselines; unclear generalization beyond Ising/binary.
- Clarity issues in Section 4 and notation; some “standard results” lack references/proof sketches.

Suggestions for improvement
- Enforce and report experiments with c < 1 (guaranteed regime), and separately explore c > 1 with empirical mixing diagnostics.
- Include wall-clock comparisons that count projection, and plot accuracy vs total time.
- Add larger-scale experiments and additional samplers (block Gibbs, Swendsen–Wang).
- Provide full details and references for Theorems 6–7 and Lemma 5; clean up notation.
- Analyze sensitivity to step size, pool size, and MCMC steps; use independent samples for unbiased gradient estimates.
- Clarify the dependency matrix approach and its tractable upper bound; discuss extension paths beyond binary variables.

Overall evaluation
An appealing and potentially impactful idea with promising preliminary results, but the current submission oversteps its guarantees in experiments, under-accounts compute, and needs clearer exposition. I lean toward a weak reject in its current form; with the clarifications and fairer, larger-scale experiments noted above, this could become a strong contribution.
