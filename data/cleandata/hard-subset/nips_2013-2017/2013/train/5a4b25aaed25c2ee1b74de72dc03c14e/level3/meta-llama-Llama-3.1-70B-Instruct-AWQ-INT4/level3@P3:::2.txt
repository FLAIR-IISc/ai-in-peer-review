This paper proposes a novel approach to speeding up Gibbs sampling by projecting Ising model parameters onto a set that guarantees fast mixing. The authors achieve this by formulating a constrained optimization problem under various divergences, including Euclidean distance, KL-divergence, and piecewise KL-divergence.

The main idea is to take an arbitrary set of parameters and project them onto a set that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion for fast mixing. This is done by iteratively thresholding the singular value decomposition of the parameter matrix, which is a clever and efficient approach. The authors also consider more probabilistic divergences, such as the KL-divergence, and develop a stochastic approximation approach to minimize it.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to combine stochastic and deterministic inference methods, which is an under-explored area of research. The authors' approach to using a "pool" of samples to estimate gradients and then projecting in the Euclidean norm is a nice example of this combination.

However, there are several weaknesses in the paper that need to be addressed. Firstly, the description of the dual of the projection problem is unclear, and it would be helpful to provide more details on how the dual is derived. Secondly, it is not clear how the constraints zij * dij = 0 guarantee that the new parameterization spans the same space as the original parameters. Could it be a smaller space? This needs to be clarified.

Furthermore, the overall procedure is ambiguous, and it is not clear whether the authors fully optimize the projection and then sample, or interleave projection updates with sampling. An algorithm box would be helpful to clarify the procedure. Additionally, it is not clear whether the projected gradient descent strategy is guaranteed to converge, and more details on the convergence properties of the algorithm would be helpful.

The experiments in the paper are also unconvincing, as the choice of 30K Gibbs iterations is arbitrary, and iteration-based comparisons are unfair versus deterministic methods. Moreover, there are no timing comparisons, and the projection runtime is not accounted for, so the practical benefit of the approach is unclear. The plots also lack error bars, so the statistical significance of the results is unknown.

Finally, there are no comparisons to blocked Gibbs or Gogate's Lifted Gibbs Sampler, which are relevant baselines for this type of research. Overall, while the idea is promising, the paper needs clearer method development and stronger, fairer experimental evaluation.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, but the claims are not well-supported by theoretical analysis or experimental results. The authors are not careful about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, but some parts are unclear, such as the description of the dual of the projection problem. The paper could benefit from an algorithm box to clarify the procedure.

**Originality:** The paper proposes a novel approach to speeding up Gibbs sampling, but it is not clear how this work differs from previous contributions. The authors could benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work.

**Significance:** The results are not important, as they are not well-supported by experimental results. The paper does not address a difficult problem in a better way than previous research, and it does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper proposes a novel approach to speeding up Gibbs sampling.
* The authors combine stochastic and deterministic inference methods, which is an under-explored area of research.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The paper lacks clear method development and strong experimental evaluation.
* The experiments are unconvincing, and the practical benefit of the approach is unclear.
* There are no comparisons to relevant baselines, such as blocked Gibbs or Gogate's Lifted Gibbs Sampler.
