This paper proposes a principled approximation scheme for inference in Ising MRFs by projecting a given model onto a set of fast-mixing models defined by a spectral-norm bound on the absolute edge-strength matrix, while preserving the original sparsity pattern. The projection is defined under several divergences between Gibbs distributions: Euclidean, KL(θ||ψ), a piecewise KL built on tractable subgraphs, and the reverse KL KL(ψ||θ). Euclidean projection is solved via a dual formulation with sparsity constraints and L-BFGS-B; the other divergences use projected-gradient with Euclidean proximal steps. For reverse KL, the projection procedure nests Gibbs sampling on the projected (guaranteed fast-mixing) model. Experiments on small grids and random graphs compare marginal errors to exact marginals and report accuracy–time tradeoffs vs LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original model. Results consistently favor the proposed projections—especially reverse KL—over TRW/MF/LBP, and show advantages over original-parameter Gibbs at comparable runtimes.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects to variational approximations (MF, structured MF, TRW, planar decompositions) and to mixing-time criteria (Dobrushin; spectral norm bounds of Dyer/Goldberg/Jerrum; Hayes). The novelty is to use “fast mixing” as the tractable family and explicitly project onto it while preserving graph structure.

Quality: The technical development is sound, with a clear dual for Euclidean projection and reasonable algorithms for other divergences. Empirical claims are well supported on small models, though runtime accounting needs clarification (see below).

Clarity: Generally clear, with a good background on mixing bounds. Please clarify algorithmic details and figure axes as requested below.

Originality: The idea of projecting onto fast-mixing sets under multiple divergences, with graph-structure preservation and a workable reverse-KL scheme, is novel.

Significance: Promising direction that bridges MCMC mixing theory and approximate inference. Practical impact depends on scalability and runtime.

Requests for clarification and suggestions:
- Sec. 5.1 projected-gradient: the inner Euclidean projection uses L-BFGS-B; please specify stopping criteria/tolerances for the inner loop, and how nesting affects convergence guarantees.
- Figure 2: the x-axis “number of samples” is unclear since sampling is only used for reverse KL; likely you intend runtime. Please report absolute running times (seconds) including projection overhead, and add runtimes for LBP/TRW/MF for a fair comparison.
- You use a spectral bound of 2.5 despite Theorem 4 requiring <1.0. How do you ensure fast mixing in practice? Provide diagnostics (e.g., autocorrelation, effective sample size) or theoretical justification.
- Are node fields altered by projection? Please state explicitly.
- Scalability: add experiments on larger graphs (e.g., tractable random-field ensembles such as those used by Boris Flach) to assess SVD/projection costs and reverse-KL sampling overhead.

Minor edits: line 222 “onto the tree” → “onto graph T”; line 226 “use subgradient” instead of “derivative.”

Pros: conceptually novel; solid derivations; strong empirical accuracy; preserves graph structure. Cons: runtime/overhead unclear; conservative mixing bound; limited scale; hyperparameter sensitivity for reverse KL.

Overall: interesting and convincing empirically, but clarify runtimes and scalability to strengthen practical utility.
