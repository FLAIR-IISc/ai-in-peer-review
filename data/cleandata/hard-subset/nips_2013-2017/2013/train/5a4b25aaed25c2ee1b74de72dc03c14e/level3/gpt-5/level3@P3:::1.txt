Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes an inference strategy for strongly coupled pairwise Ising models that “projects” model parameters onto a nearby parameter set that guarantees rapid mixing for single-site Gibbs. The fast-mixing set is defined via spectral-norm bounds on the dependency matrix, building on results of Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum and Hayes. The authors present several projections: an l2 projection via an SVD-thresholding-based dual, a tractable piecewise KL built from low-treewidth subgraphs, and a stochastic optimization of reverse KL KL(ψ||θ) with repeated Euclidean projections; they discuss inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ) for toy cases. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs show regimes where sampling from the projected model yields better marginals within fixed sampling budgets than Gibbs on the original model and several variational baselines.

The work connects to classic Dobrushin-type conditions and spectral criteria for rapid mixing, to VI (MF, TRW, LBP), and to piecewise/tree-based approximations. The central idea—treat the set of rapidly-mixing models as the “tractable family” and explicitly project onto it for approximate inference—appears novel and is elegant.

Quality
- Theory: Using ||R||2 < 1 as a sufficient condition and the bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is standard, but several technical points need tightening. Lemma 5 is stated with only a pointer (“simple modifications”) to Hayes; please provide a proof sketch or a precise citation. Theorem 6 (“standard result”) is used without a reference; cite a matrix analysis source and clarify the c/2 thresholding (is this due to symmetric splitting over ± edges?). The dual for the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is important; Section 4 should offer more intuition for the variables (M, Λ), the Πc[·] operator, and the triple-dot product, and state assumptions ensuring strong duality and convergence.
- Guarantees vs practice: The paper commonly enforces a spectral bound of 2.5 “in practice,” beyond the proven < 1 threshold. This breaks the guarantee that ψ is fast-mixing, yet it is central to empirical performance. Please either (i) provide empirical evidence of mixing times as the bound varies and justify c > 1 with theory (e.g., model classes where the bound is known loose), or (ii) keep c ≤ 1 in the main results and relegate c > 1 to an ablation.
- Complexity: The computational cost of the projection is nontrivial (LBFGS-B over Λ, M with repeated SVD-like operations and spectral projections). The paper states that projection time is not counted in Figure 2 comparisons. This invalidates the time-accuracy claims. Report wall-clock time including projection, gradient sampling, and final sampling, and compare against baselines under the same budget.
- Dependency matrix tractability: R is defined via a max over configurations; for Ising you upper bound Rij by tanh|βij|, converting R ≤ |B| elementwise. Please make this explicit and discuss tractability and tightness. Also discuss whether the “maximization” implicit in R matters for non-binary variables, where analogous bounds are weaker.
- Experiments: Results are limited to small graphs (10-node ER, 8×8 grids). It is unclear whether gains persist as size grows. Provide experiments on larger grids (e.g., 16×16, 32×32) and/or symmetric models with known/tractable marginals (planar zero-field Ising, strong/weak coupling regimes), and use very long chains or coupling-from-the-past where possible to establish ground truth. Include the “best-case” inclusive-KL projection via junction tree on toy models to bound the achievable error. The reported gap between MF (reverse-KL with zero spectral norm) and reverse-KL projection is large; analyze why—e.g., how much of the gain comes from allowing weak edge couplings vs improved marginals via sampling?
- Generality: The approach is tailored to binary Ising. Extending to multi-state MRFs is flagged as future work, but the paper should discuss what bounds on R (or alternatives) would be needed and whether analogous projections are computationally feasible.

Clarity
- Section 4 is hard to follow. Define g(Λ, M), Πc[·], Z, and the “triple dot product” before use; give a short roadmap and intuition for the dual; and explain how sparsity is preserved despite spectral projections that tend to densify matrices.
- Several “standard results” (e.g., Theorem 6) and Lemma 5 need references and/or proof sketches.
- Notation: In the Introduction, the description of mean-field says “given intractable q, minimize KL(p||q) over p ∈ TRACT,” which reverses the conventional VI roles (typically minimize KL(q||p), q ∈ TRACT). Please fix throughout. Also ensure consistent use of θ (target) and ψ (approximate) across sections.
- Theorem numbering and cross-referencing (e.g., Theorem 4 vs Section 4) should be cleaned up to avoid confusion.

Originality
- Projecting onto a fast-mixing set as an inference primitive is, to my knowledge, new, although the bounds and SVD thresholding are classical. The piecewise KL over low-treewidth subgraphs is a reasonable twist on piecewise likelihood/mixtures of trees. The reverse-KL-with-projection algorithm can be viewed as a generalization of MF.

Significance
- If borne out at scale and with fair timing, the idea could influence sampling-based inference and MCMC-based learning by providing principled constraints that ensure rapid mixing. However, current experiments are small, time accounting is incomplete, and guarantees are relaxed in practice, making significance uncertain. The applicability beyond Ising is also unclear.

Suggestions for improvement
- Report full wall-clock budgets including projection; provide curves that trade projection effort vs inference time; add ablations over spectral bound c.
- Add larger-model experiments and scenarios with tractable ground truth (planar zero-field; long chains; CFTP).
- Explore and report the inclusive-KL projection on toy models as an upper bound; discuss why reverse-KL projection substantially outperforms MF and what structural couplings it preserves.
- Detail the computational complexity per projection iteration (SVD or power method complexity, number of LBFGS-B steps) and memory; discuss scalability.
- Clarify dependency-matrix bounds and generalization to multi-state variables.
- Improve Section 4 presentation; add references for Theorem 6; provide proof sketch or appendix details for Lemma 5; fix KL notation in Sec. 1.

Pros
- Elegant, novel framing of “tractability” via rapid mixing and projection.
- Sensible use of recent spectral mixing bounds; practical algorithms proposed.
- Promising empirical regimes where projected models beat variational methods and original-model Gibbs under time limits.

Cons
- Timing comparisons exclude projection cost; current claims about time-accuracy are not supported.
- Guarantees not enforced in practice (c > 1), undermining the main premise.
- Limited experimental scale and lack of ground-truth checks; uncertain scalability.
- Clarity and citation issues; key results stated without adequate references or proofs.
- Unclear generalization beyond binary Ising.

Overall
An appealing idea with solid connections to mixing-time theory and some promising initial results, but the experimental methodology and clarity need substantial strengthening, and the practical scalability remains unclear. I would lean toward rejection in its current form, but I encourage the authors to revise with fair timing, larger models, and clearer theory/presentation.
