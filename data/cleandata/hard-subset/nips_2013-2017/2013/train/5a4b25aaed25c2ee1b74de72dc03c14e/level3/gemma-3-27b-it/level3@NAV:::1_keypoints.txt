- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto nearby “fast-mixing” models using spectral bounds to speed Gibbs sampling.
- Explores several projection methods using different divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL).
- Experiments on small Ising models show projected models can outperform variational methods and original-model Gibbs within fixed computation time.
- Builds on prior rapid-mixing results and presents projecting to rapid-mixing models as a novel inference tool.
- Observes mean-field as a special case of reversed-KL projection.
- Major scalability concern: experiments limited to very small models, unclear if gains hold for larger, realistic problems.
- Critical omission: projection computation cost is not included in timing comparisons, potentially biasing results.
- Applicability beyond binary variables is unclear because convergence bounds are harder to derive for non-binary states.
- Clarity issues: several “standard results” (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5) lack references or proof sketches, hurting reproducibility.
- Insufficient discussion of the dependency matrix R, its maximization, and tractability implications.
- Section 4 (notation g, M, Lambda and Theorem 4) is opaque and needs reorganization for readability.
- Notational inconsistency in KL divergence usage in Section 1 should be corrected.
- Experimental flaws: omission of projection computation time in figures and reliance on overly simplistic models.
- Suggested experimental improvements: use symmetric models with tractable marginals or long-chain sampling as ground truth, and test larger models.
- The large error gap for reverse-KL projection is observed but not adequately explained.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel application of rapid-mixing theory, empirical improvements in small-scale tests, and a well-motivated problem.
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability doubts, omitted projection cost, unclear technical exposition, and limited experimental validation.
- Overall recommendation: reject now, with encouragement to address scalability, clarity, and expand empirical evaluation.
