Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a new notion of tractability for inference in Ising models: instead of requiring exact inference on low-treewidth structures, it constrains parameters so that univariate Gibbs sampling is provably fast-mixing. The sufficient condition is formulated via the dependency matrix R; for Ising models, Rij ≤ tanh(|βij|) and fast mixing is guaranteed whenever ||R||2 < 1 (Hayes; Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum). Building on this, the authors propose “projecting” arbitrary Ising parameters θ onto a fast-mixing set Ψ under different divergences:

- Euclidean projection: compute ψ = argminψ∈Ψ ||θ − ψ||2, solved via a dual that enforces both the spectral-norm constraint and sparsity (mask Z), avoiding graph densification inherent in simple singular value thresholding.
- KL(θ||ψ) (“zero-avoiding”): theoretically appealing but impractical at scale because it requires expectations under θ.
- Piecewise KL: approximate KL by restricting to tractable subgraphs (trees/low-treewidth), inspired by piecewise likelihood and mixtures of trees.
- KL(ψ||θ) (“zero-forcing”): practical because expectations are under ψ; minimized via stochastic approximation with a “sample pool” and projected gradient descent back onto Ψ. This connects to mean-field as a limiting case where the spectral norm bound is zero.

Experiments compare projected models (with Gibbs on ψ) against LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on θ on 8×8 grids and small random graphs, across interaction regimes. The main empirical claim is that when interactions are strong and sampling time is limited, Gibbs on projected fast-mixing parameters outperforms Gibbs on the original parameters and common variational baselines.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The overall idea is sound and well-grounded in mixing-time theory. The Euclidean projection dual (Theorem 7) is potentially useful, but the derivation and constraints are not sufficiently clear. In particular:
  - The optimization uses D = |B| and enforces Z ∘ D = 0 to preserve sparsity, but the paper does not clearly explain how a feasible B (with signs) is recovered from D, nor whether the feasible set defined by D = |B| and the mask is a tight characterization of the spectral-norm constraint on R = |B|. Does this introduce additional conservatism (i.e., a smaller feasible set than necessary)?
  - The dual objective g(Λ, M) and its gradients are stated, but the steps from the primal (Eq. 1) to the dual (Eq. 2–4) are hard to follow. A more complete derivation and a discussion of convexity/duality gap would strengthen confidence.
- Algorithmic procedure: For KL(ψ||θ), the paper alternates stochastic gradient steps using samples from ψ with Euclidean projections back to Ψ. However, the procedure is described narratively and remains ambiguous (e.g., when exactly projection is applied, whether one fully optimizes Euclidean projection per iteration, and how sampling, gradient estimation, and projection are interleaved). An explicit algorithm box would resolve this.
- Convergence: The paper cites Ergodic Mirror Descent for justification, but the experiments adopt a fixed step size and a single Gibbs pass per iteration; these settings do not meet the conditions for standard convergence guarantees. A discussion of what is guaranteed under the chosen schedule (and what is not) is needed.
- Use of a relaxed spectral bound: Although the theory guarantees fast mixing for ||R||2 < 1, the experiments use 2.5 “because it works.” This undermines the stated guarantee and should be justified empirically (e.g., show empirical mixing diagnostics/autocorrelation under this choice) or replaced with adaptive tuning that respects the guarantee.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written, but several key pieces are unclear:
- The exact projection mapping from (A, Z, c) to ψ should be detailed, including how signs are handled and how sparsity is preserved. Clarify whether the constraint Z ∘ D = 0 suffices to ensure the projected ψ lies in the same edge set; explain whether any interactions migrate to non-edges through the dual update.
- The full optimization loop for KL(ψ||θ) (sampling, gradient estimation, projection, step sizes, stopping criteria) should be presented as a clear algorithm with inputs/outputs.
- The experimental methodology should include error bars and statistical tests, and it should be clear how many runs/seeds are aggregated for each curve.

Originality
The framing of fast-mixing distributions as a tractable family and the idea of projecting onto this family under divergences is novel and interesting, and the dual projection approach that preserves sparsity is a useful technical contribution. The “piecewise KL” idea, while inspired by existing piecewise likelihood/mixtures-of-trees, is a reasonable and novel adaptation to this setting.

Significance
If validated more thoroughly, this approach could become a practical tool: when strong couplings make standard Gibbs painfully slow, a principled projection to a fast-mixing approximation with guaranteed rapid convergence could offer a favorable time–accuracy tradeoff. The connection to mean-field as a special case is appealing. However, stronger empirical evidence and clearer algorithms are needed to assess practical impact.

Empirical evaluation
- Fairness: Comparisons based on iteration counts (e.g., fixed 30k Gibbs iterations) are not wall-clock fair across methods, and projection time is excluded. Please include wall-clock or flop-normalized budgets that account for both projection and sampling.
- Baselines: Include stronger MCMC baselines designed for slow-mixing regimes (e.g., blocked Gibbs, tree/block sampling, Swendsen–Wang for Ising, and lifted Gibbs sampling as in Gogate et al.) and report their performance under the same compute budget.
- Diagnostics: Report effective sample sizes, autocorrelations, or spectral gap proxies to substantiate “fast mixing” empirically, especially when using c = 2.5. Provide error bars across trials.
- Ablations: Show sensitivity to the spectral norm bound c, pool size, number of Gibbs updates per iteration, and step size; compare Euclidean vs piecewise vs KL(ψ||θ) projections under matched budgets.

Pros
- Conceptually novel tractable family (fast-mixing) with solid theoretical underpinnings.
- Practical projected SGD method for KL(ψ||θ), connecting to and generalizing mean-field.
- Dual Euclidean projection that aims to preserve sparsity while enforcing a spectral constraint.

Cons
- Dual derivation and constraints are under-specified; unclear mapping back to signed parameters.
- Ambiguity in the overall algorithm; lack of an explicit, reproducible procedure; no convergence guarantees under the chosen schedule.
- Experimental evaluation is not wall-clock fair, lacks error bars, excludes projection cost, and omits strong sampling baselines.
- Use of a spectral bound above the theoretical threshold weakens the main guarantee without empirical mixing diagnostics.

Questions for the authors
- How is B (with signs) constructed from D = |B| in the dual projection? Is sign(A) used, or are signs optimized?
- Is the feasible set defined by Z ∘ D = 0 and ||D||2 ≤ c equivalent to the desired constraint on R = |B|, or strictly smaller?
- Do you have empirical mixing diagnostics demonstrating rapid mixing for c = 2.5 across your instances?
- Can you provide a clear algorithm box for the KL(ψ||θ) projected SGD, including projection frequency, stopping criteria, and step-size policy?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and relevant to NIPS, and the technical direction is interesting. However, the method description (especially the dual projection) needs clarification, and the empirical evaluation must be strengthened with fair timing, stronger baselines, error bars, and mixing diagnostics. I encourage resubmission after addressing these issues.
