- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set to guarantee rapid Gibbs convergence.  
- Uses spectral bounds on the dependency matrix to define a tractable parameter subset and develops projection methods under Euclidean, KL, and piecewise-KL divergences.  
- Main technical contribution is a dual method for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) applying spectral norm constraints to Ising models.  
- Strength: elegant theoretical link between spectral bounds and mixing times, building on prior work (Dyer et al., Hayes).  
- Strength: empirical results on small models show projected Gibbs sampling can outperform variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) under fixed budgets.  
- Conceptually fresh idea to use fast-mixing models as a tractable family for inference.  
- Major concern: experiments omit the computational cost of projection (e.g., 30k+ Gibbs iterations for Euclidean projection), biasing comparisons.  
- Major concern: scalability not demonstrated—experiments limited to very small graphs (10 nodes, 8x8 grids), so generalizability is unclear.  
- Presentation issues: missing proofs for Theorem 6 and Lemma 5, confusing notation in Section 4 (g, M, Lambda), and reversed KL convention in Section 1.  
- The paper does not address the looseness of the spectral norm bound (conservative ~1.76× for 2D Ising) or analyze its practical implications.  
- Fails to explore potentially stronger alternatives like KL(θ||ψ) projection or discuss when such projections give best-case behavior.  
- Recommendation: needs larger-scale experiments, clearer technical exposition, and explicit accounting/discussion of projection computational cost to establish practical significance; current contribution limited to binary Ising models.
