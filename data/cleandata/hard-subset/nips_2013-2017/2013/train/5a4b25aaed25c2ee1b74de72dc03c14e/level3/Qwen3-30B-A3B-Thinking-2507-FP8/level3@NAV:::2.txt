

This paper presents an interesting approach to accelerating Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set defined by the Dobrushin criterion. The core idea—using constrained optimization under various divergences to find parameters that guarantee rapid mixing—is novel and potentially valuable for practitioners facing slow mixing in high-treewidth models. The authors explore multiple divergence measures (Euclidean, piecewise KL, reversed KL) and demonstrate that their projected parameters yield better accuracy than standard variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) within limited sampling time, which aligns with recent work on "tractable" inference families (e.g., Hazan & Shashua, 2008).

The paper's main strengths lie in its conceptual contribution to defining "fast-mixing" as a tractability criterion and its exploration of combining stochastic (reversed KL) and deterministic (Euclidean, piecewise) inference. However, several critical issues undermine the paper's technical contribution. The dual formulation in Theorem 7 is poorly explained, and the constraints zij*dij=0 raise concerns about whether the projected space remains equivalent to the original parameter space (could it be a smaller subspace?). The experimental procedure is ambiguous: it's unclear whether projection is performed once before sampling or interleaved with sampling, and the lack of an algorithm box makes replication difficult. The projected gradient descent's convergence is unaddressed, and the 30K Gibbs iterations for projection (vs. none for baseline) is arbitrary and makes iteration-based comparisons unfair to deterministic methods.

Experiments suffer from multiple weaknesses: no timing comparisons (ignoring projection runtime), missing error bars, and no comparisons to blocked Gibbs or Gogate's lifted sampler. The claim that "Gibbs sampling on projected parameters is more accurate" is misleading without accounting for total computational cost. While the idea is promising, the technical execution lacks clarity, and the evaluation fails to demonstrate practical benefits. The paper would benefit significantly from a clearer methodological description, rigorous convergence guarantees, and a fairer experimental comparison that includes runtime. The core concept has potential but requires substantial refinement before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field.
