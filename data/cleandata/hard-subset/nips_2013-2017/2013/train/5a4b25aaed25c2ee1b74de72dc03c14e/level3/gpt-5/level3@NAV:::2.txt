Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a subset that guarantees rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs by enforcing Dobrushin-style spectral constraints on the dependency matrix. The projection is posed as a constrained optimization under several divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, and reverse KL), with a dual formulation enabling SVD-thresholding while preserving sparsity. The stochastic optimization for KL(ψ||θ) interleaves sampling and projection, tying MCMC and variational ideas. This connects to classic mixing-time bounds (Dobrushin; Hayes; Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum) and complements variational methods (MF/TRW/LBP, GBP, planar/structured approximations, piecewise training), offering an alternative tractable family defined by fast-mixing rather than low treewidth. The idea of combining deterministic projections with stochastic gradients is interesting and relatively under-explored in NeurIPS literature.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation: when Gibbs on the target is slow, project to a fast-mixing surrogate and sample there.
- Technical novelty in formulating and solving the constrained projection with sparsity plus spectral norm via a dual and SVD thresholding.
- Exploring multiple divergences provides a useful view of the design space.
- Potentially impactful if the approach yields better time–accuracy tradeoffs in practice.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Dual is under-specified. Please provide a clearer derivation (Lagrangian, handling D=|B|, conditions for strong duality), and explain the roles and dimensions of Λ and M, plus convergence/termination criteria of LBFGS-B.
- Clarify how ZijDij=0 with D=|B| preserves the original parameter space on edges. Does the combination with spectral projection reduce feasible sign patterns or shrink the space? A short proof or counterexample would help.
- The overall procedure is ambiguous. Do you fully solve the projection per outer step or interleave projection and sampling every iteration? An explicit algorithm box with initialization, gradient estimation, projection step, constraint checks, step-size schedule, and stopping rules is needed.
- Convergence: Is projected (stochastic) gradient descent guaranteed to converge under your constraints? If you appeal to Ergodic Mirror Descent, state assumptions (mixing per iteration, diminishing steps) and whether your fixed 0.1 step and single sweep meet them.
- Experimental design is unconvincing. Use wall-clock budgets including projection time; report runtime per method; add error bars over 50 trials; justify the 30K-iteration choice; compare against stronger MCMC baselines (blocked Gibbs, Swendsen–Wang, tree sampling) and lifted Gibbs (Gogate). Include mixing diagnostics or effective sample sizes. The use of a spectral bound 2.5 despite a 1.0 guarantee should be justified (measured spectral gaps or empirical R norms).
- Ablations: show accuracy vs spectral bound; piecewise subgraph choices; impact of pool size/steps; convergence plots.

Qualitative assessment:
- Quality: Conceptually sound, but theoretical and empirical support are currently insufficiently rigorous.
- Clarity: Needs significant improvement in Sections 4–5 and in the algorithmic description.
- Originality: Good; the projection-to-fast-mixing perspective is novel.
- Significance: Promising, but requires clearer method development and fair, time-normalized experiments to demonstrate practical benefit.

Pros: novel idea; potentially useful hybrid inference; dual-based projection. Cons: methodological ambiguities; lack of convergence guarantees; unfair and incomplete empirical evaluation.
