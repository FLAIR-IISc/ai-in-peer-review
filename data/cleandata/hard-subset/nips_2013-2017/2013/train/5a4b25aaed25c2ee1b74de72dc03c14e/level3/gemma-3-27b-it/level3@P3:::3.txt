This paper proposes a novel approach to inference in Ising models by projecting model parameters onto a space guaranteeing fast mixing of Gibbs sampling. The core idea – trading off model fidelity for faster convergence – is well-motivated, addressing a key bottleneck in applying MCMC methods to complex graphical models. The use of different divergence measures to define the projection and the resulting algorithmic variations are valuable contributions. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over existing methods like TRW, MF, and LBP, and competitive performance with Gibbs sampling, particularly given runtime constraints.

The work builds upon a rich literature in approximate inference, particularly methods leveraging tractable structures (e.g., mean-field, belief propagation). It distinguishes itself by focusing on the mixing properties of the model itself, rather than approximating the marginals directly. This is a subtle but important shift. Related work in spectral norm bounds for mixing times (e.g., [11], cited in the paper) is appropriately acknowledged. The paper clearly situates itself within the context of NIPS research, referencing relevant contributions in graphical models and optimization.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing space is a fresh perspective on approximate inference.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The connection to spectral norm bounds for mixing times provides a solid theoretical foundation. Theorem 7, offering a dual formulation for the Euclidean projection, is a valuable technical result.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods, particularly the reversed KL divergence projection.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with clear explanations of the key concepts.
* **Comprehensive comparison:** The comparison against a range of established inference algorithms provides a strong baseline for evaluating the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Runtime concerns:** Despite the accuracy improvements, the relatively high runtime remains a significant concern. This limits the practical utility of the methods, especially for larger models.
* **Ambiguous runtime reporting:** The x-axis in Figure 2 labeled “number of samples” is misleading, as sampling is only used for the reversed KL projection. Reporting absolute running times in seconds for all methods, including LBP, TRW, and MF, is crucial for a fair comparison.
* **Scalability limitations:** The experiments are limited to relatively small models. Evaluating scalability on larger, more complex instances is essential to assess the real-world applicability of the approach. Exploring tractable random-field models, such as those from Boris Flach, would be beneficial.
* **Spectral norm bound tightness:** The discussion in Section 7 acknowledges the potential looseness of the spectral norm bound. Investigating tighter bounds or adaptive strategies for setting the bound could improve performance.

**Clarifications:**

* **Sec. 5.1 – Projected-gradient stopping criteria:** The nested loops within the projected-gradient algorithm in Section 5.1 require clarification. Specifically, what are the stopping criteria for the inner L-BFGS-B iterations? Are fixed iterations used, or is a convergence tolerance employed?
* **Fig. 2 – X-axis Label:** As mentioned above, the x-axis label in Figure 2 ("number of samples") is misleading and should be replaced with "runtime" (in seconds).

**Minor Edits:**

* Line 222: Replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T".
* Line 226: Use "subgradient" instead of "derivative".

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to approximate inference.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
* The work addresses a challenging problem in graphical model inference and offers a potentially valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The runtime performance is a significant limitation, potentially hindering practical adoption.
* The scalability of the methods remains unproven.
* Clarification is needed regarding the stopping criteria for the inner iterations of the projected-gradient algorithm and the runtime reporting in Figure 2.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting and convincing paper with a potentially valuable contribution to the field of graphical model inference. The empirical results are strong, and the theoretical foundation is solid. However, the practical utility of the methods is currently limited by the relatively high runtime. Addressing the clarifications and concerns raised above, particularly regarding runtime reporting and scalability, would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon a satisfactory rebuttal addressing the concerns outlined above. A detailed response clarifying the runtime behavior and outlining plans for scalability testing is critical.
