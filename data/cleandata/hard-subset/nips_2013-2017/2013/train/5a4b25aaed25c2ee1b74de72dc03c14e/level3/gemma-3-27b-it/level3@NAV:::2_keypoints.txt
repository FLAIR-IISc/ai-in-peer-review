- Novel idea: project Ising model parameters to satisfy the Dobrushin criterion to accelerate Gibbs sampling.  
- Builds on prior tractable approximation work (mean-field, tree-based methods).  
- Promising hybrid of stochastic (gradient descent) and deterministic (spectral projection) inference, but under-explored.  
- Strong theoretical development: projection operator (Theorem 7) and dual derivation for Euclidean projection are notable contributions.  
- Practical description is unclear, making implementation difficult to follow.  
- Algorithmic flow is ambiguous: unclear if projection is one-time or interleaved with sampling—an algorithm box is needed.  
- Convergence of the projected gradient descent strategy is not addressed.  
- Experimental evaluation is unconvincing and potentially biased by choices like 30,000 Gibbs iterations.  
- Iteration-based comparisons are misleading given differing per-iteration computational costs.  
- No timing analysis for the projection step, so practical runtime benefits are unknown.  
- Plots lack error bars, preventing assessment of statistical significance.  
- Missing comparisons to relevant samplers (e.g., blocked Gibbs, Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs Sampler).  
- Authors acknowledge looseness in the spectral norm bound, indicating potential limitations.  
- Overall: promising theory but needs clearer method description, convergence analysis, and fairer, timed, statistically-analyzed experiments.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel projection approach, solid theoretical work, exploration of multiple divergence measures.  
- Cons for acceptance: unclear algorithmic description, weak experiments, missing timing/statistical analysis, lack of relevant baselines, and no convergence guarantee.
