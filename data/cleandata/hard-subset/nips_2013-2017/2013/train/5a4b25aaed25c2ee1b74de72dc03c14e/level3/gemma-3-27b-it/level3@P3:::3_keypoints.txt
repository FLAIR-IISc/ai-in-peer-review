- Core idea: Project Ising model parameters onto a space guaranteeing fast-mixing Gibbs sampling, trading model fidelity for faster convergence.  
- Contribution: Uses multiple divergence measures to define projections and derives algorithmic variations, including a dual formulation for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7).  
- Motivation: Addresses MCMC mixing bottlenecks in complex graphical models by focusing on model mixing properties rather than directly approximating marginals.  
- Empirical claim: Experiments show consistent improvements over TRW, MF, and LBP, and competitive performance with Gibbs sampling under runtime constraints, with reversed KL projection particularly effective.  
- Novelty: Projection to a fast-mixing parameter space is a fresh perspective on approximate inference.  
- Theoretical grounding: Builds on spectral norm bounds for mixing times and provides solid theoretical support.  
- Presentation: Paper is well-written, logically organized, and situates itself within relevant literature.  
- Baselines: Comprehensive comparison against established inference methods strengthens the evaluation.  
- Runtime weakness: High runtime of the proposed methods is a major practical concern, especially for larger models.  
- Misleading plot labeling: Figure 2’s x-axis “number of samples” is misleading; runtime in seconds should be reported for all methods.  
- Scalability concern: Experiments are limited to small models; scalability to larger, complex instances is unproven and should be tested (e.g., tractable random-field models).  
- Spectral bound looseness: The potential looseness of the spectral norm bound warrants investigation of tighter or adaptive bounds.  
- Clarification needed: Specify stopping criteria for inner L-BFGS-B iterations in the projected-gradient algorithm (fixed iterations vs. convergence tolerance).  
- Figure correction: Replace Figure 2 x-axis label with "runtime (seconds)".  
- Minor edits: Change "onto the tree" (Line 222) to "onto graph T" and use "subgradient" instead of "derivative" (Line 226).  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, theoretically grounded method with compelling empirical results addressing a challenging inference problem.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Significant runtime and unproven scalability may limit practical adoption; clarifications on inner-loop stopping and runtime reporting are needed.  
- Overall recommendation: Interesting and convincing paper recommended for acceptance with minor revisions contingent on addressing runtime reporting, clarifying inner-loop behavior, and outlining scalability evaluation plans.
