This paper proposes a novel approach to improve the efficiency of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model parameters onto a set that guarantees fast mixing. The authors explore various divergences to achieve this projection, including Euclidean distance, KL-divergence, and piecewise KL-divergence. The main idea is to find a set of parameters that are close to the original parameters but satisfy the Dobrushin criterion for fast mixing.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear overview of the challenges in inference in high-treewidth graphical models. The proposed approach is interesting and under-explored, and the combination of stochastic and deterministic inference is a promising direction.

However, there are several areas where the paper can be improved. The description of the dual of the projection problem is unclear, and the constraints zij * dij = 0 are not well-explained. It is unclear how these constraints guarantee that the new parameterization spans the same space as the original parameters. The overall procedure is also ambiguous, and it is unclear whether the authors fully optimize the projection and then sample or interleave projection updates with sampling.

The experimental evaluation is also unconvincing. The choice of 30,000 Gibbs iterations is arbitrary, and the iteration-based comparisons are unfair versus deterministic methods. There are no timing comparisons, and the projection runtime is not accounted for, making it unclear what the practical benefit of the proposed approach is. The plots also lack error bars, making it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the results.

Furthermore, there are no comparisons to other state-of-the-art methods, such as blocked Gibbs or Gogate's Lifted Gibbs Sampler. The authors should consider including these comparisons to demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.

In terms of the quality of the paper, the authors are technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. However, the clarity of the paper could be improved, and the authors should consider providing more details on the implementation of their approach.

Overall, the paper has a promising idea, but it needs clearer method development and stronger, fairer experimental evaluation. With some revisions to address these issues, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
