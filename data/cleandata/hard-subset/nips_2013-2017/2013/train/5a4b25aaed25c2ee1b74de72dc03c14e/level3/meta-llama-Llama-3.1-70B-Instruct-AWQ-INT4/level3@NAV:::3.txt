The paper "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing" presents an innovative approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models. The authors propose projecting the intractable distribution onto a "fast-mixing" set, which is guaranteed to converge rapidly to the stationary distribution under Gibbs sampling. This approach is based on a novel notion of "tractability" that focuses on parameter-space conditions rather than graph structure.

The paper is technically sound, with a clear and well-organized presentation of the theoretical framework and algorithmic developments. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the mixing time bounds for Ising models and derive a projection algorithm for the Euclidean distance. They also extend this approach to more probabilistic divergences, including a novel "piecewise" divergence and the reversed KL-divergence.

The experimental evaluation is convincing, with a comparison to several standard variational methods and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters. The results show that the proposed methods are more accurate than variational approximations and can outperform Gibbs sampling with finite time.

However, there are some limitations and potential areas for improvement. The spectral norm bound used to guarantee fast mixing is somewhat conservative, and a tighter bound would be more informative. Additionally, the authors acknowledge that the practical utility of the approach may be limited by high runtime, which needs clarification in the rebuttal.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and original contribution to the field of approximate inference. The ideas are well-motivated, and the technical developments are sound. While there are some limitations, the paper has the potential to advance the state of the art in approximate inference.

**Strengths:**

* Novel notion of "tractability" based on parameter-space conditions
* Clear and well-organized presentation of the theoretical framework
* Convincing experimental evaluation
* Original contribution to the field of approximate inference

**Weaknesses:**

* Spectral norm bound is somewhat conservative
* Practical utility may be limited by high runtime
* Limited scalability to larger models

**Suggestions:**

* Consider including experiments on larger models to test scalability
* Investigate tighter bounds on the spectral norm to guarantee fast mixing
* Clarify the practical utility of the approach in terms of runtime
