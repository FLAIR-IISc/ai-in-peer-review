Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes to approximate intractable Ising models with “fast-mixing” models—parameter settings for which single-site Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to mix rapidly. The core technical idea is to project a given parameter vector θ onto a set Ψ that enforces a spectral-norm bound on the dependency matrix R (sufficient for rapid mixing, following Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum and Hayes). The authors develop: (i) an Euclidean projection using a dual characterization that combines SVD thresholding with sparsity constraints to preserve the original graph structure; (ii) a projection under the inclusive KL(θ||ψ), which is conceptually appealing but impractical at scale because it requires expectations under θ; (iii) a “piecewise” KL based on tractable subgraphs; and (iv) a projection under the reverse KL(ψ||θ), optimized by stochastic approximation using samples from ψ, which remains fast-mixing.

This contributes a novel angle on “tractable families”: instead of enforcing low treewidth (as in mean-field, structured mean-field, TRW, planar approximations), the family is defined by fast-mixing conditions for Gibbs. The paper leverages classical mixing-time criteria (Dobrushin-type and spectral-norm bounds), relates to mean-field via KL(ψ||θ), and is complementary to tree-based approximations (LBP/TRW), planar decompositions (Globerson–Jaakkola), and block/tree sampling methods (Hamze–de Freitas, Swendsen–Wang).

Quality:
- Theory: The spectral-norm condition ||R||2 < 1 provides a clean, sufficient guarantee for rapid mixing (Theorem 4) and the Ising-specific bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| (Lemma 5) is appropriate. The Euclidean projection via Theorem 7 is technically interesting and addresses both spectral norm control and sparsity preservation via a dual with masking.
- Clarity of the optimization: The constraint ZijDij = 0 enforces the sparsity pattern via a mask Z, while D = |B| ties the controlled matrix to the absolute values of pairwise parameters. However, it remains unclear in the text how this ensures the projected parameterization “lives on the same space” (i.e., exactly the same edge set), rather than a strict subset; an explicit statement that the mask forbids creation/removal of edges and that signs are reintroduced after operating on |B| would help.
- Reverse KL optimization: The stochastic projected gradient approach is reasonable, and the link to Ergodic Mirror Descent is appropriate. But the convergence discussion is incomplete: the experiments use a fixed step size and a fixed number of Gibbs updates per iteration, whereas EMD requires schedules for both. At minimum, the method should be presented as heuristic with a brief discussion of when the conditions for convergence are met.
- Guarantees vs practice: In experiments the spectral bound used is 2.5 (vs the theoretical guarantee c = 1). This relaxes the guarantee and should be framed explicitly as a heuristic trade-off; reporting empirical diagnostics that mixing remains fast under this choice would strengthen the claims.

Clarity:
The manuscript is generally well written, but several points would benefit from clarification:
- Provide a compact algorithm box for each projection (Euclidean and reverse KL), indicating the interleaving of sampling, gradient updates, and Euclidean projections, along with inputs/outputs and stopping criteria.
- Clarify the role of Z, D = |B|, and how signs of interactions are handled post-projection; state explicitly that the projected matrix preserves the edge set and symmetry.
- State whether systematic scan vs random-scan Gibbs is used in theory and in practice, and which mixing bound applies.
- For the piecewise KL, specify precisely how the set of subgraphs T is chosen (coverage constraints, treewidth), and how the max over T is computed in practice.
- Include references and brief discussion for block/tree sampling and cluster methods to situate the univariate Gibbs focus.

Originality:
- Using “fast-mixing” distributions as an approximation family and projecting onto that set is a fresh and compelling idea. The dual Euclidean projection that preserves sparsity is a nice technical contribution. The piecewise KL divergence and the reinterpretation of mean-field as a degenerate fast-mixing constraint help position the work relative to classical variational methods.

Significance:
- If backed by stronger empirical evidence, the approach could be impactful: it offers a practical path when exact inference is hard and standard MCMC mixes slowly. However, the current empirical study is limited (small graphs; fixed iteration counts; no wall-clock comparisons; no comparison to block/cluster Gibbs), which makes it hard to assess practical gains across a range of regimes.

Experiments:
- Use wall-clock time (or equivalent total compute budget) rather than a fixed number of iterations when comparing to deterministic methods and to Gibbs on original parameters; include projection overhead in the accounting.
- Include error bars (e.g., mean ± std or 95% CI) over the 50 trials and statistical tests where appropriate.
- Report diagnostics that the “fast-mixing” ψ actually mixes faster: autocorrelation times, effective sample size per second, or spectral gap proxies. Show sensitivity to the spectral bound c (e.g., c ∈ {0.8, 1.0, 1.5, 2.5}) and the fraction of runs violating the theoretical bound.
- Compare to stronger MCMC baselines: block Gibbs (e.g., checkerboard or tree blocks), Swendsen–Wang or related cluster methods, and tree sampling; discuss scope relative to lifted Gibbs if considered out of scope.
- Provide ablations across divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, reverse KL), sample-pool size, step-size schedule, and number of Gibbs updates per iteration. Report the final objective values to correlate optimization progress with marginal errors.

Pros:
- Conceptually novel use of fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractable approximation family.
- Solid theoretical grounding for the mixing-time criterion; clear Ising-specific bounds.
- Practical dual algorithm for Euclidean projection that preserves sparsity.
- Reverse-KL projected stochastic gradient is implementable and potentially broadly useful.

Cons:
- Incomplete clarity on the dual constraints and full algorithmic procedure; no consolidated algorithm pseudocode.
- Convergence guarantees for the stochastic projected method are not established under the experimental settings.
- Empirical evaluation is limited, lacks error bars and wall-clock accounting, and omits comparisons to block/cluster MCMC.
- Use of a spectral bound above the theoretical threshold weakens the main guarantee without compensating empirical justification.

Questions for the authors:
- Please clarify how D = |B| and the mask Z together ensure the projected ψ preserves the original edge set and does not shrink the model class.
- Do you interleave sampling and projection throughout optimization, or complete optimization before any final sampling? A step-by-step algorithm would help.
- Can you provide a schedule for step sizes and number of Gibbs updates per iteration that meets EMD conditions, or explicitly position the current settings as a heuristic?
- How sensitive are results to the spectral bound c? Can you report mixing diagnostics to justify c = 2.5?

Overall assessment:
A compelling idea with a neat projection algorithm and promising preliminary results. With clearer algorithms, justified optimization settings, and stronger, budget-aware empirical comparisons (including error bars and MCMC baselines), this work could make a meaningful contribution. In its current form, I find the paper interesting but would like to see the experimental section strengthened and the algorithmic presentation clarified.
