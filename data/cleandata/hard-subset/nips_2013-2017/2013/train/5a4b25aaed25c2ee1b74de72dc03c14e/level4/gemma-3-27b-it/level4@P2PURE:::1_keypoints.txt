- Paper proposes projecting pairwise Ising models onto nearest fast-mixing models to accelerate Gibbs sampling and improve marginal estimates.
- Core idea: find parameters close to original model while ensuring spectral properties for rapid mixing.
- Experiments on small toy Ising models show improved marginal accuracy vs. Gibbs, mean field, and belief propagation under fixed compute budgets.
- Novelty: framing approximate inference as projection onto rapidly mixing models, building on established mixing condition results [4,8].
- Major concern: scalability to larger, realistic models is unclear and not demonstrated.
- The projection step appears computationally expensive, and its runtime cost is not properly included in comparisons.
- Generalizability to non-binary state spaces is uncertain because convergence bounds are harder to establish.
- Presentation issues: several “standard results” are stated as theorems without references or proof sketches (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5).
- Dependency matrix R needs clearer discussion; tractability is unclear due to the maximization in its definition.
- Section 4 is hard to follow: variables (g, M, Lambda) introduced without definition and Theorem 4 uses unexplained notation.
- Notational inconsistency: KL(q||p) is used ambiguously and q/p roles are reversed in Section 1.
- Experiments: Figure 2’s time comparison likely omits projection time—this must be clarified and included if applicable.
- Recommend additional experiments on larger/intractable regimes, using symmetry-based exact marginals or long-run sampling as ground truth.
- Suggest evaluating KL(θ||ψ) as a “best-case” sampling metric and discussing why reverse KL projection differs substantially from mean field.
- Overall: the approach is innovative and leverages recent mixing theory, but experimental scope, cost accounting, and clarity issues limit confidence in broad applicability.
