The authors introduce a novel approach to enhance the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by reparameterizing the original model to satisfy the Dobrushin criterion, which is essential for rapid mixing. This reparameterization is formulated as a constrained optimization problem, with constraints ensuring that the new parameters remain within the same space as the original parameters. The authors investigate this projection under various distance and divergence measures.

In my view, the integration of stochastic and deterministic inference principles is an underexplored area, making this idea intriguing. Although augmenting the original parameters to improve mixing time is a straightforward concept, the description of the dual of the projection problem is somewhat unclear. For instance, it is unclear how the zij*dij = 0 constraints guarantee that the new parameterization is defined over the same space (could it not be defined over a smaller space?). Furthermore, the overall procedure is ambiguous - is it necessary to complete the projected gradient operations before running the Gibbs sampler, or can the sampling and gradient updates be interleaved? An algorithm description box would be beneficial in clarifying this process. Additionally, is the proposed projected gradient descent strategy guaranteed to converge?

I found the experimental results to be somewhat unconvincing. In the first set of experiments, it is unclear why the Gibbs sampler was run for 30,000 iterations. Given that the comparison is between a sampling method and deterministic methods, a time-based comparison would seem more appropriate. Moreover, the absence of error bars on the charts makes it difficult to determine the significance of the results. The second set of results demonstrates that the projection leads to faster mixing when comparing the original Gibbs sampler with Gibbs samplers under different projections. However, the time required to perform the projection operation is not accounted for in this comparison (e.g., if the projection takes one minute and the naive Gibbs sampler can generate 10,000 samples in that time, the projection may not be worthwhile). Lastly, it is surprising that there was no comparison to blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate's "Lifted Gibbs Sampler".

Overall, this is an interesting idea, but the development of the projection problem and the proposed method could benefit from further refinement. A more comprehensive set of experiments is necessary to convincingly demonstrate the utility of the method.
