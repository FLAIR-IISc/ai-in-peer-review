- Proposes projecting pairwise Ising models onto "fast-mixing" models to improve Gibbs sampling mixing rates.
- Introduces several projection methods to find parameters closest to the original model while satisfying a spectral bound for rapid mixing.
- Experimental results show improved marginal estimates in some regimes compared to variational methods and Gibbs on the original model, on small Ising examples.
- Novelty lies in using nearest rapidly-mixing model as an approximation, though technical basis relies heavily on prior rapid-mixing results [4,8].
- Concern that results may not scale to larger models; unclear how mixing-time improvements behave with problem size.
- The projection step is computationally expensive and its cost appears not to be included in runtime comparisons.
- Unclear whether the approach generalizes beyond binary-state models, since convergence bounds are harder to establish more generally.
- Presentation issues: missing proofs/sketches (e.g., Lemma 5, Theorem 6) and repeated references to "standard results" without adequate attribution.
- Need clarification and discussion of the dependency matrix R, its properties, and tractability (maximization concerns motivating Lemma 5).
- Section 4 is hard to follow (symbols g, M, Lambda referenced before definition); Theorem 4 uses poorly explained notation.
- Paper restates some results from [8] that may not be necessary; suggest focusing or shortening those parts.
- Notation issues: KL(q||p) used inconsistently for both directions; convention of q as true distribution and p as approximation is reversed relative to common practice.
- Experimental comparisons are limited: Figure 2 omits projection computation time and should be revised or clarified.
- Recommend additional experiments on larger or symmetric models, or using long runs/junction tree where possible; investigate KL(θ||ψ) as a best-case benchmark.
- Large discrepancy between mean field error and reverse KL projection error needs explanation, since mean field is a degenerate reverse-KL case.
- Overall: idea is elegant and leverages recent mixing bounds, but concerns remain about experimental validity, scalability, and applicability range.
