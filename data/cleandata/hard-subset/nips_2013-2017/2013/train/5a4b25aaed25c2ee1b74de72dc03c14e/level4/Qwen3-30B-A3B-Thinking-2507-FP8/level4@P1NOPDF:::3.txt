

The paper proposes a novel approach to improve Gibbs sampling inference in Markov random fields (MRFs), specifically two-label models (Ising models), by addressing slow mixing rates. The core idea involves projecting the input model—potentially slow-mixing—onto a set of fast-mixing models while preserving the graph structure. Fast-mixing models are characterized by a spectral norm constraint on the absolute edge strength matrix. Projection is defined via divergences of Gibbs distributions: Euclidean distance uses dualization with LBFGS-B, while KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences employ projected gradient methods. Notably, reversed KL projection requires Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model for computation.  

Experiments compare approximated marginals against ground truth on small random models, evaluating the proposed methods (all divergences) alongside loopy BP, TRW, mean-field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original model. Both accuracy and runtime-accuracy trade-offs are assessed. Results demonstrate consistent superiority over TRW, MF, and loopy BP in accuracy, and for practical runtimes, also over original-model Gibbs sampling. Among the proposed methods, reversed KL divergence yields the best performance.  

**Comments:**  
1. Section 5.1 describes a projected gradient algorithm with nested loops (inner loop: LBFGS-B). Please clarify the stopping criterion for the inner LBFGS-B iterations.  
2. Figure 2 (and supplement) labels the x-axis "number of samples," but sampling is only used in reversed KL projection. This is misleading; the axis should represent algorithm runtime (e.g., in seconds). Additionally, report absolute runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF to enable fair accuracy-runtime comparisons across all methods. Without this, the experimental interpretation is unclear.  
3. Consider extending experiments to larger models. The paper by Flach (2013, *IEEE TPAMI*, available online) presents random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition functions—ideal for testing scalability.  
4. Line 222: Replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T."  
5. Line 226: Should "derivative" be "subgradient"?  
6. The paper presents convincing empirical results. However, the practical utility may be limited by high runtime costs (e.g., reversed KL requires multiple Gibbs samples). This trade-off requires explicit clarification in the rebuttal.
