This work addresses the challenge of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, proposing a novel approach based on projecting the original model onto nearby “fast-mixing” models. The core idea is to identify parameters that approximate the original model while satisfying spectral conditions guaranteeing rapid mixing. The authors demonstrate, through experiments, that this projection can lead to improved marginal estimates compared to standard Gibbs sampling and established variational inference techniques (mean field and belief propagation) for certain parameter regimes.

The technical foundation of the paper is firmly rooted in existing literature on conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]. However, the specific strategy of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method appears to be a novel contribution. 

The experimental evaluation focuses on small “toy” Ising models, where the proposed method shows promising improvements over strong baseline methods. A primary concern, however, is the potential scalability of these results to larger, more complex models. The experiments primarily compare mixing times, and it is difficult to extrapolate these findings to larger problem instances. Furthermore, the computationally expensive projection step required to construct the fast-mixing model is not fully accounted for in the reported timings. The method’s generalizability is also questionable, as establishing convergence bounds becomes significantly more challenging with non-binary states.

Regarding clarity, the presentation is generally clear, though improvements are needed. Several instances refer to “standard results” and present them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without providing references or even brief proof sketches. Lemma 5 is stated without proof, despite the referenced work only proving it for a specific zero-field case. The properties of the dependency matrix *R* should be discussed, specifically addressing its tractability given the maximization involved – and clarifying its connection to Lemma 5. The latter half of Section 4 is difficult to follow, with undefined variables (*g*, *M*, *Lambda*) introduced before their definition and notation in Theorem 4 lacking sufficient explanation. A reduction in restating results from [8] that are not central to the current work might improve focus. Finally, the notation for KL divergence in Section 1 is inconsistent, and the assignment of *q* and *p* is reversed compared to common convention.

Concerning the experiments, the time comparison in Figure 2 does not appear to include the cost of the projection step. While Section 6.1 alludes to this, the statement is ambiguous and requires clarification. If the projection time is excluded, results including it should be presented, as the current comparison unfairly benefits the proposed method. The absence of experiments on larger models is a significant limitation, given the known dependence of Gibbs mixing times on problem size. Exploring regimes where junction tree inference is intractable—perhaps through exploiting symmetries to compute true marginals or using long sampling runs as a ground truth—would strengthen the evaluation. 

While acknowledging the intractability of KL(θ||ψ) in general, exploring it as a potential “best case” performance metric for sampling in the approximate model would be valuable. The paper correctly identifies mean field as a degenerate case of reverse KL projection, but the substantial difference between mean field error and the error from a true reverse KL projection warrants further discussion.

In summary, the idea of approximating slow-mixing models via projection onto the closest fast-mixing model is a compelling one, and the work effectively leverages recent advances in mixing bounds. However, concerns remain regarding the experimental evaluation and the limited applicability of the approach to more complex and larger-scale models.
