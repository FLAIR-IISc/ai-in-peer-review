Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes using “fast-mixing” Ising models as a tractable family for approximate inference. Instead of requiring a low-treewidth structure, the authors enforce parameter-space conditions that guarantee rapid mixing of single-site Gibbs sampling—specifically, a spectral norm bound on the dependency matrix implied by the pairwise weights. Given an original (potentially slow-mixing) Ising model, they project its parameters onto the set that satisfies a fast-mixing condition, using several divergences: Euclidean distance, inclusive KL (θ||ψ), a piecewise KL built from tractable subgraphs, and reverse KL (ψ||θ). For reverse KL, they use stochastic approximation with sampling from the projected, fast-mixing model. The approach draws on mixing-time criteria from Dyer et al. [4] and Hayes [8], and leverages standard SVD-based spectral norm projections; the dual formulation in Theorem 7 enforces sparsity and the absolute-value constraint.

Empirically, on small grids and random graphs, Gibbs sampling on the projected fast-mixing model often yields more accurate marginals within a fixed time budget than Gibbs on the original parameters, and compares favorably to LBP, TRW, and mean-field. The paper also discusses the looseness of the spectral norm condition (e.g., vs. the 2D Ising critical threshold [11]).

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: Using projection onto a fast-mixing parameter set as an inference primitive appears original and well-motivated. It provides a principled way to trade off bias (from modifying parameters) for dramatically improved sampler convergence.
- Solid grounding in mixing-time theory: The method is anchored in sufficient conditions from [4,8], and the Ising-specific bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is used to connect parameters to the dependency matrix.
- Algorithmic contributions: A dual formulation for Euclidean projection that maintains graph sparsity while enforcing spectral constraints; a practical reverse-KL projection via stochastic approximation and Gibbs on ψ.
- Empirical promise: On small problems with strong couplings and limited sampling time, the projected models can outperform both standard variational methods and short-run Gibbs on the original model.

Weaknesses and concerns:
Quality/soundness:
- Several “standard” results are used without citation or proof sketch. For example, Theorem 6 (thresholding singular values to project under spectral norm) needs a reference (Eckart–Young–Mirsky style). Lemma 5 (Rij ≤ tanh|βij|) is cited to [8] only for a special case; either provide a proof or an explicit reference for the general case with fields.
- The paper uses a looser spectral bound in experiments (e.g., 2.5 instead of 1.0) while still claiming rapid mixing. This requires justification or empirical evidence that mixing remains fast under this choice, and a discussion of how it affects guarantees.
- The dependency matrix R is defined via a max over configurations and is not directly computable for large models; it would help to explicitly state this and explain that the method relies on the Ising-specific bound in terms of |βij|.

Clarity/organization:
- Section 4 (projection) is hard to follow. Variables and functions (g, M, Λ) are referenced before being defined, and Theorem 7 introduces notation (Z, Πc, triple dot products) with minimal explanation. A step-by-step derivation or at least a clear summary of the dual, constraints, and optimization routine (LBFGS-B with bound constraints) would help.
- Notation for KL divergences is inconsistent in the introduction. The direction KL(q||p) is used for both inclusive and reverse KL; please standardize and align with common conventions (θ = target/original, ψ = approximation).
- Some background on R’s properties (norm choices, relation between ||R||2 and ||R||1, symmetric vs. asymmetric cases, random vs. systematic scan) appears but could be streamlined to only what is needed for the proposed method.

Experiments:
- Computational cost: The time comparisons do not appear to include the (potentially substantial) time spent computing the projection, especially for reverse KL with many Gibbs steps and repeated Euclidean projections. Please report wall-clock time including projection, or provide a compute-budgeted comparison (e.g., total time fixed across methods).
- Scale: Results are confined to small graphs. Since mixing time depends on problem size, experiments on larger instances are important. Options include models with symmetries enabling exact marginals, or using very long-run sampling as ground truth.
- Best-case baseline: On small graphs where junction tree is feasible, optimizing inclusive KL (θ||ψ) could serve as a best-case projection baseline to assess the potential of the approach.
- Mean-field gap: Since mean-field arises as a degenerate reverse-KL projection with zero spectral norm, the empirical gap between MF and reverse-KL projection (with a nonzero spectral bound) deserves discussion. Is the improvement mainly due to allowing limited pairwise couplings, better mixing, or properties of the reverse KL objective?

Originality:
- The use of fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractable family, and projection onto that set for approximate inference, is a novel angle relative to tree-based or factorized variational approximations and to standard sampling accelerations.

Significance:
- If the projection cost is modest and fast-mixing guarantees hold at useful coupling strengths, this could be a practical tool for time-limited inference in strongly coupled models. However, impact is currently limited by small-scale experiments, unclear end-to-end runtime comparisons, and uncertain generalization beyond binary Ising models.

Suggestions/questions:
- Provide citations or proofs for Theorem 6 and Lemma 5, and clarify the computability of R.
- Clean up Section 4 with clearer definitions, derivation overview, and algorithmic steps; define all notation before use.
- Standardize KL notation and roles of θ and ψ throughout.
- Include projection time in all time–accuracy curves, or add compute-equalized comparisons.
- Add larger-scale experiments, and a best-case inclusive-KL projection on small graphs for context.
- Justify the choice of spectral bound (e.g., 2.5) empirically (mixing diagnostics, spectral gap estimates) or theoretically.
- Comment on extending the approach beyond binary variables and single-site Gibbs (e.g., block Gibbs, other MCMC), and on applicability to general MRFs.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, well-motivated idea leveraging recent mixing-time theory.
- Clear algorithmic instantiation with both Euclidean and KL-based projections.
- Promising empirical results in strong-coupling, time-limited regimes.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited empirical scale and incomplete accounting of projection cost.
- Clarity issues in the projection section and missing citations/proofs for key claims.
- Uncertain generalization beyond Ising and reliance on a conservative mixing bound.
