

The paper addresses a critical challenge in Gibbs sampling for Markov Random Fields (MRFs), focusing on the Ising model (two labels). It argues that inference success hinges on the Markov chain's mixing rate, proposing a novel approach: (1) project the input model onto a space of fast-mixing models (defined by bounding the spectral norm of absolute edge strengths), and (2) perform inference on this projected model. The projection preserves the graph structure and is defined via divergences (KL, reversed KL, piecewise KL) between Gibbs distributions. For Euclidean distance, projection uses dualization and LBFGS-B; for other divergences, projected gradient methods are employed, with reversed KL requiring Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model for computation.

Experiments compare the proposed methods (with all divergences) against loopy BP, TRW, mean-field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original model using small random Ising models. Metrics include marginal accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy trade-offs. Results show the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and loopy BP in accuracy. For reasonable runtimes, they also surpass Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed methods, reversed KL divergence achieves the best performance.

**Major Comments:**  
1.  **Figure 2 Axes & Runtime Reporting (Critical):** The horizontal axis in Figure 2 (and supplement) is labeled "number of samples," but sampling is only used in reversed KL. This is misleading; the axis should represent *runtime* (in seconds) to enable fair comparison across all methods. The paper must report *absolute runtimes* for LBP, TRW, and MF alongside the proposed methods. Without this, the accuracy-runtime comparison is uninterpretable. Please clarify and correct the axis label and reporting.  

2.  **Projection Algorithm Details (Section 5.1):** The projected gradient algorithm involves nested loops (inner loop: LBFGS-B). Please specify the stopping criterion for the inner LBFGS-B iterations (e.g., tolerance, max iterations). This is essential for reproducibility and understanding computational cost.  

3.  **Model Scale (Critical):** Experiments are limited to small random models. To strengthen claims of practical utility, please compare with larger models. The paper by Boris Flach (TPAMI, 2013) provides a class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition functions—ideal for scaling tests.  

**Minor Comments:**  
- p. 222: Replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T".  
- p. 226: Replace "ssy" with "say" (e.g., "subgradient" instead of "derivative").  
- The paper states "reversed KL divergence requires Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model for projection." Clarify that this sampling is *only* for the reversed KL projection step, not for inference on the original model.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper presents a theoretically sound approach to improving Gibbs sampling via model projection, supported by convincing empirical results on small models. The reversed KL method demonstrates clear advantages in accuracy and runtime efficiency. However, the current experimental evaluation lacks critical details for fair comparison (runtime axes, absolute timings, larger models), which significantly impacts the perceived practical utility. The high runtime of the projection step (especially for reversed KL) needs explicit discussion in the rebuttal to address potential limitations.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Weak Accept** (with major revisions required). The core idea is strong and well-motivated, and the empirical results are promising. Addressing the runtime reporting, projection details, and scaling experiments is essential to validate the practical claims. Without these, the contribution’s significance is diminished. The paper has potential to be a valuable resource for improving MRF inference if these issues are resolved.
