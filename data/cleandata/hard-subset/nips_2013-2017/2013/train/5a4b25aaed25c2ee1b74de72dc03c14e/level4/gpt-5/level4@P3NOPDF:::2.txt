Summary
The paper proposes to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the original parameterization onto a set that satisfies the Dobrushin condition, which ensures fast mixing. The projection is framed as a constrained optimization problem under various distance/divergence measures, with constraints intended to preserve the model’s domain. The resulting parameters are then used with Gibbs sampling.

Strengths
- The idea of leveraging a deterministic projection to place the model in a provably fast-mixing regime, then using stochastic sampling, is interesting and relatively underexplored.
- Framing the projection as an optimization problem is natural and potentially widely applicable.
- The paper touches on multiple divergences/distance measures, which could offer flexibility depending on the downstream objective.

Clarity and Technical Concerns
- The dual formulation and the role of the constraints need clearer motivation and interpretation. In particular, how do constraints like z_ij*d_ij = 0 ensure the projected model remains defined over the same space as the original? As written, it is not obvious that this prevents collapsing to a smaller effective parameter space. An intuitive explanation and a formal argument (e.g., via KKT conditions and feasibility preservation) would help.
- The overall procedure is unclear. Is the projection performed to convergence first, followed by a standard Gibbs sampler on the projected model, or are projection steps interleaved with sampling (e.g., a projected stochastic or online scheme)? A concise algorithm box with inputs, outputs, stopping criteria, and complexity would greatly improve readability.
- Convergence guarantees: Under what conditions on the chosen distance/divergence and feasible set is projected gradient descent guaranteed to converge (e.g., convexity/smoothness, step-size rules)? If the objective is convex and the feasible region defined by the Dobrushin constraints is convex, cite standard rates; otherwise, characterize local convergence or provide empirical evidence of robustness. Clarify whether projection onto the Dobrushin set is itself efficient and how it is computed.

Experimental Evaluation
- Measurement protocol: Reporting results after a fixed number of Gibbs iterations (e.g., 30k) is difficult to interpret when compared against deterministic baselines. A wall-clock time budget, or sample complexity to reach a target error under equal time, would be more informative. Please explain why 30k iterations were chosen and add time-based comparisons.
- Statistical significance: Include error bars (e.g., across multiple random seeds/initializations) and confidence intervals for key metrics. Without variability estimates, it is hard to assess significance.
- End-to-end cost: The time to compute the projection is not accounted for. A fair comparison should include projection overhead amortized over sampling, and report end-to-end time to reach a given estimation error. For example, if the projection takes one minute, how many samples from the naive Gibbs can be drawn in that same minute, and does the projected approach still win under equal-time evaluation?
- Baselines: Please include comparisons to stronger MCMC baselines such as blocked Gibbs and lifted/blocked variants (e.g., Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs Sampler). These are standard ways to improve mixing and would help isolate the benefits of the proposed projection.
- Diagnostics: Include mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, effective sample size, potential scale reduction) and, where possible, bounds or empirical proxies related to the Dobrushin coefficient before and after projection. Also report how the projection affects downstream quantities of interest (marginal error, log-likelihood, predictive performance) to show the fidelity/mixing trade-off.
- Ablations: Since the method examines multiple divergences, provide an ablation comparing them. How sensitive are results to the distance choice, regularization parameters, and stopping criteria?
- Practicality: Report sizes of models tested, sparsity/density, and coupling strengths. It would be helpful to quantify how much the parameters are changed by the projection (norms of the difference; visualization of coupling shrinkage) and how often the original models already satisfy Dobrushin.

Presentation and Reproducibility
- Add a clear algorithmic summary: input model, divergence choice, constraint set, projection routine, stopping conditions, and the subsequent sampling procedure.
- Clarify feasibility: how do you ensure the constraints retain the model’s support and parameter domain? Provide a brief proof sketch or reference showing feasibility is preserved and no variables/interactions are inadvertently eliminated.
- Detail the optimization: step-size selection (fixed, backtracking, adaptive), number of iterations, tolerance, and projection subproblem solution. If the projection is approximate, discuss its impact on guarantees.
- Provide implementation details and release code or pseudocode sufficient for replication.

Overall Assessment
This is a compelling and timely idea that bridges deterministic optimization with stochastic sampling to obtain practical, fast-mixing samplers under a well-known sufficient condition. However, the current presentation of the projection problem (especially the dual and constraint interpretation) leaves important questions unanswered, and the empirical section does not yet make a convincing case for end-to-end effectiveness. Strengthening the technical exposition, clarifying the algorithmic workflow and convergence properties, and substantially bolstering the experimental evaluation with fair, time-based, and statistically sound comparisons—particularly against stronger Gibbs variants—would significantly improve the paper and better demonstrate the utility of the approach.
