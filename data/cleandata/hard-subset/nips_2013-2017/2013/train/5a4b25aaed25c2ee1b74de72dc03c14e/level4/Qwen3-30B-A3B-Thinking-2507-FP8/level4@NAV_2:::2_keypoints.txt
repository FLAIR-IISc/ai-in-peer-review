- Proposes accelerating Gibbs sampling by projecting Ising-model parameters onto a fast-mixing set using a spectral-norm constraint (||R||₂ < 1).  
- Redefines "tractability" via mixing-time guarantees rather than graph structural constraints.  
- Provides a dual optimization framework for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) that is technically novel.  
- Derives theoretical results (Theorems 4–7) establishing the fast-mixing parameterization.  
- Explores alternative divergences for projection, including piecewise KL and zero-forcing KL.  
- Experiments compare projected Gibbs against LBP, TRW, and naive mean-field and show finite-sample accuracy gains.  
- Originality rated high for the parameter-space focus and the dual formulation.  
- Significance moderate: addresses practical MCMC bottlenecks and has potential learning/inference applications.  
- Quality moderate: sound theoretical foundation and standard experimental design, with conservative spectral-norm bounds acknowledged.  
- Clarity issues: dual derivation lacks intuition; constraint ZᵢⱼDᵢⱼ = 0 and its impact on parameter reduction are unclear.  
- Algorithmic ambiguity: unclear whether projection and Gibbs steps are sequential or interleaved; pseudocode is requested.  
- Missing convergence proof for projected gradient descent on KL(ψ||θ).  
- Time-accounting flaw: projection cost (e.g., 30k Gibbs iterations) is not included in time-accuracy comparisons, invalidating runtime claims.  
- Experimental weaknesses: missing important baselines (blocked Gibbs, lifted Gibbs), no error bars, and no time-normalized metrics to support claims.  
- Originality caveat: piecewise KL approach resembles tractable-subgraph methods (Saul & Jordan; Sutton & McCallum), though implementation differs.  
- Overall: promising direction with strong theory, but requires clarification of optimization, rigorous time-normalized experiments, convergence guarantees, and comparisons to recent MCMC methods.
