

The success of inference via Gibbs sampling in Markov random fields (MRFs), specifically for two-label models (i.e., Ising models), critically depends on the mixing rate of the underlying Monte Carlo Markov chain. The paper proposes the following approach for inference:  
1) Project the input model (which may not mix rapidly) onto the set of models that do mix rapidly.  
2) Perform inference on the resulting fast-mixing model.  
The space of fast-mixing models is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute Ising edge strengths. "Projection" is defined via divergences of Gibbs distributions and enforces preservation of the graph structure. Euclidean distance projection is achieved by dualizing the initial problem and using L-BFGS-B. For other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL), the projected gradient algorithm is employed. Notably, reversed KL divergence requires Gibbs sampling (on the fast-mixing model) to compute the projection.  

Extensive experiments on small random models compare approximated marginals against true marginals. The evaluated methods include the proposed approach (with all divergences) and loopy BP, TRW, mean-field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original model. Both accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy trade-offs are assessed. Results demonstrate that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and loopy BP in accuracy, and for reasonable runtime ranges also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed methods, reversed KL divergence yields the most consistent performance.  

Comments:  
- The projected gradient algorithm in Section 5.1 in fact features two nested loops, with the inner loop implementing L-BFGS-B. Please specify the stopping criterion for the inner iterations.  
- The horizontal axis in Figure 2 (and the supplement) is labeled "number of samples," but sampling is only used for reversed KL divergence. This axis should instead represent algorithm runtime. Similarly, reporting the runtime of loopy BP, TRW, and MF would ensure a fair accuracy-runtime comparison across all tested algorithms; absolute running times in seconds are required for interpretation.  
- Consider extending experiments to larger models. A promising option is to use models from Flach's work ("A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function," TPAMI, to appear), which enable polynomial-time inference.  
- Line 222: Replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T."  
- Line 226: Should "derivative" be replaced with "subgradient"?  
The paper presents an interesting approach with convincing empirical results. However, practical utility may be limited due to high runtime (this requires clarification in the rebuttal).
