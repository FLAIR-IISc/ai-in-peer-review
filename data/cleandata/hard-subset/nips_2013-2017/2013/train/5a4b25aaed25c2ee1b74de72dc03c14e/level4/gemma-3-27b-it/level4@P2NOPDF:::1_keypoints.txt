- Paper proposes projecting a slow-mixing pairwise Ising model onto a nearest “fast-mixing” model using a spectral bound to enable faster Gibbs sampling.

- The projection-as-approximate-inference idea appears novel, though it builds heavily on established rapid-mixing results [4,8].

- Experiments on small Ising “toy” models show improved marginal estimates over standard Gibbs, mean field, and belief propagation for a given reported computational cost.

- Major concern: scalability to larger, more complex models is unclear and not investigated.

- The computational cost of the projection step appears omitted or unclear in timing comparisons (Figure 2), possibly biasing results.

- The projection step may be computationally expensive; its tractability and cost should be explicitly accounted for and reported.

- Generalization beyond binary-state Ising models is potentially difficult because convergence bounds are harder to establish for more general settings.

- Paper relies on “standard results” presented as theorems without sufficient references or proof sketches (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5).

- Properties and computation of the dependency matrix R are insufficiently discussed; the maximization in its calculation may be intractable and should be acknowledged.

- The second half of Section 4 is hard to follow: variables g, M, Λ are used before being introduced and Theorem 4 uses poorly explained notation.

- Suggest streamlining restatements of results from [8] and focusing on the novel optimization components that are central to the contribution.

- Notation inconsistency for KL divergence in Section 1 (KL(q||p) used inconsistently and reverses typical convention) needs correction.

- Experiments lack evaluations on larger instances or regimes where junction tree is intractable; symmetry-based tests or long-run sampling proxies for ground truth are suggested.

- Authors should clarify whether KL(θ||ψ) was considered as a “best-case” benchmark and, where possible, use junction tree to benchmark toy models.

- The paper notes mean field is a reverse-KL special case, but the large performance gap between mean field and reverse-KL projection deserves further discussion.
