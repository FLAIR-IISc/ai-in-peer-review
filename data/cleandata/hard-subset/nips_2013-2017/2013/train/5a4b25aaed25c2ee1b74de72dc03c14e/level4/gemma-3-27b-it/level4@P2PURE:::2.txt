The authors present a method to accelerate Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the original parameterization onto a new setting that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion for fast mixing. This projection is formulated as a constrained optimization problem, with constraints designed to maintain the parameter space, and is analyzed using several distance and divergence measures. 

I believe that combining stochastic and deterministic inference techniques is a relatively unexplored and promising research direction, and this work represents an interesting contribution. While the core idea of augmenting the original parameters to improve mixing time is conceptually simple, the description of the dual formulation of the projection problem lacks clarity. Specifically, the justification for how the constraints zij*dij = 0 ensure the new parameterization remains within the original space is unclear – could these constraints potentially restrict the space to a smaller subset? Furthermore, the overall procedure is not fully apparent; it is unclear whether projected gradient iterations are run to completion *before* initiating Gibbs sampling, or if sampling and gradient updates are interleaved. An algorithm description box would greatly improve understanding. Finally, is the convergence of the proposed projected gradient descent strategy guaranteed?

The experimental evaluation is currently unpersuasive. In the first set of experiments, the rationale for running the Gibbs sampler for 30,000 iterations is not provided. Given the comparison to deterministic methods, evaluating performance based on runtime would be more appropriate. Moreover, the figures lack error bars, making it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the observed results. The second set of results demonstrates that projection can indeed accelerate mixing compared to a naive Gibbs sampler. However, the computational cost of performing the projection itself is not accounted for in this comparison; for instance, if the projection requires a minute, but the naive Gibbs sampler can generate 10,000 samples in that same time, the projection may not be beneficial. Lastly, a comparison to established techniques such as blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate's "Lifted Gibbs Sampler" is missing.

Overall, this is a compelling idea with potential. However, further development of the projection problem and proposed method, along with a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, is needed to fully demonstrate the utility of this approach.
