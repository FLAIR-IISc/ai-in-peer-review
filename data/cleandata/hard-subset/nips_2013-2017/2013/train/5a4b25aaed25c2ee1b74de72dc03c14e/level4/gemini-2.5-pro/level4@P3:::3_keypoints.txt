- Problem: Inference in Ising models is hard when both high treewidth and strong interactions make standard methods intractable.  
- Key idea: Define "tractable" models by parameters that guarantee fast mixing of Gibbs sampling (bound spectral norm of absolute interactions).  
- Method: Two-step approach — (1) project original parameters onto the fast-mixing set while preserving graph sparsity, (2) run Gibbs sampling on the projected model.  
- Algorithms: Euclidean projection solved via a dual formulation and LBFGS-B; inclusive KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL projections via projected gradient descent.  
- Practical note: Reversed KL is especially practical because gradients can be estimated by sampling from the fast-mixing projected model.  
- Empirical results: Experiments on small grids and random graphs show the proposed methods (especially reversed KL) yield more accurate marginals than LBP, TRW, MF.  
- Time-constrained advantage: In limited-time settings, projected-model Gibbs gives more accurate marginals than running Gibbs on the original slow-mixing model.  
- Strength — originality: Novel conceptualization of tractability via mixing-time guarantees rather than graphical structure.  
- Strength — technical quality: Sound theoretical derivations (dual for Euclidean projection, KL gradients) and insightful connection between reversed KL and mean-field.  
- Strength — empirical validation: Strong, well-presented experiments and effective runtime-accuracy comparisons versus standard Gibbs.  
- Weakness — experimental clarity: Figure 2's x-axis labeled "number of samples" is ambiguous for non-sampling methods and projection-phase usage; request clearer definition or wall-clock time.  
- Weakness — missing baseline runtimes: Need reported runtimes for LBP, TRW, MF to fairly assess trade-offs and overhead of projection.  
- Weakness — projection cost: Projection involves an inner Euclidean projection solved by iterative L-BFGS-B; need discussion of overall runtime and stopping criteria for inner loop to balance accuracy and speed.  
- Weakness — scale of experiments: Evaluations are limited to small graphs (10-node random, 8x8 grids); larger-scale experiments are needed to demonstrate practical relevance.  
- Minor points: Suggest rewording line 222 to "projection onto the graph T" and using "subgradient" (line 226) instead of "derivative".  
- Conclusion: Interesting and well-executed work with strong potential; main concerns are unclear runtime reporting and possibly high projection cost, which should be addressed.
