- Paper proposes projecting Ising MRFs onto a space of fast-mixing models (bounded spectral norm of absolute edge strengths) and performing inference on the projected model.  
- Projection preserves graph structure and is defined via several divergences: Euclidean, KL, reversed KL, and piecewise KL.  
- Euclidean projection solved via dualization and LBFGS-B; other divergences use projected gradient, with reversed KL requiring Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model during projection.  
- Experiments on small random Ising models compare proposed methods (all divergences) to loopy BP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original model using marginal accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy.  
- Results: proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and loopy BP; for reasonable runtimes they also beat Gibbs on the original model; reversed KL gives the best performance.  
- Major concern: Figure 2’s horizontal axis labeled “number of samples” is misleading—should be runtime (seconds); absolute runtimes for all methods must be reported for fair comparison.  
- Major concern: need to specify stopping criteria (tolerance/max iterations) for inner LBFGS-B in the projected gradient algorithm for reproducibility and cost assessment.  
- Major concern: experiments are limited to small models; authors should evaluate larger models (e.g., Flach TPAMI 2013 random fields) to validate scalability and practical utility.  
- Minor: wording/typo fixes (e.g., “onto the tree” → “onto graph T”; “ssy” → “say”/“subgradient”).  
- Minor: clarify that Gibbs sampling for reversed KL is used only during the projection step, not for inference on the original model.  
- Overall assessment: method is theoretically sound and promising, reversed KL is particularly effective, but missing runtime details and scalability experiments weaken practical claims.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept pending major revisions addressing runtime reporting, projection algorithm details, and larger-scale experiments.
