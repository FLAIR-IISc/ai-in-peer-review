## Review of NIPS 2013 Submission: [Paper Title]

This paper addresses the challenging problem of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions. The core idea is to project the original, slow-mixing model onto a nearby model that provably mixes rapidly, effectively performing approximate inference in a transformed space. This is achieved by identifying parameters that minimize a distance to the original model while satisfying spectral bounds known to guarantee rapid mixing. The authors demonstrate, through experiments on small Ising models, that this projection approach can improve marginal estimate accuracy compared to standard Gibbs sampling and existing variational methods (mean field and belief propagation).

The technical contribution builds heavily, and appropriately, on established results regarding conditions for rapid mixing [4, 8]. While the underlying principles are well-grounded in prior work, the specific concept of projecting onto the *nearest* rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference strategy appears novel and represents a worthwhile exploration. The experimental results on “toy tiny Ising models” are encouraging, showing improvements over a solid set of baselines. However, a significant concern is the potential scalability of the proposed method to larger, more realistic models. The experiments primarily assess mixing times, and extrapolating these results to larger problem sizes is difficult. Furthermore, the computationally expensive projection step required to construct the fast-mixing model appears to be unaccounted for in the current analysis. Finally, the applicability of this approach is likely limited to binary states due to the increased complexity of establishing convergence bounds for more general state spaces.

**Clarity & Presentation:**

The paper is generally clearly written and reasonably accessible, given the technical depth of the subject matter. However, several points require reorganization and clarification. 

Firstly, the paper frequently references “standard results” and presents them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without providing references or even a brief proof sketch. This hinders reproducibility and understanding for readers less familiar with the underlying theory. Lemma 5 is presented without proof, and the cited reference only addresses a special case (zero-field). 

Secondly, a more thorough discussion of the dependency matrix *R* is needed. Specifically, the authors should acknowledge whether finding the maximization defining *R* is tractable. (I suspect intractability is the motivation for invoking Lemma 5.)

Thirdly, the second half of Section 4 is difficult to follow. Prior to Theorem 7, the text refers to variables *g*, *M*, and *Lambda* before they are defined, creating confusion. Moreover, Theorem 4’s statement includes notation that lacks adequate explanation. While leveraging results from [8] is acceptable, reducing the emphasis on restating those results—particularly those not directly utilized—would improve clarity.

Finally, in Section 1, the notation *KL(q||p)* is used to represent both forward and reverse KL divergence, which is inconsistent with standard convention. The initial paragraph defines *q* as the true distribution and *p* as the tractable approximation, reversing the typical roles found in most related literature.

**Experiments:**

The timing comparisons presented in Figure 2 do not appear to include the computational cost of the projection step. A somewhat ambiguous statement in Section 6.1 alludes to this, but it is insufficiently clear. The authors should clarify whether the projection time is included and, if not, present results incorporating this cost. Currently, the proposed methods appear to benefit from using the output of a sophisticated variational optimization without any performance penalty, which weakens the claim of improvement over standard Gibbs sampling. 

The lack of experiments on larger models is a significant omission, given the known dependence of Gibbs mixing times on problem size. While computational limitations are understandable, alternative strategies could be explored. These include running experiments in regimes where junction tree inference is intractable, such as models with symmetries allowing for the computation of true marginals, or using the output of very long sampling runs as a proxy for the ground truth.

Considering the intractability of *KL(θ||ψ)* in general, exploring *KL(θ||ψ)* as a “best-case” performance metric for sampling in the approximate model would be insightful. Junction tree inference could be employed for the current toy models to facilitate this analysis.

The paper notes that mean field can be viewed as a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection. However, a substantial discrepancy exists between the error observed in mean field and that resulting from a true reverse KL projection. A discussion of this difference is warranted.

**Overall:**

The idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting onto the closest fast-mixing model is innovative and leverages recent advances in mixing bound theory effectively. However, the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient consideration of computational costs and scalability, and the limited range of applicable models raises concerns. Addressing the clarity issues outlined above would further strengthen the paper. While the approach shows promise, more extensive evaluation and a deeper discussion of limitations are necessary before a strong recommendation can be made.
