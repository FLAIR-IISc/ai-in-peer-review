This paper introduces a novel method to accelerate Gibbs sampling for Ising models by projecting the original model parameters into a new parameterization satisfying the Dobrushin criterion, a condition known to guarantee fast mixing. The authors formulate this projection as a constrained optimization problem, exploring its properties under various distance and divergence measures.

I find the core idea of combining stochastic and deterministic inference techniques—an area I believe is relatively underexplored—to be quite promising. Augmenting the original parameters to improve mixing time is a conceptually sound approach. However, certain aspects of the paper require further clarification. Specifically, the derivation and explanation of the dual problem for the projection were unclear to me. The rationale behind the *z<sub>ij</sub>* *d<sub>ij</sub>* = 0 constraints, intended to maintain the parameter space, is not immediately obvious; it's not clear why these constraints prevent the new parameterization from collapsing to a smaller space. Furthermore, the overall procedure remains somewhat ambiguous. It would be helpful to understand whether the projected gradient descent is run to completion *before* initiating Gibbs sampling, or if sampling and gradient updates are interleaved. An algorithmic description, perhaps in a dedicated box, would significantly enhance clarity. Finally, the convergence properties of the proposed projected gradient descent strategy are not discussed and should be addressed.

The experimental evaluation, while illustrating the potential of the approach, is not entirely convincing in its current form. In the initial set of experiments, the rationale for running the Gibbs sampler for 30,000 iterations is unclear. Given the comparison between a sampling method and deterministic alternatives, evaluating performance based on computational time would likely provide a more equitable assessment. Critically, the figures lack error bars, hindering the reader's ability to determine the statistical significance of the observed results.

The second set of experiments demonstrates that utilizing the projected Gibbs sampler leads to faster mixing compared to the naive Gibbs sampler. However, the computational cost of performing the projection itself is not considered within this comparison. A full accounting of the runtime, including the projection step, is crucial for a fair evaluation. For instance, if the projection requires one minute while the naive Gibbs sampler can generate 10,000 samples in the same timeframe, the practical benefit of the projection may be diminished.

Finally, the absence of comparisons to established techniques such as blocked Gibbs sampling or Gogate's “Lifted Gibbs Sampler” is a notable omission. These methods represent common approaches to accelerating Gibbs sampling and would provide valuable context for assessing the proposed method’s relative advantages.

In summary, this paper presents a compelling idea with the potential to improve Gibbs sampling for Ising models. However, the mathematical development of the projection problem and the proposed method would benefit from greater clarity, and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation – including runtime analysis, statistical significance testing, and comparisons to relevant baseline methods – is needed to fully demonstrate its practical utility.
