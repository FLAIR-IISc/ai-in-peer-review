The authors propose a method to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling for Ising models. The core idea is to project the model's original parameters onto a new parameter set that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion for fast mixing. This projection is formulated as a constrained optimization problem, designed to ensure the new parameters are defined over the same graphical structure as the original, and the authors explore this projection under several distance and divergence measures.

The general approach of combining principles from stochastic and deterministic inference is under-explored, making this a novel and interesting contribution. While the high-level idea of modifying the parameters to improve mixing is straightforward, I found the technical details of the projection problem to be unclear. For instance, the description of the dual problem is difficult to follow. Specifically, it is not clear how the `zij*dij = 0` constraints guarantee that the new parameterization is supported on the same graphical structure as the original; could these constraints result in a sparser model (i.e., a smaller space)?

Furthermore, the overall algorithm is ambiguous. Is the projection performed once to completion before running the Gibbs sampler, or are the sampling and gradient updates interleaved? An algorithm box would be invaluable for clarification. A related question is whether the proposed projected gradient descent strategy is guaranteed to converge.

The experimental evaluation is not yet convincing and requires significant improvement. In the first set of experiments comparing the proposed sampler to deterministic methods, the choice of 30,000 iterations for the Gibbs sampler seems arbitrary. Since the comparison is between sampling-based and deterministic methods, a comparison based on wall-clock time would be more appropriate and fair. Crucially, the plots lack error bars, making it impossible for the reader to assess the statistical significance of the reported results.

The second set of experiments compares the naive Gibbs sampler with the proposed samplers using different projections, demonstrating that the projection leads to faster mixing in terms of sample count. However, this comparison overlooks a critical factor: the computational cost of the projection itself. This cost must be accounted for. For example, if the projection takes one minute, a naive Gibbs sampler might generate thousands of effective samples in that same time, potentially negating the benefits of faster mixing. The total time to reach a certain level of accuracy (including both projection and sampling time) should be the primary metric. Finally, the experiments would be strengthened by a comparison to other well-known methods for improving Gibbs sampling performance, such as blocked Gibbs sampling or Gogate's "Lifted Gibbs Sampler."

In summary, the paper presents a promising and innovative idea. However, the manuscript would be significantly strengthened by a clearer and more rigorous development of the projection problem and its associated optimization procedure. Most importantly, a more comprehensive set of experiments—including fair time-based comparisons, statistical significance tests, and a broader range of relevant baselines—is necessary to convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.
