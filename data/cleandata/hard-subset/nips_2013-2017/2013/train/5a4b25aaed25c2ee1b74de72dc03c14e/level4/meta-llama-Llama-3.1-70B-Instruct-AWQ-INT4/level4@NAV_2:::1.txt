This paper presents a novel approach to improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, known to be "slow-mixing". The authors propose projecting the original model onto a "fast-mixing" model, identifying parameters that are as close as possible to the original model but weak enough to satisfy a spectral bound establishing rapid mixing. The technical content builds heavily on prior results establishing conditions for rapid mixing, but the idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method is a new contribution.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it accessible to readers familiar with the technical content. The authors provide a thorough background on mixing times in Markov chains and the Ising model, and the presentation of their approach is easy to follow. The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving marginal estimates for a given computation time, compared to variational methods and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

However, there are some concerns regarding the scalability of the approach to larger models. The experiments involve comparisons of mixing times of samplers on different models, and it is challenging to judge how these will scale with problem size. Additionally, the computationally expensive projection step required to build the fast-mixing model is not accounted for in the time comparisons. Furthermore, it is unclear whether the approach can be generalized beyond binary states, as establishing convergence bounds is more challenging in this case.

The paper could benefit from some clarifications and reorganization in certain sections. For instance, the properties of the dependency matrix R could be discussed in more detail, and the proof of Lemma 5 could be provided or referenced. The second half of Section 4 is dense and could be improved with additional explanations and notation.

In terms of experiments, it would be beneficial to include the computation required for projection in the time comparisons. Additionally, experiments on larger models or models with symmetries that allow for tractable computation of true marginals would be valuable in assessing the scalability of the approach. Exploring the "zero-avoiding" KL-divergence as a potential "best case" for sampling in an approximate model could also provide interesting insights.

The idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is elegant, and the authors' use of recent work on mixing bounds is well-executed. However, addressing the concerns mentioned above would strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models
* Well-organized and clearly written presentation
* Effective experiments demonstrating the improvement of marginal estimates

Weaknesses:

* Concerns about scalability to larger models
* Computationally expensive projection step not accounted for in time comparisons
* Limited range of models to which the approach is potentially applicable
* Some sections could benefit from clarification and reorganization

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a new and elegant approach to approximate inference in Ising models
* The technical content is sound and well-executed
* The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* Concerns about scalability and applicability to larger models
* Limited evaluation of the approach in terms of computation time and generalizability
* Some sections require clarification and reorganization.
