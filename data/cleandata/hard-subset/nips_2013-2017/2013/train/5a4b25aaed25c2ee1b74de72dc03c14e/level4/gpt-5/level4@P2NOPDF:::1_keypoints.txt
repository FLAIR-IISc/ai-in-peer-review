- Proposes projecting pairwise Ising models to the nearest fast-mixing model by weakening parameters to satisfy a spectral bound — novel and appealing.
- Builds on prior sufficient conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]; using projection as an approximate-inference method appears new.
- Experiments on small Ising problems show improved marginal estimates vs mean field, belief propagation variants, and Gibbs on the original model under fixed compute.
- Main concern: scalability — unclear how results and mixing-time improvements scale with problem size.
- The projection step seems computationally expensive and its cost appears omitted from timing comparisons; this must be clarified and included.
- Extending the approach beyond binary variables is likely difficult because analogous convergence bounds for general models are hard to derive.
- Clarity issue: several “standard results” are presented (e.g., Theorem 6) without citation or proof sketch; Lemma 5 lacks proof and cited ref covers only the zero-field case.
- Need discussion of the dependency matrix R’s properties and tractability; the maximization may be intractable and should be addressed (presumably via Lemma 5).
- Section 4 is hard to follow: symbols (g, M, Λ) and notation in Theorem 4 are used before definition; provide clearer definitions, intuition, and derivations.
- Notation problems in Section 1: KL(q||p) is used for both directions and q/p are swapped from common practice — revise and flag deviation.
- Experimental fixes requested: include projection overhead in timings; otherwise comparisons unfairly give the method a variational optimization “for free.”
- Recommend experiments on larger models (or symmetric models with exact marginals or very long runs) to assess scaling and mixing-time dependence on size.
- Suggest computing KL(θ||ψ) using junction tree on small models as a “best case” benchmark for sampling-from-approximate-model performance.
- Discuss why mean field (degenerate reverse-KL case) differs substantially in error from the full reverse-KL projection.
- Overall: idea is attractive and leverages recent mixing bounds, but requires clearer exposition, inclusion of projection costs, and larger/more challenging experiments to strengthen the contribution.
