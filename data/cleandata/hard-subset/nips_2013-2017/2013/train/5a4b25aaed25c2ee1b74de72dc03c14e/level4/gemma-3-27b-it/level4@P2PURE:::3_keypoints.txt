- Paper proposes projecting slow-mixing Ising models onto a space of fast-mixing models (by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute edge strengths) and performing inference on the projected model.  
- Projection is defined via divergences between Gibbs distributions while preserving the original graph structure.  
- Euclidean projection is solved by dualizing the problem and using LBFGS-B.  
- KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL projections are handled with a projected gradient algorithm; reversed KL projection requires Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing projected model.  
- Experiments on small random models compare approximate marginals against true marginals for the proposed methods, LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original model.  
- Results show the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy and beat Gibbs on the original model for reasonable runtimes; the reversed KL variant performs best.  
- Missing detail: stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B iterations in the projected gradient algorithm (section 5.1) are not specified.  
- Plot clarification needed: Figure 2’s horizontal axis labeled “number of samples” is unclear (sampling only used for reversed KL); likely denotes runtime and should be clarified and reported in seconds.  
- Runtime reporting incomplete: absolute running times for LBP, TRW, and MF should be provided for fair accuracy-runtime comparisons.  
- Suggestion to test larger models (e.g., models from Flach’s work with tractable partition functions) to assess scalability.  
- Minor edits: change “onto the tree” to “onto graph T” (line 222) and “Derivative” to “subgradient” (line 226).  
- Overall assessment: empirically convincing paper, but practical utility may be limited by potentially high runtime—this should be addressed in the rebuttal.
