Success of inference by Gibbs sampling in Markov Random Fields (MRFs), specifically in the Ising model with two labels, strongly depends on the mixing rate of the underlying Monte Carlo Markov chain. This paper proposes an approach to inference that involves: 1) projecting the input model, which may not mix quickly, onto the set of models that do mix fast; and 2) performing inference on the resulting, fast-mixing model. The space of fast-mixing models is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute values of Ising edge strengths. “Projection” is defined in terms of divergences of Gibbs distributions, and is constrained to preserve the graph structure. Projection using Euclidean distance is achieved by dualizing the initial task and employing LBFGS-B. For other divergences – KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences – a projected gradient algorithm is used. Notably, the reversed KL divergence requires Gibbs sampling (on a fast-mixing model) to compute the projection. 

Extensive experiments on small, random models compare the approximated marginals to the true marginals. The methods tested include the proposed approach (using each of the aforementioned divergences), Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), Mean Field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The evaluation considers both accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy tradeoffs. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and, for comparable runtimes, also surpass Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed methods, the reversed KL divergence consistently yields the best performance.
