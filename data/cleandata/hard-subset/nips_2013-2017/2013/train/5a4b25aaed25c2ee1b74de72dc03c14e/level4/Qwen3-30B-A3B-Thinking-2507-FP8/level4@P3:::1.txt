

This paper introduces an innovative approach to approximate inference in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set where Gibbs sampling converges rapidly. The authors leverage theoretical bounds on mixing times (specifically, the spectral norm condition from Hayes [8]) to define a tractable parameter space, then propose four methods to project arbitrary Ising model parameters onto this space under different divergences. The core contribution is the novel application of parameter projection as an inference technique rather than merely a theoretical construct.

The work builds meaningfully on prior NIPS research in mixing times (Dyer et al. [4], Hayes [8]) and approximate inference (Globerson and Jaakkola [6], Wainwright et al. [20]). While prior work has focused on tractable inference structures (e.g., tree-based approximations), this paper introduces "fast-mixing" as a new notion of tractability—where the parameter space itself guarantees rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling, rather than restricting the model structure. This represents a significant conceptual shift that connects parameter space properties to mixing behavior.

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, with proper theoretical grounding in mixing time bounds and parameter projection. The authors derive a dual algorithm for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) and explore practical divergences (KL, piecewise KL, reverse KL). However, the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the spectral norm bound's tightness (Section 7). For the 2D Ising model, the spectral bound (β < 0.25) is significantly looser than the known threshold (βc ≈ 0.44), suggesting the constraint may be conservative. While the experiments demonstrate improved marginal accuracy with limited sampling time, the authors do not sufficiently address whether the projected models maintain sufficient statistical fidelity for larger-scale applications.

**Clarity**: The paper's presentation suffers from several clarity issues. Theorem 6 is referenced without proof or intuition, and Lemma 5 (which extends Hayes' result to general Ising models) is left unproven despite being central to the approach. The dependency matrix R is introduced without sufficient explanation of its computational intractability (the max over x,x' makes direct calculation impossible). Section 4's optimization formulation is particularly opaque, with notation (g, M, Λ) introduced before definition. The KL divergence notation is inconsistent throughout (q and p are swapped between sections), which is confusing. The paper would benefit from a clearer roadmap and more intuitive explanations of key theoretical constructs.

**Originality**: The core idea—projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set as an inference method—is novel and well-motivated. While mixing time bounds have been studied extensively, this paper's application to parameter projection for inference represents a fresh perspective. The four divergence approaches (Euclidean, piecewise KL, reverse KL) each offer distinct computational trade-offs, with the reverse KL approach being particularly well-motivated as a generalization of mean-field. The paper convincingly demonstrates that this approach differs from prior work (e.g., mean-field, loopy BP) by leveraging mixing time properties rather than structural constraints.

**Significance**: The method shows promising results on small-scale Ising models, with marginal accuracy improvements over variational methods (mean-field, TRW) and original-model Gibbs sampling when sampling time is limited. However, the significance is limited by the restricted experimental scope. The experiments use small grids (8x8) and tiny random graphs (10 nodes), making it unclear how the approach would scale to larger problems where mixing times become critically important. The paper does not address whether the projection cost (which appears substantial) is justified by the marginal gains in inference accuracy. Crucially, the experimental comparisons in Figure 2 omit the projection time, creating an unfair advantage for the proposed methods.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance**:

*Pro*:
- Novel theoretical insight connecting parameter space constraints to mixing times
- Well-motivated application of projection as an inference technique
- Demonstrated improvements over strong baselines on small-scale problems
- Careful exploration of multiple divergence measures with practical implementations

*Con*:
- Limited experimental scope (small graphs only, no scaling analysis)
- Projection time omitted from computational comparisons
- Spectral norm bound appears conservative (Section 7)
- Significant clarity issues in theoretical presentation
- Unclear path to generalization beyond binary Ising models

This paper presents an interesting and conceptually fresh approach to approximate inference, with solid theoretical foundations. However, the experimental evaluation is insufficient to demonstrate the method's practical value for real-world problems where mixing times are most critical. The omission of projection cost from computational comparisons is particularly problematic, as it creates an unrealistic advantage for the proposed methods. The clarity issues also make it difficult to fully assess the technical contribution.

I recommend rejection due to the limited scope of the experiments and the lack of computational cost analysis. The method shows promise but requires more thorough evaluation on larger models and with proper accounting of all computational costs. The authors should address the clarity issues and provide a more convincing demonstration of scalability before the work can be considered significant for NIPS.
