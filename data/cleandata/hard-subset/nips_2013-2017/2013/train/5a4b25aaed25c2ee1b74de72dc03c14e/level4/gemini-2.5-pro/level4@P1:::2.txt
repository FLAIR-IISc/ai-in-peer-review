This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models. The core idea is to address the problem of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling, which often occurs when interaction strengths are high. Instead of using a structurally simple model (e.g., a fully-factorized or tree-structured distribution) for the approximation, the authors propose using a "fast-mixing" model. They define the set of fast-mixing models as those whose interaction parameters satisfy a spectral norm condition that guarantees rapid convergence of the Gibbs sampler. The main contribution is a method to project an arbitrary (and potentially slow-mixing) Ising model's parameters onto this set of fast-mixing parameters. The authors formulate this projection as a constrained optimization problem and explore solutions under four different divergences: Euclidean distance, the "zero-avoiding" KL-divergence KL(q||p), a novel piecewise KL-divergence, and the "zero-forcing" KL-divergence KL(p||q).

### Strengths

The central idea of this paper is novel and interesting. It addresses a significant and practical challenge in MCMC methods. The connection between the theoretical literature on MCMC mixing times (specifically, criteria related to the Dobrushin condition) and practical approximate inference is an under-explored and promising direction. Formulating the problem as a projection onto a set of "tractable" models, where tractability is defined by rapid mixing rather than structural simplicity, provides a fresh perspective on variational inference. The development of methods for multiple divergences, including the novel piecewise KL-divergence, is also a valuable contribution.

### Weaknesses

While the core idea is strong, the paper could be improved in its technical exposition and experimental validation.

**1. Clarity of the Method:**
*   The description of the projection, particularly the dual formulation in Theorem 7, could be improved for clarity. For instance, it is stated that the constraint `ZijBij = 0` enforces that the new parameters B obey the original graph structure. While this prevents the introduction of new edges, it seems to allow for the removal of existing edges (i.e., setting `Bij = 0` where `Aij` was non-zero). A more detailed explanation of how this preserves the intended parameter space would be beneficial.
*   The overall procedure for the KL-divergence projections is also somewhat ambiguous. It is unclear if the projected gradient steps are performed to convergence before running the final Gibbs sampler, or if the sampling and gradient updates are interleaved in some way. A formal algorithm box outlining the steps for the stochastic approximation would greatly enhance the paper's clarity and reproducibility.
*   Additionally, while the paper cites Ergodic Mirror Descent, the convergence guarantees of the proposed projected gradient descent strategy should be discussed more explicitly in the context of the specific implementation.

**2. Experimental Evaluation:**
The experiments demonstrate the potential of the method, but they are not entirely convincing in their current form.
*   **Fairness of Comparison:** The first set of experiments compares the proposed method against deterministic alternatives like LBP and TRW based on a fixed number of Gibbs iterations (30,000). A more equitable comparison would be based on wall-clock time, as the deterministic methods may be significantly faster per-iteration, and the goal of any approximate inference method is to achieve a good accuracy/time trade-off.
*   **Overhead of Projection:** The time-accuracy plots (Figure 2) compare Gibbs sampling on the projected parameters against Gibbs on the original parameters. However, these plots do not appear to account for the computational overhead required to perform the projection itself. This cost can be substantial, especially for the iterative KL-divergence methods. To demonstrate the practical utility of the method, the total time (projection time + sampling time) should be compared against the time taken by the baseline Gibbs sampler. It is possible that for the time it takes to compute the projection, the naive sampler could have already run for many iterations and achieved a comparable or better accuracy.
*   **Statistical Significance:** The plots lack error bars, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the reported improvements. Given the randomness in graph generation and parameter initialization, including error bars is essential for validating the claims.
*   **Missing Baselines:** The comparison could be strengthened by including more advanced MCMC baselines that are also designed to improve mixing. For example, comparing against block Gibbs sampling or more recent techniques like the "Lifted Gibbs Sampler" (Gogate and Domingos, UAI 2011) would provide a clearer picture of where the proposed method stands in relation to the state of the art in sampling.

### Conclusion

Overall, this paper introduces a very promising and original idea. However, the development of the projection problem and the proposed algorithm could be clarified, and a more thorough and convincing set of experiments is needed to demonstrate the method's practical utility. With improvements to the exposition and a bolstered experimental section, this could be a strong contribution.
