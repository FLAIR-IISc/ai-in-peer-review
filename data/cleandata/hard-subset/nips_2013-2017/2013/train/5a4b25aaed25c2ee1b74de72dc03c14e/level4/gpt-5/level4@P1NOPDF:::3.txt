The success of Gibbs-sampling–based inference in binary MRFs (Ising models) depends critically on the mixing rate of the underlying Markov chain. The paper proposes the following approach:
1) Project the input model (which may mix slowly) onto the set of fast-mixing models.
2) Perform inference on the resulting fast-mixing model.

The fast-mixing set is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute Ising edge strengths. “Projection” is defined via divergences between Gibbs distributions, while preserving the original graph structure. The Euclidean projection is obtained by dualizing the problem and solving with L-BFGS-B. For other divergences—KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL—the authors use a projected-gradient algorithm. For reverse KL, Gibbs sampling (on the fast-mixing model) is required to compute the projection.

Experiments: The paper presents extensive tests on small random models, comparing approximate marginals to exact marginals. The proposed methods (with each of the above divergences) are evaluated against loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling on the original model. Both accuracy and accuracy–runtime trade-offs are considered. Results indicate that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and, over a reasonable runtime range, also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed variants, reverse KL performs best.

Comments and questions:
- The projected-gradient algorithm in Section 5.1 has two nested loops, with L-BFGS-B as the inner loop. Please provide details on the stopping criteria for the inner iterations (tolerances, maximum iterations, and how these choices interact with the outer loop), since they directly affect runtime and accuracy.
- In Figure 2 (and in the supplement), the horizontal axis is labeled “number of samples,” but sampling is used only for reverse KL. I believe the x-axis should represent wall-clock runtime. Please clarify what is plotted, and report wall-clock time in seconds. For a fair accuracy–runtime comparison, please also report runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF.
- Please consider experiments on larger models. A natural option is the class of complete-graph models from: Boris Flach, “A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function,” TPAMI (to appear; available online), which allow polynomial-time inference.

Minor edits:
- Line 222: replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T.”
- Line 226: “subgradient” seems more appropriate than “derivative.”

Overall assessment: This is an interesting paper with convincing empirical results. However, practical utility may be limited by runtime; the rebuttal should clarify computational costs and provide absolute timings to substantiate the claimed trade-offs.
