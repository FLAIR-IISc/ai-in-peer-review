This paper aims to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, which are known to be "slow-mixing". To achieve this, the authors propose projecting onto "fast-mixing" models by identifying parameters that are as close as possible to the original model, but weak enough to satisfy a spectral bound establishing rapid mixing. Experiments demonstrate that this approach can lead to improved marginal estimates for a given computation time, compared to variational methods (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

The technical content of the paper builds heavily on prior results establishing conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]. However, the idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method appears to be novel. While the results show some nice improvements over a range of baselines on "toy tiny Ising models", I have concerns about the scalability of these results to larger models. The experiments involve comparisons of mixing times of samplers on different models, which are difficult to extrapolate to larger problem sizes. Furthermore, the computationally expensive projection step required to build the fast-mixing model seems to be overlooked in the analysis, and its cost is not accounted for. Finally, it is unclear whether the approach can be generalized beyond binary states, as establishing convergence bounds for more general cases is significantly more challenging.

In general, the presentation is clear and reasonably accessible, given the technical content. However, there are some areas that require reorganization and clarification. The paper frequently refers to "standard results" without providing references or proof sketches, including Theorem 6. Additionally, Lemma 5 is left unproven, and the reference only proves the special case of zero-field. It would be helpful to discuss the properties of the dependency matrix R, including its tractability, and to clarify why Lemma 5 is invoked. The second half of Section 4 is particularly difficult to follow, with references to g, M, and Lambda before they are introduced. The statement of Theorem 4 also includes unexplained notation. If the optimization is to be discussed, more explanation is needed. It may be helpful to reduce the time spent restating results from [8] that are not essential to the paper.

In Section 1, the notation for KL divergence is used inconsistently, with KL(q||p) used for both directions of the divergence. The notation should be shifted for one of the directions. Additionally, the use of q for the true distribution and p for the tractable approximation is opposite to the convention in most related literature.

The experimental results appear to have some issues. The time comparison in Figure 2 does not include the computation required for projection, which is only vaguely mentioned in Section 6.1. It is essential to clarify this point and, if possible, show results with the projection time included. As it stands, the proposed method seems to benefit from the output of a sophisticated variational optimization without penalty, which undermines the improvement over standard Gibbs sampling.

It is disappointing that the experiments do not include larger models, given that Gibbs mixing times often depend on problem size. There are alternative options for running experiments in regimes where junction trees are intractable, such as using models with symmetries that allow true marginals to be computed or taking the output of a very long sampling run as truth. Although KL(\theta||\psi) is intractable in general, it would be interesting to explore this as a potential "best case" for how sampling in an approximate model would perform. Junction trees could be used for the toy models in the submission.

The paper notes that mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection, yet there is a significant difference between mean field error and the error from reverse KL projection. This discrepancy deserves discussion.

Overall, the idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is elegant, and the paper leverages recent work on mixing bounds effectively. However, there are concerns about the experimental comparisons and the limited range of models to which this approach is potentially applicable.
