- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a parameter set satisfying the Dobrushin condition to speed Gibbs sampling, framed as a constrained optimization with multiple divergence choices.
- Timely and under-explored idea that connects deterministic optimization with stochastic inference.
- Clear motivation: enforcing a sufficient mixing condition can make Gibbs practical on challenging instances.
- Evaluating multiple divergences for projection is valuable for understanding trade-offs.
- Dual formulation of the projection problem is unclear; need intuition or proof sketch for constraints like z_ij * d_ij = 0 and whether projection preserves the original parameter space versus a strict subset.
- Workflow ambiguity: unclear if projection is done to convergence before sampling or interleaved with sampling; provide pseudocode, stopping criteria, and warm-starting details.
- Convergence guarantees are not stated: need conditions (step-size rules, Lipschitz/convexity assumptions) and whether feasibility w.r.t. Dobrushin constraints holds throughout iterations or only at convergence.
- Projection feasibility concerns: are there cases where Dobrushin constraints make projection infeasible (e.g., strong couplings), and how is that handled?
- Runtime fairness: compare methods by wall-clock time or computational budget rather than fixed Gibbs iterations; report time-normalized performance and ESS/sec.
- Report variability and significance: include error bars, standard errors across seeds, and statistical tests due to sampling stochasticity.
- Account for projection cost: include projection overhead in end-to-end comparisons and amortize it over the sampling horizon.
- Missing stronger baselines: compare against blocked Gibbs and symmetry-exploiting samplers like Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs to contextualize gains.
- Provide practical details: step sizes, stopping criteria for projection, verification of Dobrushin condition post-projection, and alignment of projection divergence with evaluation metric.
- Robustness analysis needed: sensitivity to initialization, graph structure (degree, frustration), coupling strength, and behavior near the Dobrushin threshold.
- Include qualitative mixing diagnostics: autocorrelation plots, effective sample size, and Gelman–Rubin statistics to substantiate “faster mixing.”
- Overall: compelling idea but needs clearer dual/projection theory, explicit algorithmic description with convergence, and fairer, more comprehensive experiments to be convincing.
