This paper addresses the well-known problem of slow convergence in Gibbs sampling for Ising models with strong couplings. The central contribution is a novel approximate inference strategy: instead of approximating the model with a structurally simpler one (e.g., a tree), the authors propose projecting the original model's parameters onto the nearest set of parameters that is guaranteed to be "fast-mixing". This guarantee is established by enforcing a spectral norm constraint on the interaction matrix, drawing on prior work on MCMC mixing times [4, 8]. The authors derive projection methods for several divergences, including Euclidean distance and approximations of KL-divergence, and demonstrate that sampling from the projected model can yield more accurate marginals than other methods within a fixed time budget.

The key strength of this paper is its conceptually novel and elegant approach to approximate inference. Shifting the notion of a "tractable" model from one where exact inference is possible to one where Gibbs sampling is provably fast is a creative and promising idea. The application of spectral bounds on mixing times as a constraint for an optimization problem is clever. The experimental results on small-scale models show promising improvements over established baselines like Loopy Belief Propagation, TRW, and standard Gibbs sampling, suggesting the method has merit.

Despite the novelty of the idea, the paper has significant weaknesses in its current form, primarily concerning the clarity of its technical contributions and the fairness of its experimental evaluation.

**Clarity and Presentation:**
While the paper is generally well-written, several sections would benefit from increased clarity and rigor.
*   Several key results are stated without adequate support. For instance, Theorem 6 is presented as a "standard result" but lacks a citation. Similarly, Lemma 5, an extension of a result from [8] to the non-zero field case, is provided without a proof or sketch, leaving the reader to verify it.
*   The derivation of the dual method in Section 4 is particularly opaque. The text introduces variables (g, M, Λ) before they are defined, and the notation in Theorem 7 is not sufficiently explained, making this core technical contribution difficult to follow. More detail is needed here.
*   The properties of the dependency matrix R could be explained more thoroughly, particularly regarding the difficulty of its direct computation, which motivates the use of the bound in Lemma 5.
*   There are minor notational inconsistencies that could be confusing, such as using KL(q||p) for both forward and reverse divergences in the introduction and an unconventional choice of 'q' for the true distribution where 'p' is more common.

**Experimental Evaluation:**
The experimental validation, while showing promise, has several limitations that weaken the paper's conclusions.
*   A critical issue is that the computational cost of the projection step appears to be excluded from the runtime comparisons. The paper notes that this projection can require tens of thousands of Gibbs iterations. By not accounting for this significant pre-computation, the comparison with standard Gibbs sampling is not on equal footing. For the claims to be convincing, results must account for the total computational effort.
*   The experiments are confined to small models (10-node random graphs and 8x8 grids) where exact marginals are computable. The true challenge of slow mixing, however, often manifests more severely in larger models. It would be more compelling to see experiments on larger-scale problems where exact inference is intractable. This could be achieved by using models with known ground-truth marginals (due to symmetry) or by comparing against a very long MCMC run as a "gold standard".
*   The connection between the proposed reverse KL projection and mean-field (which is described as a degenerate case) is intriguing. However, the large performance gap between the two methods is not discussed. An analysis of why constraining the spectral norm offers such a significant advantage over constraining it to zero would strengthen the paper.
*   For the small models studied, it would be illuminating to also compute the projection under the "zero-avoiding" KL(θ||ψ) using exact inference. While intractable in general, this would serve as a valuable "oracle" to understand the potential upper bound on the performance of this projection-based approach.

**Scope and Generalization:**
The paper's applicability to more general problems is uncertain. The reliance on mixing time bounds specific to the Ising model raises questions about its extensibility to general discrete MRFs, where deriving such tight, parameter-based bounds is significantly more challenging. The scalability of the projection algorithm itself, which involves SVD and iterative optimization, is also a potential concern for very large models.

**Summary:**
This paper introduces a highly original and promising idea for approximate inference by projecting slow-mixing models onto a fast-mixing set. However, the current manuscript is weakened by a lack of clarity in key technical sections and an experimental evaluation that does not fairly account for the method's computational cost and is limited to small-scale problems. While the core concept is strong, the paper would require substantial revision to its experimental analysis and presentation to be considered for acceptance.
