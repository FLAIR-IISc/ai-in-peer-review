- Proposes projecting pairwise Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set to speed Gibbs sampling convergence — a novel approximate-inference idea.  
- Builds on prior rapid-mixing conditions and introduces projection to the nearest fast-mixing model as the core contribution.  
- Presents multiple projection objectives: Euclidean distance, piecewise KL-divergence, and reverse KL-divergence.  
- Experiments show improved marginal estimates for a given computation time versus variational methods and Gibbs on the original model.  
- Major scalability concern: results and mixing-time comparisons may not extend to larger models where mixing depends on problem size.  
- Time comparisons omit the computationally expensive projection step, biasing runtime results.  
- Unclear generalizability beyond binary states; convergence bounds for more general cases are challenging.  
- Presentation issues: "standard results" are cited without references or sketches, and some proofs (e.g., Lemma 5) are missing.  
- Properties and tractability of the dependency matrix R are not sufficiently discussed.  
- The second half of Section 4 is hard to follow; the optimization in Theorem 7 needs clearer explanation.  
- Experimental clarifications needed: explicitly include projection time in runtimes and test on larger models.  
- Suggest exploring zero-avoiding KL-divergence as a possible best case for sampling in approximate models.  
- The large discrepancy between mean-field error and reverse-KL projection error requires discussion, since mean field is a degenerate reverse-KL case.  
- Strengths: novel approach, elegant use of mixing bounds, and empirical improvements in marginal estimates.  
- Weaknesses: scalability doubts, unaccounted projection cost, limited applicability range, and presentation/clarity problems.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address presentation, include projection-time discussion, and better discuss limitations and applications.
