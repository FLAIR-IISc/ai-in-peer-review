- Paper aims to speed up Gibbs mixing in strongly interacting pairwise Ising models by projecting parameters onto “fast-mixing” models satisfying a spectral bound.  
- Novelty: projecting to the nearest rapidly-mixing model as an approximate inference method appears new, though built on prior rapid-mixing results [4,8].  
- Experiments show improvements in marginals for small/tiny Ising models, outperforming standard Gibbs and variational baselines in some regimes.  
- Major scalability concern: results are demonstrated only on tiny toy models and it is unclear they will extend to larger problem sizes where mixing depends on size.  
- Computational cost concern: the expensive projection step seems not to be included in time comparisons (Figure 2) and must be accounted for.  
- Uncertainty about generalization beyond binary-state models, since deriving convergence bounds for more complex state spaces is hard.  
- Clarity issues: paper cites “standard results” as theorems without citations or proof sketches; Lemma 5 is unproven and reference only covers a special case.  
- The dependency matrix R is under-discussed; its maximization step seems potentially intractable but this is not explicitly stated.  
- Section 4’s second half is hard to follow—symbols (g, M, Λ) and notation in Theorem 7 are introduced poorly and need clearer exposition.  
- Notational inconsistency: KL(q||p) is used for both directions and the roles of p and q are reversed from common conventions in Section 1.  
- Experimental design flaw: projection time omission gives the method an unfair advantage and undermines the credibility of time-based comparisons.  
- Missing larger-scale experiments: authors could use symmetric models or long-run sampling as ground truth when junction tree is intractable to test scalability.  
- Suggested additional analyses: evaluate KL(θ||ψ) as a best-case reference (possible on toy models via junction tree) and discuss the gap between mean-field and reverse KL projection errors.  
- Overall: idea is compelling and leverages recent mixing bounds elegantly, but experimental comparisons, projection cost accounting, clarity, and applicability remain significant concerns.
