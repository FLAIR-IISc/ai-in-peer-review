- Proposes projecting slow-mixing pairwise Ising models onto the nearest fast-mixing model to accelerate Gibbs sampling.  
- Novel idea of using model projection for approximate inference, building on prior rapid-mixing results.  
- Demonstrates improved marginal estimates over mean field, belief propagation, and standard Gibbs in small toy Ising models.  
- Core idea is promising and technically sound within the paper’s limited scope.  
- Experiments are confined to tiny toy models, providing little evidence of scalability to larger systems.  
- Projection computation cost is omitted from runtime comparisons, making reported speedups potentially misleading.  
- Comparing mixing times on small models does not reveal how performance scales with problem size.  
- Method appears restricted to binary states; extending convergence bounds beyond binary is likely intractable, limiting applicability.  
- Theorem 6 and Lemma 5 lack proper references/proofs; Lemma 5’s reliance on a zero-field special case is problematic.  
- Dependency matrix R is treated without addressing intractability from required maximization.  
- Section 4 is opaque: Theorem 7 cites undefined quantities (g, M, Λ) and the optimization description is insufficiently explained.  
- Over-reliance on restating results from [8] obscures the paper’s novel contributions.  
- KL divergence notation is misused and inconsistent with standard conventions, causing confusion.  
- Experimental recommendations: include projection time in runtimes, test larger/symmetric models or long-sampling baselines, and use junction tree for KL-based “best-case” benchmarks.  
- Overall: technically interesting and elegant idea but lacks empirical validation, scalability evidence, and clearer technical presentation to be practically impactful.
