- Paper studies how Gibbs sampling success in two-label Ising MRFs depends on mixing rate and proposes projecting slow-mixing models onto a fast-mixing set by bounding the spectral norm of the absolute edge-strength matrix while preserving graph structure.  
- Projection is performed using divergences between Gibbs distributions: Euclidean projection via dualization with L-BFGS-B; KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL via projected gradient methods.  
- The reversed KL projection requires running Gibbs sampling on the projected (fast-mixing) model during optimization.  
- Experiments on small random models compare approximated marginals from all proposed methods, loopy BP, TRW, mean-field, and original-model Gibbs sampling against true marginals.  
- Results: proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP, and are competitive with original-model Gibbs sampling within reasonable runtimes; reversed KL performs best among proposed approaches.  
- Request to specify stopping criteria for inner L-BFGS-B iterations in the projected-gradient algorithm (Section 5.1).  
- Clarify Figure 2’s horizontal axis (labeled “number of samples” despite sampling only used for reversed KL), and report absolute runtimes (seconds) for all algorithms (LBP, TRW, MF) for fair accuracy-runtime comparison.  
- Suggest extending experiments to larger models, e.g., tractable partition-function models from Flach (TPAMI, 2024).  
- Minor edits: change “onto the tree” to “onto graph T” (Line 222) and use “subgradient” instead of “derivative” (Line 226).  
- Overall assessment: interesting and convincing approach with strong empirical results, but potentially high runtime that should be clarified in rebuttal.
