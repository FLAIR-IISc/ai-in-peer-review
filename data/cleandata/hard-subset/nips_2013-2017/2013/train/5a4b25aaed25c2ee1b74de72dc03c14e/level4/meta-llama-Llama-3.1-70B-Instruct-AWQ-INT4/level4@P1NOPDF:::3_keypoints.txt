- Paper proposes projecting an input Ising MRF onto the set of fast-mixing models, then performing inference on the projected model.
- Fast-mixing models are defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute edge strengths.
- Projection is defined via divergences between Gibbs distributions and is constrained to preserve graph structure.
- Euclidean projection is solved by dualization and LBFGS-B; KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL use a projected gradient algorithm.
- Reversed KL projection requires running Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model to compute the projection.
- Experiments on small random models compare proposed methods to loopy BP, TRW, mean field, and Gibbs on the original model.
- Evaluation measures both accuracy and runtime-versus-accuracy; proposed methods outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy.
- For a reasonable runtime range, proposed methods also beat Gibbs on the original model; reversed KL performs best among proposals.
- Clarification requested: projected gradient algorithm has nested loops with LBFGS-B as inner loop — need stopping criterion details for inner iterations.
- Clarification requested: x-axis labeled “number of samples” appears misleading and should likely represent runtime; absolute running times in seconds should be reported.
- Request to report runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF to enable fair runtime-accuracy comparisons.
- Suggestion to test larger models, e.g., models from Flach’s paper with tractable partition functions.
- Minor corrections: change “onto the tree” to “onto graph T” and use “subgradient” instead of “derivative.”
- Overall assessment: interesting paper with convincing empirical results, but practical utility may be limited by high runtime and requires clarification in rebuttal.
