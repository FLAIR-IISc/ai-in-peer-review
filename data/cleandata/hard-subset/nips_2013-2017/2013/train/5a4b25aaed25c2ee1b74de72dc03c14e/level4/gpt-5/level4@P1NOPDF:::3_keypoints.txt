- Proposal: project an input Ising model onto the set of fast-mixing models (bound spectral norm of the matrix of absolute edge strengths) and perform inference on the projected model.  
- Projection defined via divergences between Gibbs distributions, preserving the original graph structure.  
- Euclidean projection obtained by dualizing the problem and solving with L-BFGS-B.  
- KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL projections implemented via a projected-gradient algorithm; reverse KL projection requires running Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model.  
- Reviewer requests details on stopping criteria for the nested projected-gradient/L-BFGS-B loops (tolerances, max iters, interaction with outer loop) because they affect runtime and accuracy.  
- Experiments: extensive tests on small random models comparing approximate marginals to exact marginals.  
- Results: proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and loopy BP in accuracy, and in a reasonable runtime range also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model; reverse KL performs best among variants.  
- Reviewer asks to clarify Figure 2’s x-axis (labeled “number of samples”)—likely wall-clock time—and requests reporting wall-clock runtimes in seconds and runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF for fair comparison.  
- Suggestion to add experiments on larger models, e.g., complete-graph models with tractable partition functions (Flach).  
- Minor edits: change “onto the tree” to “onto graph T” (Line 222) and use “subgradient” instead of “derivative” (Line 226).  
- Overall: interesting paper with convincing empirical results, but practical utility may be limited by runtime—rebuttal should clarify computational costs and provide absolute timing evidence.
