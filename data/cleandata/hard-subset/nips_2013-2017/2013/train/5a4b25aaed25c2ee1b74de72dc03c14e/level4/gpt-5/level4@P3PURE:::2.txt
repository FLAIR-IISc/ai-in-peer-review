Summary:
The paper proposes a method to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model’s original parameterization onto a new parameter set that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion for fast mixing. The projection is formulated as a constrained optimization problem, with constraints intended to ensure that the new parameters are defined over the same space as the original parameters. The authors examine this projection under several distance/divergence measures.

Strengths:
- The idea of combining stochastic and deterministic inference is under-explored, and this work takes a promising step in that direction.
- Projecting parameters to satisfy a sufficient mixing condition (Dobrushin) is intuitive and potentially useful in practice.

Clarity and technical questions:
- The description of the dual of the projection problem is unclear. In particular, how do the constraints z_ij*d_ij = 0 ensure that the new parameterization is over the same space as the original? Couldn’t they yield a parameterization over a strictly smaller space?
- The overall procedure is not fully specified. Do you run the projected gradient operations to completion and then run a Gibbs sampler on the projected model, or do you interleave sampling with gradient updates? An explicit algorithm box would clarify the workflow.
- Is the proposed projected gradient descent strategy guaranteed to converge? If so, under what conditions (e.g., step sizes, convexity of the chosen divergence, feasibility of the constraint set)?

Experimental evaluation:
- The first set of experiments: Why was the Gibbs sampler run for 30K iterations? Since you compare a sampling method against deterministic methods, a comparison based on wall-clock time would be more appropriate and fair.
- The plots lack error bars. Without variability estimates, it is difficult to assess statistical significance and robustness of the reported improvements.
- The second set of results shows that projected Gibbs mixes faster than the original/naive Gibbs sampler under different projections. However, the time to perform the projection is not included. If the projection step takes substantial time (e.g., 1 minute), a naive Gibbs sampler might generate many samples in that same window (e.g., 10k), making the projection potentially not worthwhile. A fair comparison should include the projection overhead and assess end-to-end efficiency.
- Important baselines are missing: there is no comparison to blocked Gibbs samplers or to Gogate’s “Lifted Gibbs Sampler.”

Overall assessment:
This is a very nice and interesting idea, and the direction is promising. However, the formulation and exposition of the projection problem (especially the dual and constraint interpretation) need clarification, and the experimental section should be strengthened with fair time-based comparisons, error bars, accounting for projection overhead, and comparisons to stronger baselines such as blocked Gibbs and lifted Gibbs. As it stands, I am not fully convinced of the practical utility of the method, but with clearer development and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation, the contribution could be compelling.
