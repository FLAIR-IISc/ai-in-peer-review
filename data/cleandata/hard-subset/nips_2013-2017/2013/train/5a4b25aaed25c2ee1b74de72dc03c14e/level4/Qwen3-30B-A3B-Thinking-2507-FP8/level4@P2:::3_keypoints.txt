- Proposes accelerating inference by projecting Ising-model parameters onto a fast-mixing subset defined by a spectral-norm constraint.  
- Defines "fast-mixing" as Gibbs sampling converging rapidly when the spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix is below 1.0.  
- Derives projection methods for multiple divergences (Euclidean using dual/LBFGS-B, piecewise KL on low-treewidth subgraphs, reversed KL via stochastic Gibbs approximation).  
- Experiments on 8×8 grids and 10-node graphs show all proposed methods beat LBP/TRW/MF in marginal accuracy, with reversed KL performing best.  
- Shows projecting parameters before sampling gives better finite-time accuracy than direct Gibbs on slow-mixing models.  
- Strength: solid theoretical foundation (sufficient fast-mixing condition, rigorous projection derivations including Theorems 4 and 7).  
- Strength: clear presentation and experimental design, with useful definitions (dependency matrix, spectral constraint).  
- Strength: original focus on parameter-space conditions for rapid mixing, distinct from graph-structure approaches.  
- Strength: practical significance—empirical superiority and potential for learning with fast-mixing constraints.  
- Weakness: experimental ambiguity—Figure 2’s "number of samples" axis misrepresents runtime; absolute runtimes for all baselines must be reported.  
- Weakness: potentially high runtime overhead for projection (e.g., ~30k Gibbs iterations reported); the overhead should be quantified and discussed.  
- Weakness: the spectral-norm threshold (1.0) is conservative for 2D Ising models (critical βc ≈ 0.44); using looser practical bounds (e.g., 2.5) needs justification.  
- Weakness: limited scalability—experiments use small graphs (≤10 nodes); comparisons on larger models are needed to strengthen claims.  
- Recommendation: Accept, contingent on clarifying runtime metrics, quantifying projection overhead, and justifying the spectral bound in revisions.
