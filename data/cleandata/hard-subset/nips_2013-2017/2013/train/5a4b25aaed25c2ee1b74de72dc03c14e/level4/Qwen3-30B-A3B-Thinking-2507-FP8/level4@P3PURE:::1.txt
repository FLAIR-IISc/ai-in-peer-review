

The paper proposes a novel approach to accelerate Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions by projecting the original model onto the nearest "fast-mixing" model satisfying a spectral bound for rapid mixing. This projection identifies parameters as close as possible to the original model while ensuring weak interactions sufficient for rapid mixing. Experiments demonstrate improved marginal estimates within fixed computation time compared to variational methods (mean field and belief propagation variants) and standard Gibbs sampling on the original model, particularly on toy-sized Ising models. The technical foundation relies on established rapid mixing conditions [4,8], but the specific strategy of projecting to the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method appears original.

While the results on small-scale models are promising against a strong baseline set, the paper warrants significant skepticism regarding scalability to larger problems. Key concerns include: (1) The experimental evaluation compares mixing times across different models but provides no evidence of scalability with problem size, as mixing behavior typically deteriorates with model size; (2) The computationally expensive projection step required to construct the fast-mixing model is unaccounted for in runtime comparisons; (3) Generalization beyond binary states appears unlikely, as extending convergence bounds to non-binary cases is substantially more challenging.

**Clarity Issues:**  
The presentation is generally accessible but requires refinement:  
- Theorem 6 cites "standard results" without reference or proof sketch. Lemma 5 (unproven) cites a reference proving only the zero-field special case.  
- The dependency matrix **R** (defined via maximization) lacks discussion of its tractability; the paper presumes this is intractable (hence Lemma 5's necessity).  
- Section 4's second half is confusing: Theorem 7 references *g*, *M*, and Λ before their introduction, and Theorem 4 uses unexplained notation. Redundant restatements of [8] could be reduced.  
- Section 1 misuses KL divergence notation: It uses KL(*q*||*p*) for both directions, but conventionally *p* denotes the true distribution and *q* the approximation. Here, *q* is the true distribution and *p* the approximation—contrary to standard literature.

**Experiments:**  
The runtime comparison in Figure 2 excludes projection cost, as ambiguously noted in Section 6.1. This omission undermines the claimed advantage, as the method effectively leverages a sophisticated variational optimization without computational penalty. The paper must clarify and re-evaluate results including projection time. Experiments on larger models are absent, despite the critical dependence of mixing times on problem size. Alternative approaches (e.g., symmetric models with tractable marginals or long-sampling baselines) could have strengthened the evaluation. The unexplored potential of KL(θ||ψ) as a "best-case" benchmark for sampling in the approximate model (e.g., using junction tree for toy models) is notable. Additionally, the substantial error gap between mean field (a degenerate reverse KL projection) and reverse KL projection requires explicit discussion.

**Overall:** The core idea—approximating slow-mixing models via projection to the nearest fast-mixing model—is elegant and effectively leverages recent mixing bound theory. However, unresolved concerns about scalability, unaccounted computational costs, and limited experimental scope significantly temper the contribution’s impact. The method’s applicability appears restricted to small binary models, with no clear path to broader generalization.
