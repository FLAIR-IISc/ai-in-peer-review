- Paper proposes projecting Ising MRFs onto a space of fast-mixing models (bounded spectral norm of absolute edge-strength matrix) and performing inference on the projected model.  
- Projection is defined using divergences between Gibbs distributions while preserving the original graph structure.  
- Implementation: Euclidean projection solved via a dual formulation with LBFGS-B; KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL projections use a projected gradient algorithm.  
- Reversed KL projection requires running Gibbs sampling inside the projection step to estimate expectations.  
- Experiments: small, random Ising models; estimated marginals compared against ground truth.  
- Compared methods: loopy BP (LBP), tree-reweighted BP (TRW), mean field (MF), and Gibbs on the original model.  
- Results: proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy.  
- For comparable runtimes, projected methods outperform standard Gibbs on the original (slow-mixing) model.  
- Reversed KL projection yields the best performance among the proposed divergences.  
- Question: report stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B iterations used in the projected-gradient nested optimization to clarify projection cost.  
- Comment: the x-axis labeled “number of samples” in Figure 2 likely represents runtime; request clarification and absolute runtimes in seconds.  
- Request: report runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF to enable fair accuracy-runtime comparisons.  
- Suggestion: expand experiments to larger models (e.g., Flach’s TPAMI models with tractable partition functions) to strengthen conclusions.  
- Minor textual edits: change “onto the tree” to “onto graph T” (Line 222) and use “subgradient” instead of “derivative” (Line 226).  
- Overall assessment: interesting and well-motivated with convincing empirical gains, but practical utility is unclear without detailed computational complexity and absolute runtime/scalability analysis.
