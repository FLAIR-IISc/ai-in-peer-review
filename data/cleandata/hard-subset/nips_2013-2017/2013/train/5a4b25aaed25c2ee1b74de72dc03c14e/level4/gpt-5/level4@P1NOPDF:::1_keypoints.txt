- Paper proposes projecting a slow-mixing Ising model onto a nearest “fast-mixing” model and sampling there to speed Gibbs-based inference.
- The projection-then-sample idea is novel and interesting, leveraging prior sufficient rapid-mixing conditions.
- Empirical results on small toy Ising models show improved marginal estimates versus standard Gibbs and variational baselines under a fixed compute budget.
- Heavy reliance on prior mixing bounds [4,8]; the core contribution is the projection idea rather than new mixing theory.
- Main concern: scalability—unclear how mixing-time comparisons and benefits scale with larger problem sizes.
- The computational cost of the projection step appears substantial but is not included in runtime comparisons, weakening claims of superiority.
- Extending the approach beyond binary variables is nontrivial because analogous convergence bounds are hard to establish.
- Clarity issues: several “standard results” and lemmas (e.g., Lemma 5, Theorem 6) are stated without citations, proofs, or proof sketches.
- Need clearer discussion of the dependency matrix R—its maximization may be intractable and Lemma 5’s role should be clarified.
- Section 4 is hard to follow: notation (g, M, Λ, Theorem 4 symbols) is introduced out of order and needs more explanation or reorganization.
- Notation problems in Sec. 1: KL(q||p) used ambiguously for both directions and q/p roles are swapped relative to common conventions.
- Experiments: ambiguous whether projection time is included in Figure 2 timing—if excluded, results are misleading and should be recomputed including projection cost.
- Experiments lack larger models; recommend alternatives (symmetric models with exact marginals or very long diagnosed MCMC runs) to evaluate scaling.
- Suggest evaluating KL(θ||ψ) on small models (via junction tree) as a “best case” benchmark for sampling in the approximate model.
- The large performance gap between mean-field and the reverse-KL projection deserves discussion about model structure or projection constraints that explain the improvement.
- Overall: promising and elegant idea but tempered by scalability concerns, omitted projection costs, clarity issues, and non-binary generalization challenges; stronger experiments and clearer presentation needed.
