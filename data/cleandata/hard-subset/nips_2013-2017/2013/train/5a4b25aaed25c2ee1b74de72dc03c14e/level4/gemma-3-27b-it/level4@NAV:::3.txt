The paper addresses the well-known issue of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for Markov Random Fields (MRFs), specifically Ising models. The core idea is to improve inference by first projecting the original, potentially ill-conditioned model onto a space of models guaranteed to mix rapidly, and then performing inference on this projected model. This fast-mixing model space is characterized by bounding the spectral norm of the absolute edge weights. The projection is formulated using divergences between Gibbs distributions, constrained to preserve the underlying graph structure. The authors implement projection via Euclidean distance (solved using a dual formulation and LBFGS-B) and several other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL) using projected gradient descent, with the reversed KL divergence requiring Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model to compute the projection. 

The proposed approach is evaluated extensively on small, randomly generated models. The accuracy of approximate marginals is compared to ground truth, and a runtime-vs-accuracy trade-off analysis is conducted, contrasting the proposed methods (using different divergences) against loopy Belief Propagation (BP), Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), Mean Field (MF), and Gibbs sampling applied directly to the original model.  Results demonstrate consistent accuracy improvements over TRW, MF, and LBP. Furthermore, for comparable runtimes, the proposed methods surpass the performance of standard Gibbs sampling. The reversed KL divergence consistently yields the best results amongst the proposed divergence choices.

However, several points require clarification. The projected gradient algorithm detailed in Section 5.1 contains nested loops (an outer loop and an inner LBFGS-B iteration). The stopping criterion for the inner LBFGS-B iterations should be explicitly stated. Additionally, the x-axis in Figure 2 (and the supplementary material), labeled “number of samples,” is misleading, as sampling is only used within the reversed KL divergence scheme. The horizontal axis likely represents algorithm runtime, and the runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF should also be reported to enable a fair and comprehensive accuracy-runtime comparison.  Providing absolute running times in seconds would be beneficial. Finally, expanding the experimental evaluation to include larger models would strengthen the conclusions.  Consideration should be given to using models from Flach (TPAMI, online), which offer tractable polynomial-time inference as a benchmark.

Minor textual corrections are suggested on lines 222 ("onto the tree" -> "onto graph T") and 226 ("derivative" -> "subgradient"). Overall, this is a promising paper with convincing empirical results. While the practical applicability may be limited by runtime considerations – a point that requires further discussion in the rebuttal – the approach is theoretically sound and demonstrates significant potential.
