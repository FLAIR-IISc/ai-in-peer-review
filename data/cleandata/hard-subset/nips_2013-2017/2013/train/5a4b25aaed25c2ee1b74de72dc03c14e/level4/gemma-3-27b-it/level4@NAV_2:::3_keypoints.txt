- Paper proposes projecting a possibly slow-mixing Ising model onto the set of fast-mixing models (bounded spectral norm of absolute edge-strength matrix) and performing inference on the projected model.  
- Projection is defined via divergences between Gibbs distributions and constrained to preserve the graph structure.  
- Euclidean projection is solved by dualizing and using LBFGS-B; KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL use a projected-gradient algorithm.  
- Reversed KL projection requires Gibbs sampling on the projected (fast-mixing) model to compute the projection.  
- Experiments on small random models compare the proposed methods to loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original model, evaluating both accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy.  
- Results: proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and for a reasonable runtime range outperform Gibbs on the original model; reversed KL performs best.  
- Concern: projected-gradient algorithm has nested loops with LBFGS-B as inner loop—need specification of inner-loop stopping criteria.  
- Concern: Figure 2’s horizontal axis labeled “number of samples” is unclear (sampling only used for reversed KL); request to clarify and report absolute runtimes (seconds) for all methods (including LBP, TRW, MF) for fair comparison.  
- Suggest expanding experiments to larger models, e.g., models from Flach’s paper that permit polynomial inference.  
- Minor edits: change “onto the tree” to “onto graph T” (line 222) and use “subgradient” rather than “derivative” (line 226).  
- Overall assessment: interesting and empirically convincing paper, but practical utility may be limited by high runtime—this needs clarification in the rebuttal.
