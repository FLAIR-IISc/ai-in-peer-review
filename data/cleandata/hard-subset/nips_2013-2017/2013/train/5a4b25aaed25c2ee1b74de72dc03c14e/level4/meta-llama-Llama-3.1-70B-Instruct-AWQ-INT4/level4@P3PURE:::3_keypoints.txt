- Proposes projecting an input Ising MRF onto a set of fast-mixing models by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute edge strengths.  
- Two-step approach: (1) project the input model onto fast-mixing set, (2) perform inference on the projected model.  
- Defines projection via divergences between Gibbs distributions while preserving the graph structure.  
- For Euclidean projection: use dualization and LBFGS-B; for KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL: use a projected gradient algorithm.  
- Reversed KL projection requires Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model to compute the projection.  
- Experiments run on small random models, comparing approximated marginals to true marginals.  
- Baselines include loopy BP, TRW, mean field, and Gibbs sampling on the original model.  
- Results: proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and beat Gibbs on the original model for a practical range of runtimes.  
- Among proposed variants, reversed KL performs best consistently.  
- Clarify stopping criteria for inner LBFGS-B iterations in the projected-gradient algorithm (Section 5.1).  
- Clarify and fix the horizontal axis in Figure 2 (labelled “number of samples”); recommend using runtime and reporting absolute seconds.  
- Report runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF to ensure fair accuracy-vs-runtime comparisons.  
- Suggest evaluating larger models (e.g., models from Boris Flach) to test scalability.  
- Minor corrections: replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T” (p.222) and use “subgradient” instead of “derivative” (p.226).  
- Overall assessment: interesting and empirically convincing approach, but practical utility may be limited by high runtime—needs clarification in rebuttal.
