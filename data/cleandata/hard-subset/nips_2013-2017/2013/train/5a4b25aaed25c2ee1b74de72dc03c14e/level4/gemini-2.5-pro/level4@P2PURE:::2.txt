This paper proposes a method to accelerate the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models. The core idea is to project the original model parameters onto a new parameterization that satisfies the Dobrushin uniqueness condition, which guarantees rapid mixing. The authors formulate this projection as a constrained optimization problem, with constraints designed to preserve the support of the original parameterization, and they evaluate this approach under several distance and divergence measures.

The central idea is compelling, as the combination of principles from stochastic and deterministic inference is an under-explored and promising research direction. However, while the high-level concept of modifying parameters to improve mixing is intuitive, the technical exposition could be improved. The description of the dual formulation of the projection problem lacks clarity. For instance, it is not explained how the `zij*dij = 0` constraints guarantee that the new parameterization has the same support as the original; could these constraints inadvertently restrict the parameterization to a smaller subgraph? Furthermore, the overall algorithmic procedure is ambiguous. Is the projection performed once to completion before running the Gibbs sampler, or are the gradient updates interleaved with the sampling steps? An algorithm pseudocode block would be beneficial. Finally, the authors should address whether the proposed projected gradient descent method is guaranteed to converge.

The experimental evaluation is also not fully convincing and could be strengthened. In the first experiment, which compares the proposed sampling method to deterministic alternatives, the rationale for running the Gibbs sampler for a fixed 30,000 iterations is not provided. A comparison based on wall-clock time would be more equitable and informative. Moreover, the plots lack error bars, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the reported results. The second experiment demonstrates that the projection leads to faster mixing compared to a naive Gibbs sampler. However, this comparison does not account for the computational overhead of the projection step itself. For example, if the projection requires one minute of computation, during which the naive Gibbs sampler could have generated thousands of samples, the practical benefit of the proposed method is unclear. Lastly, the experiments would be more compelling with comparisons to other advanced MCMC techniques designed to improve mixing, such as blocked Gibbs sampling or Gogate's Lifted Gibbs Sampler.

In summary, the paper presents a novel and interesting idea. However, the submission would be significantly strengthened by clarifying the theoretical development of the projection problem and the associated optimization algorithm. A more thorough experimental evaluation is also necessary to convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.
