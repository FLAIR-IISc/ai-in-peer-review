The authors propose a method for improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model's original parameterization onto a new parameter setting that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion, which is necessary for fast mixing. They formulate this projection as a constrained optimization problem, where the constraints ensure that the new parameters are defined over the same space as the original parameters. The authors examine this projection under several distance/divergence measures.

In my opinion, methods that combine principles from stochastic and deterministic inference are an under-explored area, making this an interesting idea. While the concept of augmenting the original parameters to improve mixing time is straightforward, I found the description of the dual of the projection problem to be somewhat unclear. Specifically, it is not immediately apparent how the zij*dij = 0 constraints ensure that the new parameterization is over the same space (could it not be over a smaller space?). I was also unsure about the overall procedure: do you perform the projected gradient operations to completion and then run a Gibbs sampler, or do you interleave sampling with the gradient updates? An algorithm description box would help clarify this process. Furthermore, is the proposed projected gradient descent strategy guaranteed to converge?

I also found the experiments to be somewhat unconvincing. In the first set of experiments, it is unclear why the Gibbs sampler was run for 30,000 iterations. Since the comparison is between a sampling method and deterministic methods, a comparison based on time would seem more fair. Additionally, the absence of error bars on the charts makes it difficult for the reader to determine the significance of the results. The second set of results compares the original Gibbs sampler with Gibbs samplers under different projections and demonstrates that the projection does lead to faster mixing. However, the time required to perform the projection operation is not accounted for in this comparison. For instance, if the projection takes one minute, and the naive Gibbs sampler can generate 10,000 samples in that time, then the projection might not be worthwhile. Lastly, it is surprising that there was no comparison to blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate's "Lifted Gibbs Sampler".

All in all, this is a very nice idea. However, the development of the projection problem and the proposed method could benefit from further refinement, and a more comprehensive set of experiments is needed to convincingly demonstrate the utility of the method.
