This review provides a summary of the paper, followed by a discussion of its strengths and weaknesses, and concludes with a list of detailed comments for the authors.

### **Summary**

The paper addresses the challenge of inference in Ising models, particularly in regimes with strong interactions where MCMC methods like Gibbs sampling are known to mix very slowly. The core idea is to approximate a given "slow-mixing" model with a "fast-mixing" one. Instead of defining a tractable family of approximations based on graph structure (e.g., trees), the authors define it based on parameter-space conditions that guarantee rapid mixing. Specifically, they use a known result [4, 8] which ensures fast mixing if the spectral norm of the interaction matrix is less than one.

The main contribution is a set of algorithms for projecting the parameters of an arbitrary Ising model onto this fast-mixing set. The authors explore projections under four different divergences:
1.  **Euclidean distance:** A method is derived to find the closest fast-mixing parameters in the L2 sense.
2.  **"Zero-avoiding" KL divergence (KL(original||approx)):** Noted to be of theoretical interest as it requires intractable inference on the original slow-mixing model.
3.  **Piecewise KL divergence:** A novel, tractable approximation to the KL divergence based on tractable subgraphs.
4.  **"Zero-forcing" KL divergence (KL(approx||original)):** This projection can be optimized via stochastic gradient descent, as it only requires sampling from the fast-mixing approximation.

Experiments on small grid and random graphs show that sampling from the projected models can yield more accurate marginal estimates than standard variational methods (Mean Field, LBP, TRW) and, crucially, can also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model when the computational budget (number of samples) is limited.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of the Approach:** The central idea of defining a tractable approximate family based on dynamic properties (fast mixing) rather than graphical structure is novel and elegant. It offers a new and interesting perspective on the problem of approximate inference.

2.  **Theoretical Grounding:** The approach is well-motivated by leveraging established results from the MCMC mixing time literature [4, 8]. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed family of approximations.

3.  **Promising Empirical Results:** On the models tested, the proposed methods demonstrate a clear advantage over several widely-used variational baselines. The comparison against Gibbs sampling on the original parameters highlights a practical trade-off: for a finite computational budget, a slightly biased but fast-mixing sampler can be more accurate than a slow-mixing, asymptotically exact one.

### **Weaknesses and Concerns**

My primary concerns relate to the experimental evaluation and the potential scalability and generalizability of the proposed method.

1.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** The time-accuracy comparison in Figure 2 appears to exclude the computational cost of the projection step itself. A statement in Section 6.1 suggests this, noting that the projection involves up to 30,000 Gibbs iterations for the KL(ψ||θ) method. This makes the comparison with standard Gibbs sampling unfair, as the proposed methods are effectively given a significant, uncounted computational budget to perform a sophisticated variational optimization. For the claims to be convincing, the total computational cost, including projection, must be accounted for.

2.  **Scalability:** The experiments are confined to small models (e.g., 8x8 grids) where exact inference via the junction tree algorithm is possible for evaluation. However, the practical utility of this approach is most apparent in larger models where MCMC is the only viable tool. It is difficult to extrapolate from these results how the accuracy trade-offs will scale with the number of variables. The performance of Gibbs sampling can degrade polynomially or exponentially with model size, and it is unclear how the proposed approximation's quality will behave in such regimes.

3.  **Limited Scope:** The method is presented only for pairwise binary Ising models. The approach relies critically on Lemma 5, which provides a simple bound on the dependency matrix. It is unclear how this method would extend to models with higher-order potentials or non-binary variables, where deriving analogous tractable conditions for fast mixing is significantly more challenging. This potential limitation on the applicability of the work should be discussed.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

While the paper is generally well-written, several sections would benefit from clarification to improve reproducibility and reader understanding.

*   **Clarity of Section 4:** The second half of Section 4 is very difficult to follow. The symbols `g`, `M`, and `Λ` are used in the text preceding Theorem 7 before they are formally defined. The theorem statement itself lacks sufficient explanation of the notation (e.g., the "triple dot product"). This section requires significant revision for clarity.

*   **Missing Citations and Proofs:** Theorem 6 is stated as a "standard result" but is not cited. Lemma 5 is presented without proof, with a note that the cited proof for a special case can be modified. Including a proof sketch in the appendix or a more direct reference would strengthen the paper's technical soundness.

*   **Tractability of the Dependency Matrix R:** The paper relies on bounding the dependency matrix `R`. It should be explicitly stated that computing `R` itself is generally intractable due to the maximization over all configurations, which is why the bound in Lemma 5 is necessary.

*   **KL Divergence Notation:** In the introduction, the notation `KL(q||p)` is used for both directions of the KL divergence. For clarity, it would be better to use distinct notation (e.g., `KL(p||q)` vs. `KL(q||p)`) and perhaps adhere to the common convention of using `p` for the true distribution and `q` for the approximation.

*   **Analysis of the "Best-Case" Scenario:** While minimizing KL(θ||ψ) is intractable for large models, it is tractable for the toy models used in the experiments (via the junction tree algorithm). Including these results would provide a valuable "best-case" benchmark, showing the maximum possible accuracy achievable by any fast-mixing approximation under this divergence.

*   **Discussion on the Gap with Mean-Field:** The paper correctly notes that Mean-Field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection (constraining the spectral norm to zero). Given this direct connection, the large performance gap between the two methods is interesting and warrants further discussion. What properties of the larger fast-mixing set allow for such a significant improvement in accuracy?

In summary, the paper introduces a promising and novel idea. However, its potential is tempered by significant concerns about the fairness of the experimental evaluation and the unclear scalability of the approach. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the contribution.
