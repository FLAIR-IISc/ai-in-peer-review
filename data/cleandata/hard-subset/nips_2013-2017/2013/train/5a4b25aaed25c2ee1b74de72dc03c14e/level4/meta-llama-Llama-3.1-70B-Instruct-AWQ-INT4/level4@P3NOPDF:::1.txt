This paper proposes an innovative approach to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, which are known to be "slow-mixing." The authors introduce several projections to "fast-mixing" models by identifying parameters that are as close as possible to the original model while satisfying a spectral bound that establishes rapid mixing. The experimental results show that this approach leads to improved marginal estimates for a given computation time in certain regimes, compared to variational methods (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

The technical content of the paper heavily relies on prior results that establish conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]. However, the idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method appears to be novel. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on "toy tiny Ising models," achieving some nice improvements over a good set of baselines, including standard sampling and contemporary variational methods.

Despite the promising results, there are concerns about the scalability of this approach to larger models. The experiments involve comparisons of mixing times of samplers on different models, which makes it challenging to judge how these results will scale with problem size. Furthermore, the computationally expensive projection step required to build the fast-mixing model seems not to be accounted for in the time complexity analysis. Additionally, it is unclear whether this approach can be generalized beyond binary states, as establishing convergence bounds for more general cases is far more challenging.

In general, the presentation is clear and reasonably accessible, given the technical content. However, there are some areas that require reorganization and clarification. The paper frequently refers to "standard results" without providing references or proof sketches, which can make it difficult for readers to follow. For instance, Theorem 6 is stated without a reference or proof, and Lemma 5 is left unproven, with the provided reference only proving a special case of zero-field. It would be helpful to discuss the properties of the dependency matrix R, including its tractability, which appears to be a challenge due to the maximization involved.

The second half of Section 4 is particularly challenging to follow, with references to g, M, and Lambda before they are introduced. The statement of Theorem 4 includes notation that is not adequately explained, making it difficult to understand the optimization process. It would be beneficial to provide more explanation and perhaps reduce the time spent restating results from [8] that are not crucial to the paper.

In Section 1, the notation for KL divergence is used inconsistently, with KL(q||p) used for both directions of the KL divergence. The notation needs to be shifted for one of the directions to maintain consistency with the literature. Additionally, the use of q for the true distribution and p for the tractable approximation is opposite to the convention used in most related literature.

The experimental results presented in Figure 2 do not include the computation required for projection, which raises concerns about the fairness of the comparison. A statement in Section 6.1 mentions this omission, but it is unclear and needs clarification. If the projection time is not included, the results should be revised to reflect this. As it stands, the proposed method appears to have an unfair advantage over standard Gibbs sampling.

It is disappointing that the paper does not include experiments on larger models, given that Gibbs mixing times often depend on problem size. There are alternative options for running experiments in regimes where junction tree is intractable, such as using a model with symmetries that allow the true marginals to be computed or taking the output of a very long sampling run as truth.

Although KL(θ||ψ) is intractable in general, it would be interesting to explore this as a potential "best case" for how sampling in an approximate model would perform. Junction tree could be used for the toy models used in the submission.

The large difference between the mean field error and the error from reverse KL projection deserves discussion, especially since mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection.

In conclusion, the idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is a nice one, and the authors leverage recent work on mixing bounds in an elegant way. However, there are concerns about the experimental comparisons and the limited range of models to which this approach is potentially applicable. Addressing these concerns and clarifying the presentation will be essential to strengthening the paper.
