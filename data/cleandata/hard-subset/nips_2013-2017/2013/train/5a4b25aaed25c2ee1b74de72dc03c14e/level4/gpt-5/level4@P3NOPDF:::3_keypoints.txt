- Paper projects slow-mixing Ising models onto a spectral-norm–bounded fast-mixing set preserving graph structure, then performs inference on the projection.
- Projection formulated as minimizing a divergence between original and projected Gibbs distributions under the fast-mixing constraint.
- Implementations provided for multiple divergences: Euclidean (dual + L-BFGS-B), KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL (requires sampling).
- Experiments on small random graphs show proposed methods outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in marginal accuracy, and often beat Gibbs on the original model for comparable runtimes.
- Reverse KL projection yields the best empirical performance among the proposed objectives.
- Strength: principled, verifiable fast-mixing constraint via spectral-norm bound.
- Strength: methodological breadth with appropriate optimization approaches for each divergence.
- Strength: empirical improvements and practical appeal of preserving graph sparsity/topology.
- Weakness: runtime reporting is unclear—figures label x-axis as “number of samples” though sampling is only used for reverse KL; absolute wall-clock times on fixed hardware are needed.
- Weakness: projected-gradient algorithm details missing—inner L-BFGS-B stopping criteria, feasibility checks for spectral-norm, and outer-loop step-size/line-search strategy need specification.
- Weakness: reverse KL relies on sampling—sampler settings (burn-in, thinning, total samples), diagnostics, and Monte Carlo variance impact on outer optimization must be provided.
- Weakness: limited scalability—experiments only on small graphs; larger tractable models (e.g., Flach complete-graph models), grids, or real networks should be included.
- Weakness: spectral-norm bound may be conservative—need discussion of bound selection, sensitivity, adaptive strategies, and treatment of node biases.
- Weakness: preserving zero-patterns may limit projection quality under tight constraints; discuss this trade-off and report how much edge weights change.
- Empirical requests: report absolute runtimes for all methods, time breakdown for projection vs inference, variance/confidence intervals and multiple seeds, and clarify x-axis semantics or convert to runtime.
- Minor edits: replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T,” use “subgradient” for non-smooth objectives, and fix grammar/notation inconsistencies.
- Overall: interesting, well-motivated, and potentially impactful approach, but needs clearer runtime comparisons, optimization details, and more scalable experiments to strengthen practical utility.
