Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a new tractable family for approximate inference in Ising models: parameter settings guaranteed to be “fast mixing” for univariate Gibbs sampling. Rather than approximating by a tree or low-treewidth model, the authors approximate by any model whose dependency matrix has spectral norm below a threshold. For Ising models, a sufficient condition for rapid mixing is ||R||2 < 1, where Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|, so a conservative sufficient condition is to constrain the spectral norm of the absolute pairwise weight matrix. The paper studies the following program: given an intractable model θ (possibly slow mixing), project θ to the set Ψ of fast-mixing parameters ψ by minimizing a divergence D(θ, ψ), then run Gibbs sampling on ψ to estimate marginals.

Four divergences are considered. (1) Euclidean projection: the authors derive a dual formulation that respects sparsity and enforces the spectral-norm constraint on |ψ| via iterative SVD thresholding combined with LBFGS-B. (2) KL(θ||ψ): theoretically appealing but impractical at scale since it requires expectations under θ. (3) A piecewise KL that computes KL on tractable subgraphs (trees or low-treewidth subgraphs) and maximizes over them. (4) KL(ψ||θ): optimized by stochastic approximation with Gibbs samples from ψ; after each gradient step, a Euclidean projection enforces fast mixing. The approach can be viewed as generalizing naive mean-field (which corresponds to ψ with zero dependency) to a richer fast-mixing family.

Experiments on grids (8×8) and small random graphs compare approximations produced by the proposed projections to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters, evaluating marginal error and time–accuracy tradeoffs. The main empirical finding is that the reverse-KL projection consistently yields more accurate marginals than LBP/TRW/MF and, for a range of time budgets, outperforms Gibbs sampling on the original model; using the exact KL(θ||ψ) on small graphs serves mainly as a sanity check.

Quality
- The theoretical setup and use of mixing-time bounds from Dobrushin/Levin–Peres–Wilmer/Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum are sound, and the bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is standard for Ising models. The dual Euclidean projection that preserves sparsity is well-motivated and appears correct at a high level.
- Some technical details need clarification/correction:
  - Theorem 6: for the constraint ||B||2 ≤ c, the singular values S′ii should be thresholded at c (not c 2). Please confirm the exact constant and notation.
  - Definition of total variation distance typically includes a factor 1/2; please state the convention clearly and consistently.
  - Piecewise KL uses a max over subgraphs; the objective is non-smooth and the proper notion is a subgradient (at ties). Please clarify the optimization method used in this non-smooth case.
  - The paper proves guarantees for random-scan Gibbs (Theorem 4) but uses systematic scan in experiments; please justify the applicability or discuss any differences.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized, with a solid contextualization relative to mean-field, structured mean-field, LBP/TRW, and planar approximations.
- Missing important implementation details: the projected gradient algorithm has two nested loops (outer divergence descent; inner Euclidean projection solved via LBFGS-B). Please specify stopping criteria for LBFGS-B and the overall projection (tolerances, max iterations), and how these interact with the outer loop.
- Figures: The x-axis in Figure 2 and the supplement is labeled “number of samples,” but sampling is used only for the reverse-KL method. If the intent is total runtime, please report wall-clock seconds and include runtime for LBP, TRW, and MF to enable fair time–accuracy comparisons across all methods.
- Minor edits:
  - Line 222: “onto graph T” instead of “onto the tree.”
  - Line 226: use “subgradient” rather than “derivative” for the max-over-subgraphs objective.
  - A few typos (e.g., “KL-divergnce”) and minor notation issues could be cleaned up.

Originality
- The idea of projecting onto a fast-mixing set defined by a spectral norm constraint, while preserving sparsity, is novel and distinct from traditional variational families defined by graph structure.
- The dual Euclidean projection with sparsity constraints and the use of KL(ψ||θ) with projected stochastic gradients are interesting contributions. The piecewise KL perspective offers a tractable compromise between exact KL and fully variational relaxations.

Significance
- Practically, the approach offers a compelling alternative when exact inference is intractable and long-run MCMC is too expensive: by enforcing fast mixing, one can get reliable short-run Gibbs estimates with better accuracy than standard variational baselines and, within a time budget, better than running Gibbs on the original model.
- Limitations reduce immediate impact: applicability is demonstrated only for small graphs and Ising models; the sufficient spectral norm bound is conservative, and the paper sometimes uses a bound of 2.5 (exceeding the theoretical guarantee) to gain accuracy—this needs empirical justification of actual mixing and a discussion of tradeoffs.

Empirical evaluation
- Strengths: comparisons to standard baselines; evaluation on both grids and random graphs; sensible piecewise subgraph choices; clear advantage of reverse-KL projection.
- Missing pieces: full runtime accounting (including projection cost), sensitivity to hyperparameters (step size, pool size, iterations), and larger-scale experiments. Consider adding larger instances or models with tractable partition function (e.g., the complete-graph constructions of Flach, TPAMI) to further validate accuracy/scaling. Reporting effective sample sizes or autocorrelation times would help substantiate the “fast-mixing” claim in practice, especially when using a spectral bound >1.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated idea: fast-mixing as a tractable family.
- Solid theoretical grounding; elegant Euclidean projection algorithm that preserves sparsity.
- Reverse-KL projection with stochastic gradients is practical and performs best empirically.
- Consistent empirical gains over LBP/TRW/MF; favorable time–accuracy tradeoffs vs Gibbs on original parameters.

Cons
- Incomplete experimental reporting of runtime; ambiguity in figures.
- Use of a spectral bound larger than the proven threshold undermines guarantees; requires empirical mixing diagnostics.
- Limited scale and scope (small graphs; Ising only); unclear scalability of the inner projection loop.
- Some technical clarifications needed (TV definition; SVD threshold constant; subgradient handling; scan schedule vs theory).

Questions for the authors
- Precisely how do you terminate the LBFGS-B inner loop in the Euclidean projection, and how sensitive are results to projection tolerances?
- What is the exact x-axis in Fig. 2 and the supplement? Please report wall-clock time and include LBP/TRW/MF runtimes.
- Why is a spectral norm bound of 2.5 used when theory suggests <1? Can you provide empirical mixing diagnostics (e.g., coupling time estimates, autocorrelation, Gelman–Rubin) to support the “still fast mixing” claim?
- How does performance scale with graph size and degree? Can you include larger models or those with tractable ground truth (e.g., complete-graph models with known partition functions)?

Recommendation
An interesting and well-executed paper with a novel angle on tractability and strong empirical promise. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying runtime reporting and the discrepancy between theoretical and practical spectral bounds, and adding more rigorous mixing/efficiency evidence.
