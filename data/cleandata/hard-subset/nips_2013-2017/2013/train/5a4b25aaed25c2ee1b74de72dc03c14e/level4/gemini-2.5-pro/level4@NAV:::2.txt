The authors present a technique for accelerating the convergence of Gibbs sampling in Ising models. The core of their approach is to reparameterize the model by projecting its original parameters onto a new set that is guaranteed to satisfy the Dobrushin condition, which ensures rapid mixing. This projection is formulated as an optimization problem subject to constraints that are designed to preserve the support of the original model. The paper investigates this formulation using several different distance and divergence measures.

The combination of principles from stochastic and deterministic inference is a valuable and under-explored research direction, and I believe the central concept of this work is promising. The high-level idea of augmenting parameters to improve mixing is intuitive and clever. However, the technical exposition of the projection's dual formulation lacks clarity. For example, it is not immediately apparent how the `zij*dij = 0` constraints enforce that the new parameterization occupies the exact same space, rather than a smaller subspace. The overall procedure is also ambiguous; it is unclear whether the projected gradient descent is run to convergence before sampling begins, or if the gradient updates and sampling steps are interleaved. A formal algorithm description in a box would be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper should address whether the projected gradient descent strategy is guaranteed to converge.

The empirical evaluation could also be strengthened to be more persuasive. In the first set of experiments, the choice to run the Gibbs sampler for a fixed 30,000 iterations seems arbitrary; when comparing sampling-based methods to deterministic ones, a comparison based on total runtime would be a more appropriate metric. Moreover, the plots lack error bars, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the results. The second set of experiments shows that the projected parameters lead to faster mixing compared to the naive Gibbs sampler. However, this comparison overlooks the computational overhead required to perform the projection itself. This cost is not factored into the analysis, but if the projection is time-consuming, it could negate the benefits of faster sampling. Lastly, the experimental section would be more complete with a comparison to other well-known methods for improving Gibbs sampling, such as blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate's "Lifted Gibbs Sampler."

Overall, this paper is based on a very nice idea. Nevertheless, the development of the projection problem and the proposed algorithm requires refinement, and a more robust set of experiments is needed to convince me of the method's practical utility.
