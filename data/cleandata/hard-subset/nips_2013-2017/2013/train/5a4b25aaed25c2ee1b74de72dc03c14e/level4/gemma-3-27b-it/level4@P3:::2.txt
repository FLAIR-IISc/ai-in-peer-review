This paper explores a compelling idea: improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting model parameters onto a space guaranteeing faster convergence. The authors formulate this projection as a constrained optimization problem under several divergence measures, aiming to find parameters that satisfy fast-mixing criteria like the Dobrushin condition while remaining close to the original parameters. This work sits within a growing body of research on improving MCMC methods, and the combination of deterministic optimization with stochastic sampling is a potentially fruitful direction. Related work in mean-field inference [14, 20], loopy belief propagation [21], and structured mean-field methods all attempt to trade off accuracy for computational efficiency, and this work presents a distinct approach to this trade-off.

The paper’s strengths lie in its novel formulation of the problem and its exploration of different divergence measures for the projection. The theoretical foundation, drawing on results from Markov chain mixing times [4, 8, 10], is sound. The exploration of different divergences – Euclidean, piecewise KL, and reversed KL – offers a nuanced investigation of the problem, and the proposed dual algorithm for the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a valuable contribution. The experimental evaluation, though needing refinement (see weaknesses below), demonstrates the potential benefits of the approach.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. While the core idea is intriguing, the description of the dual optimization problem could be clearer. Specifically, the role of the `zij*dij = 0` constraints in preserving the parameter space isn’t fully explained; it’s unclear how these constraints ensure the new parameterization remains consistent with the original graph structure. A more explicit algorithm description, perhaps in a dedicated “Algorithm” box, would significantly enhance clarity. Furthermore, the convergence properties of the projected gradient descent strategy aren't discussed. Is convergence guaranteed, and under what conditions? This is a crucial point given the potential for non-convexity in the optimization problem.

The experimental evaluation, while present, requires substantial improvement. The choice of 30,000 Gibbs iterations as a fixed evaluation point feels arbitrary and doesn’t allow for a fair comparison to deterministic methods. A time-based comparison would be more appropriate, acknowledging the computational cost of the projection step itself. The lack of error bars on the presented charts is a serious omission, making it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the observed results. The comparison to naive Gibbs sampling is useful, but the paper would benefit from including comparisons to more sophisticated MCMC techniques, such as blocked Gibbs sampling or Gogate’s “Lifted Gibbs Sampler” [18], which are designed to improve mixing. Finally, the experimental section doesn’t adequately address the trade-off between projection time and the benefits of faster mixing; a quantitative analysis of this trade-off is crucial for demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed method. The discussion of the looseness of the spectral norm bound (Section 7) also highlights a limitation that should be more prominently acknowledged in the main body of the paper.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to improving Gibbs sampling by parameter projection.
*   Sound theoretical foundation based on established results in Markov chain theory.
*   Exploration of multiple divergence measures for the projection.
*   Demonstrated potential for faster mixing in experimental evaluations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in the description of the dual optimization problem.
*   Unclear convergence properties of the projected gradient descent algorithm.
*   Insufficiently rigorous experimental evaluation (missing error bars, time-based comparison lacking, missing baselines).
*   Limited discussion of the trade-off between projection cost and mixing speed.
*   Potentially overly conservative spectral norm bound.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field. However, the current presentation suffers from clarity issues and lacks a sufficiently compelling experimental validation. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should: 1) clarify the dual optimization problem, 2) discuss the convergence properties of their algorithm, 3) significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation, and 4) provide a more thorough analysis of the trade-offs involved. Addressing these points would substantially improve the paper and increase its potential impact.
