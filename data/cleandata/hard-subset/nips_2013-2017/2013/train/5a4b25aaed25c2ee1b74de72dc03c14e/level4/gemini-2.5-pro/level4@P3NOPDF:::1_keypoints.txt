- Core idea: project a slow-mixing pairwise Ising model onto a nearby model that is provably fast-mixing using spectral bounds, and perform inference on that approximation.  
- Authors propose several projection methods and show empirically that these can yield better marginal estimates in some regimes compared to Gibbs, mean-field, and belief propagation.  
- Strength: novel, principled approach that leverages recent theoretical mixing-time bounds and appears original and elegant.  
- Experiments are small-scale (“toy tiny” Ising models), so scalability to larger, realistic models is unclear.  
- Major concern: the computational cost of the projection optimization is potentially large and seems omitted from timing comparisons, making runtime claims unfair.  
- Applicability beyond binary pairwise Ising models is uncertain because convergence guarantees used are specific to binary cases.  
- Clarity issues: several “standard results” are stated as theorems without citation or proof sketch (e.g., Theorem 6) and Lemma 5 is unproven or improperly referenced.  
- The dependency matrix R needs a fuller discussion; computing its maximization appears intractable and the paper should state this and motivate Lemma 5 explicitly.  
- Section 4 is hard to follow: variables (g, M, Lambda) introduced after use and Theorem 7’s notation is inadequately explained; the section should be rewritten for clarity.  
- Notation inconsistencies: KL divergence direction is used ambiguously and the q/p convention is reversed from common practice, risking reader confusion.  
- Experimental evaluation must explicitly state whether projection time is included in runtimes, and if not, rerun experiments including that cost.  
- Experiments should include larger models (or symmetric cases/long MCMC runs as ground truth) where junction tree is intractable to demonstrate practical usefulness.  
- Additional baseline: compute the forward KL projection KL(θ||ψ) on toy models (possible via junction tree) to establish a “best-case” comparison for this approximation family.  
- The large performance gap between the proposed projection and mean-field (a degenerate reverse-KL case) deserves further analysis and explanation.  
- Overall: promising and well-motivated idea, but major concerns about scalability, projection cost accounting, broader applicability, and technical clarity must be addressed.
