The authors propose a method to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the original model parameterization onto a new setting that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion for fast mixing. This projection is formulated as a constrained optimization problem, where the constraints ensure the new parameters are defined over the same space as the original parameters. The authors examine this projection under various distance and divergence measures.

In my opinion, combining principles from stochastic and deterministic inference is an under-explored area, making this idea interesting. Although the concept of augmenting original parameters to improve mixing time is straightforward, the description of the dual of the projection problem is unclear. For instance, it is not immediately apparent how the zij*dij = 0 constraints guarantee that the new parameterization is defined over the same space as the original parameters, rather than a smaller subspace. Furthermore, the overall procedure is not well-defined - it is unclear whether the projected gradient operations are performed to completion before running the Gibbs sampler, or if the gradient updates are interleaved with the sampling process. An algorithm description box would help clarify this.

Additionally, it is unclear whether the proposed projected gradient descent strategy is guaranteed to converge. The experiments presented are not entirely convincing. In the first set of experiments, running the Gibbs sampler for 30,000 iterations seems arbitrary. Given that the comparison is between a sampling method and deterministic methods, a comparison based on time would be more informative. Moreover, error bars are absent from the plots, making it difficult to assess the significance of the results. The second set of results demonstrates that the projection leads to faster mixing, but neglects to account for the time required to perform the projection operation. For instance, if the projection takes one minute, and the naive Gibbs sampler can generate 10,000 samples in that time, the benefits of the projection may be offset. Notably, there is no comparison to blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate's "Lifted Gibbs Sampler".

Overall, the idea is promising, but the development of the projection problem and proposed method could benefit from further refinement. A more comprehensive set of experiments is necessary to convincingly demonstrate the utility of the method.
