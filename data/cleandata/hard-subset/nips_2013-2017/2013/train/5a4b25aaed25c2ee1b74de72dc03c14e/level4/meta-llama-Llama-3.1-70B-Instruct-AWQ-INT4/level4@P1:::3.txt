The paper proposes an innovative approach to inference in Markov Random Fields (MRFs), specifically the Ising model, by projecting the input model onto a set of models that guarantee fast mixing. The success of inference by Gibbs sampling in MRFs heavily depends on the mixing rate of the underlying Monte Carlo Markov chain. The authors define the space of fast-mixing models by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute values of Ising edge strengths.

The paper explores various divergences, including Euclidean distance, KL-divergence, piecewise KL-divergence, and reversed KL-divergence, to define the "projection" onto the set of fast-mixing models. The authors derive a dual algorithm to solve the projection in Euclidean distance using LBFGS-B and employ a projected gradient algorithm for other divergences. Notably, the reversed KL-divergence requires Gibbs sampling on a fast-mixing model to compute the projection.

The extensive experimental evaluation compares the proposed methods with loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The results demonstrate that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy and, for a reasonable range of runtimes, also Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed methods, the one with reversed KL-divergence performs consistently best.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a novel approach to inference in MRFs. The experimental results are convincing, and the paper is well-written. However, there are a few minor issues that need clarification.

Firstly, the inner loop of the projected gradient algorithm in Section 5.1 uses LBFGS-B, but the stopping criteria for the inner iterations are not specified. Providing details on when the inner iterations are stopped would be helpful.

Secondly, the plots in Figure 2 (and the supplement) have a horizontal axis titled "number of samples," but sampling is only used for reversed KL divergence. It would be more accurate to label the horizontal axis as "runtime of the algorithm." Additionally, reporting the runtime of LBP, TRW, and MF would ensure a fair accuracy-runtime comparison of all tested algorithms.

Lastly, considering experimental comparisons with larger models, such as those from the paper "A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function" by Boris Flach, would be an interesting option. This would allow polynomial inference and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods.

Minor corrections:

* Replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T" on page 226.
* Consider using "subgradient" instead of "derivative" on page 226, as the gradient is not always well-defined in the context of KL-divergence.

Overall, the paper presents an innovative approach to inference in MRFs, and the experimental results are convincing. However, clarifying the minor issues mentioned above would strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed methods.
