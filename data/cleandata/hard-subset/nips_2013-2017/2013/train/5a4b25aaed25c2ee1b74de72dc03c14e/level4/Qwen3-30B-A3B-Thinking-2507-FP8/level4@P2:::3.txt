

This paper proposes a novel approach to accelerate inference in high-treewidth Ising models by projecting slow-mixing parameter sets onto a fast-mixing subset defined by a spectral norm constraint on interaction strengths. The authors formalize "fast-mixing" as Gibbs sampling converging rapidly when the spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix is below 1.0. They derive projection methods under four divergences: Euclidean (using dual optimization with LBFGS-B), piecewise KL (approximating KL on low-treewidth subgraphs), and reversed KL (using stochastic approximation via Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model). Experiments on 8×8 grids and 10-node graphs demonstrate that all proposed methods outperform standard variational approaches (LBP, TRW, MF) in marginal accuracy, especially within practical runtime limits, with reversed KL yielding the best results. Critically, they show that projecting before sampling achieves better accuracy than direct Gibbs sampling on slow-mixing models within finite time.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The theoretical foundation is sound, establishing a clear sufficient condition for fast mixing (Theorem 4) and rigorously deriving projections for multiple divergences. The Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) and stochastic gradient approach for reversed KL are technically well-executed.  
*Clarity:* The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions (e.g., dependency matrix, spectral norm constraint) and effective experimental design. The connection between fast-mixing parameters and practical inference is compelling.  
*Originality:* The focus on *parameter-space conditions* for rapid mixing (rather than graph structure) is novel and distinct from prior work (e.g., mean-field, tree-reweighted methods). It directly addresses a gap in MCMC-based inference for Ising models.  
*Significance:* The method provides a practical framework for accelerating inference in high-treewidth models, with demonstrated empirical superiority over established baselines. It opens avenues for learning with fast-mixing constraints (Section 7).

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Experimental Ambiguity:** The horizontal axis in Figure 2 (and supplements) is labeled "number of samples" but does not correspond to sampling counts for all methods (e.g., LBP/TRW/MF require no sampling). This misrepresents runtime comparisons. **The authors must clarify whether this axis denotes absolute runtime (seconds) and report absolute runtimes for all baselines (LBP, TRW, MF) to enable fair accuracy-runtime analysis.** Without this, the experiments cannot be properly interpreted.  
2. **Runtime Overhead:** The method’s practical utility is questionable due to high computational cost (e.g., 30,000 Gibbs iterations for Euclidean projection). **The authors should quantify this overhead explicitly** (e.g., "Projection adds X% runtime vs. direct Gibbs sampling") and discuss tradeoffs in the context of real-world applications.  
3. **Conservative Bound:** The spectral norm threshold (1.0) is overly conservative for 2D Ising models (Section 7), where a tighter bound βc ≈ 0.44 is known. While the paper notes using 2.5 in practice, **this should be justified** (e.g., "empirically effective despite theoretical looseness").  
4. **Scope Limitation:** Experiments are restricted to small graphs (≤10 nodes). **A comparison with larger models** (e.g., Boris Flach’s polynomial-time inference models as suggested) would strengthen significance claims.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel theoretical insight (fast-mixing parameter sets), strong empirical results against baselines, clear technical contributions, and practical relevance to inference bottlenecks.  
*Con:* Ambiguous runtime metrics, unquantified computational overhead, conservative theoretical bound, and limited scalability validation.

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept.** The paper makes a significant contribution to inference in high-treewidth models with a novel, well-executed approach. The empirical results are convincing, and the theoretical framework is robust. The weaknesses are addressable via minor revisions (clarifying runtime metrics, quantifying overhead, justifying the spectral bound), and the significance outweighs the limitations. The authors should resolve the runtime ambiguity and overhead quantification in the rebuttal to fully realize the paper’s potential.
