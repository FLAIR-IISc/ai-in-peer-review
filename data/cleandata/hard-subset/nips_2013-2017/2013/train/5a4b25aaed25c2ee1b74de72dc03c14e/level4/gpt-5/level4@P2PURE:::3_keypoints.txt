- Paper proposes projecting an Ising MRF onto a set of fast-mixing models (bounded spectral norm of absolute edge strengths) and performing inference on that surrogate.
- Projection is defined via divergences between Gibbs distributions, constrained to preserve graph structure; Euclidean projection solved by dualizing with L-BFGS-B, other divergences via projected gradient.
- Reverse KL projection requires running Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model during projection.
- Experiments on small random models compare proposed variants (Euclid, KL, piecewise-KL, reverse-KL) against loopy BP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original model, evaluating accuracy and accuracy vs runtime.
- Results show the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and across a reasonable runtime range also beat Gibbs on the original model; reverse KL performs best among variants.
- Concern: Projected gradient method has nested loops with L-BFGS-B as inner loop—stopping criteria for inner iterations not specified.
- Concern: Figure 2’s horizontal axis labeled “number of samples” is unclear and likely should be runtime; runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF are missing—report absolute runtimes in seconds for fair comparison.
- Suggestion: Include experiments on larger models (e.g., Flach’s tractable random-field class) to assess scalability and practical utility.
- Minor edits: change “onto the tree” (Line 222) to “onto graph T,” and use “subgradient” instead of “derivative” (Line 226).
- Overall: Interesting and empirically convincing paper, but practical utility may be limited by runtime—clarification needed in rebuttal.
