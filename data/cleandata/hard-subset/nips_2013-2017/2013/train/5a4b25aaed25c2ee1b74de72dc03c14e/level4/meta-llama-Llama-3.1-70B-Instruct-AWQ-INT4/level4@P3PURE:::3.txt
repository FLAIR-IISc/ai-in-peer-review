This paper proposes an approach to inference in Markov Random Fields (MRFs), specifically Ising models, by projecting the input model onto a set of fast-mixing models. The success of Gibbs sampling in MRFs heavily relies on the mixing rate of the underlying Monte Carlo Markov chain. The authors define the space of fast-mixing models by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute values of Ising edge strengths.

The proposed approach involves two steps: (1) projecting the input model onto the set of fast-mixing models, and (2) performing inference on the obtained model. The projection is defined using divergences of Gibbs distributions and is forced to preserve the graph structure. For Euclidean distance, the authors use dualization of the initial task and the LBFGS-B algorithm to obtain the projection. For other divergences, including KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences, a projected gradient algorithm is employed. Notably, the reversed KL divergence requires Gibbs sampling on a fast-mixing model to compute the projection.

The authors present extensive experiments on small random models, comparing the approximated marginals with the true marginals. The methods tested include the proposed approach with various divergences, as well as loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The experiments evaluate both accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy trade-offs. The results show that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in terms of accuracy, and also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model for a reasonable range of runtimes. Among the proposed methods, the one using reversed KL divergence performs consistently best.

However, there are several aspects that require clarification. Firstly, the projected gradient algorithm described in Section 5.1 involves two nested loops, with the inner loop being LBFGS-B. It would be helpful to provide details on when the inner iterations are stopped. Secondly, the horizontal axis in the plots in Figure 2 (and the supplement) is unclear, as it is titled "number of samples" but sampling is only used for reversed KL divergence. It would be more informative to use runtime of the algorithm as the horizontal axis, and also report the runtime of LBP, TRW, and MF to ensure a fair accuracy-runtime comparison. Providing absolute running times in seconds would also be beneficial.

Furthermore, it would be interesting to consider experimental comparisons with larger models. One potential option is to use models from the paper by Boris Flach, which allow for polynomial inference.

Additionally, there are a few minor corrections to be made. On page 222, "onto the tree" should be replaced with "onto graph T." On page 226, it would be more accurate to use the term "subgradient" instead of "derivative."

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach with convincing empirical results. However, the practical utility of the method may be limited due to high runtime, which requires further clarification in the rebuttal.
