- Proposes projecting a pairwise Ising model onto the nearest “fast-mixing” model to accelerate Gibbs sampling; novel framing grounded in prior mixing bounds.  
- Experiments show improved marginal estimation versus standard Gibbs and variational baselines on small-scale Ising models under a fixed compute budget.  
- Scalability concern: experiments are limited to tiny “toy” models (5–15 variables), so generalization to realistic problem sizes is untested.  
- Critical omission: projection computational cost (solving a non-convex optimization) is not included in runtime comparisons, potentially inflating claimed speedups.  
- Theoretical clarity issues: several “standard results” are presented as theorems without proof sketches or proper references, harming reproducibility.  
- Lemma 5 is unproven and cites a reference that only covers the zero-field case, making its applicability questionable.  
- The dependency matrix R—central to analysis—has an intractable embedded maximization that is not explicitly acknowledged or addressed.  
- Presentation problems in Section 4: Theorem 7 references symbols before definition and Theorem 4 uses unexplained notation; overall opacity and excessive restatement of prior work.  
- Notational inconsistency: KL divergence is written as KL(q‖p) with q as the true distribution and p as the approximation, contrary to standard convention and confusing the variational interpretation.  
- Paper fails to explain why mean field (reverse KL) shows substantially larger error than reverse KL projections, leaving an important empirical gap unaddressed.  
- No experiments on larger models or suggested proxies (e.g., symmetric models or long-run Gibbs) to assess scalability and real-world relevance.  
- No discussion of extending the approach beyond binary states; feasibility and mixing bounds for non-binary models are not considered.  
- Summary judgment: idea is elegant and promising, but experimental omissions and theoretical/notation issues prevent a compelling case for broader adoption without expanded experiments (including projection time) or tempered claims.
