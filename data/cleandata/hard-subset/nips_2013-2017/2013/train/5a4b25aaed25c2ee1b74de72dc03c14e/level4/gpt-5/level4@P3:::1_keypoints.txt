- Proposes projecting Ising-model parameters onto a provably fast-mixing family using a spectral-norm constraint on interaction strengths.  
- Considers multiple projection criteria: Euclidean, inclusive KL(θ||ψ), tractable piecewise KL, and reverse KL(ψ||θ) optimized by stochastic approximation.  
- Derives a dual formulation for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) that preserves sparsity and enforces the spectral constraint.  
- Leverages classic mixing-time results (Dobrushin-type, spectral criteria) and connects to variational methods (MF, LBP, TRW, piecewise training).  
- Novel idea in NIPS literature: use provably fast-mixing parameter sets as the tractable family for approximation.  
- Theory: correctly applies sufficient rapid-mixing conditions and uses tanh(|βij|) to avoid computing the dependency matrix exactly.  
- Euclidean projection result is interesting and could be a useful tool if detailed thoroughly.  
- Algorithmics: reverse-KL via stochastic approximation is reasonable because ψ is sampleable; piecewise KL is a sensible surrogate.  
- Experiments: show improvements over baselines on small graphs in regimes with strong interactions and limited sampling time.  
- Experimental shortcoming: time-accuracy comparisons omit the projection/optimization cost, biasing comparisons in favor of the proposed methods.  
- Limitation: method relies on sufficient (not necessary) conditions that can be conservative and may reduce accuracy.  
- Serious concern: experiments use a spectral bound ≈2.5, exceeding the theoretical <1 guarantee and undermining the stated mixing guarantee.  
- Clarity issue: several “standard results” (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5) lack citations or proof sketches and need clear references.  
- Clarity issue: dependency matrix R computation is intractable and needs explicit explanation that the tanh bound is used in practice.  
- Clarity issue: Section 4 and Theorem 7 introduce variables/operators (g, M, Λ, Πc, Z) before defining them; needs stepwise presentation and intuition.  
- Notation issue: inconsistent use of p, q and KL directions across the paper; standardize throughout.  
- Significance: approach could be practical if scalable, trading asymptotic correctness for guaranteed rapid mixing, but current evidence is limited to small models.  
- Scalability concern: unclear how approach and mixing gains scale to larger graphs or non-binary variables and other MCMC schemes.  
- Experimental recommendations: include total wall-clock cost (including projection), scale to larger graphs, and add KL(θ||ψ) baseline on small graphs.  
- Experimental recommendation: compare to MF as a degenerate reverse-KL case and explain why fast-mixing reverse-KL outperforms MF.  
- Experimental recommendation: justify and empirically validate any relaxed spectral bounds (>1) with mixing diagnostics.  
- Pros: novel perspective, technically interesting Euclidean projection preserving sparsity, and promising reverse-KL sampling approach.  
- Cons: omission of projection cost in experiments, limited scale, mismatch between theoretical guarantees and experimental settings, and clarity/notation problems.  
- Suggested improvements: add citations/proofs for standard results, rework Section 4 with definitions and a worked example, standardize notation, report total computational cost, and add larger-scale and KL(θ||ψ) experiments.  
- Overall: creative and potentially impactful idea with promising small-scale results, but needs clearer presentation, stronger empirical evaluation, and reconciliation between theory and experimental choices.
