This paper addresses the challenge of inference in Ising models where Gibbs sampling may converge slowly due to strong interactions. The core idea is a novel two-step approximate inference strategy: first, project the parameters of a given slow-mixing model onto the set of parameters that are guaranteed to be fast-mixing; second, perform inference by running Gibbs sampling on this new, projected model. The set of fast-mixing models is defined by a sufficient condition from the literature: the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths must be less than one.

The authors explore several ways to perform this projection, each defined by minimizing a different divergence measure while preserving the original graph structure. For the Euclidean distance between parameter vectors, they derive an efficient dual algorithm solved with LBFGS-B. For other, more probabilistically motivated divergences—namely the forward ("zero-avoiding") KL divergence, a novel piecewise KL divergence, and the reverse ("zero-forcing") KL divergence—the authors employ a projected gradient descent algorithm. Computing the gradient for the reverse KL divergence notably requires sampling from the projected (and thus, fast-mixing) model.

The main strength of the paper lies in its convincing empirical results. The authors conduct a thorough evaluation on 8x8 grids and small random graphs, comparing their projection methods against several standard baselines: Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), Naive Mean Field (MF), and standard Gibbs sampling on the original model.

The experiments demonstrate that for a fixed number of Gibbs iterations, the proposed methods consistently produce more accurate marginals than the variational baselines (LBP, TRW, MF). More importantly, the time-versus-accuracy comparison reveals a key practical benefit: for a limited computational budget, running Gibbs sampling on the projected fast-mixing model yields better estimates than running it for the same duration on the original, slow-mixing model. Among the different divergences tested, projection using the reverse KL divergence consistently delivers the best performance.

While the paper is interesting and the results are promising, there are several points that could be clarified or improved:

1.  **Algorithmic Details:** The projected gradient algorithm in Section 5.1 appears to use the LBFGS-B based Euclidean projection as an inner loop. For reproducibility and clarity, the authors should specify the stopping criterion used for this inner optimization.

2.  **Experimental Clarity:** The presentation of the experimental results could be significantly improved. The horizontal axis in the plots in Figure 2 (and the supplement) is labeled "number of samples," which is confusing as sampling is only a component of the reverse KL projection method and the final inference step. To facilitate a fair and clear comparison, I suggest the following:
    *   The horizontal axis should represent the total wall-clock time in seconds for the *entire inference procedure*, including both the projection step (if any) and the final sampling/inference step.
    *   The runtimes for the LBP, TRW, and MF baselines should also be included in these plots. Without this, it is difficult to make a fair assessment of the trade-offs between all tested algorithms.

3.  **Scalability:** The experiments are performed on small graphs (10 nodes or 8x8 grids). To better establish the practical utility of this approach, it would be valuable to see results on larger models. This would also help clarify the runtime overhead of the projection step, which is a potential concern for the method's applicability. The authors might consider using the tractable models on complete graphs from Flach (TPAMI, 2013) as a source for larger-scale ground-truth comparisons.

4.  **Minor Points:**
    *   On line 222, "onto the tree" should likely be "onto the graph T".
    *   On line 226, since the objective involves a `max` operator, "subgradient" may be a more precise term than "derivative".

In summary, this is an interesting paper that introduces a novel and well-motivated approach for approximate inference. The empirical results are strong and suggest a clear advantage over several standard methods, particularly in time-limited scenarios. However, the practical utility of the method is currently clouded by a lack of clarity regarding its computational cost and an evaluation that is limited to small-scale problems. If the authors can address the issues regarding experimental reporting and provide evidence of scalability, this could be a significant contribution.
