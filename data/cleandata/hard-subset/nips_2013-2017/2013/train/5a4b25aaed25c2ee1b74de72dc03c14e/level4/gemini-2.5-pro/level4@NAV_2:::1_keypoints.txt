- Central contribution: project original Ising-model parameters onto the nearest “fast-mixing” parameter set by enforcing a spectral-norm constraint on the interaction matrix.  
- Technical development: derive projection methods for multiple divergences (Euclidean, KL approximations) and show sampling from projected models can yield more accurate marginals within a time budget.  
- Strength: conceptually novel and elegant shift from tractability-by-structure to tractability-by-fast-mixing, leveraging spectral mixing-time bounds.  
- Strength: clever application of spectral bounds to constrain an optimization for approximate inference.  
- Empirical result: small-scale experiments show improvements over baselines (Loopy BP, TRW, standard Gibbs) on 10-node graphs and 8×8 grids.  
- Clarity issue: several key results (e.g., Theorem 6) are asserted without citation or supporting proof.  
- Clarity issue: Lemma 5 (extension to non-zero field) lacks proof or sketch, leaving verification to the reader.  
- Clarity issue: derivation of the dual method (Section 4) is opaque—variables introduced before definition and Theorem 7 notation is insufficiently explained.  
- Clarity issue: properties and computational difficulty of the dependency matrix R are under-explained, weakening motivation for Lemma 5’s bound.  
- Notational inconsistencies: confusing use of KL(q||p) for both divergences and unconventional labeling of true distribution as q.  
- Experimental fairness: projection computational cost appears excluded from runtime comparisons, unfairly favoring the proposed method.  
- Experimental scope: experiments are limited to small models where exact marginals are computable and may not reflect challenges in larger, harder-to-mix problems.  
- Comparative analysis gap: the large performance gap between reverse-KL projection and mean-field (zero-constrained case) is not analyzed or explained.  
- Missing oracle baseline: paper should compute the zero-avoiding KL(θ||ψ) projection via exact inference for small models as an “oracle” upper bound.  
- Generalization concern: reliance on Ising-specific mixing-time bounds raises questions about applicability to general discrete MRFs.  
- Scalability concern: the projection algorithm (SVD and iterative optimization) may not scale to very large models.  
- Overall summary: idea is strong and promising, but paper requires substantial revisions in technical clarity and a fairer, broader experimental evaluation before acceptance.
