The authors propose a method for improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model’s original parameterization onto a new setting that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion, which is known to guarantee fast mixing. They formulate this projection as a constrained optimization problem, with constraints designed to ensure the new parameters remain within the same space as the original parameters, and examine this projection under several distance and divergence measures.

In my opinion, methods that combine principles from stochastic and deterministic inference represent an under-explored area, and as a result, I find this to be an interesting idea. While augmenting the original parameters to improve mixing time is conceptually straightforward, I found the description of the dual of the projection problem somewhat unclear – specifically, it is not immediately obvious how the constraints *z<sub>ij</sub>* *d<sub>ij</sub>* = 0 ensure that the new parameterization remains over the same space (couldn’t it potentially restrict the space to a smaller subset?). I was also unsure of the overall procedure: are the projected gradient operations performed to completion before running a Gibbs sampler, or is sampling interleaved with the gradient updates? An algorithm description box would greatly improve clarity. Furthermore, is the proposed projected gradient descent strategy guaranteed to converge?

I also found the experimental results to be somewhat unconvincing. In the first set of experiments, the rationale for running the Gibbs sampler for 30,000 iterations is unclear. Given the comparison to deterministic methods, a performance comparison based on time would seem more equitable. Additionally, the charts lack error bars, making it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the presented results. The second set of results demonstrate that employing the proposed projections leads to faster mixing compared to the original, naive Gibbs sampler. However, the computational cost of performing the projection itself is not accounted for in this comparison; for example, if the projection takes one minute while the naive Gibbs sampler generates 10,000 samples in that same time, the projection might not be advantageous. Finally, why was there no comparison to blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate’s “Lifted Gibbs Sampler”?

All in all, this is a very promising idea. However, the development of the projection problem and the proposed method could benefit from further refinement, and a more comprehensive set of experiments is needed to convincingly demonstrate the utility of this approach.
