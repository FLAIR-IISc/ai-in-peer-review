This work addresses the challenge of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, proposing a novel approach based on projecting the original model onto nearby, rapidly mixing alternatives. The core idea is to identify parameter settings that approximate the original model while ensuring a spectral bound guaranteeing faster convergence. Experiments demonstrate improved marginal estimate accuracy—compared to both standard Gibbs sampling and variational methods (mean field and belief propagation)—in specific scenarios, for a given computational budget.

The technical foundation is firmly built on existing literature concerning conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]. However, the authors present a genuinely new perspective by framing approximate inference as a projection onto the closest rapidly mixing model. 

On small, illustrative Ising models, the proposed methods yield tangible improvements over established baselines. My primary reservation concerns the scalability of these results to larger, more complex instances. Assessing mixing times across different models makes it difficult to predict performance as problem size increases. Additionally, the computationally demanding projection step—required to construct the fast-mixing model—appears to be unaccounted for in the current analysis. Finally, the method’s potential applicability is seemingly limited to binary states, given the inherent challenges of generalizing convergence bounds to broader state spaces.

Regarding clarity, the presentation is generally clear, considering the technical depth. However, several areas require refinement:

* The paper frequently references “standard results” and presents them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without providing citations or even brief proofs. Lemma 5 is stated without proof, and the cited reference only validates it for a specific zero-field case.
* The properties of the dependency matrix *R* warrant further discussion. Specifically, the tractability of its maximization should be addressed; I suspect it’s intractable, which would explain the invocation of Lemma 5. 
* The latter half of Section 4 is difficult to follow. The text introduces *g*, *M*, and *Lambda* prior to their definition and uses unexplained notation in the statement of Theorem 4. A more thorough explanation is needed if this optimization is central to the work. (Consider streamlining the restatement of results from [8], which appear less crucial.)
* Section 1 inconsistently uses KL(q||p) to represent both forward and reverse KL divergence; notation should be clarified. Furthermore, the initial paragraph defines *q* as the true distribution and *p* as the tractable approximation, which deviates from the convention in most related work.

Concerning the experiments, the time comparison in Figure 2 doesn't appear to incorporate the cost of the projection step. While Section 6.1 alludes to this, the statement is ambiguous and requires clarification. If projection time is excluded, results including it should be presented, as the current presentation unfairly favors the proposed methods by neglecting the overhead of a sophisticated variational optimization.

The absence of experiments on larger models is a notable drawback, given the known dependence of Gibbs mixing times on problem size. Alternative approaches could involve models with symmetries enabling the computation of true marginals or using the output of long sampling runs as a ground truth. 

Although intractable in general, exploring KL(θ||ψ) – representing the performance of sampling in the approximate model relative to the true distribution – would be valuable as a theoretical “best case” scenario. (Junction tree algorithms could be utilized for the current toy models.) 

The paper correctly identifies mean field as a degenerate case of reverse KL projection, but the significant discrepancy between mean field error and reverse KL projection error warrants discussion.

In summary, the concept of projecting slow-mixing models onto their nearest fast-mixing counterparts is innovative and leverages recent advances in mixing bound theory effectively. However, questions regarding the experimental comparisons and the method’s limited applicability necessitate further investigation.
