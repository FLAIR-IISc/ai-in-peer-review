This paper addresses the problem of slow mixing in Gibbs samplers for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions. The authors propose projecting the original model onto a set of "fast-mixing" models. This is achieved by finding parameters that are as close as possible to the original model's parameters while also satisfying a spectral bound that guarantees rapid mixing. The experiments demonstrate that in certain regimes, this approach yields improved marginal estimates for a given computation time when compared to standard Gibbs sampling on the original model and to variational methods, such as mean field and belief propagation.

The technical contribution builds heavily on prior work that established conditions for rapid mixing [4, 8]. However, the core idea of projecting a model onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as a form of approximate inference appears to be novel. On small, synthetic Ising models, the results show promising improvements over a solid set of baselines, including standard sampling and contemporary variational methods.

My primary concern is whether these results will scale to larger, more realistic models. The paper's claims warrant a higher degree of skepticism than usual in this regard. The experiments compare the mixing times of samplers on different models, making it difficult to extrapolate how these comparisons will scale with the problem size. Furthermore, the projection step required to find the fast-mixing model appears to be computationally expensive, yet this cost is not accounted for in the experimental comparisons. Finally, it is unclear whether this approach can be generalized beyond binary states, as establishing the necessary convergence bounds for general discrete state spaces is significantly more challenging.

**Clarity**

Overall, the paper is well-written and reasonably accessible, given its technical nature. However, several sections would benefit from reorganization and clarification:

*   The paper frequently refers to "standard results," sometimes stating them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without providing a reference or a proof sketch. For instance, Lemma 5 is presented without proof, and the cited reference only proves it for the special case of a zero external field.
*   The properties of the dependency matrix R need further discussion. The maximization in its definition suggests that it may not be tractable to compute directly; the authors should clarify this. I presume its intractability is the motivation for invoking Lemma 5.
*   The second half of Section 4 is very difficult to follow. The text references g, M, and Lambda before they are formally introduced. Subsequently, the statement of Theorem 7 contains unexplained notation. If this optimization is central to the paper, it requires a much clearer explanation. The authors might consider reducing the space dedicated to restating results from [8] that are not directly used to elaborate on their own optimization procedure.
*   In Section 1, the notation for KL divergence is used inconsistently. For example, KL(q||p) appears to be used for both forward and reverse KL. Furthermore, the first paragraph defines q as the true distribution and p as the approximation, which is the reverse of the standard convention in most of the related literature. Adopting the standard notation would improve readability.

**Experiments**

*   The runtime comparisons in Figure 2 do not appear to include the computational cost of the projection step. A statement in Section 6.1 seems to allude to this, but it is ambiguous. The authors should clarify this point. If the projection time is indeed excluded, the experiments should be rerun to include it, providing a fair comparison. As presented, the proposed method benefits from a potentially expensive optimization step without accounting for its cost, which makes the claimed improvements over standard Gibbs sampling less convincing.
*   The lack of experiments on larger models is a significant weakness, especially since Gibbs mixing times are known to depend heavily on problem size. Even in regimes where exact inference (e.g., via the junction tree algorithm) is intractable, it is possible to perform such evaluations. For example, the authors could use models where symmetries allow for exact marginal computation or use the output of a very long MCMC run as a ground-truth reference.
*   While minimizing the forward KL divergence KL(p_theta || p_psi) is generally intractable, it would be illuminating to see it evaluated on the small models used in the paper, where the junction tree algorithm can be used to compute it. This would serve as a valuable "best-case" baseline for the performance of sampling from an approximate model.
*   The paper correctly notes that mean field is a degenerate case of the proposed reverse KL projection. However, the experiments show a large performance gap between the two. The reasons for this significant difference are not discussed and warrant an explanation.

In summary, the core idea of approximating a slow-mixing model by projecting it onto the closest fast-mixing one is novel and elegant. The paper effectively leverages recent theoretical work on mixing time bounds. However, the experimental evaluation is not entirely convincing due to the exclusion of the projection cost and the use of small-scale models. Furthermore, the applicability of the approach beyond binary Ising models remains an open question.
