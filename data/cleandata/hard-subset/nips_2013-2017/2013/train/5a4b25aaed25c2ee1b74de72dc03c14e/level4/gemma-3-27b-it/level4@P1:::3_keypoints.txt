- Paper proposes projecting an Ising MRF onto the set of fast-mixing models (bounded spectral norm of absolute edge matrix) and doing inference on that model.  
- Projection is defined via divergences between Gibbs distributions while preserving graph structure.  
- Euclidean projection is solved by dualizing and using LBFGS-B; KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL use a projected-gradient algorithm.  
- The reversed-KL projection requires running Gibbs sampling (on the projected fast-mixing model) inside the projection.  
- Experiments on small random models compare proposed methods to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original model, measuring marginal accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy.  
- Results: proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy; for a reasonable runtime range they also beat Gibbs on the original model; reversed-KL variant performs best.  
- Concern: the projected-gradient method has two nested loops (inner LBFGS-B); reviewer requests details on the stopping criterion for the inner iterations.  
- Concern: Figure 2’s horizontal axis is unclear (labeled “number of samples” though sampling is used only for reversed KL); reviewer asks for clarification and absolute running times in seconds, and runtime reporting for LBP, TRW, and MF for fair comparison.  
- Suggestion: include experiments on larger models, e.g., using Boris Flach’s class of random fields with tractable partition function.  
- Minor edits: line 222 “onto the tree” → “onto graph T”; line 226 use “subgradient” instead of “derivative.”  
- Overall verdict: interesting paper with convincing empirical results, but practical utility may be limited by potentially high runtime — needs clarification in rebuttal.
