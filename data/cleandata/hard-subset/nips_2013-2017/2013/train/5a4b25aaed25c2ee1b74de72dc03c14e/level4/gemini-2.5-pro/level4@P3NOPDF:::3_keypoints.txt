- Paper proposes projecting a slow-mixing Ising model onto a predefined fast-mixing model space, then performing inference on the projected model.  
- Fast-mixing space is defined by a bound on the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute edge strengths; projection preserves original graph structure.  
- Projections are defined by minimizing divergences between original and projected Gibbs distributions; explored divergences: Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL.  
- Euclidean projection solved via a dual problem with LBFGS-B; other divergences use a projected gradient algorithm.  
- Computing the reversed KL projection requires running Gibbs sampling on the projected (fast-mixing) model.  
- Empirical evaluation on small random models with exact marginals; comparisons against LBP, TRW, MF, and standard Gibbs sampling.  
- Results: proposed methods beat LBP, TRW, and MF in accuracy and outperform standard Gibbs for a range of runtimes; reversed KL projection performs best among proposed methods.  
- Major concern: experimental plots label horizontal axis as "number of samples" which is unclear for non-sampling methods; wall-clock runtime should be used and baseline runtimes reported.  
- Major concern: experiments only on small models; scalability and performance on larger or structured problems (e.g., Flach’s tractable random fields) need demonstration.  
- Minor comment: provide stopping criteria/details for inner LBFGS-B iterations in the projected gradient algorithm.  
- Minor wording suggestions: change "onto the tree" to "onto graph T" (Line 222) and consider "subgradient" instead of "derivative" in the optimization context (Line 226).  
- Overall: interesting, well-motivated approach with promising empirical results, but clarity on runtime comparisons and scalability experiments are necessary to strengthen the paper.
