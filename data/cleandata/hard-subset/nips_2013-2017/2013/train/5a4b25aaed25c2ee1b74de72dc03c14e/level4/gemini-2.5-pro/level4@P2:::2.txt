This paper proposes a novel approach for approximate inference in Ising models where standard Gibbs sampling may mix slowly due to strong interactions. The core idea is to project the original model parameters onto a new set of parameters that are guaranteed to be "fast-mixing." This guarantee is derived from a spectral condition on the interaction matrix (a sufficient condition for rapid mixing related to the Dobrushin criterion), which ensures that Gibbs sampling will converge quickly to the stationary distribution. The authors formulate this projection as a constrained optimization problem and explore it under four different divergences: the Euclidean distance between parameter vectors, the "zero-avoiding" KL divergence KL(q||p), a novel "piecewise" KL divergence based on tractable subgraphs, and the "zero-forcing" KL divergence KL(p||q). For the Euclidean case, a dual optimization algorithm is derived. For the more complex KL(p||q) case, which requires sampling, the authors employ a stochastic projected gradient descent method.

The work sits at an interesting intersection of MCMC-based inference and optimization-based variational methods. While variational methods approximate an intractable distribution with one from a tractable family (e.g., fully-factorized or tree-structured), this paper defines tractability in a new way—not by the ease of exact inference, but by the guaranteed efficiency of sampling. This is a creative and potentially significant contribution.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of projecting parameters into a "fast-mixing" subspace is novel and elegant. It reframes the concept of a "tractable approximation" and provides a principled way to address the critical problem of slow mixing in MCMC methods, which is a major bottleneck in their practical application. This approach of combining optimization with sampling is an under-explored and promising research direction.

2.  **Technical Contribution:** The paper provides concrete algorithms for performing this projection. The derivation of a dual method for the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) and the application of stochastic projected gradient descent for the KL divergence are solid technical contributions.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of the Proposed Method:** The presentation of the core projection algorithms could be improved.
    *   The description of the dual formulation for the Euclidean projection is difficult to follow. For instance, the role of the constraint `ZijBij = 0` could be explained more intuitively. As `Zij=1` for non-edges, this correctly forces those parameters to zero. However, it is not immediately clear how this formulation prevents existing edges from being removed (i.e., their parameters being set to zero), which would result in a sparser subgraph.
    *   The overall procedure for minimizing the KL(ψ||θ) divergence is also unclear. It is difficult to understand whether the projection and sampling steps are interleaved or performed sequentially as two separate stages. Including a clear algorithm box describing the stochastic projected gradient descent procedure would significantly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility.
    *   The paper states that the stochastic optimization procedure is an instance of Ergodic Mirror Descent and that convergence is guaranteed under certain conditions. It would strengthen the paper to discuss whether the chosen schedule (e.g., constant step size) meets these theoretical requirements for convergence.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results are promising but not fully convincing due to several limitations.
    *   **Fairness of Comparison:** The comparison between the proposed method and deterministic methods like Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP) and Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW) is based on a fixed number of Gibbs iterations. This does not provide a fair comparison, as the runtimes are not equivalent. A comparison based on total computation time (wall-clock time) would provide a much clearer assessment of the method's practical utility.
    *   **Cost of Projection:** The experiments comparing Gibbs sampling on the original versus projected parameters do not account for the computational cost of the projection itself. If the projection step is time-consuming, its cost might outweigh the benefits of faster mixing, especially for a finite time budget. A comprehensive analysis should include the projection time to evaluate the method's overall efficiency.
    *   **Missing Baselines:** The evaluation would be stronger if compared against other MCMC methods designed to improve mixing. Baselines such as block Gibbs sampling or more advanced techniques like Lifted Gibbs sampling would provide a more complete picture of where this method stands in relation to the state of the art in sampling.
    *   **Statistical Significance:** The plots do not include error bars or confidence intervals. Given that the results are averaged over 50 random trials, including error bars is essential to assess the statistical significance of the observed performance differences.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for Acceptance:**
    *   The paper introduces a highly original and well-motivated idea that bridges optimization-based and sampling-based inference.
    *   It addresses the significant and practical problem of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for models with strong couplings.
    *   The proposed framework is general and supported by concrete algorithms for different divergence measures.

*   **Arguments for Rejection:**
    *   The description of the core algorithms lacks clarity, potentially hindering reproducibility and full understanding of the method.
    *   The experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough to convincingly demonstrate the method's practical advantages. Key issues include the lack of time-based comparisons, the unaccounted cost of projection, and missing comparisons to relevant MCMC baselines.

Overall, this paper presents a very interesting and valuable idea. However, the weaknesses in the clarity of the methodology and the experimental evaluation currently prevent a full appreciation of its utility. I would be inclined to recommend acceptance if the authors can address the clarity issues and bolster the experimental section to provide a more convincing and fair comparison.
