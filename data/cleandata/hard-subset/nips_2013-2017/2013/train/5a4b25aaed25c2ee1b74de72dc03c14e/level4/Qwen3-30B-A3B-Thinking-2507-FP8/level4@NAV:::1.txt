

The paper addresses improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, which exhibit slow-mixing behavior. It proposes several projections to "fast-mixing" models by identifying parameters as close as possible to the original model while satisfying spectral bounds that guarantee rapid mixing. Experiments demonstrate improved marginal estimates for a fixed computation time in specific regimes compared to variational methods (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs sampling on the original model. While the technical foundation relies heavily on prior rapid-mixing conditions [4,8], the approach of projecting to the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method appears novel.  

For small "toy" Ising models, results show meaningful gains over strong baselines (standard sampling and modern variational methods). However, significant skepticism is warranted regarding scalability to larger models. The experiments compare mixing times across different models, but scaling with problem size is unclear. The computationally expensive projection step required to construct the fast-mixing model is not accounted for in computational costs, raising concerns about practicality. Generalization beyond binary states also seems uncertain, as convergence bounds for broader state spaces are substantially more challenging to establish.  

CLARITY:  
The presentation is generally clear but requires revisions. The paper cites "standard results" (e.g., Theorem 6) without references or proof sketches, and Lemma 5 lacks proof (the cited reference covers only the zero-field special case). The tractability of the dependency matrix R (involving maximization) should be explicitly addressed, as its intractability likely motivates Lemma 5. Section 4’s second half is difficult to follow: Theorem 7 references unexplained notation (g, M, Λ), and Theorem 4 uses undefined terms. Excessive restatement of [8] should be reduced. Section 1 uses KL(q||p) inconsistently; the first paragraph assigns q to the true distribution and p to the approximation, contradicting standard notation where q typically denotes the approximation.  

EXPERIMENTS:  
Figure 2’s time comparison excludes the projection cost, despite a vague mention in Section 6.1. This omission undermines the comparison, as the method effectively leverages a variational optimization without cost. Clarification and results including projection time are essential. Experiments on larger models are missing, despite opportunities (e.g., symmetric models with tractable marginals or long-sampling baselines). While KL(θ||ψ) is intractable, exploring it as a best-case scenario (e.g., using junction tree for toy models) would be valuable. The large error gap between mean field (a degenerate reverse KL projection) and reverse KL projection merits discussion.  

The core idea—approximating slow-mixing models via projection to the nearest fast-mixing model—is promising, and recent mixing bound work is applied elegantly. However, experimental comparisons require refinement, and the method’s applicability appears limited to small binary models.
