The success of inference via Gibbs sampling in MRFs (here restricted to two labels, i.e., an Ising model) depends critically on the mixing rate of the underlying Markov chain. The paper proposes the following inference strategy:
1) Project the input model (which may mix slowly) onto the set of models that are provably fast-mixing.
2) Perform inference on this fast-mixing surrogate model.

The set of fast-mixing models is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute values of the Ising edge strengths. “Projection” is defined via divergences between Gibbs distributions and is constrained to preserve the original graph structure. The Euclidean-distance projection is computed by dualizing the problem and using L-BFGS-B. For other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL), a projected gradient algorithm is employed. For the reverse KL divergence, Gibbs sampling (on the fast-mixing model) is required to compute the projection.

The paper presents extensive experiments on small random models, comparing approximate marginals to exact marginals. Baselines include the proposed method (for all listed divergences), loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The evaluation considers both accuracy and accuracy versus runtime. The results indicate that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy and, over a reasonable range of runtimes, also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed variants, reverse KL performs best.

Comments:
- The projected gradient algorithm in Section 5.1 in fact has two nested loops, with L-BFGS-B as the inner loop. Please provide details on the stopping criteria for the inner iterations.
- The horizontal axis in Figure 2 (and the supplement) is unclear. It is labeled “number of samples,” but sampling is used only for the reverse KL divergence. I believe the horizontal axis should represent algorithm runtime. Similarly, please report the runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF as well. This would enable a fair accuracy–runtime comparison across all methods. Please clarify this issue—without it, the experimental results are difficult to interpret—and report absolute running times in seconds.
- Please consider experiments on larger models. One interesting option is to use models from: Boris Flach, “A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function,” to appear in TPAMI (available online), which allow polynomial-time inference.

Minor edits:
- Line 222: replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T.”
- Line 226: Shouldn’t we say “subgradient” rather than “derivative”?

Overall: Interesting paper with convincing empirical results. Practical utility may be limited by runtime (this requires clarification in the rebuttal).
