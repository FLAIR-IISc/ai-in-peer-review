- Paper proposes inference by projecting a given Ising MRF onto the set of fast-mixing models, then performing inference on that projected model.
- Fast-mixing model class is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute Ising edge strengths.
- Projection is formulated using divergences between Gibbs distributions and constrained to preserve the original graph structure.
- Euclidean projection is solved by dualizing and using L-BFGS-B; KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL projections use a projected gradient algorithm.
- Reversed KL projection requires Gibbs sampling, but only on the fast-mixing (projected) model.
- Experiments on small random Ising models compare approximated marginals to true marginals and assess accuracy vs runtime trade-offs.
- Methods compared: proposed divergences, Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted BP (TRW), Mean Field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original model.
- Results: proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and for reasonable runtimes often beat Gibbs on the original model; reversed KL performs most consistently well.
- Reviewer requests the stopping criterion for the inner L-BFGS-B iterations in the projected gradient algorithm (Section 5.1).
- Figure axis labeled “number of samples” is misleading (sampling only used for reversed KL); reviewer believes it should show runtime and asks for absolute running times in seconds and runtimes for LBP/TRW/MF for fair comparison.
- Suggestion to include experiments on larger models, e.g., using Flach’s random-field class with tractable partition functions.
- Minor edits: change “onto the tree” to “onto graph T” (line 222) and change “derivative” to “subgradient” (line 226).
- Overall assessment: interesting paper with convincing empirical results but practical utility may be limited by potentially high runtime, which needs clarification in the rebuttal.
