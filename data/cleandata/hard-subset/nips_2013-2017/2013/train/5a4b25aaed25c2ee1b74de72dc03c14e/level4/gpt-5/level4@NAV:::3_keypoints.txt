- Paper proposes projecting slow-mixing Ising models onto a class of fast-mixing models (bounded spectral norm of absolute edge weights) and performing inference on the projection.
- Projection is defined via divergences between Gibbs distributions and constrained to preserve the graph structure.
- Euclidean projection solved by dualization and L-BFGS-B; KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL use a projected-gradient method.
- Reverse KL projection requires running Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model to compute the projection.
- Experiments on small random models compare proposed methods (all divergences) to loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original model, reporting accuracy and accuracy-vs-runtime.
- Results show the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and within reasonable runtime also beat Gibbs on the original model.
- Among proposed variants, reverse KL performs best consistently.
- Reviewer requests details on stopping criteria for the inner L-BFGS-B iterations in the nested projected-gradient algorithm.
- Reviewer questions the x-axis labeling in Figure 2 (and supplement) labeled “number of samples” and asks to clarify or replace with runtime and to report absolute runtimes in seconds.
- Reviewer asks to report runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF for fair accuracy–runtime comparison.
- Reviewer suggests experiments on larger models (e.g., Flach’s class of random fields with tractable partition function).
- Minor edits: replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T” and consider “subgradient” instead of “derivative.”
- Overall assessment: interesting paper with convincing empirical results, but practical utility may be limited by high runtime and needs clarification in rebuttal.
