This paper proposes an approach to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, which are known to be "slow-mixing". The authors introduce several projections to "fast-mixing" models, where parameters are identified that are as close as possible to the original model but weak enough to satisfy a spectral bound establishing rapid mixing. Experimental results demonstrate some regimes where this approach leads to improved marginal estimates for a given computation time, compared to variational methods (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

The technical content of the paper heavily relies on prior results establishing conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]. However, the idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method appears to be novel. The results offer some nice improvements over a good set of baselines (standard sampling and contemporary variational methods) on "toy tiny Ising models". Nevertheless, I have concerns that these results may not scale to larger models. The experiments involve comparisons of mixing times of samplers on different models, and it is challenging to judge how these will scale with problem size. Furthermore, there is a computationally expensive projection step required to build the fast-mixing model, the cost of which seems not to be accounted for. Additionally, it is unclear whether generalization beyond binary states will be possible, as establishing convergence bounds more generally is far more challenging.

In general, the presentation is clear and reasonably accessible, given the technical content. However, there are some places where reorganization and clarification are needed. The paper refers several times to "standard results" and even states these in the form of a theorem (e.g., Theorem 6) without reference or proof sketch. Lemma 5 is left unproven, and the reference provided only proves the special case of zero-field. The authors should discuss the properties of the dependency matrix R, including its tractability, which does not appear to be feasible due to the maximization. This is presumably why Lemma 5 is invoked. The second half of Section 4 is difficult to follow, with references to g, M, and Lambda before they are introduced. The statement of Theorem 4 includes notation that is not well-explained. If this optimization is to be discussed, more explanation is needed. Perhaps less time could be spent restating results from [8] that are not really used. In Section 1, the notation for KL(q||p) is used for both directions of KL divergence, which should be corrected. The notation needs to be shifted for one direction, and the use of q for the true distribution and p for the tractable approximation is opposite to the convention in most related literature.

The experimental results appear to have some limitations. The time comparison in Figure 2 does not include the computation required for projection, which is only briefly mentioned in Section 6.1. This should be clarified, and if the projection time is not included, the results should be revised to account for it. As it stands, the proposed method seems to get the benefit of a sophisticated variational optimization without penalty, which makes the improvement over standard Gibbs less convincing. It would be beneficial to see experiments on larger models, as Gibbs mixing times often depend on problem size. Options for running experiments in regimes where junction tree is intractable include using a model where symmetries allow the true marginals to be computed or taking the output of a very long sampling run as truth. Although KL(\theta||\psi) is intractable in general, it would be interesting to explore this as a potential "best case" for how sampling in an approximate model would perform. Junction tree could be used for the toy models in the submission. The large difference between mean field error and the error from reverse KL projection, given that mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection, deserves discussion.

The idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is elegant, and recent work on mixing bounds is leveraged effectively. However, there are some concerns about experimental comparisons, and the limited range of models to which this approach is potentially applicable.
