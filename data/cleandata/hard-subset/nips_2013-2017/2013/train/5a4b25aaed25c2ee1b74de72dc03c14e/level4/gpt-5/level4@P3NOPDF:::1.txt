Summary
The paper proposes approximating slow-mixing pairwise Ising models by projecting their parameters onto the closest model that satisfies a spectral mixing condition guaranteeing rapid mixing for Gibbs sampling. Several projection schemes are considered, all aiming to minimally perturb the original parameters while enforcing a bound on a dependency matrix that implies fast mixing. Empirically, on small Ising models with strong couplings, the resulting samplers produce more accurate marginals within a fixed compute budget than mean field, variants of belief propagation, and Gibbs sampling on the original model.

Strengths
- Conceptually elegant idea: use mixing-time bounds to define a feasible set of “fast-mixing” models and project the original model into this set, then sample there. I have not seen this projection-to-fast-mixing strategy used explicitly as an approximate inference method.
- Solid theoretical grounding: the method leverages established sufficient conditions for rapid mixing [4,8], and the projection goal is well motivated.
- On small, strongly coupled models, the empirical results show meaningful accuracy improvements over standard baselines for a fixed sampling time.

Concerns and Limitations
- Scalability and computational cost: The projection step appears computationally heavy and, as far as I can tell, is not included in the time budgets of the experimental comparisons. If the projection dominates the end-to-end runtime, the comparison against Gibbs on the original model and variational methods is less persuasive.
- Unclear behavior as model size grows: Since Gibbs mixing times can depend strongly on system size and geometry, results limited to very small Ising models do not establish practical effectiveness on larger instances.
- Generalization beyond binary variables: Extending the approach to multi-valued or higher-order models seems nontrivial, as analogous convergence bounds are much harder to obtain; the paper does not make clear whether such extensions are feasible.
- Reliance on prior results without sufficient exposition: Some key claims are referred to as “standard” and stated as theorems without proof, reference, or adequate context.

Clarity and Presentation
Overall the paper is readable, but several sections would benefit from reorganization and precision:
- Referenced “standard results.” For example, Theorem 6 is stated without an explicit reference or proof sketch, and Lemma 5 is left unproven with a citation that, to my understanding, only treats the zero-field case. Please either supply references that match your generality or provide proof sketches and conditions under which your statements hold.
- Dependency matrix R. The properties and computational tractability of R should be discussed clearly. As written, it appears to involve a maximization that may be intractable; if so, state this and explain how Lemma 5 is used to circumvent it, including any approximations or bounding strategies and their computational cost.
- Section 4 exposition. The second half is difficult to follow. Variables g, M, and Λ are referenced before being introduced, and the statement of Theorem 4 uses notation that is not fully explained. If the optimization is central, please (i) introduce all quantities before use; (ii) state the objective, constraints, and norms unambiguously; and (iii) explain the algorithmic steps and complexity rather than restating results from [8] that you do not directly use.
- Notation for KL divergences and model roles. In Section 1, KL(q||p) is used for both directions. Also, the text initially uses q for the true distribution and p for the approximation, which is opposite to common practice in this literature. Please standardize the notation and fix the KL direction where needed.

Experiments
- Accounting for projection time. Figure 2 appears to compare only sampling times, excluding the cost to compute the projection. Section 6.1 seems to acknowledge this but is ambiguous. Please:
  - Report end-to-end wall-clock times that include projection, burn-in, and sampling.
  - If the projection is amortized (e.g., reused across multiple runs), state this and show both amortized and non-amortized results.
  - Provide complexity and practical runtimes of the projection step as a function of graph size and density.
- Larger models and scaling. To address concerns about scalability:
  - Include experiments on larger grids or random graphs where junction tree is intractable.
  - Use settings where ground truth marginals are available via symmetry, or use very long, well-tempered sampling runs as a reference.
  - Report how accuracy and total runtime scale with the number of nodes and edge strength.
- Best-case projection objective. Although KL(θ||ψ) between model distributions is generally intractable, on your toy models junction tree is feasible. Exploring this objective would help bound the potential of “sampling in an approximate model,” and clarify where your fast-mixing projection sits relative to an ideal reverse-KL projection.
- Mean field vs. reverse KL projection. Since mean field is a degenerate case of reverse-KL projection, the large empirical gap between mean field and your reverse-KL-based projection deserves discussion. Is the gain primarily due to permitting weak pairwise terms, to the fast-mixing constraint, or to optimization quality?
- Reporting and fairness. Clearly specify:
  - The computational budgets (iterations, samples, wall-clock) allotted to each method.
  - Initialization, burn-in, and thinning protocols.
  - Variance across random seeds and confidence intervals on error metrics.

Originality and Significance
The projection-to-fast-mixing idea is, to my knowledge, novel and interesting. If the approach can be made computationally practical and shown to retain benefits on larger models, it could be a useful addition to the toolbox for inference in strongly coupled systems. At present, the scope seems largely limited to binary pairwise models and small problem sizes.

Recommendations for Improvement
- Provide rigorous references or proofs for the “standard” theorems and lemmas you rely on, and clarify the precise conditions under which they hold.
- Explain the dependency matrix R, its computation or approximation, and the role of Lemma 5 in making the optimization tractable.
- Rework Section 4 to introduce all notation before use and to present the optimization problems clearly with algorithmic details and complexity.
- Revise notation in Section 1 to fix the KL direction and align symbol roles with common conventions.
- Include end-to-end timing with projection cost and add experiments on larger models, along with a discussion of scaling.
- Explore the reverse-KL objective on the small models (where tractable) and analyze why the proposed method outperforms mean field despite both being reverse-KL-flavored.

Overall Assessment
A thoughtful and well-motivated paper that leverages mixing-time theory in a novel way. The idea has merit and the initial results are encouraging, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by clearer exposition of the theory, full accounting of computational costs, and experiments that probe scalability and fairness more thoroughly.
