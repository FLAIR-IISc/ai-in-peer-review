This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models by redefining the notion of a "tractable" model. Instead of relying on structural properties like low treewidth, the authors define tractability based on dynamic properties, specifically whether the model's parameters guarantee that Gibbs sampling will be "fast mixing." The core idea is to take an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing Ising model and project its parameters onto the set of parameters that are guaranteed to be fast mixing. The condition for fast mixing is derived from prior work, which shows that if the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths is less than one, the mixing time is bounded.

The paper explores this projection under four different divergences:
1.  **Euclidean Distance:** The authors derive an efficient dual algorithm to find the closest fast-mixing parameter set in the L2 norm, which relies on iteratively thresholding a singular value decomposition.
2.  **"Zero-avoiding" KL-divergence (KL(q||p)):** This is presented as a theoretically sound but practically intractable option, as it requires computing expectations with respect to the original, slow-mixing distribution `q`.
3.  **Piecewise KL-divergence:** A novel and tractable approximation to the KL-divergence, computed over a collection of low-treewidth subgraphs of the original model.
4.  **"Zero-forcing" KL-divergence (KL(p||q)):** This divergence is more practical as it only requires expectations with respect to the projected, fast-mixing distribution `p`. The authors propose minimizing it using a stochastic projected gradient descent method.

Experiments on grid and random graphs compare these projection methods against standard approximate inference algorithms (Loopy BP, TRW, Mean Field) and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters. The results suggest that sampling from the projected parameters can yield more accurate marginals than both the variational methods and Gibbs on the original parameters when the sampling budget is limited.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The central contribution—framing approximate inference as a projection onto a dynamically tractable (fast-mixing) set rather than a structurally tractable one—is a creative and thought-provoking idea. It opens up a new perspective on approximation that could inspire further research connecting the MCMC and variational inference literature.
2.  **Technical Contribution:** The paper provides a solid technical contribution in Theorem 7, which details a dual method for performing the Euclidean projection while preserving the graph's sparsity pattern.
3.  **Comprehensive Approach:** The exploration of the projection problem under several different divergences, highlighting their theoretical properties and practical trade-offs, is thorough and well-motivated.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper presents a very promising idea, but its execution in terms of clarity and experimental validation could be substantially improved.

1.  **Clarity of the Algorithm:** The overall procedure, especially for the stochastic KL-divergence minimization, is difficult to follow. The paper would benefit greatly from an algorithm box that clearly outlines the steps. For instance, is the projection a one-time pre-processing step after which a standard Gibbs sampler is run? Or are the projection steps (gradient updates) and sampling steps interleaved? The text mentions an "Ergodic Mirror Descent" framework, but the practical implementation details and how the "pool of samples" is managed remain vague.

2.  **Clarity of the Projection Formulation:** In Section 4, the purpose of the constraint `ZijBij = 0` could be explained more explicitly. As Z is defined as the indicator of non-edges, this constraint ensures that the projection does not introduce new edges, preserving the original graph structure (or a subgraph thereof). Stating this directly would improve readability.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are the weakest part of the paper and are not sufficient to convincingly demonstrate the utility of the proposed method.
    *   **Accounting for Projection Cost:** The primary weakness is that the experiments do not account for the computational cost of performing the projection. The plots showing accuracy versus the number of Gibbs samples are misleading because they ignore the potentially significant time spent in the stochastic gradient descent procedure to find the projected parameters. A fair comparison, particularly against Gibbs on the original parameters, must be based on total wall-clock time, including both projection and sampling. It is plausible that the time spent on projection could have been used by the naive Gibbs sampler to run for many more iterations and achieve better accuracy.
    *   **Missing Error Bars:** The plots lack error bars, which makes it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the results. Given that the results are averaged over 50 random trials, this information is essential for a rigorous evaluation.
    *   **Missing Baselines:** The comparison focuses on variational methods and single-site Gibbs. However, a major motivation for this work is improving mixing time. Therefore, it would be much more compelling to compare against other MCMC techniques designed for this purpose, such as **block Gibbs sampling** or, in the case of attractive interactions, the **Swendsen-Wang algorithm**. These are standard and highly relevant baselines that are conspicuously absent.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper introduces a highly original and conceptually interesting idea that bridges MCMC and variational inference by defining tractability in terms of mixing time. The technical derivations for the Euclidean projection are sound.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The experimental evaluation is critically flawed because it fails to account for the computational overhead of the projection, making the primary claims about efficiency and accuracy unsubstantiated. Key MCMC baselines are missing, and the overall algorithm lacks clarity.

Overall, this paper contains a novel and valuable idea. However, the current manuscript requires significant revisions, particularly in the experimental section, to provide convincing evidence for its practical utility. The lack of a fair time-based comparison and the absence of stronger MCMC baselines are major shortcomings that prevent a confident recommendation for acceptance at this time.
