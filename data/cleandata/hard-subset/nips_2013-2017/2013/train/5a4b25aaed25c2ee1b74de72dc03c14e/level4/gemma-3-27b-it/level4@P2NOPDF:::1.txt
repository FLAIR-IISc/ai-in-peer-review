This paper investigates improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, which are known to exhibit slow mixing. The authors propose a novel approach: projecting the original model onto a “fast-mixing” model by identifying parameters as close as possible to the original while ensuring rapid mixing via a spectral bound. Through experiments on small Ising models, they demonstrate improved marginal estimates compared to standard Gibbs sampling and contemporary variational methods (mean field and belief propagation) for a given computational cost.

The technical foundation relies heavily on established results concerning rapid mixing conditions [4,8]. However, the core idea of projecting onto a nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference technique appears to be novel. While the experimental results on “toy” Ising models are promising, showing improvements over strong baselines, a significant concern is the potential lack of scalability to larger, more complex models. The experiments focus on comparing mixing times, making it difficult to extrapolate performance to larger problem instances. Moreover, the computationally expensive projection step required to construct the fast-mixing model does not appear to be fully accounted for in the reported timings. A further limitation is the potential difficulty of generalizing the method beyond binary states, as establishing convergence bounds becomes considerably more challenging in more general settings.

**Clarity & Presentation:**

The paper is generally well-written and reasonably accessible, considering the technical depth. However, several areas could benefit from reorganization and clarification. Specifically:

*   The paper frequently refers to “standard results” and presents them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without providing references or even brief proof sketches. Lemma 5 is stated without proof, despite the referenced work only proving it for a specific zero-field case.
*   The properties of the dependency matrix *R* should be discussed in more detail. The maximization involved in its calculation suggests it may not be tractable; this limitation should be explicitly acknowledged. (This likely motivates the invocation of Lemma 5).
*   The second half of Section 4 is difficult to follow. Prior to Theorem 7, the variables *g*, *M*, and *Λ* are referenced before being properly introduced. Furthermore, the statement of Theorem 4 utilizes notation that lacks sufficient explanation. Greater clarity is needed if this optimization process is central to the contribution. (Consider streamlining the restatement of results from [8], which appear less critical to the core argument.)
*   In Section 1, the notation for KL divergence, KL(*q*||*p*), is used inconsistently, representing both directions. The initial paragraph reverses the typical convention in related literature, assigning *q* to the true distribution and *p* to the tractable approximation.

**Experiments & Evaluation:**

The time comparison presented in Figure 2 does not seem to include the computational cost of the projection step. While Section 6.1 contains an ambiguous statement regarding this point, it lacks clarity. The authors should clarify whether projection time is included and, if not, present results incorporating it. Currently, the proposed methods appear to benefit from the results of a sophisticated variational optimization without incurring the associated computational cost, which diminishes the significance of the observed improvements over standard Gibbs sampling.

The absence of experiments on larger models is disappointing, particularly given the known dependence of Gibbs mixing times on problem size. Opportunities exist for evaluating the approach in regimes where junction tree inference is intractable. This could include using models with symmetries that allow for computation of true marginals or employing the output of long sampling runs as a proxy for the ground truth.

While acknowledging the intractability of KL(*θ*||*ψ*) in general, exploring it as a “best-case” performance metric for sampling in the approximate model would be insightful. Junction tree inference could be utilized for the toy models used in the submission to provide a benchmark.

The paper correctly identifies mean field as a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection. However, the significant discrepancy between mean field error and the error achieved through reverse KL projection warrants further discussion.

In summary, the idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the nearest fast-mixing model is promising and leverages recent advances in mixing bound theory effectively. However, concerns remain regarding the experimental evaluations and the potential limitations in the applicability of this approach to larger and more general models. Further investigation into scalability and computational cost is necessary.
