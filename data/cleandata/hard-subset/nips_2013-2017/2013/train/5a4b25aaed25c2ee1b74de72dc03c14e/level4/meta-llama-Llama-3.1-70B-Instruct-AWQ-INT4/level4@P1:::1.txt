This paper proposes an innovative approach to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, which are known to be "slow-mixing". The authors introduce a method to project the original model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set, ensuring that the projected parameters are as close as possible to the original model while satisfying a spectral bound that guarantees rapid mixing. This approach is novel and leverages recent results on mixing bounds.

The technical content is sound and well-grounded in prior work, particularly in the area of rapid mixing conditions [4,8]. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on small Ising models, showing improved marginal estimates for a given computation time compared to variational methods (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

However, there are concerns about the scalability of this approach to larger models. The experiments involve comparisons of mixing times of samplers on different models, which can be challenging to interpret and may not generalize well to larger problem sizes. Moreover, the computationally expensive projection step required to build the fast-mixing model is not accounted for in the time comparisons, which may impact the overall efficiency of the approach.

Additionally, it is unclear whether this approach can be generalized beyond binary states, as establishing convergence bounds for more complex models is a more significant challenge. The authors should discuss these limitations and potential avenues for future research.

Regarding clarity, the presentation is generally clear and accessible, but there are areas that require improvement. The paper often refers to "standard results" without providing references or proof sketches, which can make it difficult for non-experts to follow. For instance, Theorem 6 is stated without proof, and Lemma 5 is left unproven, with the reference only proving a special case of zero-field. The properties of the dependency matrix R, such as its tractability, should be explicitly stated.

The second half of Section 4 is particularly challenging to follow, with unclear notation and a lack of explanation for the optimization procedure. The authors should consider reorganizing this section and providing more context for the optimization.

In Section 1, the notation for KL divergence is inconsistent, using q for the true distribution and p for the tractable approximation, which is opposite to the standard notation in the literature.

The experimental evaluation has some limitations. The time comparison in Figure 2 does not include the computation required for projection, which may impact the overall efficiency of the approach. The authors should clarify this point and provide results with projection time included. It would also be beneficial to include experiments on larger models, using alternative methods such as junction tree or long sampling runs as a baseline.

The comparison between mean field and reverse KL projection is interesting, but the significant difference in error between the two methods deserves more discussion. Additionally, exploring the "zero-avoiding" KL divergence as a potential "best case" for sampling in an approximate model would be valuable, even if it is intractable in general.

In conclusion, the idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is innovative and elegant. However, there are concerns about the scalability and applicability of this approach, and the experimental comparisons could be improved. With some revisions to address these concerns, this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to improving Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models
* Sound technical content, well-grounded in prior work
* Effective experiments demonstrating improved marginal estimates

Weaknesses:

* Concerns about scalability to larger models
* Computationally expensive projection step not accounted for in time comparisons
* Unclear generalizability beyond binary states
* Some areas of the presentation require improvement
* Experimental evaluation has limitations, including lack of larger models and inconsistent notation
