The success of Gibbs sampling in Markov Random Fields (MRFs), particularly in the context of the Ising model with two labels, heavily relies on the mixing rate of the underlying Monte Carlo Markov chain. The authors propose an approach to inference that involves two primary steps:

1) Projecting the input model, which may not exhibit fast mixing, onto the set of models that do mix rapidly.
2) Performing inference on the resulting fast-mixing model.

The space of fast-mixing models is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute values of Ising edge strengths. The "projection" is defined in terms of divergences of the Gibbs distribution, with the constraint of preserving the graph structure. For Euclidean distance, the authors employ a dualization of the initial task and utilize the LBFGS-B algorithm for projection. In contrast, for other divergences (including KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences), a projected gradient algorithm is used. Notably, the reversed KL divergence requires Gibbs sampling on a fast-mixing model to compute the projection.

The authors present extensive experiments on small random models, comparing the approximated marginals with the true marginals. The methods under evaluation include the proposed approach (with all aforementioned divergences) and loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The experiments not only assess accuracy but also provide a runtime-vs-accuracy evaluation. The results demonstrate that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in terms of accuracy, and for a reasonable range of runtimes, also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed methods, the one employing reversed KL divergence consistently achieves the best performance.

Comments:

The projected gradient algorithm described in Section 5.1 actually comprises two nested loops, with the inner loop being LBFGS-B. It would be beneficial to provide details on the stopping criteria for the inner iterations.

The horizontal axis in the plots in Figure 2 (and the supplement) is unclear, as it is titled "number of samples" despite sampling being used only for reversed KL divergence. It is likely that the horizontal axis should represent the runtime of the algorithm. Furthermore, it would be valuable to report the runtime of LBP, TRW, and MF to ensure a fair accuracy-runtime comparison of all tested algorithms. Please clarify this issue, as it is challenging to interpret the experiments without this information. Providing absolute running times in seconds would also be helpful.

Consideration should be given to experimental comparisons with larger models. A potential option is to utilize models from the paper "A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function" by Boris Flach, which allow for polynomial-time inference.

On page 222, it would be more accurate to replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T." On page 226, it appears that "subgradient" would be a more appropriate term than "derivative."

Overall, the paper presents interesting results and convincing empirical evidence. However, the practical utility of the proposed approach may be limited due to its high runtime, which requires further clarification in the rebuttal.
