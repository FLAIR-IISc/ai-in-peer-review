The authors present a method to accelerate Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting model parameters onto a space guaranteeing fast mixing, satisfying conditions like the Dobrushin criterion. This projection is formulated as a constrained optimization problem, explored under various divergence measures.

This work addresses an interesting and relatively under-explored area – combining stochastic and deterministic inference techniques. The core idea of augmenting parameters to improve mixing time is intuitively appealing. However, the presentation of the dual formulation for the projection problem lacks clarity. Specifically, the role of the *z<sub>ij</sub>d<sub>ij</sub>* = 0 constraints in ensuring the projected parameterization maintains the original parameter space is not fully explained; it’s unclear whether this constraint might inadvertently restrict the space. Furthermore, the overall procedure remains somewhat ambiguous. An algorithm box detailing whether the projected gradient descent is completed *before* Gibbs sampling, or if the two are interleaved, would significantly improve comprehension. A discussion of the convergence guarantees of the proposed projected gradient descent strategy is also missing.

The experimental evaluation, while present, is not entirely convincing. The choice of running the Gibbs sampler for 30,000 iterations in the initial experiments is unclear. Given the comparison with deterministic methods, evaluating performance based on computational *time* would provide a fairer assessment. The figures lack error bars, hindering the assessment of statistical significance. The second set of results demonstrates faster mixing with projected Gibbs samplers compared to the original, but crucially omits the computational cost of the projection itself. For instance, if the projection takes a significant amount of time, a naive Gibbs sampler generating samples concurrently might prove more efficient. A comparison to more sophisticated sampling techniques like blocked Gibbs sampling or Gogate's "Lifted Gibbs Sampler" is also absent, potentially limiting the scope of the evaluation.

**Strengths:**

*   The problem addressed – accelerating MCMC via parameter projection – is a relevant and interesting one.
*   The approach of leveraging fast-mixing conditions to improve sampling efficiency is novel.
*   The exploration of different divergence measures for the projection provides a breadth to the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The description of the dual formulation of the projection problem lacks clarity.
*   The experimental setup could be improved by including time-based comparisons and error bars.
*   The analysis omits the computational cost of the projection itself.
*   The comparison to existing advanced sampling methods is lacking.
*   Convergence guarantees for the proposed projected gradient descent are not addressed.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel approach to improving MCMC performance.
*   The theoretical foundations are reasonably well-established, building on existing work in mixing times and optimization.
*   The experimental results, even with limitations, suggest a potential benefit from the proposed method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of clarity in the presentation of the mathematical formulation may hinder reproducibility and understanding.
*   The incomplete experimental evaluation fails to convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the method.
*   The absence of a detailed analysis of computational costs raises concerns about real-world applicability.

**Overall assessment:**

This paper presents a promising idea, but requires further development and a more comprehensive evaluation to justify acceptance. The theoretical contributions are interesting, but the presentation needs refinement. The experiments are a good starting point, but lack sufficient detail and comparison to alternative methods. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit, addressing the points raised above.
