- Proposes projecting pairwise Ising models onto nearest "fast-mixing" models to improve Gibbs sampling mixing rates using a spectral bound.  
- Several projection methods are developed to find parameters close to the original model but satisfying rapid-mixing conditions.  
- Experiments on small Ising models show improved marginal estimates within a fixed computation time versus variational methods and original Gibbs sampling in some regimes.  
- Builds heavily on prior rapid-mixing results [4,8], but the projection-as-approximate-inference idea appears novel.  
- Technical improvements are demonstrated on toy problems, but scalability to larger models is unclear.  
- The computational cost of the projection step is not clearly included in time comparisons and may be substantial.  
- Generalization beyond binary-state models is doubtful because convergence bounds are harder to establish.  
- Some "standard results" and lemmas are stated without references or proofs (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5).  
- Lemma 5 is cited inconsistently with existing work (reference proves only zero-field case).  
- The dependency matrix R and the tractability of its computation (involving a maximization) are not sufficiently discussed.  
- Section 4’s latter half is hard to follow; key symbols (g, M, Λ) are referenced before being defined and Theorem 7’s notation is underexplained.  
- KL divergence notation is inconsistent (directions and symbols q/p are swapped relative to common convention).  
- Figure 2’s time comparison likely omits projection time; paper statements are ambiguous and need clarification or corrected plots including projection cost.  
- No experiments on larger or more challenging models; suggested alternatives include symmetric models with tractable exact marginals or using long sampling runs as ground truth.  
- Exploring reverse KL (KL(θ||ψ)) as a “best-case” for approximate-model sampling and using junction tree on toy models would add value.  
- Mean field is identified as reverse-KL degenerate case, but the significant empirical difference between mean field and reverse-KL projections needs discussion.  
- Overall: promising and technically grounded idea, but experimental evaluation, scalability, computational accounting, and clarity issues limit the paper’s current impact.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel approach, technically sound basis, empirical improvements in some regimes.  
- Cons against acceptance: unclear scalability, unaccounted projection cost, questionable generality beyond binary states, and clarity problems.
