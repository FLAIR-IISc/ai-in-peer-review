This work tackles the issue of "slow-mixing" in Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions. The proposed method involves projecting the model parameters onto the closest "fast-mixing" model, defined as one whose parameters are minimally distant from the original set while satisfying a spectral bound that ensures rapid mixing. Experiments show that for certain regimes, this approach yields improved marginal estimates for a given computational budget compared to both variational methods (mean field, belief propagation) and standard Gibbs sampling on the original model.

The technical foundation heavily leverages prior results on conditions for rapid mixing [4, 8]. However, the central idea of using projection onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as a form of approximate inference appears to be a novel contribution.

On small-scale "toy" Ising models, the method shows compelling improvements over a solid set of baselines, including standard sampling and modern variational techniques. The primary concern, however, is whether these results can be extrapolated to larger-scale problems. The experiments focus on comparing mixing times of samplers on different models, and it is difficult to assess how these comparisons will scale with the size of the problem. Additionally, the approach requires a projection step to construct the fast-mixing model that appears computationally expensive, yet its cost seems to be excluded from the performance analysis. Lastly, the generalization of this method beyond binary states is unclear, as establishing the required convergence bounds for more general models is a far more challenging task.

**CLARITY:**

The paper is generally well-written and clear, considering its technical nature. However, several areas require reorganization and clarification:

*   The manuscript refers to "standard results" and presents them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without providing citations or proof sketches. Lemma 5 is left unproven, with the provided reference only covering the special case of a zero-field model.
*   A discussion of the properties of the dependency matrix R is needed. Its computation appears intractable due to the embedded maximization; please clarify if this is correct, as this would provide the motivation for invoking Lemma 5.
*   The second half of Section 4 is extremely difficult to follow. The text references `g`, `M`, and `Lambda` before they are introduced. Subsequently, the statement of Theorem 4 contains unexplained notation. If this optimization is to be discussed, it needs a more detailed explanation. Perhaps less space could be devoted to restating results from [8] that are not directly used in the paper.
*   In Section 1, the notation KL(q||p) is used for both directions of the KL divergence when the arguments should be swapped in one instance. The convention in the first paragraph, using `q` for the true distribution and `p` for the tractable approximation, is also opposite to the standard in most related literature.

**EXPERIMENTS:**

*   The timing comparisons in Figure 2 seem to omit the computation time required for the projection step. An ambiguous statement in Section 6.1 alludes to this but is unclear. This must be clarified, and if the time is indeed excluded, the results should be re-presented with the projection cost included. In its current form, the proposed method unfairly benefits from a costly pre-computation, which makes the improvement over standard Gibbs sampling less convincing.
*   It is disappointing that experiments on larger models were not included, especially since Gibbs mixing times are highly dependent on problem size. Where the junction tree algorithm is intractable, other options for establishing a ground truth exist, such as using models with symmetries that allow for exact marginal computation or using the output of a very long sampling run.
*   While KL(θ||ψ) is generally intractable, it would be insightful to see it computed here for the toy models (using junction tree). This could serve as a "best-case" benchmark for the performance of sampling in an approximate model.
*   The paper correctly points out that mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection. Given this, the large performance difference observed between the mean field error and the error from the reverse KL projection warrants discussion.

The concept of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting them to the nearest fast-mixing model is innovative, and it leverages recent work on mixing bounds in an elegant manner. Nevertheless, there are significant concerns about the experimental comparisons and the potentially limited range of models to which this approach can be applied.
