- Paper proposes projecting Ising-model parameters onto a “fast-mixing” subset (spectral-norm constraint) to speed Gibbs sampling and studies several projection divergences.  
- Empirical results show projected-parameter sampling can give more accurate marginals within a fixed time than variational baselines and original-model Gibbs on small instances.  
- The idea of explicit projection to the nearest rapidly-mixing model appears novel and builds on established mixing-time criteria.  
- Main reservations: scalability and scope — extrapolation to larger systems is unclear because comparisons mix differently parameterized models.  
- The projection step seems computationally heavy and appears excluded from wall-clock comparisons, biasing results in favor of projected methods.  
- Extending the approach beyond binary variables is nontrivial because analogous mixing bounds are hard to obtain.  
- Clarity issues: several “standard results” are stated without citation or proof sketches (e.g., Theorem 6 and Lemma 5).  
- Dependence matrix R: its properties and intractable maximization in the definition need discussion to motivate Lemma 5.  
- Sec. 4 is hard to parse: variables (g, M, Λ) and notation are used before definition and the optimization/theorem lack sufficient intuition and explanation.  
- Notation inconsistency in Sec. 1: KL(q||p) is used for both directions and the choice of q/p flips common conventions; notation should be clarified.  
- Experiments likely omit projection time from wall-clock results; authors should clarify and include projection cost in comparisons.  
- Need experiments on larger systems or alternative ways to get ground-truth marginals (symmetries or long runs) to assess scaling behavior.  
- Suggest exploring “best-case” projection using junction-tree on toy instances to contextualize approximate-projection performance.  
- The large error gap between mean-field and reverse-KL projection warrants analysis to explain the difference.  
- Overall: the approach is appealing and leverages recent theory, but experimental comparisons, computational accounting, and scope limitations need to be addressed.
