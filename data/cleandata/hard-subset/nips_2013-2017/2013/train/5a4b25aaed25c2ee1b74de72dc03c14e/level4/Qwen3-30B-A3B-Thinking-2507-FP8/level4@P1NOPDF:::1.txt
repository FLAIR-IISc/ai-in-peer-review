

The paper proposes a novel approach to accelerate Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions by projecting the original model onto the nearest "fast-mixing" model—defined as one satisfying a spectral bound for rapid mixing. The core idea identifies parameters as close as possible to the original model while ensuring weak interactions that guarantee rapid mixing. Experiments demonstrate improved marginal estimates within fixed computation time compared to mean-field, belief propagation, and standard Gibbs sampling on small toy Ising models. While the technical foundation relies on established mixing bounds [4,8], the specific strategy of using such projections as an approximate inference method appears original.

The primary concern lies in scalability. The results, confined to tiny models, offer promising improvements over strong baselines but provide little insight into how the method scales. Key issues include: (1) mixing time comparisons across models do not establish scaling behavior with problem size; (2) the computationally intensive projection step is not accounted for in runtime comparisons (Figure 2 omits this cost, making the method’s advantage appear inflated); and (3) generalization beyond binary states seems unlikely, as extending convergence bounds to non-binary cases is significantly more challenging.

**Clarity:** The presentation is generally accessible but requires substantial refinement.  
- Theorem 6 cites "standard results" without proof or reference; Lemma 5 is stated without proof (the cited work [4] proves only the zero-field case).  
- The dependency matrix *R* is not tractable due to its maximization formulation (the paper should explicitly state this, as it motivates Lemma 5).  
- Section 4’s second half is confusing: Theorem 7 references *g*, *M*, and *Λ* before definition, and Theorem 4 uses unexplained notation. Excessive restatement of [8] dilutes focus.  
- Section 1 misuses KL divergence notation: *KL(q||p)* is defined with *q* as the true distribution (reversing standard convention where *p* is true and *q* is the approximation).

**Experiments:**  
- Figure 2’s runtime comparisons exclude the projection cost, which is ambiguously addressed in Section 6.1. If omitted, the method effectively uses a variational optimization’s output without penalty, undermining its claimed advantage over standard Gibbs. Results must include projection time.  
- Experiments on larger models are absent despite the critical dependence of mixing times on scale. Alternatives like symmetric models with tractable marginals (computed via junction trees) or long-sampling baselines could validate scalability.  
- The paper notes mean-field is a degenerate reverse KL projection case but does not explain the large error gap between mean-field and reverse KL projections—this deserves analysis.  
- While *KL(θ||ψ)* is intractable, reporting it as a "best-case" benchmark (using junction trees for toy models) would strengthen the comparison.

The core idea—approximating slow-mixing models via nearest fast-mixing projections—is elegant and leverages recent mixing theory effectively. However, the experimental claims are currently overextended due to unaddressed computational costs, lack of scalability evidence, and unresolved technical ambiguities. The method’s applicability appears limited to small binary models, and the omission of projection time in comparisons significantly weakens the empirical justification. The paper would benefit from tighter technical exposition, explicit acknowledgment of computational overhead, and experiments on larger or more complex models to validate broader utility.
