

The authors propose a novel method to accelerate Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the original parameterization onto a new setting satisfying the Dobrushin criterion for fast mixing. They frame this projection as a constrained optimization problem, with constraints ensuring the new parameters remain within the original parameter space, and evaluate the projection under multiple divergence metrics.  

The core idea—augmenting parameters to improve mixing—builds on an underexplored intersection of stochastic and deterministic inference, making this a potentially significant contribution. However, the technical exposition requires substantial clarification. Specifically, the dual formulation of the projection problem is unclear: the constraints $z_{ij}^* d_{ij} = 0$ are claimed to preserve the parameter space, but it is not evident why they cannot inadvertently restrict the space. Additionally, the algorithmic procedure is ambiguous. The authors must specify whether projected gradient updates are completed *before* sampling or interleaved *during* sampling. A pseudocode box would greatly enhance clarity. Finally, the convergence guarantees of the projected gradient descent strategy are not established.  

The experimental evaluation also requires strengthening. In the first experiment, the Gibbs sampler runs for 30,000 iterations, but a time-based comparison against deterministic methods would be more appropriate for a sampling-vs-deterministic comparison. The absence of error bars on all results prevents assessment of statistical significance. In the second experiment, while projections demonstrably accelerate mixing compared to the standard Gibbs sampler, the computational cost of the projection step is unaccounted for. For instance, if projection requires 1 minute of computation, and the standard Gibbs sampler generates 10,000 samples in that time, the net benefit becomes questionable. Crucially, the authors omit comparisons to established baselines: blocked Gibbs samplers and Gogate’s "Lifted Gibbs Sampler" represent standard approaches for improving mixing in graphical models and should be included for context.  

The method’s utility hinges on resolving these gaps. The projection concept is promising, but the current presentation lacks the rigor needed to convince readers of its practical value. Key questions remain:  
1. **Parameter space preservation**: Do the constraints $z_{ij}^* d_{ij} = 0$ truly enforce equivalence to the original space, or permit reduction?  
2. **Algorithmic workflow**: Are gradient updates batched or interleaved with sampling?  
3. **Cost-benefit analysis**: How does the projection overhead compare to the sampling speedup?  
4. **Baselines**: Why exclude established techniques like blocked Gibbs or lifted sampling?  

The experiments, as presented, fail to establish whether the method offers a net improvement in *efficiency* (time per effective sample), not merely mixing rate. For example, the first experiment’s iteration count obscures the time-to-solution trade-off, and the second experiment’s omission of projection cost renders the mixing-rate comparison misleading. The authors should reframe results in terms of *time* (e.g., "samples per second" or "time to reach convergence") rather than iteration counts.  

Overall, the paper addresses an important challenge in MCMC inference with a creative approach. Yet, the technical development lacks precision, and the empirical validation is incomplete. To elevate this work, the authors must:  
- Clarify the dual problem and parameter space constraints in the main text.  
- Provide a step-by-step algorithmic description.  
- Include convergence analysis for the optimization.  
- Redesign experiments to report time-based metrics, error bars, and projection cost.  
- Compare against blocked Gibbs and lifted sampling as standard baselines.  

Without these revisions, the claim that the method "improves mixing" remains unconvincing in a practical context where computational overhead is critical. The idea holds promise, but the current manuscript does not sufficiently demonstrate its advantages over existing techniques or justify its added complexity. The contribution would be significantly strengthened with a more rigorous theoretical foundation and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation focused on efficiency rather than merely mixing speed.
