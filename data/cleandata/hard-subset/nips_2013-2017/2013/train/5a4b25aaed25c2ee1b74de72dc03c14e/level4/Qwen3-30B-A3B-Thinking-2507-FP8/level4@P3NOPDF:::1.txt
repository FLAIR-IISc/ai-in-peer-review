

The paper proposes a novel approach to accelerate Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions by projecting the original model onto the nearest "fast-mixing" model satisfying spectral mixing conditions. The core idea—identifying parameters close to the original model that ensure rapid mixing—is theoretically grounded in prior mixing bounds [4,8] and represents a promising direction for approximate inference. Experiments demonstrate measurable improvements in marginal estimation accuracy over standard Gibbs sampling and variational baselines (mean field, belief propagation) on small-scale Ising models within a fixed computational budget. The technical contribution is novel in framing projection as an approximate inference method, distinct from prior work.

However, significant concerns regarding scalability and experimental rigor temper the enthusiasm for this approach. The primary limitation is the lack of evidence that the method generalizes beyond trivially small models. The experiments are confined to "toy" models with 5–15 variables, where mixing times are easily computed but unlikely to reflect real-world behavior. Crucially, the computational cost of the projection step (which solves a non-convex optimization problem) is omitted from all runtime comparisons (Figure 2, Section 6.1). This omission is problematic: the method effectively leverages the output of a sophisticated variational optimization without accounting for its cost, making the claimed speedups over standard Gibbs sampling appear inflated. The authors must clarify whether projection time is included and, if not, provide results with it added. Without this, the comparison is misleading.

Theoretical clarity also requires substantial improvement. The paper frequently cites "standard results" as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without proof sketches or references, and Lemma 5 (unproven) cites a reference that only covers the zero-field case. This undermines reproducibility. The dependency matrix $R$ is central to the analysis but its intractability due to the embedded maximization is not explicitly acknowledged, despite Lemma 5 being invoked to circumvent this. Section 4 is particularly opaque: Theorem 7 references $g$, $M$, and $\Lambda$ before their definitions, and Theorem 4 uses unexplained notation. The authors should streamline the presentation by reducing restatements of [8] and prioritizing novel contributions.

Notational inconsistencies further weaken the argument. Section 1 uses $KL(q \| p)$ with $q$ as the true distribution and $p$ as the approximation—contrary to near-universal convention (where $q$ typically approximates $p$). This confusion risks misinterpretation of the proposed method's relationship to variational inference. Additionally, while the paper notes mean field is a degenerate case of reverse KL projection, it fails to explain the substantial error gap between mean field and reverse KL projections. This discrepancy warrants explicit discussion.

The absence of experiments on larger models is particularly concerning. Gibbs sampling performance is highly sensitive to problem size, yet no evidence is provided for scalability. The authors could have explored: (1) symmetric models where exact marginals are computable via symmetry, or (2) long-running Gibbs chains as a proxy for ground truth in larger settings. Without such experiments, the practical relevance of the method remains speculative. Moreover, the paper does not address whether the approach extends beyond binary states. Establishing mixing bounds for non-binary models is significantly more challenging, and the authors offer no indication of feasibility.

In summary, the core idea—projecting to a fast-mixing model—is elegant and well-motivated. The theoretical framing leverages recent advances in mixing bounds effectively. However, the experimental evaluation is incomplete and potentially misleading due to unaccounted projection costs and a severely limited model scope. The theoretical presentation requires significant clarification to meet NIPS standards. While the small-scale results are promising, the lack of evidence for scalability and the unresolved theoretical gaps (e.g., $R$'s tractability, non-binary extension) prevent this work from making a compelling case for broader adoption. The authors should either substantially expand experiments to larger models (including projection time) or temper their claims to the small-scale regime. As presented, the method appears to offer marginal benefits only in artificial settings where the projection cost is negligible—a scenario unlikely to hold in practice.
