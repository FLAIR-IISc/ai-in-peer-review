This paper addresses the problem of slow convergence in Gibbs sampling for Ising models with strong interactions. The core idea is to approximate a "slow-mixing" model by projecting its parameters onto the nearest "fast-mixing" model. The notion of "fast-mixing" is defined by a spectral norm condition on the interaction matrix, derived from prior work on mixing time bounds [4, 8]. The authors propose several projection methods based on different divergences (Euclidean, a novel piecewise KL, and the reverse KL). Experimental results on small grid and random graphs show that sampling from the projected model can yield more accurate marginal estimates than standard Gibbs sampling (on the original model) and several variational methods within a fixed time budget.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty of the Approach:** The central contribution—framing approximate inference as a projection onto a set of guaranteed fast-mixing models—is novel and elegant. While the individual components build on existing work, their combination into an inference algorithm is a new and interesting perspective that differs from standard variational approaches.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding:** The work is well-grounded in the existing literature on MCMC mixing times. It cleverly leverages theoretical bounds from Hayes [8] and Dyer et al. [4] as a practical constraint for defining the target set of tractable distributions.

3.  **Good Baseline Comparisons:** The experimental evaluation, though limited to small models, provides a compelling proof of concept against a strong set of baselines, including Mean Field (MF), Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters.

### Weaknesses and Concerns

1.  **Experimental Evaluation and Scalability:** My primary concern lies with the experimental evaluation and its implications for larger, more realistic problems.
    *   **Unaccounted Computational Cost:** The time comparisons in Figure 2 appear to exclude the computational cost of the projection step. Section 6.1 states, "Note that this does not take into account the computational effort deployed during projection." This significantly weakens the central claim. The projection, particularly for the KL-divergence methods, involves a non-trivial optimization process (e.g., 30,000 Gibbs iterations for the KL(ψ||θ) case). A fair comparison should include this cost in the total runtime. As presented, the proposed methods receive the benefit of a sophisticated optimization for free.
    *   **Limited Scale:** The experiments are confined to small models (8x8 grids, 10-node graphs) where exact inference via junction tree is feasible. However, the problem of slow mixing is most acute in large-scale models. It is unclear how the performance and computational cost of the projection (which involves SVD and iterative optimization) will scale with the number of variables. Experiments on larger models, where true marginals could be estimated by a very long MCMC run or are known due to symmetries, would be necessary to demonstrate broader applicability.

2.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written, but several key technical sections are difficult to follow and lack necessary details for reproducibility and verification.
    *   **Unsupported Claims:** The paper refers to several "standard results" without providing citations or proof sketches. For instance, Theorem 6 (projection via SVD thresholding) is stated without reference. Lemma 5 is presented without proof, and the provided citation [8] is noted to only cover a special case. This makes it difficult to assess the technical soundness of the work.
    *   **Inscrutable Optimization Section:** The latter half of Section 4, leading up to Theorem 7, is very difficult to parse. The symbols `g`, `M`, and `Λ` are referenced before they are formally defined in the theorem. The notation in Theorem 7 itself is dense and not adequately explained. More intuition and a clearer step-by-step derivation are needed to make this section comprehensible.
    *   **Confusing Notation:** In the introduction, the notation `KL(q||p)` is used for both `KL(p||q)` (zero-avoiding) and `KL(q||p)` (zero-forcing), which can be confusing. Additionally, the convention of using `q` for the intractable distribution and `p` for the tractable approximation is the reverse of what is common in the variational inference literature, slightly hindering readability.

3.  **Limited Scope:** The proposed approach is developed specifically for pairwise binary Ising models. Extending this framework to general discrete MRFs would be challenging, as establishing tight and computable mixing time bounds for general potentials is a significantly harder problem. This potential limitation on the method's generalizability should be discussed.

### Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors

*   **Clarity:** Could you please provide citations for Theorem 6 and a proof (or sketch) for the generalization claimed in Lemma 5?
*   **Clarity:** Could you elaborate on the properties of the dependency matrix `R`? Specifically, is computing it tractable in general? The reliance on the bound in Lemma 5 suggests it is not, which is an important detail to state explicitly.
*   **Clarity:** I strongly recommend rewriting the second half of Section 4 to improve clarity. The dual formulation in Theorem 7 is a key technical contribution and deserves a more pedagogical presentation.
*   **Experiments:** Please clarify why the projection time was excluded from the experimental comparison. I would urge you to include results that account for the full computational budget (projection + sampling) to provide a more convincing comparison with standard Gibbs sampling.
*   **Experiments:** The paper notes that Mean Field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection (with a zero spectral norm constraint). Could you provide some intuition for the large performance gap observed between MF and the proposed KL(ψ||θ) method?
*   **Experiments:** While minimizing KL(θ||ψ) is intractable for large models, it is tractable for the toy models used in your experiments (using junction tree for the expectations). It would be insightful to include this as an "oracle" or best-case projection to see the upper bound on performance for this class of methods.
