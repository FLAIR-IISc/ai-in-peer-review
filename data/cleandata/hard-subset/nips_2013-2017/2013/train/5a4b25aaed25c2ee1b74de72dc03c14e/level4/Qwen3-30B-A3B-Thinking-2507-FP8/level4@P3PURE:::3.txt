

The paper proposes a novel approach to accelerate inference in Markov random fields (MRFs) with two labels (Ising models) by projecting the input model onto a space of fast-mixing models. The core method consists of: (1) projecting the input model (which may mix slowly) onto the set of fast-mixing models, and (2) performing inference on the resulting fast-mixing model. Fast-mixing models are defined by bounding the spectral norm of the absolute edge strength matrix. The projection is defined via divergences of Gibbs distributions and constrained to preserve the graph structure. For Euclidean distance projection, the problem is dualized and solved using LBFGS-B. For other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL), a projected gradient algorithm is employed. Notably, reversed KL projection requires Gibbs sampling (on the fast-mixing model) to compute the projection.

The authors present extensive experiments on small random models, comparing approximated marginals against true marginals. Methods evaluated include the proposed approach (with all divergences), loopy belief propagation (LBP), tree-reweighted (TRW) mean field, mean field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original model. Both accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy trade-offs are assessed. Results demonstrate that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and for reasonable runtime ranges, also Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed methods, reversed KL divergence yields the best consistent performance.

**Comments:**  
1. Section 5.1 describes a projected gradient algorithm with two nested loops (inner loop: LBFGS-B). Please specify the stopping criterion for the inner LBFGS-B iterations.  
2. Figure 2 (and supplement) labels the horizontal axis as "number of samples," but sampling is only used in reversed KL divergence. This is misleading; the axis should denote runtime (e.g., in seconds). Furthermore, absolute runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF must be reported to enable fair accuracy-runtime comparisons across all methods. Without this, the experimental results are difficult to interpret.  
3. Please consider extending experiments to larger models. A promising direction is to use models from Flach (TPAMI, 2013, available online) that admit polynomial-time inference via tractable partition functions.  
4. Line 222: Replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T."  
5. Line 226: Should "derivative" be replaced with "subgradient"?  
The paper presents an interesting approach with convincing empirical results. However, the practical utility may be limited by high runtime costs (which requires clarification in the rebuttal).
