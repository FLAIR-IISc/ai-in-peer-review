The authors propose a method for improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model's original parameterization onto a new parameter setting that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion, necessary for fast mixing. They formulate this projection as a constrained optimization problem, where the constraints ensure that the new parameters are defined over the same space as the original parameters. The authors examine this projection under several distance/divergence measures.

In my opinion, combining principles from stochastic and deterministic inference is an under-explored area, making this an interesting idea. Although augmenting the original parameters to improve mixing time is a straightforward concept, I found the description of the dual of the projection problem to be somewhat unclear. Specifically, it is unclear how the zij*dij = 0 constraints guarantee that the new parameterization is defined over the same space as the original parameters, rather than a smaller space. I was also uncertain about the overall procedure: do the authors perform the projected gradient operations to completion and then run a Gibbs sampler, or do they interleave sampling with the gradient updates? An algorithm description box would help clarify this process.

Additionally, I question whether the proposed projected gradient descent strategy is guaranteed to converge. I also found the experiments to be somewhat unconvincing. In the first set of experiments, I am unclear as to why the Gibbs sampler was run for 30,000 iterations. Since the authors are comparing a sampling method to deterministic methods, a comparison based on time would seem more fair. Furthermore, the absence of error bars on the charts makes it impossible for the reader to determine the significance of the results. The second set of results compares the original Gibbs sampler with Gibbs samplers under different projections and demonstrates that the projection does lead to faster mixing. However, the time required to perform the projection operation is not accounted for in this comparison. For instance, if the projection takes one minute, and the naive Gibbs sampler can generate 10,000 samples in that time, the projection may not be worthwhile. Lastly, I am surprised that there was no comparison to blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate's "Lifted Gibbs Sampler".

Overall, this is a promising idea, but the development of the projection problem and proposed method could benefit from further refinement. A more comprehensive set of experiments is necessary to convincingly demonstrate the utility of the method.
