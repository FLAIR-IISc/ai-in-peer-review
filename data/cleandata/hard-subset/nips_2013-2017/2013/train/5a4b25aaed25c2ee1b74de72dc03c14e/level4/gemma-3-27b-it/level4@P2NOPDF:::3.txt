This paper addresses the challenge of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for inference in Markov Random Fields (MRFs), specifically Ising models with two labels. The core idea is to project the input MRF onto a space of models guaranteed to mix rapidly, and then perform inference on this projected, faster-mixing model. This space of fast-mixing models is characterized by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix representing the absolute values of the Ising edge strengths. The projection is defined using divergences between Gibbs distributions, while preserving the underlying graph structure.

The authors implement projection using two main approaches: for Euclidean distance, they employ a dual formulation solved with LBFGS-B; for other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL), they use a projected gradient algorithm. Notably, the reversed KL divergence projection requires Gibbs sampling *within* the fast-mixing projected model to compute the projection step itself.

The paper presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation on small, randomly generated models, comparing the accuracy of estimated marginals against ground truth. The proposed methods, utilizing different divergence measures, are benchmarked against loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), Mean Field (MF), and Gibbs sampling performed directly on the original, potentially slow-mixing model. The evaluation considers both accuracy and the trade-off between runtime and accuracy. Results indicate that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in terms of accuracy. Moreover, for comparable runtimes, they also surpass standard Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed approaches, the reversed KL divergence consistently yields the best performance.

**Comments and Questions:**

The projected gradient algorithm described in Section 5.1 involves nested loops, with LBFGS-B forming the inner loop.  Please provide details regarding the stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B iterations. Understanding this will clarify the computational cost of the projection step.

The horizontal axis in Figure 2 (and the supplementary material) is labeled "number of samples," but sampling is only explicitly used in the reversed KL divergence method. I believe this axis more accurately represents the *runtime* of the algorithm.  Furthermore, reporting the runtime for LBP, TRW, and MF would facilitate a fairer and more informative accuracy-runtime comparison across all tested methods. Please clarify this point and provide absolute running times in seconds.

Expanding the experimental evaluation to include larger models would strengthen the conclusions. I suggest considering models from Flach (TPAMI, available online) that possess tractable partition functions, allowing for polynomial-time inference and providing a strong baseline for comparison.

Minor textual suggestions: Line 222, replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T." Line 226, consider using the term "subgradient" instead of "derivative," as the optimization context typically involves non-differentiable functions.

Overall, this is an interesting and well-motivated paper with convincing empirical results. However, the practical utility of the proposed methods may be limited by their runtime, a point that needs further clarification in the rebuttal. A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity, alongside absolute runtime comparisons, is crucial for assessing the scalability and applicability of this approach.
