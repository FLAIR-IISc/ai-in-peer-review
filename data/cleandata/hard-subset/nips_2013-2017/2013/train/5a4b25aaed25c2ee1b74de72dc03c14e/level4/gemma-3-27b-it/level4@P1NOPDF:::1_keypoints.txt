- Aim: speed up Gibbs mixing in pairwise Ising models by projecting parameters onto the nearest “fast-mixing” model that satisfies a spectral bound.  
- Method novelty: projecting onto a nearest rapidly-mixing model as an approximate inference technique appears novel, though it builds on prior rapid-mixing results.  
- Empirical result: on small toy Ising models, the approach yields improved marginal estimates versus variational methods and original Gibbs sampling in some regimes.  
- Scalability concern: results on small models may not extrapolate to larger problem sizes where Gibbs mixing times typically worsen.  
- Computational accounting: the projection step is computationally expensive and its cost seems omitted from the reported timing comparisons.  
- Generality concern: unclear how the approach extends beyond binary-state models; convergence bounds are harder in broader settings.  
- Missing references/proofs: several “standard results” (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5) are stated without citations or proof sketches.  
- Dependency matrix R: properties and tractability of R are not discussed; the maximization in R may be intractable and needs clarification.  
- Section 4 clarity: the second half is difficult to follow—variables (g, M, Λ) and notation (e.g., in Theorem 4) are introduced or used before definition.  
- Notational confusion: KL(q||p) is used for both directions and q/p are reversed from common conventions in Section 1.  
- Timing clarification: Figure 2 and related timing results should explicitly include projection time or clarify its exclusion.  
- Larger-model experiments: paper lacks experiments on larger or more realistic models; alternatives for ground truth (symmetries, long runs) should be used.  
- KL-based evaluation: exploring KL(θ||ψ) as a best-case metric (using junction tree for toy models) would be informative.  
- Mean-field relation: the distinction between mean field as a degenerate reverse-KL case and the actual error from reverse-KL projection requires discussion.  
- Overall assessment: the idea is compelling and leverages recent mixing bounds elegantly, but experimental comparisons, computational accounting, clarity, and applicability raise significant concerns.
