This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models. Standard methods often approximate an intractable, high-treewidth distribution with a structurally simpler one (e.g., fully-factorized for mean-field or tree-structured for TRW-BP). In contrast, this work defines the set of "tractable" models not by their graphical structure, but by the property of being "fast-mixing" for Gibbs sampling. The authors leverage a known result that fast mixing is guaranteed if the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths is less than one.

The core contribution is a set of algorithms to "project" a given (potentially slow-mixing) Ising model onto this space of fast-mixing models, while preserving the original graph structure. The projection is formulated as an optimization problem to find the closest fast-mixing parameters under several divergence measures. For the Euclidean distance, the authors derive an efficient dual algorithm solved with LBFGS-B. For more principled probabilistic divergences—including a novel piecewise KL-divergence, the standard KL(q||p), and the reverse KL(p||q)—a projected gradient descent scheme is employed. Notably, minimizing the reverse KL-divergence requires sampling from the projected (fast-mixing) model, which is tractable by construction.

The paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation on small grid and random graphs. The proposed methods are compared against loopy belief propagation (LBP), tree-reweighted belief propagation (TRW), naive mean-field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing model. The results demonstrate that the proposed projection methods, especially using the reverse KL-divergence, achieve higher accuracy in marginal estimation than the variational baselines. Furthermore, the runtime-accuracy plots show that sampling from the projected model yields better estimates than sampling from the original model for a considerable time budget.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of defining tractability through fast-mixing dynamics, rather than graphical structure, is a significant and novel contribution to the field of approximate inference. It offers a new perspective on how to construct tractable approximations.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the Euclidean projection using a dual formulation and SVD thresholding is elegant and well-explained. The extension to KL-divergences via a projected stochastic gradient framework is a sensible and effective approach.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are convincing. The proposed methods consistently outperform widely-used and strong baselines (LBP, TRW, MF) in terms of accuracy. The comparison against Gibbs sampling on the original model effectively highlights the practical benefit of the approach when inference time is limited.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity of Experimental Results:** The presentation of the experiments could be improved. The horizontal axis in Figure 2 is labeled "number of samples," which is ambiguous. This appears to be the number of samples drawn *after* the projection is complete, and does not account for the computational cost of the projection itself, nor is it directly applicable to non-sampling baselines like LBP or MF. For a fair and clear comparison of the accuracy-runtime trade-off, it is essential to plot accuracy against total wall-clock time in seconds for *all* methods, including the time taken for the projection step. Without this, it is difficult to assess the practical utility of the proposed algorithms.
*   **Reproducibility:** The description of the projected gradient algorithm in Section 5.1 lacks some implementation details. The algorithm involves an outer gradient update and an inner loop to solve the Euclidean projection using LBFGS-B. The authors should specify the convergence or stopping criteria for this inner optimization loop to ensure the work can be reproduced.
*   **Scope of Evaluation:** The experiments are currently limited to small-scale models (8x8 grids, 10-node graphs). While sufficient for a proof of concept, demonstrating the method's scalability on larger and more challenging problem instances would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution. An interesting test would be to use models that admit exact polynomial-time inference despite being densely connected, such as those proposed by Flach (TPAMI, 2012), to provide a more stressful benchmark.

**Minor Comments:**
*   On line 222, in the description of the piecewise KL-divergence, the phrase "projection onto the tree" should likely be "projection onto the graph T" for generality.
*   On line 226, since the piecewise KL-divergence is defined as a maximum over several functions, using the term "subgradient" might be more technically precise than "derivative".
