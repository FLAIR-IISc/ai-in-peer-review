

The authors propose a novel approach to accelerate Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting original parameters onto a fast-mixing parameter set satisfying spectral norm constraints (||R||₂ < 1), as established in Theorem 4. This work extends prior tractability concepts at NIPS (e.g., Globerson & Jaakkola, 2006 on planar graphs; Hazan & Shashua, 2008 on variational methods) by redefining "tractability" through mixing-time guarantees rather than structural constraints. The paper derives a dual optimization framework for projection under Euclidean distance (Theorem 7) and explores alternative divergences (piecewise KL, zero-forcing KL), with experiments comparing projected Gibbs sampling against LBP, TRW, and naive mean-field.

**Strengths:**  
- **Originality (High):** The focus on parameter-space conditions for fast mixing (rather than graph structure) offers a fresh perspective distinct from prior NIPS work on tractable inference. The dual formulation for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is technically novel.  
- **Significance (Moderate):** The method addresses a practical bottleneck in MCMC for strong-interaction Ising models, with potential applications in learning and inference. The experimental results demonstrate consistent accuracy gains over variational methods under finite sampling budgets.  
- **Quality (Moderate):** Theoretical foundation (Theorems 4–7) is sound, and the experimental design aligns with standard benchmarks (e.g., Hazan & Shashua, 2008). The conservative spectral norm bound (Section 7) is honestly acknowledged.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Clarity (Low):** The dual optimization derivation (Eq. 1–2) lacks sufficient intuition. The constraint *Z*ᵢⱼ*D*ᵢⱼ = 0 ensures graph structure preservation but does not explicitly prevent parameter space reduction—this ambiguity requires clarification. The algorithmic procedure (e.g., whether projected gradient steps are completed before Gibbs sampling or interleaved) is unclear, warranting a pseudocode box.  
- **Quality (Low):** The projected gradient descent for KL(ψ||θ) lacks a convergence proof. The 30,000 Gibbs iterations for projection (Section 6.1) are compared against Gibbs sampling without accounting for projection time, invalidating the time-accuracy comparison (e.g., projection may take 1 minute while naive Gibbs generates 10k samples in that time).  
- **Significance (Low):** Experiments omit critical baselines (blocked Gibbs, Gogate’s lifted Gibbs) and lack error bars, making statistical significance uncertain. The claim that projection "generally gives better results" under finite time is unsubstantiated without time-normalized metrics.  
- **Originality (Moderate):** The piecewise KL divergence (Section 5.3) resembles existing tractable subgraph approaches (Saul & Jordan, 2002; Sutton & McCallum, 2007), though the specific implementation is novel.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel fast-mixing parameterization concept; strong theoretical grounding; practical accuracy gains in finite-time settings.  
*Con:* Ambiguous dual formulation; unconvincing experiments due to missing time accounting, error bars, and baselines; no convergence guarantee for the core optimization.  

The paper presents a promising direction but requires significant clarification of the optimization procedure, rigorous experimental validation with time-normalized comparisons, and explicit comparison to recent MCMC techniques to meet NIPS’s quality and significance standards.
