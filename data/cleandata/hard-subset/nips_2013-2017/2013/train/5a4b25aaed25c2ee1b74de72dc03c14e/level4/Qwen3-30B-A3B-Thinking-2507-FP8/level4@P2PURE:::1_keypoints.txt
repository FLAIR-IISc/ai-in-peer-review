- Paper proposes accelerating Gibbs sampling in strongly-coupled pairwise Ising models by projecting to the nearest “fast-mixing” model satisfying a spectral bound.  
- Novelty: projecting to the closest rapidly-mixing model as an approximate inference method is new relative to prior rapid-mixing results [4,8].  
- Experiments on small toy Ising models show improved marginal estimates within fixed computation time versus variational methods and Gibbs on the original model in certain regimes.  
- Major scalability concern: unclear how mixing-time improvements and the method scale with problem size; experiments on larger models are absent.  
- Critical omission: projection computation cost is excluded from timing comparisons, which may erase the reported advantage.  
- The projection step is computationally expensive and its cost must be included or justified in runtime comparisons.  
- Generalization beyond binary-state models is unlikely, since deriving convergence bounds for larger state spaces is significantly more challenging.  
- Clarity issues: several “standard results” are presented as theorems without citations or proof sketches (e.g., Theorem 6); Lemma 5 is unproven and references only a special case.  
- The tractability of the dependency matrix R (which involves a maximization) is not addressed; the paper should explicitly justify invoking Lemma 5 due to intractability.  
- Section 4 is hard to follow: Theorem 7 references variables (g, M, Λ) before they are introduced, and Theorem 4 uses unexplained notation; the optimization needs clearer exposition.  
- Notational inconsistency: KL(q || p) usage swaps the conventional meaning of q/p (q is called the true distribution but used inconsistently).  
- Experimental suggestions: include projection time in Figure 2, evaluate larger or symmetric models where exact marginals are available, or use extremely long sampling runs as ground truth.  
- Additional analyses requested: evaluate KL(θ || ψ) as a “best-case” for sampling (e.g., via junction tree on toy models) and discuss why mean-field (reverse KL) differs substantially from reverse KL projection.  
- Overall: the core idea is elegant and leverages recent mixing bounds effectively, but concerns about omitted projection cost, limited experiments, and limited applicability beyond binary models reduce the paper’s impact.
