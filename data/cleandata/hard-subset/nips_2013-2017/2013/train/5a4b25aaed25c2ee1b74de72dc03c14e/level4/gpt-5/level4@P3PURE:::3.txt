This paper addresses inference in binary MRFs (Ising models) where the success of Gibbs sampling depends critically on the mixing rate of the underlying Markov chain. The proposed approach is:
1) Project the input model (which may mix slowly) onto a set of models that are guaranteed to mix rapidly.
2) Perform inference on the resulting fast-mixing model.

The fast-mixing set is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute Ising edge strengths, and the projection is constrained to preserve the original graph structure. The projection is defined via divergences between the corresponding Gibbs distributions. For Euclidean distance, the authors dualize the problem and use L-BFGS-B. For other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL), they use a projected gradient method. For reverse KL, computing the projection requires Gibbs sampling, but crucially only on the fast-mixing model.

Experiments on small random models compare approximate marginals to ground truth. Baselines include loopy BP (LBP), TRW, mean-field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The evaluation considers both accuracy and accuracy versus runtime. The proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and for reasonable runtimes they also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed variants, the reverse KL projection performs best.

Comments and requests for clarification:
- Algorithmic details: The projected gradient algorithm in Section 5.1 in fact has two nested loops, with L-BFGS-B as the inner loop. Please provide precise stopping criteria for the inner iterations (e.g., tolerances, maximum iterations, and how inner-loop accuracy interacts with outer-loop convergence).
- Experimental reporting and fairness: In Figure 2 (and the supplement), the horizontal axis is labeled “number of samples,” but sampling is used only for the reverse KL divergence. It appears that the x-axis should represent runtime. Please clarify this and report the runtime for all methods, including LBP, TRW, and MF, to enable a fair accuracy–runtime comparison across all tested algorithms. Without this, it is difficult to interpret the experiments. Please provide absolute running times in seconds.
- Scale of experiments: Please consider experiments on larger models. A useful option is to use models from:
  Boris Flach: A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function, to appear in TPAMI (available online),
which admit polynomial-time inference and would allow benchmarking at larger scales.
- Minor edits:
  • Line 222: replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T.”
  • Line 226: Should this be “subgradient” rather than “derivative”?

Overall assessment: This is an interesting paper with a clear idea and convincing empirical results on small graphs. However, the practical utility may be limited by high runtime; this needs clarification in the rebuttal, particularly via comprehensive runtime reporting and discussion of the computational trade-offs of the proposed projections.
