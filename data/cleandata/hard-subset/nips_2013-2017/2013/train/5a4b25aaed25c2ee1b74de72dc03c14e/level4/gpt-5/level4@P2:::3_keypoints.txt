- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set defined by a spectral-norm bound on absolute pairwise couplings to enable reliable Gibbs sampling.  
- Develops an Euclidean projection algorithm that preserves graph sparsity via a dual formulation and repeated singular-value thresholding, solved with LBFGS-B.  
- Extends projection to divergences: inclusive KL (KL(θ||ψ), intractable), piecewise KL (tractable via low-treewidth subgraphs), and reversed KL (KL(ψ||θ), optimized by sampling from ψ).  
- Uses a projected-gradient scheme: take a divergence-gradient step then Euclidean-project back to the fast-mixing set.  
- Experiments on small grids and random graphs show projected models, especially reversed KL, give more accurate marginals than LBP/TRW/MF and beat Gibbs on original parameters under finite sampling.  
- Complements tree/planar approximations by defining tractability via mixing-time guarantees rather than graph structure.  
- The spectral-norm mixing condition relates to Dobrushin-type criteria and known spectral bounds for spin systems.  
- KL(ψ||θ) generalizes mean-field by enlarging the approximating family from factorized to fast-mixing distributions.  
- Piecewise KL is analogous to piecewise likelihood and mixtures of trees.  
- Strength: clear motivation—enforcing fast mixing is pragmatic when couplings are strong and other inference fails.  
- Strength: solid technical contribution in a sparsity-preserving dual projection with an efficient SVD-thresholding primitive.  
- Strength: conceptual generalization of mean-field and broad experimental comparisons with time–accuracy trade-offs.  
- Strength: reversed KL performs best among proposed divergences; connects to mixing-time theory and stochastic optimization.  
- Weakness: spectral-norm guarantee may be conservative (e.g., 2D Ising); authors use loosened bounds (2.5 vs 1) without justification or empirical mixing checks.  
- Weakness: runtime accounting is unclear and potentially unfair—Figure 2’s x-axis labeled “number of samples” hides differing costs; need wall-clock times and runtimes for LBP/TRW/MF.  
- Weakness: optimization details missing—inner LBFGS-B stopping criteria and outer loop step-size/schedule/convergence not specified.  
- Weakness: stochastic approximation hyperparameters (step size, sample-pool size, Gibbs updates, scan order) need sensitivity analysis and clarification.  
- Weakness: limited experiments on small graphs; scalability to larger or structured instances is unclear.  
- Weakness: piecewise KL underperforms true KL; more discussion on subgraph selection and potential adaptive schemes needed.  
- Suggestion: report full runtime and memory for all methods and plot accuracy vs wall-clock time.  
- Suggestion: provide mixing diagnostics (empirical autocorrelations, spectral norm of |β|, coupling estimates) when using relaxed bounds.  
- Suggestion: detail projection solver implementation (initialization, constraint handling, sparsity maintenance, numerical stability).  
- Suggestion: include ablation comparing Euclidean vs divergence projections under equal compute budgets.  
- Minor: correct typos (e.g., “onto the tree” → “onto graph T”; “derivative” → “subgradient”), clarify Figure 2 x-axis, and state whether systematic or random scan is used.  
- Overall: interesting, well-motivated, and convincing technically, but concerns remain about runtime accounting, scalability, and guarantee clarity; reviewer leans positive pending clarifications.
