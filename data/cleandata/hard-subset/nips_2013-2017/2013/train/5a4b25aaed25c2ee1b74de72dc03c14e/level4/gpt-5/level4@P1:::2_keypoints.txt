- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing subset to improve Gibbs mixing and inference under time budgets.  
- Defines fast-mixing set via dependency-matrix constraints (e.g., spectral-norm bounds) and studies projections under several divergences (Euclidean, inclusive KL, piecewise KL, reverse KL).  
- Derives a dual method for Euclidean projection using SVD thresholding while enforcing sparsity to preserve graph structure.  
- Uses exact KL on low-treewidth subgraphs for piecewise KL and stochastic-approximation with a sample “pool” for reverse KL.  
- Empirical comparisons include LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on original parameters, showing benefits when interactions are strong and time-limited.  
- Novel and under-explored direction bridging variational approximations with MCMC using fast-mixing parameter constraints.  
- Clear motivation: projecting slow-mixing models to fast-mixing sets can yield better estimates within time constraints.  
- Technically appealing and practically relevant dual formulation that preserves sparsity.  
- Broad empirical comparisons and multiple divergences give a useful perspective.  
- Unclear whether constraints that zero non-edges guarantee the projected parameters span the same model space or may shrink/zero existing edges; clarify notion of “same space.”  
- Ambiguity about overall procedure: unclear if projected gradient descent runs to convergence before Gibbs or if sampling is interleaved with parameter updates.  
- Need a concise algorithm box detailing initialization, projection steps, sampling schedule, and stopping criteria.  
- Convergence guarantees are under-specified; state precise conditions (step-size, MCMC steps per iteration) and implications of non-convex reverse KL.  
- Practical use of spectral-norm bound >1 (e.g., 2.5) contradicts theory guaranteeing mixing for ||R||2 < 1; justify and quantify empirical mixing behavior and diagnostics.  
- Experimental fairness issues: comparisons should be time-based (wall-clock) rather than iteration counts to account for projection cost.  
- Missing error bars and statistical reporting; include means and variances or confidence intervals over trials.  
- Projection cost not included in some evaluations; report total wall-clock projection + sampling vs. baseline sampling for the same time budget.  
- Missing stronger baselines focused on mixing (block Gibbs, tree/block updates, Swendsen–Wang, lifted Gibbs) to assess relative advantage.  
- No sensitivity analysis for hyperparameters (step size, pool size, iterations), reducing confidence in robustness.  
- Technical quality generally sound but some derivations and algorithmic details are under-specified; empirical spectral bound choice needs stronger justification.  
- Paper is readable but needs a self-contained algorithm description and clearer explanation of constraints’ effects on model space and optimization schedules.  
- Originality is strong: projecting onto a fast-mixing set and reverse KL optimization within it is novel, and sparsity-preserving dual projection is a nice contribution.  
- Potentially significant for time-limited inference in difficult Ising models, but empirical evidence needs fair timing, broader baselines, and statistical rigor.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel framework, practical Euclidean projection algorithm, empirical indications of improved accuracy under time constraints.  
- Cons for acceptance: clarity gaps in formulation and algorithm, incomplete/unfair experiments, and mismatch between theoretical mixing guarantees and practical spectral bounds.  
- Suggested improvements: add algorithm pseudocode, clarify constraint effects on graph structure, provide convergence statements, include wall-clock accuracy curves with error bars, compare advanced samplers, justify spectral bound choice and diagnostics, and add hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.  
- Overall: promising idea with solid potential, but requires clearer algorithmic exposition and stronger, time-aware experiments to be fully convincing.
