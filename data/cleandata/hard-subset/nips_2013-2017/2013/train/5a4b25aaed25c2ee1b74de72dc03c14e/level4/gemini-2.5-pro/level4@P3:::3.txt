This paper addresses the challenge of inference in Ising models where both high treewidth and strong interactions make standard methods intractable. The core idea is to introduce a new notion of a "tractable" model, defined not by its graphical structure (e.g., being a tree) but by having parameters that guarantee the fast mixing of a Gibbs sampler. The authors define this fast-mixing set by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths.

The proposed inference approach consists of two steps:
1.  Project the parameters of a given, potentially slow-mixing, Ising model onto the set of fast-mixing models. This projection preserves the sparsity pattern of the original graph.
2.  Run Gibbs sampling on the resulting projected model, which is guaranteed to converge quickly to its stationary distribution.

The paper develops methods for this projection under several divergences. For the Euclidean distance between parameter vectors, a dual algorithm is derived and solved using LBFGS-B. For more principled probabilistic divergences—namely the inclusive KL(θ||ψ), a novel "piecewise" KL, and the reversed KL(ψ||θ)—the projection is performed using a projected gradient descent algorithm. The reversed KL projection is particularly practical, as the required gradients can be estimated by sampling from the projected model ψ, which is itself fast-mixing.

The authors provide an extensive empirical evaluation on small grid and random graphs. They compare the accuracy of estimated marginals from their methods against loopy belief propagation (LBP), tree-reweighted belief propagation (TRW), naive mean-field (MF), and standard Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing model. The results convincingly show that the proposed methods, particularly using the reversed KL divergence, are more accurate than LBP, TRW, and MF. Furthermore, in time-limited scenarios, they produce more accurate marginals than running Gibbs sampling on the original model.

**Strengths**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The main contribution is the novel conceptualization of a "tractable" family of distributions based on guaranteed fast mixing times. This is a significant and creative departure from standard variational approaches that rely on structurally simple families (e.g., fully-factorized or tree-structured distributions). This idea is significant and could inspire new classes of approximation algorithms for inference and learning.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It provides a formal algorithm for the Euclidean projection based on a dual formulation (Theorem 7) and correctly derives the gradients required for projection under KL-divergences. The connection drawn between minimizing the reversed KL divergence and mean-field (as a degenerate case with zero spectral norm) is insightful.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are strong and well-presented. The proposed method demonstrates superior accuracy over widely-used approximate inference techniques. The runtime-accuracy comparison against standard Gibbs sampling is particularly effective, as it highlights the practical benefit of the approach when inference must be completed within a finite time budget.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors**

The primary weaknesses relate to the clarity of the experimental evaluation and the computational cost of the projection step.

*   **Clarity of Experimental Evaluation:** The runtime-vs-accuracy plots in Figure 2 are central to the paper's claims, but their interpretation is difficult. The horizontal axis is labeled "number of samples," but it is not clear what this represents for methods that do not involve sampling during inference (LBP, TRW, MF) or for the projection methods that use it only during the optimization phase.
    *   Could the authors please clarify what the x-axis represents for all methods? A more direct and interpretable metric, such as wall-clock time in seconds, would greatly improve the clarity and impact of these results.
    *   To allow for a fair comparison of the trade-offs, the runtimes for the baseline methods (LBP, TRW, MF) should also be reported. Without this, it is difficult to assess the practical utility and computational overhead of the proposed method relative to these standard techniques.
*   **Computational Cost of Projection:** The practical utility of the method depends critically on the cost of the projection itself. The projected gradient algorithm involves an inner loop of Euclidean projections, which is solved via an iterative L-BFGS-B procedure. This two-level optimization could be computationally intensive. A more detailed discussion of the overall runtime, including the projection phase, is needed. For instance, in the projected gradient algorithm from Section 5.1, what stopping criteria are used for the inner L-BFGS-B loop to balance accuracy and speed?
*   **Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are currently limited to small graphs (10-node random graphs and 8x8 grids). While this is sufficient for a proof of concept, evaluating the method on larger, more challenging models would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of practical relevance.

**Minor Points**

*   Line 222: In the definition of the piecewise KL-divergence, "projection onto the tree" might be more general if written as "projection onto the graph T".
*   Line 226: Since the piecewise KL-divergence is defined as a maximum over a set of functions, would "subgradient" be a more precise term than "derivative"?

**Conclusion**

This is an interesting and well-executed paper that introduces a novel and promising approach to approximate inference. The core idea of defining tractability via mixing time is highly original, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its potential. The main concerns are the lack of clarity in the reporting of runtimes, which obscures the crucial performance trade-offs, and the potential high computational cost of the projection algorithm itself. If the authors can satisfactorily address these points in their rebuttal, this paper would be a very strong contribution to the conference.
