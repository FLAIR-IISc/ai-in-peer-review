Summary
The paper proposes a principled approach to approximate inference in Ising models by projecting model parameters onto a set guaranteed to be fast-mixing for single-site Gibbs sampling. The fast-mixing set is defined via a sufficient condition: the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute pairwise couplings must be below a threshold. The authors develop:

- An Euclidean projection algorithm that preserves graph sparsity via a dual formulation and repeated singular value thresholding, solved with LBFGS-B.
- Extensions to probabilistic divergences between the original model θ and the projected model ψ: (i) inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ) (theoretically appealing but requires expectations under θ and is intractable for general graphs), (ii) a tractable piecewise KL built from low-treewidth subgraphs, and (iii) reversed KL KL(ψ||θ), optimized by stochastic approximation using samples from ψ (which is fast-mixing).
- A projected gradient scheme that takes a step in the divergence’s gradient and then Euclidean-projects back to the fast-mixing set.

Experiments on small grids and random graphs compare projected models (under all divergences) against LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters. The projected models, especially with reversed KL, yield more accurate marginals than LBP/TRW/MF and outperform Gibbs on the original parameters under finite sampling budgets.

Relation to prior work
This work complements tree/planar approximations (e.g., TRW, planar decomposition, structured/mean-field) by defining tractability via mixing-time guarantees rather than graph structure. The sufficient mixing condition relates to Dobrushin-type criteria and spectral-norm bounds for spin systems. Optimizing KL(ψ||θ) connects to mean-field as a limiting case (zero spectral norm). The piecewise KL echoes piecewise likelihood and mixtures of trees.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: in regimes with strong couplings, exact or variational inference is unreliable, and naive Gibbs may mix exponentially slowly; enforcing fast mixing is a pragmatic alternative.
- Technical contribution: a dual Euclidean projection that preserves sparsity and enforces the spectral constraint on |β|, with an efficient SVD-thresholding primitive.
- Conceptual generalization: projected gradient under KL(ψ||θ) extends mean-field by enlarging the approximating family from factorized to fast-mixing distributions.
- Experimental breadth: comparisons to standard baselines and time–accuracy trade-offs; consistent gains over LBP/TRW/MF; reversed KL performs best among proposed divergences.
- Clear connection to mixing-time theory and practical stochastic optimization (Ergodic Mirror Descent perspective).

Weaknesses and questions
- Conservativeness of the guarantee: the spectral-norm condition can be loose (e.g., 2D Ising criticality). The experiments often use a bound of 2.5 rather than 1.0, which weakens formal guarantees. Please justify this choice and discuss how often the guarantee is actually violated and whether mixing remains rapid empirically.
- Runtime accounting and fairness: Figure 2’s x-axis is labeled “number of samples,” but only KL(ψ||θ) uses sampling during projection; other methods incur different costs (e.g., repeated Euclidean projections, optimization). Please clarify the x-axis, include wall-clock times (including projection/optimization time) for all methods, and report LBP/TRW/MF runtimes to enable a fair accuracy–runtime comparison.
- Optimization details: Section 5.1’s projected gradient has two nested loops, with LBFGS-B used inside each projection. Please specify stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B loop (tolerances, max iterations), and for the outer loop (step-size schedule, convergence test).
- Stochastic approximation hyperparameters: performance likely depends on step size, sample-pool size, number of Gibbs updates per iteration, and total iterations. A sensitivity analysis would help. Also, clarify whether samples are updated by random or systematic scan and how this choice affects bias/variance in gradient estimates.
- Scope and scalability: experiments are limited to small graphs (8×8 grids, n=10 random graphs). It would strengthen the paper to include larger instances and/or structured models with known tractable inference, e.g., models from Flach (TPAMI) on complete graphs, to assess scaling and approximation quality.
- Piecewise KL design: the piecewise divergence underperforms the true KL. More discussion on subgraph selection (coverage, treewidth) and whether adaptive subgraph sets improve results would be useful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full runtime (seconds) and memory usage for all methods, including preprocessing and projection, and plot accuracy versus wall-clock time.
- Report mixing diagnostics (e.g., estimated spectral norm of |β| for ψ, empirical autocorrelation times, or coupling-based estimates) to support rapid-mixing claims in practice when using looser bounds.
- Detail the projection solver: initialization, constraints handling, sparsity maintenance, and numerical stability.
- Include a brief ablation comparing Euclidean versus divergence projections under equal compute budgets.

Minor comments and typos
- Line 222: “onto the tree” → “onto graph T”.
- Line 226: “derivative” → “subgradient” (when the objective is nonsmooth or maximization over T induces nondifferentiability).
- Clarify the definition of the x-axis in Figure 2 and supplemental plots; consider renaming to “wall-clock time (s)” or “total Gibbs updates,” and ensure consistency across methods.
- State whether systematic or random scan is used during training and evaluation and reconcile with the mixing-time theorems cited (random updates).

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and well-motivated paper that introduces a novel and useful perspective: projecting onto fast-mixing parameter sets to enable reliable sampling-based inference under tight time budgets. The technical development and empirical results are convincing. The main concerns are runtime accounting, scalability, and clarity on optimization details and guarantees when using relaxed spectral bounds. With clarifications and additional runtime reporting, I lean positive. Practical utility may be constrained by optimization cost unless the authors can demonstrate competitive end-to-end accuracy for equal wall-clock time across all baselines.
