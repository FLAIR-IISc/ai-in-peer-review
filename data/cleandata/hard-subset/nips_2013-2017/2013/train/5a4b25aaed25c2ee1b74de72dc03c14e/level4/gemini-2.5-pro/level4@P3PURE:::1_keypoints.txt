- Novel approach: project Ising model parameters onto the set of fast-mixing models to improve Gibbs sampling; builds on prior mixing-time bounds but projection-as-inference is novel.

- Empirical claim: for certain regimes the projected model yields better marginal estimates within fixed compute time than Gibbs on the original model, mean field, and belief propagation.

- Scalability concern: experiments are limited to small models; unclear if improvements hold for larger, realistic problems.

- Projection cost concern: the computationally expensive projection step appears omitted from timing comparisons, making evaluations unfair.

- Generalizability concern: extending the method beyond binary Ising models is doubtful because convergence bounds are harder for other state spaces.

- Missing citations/proofs: several “standard results” (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5) are presented without proper citation or proof sketch.

- Dependency matrix R unclear: its definition involves a maximization that may be intractable; the paper should clarify computation and motivation for invoking Lemma 5.

- Section 4 is confusing and disorganized: variables (g, M, Lambda) are referenced before definition and Theorem 7 uses unexplained notation; optimization procedure needs substantial revision.

- KL notation confusion: KL(q||p) is used inconsistently and the q/p convention is reversed from standard literature, risking reader confusion.

- Experimental fix: explicitly include projection time in timing comparisons or rerun experiments if it was excluded.

- Need larger-scale evaluation: run on bigger models or models with tractable exact marginals (or long MCMC ground truth) to assess real-world performance.

- Add oracle baseline: include KL(θ||ψ) projection computed via junction tree on small models to quantify the gap to an ideal projection.

- Analyze mean-field gap: explain why mean field (a degenerate reverse-KL case) performs much worse than the proposed projection.

- Overall verdict: idea is elegant and leverages theory, but paper is weakened by evaluation omissions, scalability/cost/generalization concerns, and clarity/notation issues.
