- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set defined by spectral-norm bounds to guarantee rapid single-site Gibbs mixing.  
- Considers four projection methods: Euclidean dual projection (sparse, singular-value thresholding), inclusive KL KL(θ||ψ) (theoretical), piecewise KL on tractable subgraphs, and reverse KL KL(ψ||θ) optimized by stochastic approximation.  
- Extends approximating families beyond fully factorized mean-field to a larger fast-mixing class.  
- Empirically compares these projections to LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on grids and small random graphs.  
- Complements existing tractable-structure approximations and uses the standard spectral-norm sufficient condition for mixing; links KL(ψ||θ) minimization to mean-field.  
- Strength: novel framing of “fast mixing” as the tractable family, bridging variational and MCMC approaches.  
- Strength: solid theoretical use of mixing conditions and a dual Euclidean projection algorithm that preserves sparsity.  
- Strength: practical approach of optimizing KL(ψ||θ) with stochastic approximation while enforcing fast-mixing.  
- Strength: empirical scope includes attractive and mixed couplings and standard variational baselines.  
- Weakness: unclear dual projection constraint (ZijDij = 0)—need clarification whether projection can remove edges.  
- Weakness: ambiguous algorithmic procedure—unclear if projection is done once or interleaved with sampling; requests pseudocode with update order and stopping criteria.  
- Weakness: lacks explicit convergence conditions for stochastic projected updates (step-size schedule, number of Markov steps, mixing assumptions).  
- Weakness: main mixing bound assumes random updates while experiments use systematic scan—needs justification or citation for systematic-scan bounds.  
- Clarity issue: Theorem 7 derivation is dense and needs an intuitive explanation of Λ and M’s roles in enforcing the spectral constraint while keeping sparsity.  
- Clarity issue: notation (Rij, D, B, absolute value) is inconsistent; must state constraint is on the spectral norm of the elementwise absolute of pairwise parameters.  
- Experimental fairness: comparisons should account for wall-clock time and projection/optimization cost rather than fixed Gibbs iterations.  
- Experimental robustness: include error bars/variance estimates and significance tests across trials.  
- Experimental concern: spectral bound often set to 2.5 despite theory requiring <1—report chain failure rates, mixing diagnostics, and empirical mixing-time estimates.  
- Experimental baseline gap: add comparisons to alternative MCMC accelerations (blocked Gibbs, tree/block sampling, Swendsen–Wang, lifted Gibbs).  
- Experimental scope: extend evaluations to larger graphs and stronger couplings to show where fast-mixing projections help most.  
- Originality: combining projection to a fast-mixing set with KL(ψ||θ) is novel; piecewise KL is an interesting tractable surrogate with limited empirical payoff so far.  
- Significance: could be practically useful if projection cost is modest, but current experiments don’t demonstrate a clear time-accuracy win and the loose spectral thresholds weaken theoretical guarantees.  
- Specific suggestions: add a full algorithm box, clarify sparsity-preservation, provide convergence statements or label heuristic, report runtimes including projection cost, add error bars and hypothesis tests, and compare ESS per time against blocked/lifted Gibbs.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel, timely idea with solid theory and practical stochastic optimization, potentially impactful for time-limited inference.  
- Cons for acceptance: ambiguous algorithmic details and convergence, weak experimental accounting and baselines, and use of spectral thresholds beyond theoretical guarantees without diagnostics.  
- Overall: promising and original direction with strong motivation, but needs clearer algorithmic exposition, explicit convergence/mixing diagnostics, and stronger time-normalized empirical comparisons.
