The authors propose a method to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model's original parameterization onto a new parameter setting that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion for fast mixing. They formulate this projection as a constrained optimization problem, where the constraints ensure that the new parameters are defined over the same space as the original parameters. The authors examine this projection under several distance/divergence measures.

I find the idea of combining principles from stochastic and deterministic inference intriguing, as this area is under-explored. The concept of augmenting the original parameters to improve mixing time is straightforward, but I found the description of the dual of the projection problem somewhat unclear. Specifically, it is unclear how the zij*dij = 0 constraints guarantee that the new parameterization is over the same space as the original parameters, rather than a smaller space. Additionally, the overall procedure is not well-defined: do the authors perform the projected gradient operations to completion and then run a Gibbs sampler, or do they interleave sampling with the gradient updates? An algorithm description box would be helpful in clarifying this process.

Furthermore, it is unclear whether the proposed projected gradient descent strategy is guaranteed to converge. This is an essential consideration, as convergence is crucial for the method's validity.

I also found the experiments somewhat unconvincing. In the first set of experiments, it is unclear why the Gibbs sampler was run for 30,000 iterations. Since the authors are comparing a sampling method to deterministic methods, a comparison based on time would seem more fair. Moreover, the absence of error bars on the charts makes it difficult for the reader to assess the significance of the results. The second set of results, which compare the original/naive Gibbs sampler with Gibbs samplers under different projections, demonstrate that the projection does lead to faster mixing. However, the time required to perform the projection operation is not accounted for in this comparison. For instance, if the projection takes one minute, and the naive Gibbs sampler can generate 10,000 samples in that time, then the projection might not be worthwhile. Finally, it would be beneficial to include a comparison to blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate's "Lifted Gibbs Sampler" to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

Overall, the idea is promising, but the development of the projection problem and the proposed method could benefit from further refinement. A more robust set of experiments is needed to convincingly demonstrate the utility of the method.
