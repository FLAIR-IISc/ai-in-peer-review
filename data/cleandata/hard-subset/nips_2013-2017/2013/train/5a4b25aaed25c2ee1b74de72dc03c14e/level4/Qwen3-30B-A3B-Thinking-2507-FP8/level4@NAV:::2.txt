

The authors introduce a method to accelerate Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the original parameterization onto a new setting that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion for rapid mixing. This projection is formulated as a constrained optimization problem, with constraints ensuring the new parameters remain defined over the same space as the original, and the approach is analyzed under multiple distance/divergence metrics.  

I find the intersection of stochastic and deterministic inference principles particularly promising, as this area remains underexplored. While the core concept of parameter augmentation to enhance mixing is intuitive, the description of the dual projection problem lacks clarity—specifically, how the constraints \(z_{ij} d_{ij} = 0\) guarantee the new parameterization spans the same space (rather than a subset). Additionally, the procedural workflow is ambiguous: are projected gradient updates completed before Gibbs sampling, or are they interleaved? An explicit algorithm box would resolve this. Furthermore, convergence guarantees for the proposed projected gradient descent strategy are not established.  

The experimental evaluation requires strengthening. In the first experiment, the 30K Gibbs iterations seem arbitrary; a time-based comparison against deterministic methods would be more appropriate. The absence of error bars obscures statistical significance. In the second experiment, while projections demonstrably accelerate mixing, the computational cost of the projection step (e.g., 1 minute) is unaccounted for, making it unclear whether the net gain justifies the overhead. Crucially, comparisons to established baselines like blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate’s "Lifted Gibbs Sampler" are missing.  

Overall, the idea is compelling. However, the theoretical development of the projection framework and the experimental validation require significant refinement to substantiate the method’s practical utility.
