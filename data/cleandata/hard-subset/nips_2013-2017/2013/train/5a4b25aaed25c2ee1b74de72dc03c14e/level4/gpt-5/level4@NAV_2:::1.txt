This paper targets the problem of improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong couplings, which are typically slow to mix. The authors propose projecting model parameters onto a “fast-mixing” subset—parameters close to the original but attenuated enough to satisfy a spectral-norm condition that guarantees rapid mixing. They study several divergences for this projection. Empirically, they identify regimes where sampling with the projected parameters yields more accurate marginals within a fixed time budget than both variational baselines (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs sampling on the original model.

The work builds directly on established mixing-time criteria [4,8]. The specific idea of explicitly projecting to the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximation mechanism appears novel to me.

On small Ising instances, the results show encouraging gains over strong baselines. My main reservations concern scalability and scope. The comparisons involve mixing behavior across differently parameterized models, which complicates extrapolation to larger systems. The projection step itself appears computationally heavy and, as far as I can tell, is not included in the time accounting. Finally, extending beyond binary variables seems nontrivial, as analogous, usable mixing bounds are much harder to obtain more generally.

CLARITY:
Overall, the exposition is clear and mostly accessible given the technical content, but several sections would benefit from reorganization or added detail:
- The paper repeatedly appeals to “standard results,” sometimes elevating them to theorem statements (e.g., Theorem 6) without a citation or even a brief proof sketch. Lemma 5 is not proved; the cited reference covers only the zero-field special case.
- Please discuss properties of the dependency matrix R. The definition involves an intractable maximization in general; a direct assessment of tractability and how it motivates Lemma 5 would help.
- The latter part of Sec. 4 is very difficult to parse. Terms like g, M, and Λ are used before being defined, and the subsequent theorem introduces notation not sufficiently explained. If you keep this optimization in the main text, more intuition and connective tissue are needed (potentially trimming restatements of [8] that are not directly used).
- In Sec. 1, KL(q||p) is used for both directions of the KL; the notation should change when the arguments are swapped. Also, the choice of q for the true distribution and p for the approximation is flipped relative to common conventions in the literature.

EXPERIMENTS:
- It appears the wall-clock comparison in Figure 2 excludes the time spent performing the projection. Sec. 6.1 hints at this but is ambiguous; please clarify and, if so, include results that incorporate projection time. As currently presented, the projected methods effectively benefit from an upstream optimization at no cost, which weakens the comparison to standard Gibbs.
- It would be useful to include experiments on larger systems, since Gibbs mixing can degrade with problem size. There are feasible avenues even when junction-tree is infeasible, e.g., models with symmetries enabling exact marginals, or adopting very long runs of a gold-standard sampler as ground truth.
- While KL(θ||ψ) is generally intractable, on the toy instances here junction-tree would suffice. It would be informative to explore this “best-case” projection to contextualize the performance of sampling from approximate models.
- Mean field is an extreme case of the reverse-KL projection (as you note), yet the gap between mean-field error and reverse-KL projection error is substantial. Some analysis to explain this difference would strengthen the narrative.

In summary, projecting slow-mixing models onto the closest fast-mixing models is an appealing idea, and the paper leverages recent mixing-time results in a neat way. However, questions about the experimental comparisons and the narrow range of applicable models remain and should be addressed.
