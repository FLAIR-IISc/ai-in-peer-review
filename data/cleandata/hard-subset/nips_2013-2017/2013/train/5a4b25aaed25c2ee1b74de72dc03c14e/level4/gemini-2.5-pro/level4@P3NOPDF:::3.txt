This paper addresses the challenge of slow inference in Ising models when using Gibbs sampling, a problem caused by poor mixing rates of the underlying Markov chain. The authors propose a novel two-step approach: first, project the given model, which may be slow-mixing, onto a predefined space of fast-mixing models; second, perform inference on this new, fast-mixing projected model.

The space of fast-mixing models is elegantly defined by imposing a bound on the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute edge strengths. The projection operation, which preserves the original graph structure, is defined in terms of minimizing a divergence between the Gibbs distributions of the original and projected models. The paper explores several divergences: Euclidean distance, KL divergence, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergence. For the Euclidean case, the projection is computed by solving a dual problem with LBFGS-B. For the other divergences, a projected gradient algorithm is employed. Notably, computing the projection for the reversed KL divergence requires running Gibbs sampling on the (fast-mixing) projected model itself.

The empirical evaluation is extensive, conducted on small random models where true marginals can be computed. The proposed methods, using various divergences, are compared against standard baselines including Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), Mean Field (MF), and standard Gibbs sampling on the original model. The results are presented in terms of both final accuracy and the accuracy-runtime trade-off. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that the proposed methods consistently achieve higher accuracy than LBP, TRW, and MF. They also outperform standard Gibbs sampling for a reasonable range of runtimes. Among the proposed projection methods, the one based on reversed KL divergence consistently delivers the best performance.

This is an interesting paper with a well-motivated core idea. The approach of projecting a difficult inference problem onto a more tractable one is compelling, and the empirical results appear promising. However, several points require clarification and further consideration.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Clarity of Experimental Plots:** The primary concern lies with the experimental evaluation presented in Figure 2 (and the supplement). The horizontal axis is labeled "number of samples," but sampling is an integral part of only one method (reversed KL divergence). It is unclear how this axis applies to the other projection-based methods or the non-sampling baselines. To properly evaluate the trade-offs, the horizontal axis should represent the absolute wall-clock runtime in seconds. Furthermore, for a fair and comprehensive comparison, the runtimes for the baseline methods (LBP, TRW, MF) must also be reported and ideally integrated into these accuracy-vs-runtime plots. Without this clarification, it is very difficult to interpret the experimental results and substantiate the claims about the practical efficiency of the proposed method. The potential for high runtime is a key concern for the method's practical utility, making this clarification essential.

2.  **Scalability and Model Complexity:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small models. To better demonstrate the method's applicability, it would be valuable to include results on larger or more structured problems. For instance, testing on models from the class described by Flach ("A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function," TPAMI), which permit exact inference in polynomial time, could provide a strong benchmark for assessing scalability and performance on larger instances.

**Minor Comments and Questions:**

1.  **Algorithmic Details:** In Section 5.1, the projected gradient algorithm is described as having two nested loops, with LBFGS-B in the inner loop. Please provide more detail on the stopping criteria for these inner iterations, as this choice could significantly influence the algorithm's overall performance and runtime.

2.  **Line 222:** For precision, please consider changing "onto the tree" to "onto graph T."

3.  **Line 226:** In the context of the optimization problem, would "subgradient" be a more accurate term than "derivative"?

In summary, the paper presents a novel and promising approach for approximate inference. The empirical results are convincing, but their interpretation is hindered by a lack of clarity in the runtime comparisons. Addressing the issues raised, particularly regarding the experimental evaluation, would significantly strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution.
