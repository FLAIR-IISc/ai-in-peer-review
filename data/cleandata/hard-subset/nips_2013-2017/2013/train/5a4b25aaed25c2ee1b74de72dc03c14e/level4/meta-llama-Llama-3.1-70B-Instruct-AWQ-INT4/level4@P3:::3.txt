This paper proposes an innovative approach to inference in high-treewidth graphical models by projecting the input model onto a set of models that guarantee fast mixing. The space of fast-mixing models is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute values of Ising edge strengths. The authors explore various divergences, including Euclidean distance, KL-divergence, piecewise KL-divergence, and reversed KL-divergence, to perform this projection while preserving the graph structure.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the problem, the proposed approach, and the experimental evaluation. The use of different divergences for projection is a key contribution of the paper, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of these approaches through extensive experiments on small random models.

The experimental evaluation compares the proposed methods with loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The results show that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and for a reasonable range of runtimes, also Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed methods, the one with reversed KL-divergence performs consistently best.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to inference in high-treewidth graphical models by projecting the input model onto a set of models that guarantee fast mixing.
* The authors explore various divergences for projection, including Euclidean distance, KL-divergence, piecewise KL-divergence, and reversed KL-divergence.
* The experimental evaluation is extensive and compares the proposed methods with several state-of-the-art algorithms.

Weaknesses:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the projected gradient algorithm, including the stopping criteria for the inner iterations.
* The plots in Figure 2 and the supplement are not clearly labeled, and it is not immediately apparent what the horizontal axis represents. The authors should clarify this and consider reporting the runtime of the algorithms in seconds.
* The paper could benefit from experimental comparisons with larger models, such as those proposed in [Boris Flach: A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function].

Suggestions for improvement:

* Provide more details on the projected gradient algorithm, including the stopping criteria for the inner iterations.
* Clarify the labeling of the plots in Figure 2 and the supplement, and consider reporting the runtime of the algorithms in seconds.
