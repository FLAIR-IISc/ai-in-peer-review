This paper aims to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, which are known to be “slow-mixing.” The authors propose several projections to “fast-mixing” models; essentially, they identify parameters that are as close as possible to the original model, but weak enough to satisfy a spectral bound guaranteeing rapid mixing. Experiments demonstrate that, in certain regimes, this approach leads to improved marginal estimates for a given computation time compared to variational methods (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

The technical content builds heavily on prior work establishing conditions for rapid mixing [4, 8]. However, the idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method appears to be novel.

On small “toy” Ising models, the results show promising improvements over a strong set of baselines, including standard sampling and contemporary variational methods. My main concern is that the results’ scalability to larger models warrants significant skepticism. The experiments compare mixing times of samplers on different models, and it is difficult to extrapolate these findings to larger problem sizes. Moreover, a computationally expensive projection step is required to construct the fast-mixing model, and its cost appears to be unaccounted for. Finally, generalization beyond binary states is not clear, as establishing convergence bounds more generally is considerably more challenging.

**CLARITY:**

In general, the presentation is clear and reasonably accessible, given the technical complexity. However, some reorganization and clarification are needed:

*   The paper frequently refers to “standard results” and even states them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without providing references or even proof sketches. Lemma 5 is presented without proof; the cited reference proves the result only for the special case of a zero-field.
*   The properties of the dependency matrix *R* should be discussed. It does not appear to be tractable due to the maximization involved; please state whether this is the case. (I presume this is why Lemma 5 is invoked.)
*   The second half of Section 4 is difficult to follow. Before Theorem 7, the text references *g*, *M*, and *Λ* before they are defined. Additionally, the statement of Theorem 4 includes notation that is not adequately explained. If this optimization is to be discussed, more explanation is needed. (Perhaps less space could be devoted to restating results from [8] that are not central to the current work.)
*   In Section 1, *KL(q||p)* is used to represent both directions of the KL divergence, necessitating a shift in notation for one direction. Furthermore, in the first paragraph, *q* is used to denote the true distribution and *p* the tractable approximation – the reverse of the convention in almost all related literature.

**EXPERIMENTS:**

The time comparison in Figure 2 does not appear to include the computation required for the projection step. A somewhat ambiguous statement to this effect appears in Section 6.1, but it is unclear; please clarify and, if applicable, present results with the projection time included. As it currently stands, the proposed methods effectively benefit from the output of a sophisticated variational optimization without incurring a computational penalty, which diminishes the significance of the improvement over standard Gibbs sampling.

It is disappointing that the experiments do not include larger models, given that Gibbs mixing times often depend on problem size. There are several viable options for conducting experiments in regimes where junction tree inference is intractable, such as utilizing models where symmetries allow for the computation of true marginals, or treating the output of a very long sampling run as the ground truth.

Although computing *KL(θ||ψ)* is generally intractable, exploring it here as a potential “best-case” scenario for the performance of sampling in an approximate model would be valuable. (Junction tree inference could be used for the toy models used in the submission.)

The paper points out that mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection. However, there is a substantial difference between the error associated with mean field and the error resulting from the reverse KL projection. This discrepancy deserves discussion.

In conclusion, the idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is a compelling one, and the authors leverage recent work on mixing bounds in an elegant manner. However, there are concerns regarding the experimental comparisons and the limited range of models to which this approach is potentially applicable.
