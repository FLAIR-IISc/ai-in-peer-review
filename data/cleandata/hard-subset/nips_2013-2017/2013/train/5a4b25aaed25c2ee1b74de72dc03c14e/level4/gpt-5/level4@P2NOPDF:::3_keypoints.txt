- Paper proposes a two-step approach: project a slow-mixing Ising model onto a set of fast-mixing models, then perform inference on the projected model.
- Fast-mixing models are defined by a spectral-norm bound on the matrix of absolute Ising edge coefficients, with projection constrained to preserve the original graph structure.
- Projection is formulated using divergences between Gibbs distributions; Euclidean projection is solved via a dualized L-BFGS-B, other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, reverse KL) use projected-gradient.
- Reverse KL projection requires Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model to estimate expectations during optimization.
- Experiments on small random graphs compare proposed projections to loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original model, evaluating accuracy and accuracy-versus-runtime.
- Results: proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy; within reasonable runtimes they also outperform Gibbs on the original model; reverse KL performs best among variants.
- Request for algorithmic details: specify inner L-BFGS-B stopping conditions (tolerances, max iters, gradient norms) and outer projected-gradient stopping criteria.
- Ask how often the spectral-norm feasibility projection is applied, with what tolerance, and how feasibility is enforced after each outer step (line-search/step-size schedule).
- Clarify the runtime axis in figures: replace “number of samples” with wall-clock runtime; report absolute running times (seconds) for all algorithms and hardware/implementation details (threads, thresholds).
- Explain how sampling budgets map to runtime for reverse KL and compare those costs to other projections and baselines.
- Recommend adding experiments on larger models (e.g., models from Boris Flach) that permit polynomial-time inference to provide reliable ground truth at scale.
- Interpretation concern: without clean runtime-based comparisons across all baselines, practical trade-offs and when the approach beats well-tuned Gibbs are unclear.
- Minor edits: change “onto the tree” to “onto graph T” (Line 222) and use “subgradient” instead of “derivative” (Line 226).
- Overall assessment: well-motivated and principled projection method with convincing small-graph results (reverse KL strong), but main concern is practical runtime—need comprehensive runtime measurements, clearer stopping criteria, and larger-scale experiments to demonstrate applicability.
