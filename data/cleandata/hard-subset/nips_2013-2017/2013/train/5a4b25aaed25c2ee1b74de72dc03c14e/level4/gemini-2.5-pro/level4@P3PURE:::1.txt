### Review

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper aims to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, which are known to be slow-mixing. The proposed approach is to project the original model's parameters onto the set of "fast-mixing" models, identifying the closest parameterization that satisfies a spectral bound guaranteeing rapid mixing. Experiments demonstrate that for certain regimes, this method yields improved marginal estimates for a given computation time compared to standard Gibbs sampling on the original model and other variational methods like mean field and belief propagation.

While the technical content builds heavily on prior results establishing conditions for rapid mixing [4, 8], the core idea of using a projection onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as a form of approximate inference appears to be novel.

**Critique**

Despite the promising results on small models, there are significant concerns about the method's scalability, the fairness of the experimental comparison, and its generalizability. My main concerns are as follows:

1.  **Scalability:** The experiments are confined to small models, yet it is difficult to predict how the relative mixing times of samplers on the original and projected models will scale with the problem size. There should be healthy skepticism about whether these results will extend to larger, more realistic models.

2.  **Computational Cost of Projection:** The method requires a projection step to find the fast-mixing model, which appears to be computationally expensive. The cost of this optimization step is not accounted for in the experimental comparisons, which is a major flaw in the evaluation.

3.  **Generalizability:** The paper's applicability beyond binary Ising models is questionable, as establishing the necessary convergence bounds for more general state spaces is a significantly harder problem.

**Clarity**

The presentation is generally clear, but several sections require reorganization and clarification:

*   The authors frequently refer to "standard results," even presenting them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6), without providing citations or proof sketches. Similarly, Lemma 5 is presented without proof, and the cited source only proves it for the special case of a zero external field.
*   The properties of the dependency matrix R require further discussion. The maximization in its definition suggests it may be intractable to compute; the paper should clarify this and explain if this intractability is the motivation for invoking Lemma 5.
*   The second half of Section 4 is very difficult to follow. Before Theorem 7, the text references variables (g, M, Lambda) that have not yet been introduced. The statement of Theorem 7 then includes notation that is not adequately explained. This section on the optimization procedure needs substantial revision to be understandable. The authors might consider reducing the space dedicated to restating results from [8] in favor of a clearer explanation of their own method.
*   There is notational confusion in Section 1 regarding the KL-divergence. The notation KL(q||p) appears to be used for both directions of the divergence. Furthermore, the convention used in the first paragraph—where q represents the true distribution and p the approximation—is the reverse of the standard convention in the vast majority of related literature, which may cause confusion.

**Suggestions for Experiments**

*   The timing comparisons in Figure 2 seem to exclude the computational cost of the projection step. A statement in Section 6.1 appears to allude to this but is ambiguous. The authors must clarify this point. If the projection time is indeed excluded, the experiments should be re-run to include it. As presented, the proposed method benefits from a potentially expensive optimization step "for free," which undermines the claimed performance improvements over standard Gibbs sampling.
*   A significant weakness of the experimental evaluation is the restriction to small models. Since Gibbs mixing times are highly dependent on problem size, demonstrating performance on larger-scale problems is crucial. The authors could have explored models where the junction tree algorithm is intractable, for example, by using models with symmetries that allow for exact marginal computation or by using the output of a very long MCMC run as a ground-truth reference.
*   While minimizing KL(θ||ψ) is intractable in general, it would be informative to include it as an oracle or "best-case" baseline for the small models evaluated in the paper, since the exact value can be computed using the junction tree algorithm. This would help quantify the performance gap between the proposed projection and an ideal projection.
*   The paper notes that mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection. However, the experimental results show a large performance gap between the two. This significant difference warrants further discussion and analysis.

**Conclusion**

In summary, the core idea of projecting a slow-mixing model onto the nearest fast-mixing one for approximate inference is novel and elegant, and it effectively leverages recent theoretical work on mixing time bounds. However, the paper is weakened by several issues. The experimental evaluation is not entirely convincing due to the exclusion of projection time costs and the focus on small-scale problems. Furthermore, significant concerns remain about the method's scalability, its computational cost, and its potential for generalization beyond binary models. The presentation also requires clarification in several key sections.
