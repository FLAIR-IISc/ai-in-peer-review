- Paper proposes projecting an Ising-model MRF onto a set of fast-mixing models (spectral-norm constraint on matrix of absolute edge strengths) and performing inference on the projected model.  
- Projection is defined via minimizing divergences between Gibbs distributions while preserving graph structure.  
- Euclidean projection is solved by dualizing and using LBFGS-B; other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL) use a projected gradient algorithm.  
- Reversed KL projection requires running Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model to compute the projection.  
- Experiments on small random models compare proposed methods (all divergences) against LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original model, evaluating accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy.  
- Results show the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and beat original-model Gibbs for a reasonable runtime range; reversed KL performs best among proposed variants.  
- Reviewer asks for the stopping criteria of the inner LBFGS-B iterations in the projected gradient algorithm (Section 5.1).  
- Reviewer notes Figure 2’s horizontal axis labeled "number of samples" is likely incorrect (should be runtime) and requests runtimes for LBP, TRW, MF plus absolute runtimes in seconds for fair comparisons.  
- Reviewer suggests evaluating on larger tractable models (e.g., Flach’s random-field class) to strengthen experimental validation.  
- Minor edits: change "onto the tree" to "onto graph T" (Line 222) and consider "subgradient" instead of "derivative" (Line 226).  
- Overall assessment: interesting paper with convincing empirical results but practical utility may be limited by high runtime—needs clarification in rebuttal.
