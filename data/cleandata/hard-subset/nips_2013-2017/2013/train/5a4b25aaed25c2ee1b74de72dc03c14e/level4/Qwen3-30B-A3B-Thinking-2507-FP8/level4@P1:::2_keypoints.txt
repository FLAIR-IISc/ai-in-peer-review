- Novel approach: project Ising-model parameters onto a spectral-norm-constrained set to guarantee rapid mixing (Theorem 4).  
- Main contribution: unified projection framework under multiple divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL) with theoretical guarantees and experiments.  
- Conceptual advance: shifts "tractability" from structural approximations to parameter-space conditions ensuring fast mixing.  
- Clarification needed: constraint Z_ij D_ij = 0 enforces zeroing non-edges to preserve original graph structure—should be explicitly noted.  
- Algorithmic ambiguity: unclear whether gradient updates are completed before sampling or interleaved; a pseudocode box is needed for reproducibility.  
- Convergence concerns: require explicit discussion of convergence guarantees for projected gradient descent under the stochastic approximation (Eq. 8) and sample dependence in the pool.  
- Experimental claim: projected parameters show faster finite-time convergence and marginally better accuracy than variational baselines (LBP, TRW, MF).  
- Time-normalization missing: projection costs (e.g., ~30k Gibbs steps) aren’t accounted for, so total computational effort comparisons are needed to validate "faster mixing."  
- Statistical rigor missing: Figures 1–2 lack error bars or confidence intervals, preventing assessment of significance.  
- Missing baselines: blocked Gibbs and Gogate’s "Lifted Gibbs" are omitted, weakening benchmarking of mixing improvements.  
- Spectral bound looseness: authors note conservativeness (e.g., 2D Ising) but use ||R||_2 ≤ 2.5 in experiments without discussing practical impact.  
- Practical strengths: method is simple (e.g., Euclidean projection via SVD thresholding), broadly applicable to MRFs, and reversed KL is well-motivated for gradient estimation.  
- Summary weaknesses: unclear algorithmic procedure, incomplete experimental accounting, missing statistical validation and baselines, and underexplored spectral-bound implications.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept contingent on clearer algorithm description, time-normalized experiments, inclusion of relevant baselines, and rigorous statistical reporting.
