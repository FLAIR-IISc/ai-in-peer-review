- Proposes projecting a slow-mixing pairwise Ising model onto the nearest fast-mixing model to improve Gibbs sampling mixing rates.
- Builds on prior spectral bounds for rapid mixing; the projection-as-approximate-inference idea is novel.
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, with thorough background on mixing times and Ising models.
- Experiments show improved marginal estimates for a fixed computation time compared to variational methods and original-model Gibbs sampling.
- Concern: scalability to larger models is unclear from the experiments.
- Concern: the computational cost of the projection step is not included in timing comparisons.
- Concern: unclear whether the approach generalizes beyond binary-state models due to harder convergence bounds.
- Suggestion: discuss properties of the dependency matrix R in more detail and provide or cite the proof of Lemma 5.
- Suggestion: reorganize and clarify the dense second half of Section 4 with more explanations and notation.
- Experiment suggestion: include projection computation time in time comparisons.
- Experiment suggestion: evaluate on larger models or symmetric models where true marginals are tractable to assess scalability.
- Experiment suggestion: explore "zero-avoiding" KL-divergence as a potential best-case sampling objective for approximate models.
- Strength: novel and elegant method for approximating slow-mixing models with fast-mixing projections.
- Strength: technically sound development leveraging recent work on mixing bounds.
- Strength: empirical results support the method’s effectiveness for marginal estimation.
- Weakness: limited range of applicable models demonstrated in the paper.
- Weakness: projection step may be computationally expensive, weakening practical gains.
- Verdict split: arguments for acceptance emphasize novelty and effective experiments; arguments against emphasize scalability, missing computation accounting, and need for clarification.
