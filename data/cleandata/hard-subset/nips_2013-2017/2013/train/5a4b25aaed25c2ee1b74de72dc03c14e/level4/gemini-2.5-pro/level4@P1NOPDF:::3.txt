This paper addresses the challenge of inference in Ising models where Gibbs sampling may suffer from a slow mixing rate. The success of inference via Gibbs sampling in Markov Random Fields (MRFs), specifically the two-label Ising models considered here, is highly dependent on the mixing rate of the underlying Markov chain. This paper proposes a novel two-step approach to inference: 1) Project the original model, which may be slow-mixing, onto a set of models guaranteed to mix rapidly. 2) Perform inference on this resulting fast-mixing projection.

The authors define the space of fast-mixing models by imposing a bound on the spectral norm of the matrix formed by the absolute values of the Ising model's edge weights. The "projection" is defined in terms of minimizing a divergence between the Gibbs distributions of the original and projected models, while preserving the original graph structure. For the Euclidean distance, this projection is computed by dualizing the optimization problem and solving it with LBFGS-B. For other divergences—namely KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL—a projected gradient algorithm is employed. Notably, computing the projection for the reverse KL divergence requires Gibbs sampling, but this is performed on the fast-mixing model, not the original one.

The authors present extensive experiments on small, random models, comparing the marginals approximated by their method with the ground-truth marginals. The proposed approach (using all mentioned divergences) is benchmarked against several baselines: loopy belief propagation (LBP), tree-reweighted belief propagation (TRW), mean field (MF), and standard Gibbs sampling on the original model. The evaluation considers not only final accuracy but also the trade-off between runtime and accuracy. The experimental results indicate that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in terms of accuracy. Furthermore, they also outperform standard Gibbs sampling on the original model within a reasonable range of runtimes. Among the proposed projection variants, the one based on reverse KL divergence consistently achieves the best performance.

This is an interesting paper with convincing empirical results.

### Comments

1.  **Algorithm Details:** The projected gradient algorithm described in Section 5.1 appears to have two nested loops, with LBFGS-B forming the inner loop. Please provide details on the stopping criteria for these inner iterations.

2.  **Experimental Clarity:** The interpretation of the experimental results in Figure 2 (and the supplement) is unclear. The horizontal axis is labeled "number of samples," but sampling is only used for the reverse KL divergence variant. For a fair comparison across methods that do not use sampling, this axis should likely represent the total runtime. Additionally, the runtimes for the LBP, TRW, and MF baselines are not reported, which prevents a comprehensive accuracy-vs-runtime comparison across all methods. Please clarify the meaning of the horizontal axis and provide absolute runtimes (in seconds) for all algorithms to allow for a proper evaluation of the results. Without this clarification, it is difficult to interpret the experiments.

3.  **Model Scale:** The experiments are currently limited to small models. It would strengthen the paper to include a comparison on larger-scale problems. For instance, the models from Flach et al. ("A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function," TPAMI) could be a suitable choice, as they permit exact inference in polynomial time, providing a valuable benchmark for larger graphs.

4.  **Practicality and Runtime:** The practical utility of the proposed methods may be limited by their high runtime. This concern regarding computational cost needs to be addressed in the rebuttal, ideally in conjunction with the clarifications requested for the experimental plots.
