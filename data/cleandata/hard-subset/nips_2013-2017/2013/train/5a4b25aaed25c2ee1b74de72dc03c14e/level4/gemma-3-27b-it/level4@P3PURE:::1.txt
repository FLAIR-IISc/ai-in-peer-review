This paper aims to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, which are known to be “slow-mixing.” The authors propose several projections to “fast-mixing” models; essentially, they identify parameters as close as possible to the original model, but weak enough to satisfy a spectral bound establishing rapid mixing. Experiments demonstrate that, in certain regimes, this approach leads to improved marginal estimates for a given computation time, compared to both variational methods (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

The technical content builds heavily on prior results establishing conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]. However, the idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method appears novel. On the domain of “toy tiny Ising models,” the results offer promising improvements over a strong set of baselines including standard sampling and contemporary variational methods. My primary concern is the potential lack of scalability to larger models. Experiments involve comparisons of mixing times on different models, making it difficult to extrapolate to larger problem sizes. Moreover, the computationally expensive projection step required to construct the fast-mixing model appears unaddressed in the reported timings. Finally, the approach’s generalizability beyond binary states is questionable, as establishing convergence bounds in more general settings is significantly more challenging. 

**CLARITY:**

In general, the presentation is clear and reasonably accessible, given the technical complexity. However, reorganization and clarification are needed in several areas:

* The paper refers frequently to “standard results” and presents them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without providing references or even proof sketches. Lemma 5 is stated without proof, and the cited reference only proves the result for a special case of zero-field.
* The properties of the dependency matrix R should be discussed. Specifically, the authors should state whether it is tractable, given the maximization involved in its construction. (I presume this is why Lemma 5 is invoked.)
* The second half of Sec. 4 is difficult to follow. Before Theorem 7, the text references *g*, *M*, and *Λ* before they are defined. Additionally, the statement of Theorem 4 includes notation that is not adequately explained. If this optimization is to be discussed, further elaboration is necessary. (Perhaps some space could be saved by reducing the restatement of results from [8] which are not centrally used.)
* In Sec. 1, *KL(q||p)* is used to denote both forward and reverse KL divergence, necessitating a shift in notation. The initial paragraph uses *q* for the true distribution and *p* for the tractable approximation, which is inconsistent with nearly all related literature.

**EXPERIMENTS:**

The time comparison in Figure 2 does not appear to include the computation required for the projection step. A somewhat ambiguous statement to this effect appears in Sec 6.1; please clarify this point and, if projection time is excluded, present results that include it. As it stands, the proposed methods seem to benefit from the output of a sophisticated variational optimization without incurring a computational penalty, which diminishes the significance of the improvement over standard Gibbs sampling. 

The lack of experiments on larger models is disappointing, particularly given that Gibbs mixing times often depend on problem size. Opportunities exist to conduct experiments in regimes where junction tree inference is intractable, such as utilizing models with symmetries that allow for computation of the true marginals, or treating the output of a very long sampling run as the ground truth.

While I understand that *KL(θ||ψ)* is generally intractable, exploring it here as a potential “best case” for sampling in an approximate model would be insightful. (Junction tree could be employed for the toy models used in the submission.)

The paper points out that mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection, yet there is a substantial difference between mean field error and the error resulting from the reverse KL projection. This discrepancy warrants further discussion.

In summary, the idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is a compelling one, and recent work on mixing bounds is leveraged effectively. However, concerns regarding experimental comparisons and the limited range of models to which this approach may be applicable remain.
