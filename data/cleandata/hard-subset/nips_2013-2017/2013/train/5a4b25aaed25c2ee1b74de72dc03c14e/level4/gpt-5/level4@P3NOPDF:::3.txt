Summary
The paper addresses the challenge of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for binary MRFs (Ising models). The key idea is to project a potentially slow-mixing model onto a set of fast-mixing models—defined by a spectral-norm bound on the matrix of absolute edge strengths—while preserving the graph structure, and then perform inference on the projected model. The projection is formulated as minimizing a divergence between the original and projected Gibbs distributions, under the fast-mixing constraint. The authors implement projections for several divergences: Euclidean (via dualization and L-BFGS-B), KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL (the latter requiring sampling from the projected, fast-mixing model). Experiments on small random graphs compare marginal accuracy to ground truth and include a runtime–accuracy analysis. The proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and for reasonable time budgets also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed objectives, reverse KL performs best.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation: constraining to a certified fast-mixing set via a spectral-norm bound is elegant and provides a concrete, verifiable guarantee.
- Methodological breadth: multiple divergences are considered, with appropriate optimization methods (dualization + L-BFGS-B for Euclidean, projected gradient for the others).
- Empirical results: on the tested instances, the approach improves accuracy over strong baselines and often dominates Gibbs on the original model for comparable runtimes.
- Practical insight: preserving the graph structure is appealing for applications where sparsity or topology is intrinsic.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Runtime accounting and axes in figures: The x-axis in Figure 2 and the supplement is labeled “number of samples,” but sampling is only used for reverse KL. If the comparison is intended to be runtime–accuracy, please report absolute wall-clock time (in seconds) on a fixed hardware/software stack for all methods, including LBP, TRW, and MF. Without this, it is difficult to interpret the efficiency comparisons or to assess trade-offs fairly across algorithms with very different iteration costs.
- Projected gradient details (Section 5.1): The algorithm has two nested loops, with an inner L-BFGS-B solve. Please specify:
  - Stopping criteria and tolerances for the inner L-BFGS-B (e.g., gradient norm threshold, relative objective change, max iterations).
  - How feasibility with respect to the spectral-norm constraint is enforced and checked numerically (projection operator details and accuracy).
  - Step-size/line-search strategy for the outer loop, and any safeguards for non-smooth objectives (for piecewise KL).
- Reverse KL projection via sampling: Provide sampler parameters (burn-in, thinning, total samples), diagnostics (e.g., multiple chains, autocorrelation/effective sample size), and how Monte Carlo variance affects convergence of the outer optimization. Since the efficacy of reverse KL hinges on accurate sampling from the projected model, reproducible settings are essential.
- Scope and scalability: Experiments are limited to small random graphs. To assess practical impact, please include larger models. A useful option is the class of complete-graph models with tractable partition functions (B. Flach, “A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function,” TPAMI, in press; online) to enable comparisons with exact marginals at larger scales. Grid graphs or sparse real-world networks would also be informative.
- Conservativeness and parameter selection: The spectral-norm bound that guarantees fast mixing can be conservative. Please discuss how the bound is chosen in practice, sensitivity of results to this choice, and whether adaptive/continuation strategies are used. Clarify whether node biases are constrained and how they affect the mixing certificate.
- Impact of structure preservation: While preserving zero-patterns is desirable, it may limit the quality of the projection under tight mixing constraints. A brief discussion of this trade-off and any empirical evidence (e.g., how much edge weights are altered) would help.

Empirical evaluation
- Please report:
  - Absolute runtime in seconds for all methods (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs on original, and all projection variants).
  - The computational breakdown for the projection methods (time in projection vs. subsequent inference).
  - Variance bars or confidence intervals when sampling is involved (reverse KL), and multiple random seeds for robustness.
- Clarify the x-axis semantics across plots; if “number of samples” only applies to reverse KL, provide a separate runtime axis or convert all curves to runtime.

Minor comments and typos
- Line 222: replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T.”
- Line 226: “subgradient” is more appropriate than “derivative” when the objective is non-smooth.
- Grammar and style: a light edit for consistency (e.g., capitalization of algorithm names, uniform notation for divergences) would improve readability.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and well-motivated paper with a principled approach and convincing empirical accuracy gains on the tested problems. The idea of projecting into a certified fast-mixing set is novel and potentially impactful. However, the practical utility currently hinges on computational cost: the nested optimization (especially for reverse KL) may be expensive, and the paper does not yet present clear, fair runtime comparisons across all baselines. If the rebuttal clarifies runtime reporting (with absolute times for all methods), details the optimization/stopping criteria, and adds or commits to more scalable experiments (e.g., using the Flach models or larger graphs with exact or high-quality references), I would view the contribution as significantly strengthened.
