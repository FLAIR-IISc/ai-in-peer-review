- Positive: Novel, compelling idea to use deterministic projection onto a Dobrushin-satisfying parameter space to accelerate Gibbs sampling—an underexplored intersection of inference paradigms.

- Ambiguity in projection formulation: dual problem description is unclear, especially the role and interpretation of the constraint z_{ij} d_{ij} = 0.

- Mathematical concern: authors must prove the constraint preserves the model support and does not effectively eliminate edges (e.g., when d_{ij}=0).

- Algorithmic ambiguity: unclear whether projected gradient updates are done as preprocessing or interleaved with Gibbs; a concise Algorithm 1/pseudocode is needed.

- Experimental flaw: comparisons use iteration counts (e.g., Gibbs 30k iters) rather than wall-clock time, which is misleading given differing per-iteration costs.

- Missing uncertainty quantification: figures lack error bars, preventing assessment of statistical significance.

- Omitted time cost: projection computation time is not reported, so claimed faster mixing may not translate to net runtime gains.

- Incomplete baselines: important alternatives like blocked Gibbs and Gogate et al.'s Lifted Gibbs sampler are not compared, weakening claims of advantage.

- Theoretical gap: no convergence guarantees or rate bounds for the non-convex constrained projected gradient descent are provided.

- Recommendations: provide formal proof of constraint effects, clear pseudocode, time-based experiments with error bars and additional baselines, and convergence analysis to substantiate the method.

- Overall: promising idea but current technical clarity, theoretical support, and experimental validation are insufficient to demonstrate superiority over existing approaches.
