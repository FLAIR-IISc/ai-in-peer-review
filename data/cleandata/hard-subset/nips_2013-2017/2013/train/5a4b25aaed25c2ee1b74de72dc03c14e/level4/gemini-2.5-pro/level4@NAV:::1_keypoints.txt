- Tackles slow-mixing in Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions by projecting parameters to the nearest fast-mixing model.  
- Fast-mixing model defined via a spectral bound ensuring rapid mixing; projection minimizes distance subject to that bound.  
- Empirical results on toy models show improved marginal estimates within a fixed computational budget versus variational methods and standard Gibbs.  
- Core technical foundation builds on prior rapid-mixing conditions ([4,8]), but using projection onto a rapidly mixing model for approximate inference appears novel.  
- Main concern: unclear if toy-scale improvements will extrapolate to larger-scale problems where mixing behavior changes with size.  
- Projection step may be computationally expensive, and its runtime appears excluded from performance comparisons.  
- Generalization beyond binary-state (Ising) models is unclear because analogous convergence bounds are harder to establish.  
- Clarity issues: several "standard results" are presented as theorems without citations or proof sketches (e.g., Theorem 6); Lemma 5 lacks proof for the general case.  
- The properties and computational tractability of the dependency matrix R need discussion, since its embedded maximization may be intractable and motivates Lemma 5.  
- Section 4 is hard to follow: symbols (g, M, Lambda) referenced before introduction and Theorem 4 uses unexplained notation; needs reorganization and clearer exposition.  
- Notation/confusion: KL(q||p) is used inconsistently and the q/p convention in Section 1 is opposite common literature practice.  
- Experiments: timing comparisons (Figure 2) likely omit projection cost—this must be clarified or results re-presented with projection time included.  
- Experiments should include larger models or alternative ground-truth strategies (symmetries, long runs) since junction tree is intractable for large cases.  
- Suggest computing KL(θ||ψ) on toy models via junction tree as a best-case benchmark, and discuss why mean-field (reverse KL) performs much worse than the reverse-KL projection used.  
- Overall: idea is innovative and leverages recent mixing bounds, but significant concerns remain about experimental fairness, scalability, and applicability beyond binary Ising models.
