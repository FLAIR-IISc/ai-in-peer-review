This paper proposes a novel approach to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions. The core idea is to project the original model parameters onto a set of parameters that guarantee fast mixing, under several divergences. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem, building on prior results establishing conditions for rapid mixing. They also present experimental results showing some regimes where this approach leads to improved marginal estimates for a given computation time, compared to variational methods and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

The technical content of the paper is sound, and the idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method is innovative. However, there are some concerns regarding the scalability of the approach to larger models. The experiments involve comparisons of mixing times of samplers on different models, which are difficult to scale with problem size. Additionally, the computationally expensive projection step required to build the fast-mixing model is not accounted for in the results.

The presentation of the paper is generally clear, but there are some areas that require reorganization and clarification. For instance, the paper refers to "standard results" without providing references or proof sketches. The properties of the dependency matrix R are not thoroughly discussed, and the second half of Section 4 is difficult to follow due to the introduction of new notation without sufficient explanation.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, but there are some limitations. The time comparison in Figure 2 does not include the computation required for projection, which is unclear and should be clarified. The paper would benefit from experiments on larger models to demonstrate the scalability of the approach. Furthermore, exploring the "zero-avoiding" KL-divergence as a potential "best case" for sampling in an approximate model would be interesting.

The difference in error between mean field and the reverse KL projection deserves discussion, as mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection. Overall, the idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is elegant, but there are concerns about experimental comparisons and the limited range of models to which this approach is potentially applicable.

Strengths:

* Innovative approach to improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models
* Thorough analysis of the problem, building on prior results establishing conditions for rapid mixing
* Experimental results showing improved marginal estimates for a given computation time

Weaknesses:

* Concerns about scalability to larger models
* Computationally expensive projection step not accounted for in results
* Limited experimental evaluation, with no experiments on larger models
* Unclear time comparison in Figure 2
* Limited discussion of the properties of the dependency matrix R
* Difficulty in following the second half of Section 4 due to new notation

Arguments for acceptance:

* Innovative approach to improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling
* Thorough analysis of the problem
* Experimental results showing improved marginal estimates

Arguments against acceptance:

* Concerns about scalability to larger models
* Limited experimental evaluation
* Unclear time comparison in Figure 2
* Difficulty in following certain sections of the paper

Recommendation:

* Accept the paper, but require the authors to address the concerns about scalability, experimental evaluation, and clarity of presentation.
* Request additional experiments on larger models to demonstrate the scalability of the approach.
* Ask the authors to clarify the time comparison in Figure 2 and provide results with projection time included.
* Suggest reorganizing and clarifying certain sections of the paper, particularly the discussion of the dependency matrix R and the second half of Section 4.
