- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing family defined by a spectral-norm constraint and running Gibbs on the projection.  
- Uses spectral bound ||R||2 < 1 (with Rij ≤ tanh|βij|) as a sufficient condition; enforces constraint on the absolute weight matrix.  
- Studies four divergences for projection: Euclidean (dual formulation), KL(θ||ψ) (impractical), piecewise KL (max over tractable subgraphs), and reverse KL (optimized with projected stochastic gradients).  
- Dual Euclidean projection preserving sparsity via iterative SVD thresholding and LBFGS-B is well-motivated and appears correct at a high level.  
- Theoretical grounding uses standard mixing-time bounds (Dobrushin/Levin–Peres–Wilmer/Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum) and the Rij ≤ tanh|βij| bound.  
- Technical corrections needed: Theorem 6 SVD threshold constant (should be c not c2) must be clarified.  
- Clarify convention for total variation distance (factor 1/2) and state it consistently.  
- Piecewise KL objective is non-smooth (max over subgraphs); optimization should use subgradients and this must be clarified.  
- Paper proves guarantees for random-scan Gibbs (Theorem 4) but experiments use systematic scan; justify applicability or discuss differences.  
- Missing implementation details: stopping criteria, tolerances, and max iterations for inner LBFGS-B and interaction with outer projection loop.  
- Figures ambiguous: x-axis labeled “number of samples” but sampling used only for reverse-KL; recommend reporting wall-clock times and including runtimes for LBP/TRW/MF.  
- Several minor edits and typos (e.g., “onto graph T”, use “subgradient”, fix “KL-divergnce”).  
- Novelty: projecting onto a spectral-norm-defined fast-mixing family while preserving sparsity is a new variational idea distinct from graph-structured families.  
- Contributions: dual Euclidean projection with sparsity and reverse-KL with projected stochastic gradients are interesting and practical.  
- Empirical finding: reverse-KL projection gives consistently more accurate marginals than LBP/TRW/MF and outperforms Gibbs on the original model under time budgets.  
- Experiments are limited to small grids and random graphs (8×8); scalability and broader applicability (beyond Ising) are untested.  
- Concern: authors sometimes use spectral bound 2.5 (exceeding theoretical guarantee of <1); this undermines guarantees and needs empirical mixing diagnostics.  
- Missing empirical reporting: full runtime accounting (including projection cost), sensitivity to hyperparameters, and larger-scale experiments.  
- Suggest reporting effective sample sizes or autocorrelations to substantiate “fast-mixing” in practice, especially when using bounds >1.  
- Pros: clear idea, solid theory, elegant projection algorithm, and strong empirical performance of reverse-KL.  
- Cons: incomplete runtime reporting, use of unjustified spectral bounds, limited scale, unclear scalability of inner projection, and several technical clarifications needed.  
- Questions for authors: LBFGS-B termination criteria and sensitivity; exact x-axis in figures and wall-clock times for all methods; justification and diagnostics for using spectral norm 2.5; scaling with graph size/degree and inclusion of larger or tractable-ground-truth models.  
- Recommendation: lean toward acceptance pending clarifications on runtime reporting, spectral-bound discrepancy, and additional mixing/efficiency evidence.
