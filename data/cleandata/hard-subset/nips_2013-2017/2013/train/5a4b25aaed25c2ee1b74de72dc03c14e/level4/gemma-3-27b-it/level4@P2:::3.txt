This paper addresses the challenge of slow mixing in Ising models, which hinders efficient inference. The core idea is to project model parameters onto a space guaranteed to have faster mixing properties. This is achieved by bounding the spectral norm of the interaction matrix and defining a projection based on various divergences, including Euclidean distance, KL-divergence, piecewise KL-divergence, and a reversed KL-divergence. The authors develop algorithms for performing this projection, leveraging techniques like singular value decomposition and stochastic gradient descent. Extensive experiments on small random Ising models demonstrate that the proposed methods generally outperform loopy belief propagation, tree-reweighted belief propagation, and naive mean field, and can achieve comparable or better accuracy than Gibbs sampling with the original parameters, especially when limited computational resources are available.

The paper builds upon the well-established literature on inference in graphical models, noting the difficulties associated with high treewidth (e.g., [14, 16, 20, 21, 19, 6]). It positions its approach as an alternative to methods that approximate by restricting the model structure (e.g., mean field) and instead focuses on manipulating the parameter space to ensure desirable mixing properties. The use of spectral norm bounds for fast mixing connects to prior work on Markov chain mixing times [10, 4, 8, 11]. The exploration of different divergence measures for projection is also a valuable contribution, building upon existing divergence-based inference techniques [12, 17, 15].

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Approach:** The idea of projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing space is a fresh perspective on improving MCMC inference.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper grounds its approach in established results from Markov chain theory, specifically relating spectral norm to mixing time [8].
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation compares the proposed methods against a range of relevant baselines, including both variational methods and standard Gibbs sampling.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The potential to accelerate Gibbs sampling, especially in situations where computational time is limited, is a significant benefit.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains complex concepts in a relatively accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability:** The experiments are limited to relatively small models. The computational cost of the projection step, particularly for the reversed KL-divergence, may become prohibitive for larger, more realistic instances.
*   **Runtime Clarity:** The use of "number of samples" on the x-axis of Figure 2 (and the supplement) is misleading, as sampling is only directly relevant to the reversed KL-divergence method. Reporting absolute running times in seconds for all algorithms would allow for a fairer comparison of accuracy-runtime trade-offs.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The details of the inner loop stopping criteria for the projected gradient algorithm (Section 5.1) are not fully specified. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the algorithm.
*   **Conservative Bound:** The spectral norm bound for fast mixing appears overly conservative compared to established theoretical results for specific Ising model configurations (e.g., the 2D Ising model [11]).
*   **Limited Comparison:** The paper does not compare to recent work on random fields with tractable partition functions, such as [Flach, TPAMI, to appear], which could provide a stronger benchmark for assessing the method’s efficiency.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to improving MCMC inference in Ising models.
*   The experimental results demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods on the tested instances.
*   The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail to allow for replication.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The scalability of the proposed methods is a concern.
*   The lack of clarity regarding runtime measurements hinders a comprehensive evaluation.
*   The potentially conservative spectral norm bound may limit the practical impact of the approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with a promising idea. While scalability and runtime clarity are concerns, the novelty of the approach and the positive experimental results justify acceptance. The authors should address the concerns regarding runtime reporting and clarify the inner loop stopping criteria in a rebuttal. Exploring comparisons with models from [Flach, TPAMI] could further strengthen the paper.
