

The paper addresses the challenge of inference in high-treewidth Ising models by proposing a novel notion of "tractability" based on fast-mixing parameter conditions rather than structural constraints. It defines fast-mixing models via a spectral norm bound on the absolute interaction matrix (|||β|||₂ < 1), then develops projection algorithms to map arbitrary parameters onto this set under multiple divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, reversed KL). The Euclidean projection uses a dual formulation with LBFGS-B, while reversed KL leverages Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing target for stochastic gradient estimation. Experiments on 8×8 grids and 10-node graphs demonstrate that the projected models consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in marginal accuracy for finite sampling budgets, with reversed KL projection yielding the strongest results. The work extends NIPS themes from Globerson & Jaakkola (2006) and Hazan & Shashua (2005) by shifting focus from structural tractability to parameter-space mixing guarantees.

**Strengths:**  
- **Originality (High):** Introduces a fundamentally new criterion for tractable inference (fast-mixing parameters) distinct from prior NIPS work on tree-based approximations. The spectral norm bound and projection framework are novel contributions.  
- **Quality (High):** Rigorous theoretical foundation (Theorems 4, 7) and well-executed experiments with clear evaluation metrics (mean absolute marginal error). The reversed KL approach is technically sound and effectively leverages fast-mixing properties.  
- **Significance (High):** Provides a practical solution for inference under time constraints in strong-interaction Ising models, with clear advantages over variational methods and naive Gibbs sampling when sampling time is limited. The framework generalizes to other MRFs (Section 7).  
- **Clarity (Good):** Well-structured, with precise definitions and clear algorithmic descriptions. Minor improvements to Figure 2 labeling and runtime reporting would enhance reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Clarity (Moderate):** The horizontal axis in Figure 2 (and supplement) is ambiguously labeled "number of samples" despite sampling being used only for reversed KL projection. This misrepresents the x-axis as a sampling count rather than runtime (seconds), complicating cross-method comparison. *Recommendation:* Clarify x-axis as "runtime (seconds)" and report absolute runtimes for all methods (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs) to enable fair accuracy-runtime analysis.  
- **Quality (Moderate):** The inner loop stopping criterion for the LBFGS-B optimizer (Section 5.1) is unspecified. *Recommendation:* State the convergence tolerance or maximum iterations for inner optimization.  
- **Significance (Moderate):** The spectral norm bound (|||β|||₂ < 1) is conservative (e.g., for 2D Ising, β_c ≈ 0.44 vs. bound at β=0.25; Section 7). A tighter bound would strengthen practical utility. *Recommendation:* Discuss whether the bound could be tightened using techniques from Lubetzky & Sly (2010).  
- **Originality (Moderate):** The piecewise KL approach (Section 5.3) lacks comparison to recent tractable subgraph methods (e.g., Saul & Jordan, NIPS 2002; Sutton & McCallum, ML 2005). *Recommendation:* Briefly contextualize against these works.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel fast-mixing framework with strong theoretical grounding; superior empirical results for time-constrained inference; clear extension to general MRFs.  
*Con:* Ambiguous experimental metrics (Figure 2), unspecified inner-loop stopping, and conservative spectral bound limit immediate practical impact.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The work makes a significant, well-executed contribution to inference methodology that advances the state of the art. Addressing the clarity issues (Figure 2 labeling, runtime reporting) would strengthen the paper further. The conservative spectral bound is a known limitation (Section 7) and does not undermine the core contribution.
