Summary
This paper proposes projecting the parameters of an Ising model onto a set that guarantees rapid mixing for single-site Gibbs sampling. The fast-mixing set is defined by spectral-norm bounds on the dependency matrix (building on Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum and Hayes). The authors consider several projections: (i) Euclidean projection via a dual formulation that effectively thresholds singular values while preserving sparsity, (ii) the inclusive KL divergence KL(θ||ψ), which is largely of theoretical interest due to intractable expectations under θ, (iii) a “piecewise” KL that computes exact KL on tractable subgraphs, and (iv) the reversed KL KL(ψ||θ), optimized by stochastic approximation using samples from ψ, which is enforced to be fast mixing. This extends the approximating family beyond fully factorized mean-field to a larger, fast-mixing class. Experiments compare these projections with LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original parameters on grids and small random graphs.

Relation to prior work
The approach complements tractable-structure approximations such as mean-field, structured mean-field, TRW, and planar approximations (Globerson & Jaakkola) by focusing on parameter constraints that imply rapid mixing rather than exact tractability. The connection between KL(ψ||θ) minimization and mean-field is clearly articulated. The fast-mixing condition follows the spectral norm criterion (a strengthening of Dobrushin’s l1 condition) and its sufficiency is standard in the mixing literature.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Treating “fast mixing” as the tractable family and projecting onto it is a fresh angle that bridges variational and MCMC approaches.
- Theory: Clear use of sufficient conditions for rapid mixing; a dual Euclidean projection algorithm that preserves sparsity is technically interesting and potentially useful beyond this paper.
- Practical angle: Optimizing KL(ψ||θ) with stochastic approximation is a reasonable way to exploit the fast-mixing constraint while retaining a probabilistic objective.
- Empirical scope: Evaluations on grids and random graphs with both attractive and mixed couplings, and comparisons to standard variational baselines.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality
- Dual projection clarity: The constraint ZijDij = 0 is intended to preserve the sparsity pattern (Zij=1 for non-edges). Please clarify this in the text and explain whether the projection can inadvertently eliminate edges (i.e., produce a strictly smaller edge set) and if so, whether this is intended.
- Algorithmic procedure: It is unclear whether you (a) fully solve the projection/minimization and then run Gibbs, or (b) interleave sampling, gradient steps, and Euclidean projections. A concise algorithm box with pseudocode, indicating update order, stopping criteria, and when sampling occurs, would help.
- Convergence: You reference Ergodic Mirror Descent for stochastic projected updates. Please state explicit conditions (step-size schedule, number of Markov steps per iteration, mixing assumptions) under which your procedure is guaranteed to converge, and whether your fixed step size satisfies them.
- Update scheme: The main mixing-time bound is for random updates; experiments use systematic scan. Please justify applicability or cite the corresponding systematic-scan bound you rely on.

Clarity
- The derivation of Theorem 7 (dual form) is dense. A short intuitive explanation and a clearer role for Λ and M in enforcing the spectral constraint on |B|, while maintaining sparsity, would improve readability.
- Notation: Keep Rij, D, B, and the absolute value operator consistent. Make explicit that the constraint is on |||B|||2 (spectral norm of the elementwise absolute of pairwise parameters).

Experimental evaluation
- Fairness of comparisons: Several plots use a fixed number of Gibbs iterations (e.g., 30k). For a fair comparison to deterministic methods and across different projection costs, please compare wall-clock time or total computational budget, including the cost of projection/optimization (which can be substantial).
- Error bars/significance: Include variance estimates (e.g., standard error across trials). As presented, it is hard to judge statistical significance.
- Spectral bound choice: Many experiments use a spectral bound of 2.5 even though the theory guarantees mixing for bounds <1. Please quantify how often the chains fail to mix under your chosen bound, and report diagnostics or empirical mixing-time estimates to justify that the constraint remains effective.
- Baselines: Since your motivation is mixing, comparisons to alternative MCMC schemes are important. Please include blocked Gibbs, tree/block sampling, Swendsen–Wang, or lifted Gibbs sampling (e.g., Gogate et al.) to contextualize the benefit of your projections relative to standard acceleration techniques.
- Scale and regimes: Results focus on small graphs (e.g., 8×8 grids, n=10 random graphs). It would be helpful to show performance and time-accuracy tradeoffs on larger graphs and stronger couplings, where the benefit of fast-mixing projections might be most pronounced.

Originality
- Projecting to a fast-mixing set and optimizing KL(ψ||θ) within that set is a novel combination. The piecewise KL divergence is an interesting, tractable surrogate, although its empirical benefits appear limited in the current form.

Significance
- If the cost of projection is modest relative to subsequent sampling, this approach could be practically useful for time-limited inference. However, the current experiments do not convincingly demonstrate a net time-accuracy advantage when accounting for projection overhead and alternative MCMC baselines. The looseness of the spectral bound (and empirical use of a larger threshold) also weakens the strength of the guarantees.

Specific suggestions
- Add an algorithm box detailing the full optimization/sampling loop, including projection frequency, step sizes, and stopping criteria.
- Clarify the sparsity-preservation mechanism of the dual projection and whether edges may be zeroed out.
- Provide convergence statements (or clearly mark the method as heuristic with empirical evidence).
- Report runtime and include projection/optimization cost in all time-accuracy comparisons.
- Add error bars and, if possible, hypothesis tests across trials.
- Compare against blocked or lifted Gibbs and report improvements in effective sample size per time.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and timely idea linking variational projections with fast-mixing constraints.
- Solid theoretical underpinnings and a practical stochastic optimization approach.
- Potentially impactful for practitioners needing reliable mixing under time constraints.

Cons for acceptance
- Ambiguity in the algorithmic procedure and convergence guarantees.
- Experimental evaluation lacks runtime accounting, error bars, and comparisons to stronger MCMC baselines.
- Use of a spectral threshold beyond the theoretical guarantee without mixing diagnostics.

Overall
A promising and original direction with solid theoretical motivation. With clearer algorithmic presentation, explicit convergence/mixing diagnostics, and stronger, time-normalized empirical comparisons (including advanced MCMC baselines), this work would make a compelling contribution. As it stands, I am positive about the idea but would like to see the empirical case strengthened and the procedure clarified.
