The authors propose a method for improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the models' original parameterization onto a new parameter setting that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion needed for fast mixing. They formulate the projection as a constrained optimization problem, where the constraints are needed to ensure that the new parameters are defined over the same space as the original parameters, and examine this projection under several distance/divergence measures.

The idea of combining principles from stochastic and deterministic inference is under-explored, making this an interesting concept. However, the description of the dual of the projection problem could be clarified, particularly regarding how the zij*dij = 0 constraints ensure that the new parameterization is over the same space. An algorithm description box would be beneficial in illustrating the overall procedure, including whether the projected gradient operations are performed to completion before running a Gibbs sampler, or if sampling is interleaved with gradient updates.

Additionally, it would be helpful to confirm whether the proposed projected gradient descent strategy is guaranteed to converge.

The experiments presented are somewhat unconvincing. The first set of experiments raises questions, such as why the Gibbs sampler was run for 30,000 iterations. A comparison based on time would seem more fair, especially when comparing a sampling method to deterministic methods. Furthermore, error bars are missing from the charts, making it difficult to determine the significance of the results.

The second set of results, comparing the original/naive Gibbs sampler with Gibbs samplers under different projections, demonstrates that the projection does lead to faster mixing. Nevertheless, the time required to perform the projection operation is not accounted for in this comparison, which may impact the overall utility of the method.

Notably, the comparison is missing blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate's "Lifted Gibbs Sampler," which would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

Overall, the idea is promising, but the development of the projection problem and proposed method could benefit from further clarification and refinement. A more robust set of experiments is necessary to convincingly demonstrate the utility of the method.

Strengths:

* The paper proposes an innovative approach to improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models.
* The idea of combining stochastic and deterministic inference principles is under-explored and has potential.

Weaknesses:

* The description of the dual of the projection problem could be clarified.
* The overall procedure, including the relationship between projected gradient operations and Gibbs sampling, is unclear.
* The convergence of the proposed projected gradient descent strategy is not confirmed.
* The experiments are limited, and the comparisons are not comprehensive.
* The time required to perform the projection operation is not accounted for in the comparisons.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents an original and promising approach to improving Gibbs sampling in Ising models.
* The idea of combining stochastic and deterministic inference principles has potential for further development.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper requires further clarification and refinement of the projection problem and proposed method.
* The experiments are limited, and the comparisons are not comprehensive.
* The time required to perform the projection operation is not accounted for in the comparisons.

Recommendation:

* The paper has potential, but it requires revisions to address the mentioned weaknesses.
* The authors should provide a clearer description of the dual of the projection problem and the overall procedure.
* The convergence of the proposed projected gradient descent strategy should be confirmed.
* The experiments should be expanded to include comprehensive comparisons and account for the time required to perform the projection operation.
* The authors should consider adding comparisons to blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate's "Lifted Gibbs Sampler."
