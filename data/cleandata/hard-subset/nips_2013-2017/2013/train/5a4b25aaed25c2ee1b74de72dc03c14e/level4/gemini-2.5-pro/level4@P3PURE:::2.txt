The authors propose a method to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model's original parameters onto a new parameter set that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion for fast mixing. This projection is formulated as a constrained optimization problem, where the constraints are designed to ensure the new parameters are defined over the same space as the original ones. The paper examines this projection under several distance and divergence measures.

This work addresses the under-explored area of combining principles from stochastic and deterministic inference, and the core idea is quite interesting. However, several aspects of the paper require clarification and improvement.

While the high-level idea of augmenting parameters to improve mixing time is straightforward, the technical description of the dual of the projection problem lacks clarity. For instance, it is not clear how the `zij*dij = 0` constraints ensure that the new parameterization is over the same space as the original; could these constraints not simply result in a parameterization over a smaller space? Furthermore, the overall procedure is ambiguous. Is the projected gradient descent run to completion before initiating the Gibbs sampler, or are the gradient updates interleaved with the sampling steps? The inclusion of an algorithm box would greatly enhance clarity. Additionally, the paper should address whether the proposed projected gradient descent strategy is guaranteed to converge.

The experimental evaluation could also be strengthened in several key areas.
1.  In the first set of experiments comparing the proposed method to deterministic approaches, the choice of 30,000 iterations for the Gibbs sampler seems arbitrary. A comparison based on wall-clock time would be more appropriate and fair. Moreover, the results are presented without error bars, making it impossible for the reader to assess their statistical significance.
2.  The second set of experiments demonstrates that the projection leads to faster mixing compared to a naive Gibbs sampler. However, a critical omission is the computational cost of the projection step itself. This overhead is not accounted for in the comparison. For the method to be practically useful, the time saved during sampling must outweigh the time spent on the initial projection. For example, if the projection takes one minute, during which a naive sampler could have already generated thousands of samples, the practical benefit is questionable.
3.  Finally, the experiments would be more convincing with the inclusion of stronger MCMC baselines, such as blocked Gibbs sampling or related work like Gogate's "Lifted Gibbs Sampler."

In summary, this paper presents a very interesting idea. However, the technical development of the projection problem requires further clarification, and a more thorough set of experiments is needed to convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.
