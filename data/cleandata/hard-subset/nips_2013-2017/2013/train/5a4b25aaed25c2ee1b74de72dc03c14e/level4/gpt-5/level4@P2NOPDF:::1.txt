The paper aims to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, which are known to mix slowly. It proposes projecting the original model onto a nearby model that is guaranteed to be fast-mixing by weakening parameters just enough to satisfy a spectral bound. Experiments show regimes where this yields better marginal estimates within a fixed computation budget than mean field, belief propagation variants, and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

The technical development builds on prior results that provide sufficient conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]. I have not seen the idea of projecting onto the closest rapidly mixing model used explicitly as an approximate inference method before, which is a novel and appealing angle.

On small Ising problems, the results show noticeable improvements over strong baselines (standard sampling and contemporary variational methods). My main concern is scalability. The experiments compare mixing times of samplers on different models, and it is difficult to infer how these results scale with problem size. The projection step required to construct the fast-mixing model also appears computationally expensive, and its cost does not seem to be included in the comparisons. Finally, extending the approach beyond binary variables may be challenging, since deriving analogous convergence bounds for more general models is substantially harder.

CLARITY:
- The manuscript repeatedly invokes “standard results,” sometimes as formal theorems (e.g., Theorem 6), without citation or a proof sketch. Lemma 5 is left without proof, and the cited reference covers only the zero-field case.
- Please discuss properties and tractability of the dependency matrix R. The maximization suggests it may be intractable; if so, state this and explain how this issue is addressed (presumably via Lemma 5).
- The latter half of Section 4 is very hard to follow. The text references g, M, and Λ before defining them, and Theorem 4 introduces notation that is not explained. If this optimization is included, provide clearer definitions, intuition, and derivations; consider shortening restatements of [8] that are not directly used.
- In Section 1, KL(q||p) is used for both directions of KL divergence; the notation should change when the direction changes. Also, q denotes the true distribution and p the approximation, which is opposite to common practice; please flag this deviation to avoid confusion.

EXPERIMENTS:
- It appears that Figure 2’s timing does not include the cost of the projection step. Section 6.1 mentions this ambiguously; please clarify and, if projection costs were excluded, report results that include them. As-is, the method effectively benefits from a sophisticated variational optimization “for free,” which weakens the comparison to standard Gibbs.
- It would be valuable to include experiments on larger models, since Gibbs mixing times often depend strongly on problem size. Feasible options include models with symmetries that allow exact marginals, or using very long runs to approximate ground truth.
- While KL(θ||ψ) is generally intractable, it would still be informative as a “best case” benchmark for how sampling from an approximate model might perform. For the small models used here, junction tree could make this feasible.
- The paper notes that mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection, yet the error gap between mean field and the full reverse KL projection is large. This discrepancy deserves discussion.

Overall, approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the nearest fast-mixing model is an attractive idea, and the paper leverages recent mixing bounds elegantly. However, the empirical comparisons and scope raise concerns about scalability, fairness of timing comparisons (due to unaccounted projection costs), and applicability beyond binary models. With clearer exposition, inclusion of projection overhead, and experiments on larger or more challenging instances, the contribution would be significantly strengthened.
