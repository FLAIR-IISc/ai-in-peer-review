- Proposes projecting pairwise Ising models onto “fast-mixing” models to improve Gibbs sampling mixing rates—novel and interesting idea.
- Leverages prior theoretical work on rapid-mixing conditions; the specific idea of projection to the nearest rapidly-mixing model appears to be new.
- Experiments on small/tiny Ising models show improved marginal estimates and outperform several variational methods and original-model Gibbs for fixed compute time.
- Major scalability concern: results are limited to toy models and it’s unclear how performance and mixing comparisons scale to larger problems.
- The projection step appears computationally expensive and its cost is not clearly accounted for in timing comparisons.
- Figure 2’s time comparison is ambiguous about whether projection time is included; if excluded, results are misleading and should be recomputed including projection cost.
- Hard to assess sampler mixing-time comparisons across models as problem size grows; alternative experimental regimes (symmetries, long-run ground truth, junction tree) are suggested.
- Unclear feasibility of generalizing the approach beyond binary states due to difficulty of establishing convergence bounds.
- Paper cites “standard results” without sufficient citations or proofs (e.g., Theorem 6; Lemma 5 only proven for zero-field).
- The dependency matrix R’s properties and tractability are not discussed; the maximization involved may be intractable and needs clarification.
- Section 4 is poorly organized and hard to follow—variables (g, M, Lambda) and notation in Theorem 4 are introduced without explanation.
- KL divergence notation is used inconsistently (KL(q||p) used for both directions); notation should follow conventional usage.
- Suggests exploring KL(θ||ψ) as a “best case” comparison and using junction tree for toy models to obtain true marginals.
- The large gap between mean field error and reverse-KL projection error warrants discussion, since mean field is a degenerate reverse-KL case.
- Overall: conceptively strong and elegant use of mixing bounds, but experimental comparisons, computational accounting, clarity, and applicability to larger/more general models need substantial improvement.
