- Paper proposes improving Gibbs sampling inference by projecting an input Ising MRF onto the set of fast-mixing models, then performing inference on that projected model.
- Fast-mixing models are defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute Ising edge strengths.
- Projection is defined using divergences between Gibbs distributions while preserving the original graph structure.
- Euclidean-distance projection solved by dualizing the problem and optimizing with L-BFGS-B.
- Projections under KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL use a projected gradient algorithm; reverse KL requires Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model to compute the projection.
- Experiments on small random models compare estimated marginals against ground truth and evaluate accuracy vs. runtime.
- Results: proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and loopy BP in accuracy; for reasonable runtime ranges they also beat Gibbs on the original model.
- Among proposed divergences, reverse KL performs best consistently.
- Reviewer requests details on stopping criteria for the inner L-BFGS-B loop in the projected gradient algorithm.
- Reviewer notes plot x-axes labeled “number of samples” are misleading (sampling only used for reverse KL) and asks to replace with runtime and report absolute runtimes (seconds) for all methods including LBP, TRW, MF.
- Reviewer suggests evaluating larger models (e.g., Flach’s class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function).
- Minor fixes requested: change “onto the tree” to “onto graph T” (p.222) and replace “derivative” with “subgradient” (p.226).
- Overall: interesting approach with convincing empirical results, but practical utility may be limited by high runtime and requires clarification in rebuttal.
