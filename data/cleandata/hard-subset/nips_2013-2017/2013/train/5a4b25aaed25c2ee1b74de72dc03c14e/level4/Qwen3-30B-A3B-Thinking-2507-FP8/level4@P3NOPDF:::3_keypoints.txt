- Proposes projecting slow-mixing Ising models onto a fast-mixing model family defined by spectral constraints, then performing inference on the projection.  
- Projection minimizes divergences (KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL) between Gibbs distributions; Euclidean projection solved via dualization and LBFGS-B.  
- Reversed KL projection requires Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model, increasing runtime cost.  
- Experiments compare methods against loopy BP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on original models using small random Ising instances.  
- Evaluation measures marginal accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy trade-offs; reversed KL consistently yields the best accuracy.  
- Empirical results show consistent superiority over TRW, MF, and loopy BP, and competitive runtime-accuracy versus Gibbs on the original model for reasonable runtimes.  
- Strength: spectral constraint gives a clear, mathematical definition of fast-mixing models.  
- Strength: unified projection framework handles multiple divergence measures within one optimization scheme.  
- Strength: thorough empirical evaluation highlighting reversed KL as strongest performer.  
- Major concern: inner LBFGS-B stopping criteria for the projected gradient algorithm are unspecified, hindering reproducibility.  
- Major concern: Figure 2’s x-axis labeled “number of samples” is misleading—should be runtime (seconds); absolute runtimes for loopy BP, TRW, and MF are missing.  
- Major concern: experiments are limited to small random models; need larger/scalable benchmarks (e.g., Flach et al., TPAMI 2013) to validate practical utility.  
- Minor: wording fixes (e.g., “onto graph T,” “derivative”→“subgradient”) and clarification that reversed KL’s runtime overhead must be quantified and justified.  
- Overall: technically sound and novel with promising results, but runtime reporting and scalability limitations weaken practical claims.  
- Recommendation: Weak Accept, conditional on clarifying runtime metrics and providing larger-scale validation.
