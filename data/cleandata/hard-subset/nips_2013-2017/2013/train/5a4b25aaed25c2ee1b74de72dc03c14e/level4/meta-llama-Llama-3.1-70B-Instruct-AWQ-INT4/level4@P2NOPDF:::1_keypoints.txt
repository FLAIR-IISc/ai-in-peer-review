- Proposes projecting slow-mixing pairwise Ising models onto nearest “fast-mixing” models using spectral bounds to guarantee rapid mixing.
- Novelty lies in using projection onto rapidly mixing models for approximate inference, building on prior mixing-condition results.
- Experiments show improved marginal estimates in some regimes, outperforming standard variational methods and Gibbs on the original model for small Ising instances.
- Heavy reliance on prior theoretical results [4,8], but the projection-as-approximation idea is new.
- Scalability is questionable: experiments are limited to tiny toy models and may not reflect behavior on larger problems where mixing depends on size.
- The computationally expensive projection step is omitted from time comparisons (Figure 2), inflating perceived speedups.
- Unclear whether the approach generalizes beyond binary states, since convergence bounds are much harder in more general cases.
- Presentation omissions: several “standard results” lack references or proof sketches (e.g., Theorem 6), and Lemma 5 is unproven with only a special-case reference provided.
- Dependency matrix R properties and tractability are not discussed; the maximization to compute R appears intractable and should be acknowledged.
- Section 4 becomes hard to follow due to unexplained notation (g, M, Lambda) introduced before Theorem 7 and unclear notation in Theorem 4.
- Inconsistent KL divergence notation and reversed q/p convention in Section 1, conflicting with common literature practice.
- Experimental ambiguities: projection time should be included in comparisons and clarified, otherwise method benefits may be overstated.
- Experiments should include larger models or alternatives (symmetry-exploiting models, long-run sampling as ground truth) since junction trees are infeasible for bigger instances.
- Suggests exploring KL(θ||ψ) (reverse KL) as a “best case” baseline using junction trees on toy models, since reverse KL is intractable but informative.
- The large error gap between mean field and reverse KL projection needs discussion, given mean field is a degenerate case of reverse KL.
- Overall: elegant idea leveraging mixing bounds, but concerns remain about experimental comparisons, scalability, computational cost accounting, and clarity of presentation.
