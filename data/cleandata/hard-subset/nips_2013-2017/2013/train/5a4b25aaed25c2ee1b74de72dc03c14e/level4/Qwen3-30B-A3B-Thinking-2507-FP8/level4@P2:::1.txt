

This paper proposes an innovative approach to improve inference in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, which are known to be slow-mixing. The authors introduce a novel method of projecting model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" parameter set that guarantees rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling, using four different divergence measures. The main technical contribution is a dual algorithm for Euclidean projection and extensions to KL divergences, with experimental results showing improved marginal estimates compared to standard variational methods and original Gibbs sampling for small-scale models.

The work builds on established rapid mixing conditions [4,8] but offers a fresh perspective by framing parameter projection as a practical inference strategy rather than just a theoretical condition. This represents a meaningful contribution to the literature on approximate inference, as it provides a systematic way to transform slow-mixing models into fast-mixing ones while preserving as much of the original model structure as possible. The paper's approach is conceptually elegant, leveraging recent advances in mixing time analysis to create a new inference paradigm.

**Quality:** The technical content is sound and well-motivated. The authors correctly identify the dependency matrix R as central to mixing time analysis and establish a clear connection between spectral norms of interaction matrices and rapid mixing. The projection algorithms are theoretically well-founded, with Theorem 7 providing a novel dual approach to the Euclidean projection problem. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the projection step and its scalability to larger models. The experimental section lacks thorough analysis of how the projection cost scales with model size, which is a significant limitation given the paper's claims about practical utility.

**Clarity:** The presentation has several clarity issues that undermine its accessibility. The paper frequently references "standard results" without sufficient explanation or proper attribution (e.g., Lemma 5 refers to a proof for zero-field Ising models but states the result generally). Notation inconsistencies are problematic, particularly the inconsistent use of KL(q||p) where q and p are sometimes reversed from standard conventions. The latter half of Section 4 is particularly difficult to follow due to abrupt introduction of variables (g, M, Λ) without proper context. The paper would be significantly improved by adding a concise explanation of the dependency matrix R's computational challenges and why Lemma 5 is necessary, rather than simply stating the result.

**Originality:** The core idea of projecting onto fast-mixing models is novel in the context of approximate inference, though it builds on established mixing time literature. The authors successfully demonstrate how this approach generalizes mean-field inference (which is a degenerate case when spectral norm is constrained to zero). This represents a meaningful extension of existing work on tractable inference methods, as it provides a more flexible family of approximations than traditional mean-field or tree-based approaches.

**Significance:** The method shows promise for small-scale models, with experiments demonstrating improved marginal estimates within practical time constraints compared to both variational methods and original Gibbs sampling. However, the significance is limited by the paper's focus on toy models. The authors fail to address how the method would perform on larger, more realistic models where mixing times typically scale exponentially with problem size. The lack of experiments on models where junction tree inference is intractable (e.g., using models with symmetries that allow exact marginal computation) significantly weakens the paper's claims about practical utility.

**Major Concerns:** The most significant weakness is the lack of scalability analysis. The experiments only use 8x8 grids and 10-node random graphs, which are far too small to assess how the method would perform on realistic problems. The computational cost of the projection step is not properly accounted for in the time comparisons (as noted in Section 6.1), which undermines the claimed performance advantages. The paper also fails to address how the method would extend beyond binary Ising models, which is a substantial limitation given that most real-world applications involve more complex state spaces.

**Recommendation:** This paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution that could be valuable for small-scale inference problems. However, the experimental evidence is insufficient to support claims about practical utility, and the scalability concerns are too significant to ignore. I recommend rejection unless the authors can provide stronger evidence of scalability, clarify the presentation, and include experiments on larger models where the mixing time issue becomes genuinely problematic. The method shows promise but requires more substantial validation to be considered a significant contribution to the field.
