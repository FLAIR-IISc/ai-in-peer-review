- Paper proposes a two-step inference approach: project a possibly slow-mixing Ising model onto a fast-mixing model, then perform inference on the fast-mixing model.  
- Fast-mixing model class is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute Ising edge strengths.  
- Projection is defined via divergences between Gibbs distributions and preserves the original graph structure.  
- Projection methods include Euclidean (dualization + LBFGS-B) and divergences (KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL) using a projected gradient algorithm.  
- Reversed KL projection requires Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model to compute the projection.  
- Experiments on small random models compare approximated marginals to true marginals and include baselines LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original model.  
- Proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy and beat Gibbs on the original model for a reasonable runtime range.  
- Among proposed variants, reversed KL consistently achieves the best performance.  
- Reviewer requests details on stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B loop in the projected gradient algorithm.  
- Reviewer notes the x-axis labeled "number of samples" is misleading; suggests using runtime (seconds) and reporting absolute runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF for fair comparison.  
- Reviewer recommends experiments on larger models, e.g., using Flach’s tractable random-field models.  
- Minor wording suggestions: change "onto the tree" to "onto graph T" and use "subgradient" instead of "derivative" on page 226.  
- Overall assessment: interesting and empirically convincing results, but practical utility may be limited by high runtime—request clarification in rebuttal.
