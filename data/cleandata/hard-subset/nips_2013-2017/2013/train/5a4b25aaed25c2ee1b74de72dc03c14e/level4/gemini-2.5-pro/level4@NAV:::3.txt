This paper addresses the challenge of inference in Ising models where the performance of Gibbs sampling is highly dependent on the mixing rate of the corresponding Monte Carlo Markov Chain. The authors propose a novel two-step approach: first, they project the given model, which may have a slow mixing rate, onto a space of models guaranteed to mix fast; second, they perform inference on this new, fast-mixing model.

The set of fast-mixing models is characterized by imposing a bound on the spectral norm of the matrix containing the absolute values of the edge interaction strengths. The projection itself is defined by minimizing a divergence between the Gibbs distributions of the original and projected models, with the constraint that the graph structure is preserved. For the Euclidean distance, this projection is computed by solving a dual problem using LBFGS-B. For other divergences, namely KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL, a projected gradient algorithm is employed. Notably, computing the projection for the reverse KL divergence requires running Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing target model.

The authors present a thorough experimental evaluation on small, random models, comparing the marginals estimated by their method against the ground truth. The proposed approach, tested with all mentioned divergences, is benchmarked against loopy belief propagation (LBP), tree-reweighted belief propagation (TRW), mean field (MF), and standard Gibbs sampling on the original model. The evaluation considers both final accuracy and the accuracy-runtime trade-off. The results indicate that the proposed methods consistently achieve higher accuracy than TRW, MF, and LBP. They also outperform standard Gibbs sampling on the original model for a significant range of runtimes. Among the proposed variants, the method using reverse KL divergence consistently delivers the best performance.

**Comments:**

*   The projected gradient algorithm detailed in Section 5.1 seems to involve a nested loop structure, where the inner loop is an LBFGS-B optimization. The authors should provide details on the termination criteria for these inner loop iterations.

*   The interpretation of the experimental results in Figure 2 (and the supplement) is difficult due to unclear plot axes. The horizontal axis is labeled "number of samples," yet sampling is only integral to the reverse KL divergence method. For a meaningful comparison of performance trade-offs, this axis should likely represent the algorithm's runtime. To this end, the runtimes for the LBP, TRW, and MF baselines should also be included. A fair accuracy-runtime comparison across all tested algorithms is crucial. The authors are requested to clarify this and report absolute runtimes in seconds.

*   The paper would be strengthened by an experimental comparison involving larger-scale models. One interesting possibility is to use the class of models from Flach ("A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function," TPAMI), which permit exact inference in polynomial time and would serve as an excellent benchmark.

**Minor Points:**

*   Line 222: Suggest replacing "onto the tree" with "onto graph T".
*   Line 226: Would "subgradient" be a more precise term than "derivative" in this context?

In summary, this is an interesting paper with convincing empirical results. Its practical utility, however, could be limited by its computational cost, a point that requires clarification in the author rebuttal.
