

The authors propose a novel approach to accelerate Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting original parameters onto a constrained set satisfying spectral norm conditions for rapid mixing (Theorem 4). The core contribution is a unified framework for parameter projection under multiple divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL), with theoretical guarantees and empirical validation. This work extends the notion of "tractability" beyond structural constraints (e.g., tree-structured approximations) to parameter-space conditions ensuring fast mixing, which is a meaningful conceptual advance over existing variational methods.

The paper’s technical development is largely sound, though two aspects require clarification. First, the constraint $Z_{ij}D_{ij} = 0$ (Theorem 7) ensures the projected parameters respect the original graph structure by zeroing out non-edges in $D = |B|$, not reducing the graph size. A brief note on this would prevent confusion. Second, the algorithmic procedure—whether gradient updates are completed before sampling or interleaved—is ambiguous. A pseudocode box would significantly improve reproducibility. The projected gradient descent (Section 5.1) is well-motivated, but convergence guarantees under the specific stochastic approximation (Equation 8) warrant explicit discussion, particularly regarding the dependence between samples in the "pool."

Experiments (Section 6) demonstrate that projected parameters yield faster convergence than original Gibbs sampling under finite time, with marginal accuracy improvements over variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF). However, the evaluation lacks critical comparisons: 
1. The 30,000 Gibbs iterations for the original model (Section 6.1) are not time-normalized against projection costs. Since projection (e.g., Euclidean) requires ~30,000 Gibbs steps (as stated), a fair comparison must account for total computational effort. Without this, the claimed "faster mixing" is misleading—e.g., if projection takes 1 minute and original Gibbs generates 10k samples in that time, the projected method may not be competitive. 
2. Error bars or confidence intervals are absent in Figures 1–2, making statistical significance unassessable. 
3. The omission of blocked Gibbs and Gogate’s "Lifted Gibbs" (which address mixing via variable grouping) weakens the benchmarking. The paper’s Section 7 notes the spectral bound ($\|R\|_2 < 1$) is conservative (e.g., for 2D Ising, $\beta_c \approx 0.44$ vs. bound $\beta=0.25$), yet the experiments use $\|R\|_2 \leq 2.5$. A discussion of how this looseness impacts practical utility would strengthen the analysis.

Despite these gaps, the core idea is compelling: projecting parameters to enforce rapid mixing provides a principled alternative to structural approximations. The reversed KL approach (Section 5.4) is particularly well-motivated, as it leverages Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing set for gradient estimation. The method’s simplicity (e.g., Euclidean projection via SVD thresholding) and applicability to general MRFs (Section 7) are notable strengths.

**Strengths:**  
- Conceptual novelty in redefining tractability via parameter constraints.  
- Unified framework for multiple divergences with clear theoretical foundations.  
- Empirical evidence that projected parameters improve finite-time accuracy over variational methods.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- Algorithmic procedure lacks clarity on gradient-sampling interplay.  
- Experimental comparison ignores total computational cost (projection + sampling).  
- Missing rigorous statistical validation (error bars) and key baselines (blocked Gibbs).  
- Spectral bound looseness (Section 7) is acknowledged but not fully leveraged in evaluation.  

**Recommendation:** **Weak Accept**. The work makes a valuable theoretical contribution to inference in high-treewidth models, but the experimental validation requires substantial strengthening to demonstrate practical utility. The technical foundation is robust, yet the current evaluation does not sufficiently address the computational trade-offs central to the method’s appeal. With clearer algorithmic description, time-normalized experiments, and inclusion of relevant baselines, this would be a strong contribution. As-is, the paper’s significance is partially obscured by methodological ambiguities and incomplete benchmarking.
