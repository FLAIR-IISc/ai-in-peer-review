

The paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models by projecting slow-mixing parameters onto a set of fast-mixing parameters, defined by a spectral norm constraint on the interaction matrix (||R||₂ < 1). This work extends NIPS’ longstanding focus on tractable inference methods beyond traditional tree-based approximations (e.g., mean-field [14], loopy BP [21], or planar graph decompositions [6]) by redefining "tractability" as parameter-space conditions guaranteeing rapid Gibbs mixing, rather than structural constraints. The authors formalize this via four divergence-based projections (Euclidean, piecewise KL, reversed KL, and zero-avoiding KL), with the reversed KL variant proving most effective empirically. Experiments on small graphs demonstrate consistent accuracy gains over variational methods (LBP, TRW, MF) and Gibbs sampling on original parameters within finite time budgets.

**Strengths**  
*Originality and Significance*: The core idea—leveraging spectral constraints for fast mixing instead of structural simplification—is a compelling conceptual shift. It directly addresses a gap in prior NIPS work (e.g., Hazan & Shashua [9] focused on tree-structured approximations, not parameter-space constraints), offering a new lens for inference in high-treewidth models. The paper’s theoretical foundation (Theorem 4) and practical projection algorithms (Theorem 7, Section 5.4) are well-grounded and extend existing mixing-time literature [4, 8]. Empirically, the reversed KL method consistently outperforms baselines in accuracy-runtime trade-offs, a significant contribution to practitioners seeking efficient inference without sacrificing quality. The clear connection to mean-field (as a degenerate case) further situates the work within the broader NIPS inference landscape.

*Quality and Clarity*: The technical development is rigorous, with precise definitions (e.g., dependency matrix R, spectral norm constraint) and thorough experimental design. The paper adequately distinguishes its approach from prior work (e.g., Section 7 contrasts the spectral bound with the Ising model’s known critical threshold βc ≈ 0.44). Writing is clear and well-organized, with algorithms presented accessibly. The appendix provides sufficient detail for reproducibility (e.g., dual optimization via LBFGS-B), meeting the "expert reproducibility" standard.

**Weaknesses**  
*Runtime Interpretation*: The most critical flaw lies in the experimental evaluation. Figure 2 (and supplement) labels the x-axis "number of samples," but sampling is only used in the reversed KL method (Section 5.4), while other methods (LBP, TRW, MF, original Gibbs) do not involve sampling. This misrepresents the comparison: the axis should reflect *computational runtime* (seconds), not samples. Without absolute runtimes, the accuracy-runtime trade-off is uninterpretable (e.g., is "100 samples" equivalent to 0.1s or 10s?). The authors must clarify whether Figure 2’s axis denotes sampling steps or wall-clock time, and report absolute runtimes for all methods to enable fair comparison.  

*Practical Scope*: The spectral norm bound (||R||₂ < 1) is conservative (Section 7: for 2D Ising, it requires β < 0.25 vs. the known βc ≈ 0.44), limiting applicability. While the authors empirically use a looser bound (||R||₂ ≤ 2.5), the theoretical justification remains weak. The paper also lacks experiments on larger graphs (e.g., >10 nodes), despite citing Flach’s polynomial-time models [Boris Flach, TPAMI] as a potential benchmark. Without scalability validation, the practical utility remains uncertain for real-world problems where graphs are large.  

*Minor Clarity Issues*: Section 5.3 should replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T" (p. 222) for consistency. The projected gradient algorithm (Section 5.1) requires explicit stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B loop (e.g., "iterations or tolerance"), which the draft review correctly flags.

**Pro/Con Summary**  
*Pro*: Novel framework for fast-mixing inference; strong empirical results on small graphs; clear algorithms; significant theoretical contribution to mixing-time literature.  
*Con*: Runtime comparison misreported (critical flaw); spectral bound too conservative; no large-scale experiments; minor notational inconsistencies.

**Recommendation**  
This paper makes a valuable contribution to inference in graphical models, with a clear theoretical advance and convincing empirical results. However, the runtime interpretation error undermines the primary claim of efficiency, and the lack of scalability analysis limits its impact. The authors should address these issues in the rebuttal: (1) clarify Figure 2’s x-axis and report absolute runtimes for all methods; (2) discuss the spectral bound’s looseness and explore tighter constraints; (3) add experiments on larger graphs (e.g., 20–50 nodes) or Flach’s models [Boris Flach]. With these revisions, the paper would be a strong fit for NIPS. As-is, it merits borderline acceptance pending clarification.
