This paper proposes a novel approach to improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the original model parameters onto a new set that guarantees fast mixing. The authors formulate this projection as a constrained optimization problem, exploring various distance and divergence measures. This idea of combining stochastic and deterministic inference principles is intriguing, and the paper's focus on fast-mixing models is well-motivated.

The paper's technical contributions are significant, particularly in deriving the projection algorithm and exploring different divergences. The authors' use of the Dobrushin criterion to establish fast mixing conditions is well-founded, and their extension to various divergences, including the Euclidean distance, KL-divergence, and piecewise KL-divergence, is thorough. The experimental evaluation, although limited, demonstrates the potential of the proposed approach in improving the accuracy of Gibbs sampling.

However, there are some areas that require clarification and improvement. The description of the dual projection problem and the constraints could be more detailed, particularly in explaining how the zij*dij = 0 constraints ensure that the new parameterization is over the same space as the original parameters. An algorithm description box or a more detailed step-by-step explanation would be helpful in clarifying the overall procedure.

Additionally, the convergence properties of the proposed projected gradient descent strategy could be more thoroughly discussed. The authors mention that the strategy is an instance of Ergodic Mirror Descent, but a more detailed analysis of the convergence conditions and rates would be beneficial.

The experimental evaluation, while promising, has some limitations. The comparison of the proposed approach with other methods, such as loopy belief propagation and naive mean-field, is useful, but the evaluation could be more comprehensive. For example, the authors could compare the proposed approach with blocked Gibbs samplers or other advanced Gibbs sampling techniques. Furthermore, the experimental setup could be more carefully controlled, with clear explanations of the hyperparameter choices and the computational resources used.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting and promising approach to improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models. While there are some areas that require clarification and improvement, the paper's technical contributions and experimental evaluation demonstrate the potential of this approach.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models
* Thorough derivation of the projection algorithm and exploration of different divergences
* Promising experimental evaluation demonstrating the potential of the proposed approach

Weaknesses:

* Some areas of the paper could be more thoroughly explained, such as the dual projection problem and the convergence properties of the proposed algorithm
* Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive and carefully controlled
* Comparison with other advanced Gibbs sampling techniques could be more thorough

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and promising approach to improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models
* The technical contributions are significant, and the experimental evaluation demonstrates the potential of the proposed approach
* The paper's focus on fast-mixing models is well-motivated, and the authors' use of the Dobrushin criterion is well-founded

Arguments against acceptance:

* Some areas of the paper could be more thoroughly explained, which may impact the clarity and reproducibility of the results
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive and carefully controlled, which may impact the validity and generalizability of the results.
