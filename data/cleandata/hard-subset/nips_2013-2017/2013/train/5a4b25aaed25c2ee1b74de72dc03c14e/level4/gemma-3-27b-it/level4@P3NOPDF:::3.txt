## Review of NIPS 2013 Submission: [Paper Title - please fill in]

This paper addresses the critical issue of slow mixing in Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) inference for Ising models (MRFs with two labels). The authors propose a novel approach to accelerate inference by projecting the input model onto a space of models guaranteed to mix rapidly. This projection is defined via divergences between Gibbs distributions, constrained to preserve the underlying graph structure. The paper details projection methods based on Euclidean distance (solved with dualization and LBFGS-B) and several divergences – KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL – implemented using projected gradient descent. 

The core idea is elegant and potentially impactful. The authors convincingly argue that by operating on a fast-mixing approximation of the original model, inference can be significantly improved. The proposed projection framework provides a principled way to create such approximations. The paper is well-structured, clearly outlining the problem, proposed solution, and experimental evaluation.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the proposed methods (using different divergence measures) against established algorithms – Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), Mean Field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The authors rightly focus not only on accuracy but also on the runtime-accuracy trade-off. The results demonstrate consistent performance improvements over TRW, MF, and LBP in terms of accuracy. Importantly, the proposed methods often outperform standard Gibbs sampling, particularly within a reasonable runtime budget. Notably, the reversed KL divergence consistently yielded the best performance among the proposed methods.

However, several points require clarification and potential revision. 

First, concerning the implementation details of the projected gradient algorithm (Section 5.1), the authors state it involves nested loops, with LBFGS-B forming the inner loop. Crucially, the stopping criterion for the inner LBFGS-B iterations is not specified. Providing this detail is essential for reproducibility and understanding the computational cost.

Second, the interpretation of the horizontal axis in Figure 2 (and the supplementary material) is unclear. While labeled “number of samples,” sampling is only directly employed within the reversed KL divergence method. It seems more appropriate to label this axis as “runtime” of the algorithm, measured in seconds. Furthermore, a fair comparison of the runtime-accuracy trade-off would benefit from the inclusion of runtime results for LBP, TRW, and MF alongside the proposed methods. Reporting absolute running times would greatly enhance the clarity and interpretability of the results.

Third, while the experiments on small, random models are informative, extending the evaluation to larger, more complex models would significantly strengthen the paper.  The authors might consider comparing their methods against models where exact or efficient polynomial-time inference is possible, such as those described in [Boris Flach: A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function, to appear in TPAMI, available online].  This would provide a more robust assessment of the scalability and accuracy of the proposed approach.

Finally, minor points: on line 222, replacing “onto the tree” with “onto graph T” would improve clarity. On line 226, the use of “subgradient” may be more precise than “derivative” in the context of the projected gradient algorithm.

Overall, this is an interesting and promising paper with convincing empirical results. While the practical utility may be limited by the reported runtime (which needs clarification in the rebuttal), the proposed approach offers a valuable contribution to the field of approximate inference in MRFs.  Addressing the points raised above will further strengthen the paper and its impact.



**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. I believe the core idea and experimental results are strong enough to warrant acceptance, *pending* satisfactory responses to the questions raised regarding implementation details, runtime reporting, and scalability.
