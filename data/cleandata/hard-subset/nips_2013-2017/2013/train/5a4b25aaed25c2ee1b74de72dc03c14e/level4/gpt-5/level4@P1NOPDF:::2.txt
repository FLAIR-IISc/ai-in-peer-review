The paper proposes improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling for Ising models by projecting the model’s original parameterization onto a new parameter setting that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion for fast mixing. The projection is formulated as a constrained optimization problem—constraints are intended to ensure the new parameters remain in the same space as the original—and the authors explore several distance/divergence measures for this projection.

I find the idea interesting. Combining stochastic and deterministic inference is underexplored, and modifying parameters to accelerate mixing is a natural direction. However, the presentation of the projection’s dual is unclear. In particular, how do the zij*dij = 0 constraints ensure that the new parameterization lies in the same space as the original? Couldn’t they enforce a smaller space? Clarifying the role and interpretation of these constraints would help.

The overall procedure is also ambiguous. Do you run the projected gradient steps to completion and then run a Gibbs sampler on the projected model, or do you interleave sampling with gradient updates? A concise algorithm box or pseudocode would significantly improve clarity. Relatedly, is the proposed projected gradient descent strategy guaranteed to converge under your objectives and constraints? Please state the conditions (e.g., smoothness, step-size rules) and whether convergence is to a global or local optimum.

The experiments are not fully convincing. In the first set, why fix the Gibbs sampler to 30K iterations? Since you compare against deterministic methods, a wall-clock time comparison would be more appropriate. Also, the plots lack error bars, making it hard to assess statistical significance or variability across runs.

In the second set, you compare the original/naive Gibbs sampler to Gibbs samplers under different projections and show faster mixing after projection. However, the computational cost of computing the projection is not included. If, for example, projection takes one minute and the naive Gibbs sampler can produce 10K samples in that time, the projection may not be advantageous. Please report total time-to-accuracy, including projection plus sampling, to make the comparison fair.

Finally, it is surprising that there is no comparison to stronger baselines such as blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs Sampler. Including these would better contextualize the gains from your approach.

Overall, this is a nice idea with promise. To strengthen the paper, I recommend clarifying the dual formulation and algorithmic workflow (with a convergence discussion), and bolstering the empirical evaluation with time-based comparisons, uncertainty estimates, accounting for projection cost, and comparisons to more advanced Gibbs variants.
