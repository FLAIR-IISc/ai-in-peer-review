This paper addresses the issue of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions. The proposed method involves projecting the original model's parameters onto a "fast-mixing" model. This is achieved by finding the closest set of parameters that satisfy a spectral bound known to guarantee rapid mixing. Experimental results demonstrate that in certain regimes, this approach yields more accurate marginal estimates for a given computational budget compared to standard Gibbs sampling and common variational methods, such as mean field and belief propagation.

While the technical framework relies heavily on prior work that establishes conditions for rapid mixing [4, 8], the core idea of using projection onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as a form of approximate inference appears to be novel. The results on small-scale Ising models show promising improvements over a solid set of baselines.

However, there are several significant concerns. The primary weakness is the uncertain scalability of the approach. The experiments are confined to small models, making it difficult to predict how the relative performance and mixing times will scale with the problem size. Furthermore, the method requires a projection step to find the fast-mixing model, which appears to be computationally expensive. This cost seems to have been omitted from the experimental time comparisons, which is a major oversight. Finally, the paper does not address the generalization of this method beyond binary variables. Extending this approach is likely to be challenging, as establishing the necessary convergence bounds for more general state spaces is a significantly harder problem.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written, but several sections require clarification:

*   The authors frequently refer to "standard results" (e.g., Theorem 6) without providing citations or proof sketches. Similarly, Lemma 5 is presented without proof, and its provided reference only covers the special case of a zero external field.
*   The properties of the dependency matrix R should be discussed in more detail. In particular, it appears intractable to compute due to the maximization in its definition. The authors should clarify if this is the case and whether this is the motivation for invoking Lemma 5.
*   The second half of Section 4 is very difficult to follow. The text references variables `g`, `M`, and `Lambda` before they are defined, and the statement of Theorem 4 contains unexplained notation. This section on optimization requires a more thorough explanation to be comprehensible. The authors could potentially make space for this by reducing the discussion of results from [8] that are not directly used in this work.
*   The notation in Section 1 is inconsistent. `KL(q||p)` appears to be used for both the forward and reverse KL divergence. Additionally, the convention used in the first paragraph—where `q` is the true distribution and `p` is the approximation—is the reverse of what is standard in the literature, which may cause confusion.

**Experiments:**

*   The time comparisons in Figure 2 do not appear to account for the computational cost of the projection step. The authors should clarify whether this is the case and, if so, provide revised results that include this overhead. As presented, the method benefits from a potentially expensive optimization step without penalty, which weakens the claimed performance improvements over standard Gibbs sampling.
*   The evaluation is limited to small models where exact inference is tractable. It would have been more compelling to see experiments on larger-scale problems where methods like the junction tree algorithm are infeasible. For such cases, ground truth could be established by using models with symmetries that allow for exact marginal computation or by using the output of a very long MCMC run as a high-quality approximation.
*   While minimizing `KL(θ||ψ)` is generally intractable, it would be possible to compute for the small models in this paper using the junction tree algorithm. Including this as a baseline would provide a valuable "best-case" benchmark for the performance of sampling from a projected model.
*   The paper notes that mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection. However, the experiments show a large performance gap between the two methods. This discrepancy is surprising and warrants further discussion.

In summary, the core idea of projecting a slow-mixing model onto the nearest fast-mixing one is innovative and leverages recent work on mixing bounds in an elegant manner. However, the paper is weakened by significant concerns regarding the experimental evaluation and the unclear scalability and generalizability of the proposed method.
