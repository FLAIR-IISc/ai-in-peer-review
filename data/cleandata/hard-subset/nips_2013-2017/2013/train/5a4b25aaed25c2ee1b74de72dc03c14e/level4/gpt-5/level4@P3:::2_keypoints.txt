- Proposes approximating intractable Ising models by projecting parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set Ψ that enforces a spectral-norm bound on the dependency matrix, enabling rapid Gibbs mixing guarantees.
- Develops four projection methods: Euclidean (dual with SVD thresholding and sparsity mask), inclusive KL(θ||ψ), piecewise KL on tractable subgraphs, and reverse KL(ψ||θ) optimized by stochastic approximation using samples from ψ.
- Frames the approach as a novel tractable-family alternative to low-treewidth/planar/tree-based approximations by defining tractability via fast-mixing conditions.
- The spectral-norm condition ||R||2 < 1 and Ising-specific bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| give a clean sufficient mixing guarantee (Theorem 4, Lemma 5).
- The Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a technically interesting dual formulation that enforces spectral control while preserving sparsity via a mask.
- The sparsity/masking constraint (ZijDij = 0 with D = |B|) is not clearly explained in terms of ensuring the projected parameterization exactly preserves the original edge set and signs.
- Reverse-KL via stochastic projected gradients is reasonable and linked to Ergodic Mirror Descent, but convergence discussion is incomplete given fixed step sizes and fixed Gibbs updates used in experiments.
- The use of spectral bound c = 2.5 in experiments relaxes the theoretical guarantee (c = 1) and should be explicitly treated as a heuristic with empirical mixing diagnostics.
- The manuscript needs compact algorithm boxes for each projection (inputs/outputs, interleaving of sampling/updates/projections, stopping criteria).
- Clarify the roles of Z and D = |B| and explicitly state that symmetry and the original edge set are preserved and how signs are reintroduced after operating on |B|.
- State whether theory and practice use systematic-scan or random-scan Gibbs and which mixing bound applies.
- For the piecewise KL, precisely define how subgraphs T are chosen (coverage, treewidth) and how the max over T is computed in practice.
- Add references/discussion of block/tree sampling and cluster methods to situate the focus on univariate Gibbs.
- The idea of using fast-mixing distributions as an approximation family is original and compelling, and the dual Euclidean projection is a nice technical contribution.
- With stronger empirical evidence the approach could be impactful, but current experiments are limited (small graphs, fixed iterations, no wall-clock timing, no block/cluster MCMC comparisons).
- Experiments should report wall-clock time including projection overhead, use error bars and statistical tests over trials, and compare against stronger MCMC baselines (block Gibbs, Swendsen–Wang, tree sampling).
- Report mixing diagnostics for ψ (autocorrelation, ESS/sec, spectral gap proxies) and sensitivity analyses over spectral bound c values and fraction of runs violating the theoretical bound.
- Provide ablations across divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, reverse KL), sample-pool size, step-size schedules, number of Gibbs updates per iteration, and report final objective values.
- Pros: novel fast-mixing approximation family, solid theoretical mixing criterion, practical Euclidean projection preserving sparsity, and implementable reverse-KL stochastic projection.
- Cons: unclear dual constraints and algorithmic procedure, no established convergence guarantees under experimental settings, limited empirical evaluation lacking error bars and wall-clock accounting, and use of spectral bound above theory weakens guarantees.
- Ask authors to clarify how D = |B| and mask Z ensure ψ preserves the original edge set without shrinking model class.
- Ask whether sampling and projection are interleaved or done sequentially, and request a step-by-step algorithm.
- Request a step-size and Gibbs-update schedule satisfying EMD conditions or explicit positioning of current settings as heuristic.
- Ask for sensitivity results to the spectral bound c and mixing diagnostics to justify using c = 2.5.
- Overall: compelling idea with a neat projection algorithm and promising preliminary results, but needs clearer algorithms, justified optimization, and stronger, budget-aware empirical comparisons to be a substantial contribution.
