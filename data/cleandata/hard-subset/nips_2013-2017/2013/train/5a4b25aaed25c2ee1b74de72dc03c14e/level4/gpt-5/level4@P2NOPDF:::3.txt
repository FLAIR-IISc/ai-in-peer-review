This paper studies inference in binary MRFs (Ising models) via Gibbs sampling, whose success critically depends on the Markov chain’s mixing rate. The authors propose a two-step approach:
1) Project the original model (which may mix slowly) onto the set of fast-mixing models.
2) Perform inference on the resulting fast-mixing model.

Fast-mixing models are defined by a bound on the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute Ising edge coefficients. The projection is formulated using divergences between Gibbs distributions and is constrained to preserve the original graph structure. For Euclidean distance, the authors dualize the problem and solve it with L-BFGS-B. For other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL), they use a projected-gradient method. Computing the reverse KL projection requires Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model to estimate expectations.

The experimental evaluation on small random graphs compares approximate marginals to ground truth, contrasting the proposed projections (for the various divergences) with loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The authors report both accuracy and accuracy-versus-runtime. Results indicate that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and, within a reasonable runtime range, also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed variants, reverse KL performs best.

Comments and questions:
- Algorithmic details and stopping criteria: The projected-gradient procedure in Section 5.1 has two nested loops, with L-BFGS-B as the inner loop. Please specify:
  - The stopping conditions for the inner L-BFGS-B iterations (tolerances, max iterations, gradient norm thresholds).
  - How often the spectral-norm feasibility projection is applied and with what tolerance.
  - Any line-search or step-size schedule for the outer loop, and how feasibility is enforced after each step.
- Clarify the runtime axis in figures: In Figure 2 (and the supplement), the horizontal axis is labeled “number of samples,” but sampling is only used for the reverse KL variant. This label is confusing for the other methods. It would be more appropriate to use wall-clock runtime on the x-axis for all methods, enabling a fair accuracy–runtime comparison. Please:
  - Report absolute running times in seconds for all algorithms, including LBP, TRW, and MF.
  - Specify hardware details and implementation settings (e.g., number of threads, stopping thresholds).
  - Clarify how sampling budgets translate to runtime for reverse KL and how this compares to the other projections.
- Scope of experiments: Consider adding experiments on larger models to assess scalability and practical utility. One option is to use models from:
  - Boris Flach, “A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function,” IEEE TPAMI (to appear; available online),
  which permit polynomial-time inference and thus provide reliable ground truth on larger instances.
- Interpretation of results: The reported accuracy gains are compelling. However, without a clean runtime-based comparison across all baselines (including deterministic methods), it is difficult to evaluate the practical trade-offs. This is especially important given the nested optimization and the need for sampling in the reverse KL projection. Clarifying runtimes may also help explain when the proposed approach is preferable to well-tuned Gibbs sampling on the original model.
- Minor edits:
  - Line 222: Replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T.”
  - Line 226: “Subgradient” is more appropriate than “derivative” in this context.

Overall assessment:
This is an interesting and well-motivated paper that introduces a principled projection onto a fast-mixing set defined by a spectral-norm constraint, coupled with clear optimization formulations for multiple divergences. The empirical results are convincing on small random graphs, and the reverse KL projection in particular shows strong performance. The main concern is practical runtime: the nested optimization and sampling requirements could limit applicability unless the computational costs are clearly quantified and shown to be competitive. Providing comprehensive runtime measurements (absolute seconds), clarifying stopping criteria, and adding results on larger models would significantly strengthen the paper’s case for practical utility.
