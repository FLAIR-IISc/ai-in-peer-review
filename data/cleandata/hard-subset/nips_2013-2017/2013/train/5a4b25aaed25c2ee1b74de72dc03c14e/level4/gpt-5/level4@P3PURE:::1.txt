Summary
The paper aims to accelerate Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong couplings, a regime where standard Gibbs is known to mix slowly. The authors propose projecting the original model onto a “nearest” fast-mixing model: parameters are adjusted to be as close as possible to the original while satisfying a spectral bound that guarantees rapid mixing. Experiments on small Ising models show regimes where this yields improved marginal estimates for a fixed computation budget, compared to variational methods (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs on the original model.

Relation to prior work and novelty
The technical development leans heavily on established sufficient conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]. While those results are standard, I have not previously seen the idea of explicitly projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model and then sampling from it used as an approximate inference strategy. This projection-based perspective appears novel and well motivated.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing approach: approximate a slow-mixing model by the closest provably fast-mixing one.
- Elegant use of recent mixing-time bounds to guide the projection.
- On small “toy” Ising instances, the approach shows solid improvements over a strong set of baselines (standard Gibbs and contemporary variational methods).

Concerns and limitations
- Scalability is unclear. The experiments compare mixing behavior across different models (original vs. projected), and it is difficult to infer how performance and computational cost scale with graph size and coupling strength.
- The projection step itself appears computationally expensive; its cost does not seem to be included in the time comparisons and may dominate for larger models.
- Generalization beyond binary (Ising) variables is not obvious. Extending the convergence guarantees to multi-state models is substantially more challenging, casting doubt on applicability to broader graphical models.

Clarity
In general the paper is readable, but several parts need reorganization or added detail:
- The paper refers to “standard results” and even states them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without citation or proof sketch. Lemma 5 is stated without proof; the cited reference handles only the zero-field special case.
- Please discuss the computational properties of the dependency matrix R. Due to the maximization it involves, computing R does not appear tractable; if so, state this explicitly (I assume this motivates invoking Lemma 5).
- The second half of Sec. 4 is very hard to follow. Before Theorem 7, the text refers to g, M, and Λ before they are defined. The statement of Theorem 4 also uses unexplained notation. If the optimization in Sec. 4 is central, it needs more exposition and clearer definitions. Conversely, space could be saved by avoiding the restatement of results from [8] that are not directly used.
- In Sec. 1, the notation KL(q||p) is used for both directions of KL; the symbol order needs to switch accordingly. Also, q is used for the true distribution and p for the tractable approximation, which reverses the convention in most related literature; consider aligning with standard notation to reduce confusion.

Experiments
- Figure 2’s time comparison appears not to include the cost of computing the projection. Sec. 6.1 contains an ambiguous statement on this point. Please clarify explicitly and, if projection time was excluded, add results with the projection time included. As written, the proposed method benefits from a sophisticated variational optimization at no time cost, which makes the improvement over standard Gibbs less convincing.
- It is disappointing not to see larger-scale experiments, given that Gibbs mixing times typically depend strongly on problem size. There are feasible setups even when junction tree is intractable, such as models with symmetries that allow exact marginals, or using very long MCMC runs as a reference.
- While KL(θ||ψ) is generally intractable, it would be informative to explore it as a “best-case” objective for projecting to a sampling-friendly model, at least on the toy problems where junction tree is feasible.
- The paper notes that mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection, yet the error gap between mean field and the reverse-KL-based projection is large. This discrepancy deserves discussion.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a compelling idea—approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the nearest fast-mixing model—and leverages modern mixing bounds in a clean way. However, questions about computational overhead, fairness of time comparisons, scalability, and the narrow scope of models with available guarantees limit the current impact. Addressing the clarity issues and strengthening the experimental evaluation (especially with projection time included and on larger models) would significantly improve the paper.
