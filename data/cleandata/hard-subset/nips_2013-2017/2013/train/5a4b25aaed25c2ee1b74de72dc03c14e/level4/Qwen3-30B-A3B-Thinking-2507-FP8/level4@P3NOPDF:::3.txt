

The paper proposes a novel approach to accelerate inference in Markov Random Fields (MRFs) by projecting slow-mixing Ising models onto a space of fast-mixing models defined via spectral constraints on edge strengths. The core idea involves: (1) projecting the input model onto the set of fast-mixing models while preserving graph structure, and (2) performing inference on the projected model. The projection minimizes divergences (KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL) between Gibbs distributions, with Euclidean projection solved via dualization and LBFGS-B. For reversed KL, projection requires Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model itself.  

Experiments compare the proposed methods against loopy BP, TRW, mean-field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original model using small random Ising models. The evaluation includes accuracy (marginal approximation) and runtime-vs-accuracy trade-offs. Results demonstrate consistent superiority over TRW, MF, and loopy BP in accuracy, and for reasonable runtimes, also over Gibbs sampling on the original model. The reversed KL variant consistently achieves the best accuracy.  

**Strengths:**  
- The spectral constraint provides a clear, mathematically grounded definition of fast-mixing models.  
- The projection framework unifies multiple divergence measures under a single optimization scheme.  
- Empirical results are thorough, covering both accuracy and efficiency, with reversed KL emerging as the strongest performer.  

**Major Concerns:**  
1. **Projection Algorithm Clarity:** Section 5.1 describes a projected gradient algorithm with nested LBFGS-B iterations. The stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B loop are unspecified. Please clarify: What convergence tolerance or maximum iteration count is used for the inner loop? This is critical for reproducibility.  
2. **Figure 2 Interpretation:** The horizontal axis in Figure 2 (and supplement) is labeled "number of samples," yet sampling is only used in reversed KL projection. For consistency, the axis should represent *runtime* (in seconds), as the comparison is runtime-accuracy. Moreover, absolute runtimes for loopy BP, TRW, and MF are missing. Without these, the runtime comparisons are incomplete and potentially misleading. Please report absolute runtimes for all methods to enable fair accuracy-runtime analysis.  
3. **Scalability Validation:** Experiments are limited to small random models. To strengthen claims about practical utility, comparisons with larger models are essential. The authors should test on models from Flach et al. (TPAMI, 2013), which allow polynomial-time inference on complete graphs. This would demonstrate performance on non-trivial, scalable instances.  

**Minor Issues:**  
- Line 222: Replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph \( \mathcal{T} \)."  
- Line 226: Replace "derivative" with "subgradient" (as the objective is non-differentiable).  
- The phrase "Practical utility can be limited due to high runtime" requires elaboration. While reversed KL achieves the best accuracy, its runtime (especially during projection) must be quantified against the alternatives. Clarify whether the runtime overhead is justified by the accuracy gains in the rebuttal.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a technically sound paper with a compelling theoretical foundation for accelerating MRF inference. The empirical results are convincing within the scope of the experiments, and the reversed KL method shows significant promise. However, the runtime interpretation in Figure 2 and the absence of absolute runtimes for baseline methods undermine the comparative claims. The scalability gap (small models only) also limits the practical impact. Addressing these points—particularly the runtime reporting and larger-scale validation—would substantially strengthen the contribution. The paper is well-motivated and the approach is novel, but the current evaluation lacks the depth needed to fully assess real-world applicability.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Weak Accept** (pending clarification of runtime metrics and scalability). The technical contribution is solid, but the experimental evaluation requires critical revisions to ensure the claims about runtime-accuracy trade-offs are valid. The proposed methods show clear potential, but the current results do not fully support the practical utility claim without addressing the concerns above.
