This paper proposes to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the original parameters onto a fast-mixing subset of parameters. The fast-mixing set is defined via constraints on the dependency matrix (e.g., spectral-norm bounds that imply rapid mixing), and the authors study projections under several divergences: Euclidean distance, inclusive KL, a piecewise KL based on tractable subgraphs, and the reverse KL optimized via stochastic approximation. For Euclidean projection, they derive a dual method that leverages SVD thresholding while enforcing sparsity to preserve the original graph structure. The piecewise KL uses exact KL on low-treewidth subgraphs, and the reverse KL is optimized with a “pool” of samples and projected gradient steps. Experiments compare these projections to LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original parameters, showing advantages when interactions are strong and time is limited.

Strengths
- Interesting and under-explored direction that bridges variational ideas with MCMC, using fast-mixing parameter constraints as a tractable family. This complements tree-based and mean-field approximations and relates to classic mixing-time results (e.g., Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum, Hayes).
- Clear motivation: when the original model is slow mixing, projecting to a fast-mixing set can yield better estimates under time budgets.
- The dual formulation for Euclidean projection is technically appealing and practically relevant for preserving sparsity.
- Broad empirical comparisons (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs) and several divergences provide a useful perspective.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Clarity of the optimization/projection setup: The constraints Zij D_ij = 0 (with Zij indicating non-edges) enforce zeros off the original edge set, but do they guarantee the projected parameters span the same space, or could the projection zero out some existing edges, effectively shrinking the model? Please clarify the intended notion of “same space” (graphical structure vs. parameter subspace) and whether edge-weight shrinkage to zero is acceptable.
- Overall procedure: It was unclear whether the algorithm performs projected gradient descent to convergence and then runs Gibbs, or whether sampling is interleaved with parameter updates (the “pool” suggests the latter). A concise algorithm box would help, including initialization, projection steps, sampling schedule, and stopping criteria.
- Convergence guarantees: You mention Ergodic Mirror Descent guarantees with appropriate step-size and mixing schedules. Please state the precise conditions (step-size, number of MCMC steps per iteration) under which your projected stochastic procedure converges, and whether the non-convex reverse KL affects guarantees.
- Use of a looser spectral-norm bound in practice: The theory guarantees fast mixing under ||R||2 < 1, yet the experiments use a bound of 2.5 to “preserve rapid mixing” and improve accuracy. This seems to compromise the guarantee. Please justify this choice, quantify how often mixing is still fast under this setting, and discuss diagnostics or empirical checks used to ensure mixing.
- Experimental design:
  - Comparisons to deterministic methods should be time-based for fairness. Reporting “30K iterations” for Gibbs without wall-clock comparisons to LBP/TRW/MF and to your projection procedures obscures computational trade-offs.
  - Error bars are missing; statistical significance is unclear. Please include means and variances (or confidence intervals) over trials.
  - For the second set of experiments, the projection cost is not accounted for. A fair evaluation should compare total wall-clock time: projection + sampling on projected model vs. Gibbs on the original model for the same time budget, with accuracy-time curves.
  - Missing baselines: Given the focus on mixing, comparisons to block Gibbs, tree/block updates, Swendsen–Wang, or related advanced samplers (and, where applicable, lifted Gibbs) would strengthen the case that projection is preferable when these alternatives are available.
  - Sensitivity to hyperparameters (step size, pool size, number of iterations/updates) is not explored. Even a brief sensitivity analysis would increase confidence in robustness.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The technical components are sound overall, but some derivations and algorithmic details are under-specified. The use of an empirical spectral bound above the theoretical threshold should be justified more rigorously.
- Clarity: The paper is generally readable but would benefit from a self-contained algorithm description, clearer explanation of constraints and their effect on the model space, and more explicit optimization schedules and stopping criteria.
- Originality: The idea of projecting onto a fast-mixing set and optimizing reverse KL within that set is novel and timely. The dual projection approach that preserves sparsity is a nice contribution.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for time-limited inference in difficult Ising models. Empirical evidence is promising but would be more convincing with fair timing comparisons, broader baselines, and statistical rigor.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and plausible framework that unifies variational projection and MCMC via fast-mixing constraints.
- Practical algorithm for Euclidean projection with sparsity preservation.
- Empirical indications that projected models yield better accuracy than standard approximations under time constraints.

Cons for acceptance
- Clarity gaps in the dual formulation, constraints, and full algorithmic procedure.
- Incomplete and potentially unfair experimental comparisons (no wall-clock accounting, no error bars, limited baselines).
- Theoretical guarantees vs. practice mismatch for the spectral-norm threshold.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add an algorithm box detailing the projected gradient procedure, sampling interleaving, projection operator, and convergence/stopping criteria.
- Clarify how the constraints maintain the same graphical structure and whether edge weights can be driven to zero.
- Provide convergence statements (or caveats) for the projected stochastic optimization under reverse KL.
- Report wall-clock time vs. accuracy curves, include projection cost, and add error bars.
- Compare against block/tree samplers and, where appropriate, lifted Gibbs.
- Justify and analyze the choice of spectral bound > 1, including diagnostics indicating preserved fast mixing.
- Include sensitivity analysis for step size, pool size, and number of iterations/updates.

Overall, this is a very nice idea with solid potential. With clearer algorithmic exposition and stronger, time-aware experiments (with statistical reporting and broader baselines), the paper would make a compelling contribution.
