- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set (spectral-norm constraint) to enable tractable Gibbs sampling for approximate inference.  
- Uses multiple divergence objectives for projection: Euclidean, inclusive KL (θ||ψ), piecewise KL, and reverse KL (ψ||θ) with stochastic approximation.  
- Builds on mixing-time results from Dyer et al. and Hayes and uses SVD-based spectral-norm projections; Theorem 7 gives a dual that enforces sparsity and absolute-value constraints.  
- Empirically, projected fast-mixing models often yield more accurate marginals within fixed time than Gibbs on original parameters and compare favorably to LBP, TRW, and mean-field on small graphs.  
- Conceptually novel: treats fast-mixing parameter sets as a principled, tractable family trading parameter bias for sampler convergence.  
- Solid theoretical grounding: connects Ising parameters to dependency matrix via Rij ≤ tanh|βij| and uses established mixing-time criteria.  
- Algorithmic contribution: dual formulation for Euclidean projection that preserves graph sparsity while enforcing spectral constraints.  
- Practical method for reverse-KL projection using stochastic approximation and Gibbs sampling on the projected model.  
- Promising empirical regime: strong couplings and limited sampling time where projected models outperform standard variational methods and short-run Gibbs.  
- Missing citations/proofs for some “standard” results (e.g., Theorem 6 spectral projection, Lemma 5 general case) — needs references or proofs.  
- Uses a looser spectral bound in experiments (e.g., 2.5 vs. theoretical 1.0) without justification or evidence that mixing remains rapid under that choice.  
- Dependency matrix R is defined via a max over configurations and is not directly computable at scale; paper should state reliance on the Ising-specific |βij| bound explicitly.  
- Section 4 (projection) is hard to follow: variables and notation are introduced before definition and Theorem 7 uses unexplained symbols; needs clearer derivation and algorithmic steps.  
- Inconsistent KL notation and roles for θ and ψ; standardize divergence direction conventions throughout.  
- Background on R’s properties is somewhat scattered and could be streamlined to what’s necessary for the method.  
- Experiments omit projection computation time, especially costly reverse-KL loops; wall-clock time including projection should be reported or comparisons compute-equalized.  
- Scale is limited to small graphs; larger instances or symmetric models with exact marginals are needed to assess scalability and mixing-time dependence on size.  
- Missing strong baselines: inclusive-KL projection on small graphs could serve as a best-case comparison.  
- The empirical gap between mean-field and reverse-KL projection needs analysis to determine whether gains come from allowing pairwise couplings, improved mixing, or objective properties.  
- Potential broader impact if projection cost is modest and mixing guarantees hold at useful couplings, but current evidence is limited by small-scale experiments and uncertain generalization beyond binary Ising models.  
- Suggested fixes: add proofs/citations for key lemmas, clean up Section 4 notation/derivation, standardize KL notation, include projection time in results, run larger-scale experiments, test inclusive-KL baseline, and justify chosen spectral bound.  
- Acceptance pros: novel, well-motivated idea with clear algorithmic instantiation and promising empirical results in time-limited, strong-coupling settings.  
- Acceptance cons: limited empirical scale, incomplete accounting of projection cost, clarity and citation issues in projection section, and uncertain generalization beyond Ising models.
