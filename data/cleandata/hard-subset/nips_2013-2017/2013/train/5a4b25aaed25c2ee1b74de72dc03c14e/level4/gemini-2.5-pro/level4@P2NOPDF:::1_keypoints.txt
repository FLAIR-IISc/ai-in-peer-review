- Paper proposes projecting a slow-mixing pairwise Ising model onto the nearest “fast-mixing” model (defined by a spectral bound) as an approximate inference method.  
- The projection-based inference idea is novel despite relying heavily on existing rapid-mixing conditions [4,8].  
- Experiments on small models show the approach can yield more accurate marginals than Gibbs sampling, mean field, and belief propagation for a fixed computational budget.  
- Major concern: experiments are limited to small toy models where exact inference is tractable, leaving scalability to larger, realistic models unexplored.  
- Critical omission: the runtime/cost of the projection optimization step is not included in timing comparisons, making performance claims potentially unfair.  
- The relationship between mixing times and model size is not investigated, raising questions about practical applicability as models scale.  
- Applicability beyond binary Ising models is unclear because convergence bounds are harder to establish for non-binary states.  
- Unsupported claims and missing proofs: several theorems/lemmas are stated without citations or proofs (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5); references do not fully cover claimed generality.  
- Properties and computational tractability of the dependency matrix R are insufficiently discussed; maximization in its definition may be intractable and needs explicit treatment.  
- Optimization section lacks clarity: notation (g, M, Lambda) is introduced before definition, and Theorem 7’s optimization is insufficiently explained.  
- Notational inconsistencies and nonstandard conventions (e.g., KL direction and using q/p labels) hinder readability and could confuse readers.  
- Experimental analyses should include projection time, evaluation on larger/intractable models (or alternatives like symmetric models or long MCMC runs), and KL(θ||ψ) “best-case” comparisons via junction tree.  
- The observed large performance gap between mean field and the reverse-KL projection deserves discussion and explanation.  
- Overall: concept is elegant and promising, but methodological clarity, missing proofs, incomplete experiments, and scalability/applicability concerns must be addressed.
