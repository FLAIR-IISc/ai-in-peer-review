- Paper proposes projecting an Ising-model MRF onto the set of fast-mixing models (via a spectral-norm bound on the matrix of absolute edge strengths) and performing inference on the projected model.  
- Projection is defined by divergences between Gibbs distributions and constrained to preserve the original graph structure.  
- Euclidean-distance projection is solved by dualizing and using LBFGS-B.  
- KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL projections are solved with a projected-gradient algorithm; reversed KL requires Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model to compute the projection.  
- Experiments on small random models compare proposed methods (various divergences) against LBP, TRW, Mean Field, and Gibbs on the original model, evaluating accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy tradeoffs.  
- Results: proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and for comparable runtimes also beat Gibbs sampling on the original model; reversed KL performs best among the proposed methods.  
- Reviewer requests clarification of stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B iterations in the projected-gradient algorithm (Section 5.1).  
- Reviewer flags incorrect x-axis labeling ("number of samples") in Figure 2 and supplemental plots—suggests axis should be runtime and requests reporting absolute runtimes (seconds) for all methods including LBP, TRW, MF.  
- Reviewer asks for experiments on larger models (e.g., Flach’s class of random fields with tractable partition functions) to assess scalability and practical utility.  
- Minor edits: line 222 “onto the tree” → “onto graph T.”  
- Minor notation: line 226 “Derivative” should probably read “subgradient.”  
- Overall assessment: interesting paper with convincing empirical results, but practical utility may be limited by high runtime—this concern needs clarification in the rebuttal.
