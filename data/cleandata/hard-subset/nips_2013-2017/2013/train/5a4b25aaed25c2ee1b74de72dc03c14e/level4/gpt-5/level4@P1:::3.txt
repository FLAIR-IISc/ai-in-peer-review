Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles approximate inference in Ising models by introducing a new notion of tractability: instead of restricting to low-treewidth structures (as in mean-field, structured mean-field, LBP, TRW, planar decompositions, or mixtures of trees), it restricts to parameter settings that guarantee rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs sampling. For Ising models, a sufficient condition for fast mixing is that the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute edge strengths is below a threshold, following bounds on mixing via dependency matrices.

The proposed two-step approach is:
1) Project an arbitrary Ising parameterization (which may be slow-mixing) onto the set of fast-mixing parameters, while preserving the graph structure, under a chosen divergence.
2) Run Gibbs sampling on the projected model, which is then guaranteed to mix quickly.

The paper considers four divergences:
- Euclidean distance on parameters: provides a dual formulation leading to a projection via iterative SVD thresholding, solved with LBFGS-B while enforcing graph sparsity.
- KL(θ||ψ) (“inclusive,” zero-avoiding): theoretically appealing but requires expectations under the original, potentially slow-mixing model θ; used only on small graphs.
- Piecewise KL: approximates KL by dropping edges to form tractable subgraphs (trees or low-treewidth graphs) and maximizing over these; easy to compute but empirically weaker than true KL.
- KL(ψ||θ) (“exclusive,” zero-forcing): enables stochastic projected gradient, since expectations are under ψ, which is constrained to be fast-mixing and thus amenable to Gibbs sampling.

For the latter three divergences, the paper employs projected gradient: take a gradient step on the divergence, then project back to the fast-mixing set using the Euclidean projection routine.

Experiments compare the projected models (under all divergences above) against LBP, TRW, naive mean-field, and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters. On small random graphs and grids, the proposed methods consistently outperform LBP, TRW, and MF in marginal accuracy. Within the proposed family, the KL(ψ||θ) projection performs best. With limited sampling time, Gibbs on the projected model also outperforms Gibbs on the original parameters.

Quality:
- The approach is technically sound and builds on known sufficient conditions for rapid mixing via the dependency matrix and spectral norm bounds. The Euclidean projection is carefully formulated with a dual and solved using a standard constrained optimizer (LBFGS-B), preserving sparsity. The stochastic projected gradient approach for KL(ψ||θ) is reasonable given the fast-mixing constraint.
- One implementation detail is missing: the projected gradient algorithm (Section 5.1) nests LBFGS-B inside each gradient step. Please describe stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B loop (tolerances, maximum iterations), and how this interacts with the outer loop’s step size and stopping rule.

Clarity:
- Generally well-written and organized. However, the experimental plots (Figure 2 and supplement) are ambiguous: the x-axis is labeled “number of samples,” but sampling is only required for the KL(ψ||θ) method. For a fair time–accuracy comparison, the horizontal axis should reflect wall-clock runtime for all methods. Please clarify what the current x-axis measures for each method and include absolute running times in seconds. Also report runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF to make the comparison interpretable.

Originality:
- The idea of defining a tractable family by fast-mixing conditions and projecting onto it is novel and distinct from classic variational families (mean-field, TRW, GBP) and from planar or piecewise approximations. The dual Euclidean projection preserving sparsity is a nice technical contribution. The piecewise KL variant is a plausible and practical approximation.

Significance:
- The approach provides a principled way to obtain rapidly mixing approximations that can be sampled efficiently, often yielding better finite-time accuracy than running Gibbs on the original model. This bridges variational approximation and MCMC under explicit mixing-time guarantees. Practical impact will depend on runtime; clearer reporting is needed. Extending beyond Ising models would broaden significance.

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual contribution: project onto a fast-mixing family rather than a low-treewidth family.
- Solid theoretical underpinnings (dependency matrix, spectral norm bound).
- Practical algorithms, including a dual Euclidean projection that preserves sparsity.
- Strong empirical results: consistent gains over LBP, TRW, and MF; best performance with KL(ψ||θ) projection.
- Time–accuracy perspective is emphasized.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification:
- Runtime reporting is insufficiently clear. Please:
  - Explain the x-axis in all plots and provide wall-clock time (seconds).
  - Report runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF for a fair comparison.
- Provide details on the stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B loop in the projected gradient algorithm (tolerances, maximum iterations).
- Consider experiments on larger models. As an option, please evaluate on models from:
  - Boris Flach, “A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function,” TPAMI (to appear, available online),
  which allow polynomial-time inference.
- Minor edits:
  - Line 222: replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T.”
  - Line 226: “subgradient” may be more appropriate than “derivative.”

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and principled framework with theoretical guarantees.
- Practical algorithms and convincing experimental gains over strong baselines.
- Broad interest to the NIPS community at the intersection of variational inference and MCMC.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Practical utility may be limited by computational cost; current runtime reporting does not allow a fully fair comparison.
- Experiments focus on relatively small models; scalability remains to be demonstrated.

Overall assessment:
Interesting and well-motivated paper with convincing empirical results. I recommend addressing the runtime/plot clarification and providing more detailed optimization criteria in the rebuttal. Practical utility could be substantial if the runtime issues are clarified and, ideally, mitigated.
