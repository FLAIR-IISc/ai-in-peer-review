### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel approach for approximate inference in Ising models, particularly for models with high treewidth and strong interactions where standard methods like Gibbs sampling suffer from slow mixing rates. The core idea is to perform inference in two stages: first, project the parameters of the original, potentially slow-mixing model onto a set of "fast-mixing" parameters; second, run Gibbs sampling on this new, projected model to estimate marginals.

The set of fast-mixing models is defined by a sufficient condition from prior work: the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths must be less than a certain threshold (e.g., 1). The paper explores several methods for performing this projection while preserving the original graph structure.
1.  **Euclidean Projection:** The authors derive an algorithm to find the closest parameter set in the Euclidean sense by solving a dual optimization problem using L-BFGS-B.
2.  **KL-Divergence Projections:** The paper investigates projections under three different KL-divergences:
    *   **Forward KL (KL(q||p)):** This is "zero-avoiding" but intractable for slow-mixing models, serving mainly as a theoretical baseline.
    *   **Piecewise KL:** A novel, tractable approximation where the KL-divergence is computed on low-treewidth subgraphs of the model.
    *   **Reversed KL (KL(p||q)):** This is "zero-forcing" and can be optimized using a stochastic projected gradient descent method, as expectations are taken with respect to the fast-mixing distribution.

The authors conduct experiments on 8x8 grids and small random graphs, comparing their projection methods against loopy belief propagation (LBP), tree-reweighted belief propagation (TRW), naive mean-field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters. The results indicate that the proposed methods, especially the reversed KL projection, outperform the variational baselines in accuracy. Furthermore, for a limited time budget, sampling from the projected model yields more accurate marginals than sampling from the original slow-mixing model.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The paper introduces an original and compelling idea: defining a family of "tractable" distributions based on their dynamic properties (fast mixing) rather than their static graph structure (e.g., low treewidth). This is a significant conceptual contribution to the field of approximate inference.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate that the proposed projection methods, particularly the reversed KL-divergence approach, consistently outperform widely-used approximate inference techniques like LBP, TRW, and MF in terms of marginal accuracy.
3.  **Practical Relevance:** The runtime-vs-accuracy comparison is particularly valuable. It shows that for a finite computational budget—a common scenario in practice—projecting first and then sampling is a more effective strategy than running Gibbs sampling directly on a slow-mixing model. This highlights the practical utility of the approach.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement):**

1.  **Clarity of Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental results in Figure 2 are difficult to interpret. The x-axis is labeled "number of samples," but sampling is only used for the reversed KL method and for the final evaluation. It is unclear how this relates to the runtime of the other methods. For a fair and clear comparison, the runtimes for all methods, including the baselines (LBP, TRW, MF), should be reported. Plotting accuracy against absolute wall-clock time (in seconds) would provide a much clearer picture of the trade-offs involved and strengthen the paper's claims.
2.  **Algorithmic Details:** The projected gradient algorithm described in Section 5.1 seems to involve nested loops, where the projection step (`argmin ||ψ' - ψ||2`) is itself an iterative optimization solved by L-BFGS-B. The manuscript would benefit from clarifying the termination criteria for this inner optimization loop, which is crucial for reproducibility.
3.  **Limited Scale of Experiments:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small models (8x8 grids, 10-node graphs). The practical significance and scalability of the approach would be more convincingly demonstrated on larger-scale problems. The authors might consider evaluating their method on models where exact inference is still possible but computationally demanding, such as those from Flach (TPAMI, 2013), which allow for polynomial-time inference on complete graphs.
4.  **Computational Overhead:** The projection step, especially for KL-divergences, introduces significant computational overhead. While the final sampling is fast, the total time (projection + sampling) could be substantial. A more explicit discussion of this overhead is needed to fully assess the method's practicality.

### Detailed Comments

*   **Line 222:** The phrase "projection onto the tree" appears to be a typo. Given the context of using a set of graphs `T`, it should likely read "projection onto graph T."
*   **Line 226:** The piecewise KL-divergence is defined as the maximum of several functions, making the objective non-smooth. Consequently, using the term "subgradient" instead of "derivative" would be more technically precise.

Overall, this is an interesting paper with a novel idea and promising empirical results. However, its contribution would be significantly strengthened by addressing the lack of clarity in the experimental runtime comparisons and providing more detail on the algorithmic implementation.
