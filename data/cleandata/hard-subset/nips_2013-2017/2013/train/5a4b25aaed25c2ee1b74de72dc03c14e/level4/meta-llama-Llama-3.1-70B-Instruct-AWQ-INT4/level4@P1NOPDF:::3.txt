The success of inference by Gibbs sampling in Markov Random Fields (MRFs), specifically the Ising model with two labels, heavily relies on the mixing rate of the underlying Monte Carlo Markov chain. The authors propose an approach to inference that involves the following steps:

1. Project the input model, which may not mix rapidly, onto the set of models that do mix rapidly.
2. Perform inference on the obtained model that mixes rapidly.

The space of fast-mixing models is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute values of Ising edge strengths. "Projection" is defined by divergences of Gibbs distribution, and it is forced to preserve the graph structure. Projection in Euclidean distance is obtained by dualizing the initial task and using LBFGS-B. For other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences are implemented), a projected gradient algorithm is used. Notably, in the case of reversed KL divergence, Gibbs sampling on a fast-mixing model must be performed to compute the projection.

The authors present extensive experiments on small random models, comparing the approximated marginals with the true marginals. The methods tested include the proposed approach (with all the above divergences) and loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The experiments evaluate not only accuracy but also runtime-vs-accuracy. The results show that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and for a reasonable range of runtimes, also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed methods, the one with reversed KL divergence consistently performs the best.

Comments:

The projected gradient algorithm from Section 5.1 actually has two nested loops, with the inner loop being LBFGS-B. Please provide details on when the inner iterations are stopped.

The horizontal axis in the plots in Figure 2 (and the supplement) is unclear. Titled "number of samples," it seems to be misleading since sampling is only used for reversed KL divergence. I believe the horizontal axis should represent the runtime of the algorithm. Similarly, it would be beneficial to report the runtime of LBP, TRW, and MF to ensure a fair accuracy-runtime comparison of all tested algorithms. Please clarify this issue, as it is challenging to interpret the experiments without this information. Providing absolute running times in seconds would be helpful.

Consideration should be given to experimental comparison with larger models. An interesting option is to use models from the paper [Boris Flach: A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function, to appear in TPAMI, available online], which allow polynomial inference.

Minor corrections: On page 222, "onto the tree" should be replaced with "onto graph T." On page 226, it would be more accurate to say "subgradient" rather than "derivative."

This is an interesting paper with convincing empirical results. However, the practical utility of the proposed approach may be limited due to its high runtime, which needs clarification in the rebuttal.
