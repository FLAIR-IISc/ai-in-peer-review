Gibbs-based inference in MRFs (here, binary Ising models) is highly sensitive to the mixing rate of the underlying Markov chain. The paper proposes the following inference strategy:
1) Project the original (potentially slow-mixing) model onto a set of models that are provably fast-mixing.
2) Perform inference on the resulting fast-mixing model.

The fast-mixing set is specified by an upper bound on the spectral norm of the matrix formed by the absolute values of Ising pairwise couplings. “Projection” is defined via divergences between Gibbs distributions and is enforced to preserve the original graph structure. For Euclidean distance, the projection is obtained via a dual formulation solved with LBFGS-B. For other divergences (inclusive KL, piecewise KL, and the reversed KL), a projected gradient approach is used. For the reversed KL divergence, Gibbs sampling (on the fast-mixing model) is required to estimate the gradient and carry out the projection.

The authors present extensive experiments on small random instances, comparing approximate marginals to ground truth. Baselines include the proposed projections (under all listed divergences), loopy BP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters. The evaluation considers not only accuracy but also accuracy versus runtime. Results indicate the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and, over a reasonable runtime range, also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed variants, the reversed KL divergence performs best.

Comments:
- The projected gradient method in Section 5.1 effectively nests an inner LBFGS-B loop. Please provide stopping criteria and convergence tolerances for the inner optimization.
- The x-axis in Figure 2 (and the supplement) is labeled “number of samples,” yet sampling is only used for the reversed KL projection. It seems the horizontal axis should instead reflect wall-clock runtime. Likewise, please report runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF to enable a fair accuracy–runtime comparison across all methods. Clarify this point; without it, the experimental results are difficult to interpret. Please include absolute times in seconds.
- Consider adding experiments on larger models. A useful testbed would be models from:
[Boris Flach: A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function, to appear in TPAMI, available online],
which admit polynomial-time inference.
- Line 222: replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T”.
- Line 226: “subgradient” may be more appropriate than “derivative”.

Overall, this is an interesting paper with convincing empirical evidence. Practical utility may be constrained by runtime; clarification in the rebuttal would help assess this.
