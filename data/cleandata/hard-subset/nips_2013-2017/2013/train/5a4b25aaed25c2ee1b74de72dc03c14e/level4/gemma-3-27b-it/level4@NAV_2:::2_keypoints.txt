- Proposed method: project Ising-model parameters onto a new parameterization satisfying fast-mixing conditions (e.g., Dobrushin) via a constrained optimization under several distance/divergence measures.

- Motivation/strength: tackles accelerating MCMC convergence in Ising models and combines stochastic and deterministic inference in a novel, conceptually sound way.

- Theoretical contribution: analyzes the projection problem under multiple divergences.

- Dual formulation unclear: the description lacks clarity, especially how constraints like Z_ij D_ij = 0 ensure the new parameters remain within the original parameter space.

- Potential restriction concern: the Z_ij D_ij = 0 constraint might unduly restrict the feasible parameter space — needs explanation.

- Algorithmic clarity lacking: unclear whether projected gradient descent is run to completion before Gibbs sampling or interleaved; an explicit algorithm box is needed.

- Optimization guarantees missing: convergence properties or guarantees for the projected gradient descent are not discussed.

- Experimental choice issue: selecting 30,000 Gibbs iterations for some methods is arbitrary and may be misleading.

- Comparison metric issue: should use time-based comparisons when contrasting sampling methods with deterministic approximations.

- Statistical rigor lacking: figures lack error bars and significance assessments.

- Computational cost ignored: experiments do not account for the runtime cost of performing the projection, which may outweigh sampling gains.

- Missing baselines: no comparisons to established techniques like blocked Gibbs sampling or Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs Sampler.

- Overall summary: interesting idea with potential, but requires clearer development of the projection method and a more comprehensive, carefully designed experimental evaluation.

- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, presents a novel deterministic+MCMC approach, demonstrates potential mixing improvements, and provides theoretical analysis of projections.

- Arguments against acceptance: unclear dual formulation, incomplete/insufficient experiments, ignored projection cost, lack of optimization convergence guarantees, and missing relevant comparisons.
