- Novel idea: defines tractable Ising approximations by fast-mixing Gibbs dynamics (spectral norm of absolute interaction matrix < 1) rather than graphical structure.

- Core contribution: algorithms to project slow-mixing Ising parameters onto the fast-mixing parameter set while preserving graph structure via optimization under multiple divergences.

- Euclidean projection: efficient dual formulation with SVD thresholding solved by LBFGS-B.

- KL-based projections: projected gradient descent for piecewise KL, KL(q||p), and reverse KL(p||q).

- Reverse KL practicality: minimizing reverse KL requires sampling from the projected fast-mixing model, which is tractable by construction.

- Empirical results: on small grids and random graphs, projections outperform LBP, TRW, and MF in marginal accuracy.

- Best-performing method: reverse KL projection yields the highest accuracy among proposed methods.

- Sampling benefit: sampling from the projected model gives better estimates than sampling from the original model within realistic time budgets.

- Strength — originality: reframing tractability via mixing properties is a significant, novel perspective.

- Strength — technical quality: sound derivations and effective optimization strategies.

- Strength — empirical validation: convincing comparisons against strong baselines.

- Weakness — experiment clarity: Figure 2's "number of samples" axis is ambiguous and omits projection time; accuracy should be plotted against total wall-clock time for all methods.

- Weakness — reproducibility: projected gradient method lacks specified convergence/stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B Euclidean projection.

- Weakness — scope: experiments are small-scale (8x8 grids, 10-node graphs); scalability to larger, denser benchmarks is needed.

- Minor comments: wording fix—use "projection onto the graph T" instead of "projection onto the tree"; use "subgradient" rather than "derivative" for piecewise KL.
