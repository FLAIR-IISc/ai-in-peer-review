The authors propose improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model’s original parameterization onto a new parameter setting that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion for fast mixing. They cast this projection as a constrained optimization problem, with constraints intended to ensure the new parameters lie in the same space as the original ones, and analyze this projection under several distance/divergence measures.

I find the direction of combining stochastic and deterministic inference underexplored and the core idea interesting. While the notion of modifying the original parameters to accelerate mixing is straightforward, the presentation of the dual of the projection problem is somewhat unclear. For example, how exactly do the zij*dij = 0 constraints guarantee that the new parameterization lives in the same space—couldn’t it end up in a strict subset? I was also uncertain about the overall workflow: do you run the projected gradient steps to convergence and then launch Gibbs sampling, or do you interleave sampling and gradient updates? An algorithm box would greatly clarify the procedure. In addition, is the proposed projected gradient descent guaranteed to converge?

The experiments are not fully convincing. In the first set of experiments, why run the Gibbs sampler for 30K iterations? Since you are contrasting a sampling method with deterministic baselines, a time-based comparison would be fairer. Moreover, the plots lack error bars, making it difficult to assess statistical significance. The second set of experiments compares the original/naive Gibbs sampler with Gibbs samplers under different projections and shows that projection accelerates mixing. However, the time spent computing the projection is not accounted for; for instance, if the projection takes 1 minute and the naive Gibbs sampler could generate 10k samples in that period, the projection might not be worthwhile. Finally, there is no comparison to blocked Gibbs samplers or to Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs Sampler.

Overall, this is a promising idea. However, the development of the projection formulation and method needs clarification, and the experimental evaluation should be strengthened to convincingly demonstrate the method’s utility.
