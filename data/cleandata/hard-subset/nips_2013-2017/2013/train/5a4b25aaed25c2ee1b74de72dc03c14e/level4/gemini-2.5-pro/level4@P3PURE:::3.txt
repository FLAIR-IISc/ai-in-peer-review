### **Summary of the Paper**

The success of inference via Gibbs sampling in pairwise Markov Random Fields, such as the Ising models considered here, is critically dependent on the mixing rate of the underlying Markov chain. This paper proposes a two-step inference approach to address models that mix slowly: first, project the original model onto the space of fast-mixing models; second, perform inference on the resulting fast-mixing projection.

The space of fast-mixing models is defined by imposing a bound on the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths. The projection is defined with respect to a chosen divergence between the Gibbs distributions of the original and projected models, while preserving the original graph structure. The authors explore several divergences: for the Euclidean distance, the projection is found by dualizing the problem and solving it with L-BFGS-B. For KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences, a projected gradient algorithm is employed. Notably, computing the projection for the reversed KL divergence requires running Gibbs sampling on the (fast-mixing) projected model itself.

The authors present extensive experiments on small, random models, comparing the accuracy of the approximated marginals against the true marginals. The proposed methods are benchmarked against Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), Mean Field (MF), and standard Gibbs sampling on the original model. Both final accuracy and accuracy-vs-runtime trade-offs are evaluated. The results demonstrate that the proposed methods consistently achieve higher accuracy than TRW, MF, and LBP. They also outperform standard Gibbs sampling within a reasonable runtime budget. Among the proposed projection methods, the one based on reversed KL divergence consistently delivers the best performance.

### **Comments and Questions**

This is an interesting paper with a novel approach, supported by what appear to be convincing empirical results. However, there are several points that require clarification, particularly regarding the experimental setup.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Experimental Clarity:** A significant point of confusion lies in the experimental evaluation presented in Figure 2 (and the supplement).
    *   The horizontal axis is labeled "number of samples," yet sampling is only integral to the reversed KL divergence method. This makes the comparison across different methods unclear. For a fair and interpretable accuracy-vs-runtime evaluation, the horizontal axis should represent the absolute runtime in seconds for *all* algorithms.
    *   The runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF should also be reported to enable a direct comparison on the same accuracy-vs-runtime plots.
    *   Please clarify this issue. Without a fair comparison based on wall-clock time, the experimental results are difficult to interpret properly.

2.  **Algorithm Details:** The projected gradient algorithm described in Section 5.1 involves a nested loop structure, with L-BFGS-B used in the inner loop. Please provide details on the stopping criteria for these inner iterations.

3.  **Scalability and Model Size:** The experiments are currently limited to small models. It would strengthen the paper to include comparisons on larger models. A potentially interesting class of models to consider are those from Flach et al. ["A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function," TPAMI], which permit exact inference in polynomial time and would provide a valuable benchmark for accuracy and runtime.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Line 222: "onto the tree" should likely be "onto graph T".
*   Line 226: Given the context, would "subgradient" be more appropriate than "derivative"?

### **Conclusion**

The paper presents a promising idea. However, its practical utility hinges on its runtime, an aspect that requires significant clarification in the author rebuttal. If the experimental ambiguity and runtime concerns can be addressed, the proposed technique could be a valuable contribution.
