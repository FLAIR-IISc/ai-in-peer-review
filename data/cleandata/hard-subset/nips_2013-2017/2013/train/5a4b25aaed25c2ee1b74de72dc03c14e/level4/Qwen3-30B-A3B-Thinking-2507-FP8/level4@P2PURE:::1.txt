

The paper aims to accelerate Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions—known for slow mixing—by projecting onto "fast-mixing" models. Specifically, it identifies parameters as close as possible to the original model while satisfying a spectral bound guaranteeing rapid mixing. Experiments demonstrate improved marginal estimates within fixed computation time compared to variational methods (mean field, belief propagation variants) and Gibbs sampling on the original model, in certain regimes.  

The technical approach relies heavily on prior rapid-mixing conditions [4,8], but the novel aspect is projecting to the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method, which to our knowledge has not been explored before. On small "toy" Ising models, results show meaningful gains over strong baselines (standard sampling and contemporary variational methods). However, heightened skepticism is warranted regarding scalability to larger models. Experiments compare mixing times across different models, yet it is unclear how these scale with problem size. Additionally, the computationally expensive projection step required to construct the fast-mixing model is not accounted for in computational cost, and generalization beyond binary states appears unlikely due to the significant challenges in establishing convergence bounds for broader state spaces.  

**CLARITY:**  
The presentation is generally clear for the technical content, but several revisions are needed:  
*   The paper repeatedly cites "standard results" and states them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without reference or proof sketch. Lemma 5 remains unproven, though its reference only covers a special case (zero-field).  
*   The tractability of the dependency matrix \( R \) (involving maximization) should be explicitly addressed; the presumption that Lemma 5 is invoked due to intractability must be stated.  
*   Section 4’s second half is difficult to follow: Theorem 7 references \( g \), \( M \), and \( \Lambda \) before introduction, and Theorem 4 uses unexplained notation. More explanation of the optimization is required; less time should be spent restating results from [8] that are not central to the contribution.  
*   Section 1 uses \( \text{KL}(q \| p) \) for both KL directions, but notation is inconsistent with standard literature: \( q \) denotes the true distribution and \( p \) the approximation in the first paragraph, contrary to near-universal convention.  

**EXPERIMENTS:**  
Figure 2’s time comparison excludes the projection computation cost, as noted ambiguously in Section 6.1. This must be clarified, and results including projection time should be provided. As currently presented, the method effectively leverages a sophisticated variational optimization without cost, diminishing the perceived advantage over standard Gibbs.  

Experiments on larger models are notably absent, despite Gibbs mixing times typically scaling with problem size. Alternative approaches for larger-scale validation should be considered (e.g., models with symmetries enabling exact marginals, or using outputs of extremely long sampling runs as ground truth).  

While KL(\(\theta \| \psi\)) is intractable generally, its exploration as a "best case" for sampling in the approximate model would be valuable (junction tree could be applied to the toy models). Additionally, mean field is a degenerate case of reverse KL projection per the paper, yet the error gap between mean field and reverse KL projection is substantial—this discrepancy requires discussion.  

The core idea of approximating slow-mixing models via projection to the closest fast-mixing model is elegant, and recent mixing bound results are leveraged effectively. However, concerns about experimental comparisons (particularly missing projection cost) and the limited applicability beyond binary models constrain the paper’s impact.
