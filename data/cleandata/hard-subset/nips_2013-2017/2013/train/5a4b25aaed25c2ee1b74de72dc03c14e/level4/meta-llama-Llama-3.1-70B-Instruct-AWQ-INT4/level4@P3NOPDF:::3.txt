This paper proposes a novel approach to inference in Markov Random Fields (MRFs) with two labels, specifically the Ising model. The success of inference using Gibbs sampling in such models heavily relies on the mixing rate of the underlying Monte Carlo Markov chain. To address this, the authors suggest projecting the input model onto a set of models that exhibit fast mixing, and then performing inference on the resulting model.

The space of fast-mixing models is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute values of Ising edge strengths. The "projection" is defined using divergences of Gibbs distributions, ensuring that the graph structure is preserved. The authors employ different methods to obtain this projection, including dualization and the use of LBFGS-B for Euclidean distance, and a projected gradient algorithm for other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences). Notably, in the case of reversed KL divergence, Gibbs sampling is required to compute the projection, but it is performed on a fast-mixing model.

The authors present extensive experimental results on small random models, comparing the approximated marginals with the true marginals. The methods evaluated include the proposed approach (with all the aforementioned divergences), loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The experiments assess not only accuracy but also runtime-vs-accuracy tradeoffs. The results demonstrate that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in terms of accuracy, and for a reasonable range of runtimes, they also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed methods, the one using reversed KL divergence consistently achieves the best performance.

However, there are a few aspects that require clarification. Firstly, the projected gradient algorithm in Section 5.1 involves two nested loops, with the inner loop being LBFGS-B. It would be helpful to provide details on the stopping criteria for the inner iterations. Secondly, the horizontal axis in the plots in Figure 2 (and the supplement) is unclear, as it is titled "number of samples" but sampling is only used for reversed KL divergence. It would be more informative to use runtime as the horizontal axis, which would enable a fair accuracy-runtime comparison across all tested algorithms. Additionally, reporting the absolute running time in seconds would be beneficial. Lastly, considering experimental comparisons with larger models, such as those from the paper "A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function" by Boris Flach, could provide valuable insights.

Minor corrections are also suggested: on page 222, "onto the tree" should be replaced with "onto graph T", and on page 226, "derivative" should be replaced with "subgradient".

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach with convincing empirical results. However, the practical utility of the method may be limited due to high runtime, which requires further clarification in the rebuttal.
