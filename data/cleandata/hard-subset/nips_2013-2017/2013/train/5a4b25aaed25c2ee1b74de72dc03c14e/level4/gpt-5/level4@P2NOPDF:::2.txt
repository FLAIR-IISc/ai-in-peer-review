Summary
The paper proposes improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling for Ising models by projecting the model’s original parameters onto a set that satisfies the Dobrushin condition for fast mixing. The projection is posed as a constrained optimization problem ensuring the new parameters remain within the original parameter space, and multiple distance/divergence measures are considered.

Strengths
- Timely idea that bridges deterministic optimization with stochastic inference, an area that remains relatively under-explored.
- Clear motivation: enforcing a sufficient mixing condition can make Gibbs more practical on challenging instances.
- Studying multiple divergences for projection is valuable and may yield insights into trade-offs.

Clarifications and Methodological Questions
- The dual formulation of the projection problem is not clearly presented. In particular, how do the constraints of the form z_ij * d_ij = 0 guarantee that the projected parameters live in exactly the same space as the original model, rather than a strict subset? Please provide a clearer intuition or proof sketch.
- Workflow ambiguity: Is the procedure (a) run projected gradient to convergence, then execute a standard Gibbs sampler on the projected model, or (b) interleave projection updates with sampling? An explicit algorithm box or pseudocode would clarify the pipeline, stopping criteria, and any warm-starting of the sampler.
- Convergence guarantees: Under what conditions does the proposed projected gradient method converge (e.g., step-size rules, Lipschitz continuity, convexity under each divergence)? Are there guarantees of feasibility with respect to the Dobrushin constraints throughout the iterations, or only at convergence?
- Projection feasibility: Are there cases where the constraints induced by the Dobrushin criterion make the projection infeasible (e.g., overly strong couplings), and how is this handled in practice?

Experimental Evaluation
- Runtime fairness: In the first set of experiments, Gibbs is run for 30k iterations. Since you compare against deterministic methods, wall-clock time or computational budget is a fairer basis than iteration count. Please report time-normalized performance and/or effective sample size per second.
- Variability and significance: The figures lack error bars or confidence intervals. Given the stochastic nature of sampling, report means with standard errors across multiple seeds and, where appropriate, statistical tests to establish significance.
- Cost of projection: The second set of experiments shows that projections accelerate mixing versus the original Gibbs sampler. However, the time to compute the projection is not accounted for. End-to-end comparisons should include the projection overhead. For example, if the projection takes T seconds, how many samples could naive Gibbs produce in T, and what is the net utility after amortizing this cost over a sampling horizon?
- Baselines: Please compare against stronger samplers, such as blocked Gibbs and Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs Sampler. These baselines would help position the proposed approach relative to standard variance-reduction and symmetry-exploiting techniques.
- Practical settings: Provide details on step sizes, stopping criteria for the projection, and how you verify the Dobrushin condition post-projection. Also clarify whether the divergence used for projection is matched to the evaluation metric (e.g., KL vs. MSE on marginals).
- Robustness: Explore sensitivity to initialization, graph structure (e.g., degree, presence of frustration), and coupling strengths. It would help to see how performance changes as the model nears or violates the Dobrushin threshold.
- Qualitative diagnostics: Include mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation plots, effective sample size, Gelman–Rubin where applicable) to substantiate “faster mixing” beyond heuristic measures.

Overall Assessment
This is a compelling idea that marries optimization with MCMC by projecting parameters to satisfy a sufficient mixing condition. The contribution would be significantly strengthened by:
- A clearer and more rigorous treatment of the projection’s dual, including how constraints preserve the original parameter space.
- An explicit algorithmic description detailing when and how projection and sampling are performed, along with convergence conditions.
- A more comprehensive and fair experimental evaluation that includes runtime-normalized comparisons, statistical uncertainty, projection overhead, and stronger sampling baselines.

With these improvements, the work could offer a practical and theoretically motivated pathway to robust Gibbs sampling in challenging Ising models.
