The authors propose a method for improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model’s original parameterization onto a new setting that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion, which is necessary for fast mixing. They formulate this projection as a constrained optimization problem, with constraints designed to ensure the new parameters remain within the same space as the original parameters, and examine this projection under several distance and divergence measures.

In my opinion, methods that combine principles from stochastic and deterministic inference represent a relatively under-explored area, and as a result, I find this to be an interesting idea. While the concept of augmenting the original parameters to improve mixing time is straightforward, I found the description of the dual problem to be somewhat unclear – for example, it’s not immediately obvious how the constraints *z<sub>ij</sub>* *d<sub>ij</sub>* = 0 ensure that the new parameterization remains over the same space (couldn’t it potentially be restricted to a smaller space?). I was also unsure about the overall procedure: is the projected gradient descent performed to completion *before* running a Gibbs sampler, or is sampling interleaved with the gradient updates? An algorithm description box would significantly clarify this point. Furthermore, is convergence guaranteed for the proposed projected gradient descent strategy?

I also found the experiments to be somewhat unconvincing. In the first set of experiments, why was the Gibbs sampler run for 30,000 iterations? Given that the comparison is between a sampling method and deterministic methods, a comparison based on runtime would seem more appropriate. Additionally, the charts lack error bars, making it difficult for the reader to assess the statistical significance of the results. The second set of results demonstrates that Gibbs sampling with the proposed projection leads to faster mixing compared to the original, naive Gibbs sampler. However, the computational cost of performing the projection itself is not accounted for in this comparison. For example, if the projection takes one minute, while the naive Gibbs sampler can generate 10,000 samples in that same time, the projection might not be worthwhile. Finally, why was there no comparison to blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate’s “Lifted Gibbs Sampler”?

Overall, this is a very promising idea. However, the development of the projection problem and the proposed method could benefit from further refinement, and a more comprehensive set of experiments is needed to convincingly demonstrate the utility of the approach.
