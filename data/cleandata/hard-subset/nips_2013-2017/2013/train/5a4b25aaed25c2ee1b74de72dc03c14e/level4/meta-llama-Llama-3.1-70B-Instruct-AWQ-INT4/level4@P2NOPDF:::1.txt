The authors propose a novel approach to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, which are notorious for being "slow-mixing." They introduce several projections to "fast-mixing" models by identifying parameters that closely approximate the original model while satisfying a spectral bound that guarantees rapid mixing. The experimental results demonstrate some regimes where this approach yields improved marginal estimates for a given computation time, outperforming variational methods (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

The technical content of the paper heavily relies on prior results establishing conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]. However, the idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method is a novel contribution that has not been explored before.

The experimental results offer some promising improvements over a range of baselines (standard sampling and contemporary variational methods) in the context of "toy tiny Ising models." Nevertheless, I have concerns regarding the scalability of these results to larger models. The experiments involve comparisons of mixing times of samplers on different models, which makes it challenging to assess how these will scale with problem size. Furthermore, the computationally expensive projection step required to build the fast-mixing model seems to be overlooked in the analysis. Lastly, it is unclear whether this approach can be generalized beyond binary states, as establishing convergence bounds in more general cases is significantly more challenging.

The presentation is generally clear and accessible, considering the technical content. However, some reorganization and clarification are necessary in several areas:

* The paper frequently refers to "standard results" without providing references or proof sketches, such as Theorem 6. Additionally, Lemma 5 is left unproven, and the provided reference only proves a special case of zero-field.
* The properties of the dependency matrix R should be discussed, including its tractability. The maximization involved in R appears to be intractable, and it would be helpful to acknowledge this.
* The second half of Section 4 is difficult to follow due to the introduction of notation without proper explanation. Before Theorem 7, the text references g, M, and Lambda without prior introduction. The statement of Theorem 4 includes unexplained notation. If the optimization is to be discussed, more explanation is needed.
* In Section 1, the notation for KL divergence is inconsistent, and the direction of the divergence is not clearly stated. The first paragraph uses q for the true distribution and p for the tractable approximation, which is opposite to the convention in most related literature.

The experimental section has some limitations and ambiguities. The time comparison in Figure 2 does not include the computation required for projection, which is only briefly mentioned in Section 6.1. This omission makes the improvement over standard Gibbs sampling less convincing, as the proposed method appears to utilize the output of a sophisticated variational optimization without penalty. It would be beneficial to clarify this point and provide results with the projection time included.

It is disappointing that the experiments do not include larger models, given that Gibbs mixing times often depend on problem size. There are alternative options for running experiments in regimes where junction trees are intractable, such as using models with symmetries that allow for the computation of true marginals or taking the output of a long sampling run as truth.

Although KL(θ||ψ) is intractable in general, it would be interesting to explore this as a potential "best case" for how sampling in an approximate model would perform. Junction trees could be used for the toy models in the submission.

The large difference between the mean field error and the error from reverse KL projection deserves discussion, especially since mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection.

The idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is elegant, and the authors effectively leverage recent work on mixing bounds. However, there are concerns regarding the experimental comparisons and the limited range of models to which this approach is applicable.
