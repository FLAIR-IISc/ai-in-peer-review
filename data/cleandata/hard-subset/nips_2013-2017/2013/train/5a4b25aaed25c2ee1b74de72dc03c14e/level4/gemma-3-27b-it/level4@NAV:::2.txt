The authors present a novel approach to accelerate Gibbs sampling in Ising models. Their method centers on projecting the model’s original parameterization to a new setting that fulfills the Dobrushin criterion, thereby enabling faster mixing. This projection is formulated as a constrained optimization problem, with constraints designed to maintain equivalence between the original and projected parameter spaces, and is analyzed using various distance and divergence metrics.

This work tackles an important and relatively unexplored area—the synergy between stochastic and deterministic inference techniques—and represents a potentially valuable contribution. While the core concept of parameter augmentation for improved mixing is intuitive, the explanation of the dual formulation of the projection problem lacks clarity. Specifically, the rationale behind the zij*dij = 0 constraints guaranteeing the projected parameterization remains on the original space is not fully explained; it’s unclear why this constraint prevents projection onto a smaller space. Furthermore, the overall procedural flow is ambiguous: is the projected gradient descent performed to completion *before* initiating Gibbs sampling, or is there an iterative interleaving of sampling and gradient updates? A dedicated algorithmic description would significantly improve understanding. Finally, the convergence properties of the proposed projected gradient descent strategy require further investigation and justification.

The empirical evaluation, while demonstrating improvements, is not entirely persuasive. The choice of 30,000 iterations for the initial Gibbs sampler is not sufficiently motivated. Given the comparison to deterministic methods, evaluating performance based on computational time would provide a more equitable assessment. Moreover, the presentation of results lacks error bars, hindering the assessment of statistical significance. The subsequent comparison of naive Gibbs sampling with projections does demonstrate accelerated mixing, but it fails to account for the computational cost of the projection itself. A projection time of even one minute, if exceeding the time required by the naive Gibbs sampler to generate a comparable number of samples (e.g., 10,000 samples), could negate the benefits of the projection. Lastly, the absence of a comparison to established techniques like blocked Gibbs sampling or Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs Sampler limits the context and impact of the findings.

In summary, this paper presents a promising idea with strong potential. However, the technical development of the projection problem and the proposed method would benefit from further refinement, and a more comprehensive experimental evaluation is needed to convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of this approach.
