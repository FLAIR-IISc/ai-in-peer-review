The paper "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing" proposes a novel approach to inference in Markov random fields (MRFs) by projecting the input model onto the set of models that guarantee fast mixing. The space of fast-mixing models is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute values of Ising edge strengths. Projection is defined by divergences of Gibbs distribution and is forced to preserve the graph structure. The authors derive algorithms for projection in Euclidean distance, KL-divergence, piecewise KL-divergence, and reversed KL-divergence.

The paper is well-motivated, and the approach is interesting. The authors provide extensive experiments on small random models, comparing the approximated marginals with the true marginals. The methods tested are the proposed one (with all the above divergences) and loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The experiments show that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and for a reasonable range of runtimes also Gibbs sampling on the original model. Of the proposed methods, the one with reversed KL divergence performs consistently best.

The paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear. However, there are a few minor issues that need to be addressed. The projected gradient algorithm from section 5.1 has two nested loops, the inner loop being LBFGS-B. It would be helpful to provide details on when the inner iterations are stopped. Additionally, the horizontal axis in the plots in Figure 2 (and the supplement) is not clearly labeled. It is titled "number of samples," but sampling is used only for reversed KL divergence. It would be more accurate to label it as "runtime of the algorithm." Furthermore, it would be helpful to report the runtime of LBP, TRW, and MF to ensure a fair accuracy-runtime comparison of all tested algorithms.

In terms of the quality of the paper, the authors have made a significant contribution to the field of MRFs. The approach is novel, and the results are convincing. However, the practical utility of the approach may be limited due to high runtime. This needs clarification in the rebuttal.

Regarding the originality of the paper, the approach is new, and the results are interesting. However, the authors could have provided more context and comparisons with other related work in the field.

In terms of significance, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of MRFs. The approach is novel, and the results are convincing. The paper has the potential to impact the field of MRFs and related areas.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the approach is interesting. However, there are a few minor issues that need to be addressed. The authors should provide more details on the inner iterations of the projected gradient algorithm and clarify the labeling of the horizontal axis in the plots. Additionally, the authors should report the runtime of LBP, TRW, and MF to ensure a fair accuracy-runtime comparison of all tested algorithms.
