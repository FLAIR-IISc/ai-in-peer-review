This paper addresses the challenge of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions. The core idea is to perform inference not on the original, slow-mixing model, but on a nearby "fast-mixing" approximation. This approximation is found by projecting the parameters of the original model onto the set of models guaranteed to be rapidly mixing, based on established spectral bounds. The authors propose several such projections and demonstrate empirically that, in certain regimes, this approach can yield better marginal estimates for a given computational budget compared to standard Gibbs sampling and popular variational methods like mean-field and belief propagation.

The main strength of this work is its novel and elegant approach to approximate inference. The idea of projecting a difficult-to-sample model onto the nearest model that is provably fast-mixing is, to my knowledge, new. It provides a principled way to leverage recent theoretical advances on mixing time bounds [4, 8] for practical inference. The experimental results, though on small-scale models, show promising improvements over a solid set of baselines, including standard Gibbs sampling and contemporary variational methods.

Despite the novelty of the approach, there are several significant concerns regarding the scalability, experimental evaluation, and practical applicability of the proposed method.

My primary concern is scalability. The experiments are conducted on "toy tiny Ising models" where exact inference via the junction tree algorithm is feasible. However, the performance of MCMC methods, particularly their mixing times, is highly sensitive to the size of the model. It is difficult to judge from the current results how the proposed approach will scale to larger, more realistic problems.

A second major concern is the computational cost of the projection step. The process of finding the nearest fast-mixing model appears to be a computationally expensive optimization problem in its own right. The experimental results, particularly the timing comparisons in Figure 2, seem not to account for this cost. If the time required for this projection is omitted, the comparison with standard Gibbs sampling is not on equal footing, as the proposed method effectively benefits from a sophisticated pre-computation step without penalty. This significantly weakens the claim of improved performance for a given computation time.

Finally, the paper's applicability beyond binary pairwise models is unclear. The entire framework relies on established convergence bounds for Gibbs sampling, which are far more developed for the binary Ising model case. Extending these theoretical guarantees to models with higher-cardinality discrete variables or continuous states is a formidable challenge, which may limit the broader impact of this work.

Below are more detailed comments regarding clarity and the experimental setup.

**Clarity and Presentation:**

The paper is generally well-written, but several sections require significant clarification to be fully understood.
*   **Unsupported Claims:** The paper frequently refers to "standard results" and states them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without providing a citation or a proof sketch. Similarly, Lemma 5 is left unproven, and the provided reference is noted to only prove a special case (zero-field). These claims must be properly substantiated.
*   **Dependency Matrix:** The properties of the dependency matrix `R` should be discussed more thoroughly. The maximization in its definition suggests that computing it is intractable. Please state explicitly if this is the case and clarify how this motivates the use of Lemma 5.
*   **Section 4:** The second half of Section 4 is very difficult to follow. The text refers to `g`, `M`, and `Lambda` before they are formally introduced. Furthermore, the notation used in the statement of Theorem 7 is not adequately explained. This section needs to be rewritten for clarity. Perhaps some space could be saved by not restating results from [8] that are not directly used in the proposed method, and using that space for a more detailed explanation of the optimization procedure.
*   **Notation:** There are inconsistencies in the notation. In Section 1, `KL(q||p)` appears to be used for both directions of the KL divergence. More importantly, the convention of using `q` for the true distribution and `p` for the tractable approximation is the reverse of what is standard in most of the machine learning and statistics literature, which can cause confusion for readers.

**Experimental Evaluation:**

The experimental section needs to be strengthened to support the paper's claims.
*   **Projection Time:** As mentioned above, the timing comparisons in Figure 2 must be clarified. Please state explicitly whether the projection time is included. If it is not, the experiments should be re-run to include this cost, as it is integral to the proposed method.
*   **Model Scale:** It was disappointing not to see experiments on larger models where the junction tree algorithm is intractable. This is where the proposed method would be most useful. The authors could consider evaluating on models where symmetries allow for exact marginal computation, or by using the output of a very long MCMC run as a "ground truth" reference.
*   **Additional Baselines:** While the KL divergence `KL(\theta||\psi)` used for projection is generally intractable, it would be a valuable comparison for the toy models presented. Since the junction tree algorithm is available, this "forward KL" projection could be computed to establish a potential "best case" performance for this family of approximations.
*   **Mean Field Discrepancy:** The paper notes that mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection. However, the experimental results show a large performance gap between the two. This interesting result deserves further discussion and analysis. Why does the more general projection offer such a significant improvement over its degenerate counterpart in these experiments?

In summary, the paper introduces a clever and well-motivated idea for approximate inference by projecting intractable models onto the nearest provably fast-mixing counterpart. However, the current evaluation is limited and raises significant questions about the method's scalability and practical advantages once all computational costs are considered. The clarity of the technical presentation also needs improvement. I believe the paper has potential, but these major concerns would need to be thoroughly addressed in a revised version.
