The success of estimating using Gibbs sampling in MRF (here with only two labels, i.e., an Essing model) depends strongly on the mixing rate of the underlying Monte Carlo Markov chain. The paper suggests the following approach for estimation:

1) Project the input model (which is not rapidly mixed) onto a set of models that are rapidly mixed.
2) Estimate on the obtained model that is rapidly mixed.

The location of the rapidly mixed model is defined by binding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute values of the Essing edge strength. The "projection" is defined by the deviation of the Gibbs distribution. This is forced to preserve the graph structure. Projection in Euclidean distance is obtained by repeating the initial task and using LBFGS-B. For other deviations (KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL deviations are applied), the projected gradient algorithm is used. In the reverse KL deviation, Gibbs sampling (but on the rapidly mixed model) must be performed to calculate the projection.

Extensive experiments are presented on small random models that compare the estimated limits to the actual limits. The methods tested are proposed (with all the above deviations) and Gibbs sampling on LBP, TRW, MF, and the original model. Not only accuracy but also a runtime-vs-accuracy evaluation is performed. Experiments show that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and Gibbs sampling on the original model for a reasonable range of runtime. Among the proposed methods, the method with reverse KL deviation consistently performs best.
