This paper addresses the challenge of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions. The core idea is to project the model parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set, guaranteeing rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling. This is achieved by finding parameters as close as possible to the original model while satisfying a spectral bound ensuring fast mixing. Experiments demonstrate improvements in marginal estimation accuracy compared to standard variational methods and Gibbs sampling with original parameters, particularly when computational time is limited.

The technical foundation builds upon established results concerning mixing conditions [4, 8]. However, the concept of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference technique appears novel. The experimental evaluation focuses on small “toy” Ising models, showing promising improvements over existing baselines—including standard sampling and contemporary variational methods. A primary concern is the scalability of these results to larger, more complex models. The time required for the computationally intensive projection step is not fully accounted for in the reported comparisons, and the method’s generalizability beyond binary state variables is questionable due to the challenges in establishing convergence bounds in more general settings.

**Clarity:**

The presentation is generally clear and accessible, considering the technical complexity. However, several areas require reorganization and further clarification:

*   The paper frequently references “standard results” and presents them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without providing references or even brief proof sketches. Lemma 5 remains unproven, with the cited reference only covering a specific zero-field case.
*   A more detailed discussion of the dependency matrix *R* is needed. Specifically, the computational tractability (or lack thereof) of calculating *R* should be addressed. (It's presumed the maximization involved renders it intractable, which motivates the use of Lemma 5.)
*   Section 4’s second half is particularly difficult to follow. The introduction of *g*, *M*, and *Λ* before their definition hinders comprehension. Theorem 7’s statement utilizes notation that lacks sufficient explanation. If the optimization procedure is central to the contribution, a more thorough explanation is crucial. (Consider reducing the discussion of results from [8] that are not directly utilized.)
*   In Section 1, the notation for KL divergence appears inconsistent. *KL(q||p)* is used for both divergence directions, necessitating a shift in notation to align with established conventions in the field. Furthermore, the initial paragraph introduces *q* as the true distribution and *p* as the approximation, which reverses the common notation.

**Experiments:**

The time comparison in Figure 2 does not appear to incorporate the computational cost of the projection step. A statement in Section 6.1 alludes to this, but lacks clarity; please provide a clear explanation and, if applicable, present results including the projection time. As it stands, the proposed methods benefit from the results of a sophisticated variational optimization without accounting for its computational burden, diminishing the significance of the observed improvements over standard Gibbs sampling.

The absence of experiments on larger models is a notable limitation, given the dependence of Gibbs mixing times on problem size. Exploring regimes where exact inference via junction trees becomes intractable—perhaps by leveraging symmetries to compute true marginals or using a long sampling run as a proxy for truth—would strengthen the evaluation.

While acknowledging the intractability of *KL(θ||ψ)* in general, exploring it as a theoretical “best-case” performance benchmark for sampling in the approximate model would be valuable. Junction tree inference could be employed for the toy models used in the submission.

The paper correctly identifies mean field as a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection. However, the substantial difference in performance between mean field and the reverse KL projection warrants further discussion.

**Overall Assessment:**

The idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is a compelling one, and the paper leverages recent work on mixing bounds effectively. However, concerns regarding the experimental comparisons and the limited applicability of the approach to larger and more general models remain.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of projecting onto the nearest fast-mixing model for approximate inference is a novel contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper builds upon a solid theoretical foundation in Markov chain mixing times and provides a formal analysis of the projection process.
*   **Empirical Results:** While limited in scope, the experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods can outperform existing approaches in certain scenarios.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and accessible, despite the technical complexity of the subject matter.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Scalability:** The computational cost of the projection step and the lack of experiments on larger models raise concerns about the scalability of the approach.
*   **Generalizability:** The method’s applicability may be limited to binary state variables due to the challenges in establishing convergence bounds for more general settings.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The comparison of computational time does not fully account for the projection step, potentially overstating the advantages of the proposed methods.
*    **Clarity in Specific Sections:** The paper contains sections that lack clarity and explanation regarding technical details.



**Recommendation:**

I am leaning towards **reject** due to concerns about the scalability and generalizability, along with the insufficiently detailed experimental comparisons. Addressing the issues with the clarity of Section 4 and adding experiments on larger problem instances would substantially strengthen the paper.
