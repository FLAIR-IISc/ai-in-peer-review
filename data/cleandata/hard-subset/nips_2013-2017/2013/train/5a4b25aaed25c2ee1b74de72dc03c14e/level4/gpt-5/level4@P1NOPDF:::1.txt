The paper aims to accelerate Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions—settings known to be slow-mixing—by projecting the model onto a “fast-mixing” set. Concretely, it selects parameters that are as close as possible to the original model yet weak enough to satisfy a spectral bound guaranteeing rapid mixing. Experiments indicate that in some regimes this can yield better marginal estimates, for a fixed compute budget, than both variational methods (mean field and BP variants) and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

The technical development relies substantially on prior results that provide sufficient conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]. However, the idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model and then sampling there as an approximate inference scheme appears novel and interesting.

On small, toy Ising models, the method shows appealing improvements over strong baselines (standard sampling and contemporary variational methods). My main concern is scalability. The comparisons hinge on mixing time across different models, and it is unclear how these comparisons will scale with problem size. Moreover, the projection step required to construct the fast-mixing model appears computationally heavy, yet its cost is not included in the comparisons. Finally, extending the approach beyond binary variables seems nontrivial, since establishing analogous convergence bounds is significantly harder in more general settings.

CLARITY:
In general, the paper is clear and fairly accessible given the technical content, but some reorganization and clarification are needed:
- The paper repeatedly invokes “standard results,” sometimes even as a formal theorem (e.g., Theorem 6), without citation or proof sketch. Lemma 5 is left unproven, and the cited reference covers only the special zero-field case.
- Please discuss properties of the dependency matrix R. The definition appears to require a maximization that may render it intractable in general; clarify whether this is the case. (I presume Lemma 5 is introduced to avoid this intractability.)
- The second half of Sec. 4 is very difficult to follow. Before Theorem 7, the text references g, M, and Λ before they are defined. The statement of Theorem 4 also uses notation that is not explained. If this optimization is central, more explanation and intuition are needed; conversely, space could be saved by reducing the restatement of results from [8] that are not directly used.
- In Sec. 1, KL(q||p) is used for both directions of the KL divergence; the notation should be switched appropriately. Also, the first paragraph uses q for the true distribution and p for the tractable approximation, which is opposite to standard conventions in the literature; consider aligning with common usage to avoid confusion.

EXPERIMENTS:
- It appears that the timing in Figure 2 does not include the time required for the projection step. Sec. 6.1 has an ambiguous statement on this point. Please clarify and, if projection time was excluded, include results that account for it. As written, the proposed methods seem to benefit from the output of a sophisticated variational optimization without paying its computational cost, which weakens the case for superiority over standard Gibbs.
- The absence of experiments on larger models is disappointing, especially since Gibbs mixing times often depend strongly on the system size. There are feasible options even when junction tree is intractable, such as (i) models with symmetries that allow exact marginal computation, or (ii) using the output of a very long, well-diagnosed MCMC run as ground truth.
- While KL(θ||ψ) is generally intractable, it would still be informative to explore it here as a “best case” for how sampling in an approximate model might perform. On the small toy models, junction tree could be used to evaluate this.
- The paper notes that mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse-KL projection, yet there is a large gap between mean-field error and the error from the reverse-KL projection. This discrepancy deserves discussion (e.g., what structural aspects of the models or constraints in the projection account for the improvement over mean field?).

Overall, the idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the nearest fast-mixing model is appealing, and the paper leverages recent mixing bounds in an elegant way. However, questions about scalability, the omission of projection cost in runtime comparisons, and the uncertain path to non-binary generalizations temper my enthusiasm. Addressing the clarity issues and strengthening the experimental evaluation—especially with larger or more challenging models and with full accounting of computational costs—would significantly improve the paper.
