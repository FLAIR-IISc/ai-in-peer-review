The paper proposes accelerating Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model’s original parameters onto a new parameter set that lies in a fast-mixing regime (e.g., satisfies a Dobrushin-style condition). The projection is posed as a constrained optimization problem to keep the new parameters defined over the same space as the original model, and is examined under several distance/divergence measures.

I view the attempt to integrate ideas from stochastic and deterministic inference as under-explored and therefore interesting. While the intuition of adjusting parameters to improve mixing is straightforward, I found parts of the dual formulation of the projection unclear—specifically, how do the zij*dij=0 constraints ensure the new parameterization lives on the same space rather than a strict subset? I was also unsure about the overall workflow: do you first run projected gradient to convergence and then launch Gibbs, or are sampling steps interleaved with gradient updates? A compact algorithm box would clarify this. Additionally, is the proposed projected gradient descent scheme supplied with a convergence guarantee?

I was not fully convinced by the experiments. In the first set, why fix the Gibbs chain length to 30K iterations? Since a sampling method is being compared to deterministic methods, a wall-clock (or compute-normalized) comparison would be fairer. Also, the plots lack error bars, making it hard to assess statistical significance. The second set contrasts the original/naive Gibbs sampler with Gibbs under projected parameters and shows faster mixing, but the time required to compute the projection is not accounted for (e.g., if projection takes 1 minute during which naive Gibbs could draw 10K samples, the projection might not be advantageous). Finally, it would be helpful to include comparisons to blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate’s “Lifted Gibbs Sampler.”

All in all, a compelling idea. However, the development and exposition of the projection method need some tightening, and the empirical section should be strengthened (especially with time-aware comparisons and uncertainty quantification) to fully demonstrate the method’s utility.
