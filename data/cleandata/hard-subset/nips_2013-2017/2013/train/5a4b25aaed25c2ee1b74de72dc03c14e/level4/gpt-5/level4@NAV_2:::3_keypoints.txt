- Problem and approach: Gibbs inference is sensitive to mixing; authors propose projecting slow models onto a provably fast-mixing set and performing inference there.  
- Fast-mixing constraint: Fast-mixing set specified by an upper bound on the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute Ising pairwise couplings.  
- Projection definitions: Projection performed via divergences between Gibbs distributions while preserving graph structure.  
- Optimization methods: Euclidean projection solved via a dual formulation with LBFGS-B; inclusive KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL use a projected gradient approach.  
- Reversed KL detail: Reversed KL projection requires Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model to estimate gradients.  
- Experiments: Extensive tests on small random instances comparing proposed projections, LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on original parameters, evaluating accuracy and accuracy vs runtime.  
- Results: Proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and outperform original-model Gibbs over a reasonable runtime range; reversed KL variant performs best.  
- Request for optimizer details: Provide stopping criteria and convergence tolerances for the inner LBFGS-B loop in the projected gradient method.  
- Runtime reporting issue: Figure axis labeled “number of samples” is misleading (sampling used only for reversed KL); axis should reflect wall-clock runtime and absolute runtimes (in seconds) for LBP, TRW, and MF should be reported.  
- Suggestion for larger-scale evaluation: Add experiments on larger models (e.g., Flach’s random fields with tractable partition functions).  
- Minor edits: Change “onto the tree” to “onto graph T” (line 222) and use “subgradient” instead of “derivative” (line 226).  
- Overall assessment: Interesting paper with convincing empirical evidence but practical utility may be limited by runtime; clarify runtime concerns in the rebuttal.
