- Proposes a two-step inference: project slow-mixing pairwise MRFs onto a space of fast-mixing models, then perform inference on the projection.  
- Defines fast-mixing models via a bound on the spectral norm of the absolute interaction matrix and preserves the original graph structure during projection.  
- Projection methods: Euclidean distance via dualization and L-BFGS-B; KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL via projected gradient; reversed KL requires Gibbs sampling on the projected model.  
- Experiments on small random models compare approximated marginals to true marginals and benchmark against LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling.  
- Results claim proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, LBP, and Gibbs (within runtime budget); reversed KL projection performs best.  
- Major concern: Figure 2’s x-axis labeled “number of samples” is misleading—only reversed KL uses sampling; accuracy-vs-runtime should use wall-clock time for all methods.  
- Request to report runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF so comparisons on accuracy-vs-runtime are fair and interpretable.  
- Need details on stopping criteria for inner L-BFGS-B iterations within the projected gradient nested-loop algorithm.  
- Scalability concern: experiments limited to small models; recommend testing larger models and tractable complete-graph random fields (Flach et al.) as benchmarks.  
- Minor edits: change "onto the tree" (Line 222) to "onto graph T"; consider using "subgradient" instead of "derivative" (Line 226).  
- Conclusion: Idea is promising and empirically convincing, but practical utility depends on clarifying runtime and experimental ambiguities in the rebuttal.
