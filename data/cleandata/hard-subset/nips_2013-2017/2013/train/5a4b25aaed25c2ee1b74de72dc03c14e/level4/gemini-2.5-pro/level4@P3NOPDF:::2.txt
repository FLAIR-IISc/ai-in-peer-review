This paper proposes a method for improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models. The core idea is to project the model's original parameters onto a new parameter set that is guaranteed to satisfy the Dobrushin criterion for rapid mixing. The authors formulate this projection as a constrained optimization problem, exploring several distance and divergence measures, and propose a projected gradient descent algorithm to solve it.

The central idea of combining principles from stochastic MCMC methods and deterministic inference is novel and promising. It addresses the important and practical problem of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling. The approach of modifying the model's parameters to provably improve mixing, while staying "close" to the original model, is an interesting direction for research.

However, the paper in its current form has several weaknesses in its exposition and experimental evaluation that prevent a stronger recommendation.

**Clarity and Methodological Details:**

The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of the proposed method. Specifically:

1.  **Dual Formulation:** The description of the dual of the projection problem lacks clarity. For instance, it is not immediately obvious how the complementarity constraints `zij*dij = 0` ensure that the new parameterization is defined over the same graphical model structure as the original. While these constraints prevent the inclusion of new edges, it seems they could potentially lead to a sparser graph (a subgraph of the original). A more detailed explanation or a brief proof sketch would be helpful to clarify this point.

2.  **Overall Procedure:** The high-level procedure for applying the method is ambiguous. It is unclear whether the projected gradient descent is run to completion once as a pre-processing step before running the Gibbs sampler, or if the sampling and optimization steps are interleaved in some fashion. Including a clear algorithm block in the paper would significantly improve reproducibility and reader understanding.

3.  **Convergence:** The paper does not discuss whether the proposed projected gradient descent algorithm is guaranteed to converge to a solution. A discussion of the convergence properties, even if standard for this class of algorithm, would strengthen the theoretical underpinnings of the method.

**Experimental Evaluation:**

The experiments are not sufficiently rigorous to be convincing of the method's practical utility.

1.  **Comparison with Deterministic Methods:** In the first set of experiments, the proposed sampling method is compared against deterministic inference methods. The rationale for running the Gibbs sampler for a fixed 30,000 iterations is not provided. A more standard and equitable comparison would be based on wall-clock time, showing the trade-off between computational cost and solution quality. Furthermore, the plots lack error bars, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the reported results.

2.  **Overhead of Projection:** The second set of experiments compares the naive Gibbs sampler with the proposed samplers using projected parameters. While these results effectively demonstrate that the projection leads to faster mixing (in terms of sample-efficiency), they critically omit the computational cost of performing the projection itself. This overhead could be substantial, and without accounting for it, the comparison is incomplete. For example, if the time spent on projection could have been used to generate a large number of samples from the naive Gibbs sampler, the practical benefit of the proposed method becomes less clear. A fair comparison should include the projection time as part of the total runtime.

3.  **Missing Baselines:** The experiments only compare against a naive Gibbs sampler. To demonstrate the value of this contribution, it is essential to compare against other established methods for improving Gibbs sampler performance. Relevant baselines would include blocked Gibbs sampling, which is a standard technique for improving mixing, and potentially more advanced methods like Gogate's "Lifted Gibbs Sampler" (UAI 2011).

**Conclusion:**

In summary, the paper introduces a very interesting and elegant idea for accelerating Gibbs sampling. However, the manuscript requires significant revision. The description of the method needs to be clarified, particularly regarding the dual formulation and the overall algorithmic procedure. More importantly, the experimental evaluation needs to be substantially strengthened with fairer comparison metrics (i.e., wall-clock time), inclusion of error bars, accounting for the projection overhead, and comparisons to more competitive baselines. While the core idea is promising, the current evidence is insufficient to demonstrate its practical effectiveness.
