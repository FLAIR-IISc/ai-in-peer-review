- Addresses slow mixing of Gibbs samplers for pairwise Ising models by projecting the original model onto a set of “fast-mixing” models using a spectral bound.  
- Technical novelty: projecting onto the nearest rapidly-mixing model as approximate inference is novel, though it builds heavily on prior rapid-mixing conditions.  
- Empirical claim: on small synthetic Ising models, the method yields better marginal estimates for a given compute time versus standard Gibbs and variational methods in certain regimes.  
- Major scalability concern: unclear whether improvements will hold for larger, realistic models because mixing times and costs may not extrapolate.  
- Projection cost concern: the computational expense of finding the fast-mixing projection appears high and is not clearly included in runtime comparisons.  
- Generalization concern: unclear if the approach extends beyond binary states, since convergence bounds for general discrete spaces are much harder.  
- Clarity issue: several “standard results” are stated as theorems or lemmas without references or proof sketches (e.g., Lemma 5, Theorem 6).  
- Clarity issue: properties and computability of the dependency matrix R are not sufficiently discussed; its definition suggests intractability.  
- Clarity issue: Section 4’s second half is hard to follow—variables (g, M, Lambda) and notation in Theorem 7 are introduced before definition and need reorganization.  
- Notation issue: KL divergence notation is inconsistent and q/p roles are defined nonstandardly; adopt standard convention for readability.  
- Experimental validation: Figure 2’s runtime comparisons must clarify whether projection time is included; if excluded, experiments should be rerun including it.  
- Experimental scope: absence of larger-model experiments is a significant weakness; suggest using symmetric models or long MCMC runs as ground truth where exact inference is infeasible.  
- Additional baseline: evaluate forward KL (KL(p_theta || p_psi)) on small models via junction tree to provide a best-case baseline.  
- Unexplained results: the large performance gap between the proposed reverse-KL projection and mean field (a degenerate case) needs explanation.  
- Overall summary: elegant, novel idea leveraging recent mixing-time theory, but experimental evaluation is unconvincing due to excluded projection costs, small-scale tests, and unclear generality beyond binary Ising models.
