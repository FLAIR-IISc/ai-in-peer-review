- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set to guarantee rapid Gibbs convergence, using four divergence measures.  
- Main technical contribution: a dual algorithm for Euclidean projection and extensions to KL divergences (Theorem 7).  
- Reframes established rapid-mixing conditions as a practical inference strategy rather than just theory.  
- Technically sound: correctly identifies dependency matrix R and spectral-norm conditions linking interactions to mixing.  
- Projection algorithms are theoretically well-founded but computational complexity and scalability are under-discussed.  
- Experiments show improved marginal estimates over standard variational methods and original Gibbs on small models.  
- Experimental evaluation is limited to small-scale cases (8x8 grids, 10-node graphs), insufficient for real-world claims.  
- Presentation suffers from clarity issues: frequent unelaborated "standard results" and inconsistent notation (e.g., KL ordering).  
- Section 4 is hard to follow due to abrupt introduction of variables (g, M, Λ) and insufficient context.  
- Would benefit from a clearer explanation of computational challenges in computing dependency matrix R and the role of Lemma 5.  
- Originality: novel application of projection to fast-mixing models and generalizes mean-field as a special case.  
- Significance is limited by lack of experiments on larger or junction-tree-intractable models where mixing is truly problematic.  
- Major concerns: missing scalability analysis, projection cost omitted from timing comparisons, and no extension beyond binary Ising models.  
- Recommendation: reject unless authors provide stronger scalability evidence, clearer presentation, and experiments on larger/realistic models.
