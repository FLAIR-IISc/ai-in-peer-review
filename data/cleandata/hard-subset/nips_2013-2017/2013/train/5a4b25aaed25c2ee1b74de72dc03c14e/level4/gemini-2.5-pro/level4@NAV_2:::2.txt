This paper introduces an alternative notion of a "tractable" family of distributions for approximate inference. Rather than relying on low-treewidth graphical structures, the authors define tractability based on "fast-mixing" properties, where Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to converge rapidly. Specifically for Ising models, they leverage the condition that the spectral norm of the interaction matrix must be less than one. The core contribution is a method to project an arbitrary, potentially slow-mixing, Ising model's parameters onto this fast-mixing set. The authors derive a projection algorithm for the Euclidean distance and then extend this to handle KL and piecewise KL-divergences using a projected gradient descent strategy. This approach is contrasted with standard variational methods like mean-field and loopy belief propagation, which typically approximate the target distribution with one that is structurally simpler (e.g., fully factorized or tree-structured).

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of defining a tractable family of distributions based on dynamic properties (fast mixing) rather than static graphical structure is novel and significant. This approach, which combines principles from MCMC and optimization-based inference, represents a promising and relatively underexplored direction in the field.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity:** The paper would benefit from increased clarity in several key areas.
    *   The description of the dual formulation for the Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) could be clarified. For instance, it is not immediately obvious how the constraint `Zij*Bij = 0` enforces that the projected parameters `B` are defined on the same graph structure as the original parameters `A`, as opposed to a subgraph.
    *   The overall algorithm, particularly for the KL-divergence projections, is difficult to follow. It is unclear whether the projected gradient descent is run to completion before sampling begins, or if the projection and sampling steps are interleaved. A formal algorithm block would greatly enhance the paper's reproducibility.

*   **Quality:** The technical and experimental soundness could be improved.
    *   While the paper asserts that the stochastic gradient strategy is an instance of Ergodic Mirror Descent and thus guaranteed to converge, it does so without a detailed discussion of whether the specific conditions (e.g., step-size schedule, number of MCMC steps) are met in the implementation to ensure this guarantee holds.
    *   The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating a proof of concept, is not fully convincing.
        1.  The comparison between the proposed sampling-based method and deterministic methods (LBP, TRW, MF) should be based on total computation time, not a fixed number of iterations (e.g., 30,000 for Gibbs). A time-based comparison would provide a fairer assessment of practical utility.
        2.  The plots lack error bars, making it impossible to assess the statistical significance of the reported improvements over baselines.
        3.  A critical omission is the failure to account for the computational cost of the projection itself in the time-accuracy plots. If the projection step is time-consuming, its benefits might be outweighed by running the original Gibbs sampler for a longer duration. A fair comparison must include this overhead.
        4.  The experiments would be more compelling with a comparison to other advanced MCMC methods designed to improve mixing, such as block Gibbs sampling.

**Summary:**
In summary, the paper presents a highly original and promising idea for approximate inference. However, the current manuscript requires significant improvements in the clarity of the method's description and a more rigorous and fair experimental evaluation to fully demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed technique.
