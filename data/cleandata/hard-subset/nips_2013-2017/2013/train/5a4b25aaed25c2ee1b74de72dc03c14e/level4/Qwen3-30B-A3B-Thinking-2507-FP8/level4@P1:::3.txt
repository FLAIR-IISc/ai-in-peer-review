

The paper proposes a novel approach to inference in high-treewidth Ising models by projecting slow-mixing parameter sets onto a fast-mixing subset defined by a spectral norm constraint on the absolute interaction matrix. This conceptually extends prior work on tractable approximations (e.g., mean-field, loopy BP [14,16,19,21]) by shifting focus from graph structure to parameter conditions guaranteeing rapid Gibbs mixing. The authors leverage Theorem 4 (Hayes [8]) to establish that fast mixing occurs when the spectral norm of the dependency matrix is bounded below 1, and derive four projection methods to map arbitrary parameters onto this set while preserving graph structure. 

**Strengths:**  
1. **Theoretical contribution:** The core insight—using parameter-space constraints (not graph structure) to ensure fast mixing—is original and well-motivated. Theorem 7 provides an elegant dual algorithm for Euclidean projection via SVD thresholding, addressing a key technical challenge.  
2. **Experimental rigor:** The evaluation comprehensively compares projected methods (Euclidean, piecewise KL, reversed KL) against LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on original parameters across grid and random graphs. Results consistently show superior marginal accuracy (measured by mean absolute error) for all projected methods, especially reversed KL, even with limited sampling time.  
3. **Practical relevance:** The reversed KL approach (Section 5.4) is computationally feasible via Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing target, making it viable for real-world use. The empirical trade-off between projection cost and inference speed is clearly articulated.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Experimental clarity:** The horizontal axis in Figure 2 (and supplement) is labeled "number of samples" but samples are only used in reversed KL projection. This mislabeling obscures the runtime comparison. **Crucially, the paper reports relative steps, not absolute runtime (seconds), making fair comparison with LBP/TRW/MF impossible.** Including absolute runtimes (e.g., "2.3s for TRW vs. 15.7s for reversed KL projection + inference") is essential.  
2. **Spectral norm conservatism:** The authors note (Section 7) that the spectral bound (norm < 1) is overly conservative compared to the known Ising threshold (β<0.4407), requiring β<0.25 for the bound. While they use norm ≤2.5 in practice, this gap undermines the theoretical guarantee and warrants discussion.  
3. **Projection implementation:** The nested optimization (Section 5.1) uses LBFGS-B as an inner loop but omits stopping criteria. Clarifying when inner iterations terminate (e.g., "LBFGS-B runs until gradient norm < 10⁻³") is necessary for reproducibility.  
4. **Scope limitation:** The focus on Ising models (two labels) excludes broader MRFs. While future work mentions generalization, the current results lack validation on larger or non-binary models (e.g., Flach’s tractable graphs [20]).  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pros:* Novel fast-mixing parameter space concept; strong empirical evidence for marginal accuracy; reversed KL method is practical and effective.  
*Cons:* Critical experimental clarity gaps (runtime reporting); spectral bound conservatism; insufficient validation on larger models.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to inference approximation and presents compelling empirical results. However, the lack of absolute runtime data for all methods severely limits the interpretability of the "accuracy-runtime" claims. Without this, the experimental comparison remains incomplete. The spectral bound conservatism is a minor theoretical limitation but does not invalidate the core approach.  

**Specific Suggestions:**  
- **Figure 2:** Relabel horizontal axis as "Runtime (seconds)" and report absolute times for all methods (LBP/TRW/MF included).  
- **Section 7:** Explicitly state that the spectral bound is a *sufficient* but *not necessary* condition, and discuss how tighter bounds (e.g., β<0.4407) could improve practicality.  
- **Section 5.1:** Add a sentence specifying inner loop termination criteria for LBFGS-B.  
- **Future work:** Consider testing on Flach’s tractable graphs [20] to demonstrate scalability beyond small grids.  

The paper is technically sound and addresses a meaningful challenge in approximate inference. With minor revisions to experimental reporting, it would be a strong contribution to NIPS. The current work is **accept-worthy** pending clarification of runtime metrics.
