- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set defined by a spectral-norm condition to ensure rapid Gibbs sampling convergence.  
- Main contribution is a constrained optimization method to compute that projection under four divergences: Euclidean, KL(q||p), a novel piecewise KL, and KL(p||q).  
- Novel and practically relevant idea connecting MCMC mixing-time theory (Dobrushin-like criteria) with variational/approximate inference.  
- Framing tractability by rapid mixing rather than structural simplicity offers a fresh perspective on approximate inference.  
- Developing projection methods for multiple divergences, including a new piecewise KL, is a valuable technical contribution.  
- Exposition of the projection method is unclear, particularly the dual formulation (Theorem 7) and the interpretation of the constraint that seems to allow edge removal.  
- The procedure for KL-divergence projections is ambiguous—unclear if projected-gradient steps are run to convergence or interleaved with sampling; an explicit algorithm box is needed.  
- Convergence guarantees (e.g., relation to Ergodic Mirror Descent) are cited but not explicitly discussed for the implemented projected gradient strategy.  
- Experimental comparisons may be unfair by using fixed Gibbs iterations instead of wall-clock time, ignoring per-iteration cost differences with deterministic baselines.  
- Time-accuracy plots omit the computational overhead of computing the projection, which could negate practical benefits.  
- Results lack error bars, so statistical significance and variability across random trials are not assessed.  
- Missing stronger MCMC baselines (e.g., block Gibbs, Lifted Gibbs) that are designed to improve mixing, limiting comparative claims.  
- Overall: a promising, original idea that needs clearer technical exposition and more thorough, fair experiments to demonstrate practical utility.
