- Proposes projecting Ising models onto a set of fast-mixing models (bounded spectral norm of absolute edge strengths) and performing inference on the projected model.  
- Defines projection via divergences between Gibbs distributions and enforces graph-structure preservation.  
- Solves Euclidean projection by dualizing and using L-BFGS-B; uses a projected gradient algorithm for KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL.  
- Reversed KL projection requires Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model to compute the projection.  
- Experiments on small random models compare proposed methods (all divergences) to loopy BP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original model, assessing accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy.  
- Results show proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and loopy BP in accuracy and often beat Gibbs on the original model for reasonable runtimes; reversed KL performs most consistently.  
- Request to specify the stopping criterion for the inner L-BFGS-B iterations in the projected gradient algorithm (Section 5.1).  
- Figure 2’s horizontal axis labeled “number of samples” is misleading—should reflect runtime; report absolute runtimes (seconds) and include runtimes for loopy BP, TRW, and MF for fair comparison.  
- Suggest extending experiments to larger models, e.g., using Flach’s tractable random-field models for polynomial-time inference.  
- Minor edits: replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T” (Line 222) and consider “subgradient” instead of “derivative” (Line 226).  
- Overall: interesting approach with convincing empirical results, but practical utility may be limited by high runtime—clarification needed in rebuttal.
