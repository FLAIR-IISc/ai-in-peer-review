- Paper proposes projecting a slow-mixing Ising model onto a nearby model with spectral bounds that guarantee rapid mixing to enable approximate inference via Gibbs sampling.  
- The projection minimizes a distance to the original model while enforcing rapid-mixing spectral constraints.  
- Experiments on small Ising models show improved marginal estimates over Gibbs, mean field, and belief propagation baselines.  
- The idea of projecting onto the nearest fast-mixing model is novel and builds appropriately on prior rapid-mixing results.  
- Major concern: scalability to larger, realistic models is unclear and not demonstrated.  
- The computational cost of the projection step appears omitted from timing comparisons and overall analysis.  
- Applicability likely limited to binary-state models because convergence bounds for general state spaces are more complex.  
- Several clarity issues: standard results are presented as theorems without references or proof sketches, harming reproducibility.  
- Lemma 5 is given without proof and the cited reference only covers a special (zero-field) case.  
- The dependency matrix R needs a clearer discussion, including whether computing its defining maximization is tractable.  
- Section 4 has presentation problems: variables (g, M, Lambda) are referenced before definition and Theorem 4 uses insufficiently explained notation.  
- The paper overemphasizes restating results from [8], including ones not directly used, instead of focusing on new contributions.  
- Notation confusion in Section 1: KL(q||p) is used inconsistently and q/p roles are reversed relative to standard convention.  
- Experimental suggestions: include projection time in timings, test larger or differently structured models (or use long runs/junction tree where possible), and analyze KL(θ||ψ) as a best-case metric; also discuss why mean field differs from true reverse-KL projection.
