The authors present a novel approach to accelerate Gibbs sampling in Ising models. Their method centers on projecting the model’s original parameterization to a new setting that fulfills the Dobrushin criterion, thereby facilitating faster mixing. This projection is elegantly formulated as a constrained optimization problem, with constraints designed to maintain consistency between the original and projected parameter spaces, and is analyzed using various distance and divergence metrics.

This work explores a relatively nascent area – the integration of stochastic and deterministic inference techniques – and represents a potentially valuable contribution. While the core concept of parameter augmentation to enhance mixing is intuitive, the explanation of the projection problem’s dual formulation could benefit from further clarity. Specifically, the justification for how the *z<sub>ij</sub>*d<sub>ij</sub> = 0 constraints guarantee the projected parameters remain within the original parameter space is not immediately obvious; it’s unclear why this wouldn’t potentially restrict the space. Furthermore, the overall procedural flow remains somewhat ambiguous. Is the projected gradient descent executed to completion *prior* to initiating Gibbs sampling, or is there an interleaving of sampling and gradient updates? Inclusion of a dedicated algorithmic description would significantly improve understanding. Finally, the convergence properties of the proposed projected gradient descent strategy warrant discussion.

The experimental evaluation, while promising, requires strengthening. The choice of 30,000 iterations for the initial Gibbs sampler is not fully justified. Given the comparison to deterministic methods, evaluating performance based on computational time would provide a more equitable assessment. Moreover, the presentation of results lacks error bars, hindering the assessment of statistical significance. The second set of results demonstrates improved mixing with the projected Gibbs samplers, but crucially omits the computational cost of the projection itself. A projection time of, for example, one minute could negate the benefits if the naive Gibbs sampler can generate a substantial number of samples (e.g., 10,000) within that same timeframe. Finally, the absence of comparisons to established techniques like blocked Gibbs sampling or Gogate’s Lifted Gibbs Sampler limits the contextualization of the proposed method’s performance.

In summary, this is a compelling idea with significant potential. However, further refinement of the projection problem formulation and the proposed method, coupled with a more comprehensive experimental analysis, are necessary to fully demonstrate its practical utility and convince of its advantages.
