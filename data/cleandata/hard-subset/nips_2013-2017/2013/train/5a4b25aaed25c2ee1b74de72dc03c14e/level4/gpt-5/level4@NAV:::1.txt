Aims to accelerate the mixing of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong couplings, which are known to mix slowly. The paper proposes several projections onto “fast-mixing” surrogates; in essence, it finds parameters that remain as close as possible to the original while being sufficiently weakened to satisfy a spectral criterion that guarantees rapid mixing. Empirical results show regimes where this yields better marginal estimates for a fixed compute budget than variational baselines (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs run on the original model.
The technical development leans substantially on prior work that provides sufficient conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]. However, I have not previously seen the idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model used as an approximate inference technique.
On small “toy” Ising instances, the method delivers notable gains over strong baselines (standard sampling and modern variational methods). My main reservation is that these findings may not carry over to larger problems. The experiments hinge on comparing mixing behavior across different models, and it is difficult to infer how these comparisons scale with model size. In addition, the projection step required to obtain the fast-mixing model appears computationally heavy and its cost is seemingly omitted. Finally, extension beyond binary variables is uncertain, since establishing general convergence guarantees is substantially harder.

CLARITY:
- Overall the exposition is clear and fairly accessible given the technical level. That said, some restructuring and added detail would help:
- The paper repeatedly appeals to “standard results” and even presents them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without citations or proof sketches. Lemma 5 is unproved, and the cited source only handles the special zero-field case.
- Please discuss properties of the dependency matrix R. The maximization suggests it may be intractable; explicitly state whether it is (I assume this motivates invoking Lemma 5).
- The latter part of Sec. 4 is very hard to follow. Before Theorem 7, the text references g, M, and Lambda prior to defining them. Theorem 4’s statement also uses notation that is not properly introduced. If this optimization is to be presented, more explanation is needed. (Potentially compress material re-stating results from [8] that are not directly used.)
- In Sec. 1, KL(q||p) is used for both KL directions when the notation must flip for one. Also, the first paragraph sets q as the true distribution and p as the tractable approximation, opposite to common practice.

EXPERIMENTS:
- The timing comparison in Figure 2 appears to exclude the projection cost. Sec. 6.1 makes a somewhat ambiguous remark along these lines; please clarify and, if true, include results that account for projection time. As presented, the proposed approach effectively benefits from a nontrivial variational optimization at zero cost, which weakens the case against standard Gibbs.
- It is disappointing that there are no experiments on larger models, given that Gibbs mixing typically depends on problem size. Feasible options include models with symmetries enabling exact marginals, or using very long runs as a proxy for ground truth.
- While KL(θ||ψ) is generally intractable, it would still be informative as a “best case” benchmark for sampling in an approximate model. (Junction tree is viable on the toy instances used here.)
- Mean field is a degenerate instance of the reverse-KL projection, as noted, yet its error differs substantially from the reverse-KL projection’s error. This discrepancy merits discussion.

The central idea—approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model—is appealing, and the use of recent mixing bounds is elegant. Nonetheless, questions about the experimental comparisons and the narrow scope of models to which the method likely applies temper the overall impact.
