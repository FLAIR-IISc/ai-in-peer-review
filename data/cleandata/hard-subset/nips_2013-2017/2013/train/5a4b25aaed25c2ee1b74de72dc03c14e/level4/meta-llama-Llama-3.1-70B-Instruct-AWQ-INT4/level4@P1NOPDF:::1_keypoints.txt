- Paper proposes projecting pairwise Ising models onto nearest “fast-mixing” models to improve Gibbs sampling mixing and marginal estimates.
- Idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly-mixing model is novel, building on prior rapid-mixing bounds [4,8].
- Experiments show improved marginals vs. variational methods and Gibbs on the original model for small toy Ising instances.
- Major concern about scalability: results on tiny models may not extrapolate to larger instances where mixing depends on size.
- The computational cost of the projection step is not included in time comparisons and is not properly analyzed.
- Unclear whether the approach generalizes beyond binary-state models; convergence bounds for broader cases are challenging.
- Presentation issues: frequent references to “standard results” without citations or sketches (e.g., Theorem 6) and an unproven Lemma 5.
- Need clearer discussion of the dependency matrix R, its properties and tractability, and motivation for invoking Lemma 5.
- Section 4 is poorly organized and hard to follow (introduces g, M, Lambda before definition); Theorem 4 uses unexplained notation.
- Notation errors: inconsistent KL divergence notation (KL(q||p) used both ways) and unconventional use of q for true distribution vs. p for approximation.
- Experimental flaws: Figure 2 timing omits projection cost, possibly giving unfair advantage over standard Gibbs; projection time must be included.
- Experiments lack larger models; suggested alternatives include symmetric models with tractable marginals or long-run sampling as ground truth.
- Suggest exploring KL(θ||ψ) as an intractable but informative “best case” benchmark and using junction trees for toy models where possible.
- The difference between mean-field error and reverse-KL projection error is surprising and needs discussion.
- Overall: elegant idea leveraging mixing bounds, but concerns remain about experimental comparisons, scope of applicability, and clarity/rigor in presentation.
