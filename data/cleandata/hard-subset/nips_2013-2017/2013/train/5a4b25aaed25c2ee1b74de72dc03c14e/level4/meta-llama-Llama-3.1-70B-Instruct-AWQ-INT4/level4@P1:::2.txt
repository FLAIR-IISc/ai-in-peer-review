The authors propose a method for improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the models' original parameterization onto a new parameter setting that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion needed for fast mixing. They formulate the projection as a constrained optimization problem, where the constraints are needed to ensure that the new parameters are defined over the same space as the original parameters, and examine this projection under several distance/divergence measures.

The idea of combining principles from stochastic and deterministic inference is under-explored and thus interesting. However, the description of the dual of the projection problem is unclear. For instance, the constraints zij*Dij = 0 are intended to ensure that the new parameterization is over the same space as the original parameters, but it is unclear why this is the case. Can't the new parameterization be over a smaller space? Furthermore, the overall procedure is not well-defined. Specifically, it is unclear whether the projected gradient operations are performed to completion before running a Gibbs sampler or if sampling is interleaved with gradient updates. An algorithm description box would help clarify this.

Additionally, it is unclear whether the proposed projected gradient descent strategy is guaranteed to converge. The authors mention that the strategy is an instance of Ergodic Mirror Descent, but they do not provide a convergence guarantee.

The experiments are also unconvincing. In the first set of experiments, the Gibbs sampler is run for 30,000 iterations, which seems arbitrary. A comparison based on time would be more informative, as it would allow for a fairer comparison between the sampling method and deterministic methods. Moreover, error bars are missing from the charts, making it impossible to determine whether the results are significant.

The second set of results compares the original/naive Gibbs sampler with Gibbs samplers under different projections and shows that the projection leads to faster mixing. However, the time required to perform the projection operation is not accounted for in this comparison. For instance, if the projection takes one minute and the naive Gibbs sampler can generate 10,000 samples in that time, then the projection might not be worthwhile.

Lastly, there is no comparison to blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate's "Lifted Gibbs Sampler," which are relevant baselines for this work.

Overall, the idea is nice, but the development of the projection problem and proposed method could use more work, and a bolstered set of experiments is needed to convince the reader of the utility of the method.

Strengths:
- The idea of combining principles from stochastic and deterministic inference is interesting and under-explored.
- The authors examine the projection under several distance/divergence measures.

Weaknesses:
- The description of the dual of the projection problem is unclear.
- The overall procedure is not well-defined.
- The convergence guarantee of the proposed projected gradient descent strategy is unclear.
- The experiments are unconvincing due to arbitrary iteration counts, missing error bars, and unaccounted projection time.
- Relevant baselines are missing.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The idea is interesting and has the potential to contribute to the field.
- With additional work on clarifying the projection problem and procedure, and with a more comprehensive set of experiments, the paper could be significantly improved.

Arguments for rejection:
- The current presentation of the projection problem and procedure is unclear.
- The experiments are not convincing due to methodological issues.
- The paper lacks relevant baselines, which makes it difficult to evaluate the proposed method's utility.
