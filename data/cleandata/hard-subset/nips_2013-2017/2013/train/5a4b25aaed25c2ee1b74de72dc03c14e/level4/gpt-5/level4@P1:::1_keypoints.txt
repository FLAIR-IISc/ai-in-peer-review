- Core idea: Project Ising-model parameters onto a parameter set that guarantees rapid mixing (spectral-norm bound) so Gibbs sampling is fast.  
- Method: Find closest parameters to the original model under several divergences (Euclidean, inclusive KL, piecewise KL, reverse KL) subject to a dependency-matrix spectral constraint.  
- Optimization: Dual projection algorithm using thresholded SVD with dual variables to preserve sparsity and constrain |B|.  
- Reverse-KL handling: Optimized via stochastic approximation using samples from the projected fast-mixing model.  
- Experiments: On small Ising graphs, sampling from the projected model gives more accurate marginals for fixed sampling time and beats MF, LBP, TRW in tested regimes.  
- Novelty: Uses known sufficient rapid-mixing conditions as feasibility constraints for projection — a novel approximation-by-projection perspective.  
- Relation to past work: Piecewise KL connects to tree/low-treewidth approximations but is used here as a projection proxy rather than for inference.  
- Strength: Conceptually neat formulation of tractability via guaranteed mixing rather than exact inference.  
- Strength: Solid use of recent mixing results and a practical projection procedure that respects sparsity.  
- Strength: Clear empirical message under strong coupling and limited sampling time.  
- Scope limitation: Results confined to binary pairwise Ising models; extension to general discrete MRFs is nontrivial and unaddressed.  
- Scalability concern: Unclear how method scales since mixing and spectral conditions depend on graph size; experiments only on small graphs.  
- Clarity issues: Some standard results (e.g., Theorem 6) lack references or proofs; Lemma 5 cited in a more general form than proven.  
- Dependency matrix R: Paper should explicitly discuss tractability of computing R since it involves a max over configurations; current reliance on Lemma 5 for Ising needs clarification.  
- Presentation problem: Section 4’s second half is hard to follow—variables and notation (g, M, Λ, triple dot, Πc, Z) are introduced unclearly and need clearer derivation or appendix.  
- Notation inconsistency: KL divergence notation and p/q roles are inconsistent and should be standardized early.  
- Experimental fairness: Time-accuracy plots appear to exclude projection computation time; projection cost should be reported and included in comparisons.  
- Missing larger-scale tests: No experiments on larger models; alternatives (long-run sampling reference or exploiting symmetries) could assess scalability.  
- Additional baseline: Compute KL(θ||ψ) via junction tree on small graphs as a best-case target for sampling-in-ψ and report results.  
- MF vs. reverse-KL: Empirical gap is large despite MF being a degenerate reverse-KL case; discuss why nonzero interactions under spectral budget improve results.  
- Spectral bound conservatism: Acknowledge conservatism (e.g., 2D grid thresholds) and provide guidance/diagnostics for practical mixing verification.  
- Complexity clarification: Provide computational complexity of the dual projection (Theorem 7), how sparsity is preserved, and cost per LBFGS-B iteration.  
- Pros for acceptance: Novel perspective, solid theory, sparsity-respecting dual algorithm, and empirical wins in strong-coupling/time-limited regimes.  
- Cons for acceptance: Limited scope, unclear scalability, ambiguous experimental timing, clarity/notation issues, and conservative spectral bounds.  
- Recommendation: With clearer exposition, transparent experimental accounting (including projection time), and additional larger-instance or best-case small-instance evidence, the paper would be stronger.
