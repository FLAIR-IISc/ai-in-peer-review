

The authors propose a method for improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the original parameterization onto a new setting that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion required for fast mixing. They formulate this projection as a constrained optimization problem, with constraints ensuring the new parameters remain defined over the same space as the original parameters, and analyze the projection under multiple distance/divergence measures.  

I find the idea of combining stochastic and deterministic inference principles to be under-explored and thus commendable. While the core concept of augmenting parameters to improve mixing time is straightforward, the description of the dual projection problem is unclear. Specifically, it is not evident how the constraints $z_{ij} d_{ij} = 0$ ensure the new parameterization occupies the same space (rather than a smaller subspace). Additionally, the overall procedure requires clarification: are projected gradient operations completed before running Gibbs sampling, or are gradient updates interleaved with sampling? An algorithm description box would significantly improve exposition. Furthermore, the convergence guarantees of the proposed projected gradient descent strategy are not established.  

The experimental validation is also somewhat unconvincing. In the first experiment, the Gibbs sampler is run for 30,000 iterations; since the comparison involves deterministic methods, a time-based evaluation would be more appropriate. Error bars are absent in the results, preventing assessment of statistical significance. The second experiment compares original/naive Gibbs sampling with Gibbs under different projections, demonstrating faster mixing via projection. However, the time cost of performing the projection operation is not accounted for (e.g., if projection takes 1 minute, and naive Gibbs generates 10,000 samples in that time, the projection may not be worthwhile). Finally, no comparison is made to blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate’s "Lifted Gibbs Sampler," which are relevant baselines.  

Overall, the core idea is promising. However, the development of the projection problem and proposed method requires refinement, and strengthened experimental validation is necessary to convincingly demonstrate the method’s utility.
