This paper addresses the challenge of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, proposing several projections to models exhibiting faster mixing properties. The core idea is to identify parameters that closely approximate the original model while ensuring sufficient spectral properties for rapid mixing. The authors demonstrate through experiments that this approach can improve marginal estimate accuracy within a fixed computational budget, outperforming standard Gibbs sampling and contemporary variational methods like mean field and belief propagation.

The technical foundation relies heavily on established results regarding conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]. However, the novelty lies in framing the problem as a projection onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as a means of approximate inference – an approach not previously explored, to the best of my knowledge.

On small “toy” Ising models, the results show promising improvements over a strong set of baselines. My primary concern, however, is the scalability of these findings to larger, more complex models. The experimental evaluation focuses on comparing mixing times across different models, and it is difficult to extrapolate these results to larger problem sizes. Furthermore, the projection step required to construct the fast-mixing model appears computationally expensive, and its cost is not adequately accounted for in the presented analysis. Finally, the generalizability to non-binary states is unclear, as establishing convergence bounds in such cases is considerably more difficult.

Regarding clarity, the presentation is generally clear and accessible, given the technical depth. However, several areas require reorganization and further clarification:

* The paper frequently references “standard results” and presents them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without providing references or even proof sketches. Lemma 5 is presented without proof, and the cited reference only addresses a special case (zero-field).
* The properties of the dependency matrix R should be discussed more thoroughly. Specifically, it is unclear whether it is tractable due to the maximization involved—a point that likely motivates the use of Lemma 5.
* The second half of Section 4 is difficult to follow. Before Theorem 7, the text introduces g, M, and Lambda without prior definition. Furthermore, the statement of Theorem 4 uses notation that lacks sufficient explanation. A more detailed explanation of this optimization process is needed, potentially at the expense of restating results from [8] that are not central to the paper's contribution.
* In Section 1, the notation KL(q||p) is used inconsistently to represent both KL divergence directions. Moreover, the initial paragraph reverses the conventional use of q and p (q for the true distribution, p for the tractable approximation).

Concerning the experiments, the time comparison in Figure 2 does not appear to include the computational cost of the projection step. A vague statement to this effect appears in Section 6.1 but is unclear; please clarify this point and, if applicable, provide results that incorporate the projection time. As it stands, the proposed methods benefit from the output of a sophisticated variational optimization without any apparent cost, diminishing the perceived improvement over standard Gibbs sampling.

The lack of experiments on larger models is disappointing, given the known dependence of Gibbs mixing times on problem size. Exploring regimes where junction tree inference is intractable—such as using models with symmetries allowing for computation of true marginals, or employing the output of very long sampling runs as ground truth—would be valuable. 

Although computing KL(\theta||\psi) is generally intractable, exploring it here as a potential “best-case” performance metric for sampling in the approximate model would be insightful (junction tree could be used for the toy models). The paper notes that mean field is a degenerate case of reverse KL projection, yet a significant discrepancy exists between mean field error and the error resulting from a true reverse KL projection; this difference merits discussion.

In summary, the idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is innovative, and the authors effectively leverage recent advances in mixing bound theory. However, concerns remain regarding the experimental comparisons and the limited applicability of this approach to a wider range of models.
