- Proposes projecting slow-mixing pairwise Ising models onto nearby rapidly-mixing models to speed up Gibbs sampling.  
- Uses spectral bounds to guarantee faster convergence for the projected models.  
- Experiments show improved marginal estimates over standard Gibbs and variational methods on small illustrative models for a fixed compute budget.  
- Builds on existing rapid-mixing literature [4,8] but reframes approximate inference as projection onto the closest fast-mixing model—an original perspective.  
- Main concern: scalability to larger, more complex instances is unclear because mixing-time behavior may change with problem size.  
- The computational cost of the projection step is high and appears omitted from runtime comparisons, biasing experimental results.  
- Method seems limited to binary-state models; generalizing convergence bounds to larger state spaces is challenging.  
- Presentation issues: several “standard results” are stated as theorems without citations or proofs (e.g., Theorem 6; Lemma 5 only supported for a zero-field case).  
- The dependency matrix R needs more discussion, particularly whether its maximization is tractable—this likely motivates Lemma 5.  
- Section 4 is hard to follow: symbols g, M, and Lambda are introduced before definition and Theorem 4 uses unexplained notation; optimization description needs clearer exposition.  
- Notational inconsistency in Section 1: KL(q||p) is used ambiguously for forward/reverse KL and q/p are defined opposite to common convention.  
- Figure 2’s time comparison is ambiguous about including projection time; if excluded, results are unfair and should be reported including projection overhead.  
- Experiments lack larger-model evaluations; suggested alternatives include symmetric models with computable marginals or using long-run sampling as ground truth.  
- Recommend exploring KL(θ||ψ) as a theoretical “best-case” sampling performance metric (junction tree could compute this for toy models).  
- Note that mean field is a degenerate reverse-KL projection, but the large error gap between mean field and reverse-KL projections deserves discussion.  
- Overall: the projection idea is innovative and well-grounded theoretically, but experimental validation, scalability, computational accounting, and clarity need substantial improvement.
