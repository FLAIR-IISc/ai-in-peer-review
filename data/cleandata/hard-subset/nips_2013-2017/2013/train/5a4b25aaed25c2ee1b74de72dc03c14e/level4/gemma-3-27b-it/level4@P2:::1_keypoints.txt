- Addresses slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models by projecting parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set to guarantee rapid convergence.  
- Main idea: find parameters nearest to the original model subject to a spectral bound that ensures fast mixing.  
- Builds on established mixing-condition results; projecting to the nearest fast-mixing model as an inference technique appears novel.  
- Experiments on small toy Ising models show improved marginal estimates versus standard variational methods and Gibbs on original parameters, especially under tight time budgets.  
- Primary concern: scalability — projection step is computationally intensive and results on small models may not generalize to larger, complex models.  
- Time-comparison plots likely omit the projection cost; reported speed/accuracy gains may be overstated without including projection runtime.  
- Generalizability beyond binary state variables is questionable because convergence bounds are harder to establish for more general settings.  
- Several “standard results” are presented as theorems without references or proof sketches; Lemma 5 is unproven and cited reference covers only a special case.  
- The dependency matrix R needs clearer discussion regarding how (and whether) it can be computed tractably; maximization may be intractable.  
- Section 4 is hard to follow: introduces g, M, Λ before defining them and uses unexplained notation in Theorem 7; optimization procedure needs clearer exposition.  
- KL divergence notation is inconsistent and the roles of p and q are reversed from common convention, causing confusion.  
- Experimental evaluation lacks larger-model tests and regimes where exact inference is intractable; using symmetries or long runs as proxies for ground truth would strengthen claims.  
- The paper should explicitly report results including projection time and, where possible, junction-tree-based true marginals for toy models.  
- Suggest evaluating KL(θ||ψ) as a theoretical best-case benchmark (where tractable) to contextualize sampling performance on the approximate model.  
- The paper correctly notes mean field as a degenerate reverse-KL case, but the large performance gap between mean field and reverse-KL projection needs more discussion.  
- Strengths: novel idea, solid theoretical grounding in mixing-time literature, and promising empirical gains in limited settings.  
- Weaknesses: unclear scalability, limited generalizability, incomplete accounting of computational cost, and several clarity/technical gaps.  
- Needed revisions: include projection runtime in comparisons, clarify proofs and references, expand experiments to larger instances, and fix notation and Section 4 exposition.  
- Overall recommendation: leaning toward rejection due to scalability, generalizability, and experimental/compositional clarity concerns; addressing the above would substantially strengthen the paper.
