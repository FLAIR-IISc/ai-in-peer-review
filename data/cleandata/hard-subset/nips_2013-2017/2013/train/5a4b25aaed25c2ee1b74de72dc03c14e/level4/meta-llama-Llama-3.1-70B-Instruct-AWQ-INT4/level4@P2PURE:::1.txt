This paper aims to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, which are known to be slow-mixing. The authors propose several projections to fast-mixing models by identifying parameters that are as close as possible to the original model but weak enough to satisfy a spectral bound establishing rapid mixing. Experiments demonstrate that this approach leads to improved marginal estimates for a given computation time in certain regimes, compared to variational methods (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

The technical content of this paper heavily relies on prior results establishing conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]. However, the idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method appears to be novel. The results offer some nice improvements over a good set of baselines (standard sampling and contemporary variational methods) on the domain of toy tiny Ising models.

My main concern is that the results may not scale to larger models. The experiments involve comparisons of mixing times of samplers on different models, making it difficult to judge how these will scale with problem size. Furthermore, the computationally expensive projection step required to build the fast-mixing model seems not to be accounted for. Finally, it is unclear whether generalization beyond binary states will be possible, as establishing convergence bounds more generally is far more challenging.

In general, the presentation is clear and reasonably accessible, given the technical content. However, there are some places where reorganization and clarification are needed. The paper frequently refers to "standard results" without providing references or proof sketches, such as Theorem 6. Additionally, Lemma 5 is left unproven, and the reference only proves a special case of zero-field.

The properties of the dependency matrix R should be discussed in more detail. For instance, it appears that the maximization involved in computing R may not be tractable; this should be explicitly stated. The second half of Section 4 is difficult to follow, with references to g, M, and Lambda before they are introduced. The statement of Theorem 4 includes notation that is not well-explained. If this optimization is to be discussed, more explanation is needed.

In Section 1, the notation for KL divergence is inconsistent, with KL(q||p) used for both directions of the KL divergence. The notation should be shifted for one of the directions to maintain consistency with the literature. In the first paragraph, q is used for the true distribution and p for the tractable approximation, which is the opposite of the notation used in most related literature.

The time comparison in Figure 2 does not appear to include the computation required for projection. A clarification is needed, and if this is indeed the case, results with projection time included should be shown. As it stands, the proposed methods seem to get the benefit of a sophisticated variational optimization without penalty, which makes the improvement over standard Gibbs less convincing.

It is disappointing that experiments on larger models are not included, given that Gibbs mixing times often depend on problem size. Options for running experiments in regimes where junction tree is intractable include using a model where symmetries allow the true marginals to be computed or taking the output of a very long sampling run as truth.

Although KL(θ||ψ) is intractable in general, it would be interesting to explore this as a potential "best case" for how sampling in an approximate model would perform. Junction tree could be used for the toy models used in the submission.

The large difference between mean field error and the error from reverse KL projection deserves discussion, as mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection.

The idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is elegant, and recent work on mixing bounds is leveraged nicely. However, there are concerns about experimental comparisons and the limited range of models to which this approach is potentially applicable.
