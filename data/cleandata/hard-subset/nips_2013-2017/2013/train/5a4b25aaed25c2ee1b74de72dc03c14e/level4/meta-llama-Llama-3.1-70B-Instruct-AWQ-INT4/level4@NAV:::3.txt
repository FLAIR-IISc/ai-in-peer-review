The success of Gibbs sampling-based inference in Markov Random Fields (MRFs), specifically in the context of the Ising model with two labels, heavily relies on the mixing rate of the underlying Monte Carlo Markov chain. The authors propose a novel approach to inference, comprising two key steps:

1) Projecting the input model, which may not exhibit fast mixing, onto the space of models that do mix rapidly.
2) Performing inference on the resulting fast-mixing model.

The space of fast-mixing models is characterized by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute Ising edge strengths. "Projection" is defined using Gibbs distribution divergences, ensuring preservation of the graph structure. The authors employ different methods for projection, including Euclidean distance (via dualization and LBFGS-B) and other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL), which utilize a projected gradient algorithm. Notably, the reversed KL divergence requires Gibbs sampling on a fast-mixing model to compute the projection.

The paper presents extensive experimental evaluations on small random models, comparing approximated marginals with true marginals. The methods under consideration include the proposed approach (with various divergences) and existing techniques such as loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The experiments assess both accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy trade-offs, demonstrating that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in terms of accuracy. Moreover, the proposed methods also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model for a reasonable range of runtimes. Among the proposed methods, the one using reversed KL divergence consistently achieves the best performance.

Comments:

* The projected gradient algorithm in Section 5.1 involves two nested loops, with the inner loop being LBFGS-B. Please provide details on the stopping criteria for the inner iterations.
* The horizontal axis in the plots in Figure 2 (and the supplement) is unclear, as it is labeled "number of samples" despite sampling being used only for reversed KL divergence. It would be more accurate to represent the horizontal axis as the runtime of the algorithm. Additionally, please consider reporting the runtime of LBP, TRW, and MF to ensure a fair accuracy-runtime comparison of all tested algorithms. Please clarify this issue and provide absolute running times in seconds.
* Experimental comparisons with larger models would be beneficial. A potential option is to utilize models from the paper [Boris Flach: A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function, to appear in TPAMI, available online], which allow for polynomial-time inference.
* On page 222, consider replacing "onto the tree" with "onto graph T".
* On page 226, it would be more accurate to use the term "subgradient" instead of "derivative".
Overall, the paper presents interesting results and convincing empirical evidence. However, the practical utility of the proposed approach may be limited due to high runtime, which requires further clarification in the rebuttal.
