The success of Gibbs sampling for inference in Markov Random Fields (specifically, the Ising model with two labels) is highly dependent on the mixing rate of the underlying Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) process. This paper introduces a method for inference that addresses this issue by: 1) projecting the input model – which may exhibit slow mixing – onto the space of models that mix rapidly; and 2) performing inference on this fast-mixing projected model. The space of fast-mixing models is characterized by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix containing the absolute values of the Ising edge strengths. “Projection” is defined using divergences between Gibbs distributions, while preserving the original graph structure.  Euclidean projection is achieved by dualizing the original problem and employing LBFGS-B. For other divergences – Kullback-Leibler (KL), piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences – a projected gradient algorithm is used. Importantly, computing the projection with the reversed KL divergence requires Gibbs sampling, but on the fast-mixing projected model. 

The paper presents extensive experimental results on small, randomly generated models, comparing the accuracy of approximated marginals against the true marginals. The methods evaluated include the proposed approach (using each of the aforementioned divergences), Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), Mean Field (MF), and Gibbs sampling performed on the original model. The evaluation considers both accuracy and the trade-off between runtime and accuracy.  The experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in terms of accuracy, and also surpass Gibbs sampling on the original model for reasonable runtimes. Of these methods, the approach utilizing the reversed KL divergence consistently achieves the best performance.
