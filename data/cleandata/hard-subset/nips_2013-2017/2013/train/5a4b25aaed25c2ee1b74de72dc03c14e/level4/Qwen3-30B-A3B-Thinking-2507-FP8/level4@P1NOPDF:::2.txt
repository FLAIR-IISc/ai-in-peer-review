

The authors propose a novel approach to accelerate Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the original parameterization onto a new setting that satisfies the Dobrushin condition for rapid mixing. This projection is framed as a constrained optimization problem, with constraints ensuring the new parameters remain defined over the same parameter space as the original. The authors analyze this projection under multiple distance/divergence measures.

The core idea—augmenting parameters to improve mixing time—holds promise, particularly given the underexplored intersection of stochastic and deterministic inference methods. However, the exposition of the dual formulation lacks clarity. Specifically, the constraints $z_{ij}^* d_{ij} = 0$ are claimed to preserve the parameter space equivalence, but it is unclear how they prevent the new parameterization from collapsing to a strict subset of the original space. The paper also omits a precise algorithmic description: are projected gradient updates performed to convergence before sampling, or are they interleaved with Gibbs steps? A pseudocode box would significantly clarify the procedure. Furthermore, the convergence guarantees of the proposed projected gradient descent strategy remain unaddressed.

The experimental evaluation requires substantial strengthening. In the first experiment, the 30K Gibbs iterations appear arbitrarily chosen; a time-based comparison (e.g., samples per second) would be more appropriate when contrasting sampling with deterministic methods. The absence of error bars on all figures renders the statistical significance of results ambiguous. In the second experiment, while the projected Gibbs samplers demonstrate faster mixing than the naive variant, the computational cost of the projection step is entirely ignored. For instance, if projection requires one minute and the naive Gibbs sampler generates 10,000 samples in that time, the practical utility of projection is questionable. Crucially, the authors omit comparisons to established alternatives such as blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate’s "Lifted Gibbs Sampler," which represent the state-of-the-art for efficient sampling in graphical models.

Despite the promising conceptual foundation, the method’s development and validation require significant refinement. The dual formulation needs rigorous clarification, the algorithmic procedure must be explicitly defined, and the experimental design should account for all computational costs and benchmark against relevant baselines. Without these improvements, the claimed advantages remain unconvincing. The idea itself is valuable, but the current presentation and evaluation fall short of demonstrating its practical utility.
