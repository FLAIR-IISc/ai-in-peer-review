The paper proposes an innovative approach to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions. By projecting the original model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set, the authors aim to achieve rapid convergence to the stationary distribution. The technical content builds upon prior results establishing conditions for rapid mixing, but the idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method is novel.

The paper presents several projections to "fast-mixing" models, including Euclidean distance, piecewise KL-divergence, and reversed KL-divergence. Experiments demonstrate that these methods can lead to improved marginal estimates for a given computation time, compared to variational methods (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

However, there are concerns regarding the scalability of these results to larger models. The experiments involve comparisons of mixing times of samplers on different models, which can be challenging to scale with problem size. Furthermore, the computationally expensive projection step required to build the fast-mixing model is not accounted for in the time comparisons. Additionally, it is unclear whether the approach can be generalized beyond binary states, as establishing convergence bounds for more general cases is more challenging.

The presentation is generally clear and accessible, but some reorganization and clarification are needed. Specifically, the paper refers to "standard results" without providing references or proof sketches, and some proofs, such as Lemma 5, are not provided. The properties of the dependency matrix R, including its tractability, should be discussed. The second half of Section 4 is difficult to follow, and more explanation is needed for the optimization discussed in Theorem 7.

The experiments have some limitations. The time comparison in Figure 2 does not include the computation required for projection, which is unclear from the text. It would be beneficial to clarify this and provide results with projection time included. Additionally, experiments on larger models would be valuable, as Gibbs mixing times often depend on problem size. Exploring the "zero-avoiding" KL-divergence as a potential "best case" for sampling in an approximate model would also be interesting.

The large difference between the mean field error and the error from the reverse KL projection deserves discussion, as mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection. Overall, the idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is elegant, but there are concerns about experimental comparisons and the limited range of models to which this approach is potentially applicable.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions
* Leveraging recent work on mixing bounds in an elegant way
* Experiments demonstrate improved marginal estimates for a given computation time compared to variational methods and Gibbs sampling in the original model

Weaknesses:

* Concerns about scalability to larger models
* Computationally expensive projection step not accounted for in time comparisons
* Limited range of models to which the approach is potentially applicable
* Some presentation issues, such as unclear references to "standard results" and difficult-to-follow optimization discussion

Arguments for acceptance:

* Novel approach to approximate inference in pairwise Ising models
* Demonstrated improvement in marginal estimates for a given computation time
* Potential for application in various fields, such as computer vision and natural language processing

Arguments against acceptance:

* Concerns about scalability to larger models
* Limited range of models to which the approach is potentially applicable
* Some presentation issues that need to be addressed

Recommendation:

* Accept with minor revisions to address presentation issues and provide more discussion on the limitations and potential applications of the approach.
