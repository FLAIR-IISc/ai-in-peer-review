The paper addresses the challenge of slow mixing in Markov Random Field (MRF) inference, specifically within the Ising model context, where the efficiency of Gibbs sampling is heavily dependent on the underlying Markov chain’s mixing rate. The proposed approach tackles this by first projecting the potentially ill-conditioned input model onto a space of models guaranteed to mix rapidly, then performing inference on this projected, fast-mixing model. This fast-mixing model space is defined by constraining the spectral norm of the matrix representing the absolute values of Ising edge strengths.

Projection is formulated using divergences between Gibbs distributions, while preserving the original graph structure.  Euclidean projection is efficiently computed via duality and the LBFGS-B algorithm. For other divergences – Kullback-Leibler (KL), piecewise KL, and reversed KL – a projected gradient algorithm is employed. Notably, the reversed KL divergence requires Gibbs sampling, but crucially, this sampling is performed on the fast-mixing projected model.

The paper presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation on small, randomly generated models, comparing the accuracy of approximated marginals against ground truth.  The methods evaluated include the proposed approach utilizing each of the implemented divergences, as well as established inference algorithms: loopy Belief Propagation (BP), Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), Mean Field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original, potentially slow-mixing model. The evaluation considers both accuracy and the trade-off between runtime and accuracy. Results demonstrate consistent accuracy improvements of the proposed methods over TRW, MF, and LBP. Furthermore, for comparable runtimes, the proposed methods generally outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed methods, the reversed KL divergence consistently yields the best performance.

**Comments:**

The projected gradient algorithm detailed in Section 5.1 incorporates nested loops, with an inner loop utilizing LBFGS-B. Please provide clarification regarding the stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B iterations.

The horizontal axis labeling in Figure 2 (and the supplementary material) is unclear. While labeled “number of samples,” sampling is only directly employed within the reversed KL divergence method. It is likely that this axis represents the algorithm’s runtime.  Additionally, reporting the runtime for LBP, TRW, and MF would facilitate a more equitable accuracy-runtime comparison across all tested algorithms. Please clarify this point and provide absolute running times in seconds.

Consider extending the experimental evaluation to include larger models. Models from the work of Flach [“A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function,” TPAMI (online)] offer a potentially valuable benchmark, as they allow for polynomial-time inference and would provide insights into scalability.

Line 222: Replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T”.

Line 226: The term “derivative” may be more appropriately replaced with “subgradient” in this context. 

Overall, this is an interesting paper with convincing empirical results. However, the practical applicability may be limited by the observed runtime, and this aspect requires further clarification in the rebuttal.
