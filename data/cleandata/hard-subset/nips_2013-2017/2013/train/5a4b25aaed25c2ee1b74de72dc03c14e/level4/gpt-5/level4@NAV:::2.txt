The authors introduce a method to accelerate the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model’s original parameters onto a new set that satisfies the Dobrushin condition for rapid mixing. They pose this projection as a constrained optimization problem, where the constraints are intended to preserve the parameter space of the original model, and they analyze this projection under multiple distance/divergence criteria.

I believe that approaches blending stochastic and deterministic inference are underexplored, making this an intriguing direction. While the notion of modifying the parameters to improve mixing is straightforward, the presentation of the dual of the projection problem was somewhat opaque—for example, how do the zij*dij = 0 constraints ensure that the projected parameters inhabit the same space (couldn’t they restrict it to a smaller one)? I was also unclear on the overall workflow—do you run projected-gradient updates to convergence and then invoke Gibbs, or do you interleave sampling with gradient steps? An algorithm box would help. Additionally, is the proposed projected gradient descent guaranteed to converge?

I also found the experiments not entirely convincing. In the first set, why fix the Gibbs sampler at 30K iterations? Since you compare a sampling method to deterministic ones, a time-based comparison would be more appropriate. Moreover, the plots lack error bars, making it impossible to judge significance. The second set compares the vanilla Gibbs sampler to versions under different projections and shows faster mixing, but the cost of computing the projection is not included (e.g., if the projection takes 1 minute and naive Gibbs can yield 10K samples in that time, the projection may not be worthwhile). Lastly, why is there no comparison to blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate’s “Lifted Gibbs Sampler”?

Overall, this is a very nice idea. However, the formulation and exposition of the projection problem and the proposed method need refinement, and the experimental evaluation should be strengthened to demonstrate the method’s practical utility.
