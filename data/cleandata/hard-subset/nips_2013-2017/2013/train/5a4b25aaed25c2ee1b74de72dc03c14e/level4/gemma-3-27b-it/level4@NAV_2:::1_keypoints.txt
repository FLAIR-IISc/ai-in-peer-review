- Paper proposes projecting slow-mixing pairwise Ising models onto the nearest “fast-mixing” model (satisfying a spectral bound) to speed up Gibbs sampling and improve marginal estimates.  
- Empirical results on small Ising instances show improved marginal estimates in some regimes compared to mean-field, BP variants, and Gibbs on the original model.  
- The work builds tightly on prior rapid-mixing results [4,8], but the specific idea of projection to the nearest rapidly-mixing model as an inference method appears novel.  
- Main concern: experiments are limited to tiny toy models, raising serious doubts about scalability to larger, realistic problems where Gibbs mixing depends on size.  
- The computational cost of the projection step appears nontrivial and is not clearly included in the runtime comparisons (Figure 2), biasing results in favor of the proposed method.  
- Generalization beyond binary-state Ising models is unclear and likely difficult because establishing convergence bounds for more general state spaces is challenging.  
- Some technical content is unclear or under-supported: Theorem 6 and Lemma 5 lack proper references or proof sketches, and Lemma 5’s reference only covers a zero-field special case.  
- The dependency matrix R deserves more discussion—its tractability is questionable due to maximization, and this should be stated explicitly (likely motivating Lemma 5).  
- Section 4’s latter half is hard to follow: variables g, M, and Lambda are referenced before introduction, and Theorem 7 uses unexplained notation; the exposition needs reorganization and more explanation.  
- Notation inconsistency in Sec. 1: KL(q||p) is used with reversed roles for q and p compared to standard literature, which is confusing.  
- Experimental suggestions: include projection computation time in comparisons, test larger models (e.g., symmetric models with tractable marginals or using long runs as ground truth), and use junction tree for toy models to evaluate KL(θ||ψ) as a best-case baseline.  
- The large gap between mean-field error and reverse-KL projection error deserves discussion, since mean-field is a degenerate reverse-KL case.  
- The spectral-norm-based bound used for rapid mixing may be loose, which could limit practical effectiveness.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel projection-based approximate inference, demonstrated gains over several baselines in some regimes, and solid grounding in established mixing-time theory.  
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability and projection-cost concerns, limited applicability beyond binary Ising, unclear technical sections, incomplete experimental accounting, and potentially loose spectral bounds.
