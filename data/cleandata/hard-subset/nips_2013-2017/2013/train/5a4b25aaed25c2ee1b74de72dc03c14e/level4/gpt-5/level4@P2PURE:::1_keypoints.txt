- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters to the nearest "fast-mixing" model (via a spectral bound) to accelerate Gibbs sampling.  
- Heavily relies on prior sufficient conditions for rapid mixing; using projection as an approximate inference method appears novel.  
- Small toy-model experiments show improved marginal estimates versus variational methods and Gibbs on the original model for a fixed compute budget.  
- Major concern about scalability: mixing comparisons on small models may not extrapolate to larger systems.  
- Projection computation appears costly and its time seems excluded from reported runtimes, biasing the comparisons.  
- Extending the approach beyond binary variables may be infeasible because comparable convergence bounds are harder to establish.  
- Clarity issue: several “standard results” (e.g., Theorem 6) are stated without citation or proof; Lemma 5 is left unproven for the general case.  
- Clarify properties and computability of the dependency matrix R — the maximization in its definition may be intractable.  
- Section 4 is hard to follow: symbols (g, M, Λ) are referenced before definition and Theorem 4 uses inadequately explained notation.  
- Notation problems in Sec. 1: KL divergence notation is inconsistent and q/p usage reverses common convention.  
- Experiments need to explicitly include projection time in timing plots and comparisons to avoid giving the method free optimization cost.  
- Larger-scale experiments are needed; feasible alternatives include exploiting model symmetries for exact marginals or using very long sampling runs as references.  
- Suggest evaluating KL(θ||ψ) as a “best-case” sampling target (junction tree can be used for the provided toy models).  
- The large error gap between mean field (reverse-KL degenerate case) and the reverse-KL projection requires explanation.  
- Overall: elegant and appealing idea, but experimental comparison shortcomings and limited applicability raise concerns about practical impact.
