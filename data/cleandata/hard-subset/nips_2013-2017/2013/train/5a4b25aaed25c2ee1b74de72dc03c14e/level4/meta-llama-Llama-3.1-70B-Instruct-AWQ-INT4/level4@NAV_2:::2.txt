The authors propose a method for improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the models' original parameterization onto a new parameter setting that satisfies the Dobrushin criterion needed for fast mixing. They formulate the projection as a constrained optimization problem, where the constraints are needed to ensure that the new parameters are defined over the same space as the original parameters, and examine this projection under several distance/divergence measures.

The paper relates to previous work on improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling and is an interesting contribution to the field. The idea of combining principles from stochastic and deterministic inference is under-explored, and this paper provides a valuable addition to this area.

The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their method, including a detailed description of the projection problem and its dual formulation. However, some aspects of the method could be clarified further. For example, the role of the zij*dij = 0 constraints in ensuring that the new parameterization is over the same space as the original parameters could be explained more explicitly.

The experimental evaluation of the method is thorough and well-designed. However, some additional comparisons and analyses would be helpful in assessing the utility of the method. For instance, a comparison of the computational time required for the projection operation versus the time required for Gibbs sampling would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the method's efficiency.

The authors' decision to use a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of 1.0 in their experiments is justified, but it would be interesting to explore the impact of this choice on the method's performance.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and interesting approach to improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models. While some aspects of the method could be clarified further, and additional experimental comparisons would be helpful, the paper is a valuable contribution to the field.
