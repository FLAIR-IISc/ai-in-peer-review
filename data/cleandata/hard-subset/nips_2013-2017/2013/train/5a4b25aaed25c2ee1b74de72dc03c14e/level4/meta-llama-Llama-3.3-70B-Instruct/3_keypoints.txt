- Gibbs sampling effectiveness in Ising-model MRFs depends critically on the MCMC mixing rate.
- Proposed inference is two-step: project the input model onto fast-mixing models, then perform inference on that projected model.
- Fast-mixing models are defined by a spectral-norm bound on the matrix of absolute Ising edge strengths.
- Projection is defined via divergences between Gibbs distributions, with graph structure preserved as a constraint.
- Euclidean projection is solved by dualization and LBFGS-B; other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL) use a projected gradient algorithm.
- Computing the reversed KL projection requires Gibbs sampling, but only on the fast-mixing projected model.
- Experiments on small random models compare proposed methods (various divergences) to loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original model.
- Evaluation includes accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy; proposed methods outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy.
- For reasonable runtimes, the proposed methods also beat Gibbs sampling on the original model; reversed KL projection performs best overall.
- Clarification requested on termination criteria for the inner LBFGS-B loop in the projected gradient algorithm.
- Figure 2 labeling "number of samples" is misleading; suggest relabeling to runtime and reporting runtimes for LBP, TRW, MF, plus absolute times in seconds.
- Recommend evaluating larger models (e.g., Boris Flach’s polynomial-inference models) to strengthen empirical claims.
- Minor editorial fixes: replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T" and consider "subgradient" instead of "derivative."
- Overall positive empirical results, but practical utility may be limited by high runtime—this needs clarification in the rebuttal.
