- Problem & approach: Tackles slow-mixing Gibbs inference in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a guaranteed fast-mixing set, then running Gibbs on the projected model.  
- Fast-mixing criterion: Uses literature-based sufficient condition: spectral norm of absolute interaction matrix < 1.  
- Projection variants: Considers multiple projection objectives (Euclidean, forward KL, piecewise KL, reverse KL) while keeping the original graph structure.  
- Euclidean projection: Derives an efficient dual algorithm solved with LBFGS-B.  
- KL-based projections: Uses projected gradient descent; reverse KL requires sampling from the projected model to compute gradients.  
- Empirical setup: Thorough evaluation on 8x8 grids and small random graphs, comparing to LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original model.  
- Accuracy results: For a fixed number of Gibbs iterations, projected methods give more accurate marginals than variational baselines.  
- Time-vs-accuracy: For limited computational budget, Gibbs on the projected model outperforms Gibbs on the original model; reverse KL projection performs best.  
- Reproducibility request: Clarify the stopping criterion used for the LBFGS-B-based Euclidean projection inner loop.  
- Plotting/runtimes issue: Figure axes labeled "number of samples" are misleading; recommend plotting total wall-clock time including projection and final inference, and include runtimes for LBP/TRW/MF.  
- Scalability concern: Experiments are small-scale; need larger models to assess projection overhead and practical utility (suggests Flach TPAMI complete-graph models).  
- Minor edits: Change "onto the tree" → "onto the graph T" and use "subgradient" instead of "derivative" where a max operator is present.  
- Overall assessment: Novel, well-motivated method with strong empirical promise but requires clearer computational-cost reporting and larger-scale validation.
