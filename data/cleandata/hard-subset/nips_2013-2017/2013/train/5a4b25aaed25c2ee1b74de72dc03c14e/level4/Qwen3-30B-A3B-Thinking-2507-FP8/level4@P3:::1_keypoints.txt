- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set so Gibbs sampling converges rapidly, using spectral-norm mixing-time bounds (Hayes).
- Core contribution: using parameter projection as an inference method rather than a purely theoretical tool.
- Leverages prior work on mixing times and approximate inference, introducing "fast-mixing" as a new notion of tractability tied to parameter space.
- Introduces four projection methods under different divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, reverse KL).
- Derives a dual algorithm for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) and implements practical divergence-based projections.
- Reverse KL projection is well-motivated and acts as a generalization of mean-field, offering useful computational trade-offs.
- Technical grounding is sound, with appropriate use of mixing-time theory and projection formulations.
- The spectral-norm bound used appears conservative (e.g., β < 0.25 vs known 2D Ising threshold βc ≈ 0.44), raising concerns about tightness.
- Experiments show improved marginal accuracy over variational baselines and original-model Gibbs sampling when sampling time is limited.
- Experimental scope is small (8x8 grids, 10-node random graphs), leaving unclear scalability to larger, real-world problems.
- Projection computational cost is substantial and omitted from timing comparisons, biasing results in favor of the proposed methods.
- Paper does not sufficiently address whether projected models preserve statistical fidelity for larger-scale applications.
- Clarity issues: key results (Theorem 6) lack proof or intuition, and central Lemma 5 is unproven despite importance.
- Dependency matrix R is introduced without explaining its computational intractability (max over x,x' issue).
- Optimization formulation in Section 4 is opaque; notation (g, M, Λ) is introduced before definition, confusing the reader.
- KL divergence notation is inconsistent across sections (p and q swapped), harming readability.
- Overall originality is strong—the idea of parameter-space constraints guaranteeing fast mixing is novel and distinct from structural approximations.
- Significance is limited by inadequate experimental validation and lack of scaling analysis.
- Main recommendation: reject due to limited experiments, omission of projection cost, conservative spectral bound, and clarity problems; work shows promise but needs stronger evaluation and exposition.
