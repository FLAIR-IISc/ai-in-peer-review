

The authors propose a novel approach to accelerate Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the original parameterization onto a space satisfying the Dobrushin criterion, using constrained optimization under various divergence measures. The core idea—leveraging deterministic optimization to improve stochastic sampling dynamics—is compelling and represents an underexplored intersection of inference paradigms. However, the technical development and experimental validation require significant refinement to substantiate the method's utility.

The primary concern lies in the clarity of the projection formulation. The dual problem description is ambiguous, particularly regarding the constraint $z_{ij} d_{ij} = 0$. While the authors state this ensures the new parameters operate over the *same* space as the original, it is unclear how this prevents a *reduction* in the parameter space (e.g., if $d_{ij}=0$ for some edges, could the projection effectively eliminate those edges?). A precise mathematical justification for the constraint’s role in preserving the original model’s support is needed. Furthermore, the algorithmic procedure remains unspecified: are projected gradient updates completed *before* sampling, or are they interleaved with Gibbs steps? A concise Algorithm 1 would resolve this critical ambiguity and clarify whether the method is a preprocessing step or an online adaptation.

The experimental evaluation also lacks rigor. In the first experiment, comparing Gibbs sampling (run for 30,000 iterations) to deterministic methods is misleading; a time-based comparison is essential since deterministic methods typically run faster per iteration. Without accounting for wall-clock time, the results fail to address the practical trade-off between sampling speed and computational overhead. Additionally, the figures lack error bars, making it impossible to assess statistical significance. The second experiment demonstrates faster mixing under projection but neglects to quantify the *time cost* of the projection step itself. If projection requires 1 minute of computation, and the naive Gibbs sampler produces 10,000 samples in that time, the projected approach may offer no net benefit. This oversight undermines the practical relevance of the claimed speedup.

Crucially, the baseline comparisons are incomplete. The authors omit established alternatives such as blocked Gibbs sampling (a standard technique for improving mixing in Ising models) and Gogate et al.’s "Lifted Gibbs Sampler" (a state-of-the-art method for structured models). Without these comparisons, it remains unclear whether the projection method offers advantages over existing, well-optimized sampling strategies. For instance, if blocked Gibbs achieves similar mixing rates with negligible overhead, the proposed method’s novelty is significantly diminished.

The theoretical guarantees also warrant clarification. The manuscript does not establish whether the projected gradient descent converges to a global optimum or provide bounds on the convergence rate. This is particularly important given the non-convex nature of the constrained optimization problem. Without such analysis, the practical feasibility of the method remains uncertain.

In summary, the idea of using deterministic optimization to enhance Gibbs sampling is promising and merits attention. However, the current presentation falls short in two key areas: (1) the mathematical formulation lacks sufficient rigor and clarity, particularly around the constraint interpretation and algorithmic workflow; and (2) the experimental validation fails to address critical practical considerations (time costs, statistical significance, and relevant baselines). To strengthen the contribution, the authors should: (a) provide a formal proof of the constraint’s role in preserving the parameter space; (b) include a clear pseudocode for the full algorithm; (c) re-evaluate experiments with time-based comparisons, error bars, and inclusion of blocked Gibbs/lifted Gibbs baselines; and (d) establish convergence guarantees for the projection step. With these revisions, the method could make a valuable contribution to scalable inference. As presented, the results do not convincingly demonstrate the method’s superiority over existing approaches.
