- Addresses slow mixing in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a space guaranteed to mix faster via spectral-norm-bounded interaction matrices.
- Defines projection under several divergences (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL) and develops algorithms using SVD and stochastic gradient descent.
- Empirically outperforms loopy BP, tree-reweighted BP, and naive mean field on small random Ising models, and matches or beats Gibbs sampling under limited computation.
- Novel idea of parameter projection for fast mixing; well-grounded in Markov chain spectral-norm mixing theory.
- Comprehensive evaluation against relevant baselines and practically relevant for accelerating Gibbs sampling when time is limited.
- Clear and accessible presentation of concepts and methods.
- Experiments limited to small models; projection cost (especially reversed KL) may not scale to realistic sizes.
- Runtime reporting unclear—x-axis labeled "number of samples" is misleading; absolute running times in seconds are needed for fair comparisons.
- Inner-loop stopping criteria for the projected gradient algorithm are underspecified, making robustness hard to assess.
- Spectral norm bound used may be overly conservative compared to known results for specific Ising configurations (e.g., 2D Ising).
- Missing comparison to recent work on random fields with tractable partition functions (e.g., Flach, TPAMI), limiting benchmarking.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically grounded approach with strong empirical results and clear writing.
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability concerns, unclear runtime measurement, and potentially conservative theoretical bound limiting practical impact.
- Overall: solid, promising paper deserving acceptance if concerns about runtime reporting and inner-loop clarity are addressed; comparison to Flach would strengthen it.
- Minor edits: change "onto the tree" to "onto graph T"; consider using "subgradient" instead of "derivative".
