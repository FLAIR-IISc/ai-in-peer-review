- Proposes two-stage inference: project Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set, then run Gibbs sampling on the projected model.
- Defines fast-mixing family via a spectral-norm condition on absolute interaction strengths (e.g., norm < 1).
- Develops Euclidean projection algorithm via a dual formulation solved with L-BFGS-B.
- Studies three KL-projection variants: forward KL (intractable baseline), novel piecewise KL on low-treewidth subgraphs, and reversed KL optimized by stochastic projected gradient.
- Experiments on 8x8 grids and small random graphs compare projected methods to LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on original parameters.
- Empirical results show reversed KL projection often gives the most accurate marginals and projected sampling outperforms sampling the original model under limited time budgets.
- Strength: introduces a novel framework defining tractable distributions by dynamic (mixing) properties rather than static graph structure.
- Strength: strong empirical performance across baselines, especially for reversed KL projection.
- Strength: practical relevance demonstrated via runtime-vs-accuracy trade-offs favoring projection-then-sample for finite budgets.
- Weakness: experimental presentation unclear—Figure 2’s x-axis ("number of samples") is misleading and runtime for all methods is not reported.
- Weakness: algorithmic details missing—inner optimization/termination criteria for the projection (L-BFGS-B) and nested-loop behavior are unclear, hurting reproducibility.
- Weakness: limited experimental scale (only small models); scalability and practical significance on larger problems remain untested.
- Weakness: projection (particularly KL-based) may incur substantial computational overhead; total time (projection + sampling) needs clearer discussion.
- Detailed comments: typo at Line 222 ("projection onto the tree" → likely "projection onto graph T").
- Detailed comments: piecewise KL is a max of functions and non-smooth, so "subgradient" is the correct term rather than "derivative."
