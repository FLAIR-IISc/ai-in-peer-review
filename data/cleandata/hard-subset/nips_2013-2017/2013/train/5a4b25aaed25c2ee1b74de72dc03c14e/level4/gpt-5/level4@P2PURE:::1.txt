The paper aims to accelerate Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong couplings, which are known to be slow-mixing. It proposes several projections onto “fast-mixing” models: parameters are chosen to be as close as possible to those of the original model, while being weak enough to satisfy a spectral bound that guarantees rapid mixing. Experiments demonstrate regimes in which this yields improved marginal estimates for a fixed compute budget, relative to variational methods (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

The technical development relies heavily on prior results that provide sufficient conditions for rapid mixing [4,8]. However, I have not previously seen the strategy of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model used as an approximate inference technique.

On small toy Ising models, the results show clear gains over strong baselines (standard sampling and contemporary variational methods). My main concern is that one should be unusually cautious about the prospects for scaling to larger systems. The experiments compare mixing behavior of samplers across models, and it is difficult to extrapolate how these comparisons evolve with problem size. In addition, the projection step required to construct the fast-mixing model appears computationally expensive, and that cost does not seem to be included in the comparisons. Finally, extending beyond binary variables may be infeasible in practice, since establishing comparable convergence bounds in more general settings is substantially harder.

CLARITY:
- The paper repeatedly refers to “standard results,” sometimes even stating them as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without citation or proof sketch. Lemma 5 is left unproven; the cited reference establishes it only in the zero-field special case.
- Please discuss properties of the dependency matrix R. For example, the maximization in its definition seems to make it intractable; clarify whether this is indeed the case (I presume this motivates invoking Lemma 5).
- The second half of Sec. 4 is very hard to follow. Before Theorem 7, the text references g, M, and Λ before they are introduced. The statement of Theorem 4 also uses notation that is not adequately explained. If this optimization is to be presented, more exposition is needed. (Conversely, consider trimming restatements of results from [8] that are not directly used.)
- In Sec. 1, KL(q||p) is used for both directions of the KL divergence, but the notation should change for one of them. Also, the first paragraph uses q for the true distribution and p for the tractable approximation, which is the reverse of the convention in most related work.

EXPERIMENTS:
- It appears that the timing in Figure 2 excludes the cost of computing the projection. Sec. 6.1 contains a somewhat ambiguous remark to this effect; please clarify and, if projection time is excluded, include results with projection time accounted for. As presented, the proposed methods effectively benefit from the outcome of a sophisticated variational optimization at no cost, which weakens the case against standard Gibbs.
- It is disappointing not to see larger-scale experiments, given that Gibbs mixing rates often depend on problem size. There are feasible options even when junction tree is intractable, such as exploiting symmetries to obtain exact marginals, or using very long sampling runs as a reference.
- I recognize that KL(θ||ψ) is generally intractable, but it would still be informative to explore it here as a potential “best case” for sampling in an approximate model. For the toy models in the submission, junction tree could be used.
- Mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse-KL projection, as the paper notes, yet the error gap between mean field and the reverse-KL projection is large. This discrepancy merits discussion.

Overall, approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the nearest fast-mixing model is an appealing idea, and the paper leverages recent mixing bounds in an elegant way. However, there are concerns about the experimental comparisons and about the narrow range of models to which this approach may ultimately apply.
