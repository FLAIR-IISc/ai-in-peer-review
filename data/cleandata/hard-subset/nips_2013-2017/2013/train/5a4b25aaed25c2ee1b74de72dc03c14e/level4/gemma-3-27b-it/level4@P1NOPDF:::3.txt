Success of inference by Gibbs sampling in Markov Random Fields (MRFs) – here, specifically Ising models with two labels – strongly depends on the mixing rate of the underlying Monte Carlo Markov chain. This paper proposes an approach to inference based on the following idea:

1) Project the input model, which may not mix quickly, onto the set of models that *do* mix quickly.
2) Perform inference on the resulting, fast-mixing model.

The space of fast-mixing models is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute values of the Ising edge strengths. “Projection” is defined using divergences of Gibbs distributions, and is constrained to preserve the graph structure. Projection using Euclidean distance is achieved by dualizing the initial task and employing LBFGS-B. For other divergences – KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences are implemented – a projected gradient algorithm is used. The reversed KL divergence requires Gibbs sampling (but on a fast-mixing model) to compute the projection.

Extensive experiments on small, random models compare the approximated marginals with the true marginals. The methods tested include the proposed approach (with all the implemented divergences), Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), Mean Field (MF), and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The evaluation considers not only accuracy, but also the trade-off between runtime and accuracy. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy, and for a reasonable range of runtimes, also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model. Of these methods, the one employing reversed KL divergence performs most consistently well.
