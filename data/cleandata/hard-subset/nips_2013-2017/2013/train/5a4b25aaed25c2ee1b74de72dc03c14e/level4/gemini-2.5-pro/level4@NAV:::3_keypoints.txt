- Problem: Inference in Ising models where Gibbs sampling performance depends on MCMC mixing rate and can be slow.  
- Method: Two-step approach — project original model onto a fast-mixing model class, then perform inference on the projected model.  
- Fast-mixing constraint: Characterized by a bound on the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute edge interaction strengths.  
- Projection objective: Minimize a divergence between Gibbs distributions of original and projected models while preserving graph structure.  
- Optimization: Euclidean projection solved via dual problem with LBFGS-B; KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL use a projected gradient algorithm.  
- Reverse KL detail: Computing the reverse KL projection requires running Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing target model.  
- Experiments: Thorough evaluation on small random models, comparing estimated marginals to ground truth.  
- Baselines: Benchmarked against loopy BP, tree-reweighted BP, mean field, and standard Gibbs on the original model.  
- Results: Proposed methods outperform TRW, MF, and LBP, and beat standard Gibbs for a notable range of runtimes; reverse KL variant performs best.  
- Major concern: Need clarification of termination criteria for the inner LBFGS-B loop within the projected gradient algorithm.  
- Major concern: Figure axes are unclear—“number of samples” is misleading; horizontal axis should reflect runtime and include absolute runtimes (seconds) for all baselines for fair accuracy-runtime comparisons.  
- Suggestion: Include experiments on larger-scale models, e.g., Flach’s class of random fields with tractable partition functions, as additional benchmarks.  
- Minor edits: Replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T” (Line 222) and consider “subgradient” instead of “derivative” (Line 226).  
- Overall: Interesting and empirically convincing work, but practical utility may be limited by computational cost; authors should clarify runtime/cost in the rebuttal.
