Aims to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, which are known to be "slow-mixing". Several projections to "fast-mixing" models are proposed; essentially, parameters are identified which are as close as possible to the original model, but weak enough to satisfy a spectral bound establishing rapid mixing. Experiments show that, in some regimes, this leads to improved marginal estimates for a given computation time, compared to variational methods (mean field and belief propagation variants) and Gibbs sampling in the original model. 
The technical content builds heavily on prior results establishing conditions for rapid mixing [4, 8]. However, the idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method has not been explored before, to the best of my knowledge. 
The results offer some nice improvements over a good set of baselines on small, “toy” Ising models. My main concern is that the scalability of these results to larger models remains an open question. Experiments compare mixing times of samplers on different models, and it is difficult to judge how these will scale with problem size. Additionally, the computationally expensive projection step required to build the fast-mixing model’s cost appears to be unaccounted for. Finally, it is not clear that generalization beyond binary states will be possible, as establishing convergence bounds more generally is far more challenging. 

**CLARITY:**
In general, the presentation is clear and reasonably accessible, given the technical content. However, several places could benefit from reorganization and clarification: 

* The paper refers several times to “standard results” and even states these as theorems (e.g., Theorem 6) without providing a reference or proof sketch. Lemma 5 is stated without proof; the cited reference proves it only for the special case of zero-field.
* A discussion of the properties of the dependency matrix R would be valuable. For instance, it is not immediately apparent whether computing it is tractable due to the maximization involved. (I presume this is why Lemma 5 is invoked.)
* The second half of Sec. 4 is difficult to follow. Before Theorem 7, the text references *g*, *M*, and *Λ* before they are introduced. The statement of Theorem 7 itself includes notation that is not fully explained. If this optimization is central to the work, more detailed explanation is needed. (Perhaps less space could be devoted to restating results from [8] that are not directly used.)
* In Sec. 1, KL(q||p) is used for both directions of the KL divergence, which can be confusing. The notation should be consistent throughout. Also, in the first paragraph, *q* is used for the true distribution and *p* for the tractable approximation, which is the opposite of the convention in most related literature.

**EXPERIMENTS:**
The time comparison in Figure 2 does not appear to include the computation required for projection. A somewhat ambiguous statement to this effect is made in Sec 6.1, but it lacks clarity. Please clarify whether projection time is included, and if not, show results with it incorporated. As it stands, the proposed methods appear to benefit from a sophisticated variational optimization without accounting for its computational cost, which diminishes the significance of the improvement over standard Gibbs sampling. 
It is disappointing that there are no experiments on larger models, given that Gibbs mixing times often depend on problem size. Options for running experiments in regimes where the junction tree algorithm is intractable exist, such as using models with symmetries that allow for exact marginal computation or taking the output of a very long sampling run as ground truth.
While it is understood that KL(θ||ψ) is generally intractable, exploring it as a potential "best case" for sampling in an approximate model would be valuable. (The junction tree algorithm could be used for the toy models in the submission.)
Mean field is identified as a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection. However, there is a significant difference between the error of mean field and the error from reverse KL projection, and this discrepancy deserves discussion.

**Overall:**
The idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is promising, and the paper leverages recent work on mixing bounds in an elegant way. However, there are concerns regarding the experimental comparisons and the limited range of models to which this approach might be applicable.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to improving Gibbs sampling by projecting onto fast-mixing models.
*   Technically sound, building on existing results in mixing time bounds.
*   Demonstrates improvements over standard variational methods and Gibbs sampling in certain regimes.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Scalability to larger models is unclear, and experiments are limited to small instances.
*   Computational cost of the projection step is not fully accounted for in the experimental evaluation.
*   Potential limitations in generalizing beyond binary states.
*   Clarity issues in some sections of the paper.
