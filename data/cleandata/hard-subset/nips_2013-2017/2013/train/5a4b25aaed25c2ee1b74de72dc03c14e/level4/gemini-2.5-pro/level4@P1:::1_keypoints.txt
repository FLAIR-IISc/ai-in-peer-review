- Problem: addresses slow convergence of Gibbs sampling for strongly-interacting Ising models.  
- Method: project model parameters onto the set of “fast-mixing” parameters using a spectral-norm constraint.  
- Goal: find closest parameter set to the original that guarantees rapid mixing and faster MCMC.  
- Novelty: projection-to-fast-mixing is an elegant, creative use of recent MCMC mixing-time theory.  
- Perspective: offers a new definition of “tractable” models for approximate inference.  
- Empirical claim: on small grids and random graphs, the approach can produce more accurate marginals for a fixed compute budget versus mean field, LBP, TRW, and original Gibbs.  
- Scalability concern: experiments are on small models, so it’s unclear how performance scales to larger, realistic problems.  
- Projection cost omitted: projection’s computational cost appears excluded from runtime comparisons, which may bias results.  
- Generalization concern: presented for binary Ising models; extension to non-binary variables is unclear due to harder fast-mixing conditions.  
- Missing proofs/citations: several “standard” results (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5) lack citations or proofs; Lemma 5’s cited ref may only cover zero field.  
- Dependency matrix issue: definition involves maximization over configurations and may be intractable—this computational property needs explicit discussion.  
- Section 4 clarity: Euclidean projection algorithm exposition is hard to follow—variables and notation are introduced before definition and the dual is underexplained.  
- Notational inconsistencies: KL(q||p) used ambiguously for forward/reverse KL and q/p convention is reversed relative to common VI literature.  
- Experimental detail requests: clarify whether projection cost is included in Fig.2; if not, report total time (projection + sampling).  
- Evaluation scope: rely on small models with exact marginals; should include larger models or long-MCMC baselines to better assess practical utility.  
- Missing benchmark: computing the intractable KL(θ||ψ) projection via junction tree for small models would give a useful best-case reference.  
- Mean-field relation: mean field is a degenerate reverse-KL projection (zero spectral norm); the large empirical gap between mean-field and reverse-KL deserves discussion.  
- Overall verdict: novel and compelling idea but practical usefulness is uncertain unless scalability, projection cost accounting, and clarity issues are addressed.
