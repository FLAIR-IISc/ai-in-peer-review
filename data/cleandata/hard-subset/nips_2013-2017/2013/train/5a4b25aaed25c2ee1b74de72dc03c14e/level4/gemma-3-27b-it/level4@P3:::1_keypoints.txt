- Addresses slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models by projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set, trading fidelity for faster convergence.  
- Explores multiple projection methods using different divergences; small-model experiments show improvements over mean field, belief propagation, and original-parameter Gibbs when time-limited.  
- Builds on established mixing-time results; projecting to a fast-mixing model for inference appears novel.  
- Insight: mean-field inference is a degenerate case of reverse KL projection.  
- Major scalability concern: experiments are limited to small “toy” Ising models; unclear performance on larger, complex instances.  
- Projection step computational cost is not fully accounted for and could be a practical bottleneck.  
- Applicability limited to binary state spaces; extending mixing-time bounds to other variable types is an open challenge.  
- Paper is generally readable but several sections need reorganization and clarification.  
- Several theorems/lemmas are stated without sufficient references or proof sketches (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5).  
- Dependency matrix R needs fuller discussion, including intractability of its exact computation and justification for approximations.  
- Section 4’s optimization formulation is hard to follow; variables g, M, Λ and theorem notation lack context and intuition.  
- KL divergence notation is inconsistent; adopt a consistent convention (e.g., KL(q||p)).  
- Experimental timing (Figure 2) likely omits projection cost; this must be clarified and results re-presented including projection time.  
- Should include experiments on larger models or alternative ground-truth methods (symmetries, long runs) to assess scalability.  
- Recommend exploring KL(θ||ψ) divergence on toy models to benchmark best-case performance of sampling in fast-mixing approximations.  
- Need discussion explaining why reverse KL projection markedly outperforms mean field in reported errors.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty of projection idea, theoretical grounding via mixing bounds, and promising empirical results on small models.  
- Arguments against acceptance: scalability doubts, unaddressed projection cost, limited applicability beyond binary variables, and clarity/technical gaps.  
- Overall: interesting and potentially valuable approach but current concerns warrant rejection with a request for major revision addressing projection cost analysis, larger experiments, clearer technical exposition, and discussion of limitations.
