This paper addresses the challenge of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for inference in Ising models (binary Markov Random Fields). The core idea is to first project a given model, which may be slow-mixing, onto a set of models that are guaranteed to mix fast, and then perform inference on this projected model.

The set of fast-mixing models is defined by imposing a bound on the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute edge strengths. The projection itself is formulated as an optimization problem that minimizes a divergence between the Gibbs distributions of the original and projected models, while preserving the graph structure. The authors explore several divergences. For Euclidean distance, the projection is computed by solving the dual of the primal problem using LBFGS-B. For other divergences—namely KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL—a projected gradient algorithm is used. Notably, computing the projection for the reverse KL divergence requires running Gibbs sampling, but this is performed on the projected model, which is, by construction, fast-mixing.

The authors present extensive experiments on small, random models, comparing the marginals approximated by their methods against the true marginals. The proposed approach (using all four divergences) is benchmarked against several standard baselines: Loopy Belief Propagation (LBP), Tree-Reweighted Belief Propagation (TRW), Mean Field (MF), and standard Gibbs sampling on the original model. The evaluation provides not only final accuracy but also accuracy-runtime trade-off curves. The results indicate that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in terms of accuracy. Furthermore, they also outperform standard Gibbs sampling on the original model within a reasonable runtime budget. Among the proposed variants, the reverse KL divergence projection consistently achieves the best performance.

Overall, this is an interesting paper with a novel idea and convincing empirical results on the tested benchmarks. However, the practical utility of the method is currently unclear due to potentially high runtimes, an issue that requires clarification in the experimental section as noted below.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Clarity of Experimental Plots:** There is a significant lack of clarity regarding the plots in Figure 2 (and the supplement), which makes it difficult to interpret the experimental results. The x-axis is labeled "number of samples," which is confusing, as sampling is only explicitly part of the reverse KL divergence method. For the other proposed methods and the baselines, this axis is not well-defined. It seems the x-axis should represent the actual wall-clock runtime in seconds to allow for a fair comparison of the accuracy-runtime trade-off across all methods.

2.  **Comparison with Baselines:** The baselines (LBP, TRW, MF) are presented as single accuracy points without associated runtimes. To enable a comprehensive and fair comparison, their runtimes should also be reported. This could be done by showing them as vertical lines on the same accuracy-vs-runtime plots. Clarification on this point is crucial, as the current presentation hinders a proper assessment of the method's performance benefits. The authors should revise the plots to use absolute runtime (in seconds) on the x-axis for all algorithms.

3.  **Algorithmic Details:** The description of the projected gradient algorithm in Section 5.1 mentions a nested loop structure where the inner loop uses LBFGS-B. The authors should clarify the stopping criteria for these inner iterations, as this detail is important for reproducibility and for understanding the algorithm's complexity.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Scalability:** The experiments are currently limited to small, random models. To better demonstrate the scalability and practical applicability of the proposed method, it would be beneficial to include experiments on larger models. One interesting possibility could be to use the class of models from Flach ("A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function," to appear in TPAMI), which have tractable partition functions and would allow for controlled experiments on larger graphs.

**Minor Comments and Typos:**

*   **Line 222:** "onto the tree" should likely be "onto graph T" for consistency.
*   **Line 226:** Given the context of the optimization problem, would "subgradient" be a more appropriate term than "derivative"?
