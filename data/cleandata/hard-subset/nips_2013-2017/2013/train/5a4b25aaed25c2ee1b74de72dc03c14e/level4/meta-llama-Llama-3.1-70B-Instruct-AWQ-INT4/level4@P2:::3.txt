The paper "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing" explores an alternative approach to inference in high-treewidth graphical models, specifically the Ising model. The key idea is to project the input model onto a set of models that are guaranteed to mix fast, using various divergences to define the "projection" operation. The space of fast-mixing models is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute values of Ising edge strengths.

The paper relates to previous work on mixing times in Markov chains and approximate inference methods, such as mean-field inference, loopy belief propagation, and tree-reweighted belief propagation. The authors' approach can be seen as a generalization of mean-field inference, where the set of approximating distributions is expanded from fully-factorized to fast-mixing.

The technical contributions of the paper include the development of a dual algorithm for projecting onto the set of fast-mixing models using the Euclidean distance, as well as projected gradient algorithms for other divergences, including KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences. The authors also provide extensive experimental results, comparing their methods to loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling on the original model.

The strengths of the paper include its novel approach to inference in high-treewidth graphical models, its thorough theoretical development, and its convincing empirical results. The authors' use of various divergences to define the projection operation is a key contribution, as it allows for a more flexible and accurate approximation of the input model.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement. One issue is the looseness of the spectral norm bound, which may be too conservative in some cases. Additionally, the authors' use of a spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of 1.0 in their experiments may not be fully justified. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation could be improved by including larger models and providing more detailed information about the runtime of the algorithms.

In terms of clarity, the paper is generally well-written, but there are some areas where the presentation could be improved. For example, the notation and terminology could be more clearly defined, and some of the technical details could be more thoroughly explained.

Overall, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of approximate inference in graphical models, and its ideas and techniques are likely to be of interest to researchers in this area.
