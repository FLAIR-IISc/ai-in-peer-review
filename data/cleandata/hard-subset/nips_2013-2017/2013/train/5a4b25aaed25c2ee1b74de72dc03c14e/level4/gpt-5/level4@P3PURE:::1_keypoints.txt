The paper projects a slow-mixing pairwise Ising model onto the nearest model that satisfies a spectral bound guaranteeing rapid mixing, then samples from that projection to approximate marginals.  
Experiments on small Ising instances show improved marginal estimates under a fixed computation budget versus standard Gibbs and several variational baselines.

The projection-based strategy of explicitly mapping to a provably fast-mixing model is novel and well motivated.

Strengths: conceptually appealing approach; elegant use of modern mixing-time bounds to guide projection; solid empirical improvements on small toy instances.

Scalability is unclear — it's hard to infer how performance and computational cost scale with graph size and coupling strength.  
The projection optimization appears computationally expensive, and projection time seems excluded from reported time comparisons.  
Generality beyond binary Ising variables is doubtful; extending convergence guarantees to multi-state models is substantially harder.

Missing citations and proofs: several stated “standard results” (e.g., Theorem 6, Lemma 5) lack proper citation or proof sketch, and the cited reference only covers a special case.  
Computational properties of the dependency matrix R are not discussed; computing R seems intractable due to a maximization step, and this needs explicit treatment.  
Section 4 is hard to follow: symbols (g, M, Λ) are used before being defined and Theorem 4 uses unexplained notation; the optimization must be explained more clearly.  
Notation issues: KL(q||p) is used inconsistently and the paper reverses the common convention for p and q, causing potential confusion.

Experiments may unfairly exclude projection time from runtime comparisons; the paper should clarify and include projection cost in results.  
Lack of larger-scale experiments limits conclusions, since Gibbs mixing depends strongly on problem size; feasible alternatives for larger tests (symmetries, long MCMC) should be used.  
Investigate KL(θ||ψ) projection as a “best-case” objective on toy problems where it’s tractable to provide additional insight.  
The large error gap between mean field (reverse-KL degenerate case) and the reverse-KL-based projection is surprising and needs discussion.

Overall: compelling and clean idea with promising small-scale results, but concerns about computational overhead, fairness of comparisons, scalability, narrow applicability, and clarity currently limit the paper’s impact.
