## Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

This paper addresses the challenging problem of inference in Ising models, particularly when strong interactions lead to slow mixing times in Gibbs sampling. The authors propose a novel approach: projecting the model parameters onto a space guaranteed to exhibit fast mixing properties. This projection is explored using several divergence measures – Euclidean distance, KL divergence (and approximations thereof) – and practical algorithms are developed for performing this projection. The core idea is strong and potentially impactful, offering a way to improve the accuracy of Gibbs sampling, especially with limited computational resources.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly connecting the problem to existing work on approximate inference in graphical models (e.g., mean-field [14], loopy belief propagation [21], tree-reweighted BP [19]). It correctly identifies the limitations of these methods and positions its approach as an alternative that leverages the parameter space conditions for fast mixing, building upon results from Markov chain theory [4, 8, 10]. The introduction effectively explains the motivation and provides a good overview of the contributions. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of explicitly projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing space is a novel contribution.  While the concept of finding “tractable” models is not new, defining tractability in terms of mixing time and developing projection algorithms is a significant step forward.
* **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundations, based on spectral norm bounds for mixing times [8], are solid. The derivation of the Euclidean projection using singular value decomposition (Theorem 7) and the analysis of different divergence measures are well-presented.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experimental results on small Ising models are compelling. The authors demonstrate that their methods consistently outperform standard variational inference algorithms (LBP, TRW, MF) and often surpass Gibbs sampling with the original parameters, particularly when computational time is limited.  The comparison against Gibbs sampling is particularly important.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper acknowledges the trade-offs between different divergence measures and computational complexity, offering valuable insights for practitioners. The stochastic gradient descent approach for the reversed KL divergence is a practical contribution.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability Concerns:** The experiments are currently limited to small models (8x8 grids and 10-node random graphs). The computational cost of the projection step, particularly for the reversed KL divergence which involves Gibbs sampling *within* the projection procedure, raises concerns about scalability to larger, more realistic problems.
* **Ambiguous Experiment Reporting:**  The horizontal axis in Figure 2 (and the supplement) is labeled "number of samples" but appears to represent runtime, especially as it’s related to the reversed KL divergence. This is misleading. Reporting absolute running times in seconds for *all* algorithms (LBP, TRW, MF, and the proposed methods) is crucial for a fair accuracy-runtime comparison.
* **Spectral Norm Bound Tightness:** The authors acknowledge that the spectral norm bound used to define the fast-mixing set might be conservative, citing work on critical Ising models [11]. Investigating tighter bounds could potentially improve the performance of the projection algorithms.
* **Inner Loop Convergence:** Section 5.1 mentions a nested loop structure with LBFGS-B but lacks details on the stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B iterations. Clarifying this is important for reproducibility and understanding the computational cost.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to improving inference in Ising models.
* The experimental results demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods on the tested problem instances.
* The paper addresses a significant problem in the field of graphical models and has the potential to impact research in related areas.
* The authors carefully analyze the trade-offs between different divergence measures and provide practical insights.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The scalability of the proposed methods to larger models is a significant concern.
* The ambiguous reporting of experimental results hinders a clear assessment of the practical benefits.
* The potentially conservative spectral norm bound may limit the effectiveness of the projection.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a well-motivated and novel contribution. The technical details are sound, and the experimental results are promising. While the scalability concerns and reporting ambiguities require attention, they do not overshadow the overall quality and potential impact of the work. I believe the paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS conference, particularly if the authors address the concerns raised regarding scalability and experiment reporting.

**Recommendation:**  **Accept, with minor revisions.**  The authors should:

1.  Clarify the horizontal axis in Figure 2 (and the supplement) and report absolute running times for all algorithms.
2.  Provide details on the stopping criteria for the inner LBFGS-B iterations in Section 5.1.
3.  Consider including experimental results on larger models or discussing the limitations of the current approach in more detail. A discussion of comparing results against the paper [Boris Flach: A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function] could be beneficial.
4.  Replace “onto the tree” with “onto graph T” on line 222.
5. Consider using the term "subgradient" instead of "derivative" on line 226.
