This paper addresses the challenge of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for pairwise Ising models with strong interactions. The core idea is to project the model parameters onto a set guaranteed to be fast-mixing, effectively trading off fidelity to the original distribution for faster convergence during sampling. Several projection methods are explored, leveraging different divergences, and experimental results on small Ising models demonstrate potential improvements over standard variational methods (mean field, belief propagation) and Gibbs sampling with original parameters, particularly when computational time is limited.

The technical foundation builds upon established results concerning mixing time conditions [4, 8]. While the concept of projecting to a fast-mixing model for approximate inference appears novel, the paper's strength lies in its systematic exploration of different projection techniques and their empirical evaluation. The connection to mean-field inference as a degenerate case of reverse KL projection is also insightful.

However, a significant concern is the scalability of this approach. The experiments focus on relatively small “toy” Ising models, and it’s unclear how well the method will perform on larger, more complex instances. The computational cost of the projection step itself – which is not fully accounted for in the current experimental evaluation – could become a bottleneck. Furthermore, the method’s potential applicability is currently limited to binary state spaces, as generalizing mixing time bounds to more complex variable types is a challenging open problem.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and accessible, despite the technical nature of the subject. However, several areas require clarification and reorganization:

* **Missing Justification and Proofs:** Several statements are presented as “standard results” or “theorems” (e.g., Theorem 6) without sufficient referencing or even a brief proof sketch.  Lemma 5 is stated without proof, and the cited reference only covers a specific zero-field case. Providing more context or justification for these assertions would significantly enhance clarity.
* **Dependency Matrix R:** A more thorough discussion of the dependency matrix *R* is needed. The inherent intractability of calculating *R* (due to the maximization involved in its definition) should be explicitly stated, justifying the use of approximations like those in Lemma 5.
* **Section 4 – Optimization Formulation:** The second half of Section 4 is particularly difficult to follow. The introduction of variables *g*, *M*, and *Λ* lacks sufficient context before Theorem 7, and the notation within the theorem itself is not fully explained. Simplifying this section or providing a more intuitive explanation of the optimization problem would be beneficial. Perhaps less emphasis could be placed on reiterating results from [8] that are not directly utilized.
* **KL Divergence Notation:** In Section 1, the notation for KL divergence is inconsistent, switching the order of arguments.  Adopting a consistent convention (e.g., KL(q||p) for the KL divergence from *q* to *p*) would improve readability and align with the broader literature.

**Experiments:**

* **Projection Cost:** The time comparison presented in Figure 2 does not appear to include the computational cost of the projection step. The paper acknowledges this but the statement in Section 6.1 is ambiguous. A clear clarification is crucial. If the projection cost is not included, the results should be re-presented with this cost factored in to provide a more realistic comparison. The advantage of the proposed methods diminishes considerably if the projection step is computationally expensive.
* **Scalability & Model Size:** The lack of experiments on larger models is a significant limitation. While acknowledging the difficulties, exploring scenarios where true marginals can be computed (e.g., using symmetries or very long sampling runs as ground truth) would provide valuable insights into the scalability of the proposed approach.
* **KL(θ||ψ) Exploration:** Although computationally challenging, exploring the KL(θ||ψ) divergence, even on the toy models, would be insightful. It would establish a benchmark for the “best-case” performance achievable by sampling in an approximate, fast-mixing model.
* **Mean Field Comparison:** The paper correctly notes that mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection. The substantial difference in error between mean field and the reverse KL projection warrants further discussion. What explains this discrepancy?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of explicitly projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set to accelerate Gibbs sampling is a novel approach to approximate inference.
* **Technical Soundness:** The paper leverages established results on mixing time bounds and presents a theoretically grounded framework for parameter projection.
* **Promising Empirical Results:** On small models, the proposed methods demonstrate improvements over standard variational methods and, in some cases, Gibbs sampling with original parameters, given limited computational resources.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Scalability Concerns:** The experimental evaluation lacks evidence of scalability to larger, more complex models.
* **Projection Cost:** The computational cost of the projection step is not adequately addressed and may become a bottleneck in practice.
* **Limited Applicability:** The current approach is primarily applicable to binary state spaces due to the challenges of generalizing mixing time bounds.
* **Clarity Issues:** Several sections of the paper require further clarification and reorganization.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable approach to improving Gibbs sampling in Ising models. However, the concerns regarding scalability, computational cost, and clarity prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection** with a request for a major revision that addresses these concerns. Specifically, the authors should: (1) provide a more thorough analysis of the projection cost, (2) explore experiments on larger models, (3) clarify the technical details and improve the organization of the presentation, and (4) discuss the limitations of the approach in more detail.
