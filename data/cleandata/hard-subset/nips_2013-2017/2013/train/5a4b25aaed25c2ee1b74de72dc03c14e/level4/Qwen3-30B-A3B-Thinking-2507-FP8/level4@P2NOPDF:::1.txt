

This paper proposes a novel approach to accelerate Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions by projecting the original model onto the nearest "fast-mixing" model satisfying spectral mixing conditions. The method identifies parameters as close as possible to the original while ensuring rapid mixing, demonstrating improved marginal estimates over variational methods (mean field, belief propagation) and standard Gibbs sampling in specific small-scale regimes. The technical contribution leverages prior rapid-mixing results [4,8] but introduces the unexplored idea of using model projection as an approximate inference strategy.

The core idea is promising and technically sound for its scope. However, significant concerns arise regarding scalability and experimental validity. The experiments are confined to "toy tiny Ising models," and the paper provides insufficient evidence that results will generalize to larger systems. Key scalability issues include: (1) mixing time comparisons on small models offer little insight into scaling behavior with problem size; (2) the computationally expensive projection step (required to construct the fast-mixing model) is not accounted for in runtime comparisons (Figure 2), making the claimed speedups misleading; and (3) the method's restriction to binary states appears unavoidable due to the intractability of generalizing convergence bounds beyond binary models, severely limiting applicability.

**Clarity and Technical Presentation:**  
The paper suffers from critical clarity issues. Theorem 6 cites "standard results" without reference or proof sketch, while Lemma 5 (left unproven) references a proof for a zero-field special case only. The dependency matrix \(R\) is discussed without addressing its intractability due to the required maximization (a key reason Lemma 5 is invoked). Section 4’s second half is particularly opaque: Theorem 7 references undefined quantities \(g\), \(M\), and \(\Lambda\), and the optimization description lacks sufficient explanation. Over-reliance on restating results from [8] (not central to the contribution) further obscures the novel elements. Additionally, Section 1 misuses KL divergence notation: \(KL(q||p)\) is used in both directions (e.g., \(q\) as true distribution, \(p\) as approximation), contradicting standard literature conventions (where \(p\) is typically the true distribution).

**Experiments:**  
The runtime comparison in Figure 2 omits the projection cost, which is inadequately addressed in Section 6.1. This omission is critical, as the method effectively gains the benefits of a sophisticated variational optimization without computational penalty, undermining the claimed advantage over standard Gibbs. The paper should explicitly include projection time in all runtime comparisons. Furthermore, the absence of experiments on larger models (despite feasible approaches, e.g., symmetric models with known marginals or long-sampling baselines) is a major shortcoming, given that Gibbs mixing times are inherently size-dependent. While acknowledging \(KL(\theta||\psi)\) is generally intractable, the authors should explore it as a "best-case" benchmark (e.g., using junction tree for their toy models) to contextualize sampling performance. Finally, the substantial error gap between mean field (a degenerate reverse KL projection) and the proposed method warrants explicit discussion, as the paper notes mean field is a special case.

**Overall Assessment:**  
The core idea of approximating slow-mixing models via nearest fast-mixing projections is elegant and well-motivated, and the paper effectively utilizes recent mixing theory. However, the limited experimental scope (toy models only), unaddressed projection cost in runtime comparisons, and unresolved scalability concerns significantly weaken the contribution. The technical presentation requires substantial revision to clarify proofs, notation, and novel contributions. Without evidence that the method scales beyond tiny binary models and without incorporating projection costs into performance metrics, the practical impact remains questionable. The work is technically interesting but currently lacks sufficient empirical validation for broader relevance. The authors should prioritize addressing scalability concerns and experimental transparency before the method can be considered viable for larger applications.
