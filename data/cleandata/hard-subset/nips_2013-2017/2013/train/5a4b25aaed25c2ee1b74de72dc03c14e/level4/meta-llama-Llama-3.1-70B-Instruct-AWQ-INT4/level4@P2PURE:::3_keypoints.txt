- Paper proposes inference by projecting a non–fast-mixing Ising model onto the set of fast-mixing models and performing inference there.  
- Fast-mixing models are defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute Ising edge strengths.  
- Projection is formulated via divergences between Gibbs distributions, with graph structure preserved.  
- Euclidean projection solved by dualization and LBFGS-B; other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, reversed KL) use a projected gradient algorithm.  
- Reversed KL projection requires Gibbs sampling on the fast-mixing model to compute the projection.  
- Experiments on small random models compare proposed methods, loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original model, evaluating accuracy and runtime-vs-accuracy.  
- Results: proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in accuracy; for a reasonable runtime range they also outperform Gibbs on the original model.  
- The reversed KL–based method yields the best performance among the proposed approaches.  
- Methodological concern: projected gradient has nested loops with inner LBFGS-B—stopping criteria for inner iterations should be specified.  
- Experimental concern: Figure 2 x-axis labeled "number of samples" is unclear (likely runtime); need runtimes for LBP, TRW, MF and absolute times in seconds to fairly compare algorithms.  
- Suggestion to evaluate on larger/tractable models (e.g., Flach’s class of complete-graph random fields) to test scalability.  
- Minor edits: change "onto the tree" to "onto graph T" (p.222) and use "subgradient" instead of "derivative" (p.226).  
- Overall assessment: interesting and empirically convincing results, but practical utility may be limited by high runtime and needs clarification in rebuttal.
