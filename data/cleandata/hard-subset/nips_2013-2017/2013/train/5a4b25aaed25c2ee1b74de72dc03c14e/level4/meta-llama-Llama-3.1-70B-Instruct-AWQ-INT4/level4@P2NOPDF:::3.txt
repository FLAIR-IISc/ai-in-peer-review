The success of Gibbs sampling inference in Markov Random Fields (MRFs), specifically in the case of the Ising model with two labels, heavily relies on the mixing rate of the underlying Monte Carlo Markov chain. This paper proposes an approach to improve inference by projecting the input model onto the set of fast-mixing models. This is achieved through the following steps: 

1. Project the input model onto the set of models that mix rapidly.
2. Perform inference on the obtained fast-mixing model.

The space of fast-mixing models is defined by bounding the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute values of Ising edge strengths. The "projection" is defined by divergences of Gibbs distribution and is forced to preserve the graph structure. The projection in Euclidean distance is obtained by dualizing the initial task and using LBFGS-B. For other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences), a projected gradient algorithm is used. Notably, in the case of reversed KL divergence, Gibbs sampling must be performed on a fast-mixing model to compute the projection.

Extensive experiments on small random models are presented, comparing the approximated marginals with the true marginals. The methods tested include the proposed approach (with various divergences) and loopy BP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs sampling on the original model. The experiments evaluate not only accuracy but also runtime-vs-accuracy. The results show that the proposed methods consistently outperform TRW, MF, and LBP in terms of accuracy, and for reasonable runtime ranges, they also outperform Gibbs sampling on the original model. Among the proposed methods, the one using reversed KL divergence consistently performs the best.

Comments:

* In Section 5.1, the projected gradient algorithm has two nested loops, with the inner loop being LBFGS-B. Please provide details on the stopping criteria for the inner iterations.
* The horizontal axis in the plots in Figure 2 (and the supplement) is unclear, titled "number of samples." However, sampling is only used for reversed KL divergence. It would be more accurate to represent the horizontal axis as the runtime of the algorithm. Additionally, consider reporting the runtime of LBP, TRW, and MF to ensure a fair accuracy-runtime comparison of all tested algorithms. Please clarify this issue and provide absolute running times in seconds.
* Consider experimental comparisons with larger models. A potential option is to use models from the paper "A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function" by Boris Flach, which allow polynomial inference.
* On page 222, replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T."
* On page 226, it would be more accurate to say "subgradient" rather than "derivative."

This paper presents an interesting approach with convincing empirical results. However, the practical utility of the method may be limited due to high runtime, which requires further clarification in the rebuttal.
