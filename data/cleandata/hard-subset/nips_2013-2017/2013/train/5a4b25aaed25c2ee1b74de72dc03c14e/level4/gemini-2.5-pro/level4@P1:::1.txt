This paper addresses the problem of slow convergence in Gibbs sampling for Ising models with strong interactions. The authors propose a novel approximate inference method based on projecting the model's parameters onto the set of "fast-mixing" parameters. This is achieved by finding a new set of parameters that is as close as possible to the original set, while satisfying a spectral norm condition that guarantees rapid mixing. The experimental results on small grid and random graphs demonstrate that in certain regimes, this approach can yield more accurate marginal estimates for a fixed computational budget compared to several baselines, including standard variational methods (mean field, LBP, TRW) and Gibbs sampling on the original model.

The core idea of projecting a slow-mixing model onto the closest fast-mixing one is novel and elegant, representing a creative use of recent theoretical results on MCMC mixing times. The paper provides a compelling new perspective on what constitutes a "tractable" model for approximate inference.

However, the experimental evaluation and potential applicability of the method raise several concerns. My primary concern is scalability. The experiments are conducted on small models, and it is difficult to extrapolate how the performance comparisons will scale with the size of the problem, a crucial factor for MCMC methods. A related issue is the computational cost of the projection itself, which appears to be significant but is not accounted for in the experimental runtime comparisons. Finally, the approach is presented for Ising models, and it is unclear how readily it could be generalized to models with non-binary variables, where establishing practical fast-mixing conditions is considerably more difficult.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written, but several sections could be improved for clarity and completeness.

*   Several results are presented as "standard" without citation or proof (e.g., Theorem 6). Lemma 5, a key bound for the dependency matrix, is also presented without proof, and its cited reference appears to only cover the special case of a zero field. Providing citations or brief proof sketches would make the paper more self-contained.
*   The paper should explicitly discuss the computational properties of the dependency matrix R. Its definition involves a maximization over configurations, which suggests it is intractable to compute directly. Clarifying this would better motivate the subsequent use of the bound in Lemma 5.
*   The second half of Section 4, which introduces the Euclidean projection algorithm, is difficult to follow. The text refers to variables g, M, and Λ before they are defined in Theorem 7, and the notation within the theorem itself is not sufficiently explained. The clarity of this section could be significantly improved by providing a more thorough walkthrough of the optimization problem and its dual formulation.
*   There are some minor notational issues. In the introduction, the notation KL(q||p) is used to refer to both the forward and reverse KL divergences. Additionally, the convention of using 'q' for the intractable target distribution and 'p' for the tractable approximation is the reverse of what is commonly used in the variational inference literature, which could be confusing for some readers.

**Experiments:**

*   A major concern with the experimental evaluation is that the runtime comparisons in Figure 2 do not seem to include the cost of the projection step. The text in Section 6.1 is ambiguous on this point. If the projection cost is indeed excluded, this represents an unfair comparison, as the proposed methods benefit from a potentially expensive optimization procedure at no charge. The authors should clarify this and, if necessary, provide results that account for the total computation time (projection + sampling). This is critical for assessing whether the method provides a net performance gain over standard Gibbs sampling.
*   The evaluation is limited to small models where exact marginals can be computed via the junction tree algorithm. It would be more compelling to see experiments on larger models where approximate inference is actually necessary. Even without an exact ground truth, performance could be assessed by using models with symmetries that allow for exact marginal computation, or by comparing against a high-quality baseline generated from a very long MCMC run.
*   While minimizing KL(θ||ψ) is noted as being intractable for the target problems, it could be computed for the small models used in the experiments via the junction tree algorithm. Including this projection would provide a valuable "best-case" benchmark, showing the maximum potential accuracy of this family of approximations.
*   The paper makes the interesting observation that mean field is a degenerate case of the reverse KL projection (with a zero spectral norm constraint). Given this connection, the significant performance gap between the mean-field and reverse KL results in the experiments is noteworthy and warrants further discussion.

In summary, the paper introduces a novel and elegant idea for approximate inference by leveraging theoretical bounds on mixing times. However, the current evaluation raises significant questions about the practical utility and scalability of the approach, particularly concerning the unaccounted-for cost of the projection step and the restriction to small-scale models. Addressing these experimental concerns would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution.
