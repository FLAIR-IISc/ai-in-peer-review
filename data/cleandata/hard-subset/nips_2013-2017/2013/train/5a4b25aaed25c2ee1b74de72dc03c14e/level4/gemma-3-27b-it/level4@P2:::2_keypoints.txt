- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing space (e.g., satisfying Dobrushin) via constrained optimization under various divergences.  
- Novel idea of combining stochastic (Gibbs) and deterministic inference by augmenting parameters to improve mixing times.  
- Dual formulation is unclear, particularly the role and effect of the z_ij d_ij = 0 constraints and whether they unduly restrict the parameter space.  
- Overall algorithmic procedure is ambiguous; unclear if projected gradient descent is run to completion before Gibbs sampling or interleaved.  
- No discussion or guarantees provided for convergence of the projected gradient descent method.  
- Experimental choices are questionable (e.g., running Gibbs for 30,000 iterations) and lack justification.  
- Evaluations should include computation-time comparisons rather than only iteration counts to fairly compare deterministic and stochastic methods.  
- Figures lack error bars, preventing assessment of statistical significance.  
- Experiments omit the computational cost of performing the projection, which could negate mixing gains.  
- Missing comparisons to more advanced sampling methods (e.g., blocked Gibbs, Lifted Gibbs), limiting empirical scope.  
- Strengths: addresses a relevant problem, novel use of fast-mixing conditions, explores multiple divergence measures.  
- Weaknesses: unclear mathematical presentation, incomplete/insufficient experimental evaluation, omission of projection cost, and missing convergence analysis.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, reasonable theoretical grounding, experiments indicate potential benefits.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of clarity hindering reproducibility, incomplete experiments failing to show practical utility, and missing cost analysis.  
- Overall assessment: promising idea but requires clearer presentation, convergence analysis, and a more comprehensive, time-aware empirical evaluation; reviewer recommends rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit.
