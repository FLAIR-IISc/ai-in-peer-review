This paper addresses the challenge of slow mixing rates in Gibbs sampling for inference in Markov Random Fields (specifically, Ising models). The proposed approach consists of two steps: 1) project the original, potentially slow-mixing model onto a set of models guaranteed to be fast-mixing, and 2) perform inference on this fast-mixing projection.

The space of fast-mixing models is defined by imposing a bound on the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute edge strengths. The projection, which is constrained to preserve the original graph structure, is formulated as minimizing a divergence between the Gibbs distributions of the original and projected models. For the Euclidean distance, this projection is computed by dualizing the optimization problem and solving it with LBFGS-B. For other divergences (KL, piecewise KL, and reverse KL), a projected gradient algorithm is employed. Notably, computing the projection for the reverse KL divergence requires running Gibbs sampling on the (fast-mixing) projected model itself.

The paper presents extensive experiments on small, random models, comparing the approximated marginals against the ground truth. The proposed method (using all mentioned divergences) is benchmarked against loopy BP, TRW, mean field (MF), and standard Gibbs sampling on the original model. The evaluation considers not only final accuracy but also the runtime-accuracy trade-off. The results demonstrate that the proposed methods consistently achieve higher accuracy than TRW, MF, and loopy BP. Furthermore, they outperform standard Gibbs sampling when considering a reasonable runtime budget. Among the proposed variants, the projection based on reverse KL divergence consistently yields the best performance.

**Comments:**

*   The projected gradient algorithm described in Section 5.1 appears to have two nested loops, with an inner LBFGS-B loop. Please provide details on the stopping criteria for these inner iterations.

*   There is a significant lack of clarity regarding the experimental evaluation in Figure 2 (and the supplement). The horizontal axis is labeled "number of samples," yet sampling is only used for the reverse KL divergence variant. For the other methods, the axis should presumably represent the algorithm's runtime. To enable a fair comparison of the accuracy-runtime trade-offs across all tested methods, the runtimes for loopy BP, TRW, and MF should also be reported. Please clarify this ambiguity and provide absolute runtimes (e.g., in seconds), as the current presentation makes it difficult to interpret the experimental results.

*   For a more comprehensive evaluation, please consider experimenting with larger models. The models from Flach ("A class of random fields on complete graphs with tractable partition function," TPAMI), which permit exact inference in polynomial time, would be a particularly interesting benchmark.

*   Line 222: Please replace "onto the tree" with "onto graph T".

*   Line 226: Should this be "subgradient" rather than "derivative"?

This is an interesting paper with a novel approach and convincing empirical results. However, its practical utility may be limited by a potentially high runtime, a critical point that requires clarification in the author rebuttal.
