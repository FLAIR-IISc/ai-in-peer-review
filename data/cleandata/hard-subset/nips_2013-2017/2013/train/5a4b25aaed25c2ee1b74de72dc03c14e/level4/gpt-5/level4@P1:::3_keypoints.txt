- Paper introduces projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing family (spectral-norm constraint) and then running Gibbs sampling.  
- Fast-mixing condition uses spectral norm of absolute edge-strength matrix via dependency matrix bounds.  
- Two-step recipe: project arbitrary parameters to fast-mixing set (preserving graph), then run Gibbs on projected model.  
- Considers four divergences for projection: Euclidean, KL(θ||ψ), piecewise KL, and KL(ψ||θ).  
- Euclidean projection: dual formulation solved with iterative SVD thresholding and LBFGS-B while enforcing sparsity.  
- KL(θ||ψ) is theoretically appealing but requires expectations under the original slow-mixing model (used only on small graphs).  
- Piecewise KL approximates KL by optimizing over tractable subgraphs; easy to compute but empirically weaker.  
- KL(ψ||θ) enables stochastic projected gradient since expectations are under the fast-mixing ψ and can be sampled.  
- Projected gradient scheme: gradient step on divergence followed by Euclidean projection back to fast-mixing set.  
- Experiments: projected models outperform LBP, TRW, and mean-field in marginal accuracy on small random graphs and grids.  
- Within proposed methods, KL(ψ||θ) projection performs best and Gibbs on projected models beats Gibbs on original parameters given limited time.  
- Approach is technically sound and builds on known rapid-mixing sufficient conditions; Euclidean projection is carefully derived.  
- Missing implementation detail: stopping criteria and tolerances for the inner LBFGS-B loop in the projected-gradient algorithm.  
- Plotting/clarity issue: x-axis labeled “number of samples” is ambiguous; should use wall-clock runtime and report absolute runtimes for all methods.  
- Request to report runtimes for LBP, TRW, and MF to enable fair comparisons.  
- Originality: novel tractable family defined by fast-mixing conditions; dual Euclidean projection preserving sparsity is a technical contribution.  
- Significance: principled bridge between variational approximation and MCMC with mixing-time guarantees; practical impact depends on runtime.  
- Strengths: clear conceptual contribution, solid theoretical basis, practical algorithms, and strong empirical gains.  
- Weaknesses: insufficient runtime reporting and scalability unproven due to experiments on relatively small models.  
- Suggested experiments: evaluate on larger or special polynomial-time-inference models (e.g., Flach’s complete-graph random fields).  
- Minor edits: change “onto the tree” to “onto graph T” (line 222) and consider “subgradient” instead of “derivative” (line 226).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel principled framework, theoretical guarantees, practical algorithms, and convincing empirical results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: potential high computational cost and limited-scale experiments; runtime reporting currently inadequate.  
- Overall: interesting, well-motivated paper with convincing results; address runtime/plot clarification and optimization details to strengthen practical utility.
