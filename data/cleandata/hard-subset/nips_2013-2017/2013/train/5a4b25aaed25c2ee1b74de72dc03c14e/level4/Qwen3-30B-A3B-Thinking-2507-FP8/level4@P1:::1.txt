

This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set, where Gibbs sampling converges rapidly. The key insight is to find parameters as close as possible (under various divergences) to the original model while satisfying a spectral norm condition guaranteeing rapid mixing. The authors derive projection algorithms for Euclidean distance, a piecewise KL divergence, and the reverse KL divergence, demonstrating improved marginal estimates over variational methods and original-model Gibbs sampling within limited computational time. The work builds on established mixing-time results [4,8] but introduces a fresh perspective on tractability—prior work focuses on structural approximations (e.g., tree-structured mean-field), whereas this leverages parameter-space constraints for mixing guarantees.

**Strengths:**  
The core idea is elegant and well-motivated. The theoretical foundation (e.g., Lemma 5 bounding dependency matrices, Theorem 7 for Euclidean projection) is sound and clearly articulated. The experimental design is thoughtful, comparing against strong baselines (LBP, TRW, mean-field, and original Gibbs) across grid and random graphs. Crucially, the method outperforms variational approaches in marginal accuracy under finite sampling time—a compelling practical advantage. The reverse KL approach (Section 5.4) is particularly well-justified as a generalization of mean-field, and the piecewise KL divergence offers a tractable alternative to intractable KL minimization.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**  
1. **Experimental Scalability and Cost:** The most significant limitation is the lack of analysis on how projection cost scales with model size. The experiments use tiny graphs (8×8 grids, 10-node random graphs), but the projection step (e.g., Theorem 7’s dual optimization) is computationally heavy. The runtime comparisons in Figure 2 omit projection time entirely (as noted in Section 6.1), making the claimed efficiency gains misleading. If projection requires 30,000 Gibbs iterations (as stated), the "faster" method effectively gets a free variational optimization, undermining the comparison. The paper should explicitly include projection time in all runtime metrics and explore larger models where junction-tree inference becomes intractable (e.g., using symmetry to compute exact marginals).

2. **Theoretical Tightness:** The spectral norm bound (‖R‖₂ < 1) is conservative. For 2D Ising models, the known mixing threshold β_c ≈ 0.4407 is significantly higher than the bound’s β = 0.25 (Section 7), suggesting the constraint is unnecessarily restrictive. While the paper notes using a looser bound (‖R‖₂ ≤ 2.5) in practice, this weakens the theoretical guarantee and merits deeper discussion—why does the bound loosen so dramatically? A tighter bound would strengthen the approach.

3. **Clarity and Presentation:**  
   - The KL divergence notation is inconsistent: Section 1 uses KL(q||p) with q as the true distribution and p as the approximation (opposite to standard convention), causing confusion. This should be standardized to KL(p||q) for consistency with literature.  
   - Lemma 5’s proof is incomplete (the reference [8] covers only zero-field cases), and the dependency matrix R’s intractability (due to the max over configurations) is not adequately addressed.  
   - The dual optimization in Section 4 (Theorem 7) is poorly explained; g(Λ,M), M, and Λ are introduced without definition, and the notation D(Λ,M) = Π_c[R+M−Λ'Z] lacks context. A brief sketch of the derivation would aid readability.

4. **Scope Limitations:** The approach is restricted to binary Ising models. Generalizing to multi-state variables or non-binary MRFs would require new mixing bounds for dependency matrices, which is non-trivial (Section 7). The paper acknowledges this but offers no path forward, limiting its broader impact.

**Recommendation:**  
The paper presents a novel, theoretically grounded method with promising empirical results on small-scale problems. However, the omission of projection cost in runtime comparisons and the conservative spectral bound significantly undermine the practical claims. The authors should: (1) include projection time in all experiments, (2) clarify KL notation and dependency matrix properties, (3) discuss the gap between theoretical bounds and empirical thresholds, and (4) explore scalability to larger models (e.g., via symmetry-based marginals). Without these, the contribution remains a compelling "toy" result rather than a scalable inference framework. While the idea is valuable, the current evidence does not sufficiently support its applicability to larger problems, which is critical for NIPS.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel parameter-space projection approach; strong theoretical foundation; clear empirical gains over baselines under finite time.  
*Con:* Projection cost ignored in runtime; spectral bound overly conservative; presentation lacks clarity on key technical points; limited to small binary models.
