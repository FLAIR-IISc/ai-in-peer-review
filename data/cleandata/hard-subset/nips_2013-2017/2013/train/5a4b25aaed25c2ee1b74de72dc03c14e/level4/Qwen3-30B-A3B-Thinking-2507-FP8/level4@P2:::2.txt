

This paper proposes a novel approach to accelerate Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting original parameters onto a fast-mixing parameter space satisfying spectral norm constraints. The authors formulate this as a constrained optimization problem under multiple divergences (Euclidean, piecewise KL, and reversed KL), deriving efficient algorithms including a dual method for Euclidean projection via singular value thresholding. The core insight—using parameter-space conditions (not graph structure) to guarantee rapid mixing—represents a meaningful departure from standard tractable approximations like mean-field or loopy belief propagation, which typically rely on structural constraints. This aligns with recent NIPS work on alternative tractability notions (e.g., Globerson & Jaakkola, 2006; Hazan & Shashua, 2008), though the paper’s focus on spectral norm conditions for Gibbs mixing is distinct.

**Strengths:**  
The paper’s originality is strong. It addresses a well-known limitation of Gibbs sampling in high-treewidth models (e.g., strong interactions causing exponential mixing times) through a clean, theoretically grounded framework. The derivation of the dual projection method (Theorem 7) is technically sound and generalizes beyond Ising models. The experimental comparison to variational methods (LBP, TRW, mean-field) and naive Gibbs sampling demonstrates that projected parameters yield more accurate marginals within limited sampling time—a significant practical advantage. The significance is clear: for applications where sampling time is constrained (e.g., real-time inference), this method offers a tangible improvement over existing approaches. The paper also thoughtfully acknowledges limitations (e.g., spectral norm conservatism in 2D Ising models, Section 7), enhancing credibility.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Clarity & Technical Execution:* The dual formulation (Section 4) requires clarification. The constraint $Z_{ij} D_{ij} = 0$ (where $D = |B|$) ensures the projected graph structure matches the original (i.e., non-zero edges remain non-zero), but the explanation fails to explicitly state this. An algorithm box would resolve ambiguity about the procedure: *Do gradient updates complete before sampling, or are they interleaved?* The convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent (Section 5.1) is also unaddressed—this is critical for practical use. The paper states the method "guarantees convergence" but cites Ergodic Mirror Descent (Duchi et al., 2012) without verifying the required conditions (e.g., step-size decay).  

*Experiments & Significance:* The time-to-accuracy comparison (Section 6) is flawed. Figure 2 reports results after 30K Gibbs iterations, but this ignores the *computational cost of projection*. For instance, the Euclidean projection requires 30K Gibbs steps (Section 6.1), while naive Gibbs might generate 30K samples in the same time. Without time-normalized metrics (e.g., accuracy vs. runtime), the claim that "projecting generally gives better results" lacks justification. Error bars are missing in Figures 2–3, making statistical significance unclear. The comparison also omits relevant sampling methods like blocked Gibbs (Swendsen & Wang, 1987) or lifted Gibbs (Gogate & Dechter, 2011), which are standard for Ising models and could better contextualize the gains.  

*Originality & Scope:* While the spectral norm condition is novel for Gibbs mixing, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate from prior work on fast-mixing parameter sets (e.g., Hayes, 2006; Dyer et al., 2009). The 2D Ising model example (Section 7) shows the spectral bound ($\|R\|_2 < 1$) is conservative versus the known threshold ($\beta_c \approx 0.44$), but this weakness is acknowledged without exploring tighter bounds—a missed opportunity to strengthen significance.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper presents a compelling, original idea with strong theoretical grounding and practical relevance. However, the current presentation lacks clarity in the method description and experimental justification. To strengthen the contribution:  
1. Add a concise algorithm box detailing the projected gradient descent procedure (including sampling/gradient update interleaving).  
2. Report accuracy *vs. time*, not just sampling iterations, and include error bars.  
3. Compare against blocked Gibbs and lifted Gibbs to validate the method’s relative advantage.  
4. Clarify the convergence conditions for the optimization.  

With these revisions, the paper would make a significant contribution to the NIPS community by providing a practical, theoretically sound method for accelerating inference in challenging graphical models. As-is, the technical execution and experimental rigor fall short of the quality expected for NIPS. **Reject** due to insufficient evidence for the method’s utility and clarity issues in the core algorithm.
