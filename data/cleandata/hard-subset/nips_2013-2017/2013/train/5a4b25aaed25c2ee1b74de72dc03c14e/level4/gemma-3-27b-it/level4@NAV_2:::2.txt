The authors propose a method for improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model’s original parameterization onto a new parameter setting that satisfies conditions, such as the Dobrushin criterion, needed for fast mixing. They formulate the projection as a constrained optimization problem and examine this projection under several distance/divergence measures.

This work addresses an important problem: accelerating MCMC convergence in Ising models, and the combination of stochastic and deterministic inference is a relatively under-explored area. The idea of augmenting the original parameters to improve mixing time is conceptually sound. However, the description of the dual formulation of the projection problem lacks clarity – for example, the mechanism by which the constraints *Z<sub>ij</sub>D<sub>ij</sub> = 0* ensure the new parameterization remains within the original parameter space is not immediately obvious (couldn’t it restrict the parameter space?). The overall procedure also needs clarification: is the projected gradient descent run to completion before Gibbs sampling, or is there an interleaving of sampling and gradient updates? An algorithmic description box would greatly improve understanding. Furthermore, the convergence properties of the proposed projected gradient descent strategy should be discussed.

The experimental evaluation is not fully convincing. The choice of 30,000 Gibbs iterations for some methods is arbitrary and potentially misleading. Given the comparison between a sampling method and deterministic approximations, a time-based comparison would be fairer. The figures lack error bars, making it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the results. The second set of results demonstrates improved mixing with the projected Gibbs sampler, but fails to account for the computational cost of performing the projection itself (e.g., if projection takes substantial time, it may outweigh the benefits of faster sampling). Finally, the exclusion of comparisons to established techniques like blocked Gibbs sampling or Gogate’s “Lifted Gibbs Sampler” is a notable omission.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting idea with potential. However, further development of the projection problem and its associated method, along with a more comprehensive and carefully designed set of experiments, are needed to fully demonstrate the utility and practical benefits of the proposed approach. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in graphical model inference.
*   Presents a novel approach combining deterministic optimization and MCMC.
*   Demonstrates potential improvements in mixing time compared to standard variational methods.
*   Provides theoretical analysis of projections under various divergences.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The dual formulation of the projection problem lacks clarity.
*   The experimental evaluation is incomplete and lacks sufficient statistical rigor.
*   The computational cost of projection is not considered in the performance comparison.
*   The convergence guarantees of the proposed optimization algorithm are not explicitly stated.
*   Missing comparisons to relevant existing methods (e.g., blocked Gibbs sampling).
