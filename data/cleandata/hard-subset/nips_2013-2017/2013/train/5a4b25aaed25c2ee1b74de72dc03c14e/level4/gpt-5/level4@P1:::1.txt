This paper proposes approximating slow-mixing Ising models by projecting their parameters onto a set that guarantees rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs sampling. The key idea is to find parameters that are as close as possible to the original model while satisfying a spectral-norm bound on the dependency matrix, which is sufficient for fast mixing. The authors instantiate this projection under several divergences: Euclidean distance, an “inclusive” KL (KL(θ||ψ), mostly of theoretical interest due to intractable expectations under θ), a piecewise KL defined over tractable subgraphs, and the “reverse” KL (KL(ψ||θ)), optimized via stochastic approximation using samples from the projected, fast-mixing model. Experiments on small Ising models show that sampling from the projected model can yield more accurate marginals for a fixed computational budget than sampling from the original model, and performs favorably against mean field (MF), loopy BP (LBP), and TRW.

Relation to prior work: The approach builds on known sufficient conditions for rapid mixing in spin systems (e.g., Dyer et al. [4], Hayes [8]) and uses them as feasibility constraints in a projection-based approximation scheme, which appears to be novel. The piecewise KL idea connects to tree-structured or low-treewidth approximations (e.g., [15,16,17,19]), but here is used to define a proxy divergence for projection rather than for inference directly.

Strengths:
- Conceptually neat and original: formulate “tractability” via guaranteed mixing rather than exact inference, and approximate by projecting onto this class.
- Solid use of recent mixing results to obtain verifiable sufficient conditions (spectral norm bound) and an algorithmic projection procedure (thresholded SVD with dual variables to preserve sparsity and constrain |B|).
- Clear empirical message on small graphs: under strong couplings and limited sampling time, sampling from the projected model can outperform standard variational baselines and Gibbs on the original model.

Concerns and weaknesses:

Scope and generality:
- The results are limited to binary pairwise Ising models. Extending this to general discrete MRFs seems nontrivial: deriving tight, usable bounds on the dependency matrix beyond Ising is significantly harder, and the paper does not address this.
- Scaling with problem size is unclear. Mixing times and spectral conditions can depend strongly on graph size and structure; experiments on 8×8 grids and 10-node random graphs do not probe this regime.

Clarity and presentation:
- Several “standard results” are stated without reference or proof sketch (e.g., Theorem 6). Lemma 5 is used in general form but the cited proof covers only a zero-field special case; please either provide a proof sketch or a direct reference for the general statement.
- The properties and tractability of computing the dependency matrix R should be discussed explicitly. As written, R involves a maximization over configurations and is generally intractable; the paper relies on Lemma 5 to bound R for Ising models.
- The second half of Section 4 is very hard to follow. Variables g, M, and Λ are referenced before being defined; Theorem 7 (the dual formulation) introduces notation (e.g., triple dot product, Πc[·], Z) that needs clearer definitions and motivation. A concise derivation or a more detailed appendix, plus a high-level road map in the main text, would help substantially.
- Notation for KL divergences is inconsistent in Section 1: KL(q||p) is used for both directions in different contexts, and the roles of p and q are reversed from common conventions. Please standardize and clarify early.

Experiments and fairness of comparisons:
- It appears the time-accuracy plot in Figure 2 excludes the time spent computing the projection. Section 6.1 hints at this but is ambiguous. This should be made explicit, and comparisons including projection time should be reported. As it stands, the method benefits from an upfront (potentially substantial) optimization without being charged for it, which weakens the practical significance of the improvement over standard Gibbs.
- Larger-scale experiments are missing. Even if exact marginals are unavailable, one can still evaluate on larger models by using long-run sampling as a reference or exploiting symmetries that yield ground truth.
- While KL(θ||ψ) is generally intractable, on the small graphs used here it could be computed with junction tree and would serve as a “best-case” projection target for the sampling-in-ψ strategy. Including such results would be informative.
- The paper notes that mean field is a degenerate case of reverse-KL projection (corresponding to R having zero norm), yet the empirical gap between MF and reverse-KL projection is large. A discussion of why reverse-KL within the broader fast-mixing class markedly improves over MF (e.g., due to nonzero interactions under a spectral norm budget) would strengthen the narrative.

Technical points:
- The paper acknowledges that the spectral-norm bound is conservative (e.g., on the 2D grid, βc ≈ 0.4407 vs. spectral threshold at β = 0.25). More discussion of practical choices (e.g., using a bound of 2.5 in Section 6.1) and diagnostics for verifying rapid mixing in practice would be useful.
- Please clarify the computational complexity of the dual projection (Theorem 7), including how sparsity is preserved in practice and the cost per LBFGS-B iteration.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and appealing perspective on approximate inference via projection to a provably fast-mixing class.
- Solid theoretical grounding and an interesting dual projection algorithm that respects sparsity.
- Empirical wins over common variational baselines in regimes of strong coupling and limited time.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited scope (binary pairwise models) and unclear scalability.
- Ambiguities in experimental timing; lack of results including projection time and on larger graphs.
- Clarity issues in Section 4 and notation inconsistencies.
- Conservative nature of the spectral bound and limited discussion of practical implications.

Overall, I like the core idea and its clean connection to mixing-time theory. With clearer exposition, more transparent experimental accounting, and additional evidence on larger instances (or stronger “best-case” small-graph studies), the paper would make a stronger case for its practical impact.
