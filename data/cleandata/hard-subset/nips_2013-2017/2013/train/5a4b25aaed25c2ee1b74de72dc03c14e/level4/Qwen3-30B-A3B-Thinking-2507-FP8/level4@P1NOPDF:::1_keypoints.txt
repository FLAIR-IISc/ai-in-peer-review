- Proposes projecting a given pairwise Ising model onto the nearest "fast-mixing" model (via a spectral bound) to accelerate Gibbs sampling.
- Core idea is original: find parameters close to the original while enforcing weak interactions that guarantee rapid mixing.
- Experiments show improved marginal estimates within fixed compute time versus mean-field, belief propagation, and standard Gibbs on small toy Ising models.
- Main concern: scalability is unclear because results are limited to tiny models.
- Mixing time comparisons do not establish how performance scales with problem size.
- The computationally intensive projection step is omitted from runtime comparisons (Figure 2), inflating the apparent advantage.
- Generalization beyond binary states is unlikely because extending convergence bounds to non-binary cases is difficult.
- Clarity issues: Theorem 6 cites "standard results" without proof or reference, and Lemma 5 is stated without proof (existing work covers only the zero-field case).
- The dependency matrix R is intractable due to its max formulation; the paper should state this explicitly as motivation for Lemma 5.
- Section 4 is confusing: Theorem 7 references g, M, and Λ before they are defined, and Theorem 4 uses unexplained notation; excessive restatement of [8] distracts from the contribution.
- Section 1 misuses KL notation by defining KL(q||p) with q as the true distribution, reversing standard convention.
- Experimental shortcomings: projection time is ambiguously addressed or excluded, so claimed runtime gains are unsupported unless projection cost is included.
- Lack of experiments on larger models; suggested alternatives include symmetric models with tractable marginals (via junction trees) or long-sampling baselines to validate scalability.
- Paper fails to analyze why mean-field (a degenerate reverse KL case) performs much worse than reverse KL projections, a gap worth investigating.
- Suggestion: report KL(θ||ψ) as a best-case benchmark on toy models using junction trees to strengthen comparisons.
- Overall: elegant and well-motivated approach leveraging mixing theory, but empirical claims are overextended due to omitted computational costs, missing scalability evidence, and technical/notation ambiguities, limiting applicability to small binary models.
