Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an approximate inference approach for Ising models that projects a given (potentially slow-mixing) parameter vector onto a set of “fast-mixing” parameters, defined by sufficient conditions for rapid mixing of single-site Gibbs sampling. The core condition is a spectral norm bound on the dependency matrix, which, for Ising models, can be enforced by constraining the matrix of interaction strengths. The authors consider several notions of proximity between the original and projected models: Euclidean distance in parameter space, inclusive KL(θ||ψ) (theoretically attractive but generally intractable), a tractable piecewise KL based on low-treewidth subgraphs, and the reverse KL(ψ||θ), which can be optimized by stochastic approximation because expectations under ψ are estimable via fast-mixing Gibbs. They derive a dual formulation for Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) that preserves sparsity and controls the spectral norm, and they evaluate the approach against Gibbs on the original parameters, MF, LBP, and TRW on small grids and random graphs.

The work leverages classic mixing-time results for spin systems (e.g., Dobrushin-type conditions and spectral criteria; see Dyer et al. and Hayes) and connects to variational approximations such as MF, structured MF, LBP, TRW, planar decomposition, and piecewise training. The idea of using provably fast-mixing parameter sets as the tractable family and projecting onto that family is, to my knowledge, new in the NIPS literature.

Quality
- Theory: The paper correctly uses sufficient conditions for rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs. For Ising models, it employs the bound Rij ≤ tanh(|βij|), implying a spectral-norm condition on |β|. This is a clean way to avoid computing the dependency matrix exactly, which is intractable. The Euclidean projection method (Theorem 7) is interesting and, if detailed properly, could be a useful tool.
- Algorithmics: The reverse-KL optimization via stochastic approximation is reasonable given that ψ is constrained to be fast-mixing and hence sampleable. The piecewise KL is a sensible tractable surrogate.
- Experiments: Results on small graphs show improvements over common baselines in some regimes with strong interactions and limited time for sampling. However, the time-accuracy comparisons appear not to include the potentially substantial cost of the projection step, which undermines the fairness of the comparison with “Gibbs on the original parameters.”
- Limitations: The method relies on sufficient (not necessary) conditions that can be conservative. The paper sometimes uses a spectral bound of 2.5 in experiments, which exceeds the stated theoretical condition (<1) and thus breaks the guarantee; this needs careful justification.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but some parts need significant clarification.
- Theorem and lemma presentation: Several “standard results” (e.g., Theorem 6) are stated without citations or proof sketches. Lemma 5 is credited to a special case in Hayes; please give a clear reference and explain the extension beyond zero field.
- Dependency matrix: Explain explicitly that computing R exactly is intractable but that the tanh bound suffices to enforce a spectral condition without computing R, and discuss what is actually computed in practice.
- Section 4 (projection): The presentation before and in Theorem 7 is hard to follow. Variables (g, M, Λ) and operators (·, Πc) appear before being defined. Please introduce the primal, constraints, dual variables, and projection operator step-by-step, and provide intuition for the dual and the role of the masking matrix Z. A small worked example would help.
- Notation: In the introduction, the roles of p and q and the direction of KL are inconsistent with common conventions and later sections. Please standardize throughout.

Originality
The core idea—defining a tractable family via fast-mixing guarantees and projecting onto it—is novel and distinct from standard variational families (trees, planar, MF, TRW). The dual Euclidean projection preserving sparsity and enforcing a spectral constraint is also technically interesting.

Significance
If scalable, the approach could offer a practical alternative when exact inference is infeasible and standard MCMC mixes slowly, by trading asymptotic correctness for guaranteed rapid mixing. However, current evidence is limited to small models. The lack of accounting for projection time and the use of a looser spectral bound in experiments make it hard to assess end-to-end practical benefit. Extending the approach beyond binary variables and beyond single-site Gibbs is nontrivial and remains open.

Experimental evaluation
- Fairness: Please include total wall-clock (or iteration-equivalent) cost, including the projection/optimization, in the time–accuracy plots. As written (and partially acknowledged), the comparison favors the proposed methods.
- Scale: Add experiments on larger graphs where junction tree is infeasible. Options include models with exploitable symmetries for exact marginals, or using very long, well-diagnosed MCMC runs as ground truth.
- KL(θ||ψ) baseline: On small graphs, it would be informative to include results minimizing KL(θ||ψ) (with exact inference) as a best-case for the “zero-avoiding” direction.
- MF relation: Since MF is a degenerate case of reverse-KL projection (R constrained to zero), please discuss why reverse-KL under the fast-mixing constraint significantly outperforms MF, and what that says about the capacity of the fast-mixing family.
- Guarantees vs practice: Clarify and justify the choice of spectral norm bound >1 in experiments, and report empirical mixing diagnostics to support the claim that the chains are indeed fast-mixing under this looser bound.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel and appealing perspective: fast-mixing families as tractable approximators.
- Technically interesting Euclidean projection via dual thresholding that preserves sparsity.
- Reverse-KL optimization that leverages sampling under fast-mixing constraints.
- Empirical improvements over strong baselines in some regimes with strong couplings.

Cons:
- Experimental comparisons do not account for projection/optimization cost.
- Limited scale; unclear how performance and mixing gains scale with problem size.
- Theoretical guarantees are not aligned with experimental settings (spectral bound >1).
- Clarity issues in the projection section and inconsistent notation.
- Extension beyond Ising and beyond single-site Gibbs is left open.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full citations and brief proof sketches (or pointers) for “standard results,” including Theorem 6 and the generalization in Lemma 5.
- Rework Section 4: define all variables/operators before use, add intuition and, if possible, a small illustrative example of the projection.
- Standardize notation for p, q and KL directions throughout.
- Report total computational cost, including projection, in time–accuracy plots; discuss parameter sensitivity (step size, pool size).
- Add larger-scale experiments and a KL(θ||ψ) “best-case” on small graphs.
- Discuss the gap between sufficient conditions and practice; report empirical mixing diagnostics when using relaxed bounds.
- Comment on prospects and obstacles for extensions to multivalued variables and other MCMC schemes.

Overall assessment
A creative and potentially impactful idea, with solid connections to mixing-time theory and variational inference. The current version demonstrates promise on small problems, but the empirical evaluation and clarity of the projection method need strengthening, and the mismatch between guarantees and experimental settings should be addressed. With these revisions, the contribution would be significantly more convincing.
