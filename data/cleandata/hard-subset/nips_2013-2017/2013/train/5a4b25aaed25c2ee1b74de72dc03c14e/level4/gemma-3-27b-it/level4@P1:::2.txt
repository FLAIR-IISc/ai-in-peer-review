The authors propose a method for improving the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model’s original parameterization onto a new parameter setting that satisfies conditions for fast mixing, such as the Dobrushin criterion. They formulate the projection as a constrained optimization problem, exploring this projection under several distance/divergence measures. 

In my opinion, methods that combine principles from stochastic and deterministic inference represent an under-explored area, and this is therefore an interesting idea. While the core concept of augmenting the original parameters to improve mixing time is intuitive, I found the description of the dual of the projection problem a little unclear – for example, how do the  *z<sub>ij</sub>d<sub>ij</sub>* = 0 constraints ensure that the new parameterization remains defined over the same space (couldn’t it instead be over a smaller space)? I also wasn’t entirely clear on the overall procedure; is the projected gradient descent performed to completion before running a Gibbs sampler, or is there some interleaving of sampling with gradient updates? An algorithm box would help to clarify this. Furthermore, is the proposed projected gradient descent strategy guaranteed to converge?

I also found the experimental evaluation somewhat unconvincing. In the initial experiments, why was the Gibbs sampler run for 30,000 iterations? Given the comparison between a sampling method and deterministic approaches, a comparison based on runtime would seem more equitable. Also, where are the error bars on the presented charts – the reader cannot assess the statistical significance of the results. The second set of results compares the original/naive Gibbs sampler with Gibbs samplers employing different projections, demonstrating that projection can indeed lead to faster mixing. However, the computational cost of performing the projection operation is not considered in this comparison (e.g., if the projection requires one minute, and the naive Gibbs sampler can generate 10,000 samples in that time, the projection might not be worthwhile). Finally, why was there no comparison to blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate’s “Lifted Gibbs Sampler”?

Overall, this is a promising idea. However, the development of the projection problem and the proposed method could benefit from further refinement, and a more robust set of experiments is needed to convincingly demonstrate the utility of the method. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem: slow mixing in Ising models.
*   Proposes a novel approach combining deterministic projection with stochastic sampling.
*   Demonstrates improved accuracy compared to standard variational methods.
*   Provides theoretical justification for the projection algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Technical details of the dual optimization problem are unclear.
*   Experiments lack sufficient detail (e.g., error bars, runtime comparisons).
*   Comparison to relevant baselines (e.g., blocked Gibbs, Lifted Gibbs) is missing.
*   Convergence guarantees of the projected gradient descent are not discussed.
*   The looseness of the spectral norm bound for fast mixing is questionable.
