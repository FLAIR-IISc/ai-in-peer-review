This paper proposes an approach to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in pairwise Ising models with strong interactions, known to exhibit slow mixing. The method involves projecting the original model onto a "fast-mixing" model by identifying parameters that closely approximate the original model while satisfying a spectral bound for rapid mixing. Experimental results demonstrate improved marginal estimates for certain regimes, outperforming variational methods and Gibbs sampling in the original model.

The technical content builds upon prior results establishing conditions for rapid mixing. Although the idea of projecting onto the nearest rapidly mixing model as an approximate inference method is novel, its scalability to larger models is uncertain. The experiments, which involve comparing mixing times of samplers on different models, do not provide clear insights into how the approach will scale with problem size. Furthermore, the computationally expensive projection step required to build the fast-mixing model is not accounted for in the results. The generalizability of the approach beyond binary states is also questionable, as establishing convergence bounds for more complex cases is challenging.

The presentation is generally clear, but some reorganization and clarification are necessary. The paper references "standard results" without providing adequate explanations or citations. For instance, Theorem 6 is stated without proof or reference, and Lemma 5 is left unproven. The properties of the dependency matrix R, including its tractability, should be discussed. The second half of Section 4 is unclear, and the notation used in Theorem 7 requires more explanation. Additionally, the notation for KL divergence is inconsistent, and the time comparison in Figure 2 does not account for the computation required for projection.

The experimental evaluation is limited, and the results would be more convincing if they included the computation time required for projection. Experiments on larger models would provide valuable insights into the scalability of the approach. Exploring the intractable KL(\theta||\psi) as a potential "best case" for sampling in an approximate model would also be interesting. The large difference between mean field error and the error from reverse KL projection deserves discussion.

Overall, the idea of approximating slow-mixing models by projecting to the closest fast-mixing model is elegant, but concerns about experimental comparisons and the limited range of applicable models need to be addressed.
