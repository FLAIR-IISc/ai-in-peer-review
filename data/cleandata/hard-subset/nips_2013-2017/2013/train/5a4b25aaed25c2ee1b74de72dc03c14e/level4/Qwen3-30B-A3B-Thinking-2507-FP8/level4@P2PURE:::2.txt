

The authors propose a method to improve the mixing rate of Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting the model's original parameterization onto a new parameter setting satisfying the Dobrushin criterion for fast mixing. They formulate the projection as a constrained optimization problem, with constraints ensuring the new parameters remain defined over the same space as the original, and analyze this projection under multiple distance/divergence measures.  

While I find the idea of combining stochastic and deterministic inference principles intriguing—given this area remains under-explored—I have concerns about the technical development. The description of the dual projection problem is somewhat unclear, particularly regarding how the constraints $z_{ij} d_{ij} = 0$ guarantee the new parameterization operates over the same space (rather than a smaller subspace). I also seek clarification on the overall procedure: are projected gradient updates performed to completion before running Gibbs sampling, or are they interleaved with sampling steps? An explicit algorithm box would greatly aid understanding. Additionally, is convergence of the proposed projected gradient descent strategy guaranteed?  

The experimental evaluation requires strengthening. In the first experiment, the 30,000 Gibbs iterations appear arbitrary; a runtime-based comparison would be more appropriate when contrasting sampling with deterministic methods. Error bars are missing from the charts, preventing assessment of result significance. In the second experiment, while the projected Gibbs samplers demonstrate faster mixing than the naive variant, the computational cost of performing the projection is unaccounted for. For instance, if projection requires one minute, and the naive Gibbs sampler generates 10,000 samples in that time, the method’s practical utility is questionable. Finally, the absence of comparisons to blocked Gibbs samplers or Gogate’s "Lifted Gibbs Sampler" weakens the evaluation.  

Overall, the core idea is promising. However, the development of the projection problem, the proposed method, and the experimental validation require significant refinement to convincingly demonstrate the method’s utility.
