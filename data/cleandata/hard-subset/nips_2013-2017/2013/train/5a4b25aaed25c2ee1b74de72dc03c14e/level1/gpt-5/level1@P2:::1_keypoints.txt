- Paper proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing subset (via a dependency matrix R and spectral-norm bound) to make Gibbs sampling tractable.
- Fast-mixing condition used: ||R||2 < 1 with Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij| for Ising models; leverages prior mixing-time bounds.
- Contribution: sparsity-preserving Euclidean projection via dualizing a spectral-norm-constrained problem and solving with L-BFGS-B to avoid graph densification.
- Contribution: projection under multiple divergences (Euclidean, inclusive KL, piecewise KL, exclusive KL) with stochastic approximation for KL(ψ||θ).
- Experiments compare projected Gibbs to LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on original parameters on 8×8 grids and small random graphs.
- Contrasts prior tractable families (trees, planar, structured MF) and links projection to guaranteed MCMC tractability.
- Theoretical framing (||R||2 < 1 and Rij ≤ |βij|) is standard and correct, motivating the projection idea.
- Theorem 6 contains a typographical error: spectral-norm constraint should bound singular values by c (S'ii = min(Sii,c)), not c^2.
- The dual method (Theorem 7) is interesting but lacks statements on convexity, duality gap, and conditions for strong duality; primal convexity in (B,D) is not clarified.
- Using the same MCMC samples to estimate μ and form gradient products for KL(ψ||θ) introduces bias and correlation; convergence diagnostics and per-iteration mixing budgets are missing.
- Empirical relaxation of spectral threshold from c ≤ 1 to c = 2.5 undermines formal guarantees; results with c > 1 are heuristic and should be separated from guaranteed claims.
- No empirical mixing diagnostics reported (autocorrelation, effective sample size, spectral gap surrogates).
- Experimental scale is modest (8×8 grids, n=10 random graphs), limiting claims of broad applicability.
- Ambiguity in experimental protocol: inconsistent statements about whether ground-truth marginals come from junction tree or Gibbs samples need clarification.
- Compute budgets are not equalized; projection methods add substantial overhead—fair wall-clock comparisons and time–accuracy curves are needed.
- Piecewise KL approach is plausible but underexplored; sensitivity to subgraph choice, coverage, and treewidth is not analyzed.
- Clarity issues: fix spectral projection typo, use consistent notation (Hadamard product with Z), and include missing footnote.
- Provide proof sketches or references for Lemma 5 and Theorem 7 and explicitly state convexity/duality assumptions.
- Originality: novel idea of projecting to a fast-mixing set and dual projection preserving sparsity; piecewise KL is a fresh extension.
- Significance: could influence approximate inference under strong couplings by regularizing toward fast mixing, but impact limited by loose conditions, small-scale experiments, and focus on Ising models.
- Pros: clear novel perspective, practical algorithms for projections, and empirical gains over MF/LBP/TRW in limited settings.
- Cons: theoretical guarantees weakened by c > 1 use, possible theorem error, missing optimality conditions for dual method, and fairness/scope issues in experiments.
- Suggested fixes: correct theorem typos, state convexity/duality results, report experiments for c ≤ 1 and c > 1 separately, add mixing diagnostics, equalize wall-clock budgets, clarify ground-truth and sampling details, and analyze subgraph choices for piecewise KL.
- Suggested extension: discuss other fast-mixing criteria (Dobrushin, correlation decay/Weitz) and whether they can be incorporated.
- Overall: thoughtful, original, and promising work that needs tighter theoretical statements and more rigorous, fair experimental evaluation; borderline but leaning positive.
