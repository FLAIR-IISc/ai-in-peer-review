This paper introduces a novel approach for approximate inference in high-treewidth Ising models. Instead of approximating the model’s structure, as is common in variational methods like mean-field or tree-reweighted belief propagation, the authors propose approximating the model’s *parameters*. The core idea is to project the original, potentially "slow-mixing" parameters onto a set of parameters guaranteed to be "fast-mixing" for Gibbs sampling. This fast-mixing property is ensured by constraining the spectral norm of the interaction matrix, a condition derived from established MCMC theory [4, 8]. The paper develops algorithms to perform this projection under several divergences: Euclidean distance, a novel piecewise KL-divergence, and the standard forward and reverse KL-divergences.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of defining a tractable family of models by their fast-mixing properties, rather than structural simplicity (e.g., low treewidth), is a significant and original contribution. It directly addresses a critical practical challenge in MCMC methods—convergence time—and offers a new perspective on approximation.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. A key contribution is the derivation of an efficient dual algorithm for performing the Euclidean projection while preserving the graph’s sparsity structure (Theorem 7). The use of this projection within a stochastic gradient descent framework for minimizing the KL(ψ||θ) divergence is well-motivated and presents a practical algorithm.
*   **Clarity and Experiments:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its motivation and methods. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing the proposed methods against strong and appropriate baselines (LBP, TRW, MF, and time-limited Gibbs sampling). The results convincingly demonstrate that for a finite time budget, sampling from the projected parameters yields more accurate marginals than both standard variational methods and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters, especially in challenging high-interaction regimes.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The primary weakness lies in the potential looseness of the spectral norm bound used to guarantee fast mixing. The authors correctly acknowledge this limitation in their discussion, noting that the bound is conservative. More importantly, the experiments use an empirically chosen spectral norm bound of 2.5 instead of the theoretically justified 1.0. While this improves results, it weakens the claim of a theoretical “guarantee” and makes the method feel more heuristic.
*   The stochastic optimization procedure for the reverse KL projection involves several hyperparameters (e.g., step size, sample pool size). A more detailed analysis of their sensitivity would improve the paper's reproducibility and practical utility for other users.

In summary, the paper's novel conceptual framework and strong empirical results present a compelling case for acceptance. The approach is a valuable addition to the approximate inference toolkit.
