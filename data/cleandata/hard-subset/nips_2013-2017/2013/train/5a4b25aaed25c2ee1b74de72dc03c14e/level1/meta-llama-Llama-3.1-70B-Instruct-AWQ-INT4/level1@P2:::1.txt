The paper "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing" presents a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models. The authors propose a new notion of "tractability" that focuses on parameter-space conditions ensuring rapid mixing of Gibbs sampling, rather than exact inference. They develop an algorithm to project Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set, using various divergences, and demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, related work, and their approach. The technical sections are well-organized, and the proofs are relegated to the appendix, making the paper easy to follow.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their approach. The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, and the authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results. The notation is consistent, and the figures are helpful in illustrating the concepts.

Originality: The paper presents a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models. The idea of projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set is new and interesting. The authors also propose a new "piecewise" divergence, which is a useful contribution to the field.

Significance: The results are important, and the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The authors demonstrate that their approach is more accurate than variational approximations and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters, especially when the interaction strengths are strong and the time available for sampling is limited. The paper also provides a new perspective on the notion of "tractability" in graphical models.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of their approach, including theoretical and experimental results.
* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results.
* The results are important, and the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The paper assumes a specific form of the parameter-space conditions guaranteeing rapid mixing, which might not be applicable to all graphical models.
* The authors do not provide a thorough comparison with other approximate inference methods, such as loopy belief propagation or tree-reweighted belief propagation.
* The paper focuses on Ising models, and it is unclear how the approach can be extended to more general Markov random fields.
* The authors do not provide a clear discussion on the computational complexity of their approach, especially when compared to other methods.

Overall, I recommend accepting the paper, as it presents a novel and interesting approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models. The authors provide a thorough analysis of their approach, and the results are important and significant. However, I suggest that the authors address the arguments con acceptance in their final version, especially by providing a more thorough comparison with other methods and discussing the computational complexity of their approach.
