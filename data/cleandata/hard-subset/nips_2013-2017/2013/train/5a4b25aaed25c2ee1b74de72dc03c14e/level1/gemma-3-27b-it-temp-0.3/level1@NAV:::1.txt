## Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

This paper addresses the challenge of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for Ising models, particularly when interactions are strong. The authors propose projecting Ising model parameters onto a set guaranteed to be fast-mixing, leveraging the spectral norm of the dependency matrix as a mixing criterion. They explore several divergence measures for this projection – Euclidean distance (via SVD thresholding), KL-divergence (both theoretical and piecewise approximations), and a reversed KL-divergence minimized via stochastic approximation. 

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in approximate inference for graphical models, referencing key papers on mean-field inference [14, 20], loopy belief propagation [21], and tree-reweighted belief propagation [19]. It also connects to the broader literature on mixing times in Markov chains [10] and the Dobrushin criterion [11]. The novelty lies in framing fast mixing as a desirable property for parameter spaces and developing projection techniques to achieve it. 

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of slow mixing and proposes a principled approach to address it.
* **Multiple Divergence Exploration:** Investigating different divergence measures provides a nuanced understanding of the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost. The piecewise KL-divergence is a particularly interesting attempt to balance tractability and fidelity.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that projecting parameters can indeed improve accuracy compared to Gibbs sampling with original parameters, especially with limited sampling time.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The connection to spectral norm bounds for mixing times provides a solid theoretical basis for the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Conservatism of Spectral Bound:** The authors acknowledge the spectral norm bound may be conservative, potentially limiting the effectiveness of the projection.  A tighter bound would significantly strengthen the work.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the Euclidean projection is efficient, the stochastic approximation for the reversed KL-divergence requires careful parameter tuning and can be computationally expensive.
* **Limited Scalability:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small graphs. The scalability of the proposed methods to larger, more complex Ising models remains an open question.
* **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to other inference algorithms (LBP, TRW, MF) is present, but a more thorough comparison to recent advances in variational inference or alternative MCMC methods would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to improving the efficiency of Gibbs sampling.
* The empirical results demonstrate a clear benefit in terms of accuracy, particularly in challenging scenarios.
* The theoretical foundation is sound and connects to established results in Markov chain theory.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The conservatism of the spectral norm bound limits the potential impact of the work.
* The computational complexity of some of the proposed methods may hinder their practical applicability.
* The scalability to larger graphs is not demonstrated.

**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the limitations regarding the spectral bound and scalability are noteworthy, the novelty of the approach, the clear presentation, and the positive empirical results make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding the spectral bound and computational complexity. The authors should also consider expanding the experimental evaluation to larger graphs and comparing their methods to more recent state-of-the-art inference algorithms.
