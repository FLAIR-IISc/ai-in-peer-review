- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set to trade model accuracy for much faster Gibbs convergence.  
- Explores multiple divergence measures for projection: Euclidean, piecewise KL, and reversed KL.  
- Experimental results show benefits over standard inference methods in the tested configurations.  
- Well-motivated problem framing connecting slow mixing to approximate inference literature.  
- Clear and concise theoretical background on mixing times and dependency matrices with appropriate citations.  
- Introduces “fast-mixing” as a tractable modeling perspective distinct from tree-structured/low-treewidth approximations.  
- Technical development is solid, with Theorem 7 giving a practical Euclidean projection algorithm.  
- Insightful discussion of trade-offs between divergences and connection to optimization (Ergodic Mirror Descent for reversed KL).  
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope (mainly 8x8 grids and random graphs) and uses heuristic parameter tuning.  
- Relaxed spectral norm bound (e.g., 2.5) is empirically plausible but lacks strong theoretical justification and conflicts with known 2D Ising thresholds.  
- Piecewise KL divergence underperforms empirically and lacks deep analysis explaining why.  
- Notation (frequent θ and ψ) is confusing; clearer naming would aid readability.  
- Connection between theoretical results (Theorems 6 & 7) and practical implementation is not always clearly demonstrated; more examples would help.  
- Originality is moderate: novel projection idea but builds on established approximate inference and optimization concepts.  
- Significance is moderate and contingent on projection cost vs. sampling speed and accuracy loss; broader applications and complexity analysis needed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel idea, solid theory, demonstrated improvements, and exploration of multiple divergences.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experiments, weak theoretical support for relaxed bounds, poor piecewise KL performance, modest originality/significance.  
- Overall assessment: weak accept—interesting and technically sound but needs more extensive experiments and tighter theoretical analysis.
