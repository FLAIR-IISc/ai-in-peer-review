- Central idea: Project slow-mixing Ising model parameters onto a set guaranteed to be fast-mixing so Gibbs sampling becomes tractable.
- Fast-mixing criterion: Uses spectral-norm bound on absolute interaction parameters (|||β|||_2 < 1) from prior MCMC mixing-time theory.
- Novel paradigm: Instead of approximating structure or posterior, the method modifies model parameters to ensure rapid MCMC mixing.
- Euclidean projection: Presents a novel dual algorithm (Theorem 7) for sparse Euclidean projection using iterative singular value thresholding.
- Inclusive KL (KL(θ||ψ)): Theoretically motivated but intractable in practice because it requires inference in the original slow-mixing model.
- Piecewise KL: Proposes a tractable approximation to inclusive KL by maximizing KL over a set of tractable subgraphs (e.g., spanning trees).
- Exclusive KL (KL(ψ||θ)): Practical and effective; leads to projected stochastic gradient descent with gradients estimated by MCMC on ψ.
- Experiments: On grids and random graphs, projected-parameter Gibbs—especially with exclusive KL—gives more accurate marginals under limited time than Gibbs and outperforms MF, LBP, TRW in strong-interaction regimes.
- Originality/significance: Introduces a highly original paradigm addressing slow MCMC convergence with potential practical impact.
- Technical quality: Solid derivations; Euclidean projection algorithm and projected SGD formulation are valuable contributions.
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, logically organized, and provides clear background and experimental description.
- Experimental validation: Thorough comparisons to strong baselines demonstrate the method’s practical utility where others fail.
- Loose theoretical bound: The spectral-norm threshold is conservative in theory vs. practice (experiments use looser bound ≈2.5), leaving unclear how to choose it for new problems.
- Projection cost: The projection phase (especially for KL(ψ||θ)) is computationally expensive; paper does not fully compare methods under total time budget (projection + sampling).
- Limited generalizability: Developed and evaluated only for pairwise Ising models; extending to general MRFs requires nontrivial additional work.
- Acceptance arguments: Novel direction, solid theory and algorithms, strong empirical results, and clear presentation make it a valuable contribution.
- Rejection arguments: Practical utility depends on tuning the spectral-norm hyperparameter and experiments should more rigorously account for projection cost.
