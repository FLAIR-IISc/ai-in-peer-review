Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes using “fast-mixing” Ising models as a tractable family for approximate inference. Instead of constraining graph structure (as in mean-field, structured mean-field, TRW, or planar decompositions), the authors constrain parameters so that single-site Gibbs sampling is guaranteed to mix rapidly. They rely on a sufficient condition: the spectral norm of the dependency matrix R must be less than 1; for Ising models with xi ∈ {−1,+1}, they bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|, hence enforcing || |β| ||2 < 1 suffices.

They then “project” arbitrary parameters θ onto the fast-mixing set Ψ by minimizing different divergences:
- Euclidean distance: they derive a dual algorithm (Theorem 7) that uses a projection onto an operator-norm ball (via SVD singular value thresholding) while respecting sparsity (original graph) and the absolute-value coupling needed for R.
- KL(θ||ψ): conceptually natural (“zero-avoiding”) but impractical for high-treewidth θ since it requires expectations w.r.t. θ.
- Piecewise KL: approximate the KL by dropping edges to tractable subgraphs (chains, low-treewidth graphs), taking a max over subgraphs; enables exact subgraph KLs but is heuristic.
- KL(ψ||θ): practical (“zero-forcing”) because expectations are w.r.t. ψ, which is constrained to be fast-mixing; optimized by stochastic approximation using a sample pool and projected gradient steps.

Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare Euclidean, piecewise-KL, and KL(ψ||θ) projections to MF, LBP, TRW, and Gibbs on the original parameters. The authors report that projections yield more accurate marginals than MF/LBP/TRW and, under finite sampling budgets, outperform Gibbs run on the original slow-mixing parameters. They also discuss looseness of the spectral norm bound and practical choices that exceed the theory.

Quality:
- Theory: The fast-mixing condition and use of ||R||2 < 1 are standard; the bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is appropriate. The Euclidean projection with a sparsity mask is technically interesting; the dual formulation (Theorem 7) is a nice contribution. However:
  • Theorem 6 appears to have a typo: projecting onto ||B||2 ≤ c via SVD should threshold singular values at c (S′ii = min(Sii, c)), not c^2.
  • Theorem 8 (gradient of KL(ψ||θ)) seems to miss a minus sign. A standard derivation gives ∇ψ KL(ψ||θ) = −Covψ[f,f](ψ−θ). The paper’s expression equals Covψ[f,f](ψ−θ). Please clarify/correct.
  • The projection Πc[·] is non-smooth where singular values hit c. Using LBFGS-B on a non-smooth objective requires justification (e.g., subgradient arguments) or a smoothed variant; convergence guarantees are not discussed.
  • In experiments, the spectral norm bound is set to 2.5 although guarantees require <1. This breaks the theoretical mixing-time guarantee; the paper should either measure mixing times empirically to justify this choice or adhere to guaranteed regimes.
- Empirics: The evaluation is standard and informative, but several aspects limit conclusions:
  • No wall-clock or total-compute accounting; comparisons are not normalized for the (sometimes heavy) cost of projection. Including time-accuracy Pareto curves would strengthen claims.
  • Sensitivity to step size, sample pool size, and number of iterations for KL(ψ||θ) is not explored; error bars/confidence intervals are missing.
  • The piecewise-KL choice (max over subgraphs) is ad hoc; ablations (average vs max, different subgraph families) and a discussion of the induced bias would help.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The background on mixing times and dependency matrices is clear. Some notational issues (triple-dot product, Z′, and the Πc operator) could be clarified, and the potential typos in Theorems 6 and 8 should be fixed. The experimental setup is described carefully, though reporting of computational budgets and uncertainty could be improved.

Originality:
Using fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractable family, and explicitly projecting onto them under various divergences, is a novel angle relative to MF/structured MF/TRW/LBP/planar approximations. The dual projection algorithm that enforces both operator-norm and sparsity constraints is original and potentially useful beyond Ising models. The piecewise-KL objective is related to piecewise likelihood and mixtures of trees but is a distinct construction here.

Significance:
The idea of trading exactness for guaranteed rapid mixing is compelling and practically relevant in regimes where Gibbs on the true model is slow and variational methods underperform. The proposed methods can offer better finite-time accuracy than running Gibbs on the original model, and the approach could impact learning (constraining parameters to fast-mixing sets). However, the looseness of the spectral bound, lack of compute-normalized comparisons, and missing guarantees for the optimization routines temper the impact.

Pros:
- Conceptually new use of fast-mixing models as a tractable approximation family.
- Solid link to known mixing-time conditions; simple sufficiency criterion.
- Dual Euclidean projection that preserves sparsity is elegant and practical.
- KL(ψ||θ) projected SGD is a natural, scalable procedure with a clear relation to MF.
- Empirical gains over MF/LBP/TRW and finite-time Gibbs in strong-coupling regimes.

Cons:
- Theoretical–practical mismatch: experiments use a spectral bound (>1) that voids guarantees.
- Apparent sign error in Theorem 8 and possible typo in Theorem 6.
- Nonsmooth projection with LBFGS-B lacks convergence justification.
- Piecewise-KL objective is heuristic; limited analysis and mixed empirical performance.
- Experimental comparisons ignore projection cost and lack uncertainty estimates/hyperparameter sensitivity.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Either enforce ||R||2 < 1 in experiments or empirically verify mixing times for projected models when using larger bounds; report actual ||R||2 and measured mixing proxies.
- Fix Theorems 6 and 8 and add a short derivation. Discuss subgradient handling for Πc and provide convergence statements or references.
- Include compute-normalized comparisons (total wall-clock or total Gibbs updates, including those used during projection).
- Add error bars over trials and sensitivity analyses for the KL(ψ||θ) optimizer.
- Explore tighter/alternative fast-mixing criteria (e.g., weighted Dobrushin, tanh-based bounds, block-Gibbs bounds) to reduce conservativeness.
- For piecewise-KL, compare “max” vs “average” over subgraphs and provide intuition/theory for the chosen surrogate.

Overall:
This is an interesting and potentially impactful direction with a neat technical core. With corrections to the theoretical statements and a more rigorous experimental comparison (especially respecting guarantees or measuring mixing), it would make a strong contribution. As-is, I lean toward acceptance if the issues (particularly Theorems 6/8 and the guarantee mismatch) can be addressed; otherwise, a weak accept/borderline.
