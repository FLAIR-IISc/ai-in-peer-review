### Paper Summary

This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models, particularly for models with strong interactions where standard MCMC methods like Gibbs sampling mix slowly. Instead of approximating the model's graphical structure (as in mean-field or tree-reweighted belief propagation), the authors propose to approximate the model's *parameters*. The core idea is to project a given set of intractable (slow-mixing) parameters onto the set of "fast-mixing" parameters that is closest under some divergence.

The notion of "fast-mixing" is formalized using a well-known result from MCMC theory [8], which guarantees rapid mixing for Gibbs sampling if the spectral norm of the dependency matrix is less than one. For an Ising model, this provides a sufficient condition that the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths, `|| |β| ||_2`, is less than one.

The paper develops methods for this projection under four different divergences:
1.  **Euclidean Distance:** The authors derive a dual algorithm based on iterative SVD thresholding to find the closest parameter set in the L2 norm that satisfies the spectral norm constraint and preserves the original graph sparsity. This is the main technical result (Theorem 7).
2.  **Zero-Avoiding KL-Divergence (KL(q||p)):** This is presented as the most natural probabilistic divergence, but it is computationally intractable as it requires computing expectations with respect to the original, slow-mixing distribution `q`.
3.  **Piecewise KL-Divergence:** A novel and tractable approximation to the KL divergence, computed by finding the maximum KL divergence over a collection of tractable subgraphs (e.g., spanning trees) of the original model.
4.  **Zero-Forcing KL-Divergence (KL(p||q)):** This divergence is tractable because it only requires expectations with respect to the projected, fast-mixing distribution `p`. The authors propose minimizing it using projected stochastic gradient descent, where gradients are estimated via Gibbs sampling from the current fast-mixing parameter set. This is nicely framed as a generalization of mean-field inference, which constrains the spectral norm to be zero.

Experiments on 2D grids and random graphs demonstrate that Gibbs sampling on the projected parameters (especially using the zero-forcing KL) yields more accurate marginal estimates than standard variational methods like LBP, TRW, and naive mean-field. Crucially, when given a limited time budget, this approach is also shown to be more accurate than running Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing parameters.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The connection between MCMC mixing time theory and approximate inference is novel and well-motivated. The derivation of the Euclidean projection algorithm in Theorem 7 appears sound and provides a solid foundation for the subsequent methods. The claims are well-supported by a thorough experimental evaluation against relevant and strong baselines. The authors are commendably honest about the limitations of their work, particularly in the discussion section where they analyze the looseness of the spectral norm bound compared to known tight bounds for specific models like the 2D Ising grid. This self-awareness strengthens the paper.

**Clarity:**
The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is established clearly in the introduction, and the background section provides the necessary context on mixing times and dependency matrices for readers who may not be experts in MCMC theory. The progression from the simple Euclidean projection to the more complex KL-based divergences is logical and easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are described clearly, making the paper's conclusions understandable and reproducible.

**Originality:**
The central idea of this paper—approximating a distribution by projecting its parameters onto a set defined by dynamic properties (i.e., fast mixing) rather than a fixed structural family—is highly original. This presents a new and valuable perspective on approximate inference. While the constituent parts (MCMC mixing theory, SVD thresholding, stochastic gradient descent) are known, their combination to solve this problem is novel and insightful. The framing of mean-field as an extreme case of their KL projection method is particularly elegant and clarifies the relationship between this new approach and existing variational methods.

**Significance:**
The work addresses a fundamental and difficult problem: performing inference in graphical models with strong couplings. Slow MCMC mixing is a major practical barrier in many applications, and this paper offers a principled and effective method to mitigate it. The results are significant because they demonstrate that for a fixed computational budget, it can be better to run a fast sampler on an approximate model than a slow sampler on the exact one. This is a practical and powerful insight. The proposed framework is general and could inspire future work on extending this idea to other model classes, using tighter mixing time bounds, or applying it to model learning.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

The primary weakness of the work lies in the gap between the theory and the practical implementation. The theoretical guarantee of fast mixing relies on the spectral norm of the absolute weights being strictly less than 1. However, the authors state in Section 6.1 that a bound of 2.5 was used in practice as it "gives better approximation". This is a crucial point that needs more justification.
*   By using a bound greater than 1, the theoretical guarantee of fast mixing is lost. While the resulting sampler may still be "fast enough" in practice, this makes the method more of a well-motivated heuristic.
*   How was the value of 2.5 chosen? Is it tuned per model type, or is it a general heuristic? How sensitive are the results to this value?
*   It would strengthen the paper to include an ablation study showing the trade-off between the tightness of the spectral norm bound (e.g., for c = 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5) and the final marginal accuracy. This would help readers understand the practical impact of this hyperparameter.

Another point for improvement is the evaluation of computational cost. The experiments compare the accuracy of marginals after a fixed number of Gibbs sweeps, but this does not account for the computational cost of the projection itself. The KL(ψ||θ) projection, in particular, requires 60 iterations of an SGD-like procedure, with 500 samples being updated at each step. This is a non-trivial upfront cost. While the authors acknowledge this, a more complete comparison would incorporate this projection time into the total time budget. For instance, a plot of marginal error vs. *total wall-clock time* (projection + sampling) would provide a more holistic comparison against running a longer Gibbs chain on the original parameters.

Finally, the contribution of the "piecewise KL" divergence felt underdeveloped. It is introduced as a novel approximation, but the paper states it "does not perform as well as the true KL-divergence" and the experimental results do not seem to feature it prominently. If its performance is not competitive with the other proposed methods, it might be better to either remove it or provide a clearer context for when it might be useful (e.g., perhaps it is much faster to compute).

### Arguments For/Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The core concept of projecting parameters onto a dynamically-defined "fast-mixing" set is a novel and important contribution to the field of approximate inference.
2.  **Strong Technical Content:** The paper provides a solid theoretical and algorithmic basis for the proposed projection methods.
3.  **Compelling Empirical Results:** The method is shown to outperform several standard and widely used variational inference techniques, especially in the challenging strong-coupling regime.
4.  **Clarity and Honesty:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and transparent about its limitations, such as the looseness of the underlying mixing time bound.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Theory-Practice Gap:** The use of a spectral norm bound `c > 1` in experiments voids the formal guarantee of fast mixing, which is the central premise of the paper. This needs better justification.
2.  **Incomplete Cost Analysis:** The computational cost of the projection step is not factored into the experimental time-accuracy trade-off analysis.

**Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper with a novel and significant core idea, supported by solid technical work and compelling experiments. The weaknesses, while notable, are surmountable and do not undermine the overall contribution. This paper would be an excellent addition to the NIPS program. I strongly recommend acceptance.
