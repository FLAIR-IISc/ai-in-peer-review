This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in high-treewidth Ising models. The central idea is to approximate a given model, which may be "slow-mixing" due to strong interactions, by projecting its parameters onto a set of parameters that are guaranteed to be "fast-mixing". A distribution is considered fast-mixing if the spectral norm of the absolute values of its interaction parameters is less than a constant (here, `|||β|||_2 < 1`), a condition derived from prior work on MCMC mixing times (Dyer et al., 2006; Hayes, 2006). This work departs from traditional approximate inference methods like mean-field or loopy belief propagation, which typically approximate the model structure or posterior distribution, by instead modifying the model parameters themselves to ensure tractable sampling via Gibbs.

The authors develop algorithms for this projection under four different divergences:
1.  **Euclidean distance:** A direct projection of the parameter vector. The paper provides a novel dual algorithm (Theorem 7) to solve this non-trivial optimization problem, which correctly handles graph sparsity constraints and involves an iterative singular value thresholding procedure.
2.  **Inclusive KL-divergence (KL(θ||ψ)):** A theoretically motivated but computationally intractable projection, as it requires inference on the original slow-mixing model.
3.  **Piecewise KL-divergence:** A novel, tractable approximation to the inclusive KL, inspired by piecewise likelihood methods, where the KL is maximized over a set of tractable subgraphs (e.g., spanning trees).
4.  **Exclusive KL-divergence (KL(ψ||θ)):** A practical and effective approach. Since expectations are taken with respect to the fast-mixing distribution `ψ`, the gradient can be estimated via MCMC sampling. The optimization becomes a projected stochastic gradient descent, where the projection step uses the algorithm developed for the Euclidean case. This can be viewed as a powerful generalization of mean-field inference.

Experiments on grid and random graphs demonstrate that Gibbs sampling on the projected parameters, especially using the exclusive KL-divergence, yields more accurate marginals than standard Gibbs sampling (given a fixed, limited time budget) and outperforms common variational methods like LBP, TRW, and mean-field, particularly in the regime of strong interactions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core concept of projecting parameters into a "fast-mixing" space is highly original and provides a new and compelling paradigm for approximate inference. It directly tackles a fundamental challenge in MCMC: slow convergence in models with strong couplings. This approach could have a significant impact on how practitioners handle inference in such difficult models, offering a principled trade-off between asymptotic accuracy and practical computational cost.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the dual algorithm for the sparse Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is a valuable contribution in its own right. The formulation of the exclusive KL-divergence minimization as a projected stochastic gradient descent scheme is elegant and well-motivated.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides clear background on mixing time theory, logically develops the projection algorithms from the simplest Euclidean case to the more complex KL divergences, and presents the experimental setup and results comprehensibly.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The comparison against a strong set of baselines (Gibbs, MF, LBP, TRW) on standard graph structures clearly demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed method. The finding that the method excels precisely where others fail (strong interactions) is a powerful result.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Looseness of the Theoretical Bound:** The authors candidly acknowledge that the theoretical condition for fast mixing (`|||β|||_2 < 1`) is conservative. In their experiments, a much looser bound (`< 2.5`) yields better results. This raises a critical practical question: how should this bound be set for a new problem? Without a principled way to choose this hyperparameter, the "guarantee" of fast mixing is weakened, and the method requires potentially expensive empirical tuning.
*   **Computational Cost of Projection:** The review of experimental results focuses on the efficiency of sampling *after* the projection is complete. However, the projection itself, particularly for the KL(ψ||θ) case, involves a significant computational effort (e.g., 30,000 total Gibbs updates in the described setting). A more complete evaluation would compare the accuracy of the proposed method against standard Gibbs sampling on the original parameters given the *total* computational budget (projection time + sampling time). It is not entirely clear from the paper if the proposed method would still be superior under this fairer comparison.
*   **Generalizability:** The work is developed and tested exclusively for pairwise Ising models. While the authors suggest extensions to general MRFs as future work, the required technical steps—deriving dependency bounds for general potentials and developing corresponding projection algorithms—appear non-trivial and may present significant challenges.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The central idea is highly novel and introduces a new research direction for approximate inference.
*   The paper provides both strong theoretical contributions (the Euclidean projection algorithm) and compelling empirical results.
*   It addresses a critical and difficult problem in machine learning and statistics: tractable inference in strongly coupled graphical models.
*   The work is clearly presented and technically solid, making it an excellent contribution to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical utility is somewhat limited by the need to tune the spectral norm bound hyperparameter, for which no guidance is provided.
*   The experimental comparison of computational cost could be made more rigorous by including the cost of the projection phase.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and significant contribution to the field of approximate inference. The weaknesses are primarily limitations that open up clear avenues for future research, rather than fatal flaws. The strengths in originality, technical quality, and empirical validation are substantial. I strongly recommend acceptance.
