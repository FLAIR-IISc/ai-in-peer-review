- Problem: Inference in high-treewidth Ising models is intractable and Gibbs sampling mixes slowly under strong interactions.
- Proposal: Project Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" parameter set to guarantee rapid Gibbs convergence.
- Methods explored: Projections using Euclidean distance, zero-avoiding KL, piecewise KL, and zero-forcing KL divergences.
- Algorithmic/theoretical: Derives a dual algorithm for Euclidean projection and uses spectral norm constraints to guarantee rapid mixing.
- Experimental finding: Gibbs sampling on projected parameters achieves higher accuracy than on original parameters when time is limited.
- Comparative result: The method outperforms standard techniques (LBP, TRW, MF) in certain scenarios.
- Strength — novelty: Introduces tractability via fast-mixing distributions instead of low-treewidth or exact inference.
- Strength — theory: Solid theoretical grounding for spectral-norm-based mixing guarantees and projection algorithm.
- Strength — experiments: Comprehensive evaluation across graph topologies, interaction strengths, and divergences.
- Strength — clarity: Well-organized presentation with clear definitions and discussion of accuracy vs. efficiency trade-offs.
- Weakness — conservative bounds: Spectral norm bound is loose, producing overly conservative projections that limit practicality.
- Weakness — scalability: Unclear scalability to large, real-world graphs and high cost of repeated projections (e.g., in SGD).
- Weakness — limited generalization: Approach is specific to Ising models; extension to general MRFs is not developed.
- Weakness — baselines: Lacks comparisons to recent inference advances (neural variational methods, advanced MCMC).
- Arguments for acceptance: Tackles a fundamental problem with a novel, theoretically sound approach and promising empirical results.
- Arguments against acceptance: Practical utility weakened by loose bounds, scalability/generalizability concerns, and incomplete benchmarking.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address the looseness of the spectral norm bound and discuss scalability more thoroughly.
