This paper proposes a method for projecting Ising model parameters onto a set guaranteed to be fast mixing, aiming to improve the efficiency of Gibbs sampling. The core idea is sound – recognizing that slow mixing is a practical bottleneck and attempting to address it through parameter transformation. The paper connects to existing work on approximating intractable distributions by moving to tractable families (e.g., mean field, tree-reweighted belief propagation) but frames tractability in terms of mixing time rather than treewidth. The authors explore several divergence measures for the projection, including Euclidean distance, KL divergence (and approximations thereof), and a “zero-forcing” KL divergence.

**Quality:** The technical aspects are generally strong. The theorems relating to projection (Theorem 6, 7) are well-presented, and the connection to existing results on mixing times (Theorem 4) is clear. However, the practical implementation and analysis of the divergences feel somewhat incomplete. The discussion regarding the spectral norm bound being conservative (mentioned in the discussion section) suggests potential limitations to the theoretical foundation, even if the empirical results show improvement. The justification for the chosen parameters in the stochastic gradient descent for the zero-forcing KL divergence is weak – “Without intensive tuning…” isn't a compelling argument. While the authors acknowledge potential weaknesses (e.g., looseness of the spectral norm bound), a deeper analysis of these limitations would be beneficial. The connection between the proposed methods and existing mean-field approaches, especially in the context of the zero-forcing KL divergence, could also be more thoroughly explored. Is this truly a *generalization* of mean field, or merely a related approach?

**Clarity:** The paper is mostly well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the mathematical derivations are presented in a relatively accessible manner. However, the sheer number of divergences explored (Euclidean, KL, piecewise KL, zero-forcing KL) can be a bit overwhelming. A more focused exploration of 2-3 key divergences with detailed analysis might have been more effective than presenting a wider range superficially. The notation is generally consistent, but some definitions (e.g., dependency matrix R) could benefit from more intuitive explanations.

**Originality:** The idea of explicitly projecting parameters to ensure fast mixing is novel. While the use of parameter transformations to improve inference is not new, framing it specifically in terms of mixing time and exploring different divergence measures for the projection is a worthwhile contribution. The piecewise KL divergence is also a novel and interesting approximation technique. The connections to existing work (mean field, tree-reweighted BP) are appropriately acknowledged, and the authors clearly differentiate their approach.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is moderate. Improved Gibbs sampling efficiency is valuable, especially for complex models where convergence is slow. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed methods can outperform standard variational approximations and, in some cases, even Gibbs sampling with the original parameters when limited time is available. However, the gains appear modest, and the practical applicability may be limited by the computational cost of the projection step (especially for the more complex divergences). The fact that the spectral norm bound is likely conservative further limits the potential impact. The methods could be useful in scenarios where rapid approximate inference is crucial, but they are unlikely to revolutionize the field.




**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel idea of parameter projection for fast mixing.
*   Clear theoretical grounding and connection to existing literature.
*   Demonstrated improvement over standard variational methods in the experiments.
*   Exploration of multiple divergence measures provides a valuable comparative analysis.
*   Piecewise KL divergence is a potentially useful approximation technique.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The gains over standard methods are modest.
*   The computational cost of the projection step may be significant.
*   The spectral norm bound appears to be conservative.
*   The analysis of the different divergences feels somewhat incomplete.
*   Justification for experimental parameters is weak.




**Overall assessment:**

I would rate this paper as **weak accept**. The idea is solid, and the technical execution is generally good. However, the limited practical gains and some weaknesses in the analysis prevent me from recommending a strong acceptance. It represents a worthwhile contribution, but a more focused and in-depth analysis of a smaller number of divergences, along with a more thorough investigation of the limitations of the spectral norm bound, would have strengthened the paper considerably. I believe the paper is within the scope of NIPS.
