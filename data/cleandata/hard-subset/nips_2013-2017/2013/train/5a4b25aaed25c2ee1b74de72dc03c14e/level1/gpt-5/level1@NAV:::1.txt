The paper proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set—defined by spectral-norm constraints on the dependency matrix—to enable rapid convergence of univariate Gibbs. The authors develop: (i) an Euclidean projection via a dual formulation that preserves sparsity and constrains the spectral norm of |β| (extending SVD thresholding to the absolute-weights constraint), (ii) projections under several divergences: Euclidean, a tractable piecewise KL computed on low-treewidth subgraphs, inclusive KL (theoretical, requires expectations under θ), and reverse KL minimized by stochastic projected gradient using samples from the fast-mixing model. Experiments on grids and small random graphs compare against LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original parameters, showing that when interactions are strong and time is limited, sampling from the projected fast-mixing model yields more accurate marginals than sampling the original model. This work relates to classical fast-mixing criteria (Dobrushin, spectral norm; Hayes; Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum) and to variational/graph-structure approximations (MF/structured MF, TRW, piecewise and tree mixtures; Yedidia/Globerson & Jaakkola/Hazan & Shashua).

Quality: The spectral-norm guarantee and Lemma 5 are standard; the dual Euclidean projection (Theorem 7) is technically solid and practically relevant (sparsity-preserving). The stochastic scheme for reverse KL is reasonable, with an ergodic mirror-descent interpretation, though convergence claims are informal and depend on schedule choices. A notable concern is the use of a 2.5 spectral bound in experiments despite the <1 guarantee; this weakens theoretical-to-empirical alignment.

Clarity: Generally well-written. Two points need clarification: whether accuracy is computed by exact junction tree or by Gibbs estimates (the text suggests both), and whether systematic-scan updates used experimentally are covered by the stated mixing-time guarantees.

Originality: Projecting onto a fast-mixing set as an inference surrogate is a fresh perspective, distinct from tree/planar/mean-field families; the dual projection and piecewise KL are novel combinations.

Significance: The idea could impact approximate inference and MCMC-accelerated learning by providing a principled “samplable” surrogate. Empirical scope is modest (8×8 grids, n=10 random graphs), but results are promising.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel inferential lens (fast-mixing projections) with a concrete, efficient projection algorithm.
- Practical stochastic optimization for reverse KL using only samples from ψ.
- Clear empirical benefit in strong-coupling, limited-time regimes.

Cons against acceptance:
- Theory–experiment mismatch (spectral bound relaxed to 2.5 without guarantees).
- Limited experimental scale and incomplete accounting of total runtime vs baselines.
- Ambiguity in evaluation protocol and scan schedule vs theory.

Suggestions: report wall-clock including projection, sensitivity to spectral bound and optimizer hyperparameters, clarify evaluation, and expand to larger graphs.
