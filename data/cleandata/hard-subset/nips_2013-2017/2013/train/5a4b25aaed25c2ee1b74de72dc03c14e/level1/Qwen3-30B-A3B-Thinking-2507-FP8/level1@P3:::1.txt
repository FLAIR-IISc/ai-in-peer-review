

This paper proposes a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set, where Gibbs sampling converges rapidly. The core insight shifts the notion of "tractability" from low-treewidth graph structures (as in mean-field or loopy BP) to parameter-space constraints guaranteeing fast mixing. The authors derive projection methods under several divergences: Euclidean distance (using SVD thresholding), piecewise KL (computing exact KL on low-treewidth subgraphs), and reversed KL (KL(ψ||θ)) with stochastic approximation.

The paper builds on established mixing time theory (Dyer et al. 2002, Hayes 2006), particularly the dependency matrix bound for Gibbs sampling. It distinguishes itself from previous NIPS work on approximate inference (e.g., Globerson & Jaakkola 2007 on planar graphs; Saul & Jordan 2001 on tractable substructures) by focusing on parameter constraints rather than graph structure. The theoretical foundation is strong, with Theorems 4-7 correctly deriving the spectral norm condition for fast mixing and the projection algorithms.

Strengths:
1. The conceptual contribution is significant—redefining tractability as parameter constraints rather than structural approximations is novel and addresses a key limitation in inference for models with strong interactions.
2. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against LBP, TRW, naive mean-field, and Gibbs on original parameters across 8×8 grids and 10-node random graphs with varying interaction strengths. The results consistently show the proposed methods outperform variational approaches when interaction strengths are strong and sampling time is limited.
3. The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the conservative spectral bound) and provide empirical justification for using a looser bound (2.5 instead of 1) in practice, demonstrating honesty in evaluation.
4. The paper is well-written, with clear mathematical exposition and logical flow.

Weaknesses:
1. The spectral norm bound (||R||₂ < 1) is overly conservative. For 2D Ising models, the actual mixing threshold βc ≈ 0.4407, while the bound requires β < 0.25. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't explore tighter bounds (e.g., using the Dobrushin criterion ||R||₁ < 1, which is looser than ||R||₂ but easier to compute). A tighter bound would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
2. The piecewise KL approach lacks sufficient exploration of subgraph selection. The paper uses horizontal/vertical chains for grids and random spanning trees for random graphs, but doesn't compare how different subgraph choices affect performance. For instance, why not use the same subgraphs for all experiments?
3. The scalability of the method to larger graphs is unaddressed. Experiments are limited to 8×8 grids and 10-node graphs, leaving open whether the approach scales to practical problems.
4. The paper doesn't compare to other fast-mixing methods (e.g., Swendsen-Wang algorithm), which could provide a more direct benchmark for "fast mixing" approximations.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with correct derivations and well-designed experiments. The authors carefully evaluate both strengths (e.g., better accuracy under time constraints) and weaknesses (e.g., conservative bound).

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The connection between mixing time theory and the proposed method is particularly well-explained.

Originality: The core idea—projecting onto a fast-mixing parameter set—is novel compared to previous NIPS work. While mixing time bounds have been studied (e.g., Dyer et al. 2002), this is the first systematic method to project parameters onto such sets. The paper clearly differentiates itself from variational methods by focusing on parameter constraints rather than structural approximations.

Significance: The approach solves a practical problem (inference in Ising models with strong interactions where Gibbs sampling is slow), with results showing improved accuracy under time constraints compared to standard variational methods. This has potential for real-world applications where sampling time is limited, though the scope is currently restricted to Ising models.

Arguments for acceptance: Novel conceptual contribution; strong theoretical foundation; comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrating practical benefits; addresses a real limitation in existing inference methods.

Arguments against acceptance: Conservative spectral bound weakens theoretical guarantee; limited scalability; insufficient exploration of piecewise KL subgraph choices.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to approximate inference. The conceptual shift from structural to parameter constraints is insightful, the theory is sound, and the experiments convincingly demonstrate practical advantages. While the spectral bound limitation is notable, the authors acknowledge it and provide empirical justification for their approach. The method has clear potential for extension to other graphical models, though this is left for future work. I recommend acceptance as a strong contribution to NIPS.
