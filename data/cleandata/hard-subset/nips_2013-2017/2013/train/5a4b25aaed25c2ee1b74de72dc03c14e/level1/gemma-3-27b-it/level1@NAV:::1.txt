## Review of "Projecting Ising Model Parameters for Fast Mixing"

This paper addresses the challenge of slow mixing in Gibbs sampling for Ising models, particularly when interaction strengths are high. The authors propose projecting Ising model parameters onto a space guaranteeing faster mixing, leveraging the connection between spectral norm of the dependency matrix and mixing time. They explore several divergence measures for this projection – Euclidean, KL (both “zero-avoiding” and reversed), and a piecewise approximation of KL – and evaluate their performance experimentally.

The work builds on a well-established line of research in approximating intractable inference through tractable models (e.g., mean-field [14, 20], tree-reweighted BP [19]), positioning fast mixing as a distinct notion of tractability. It correctly identifies the crucial role of the dependency matrix’s spectral norm [4, 8] in determining mixing time and utilizes existing theoretical results [6, 11] to frame the problem. The proposal of utilizing different divergences for projection and the exploration of a piecewise KL approximation are novel contributions within this context.

**Strengths:**

* **Well-motivated problem:** Slow mixing is a significant bottleneck in applying MCMC methods, and finding ways to accelerate convergence is valuable.
* **Solid theoretical foundation:** The paper correctly grounds its approach in the theory of Markov chain mixing times and leverages relevant theorems.
* **Multiple divergence explorations:** Investigating different divergence measures for projection provides a nuanced comparison and highlights trade-offs. The piecewise KL divergence is a creative attempt to bridge theoretical accuracy and computational feasibility.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments demonstrate that parameter projection often improves accuracy compared to standard methods like LBP and naive mean-field, especially with limited sampling time.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational complexity:** While the Euclidean projection via SVD is efficient, the KL-based approaches, particularly the "zero-avoiding" variant, are computationally expensive. The experiments only demonstrate this on small graphs.
* **Spectral norm bound tightness:** The authors acknowledge the looseness of the spectral norm bound, citing known tighter bounds [11]. This limitation potentially undermines the effectiveness of the projection.
* **Parameter sensitivity:**  The stochastic gradient descent for the reversed KL-divergence requires careful parameter tuning, as acknowledged.  The chosen parameters might not be optimal and the sensitivity analysis is limited.
* **Clarity in discussion:** The connection between the proposed method and existing mean-field methods is somewhat unclear. The authors state the proposed method is a generalization, but this could be elaborated upon.




**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a practical problem with a sound theoretical basis.
* The exploration of different divergence measures offers valuable insights.
* Empirical results demonstrate a clear benefit of projection in certain scenarios.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The computational cost of some approaches limits scalability.
* The looseness of the spectral norm bound weakens the guarantees.
* The experimental evaluation remains limited to small graphs.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of approximate inference. While the computational limitations and the conservative spectral norm bound are concerns, the novelty of the approach and the promising empirical results suggest that this work has merit. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity issues and acknowledge the limitations regarding scalability and bound tightness. Addressing the parameter sensitivity of the reversed KL divergence could also strengthen the paper.
