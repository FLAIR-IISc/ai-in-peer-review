- Proposes projecting Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set so Gibbs sampling converges rapidly, improving marginal estimates under limited sampling time.
- Defines a sufficient fast-mixing condition: spectral norm of the interaction matrix ||R||₂ < 1, and develops projection algorithms under four divergences (Euclidean, zero-avoiding KL, piecewise KL, zero-forcing KL).
- Key insight: strong interactions slow Gibbs mixing on original parameters, but projected parameters yield more accurate marginals within practical sampling budgets.
- Builds on prior mixing-time and approximate inference literature but shifts focus to parameter-space tractability rather than graph structure or low treewidth.
- Shows mean-field as a degenerate case (spectral norm constrained to zero), positioning the approach as a generalization of existing variational methods.
- Provides solid theoretical foundations with clear proofs for the projection algorithms (Theorems 7 and 8).
- Experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on original parameters across topologies and interaction strengths.
- Piecewise KL divergence is a novel, well-motivated contribution linked to tractable subgraph and tree-based approximations.
- Authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., conservativeness of the spectral norm bound) and discuss extensions to general MRFs.
- Major weakness: the spectral norm bound is conservative (example: 2D Ising threshold discrepancy) and the paper lacks theoretical justification for the practical relaxation used.
- Experimental gaps: computational trade-offs (projection time vs. Gibbs time) are not fully explored, and implementation/hyperparameter choices lack thorough justification.
- The relationship between projection quality (choice of divergence) and inference accuracy is not analyzed in depth.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, clear, original, and significant with experimental support demonstrating benefits in strong-interaction regimes.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, strong theory, convincing empirical gains, practical relevance, and good presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: conservative theoretical bound inadequately addressed, missing practical analysis of computational trade-offs and projection-accuracy linkage.
