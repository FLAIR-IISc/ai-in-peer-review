- Proposes a novel approximation paradigm: approximate slow-mixing Ising models with fast-mixing ones defined by a spectral-norm condition (|| |β| ||_2 < 1).
- Fast-mixing condition justified by prior mixing-time results (Dyer et al., Hayes) guaranteeing rapid Gibbs convergence.
- Core idea: project original parameters θ onto the set Ψ of fast-mixing parameters under different divergence measures.
- Four projection criteria studied: Euclidean (L2), zero-avoiding KL (KL(θ||ψ)), piecewise KL, and zero-forcing KL (KL(ψ||θ)).
- Euclidean projection: main technical contribution with a dual algorithm (Theorem 7) using iterative singular-value thresholding.
- KL(θ||ψ) is probabilistically natural but intractable because it needs expectations under the slow-mixing original distribution.
- Piecewise KL: a novel, tractable approximation computed over low-treewidth subgraphs.
- KL(ψ||θ) is computationally feasible; gradients estimated via Gibbs sampling on ψ, enabling projected stochastic gradient descent with Euclidean projection steps.
- Experiments show Gibbs on projected parameters yields more accurate marginals than LBP, TRW, and Mean-Field, especially at high interaction strengths.
- For fixed time budgets, Gibbs on the projected (fast-mixing) model outperforms Gibbs on the original slow-mixing model.
- Paper is technically strong; dual Euclidean projection derivation and projected SGD formulation are solid contributions.
- Authors are transparent about the looseness of the spectral-norm bound and explicitly discuss using a looser empirical bound (2.5) in experiments.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and organized with clear motivation, background, methods, and reproducible experimental details.
- Central idea is highly original, reframing tractability in terms of MCMC dynamics rather than graphical structure.
- Piecewise KL is a creative new approximation technique within this framework.
- Results are significant and likely influential, suggesting follow-ups: tighter fast-mixing conditions, extensions beyond Ising models, and applications in learning.
- Pros: novelty, technical soundness, strong empirical results, practical relevance, and clear, honest presentation.
- Cons: gap between theory and practice due to using a heuristic spectral bound (2.5) and inherent looseness of the fast-mixing condition.
- Overall assessment: strengths substantially outweigh weaknesses; paper is a clear accept.
