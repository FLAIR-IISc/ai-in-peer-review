This paper proposes a novel approach to accelerating Gibbs sampling in Ising models by projecting model parameters onto a “fast-mixing” set. The core idea – to trade off model accuracy for faster convergence – is a compelling one, especially given the challenges of mixing in high-treewidth models. The authors explore several divergence measures for this projection, including Euclidean distance, piecewise KL, and reversed KL divergences, and present experimental results demonstrating the benefits of their approach compared to standard inference techniques.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly presenting the problem of slow mixing in Ising models and connecting it to existing work on approximate inference (mean-field, loopy belief propagation, tree-reweighted belief propagation). The theoretical background on mixing times and dependency matrices is concisely and accurately presented, drawing on relevant literature (Dyer et al., Hayes, Levin & Peres). The introduction of “fast-mixing” as a tractable model is a valuable contribution, offering a different perspective from traditional approaches focused on tree-structured or low-treewidth approximations.

The technical development of the projection algorithms, particularly Theorem 7 for Euclidean projection, is solid. The exploration of different divergences and the discussion of their trade-offs (accuracy vs. computational convenience) are insightful. The connection to Ergodic Mirror Descent when using stochastic gradient ascent for the reversed KL divergence is a nice touch, demonstrating an awareness of optimization techniques.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive in comparing against several baselines, feels somewhat limited in scope. The configurations used (8x8 grids and random graphs) are fairly standard, and the parameter tuning seems heuristic ("Without intensive tuning…"). More systematic exploration of the parameter space and different graph structures would strengthen the conclusions. The claim that using a spectral norm bound of 2.5 "can still preserve the rapid-mixing property" feels empirically justified but lacks strong theoretical backing, especially given the known critical threshold of ~0.44 for 2D Ising models. This looseness in the bound undermines the theoretical elegance of the approach.

The piecewise KL divergence, while conceptually interesting, doesn’t seem to perform as well as the other methods in the experiments. The explanation for this is weak – stating it “does not perform as well” without a deeper analysis of why is unsatisfactory.  More investigation into the choice of subgraphs T, and potentially exploring different ways of approximating the KL divergence, could be fruitful.

Clarity is generally good, but the notation could be improved. The frequent use of θ and ψ for parameters becomes confusing, and a more descriptive naming convention would be helpful.  The connection between the theoretical results (Theorems 6 & 7) and the practical implementation isn’t always immediately obvious. More concrete examples illustrating how these theorems are applied in the code would enhance understanding.

Originality is moderate. The idea of projecting parameters to improve mixing is novel, but it builds upon established concepts in approximate inference and optimization. The specific divergences explored and the algorithmic developments are incremental improvements rather than groundbreaking breakthroughs. The significance of the work is also moderate. While the proposed method shows promise for accelerating Gibbs sampling, its practical impact depends on the extent to which the gains outweigh the computational cost of the projection step and the loss of accuracy.  The paper demonstrates a tangible improvement in some cases, but a broader range of applications and a more rigorous analysis of computational complexity would be needed to establish its widespread utility.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel idea of projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set.
*   Solid theoretical foundation and clear presentation of the background.
*   Demonstrated improvement over standard inference techniques in certain configurations.
*   Exploration of multiple divergence measures.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
*   Weak theoretical justification for the relaxed spectral norm bound.
*   Suboptimal performance of the piecewise KL divergence.
*   Modest originality and significance.




**Overall assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. The idea is interesting and the technical development is sound, but the limited experimental validation and the lack of a strong theoretical justification for some of the choices raise concerns. The paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field, but it falls short of being a truly impactful work. Addressing the weaknesses identified above – particularly conducting more extensive experiments and providing a tighter theoretical analysis – would significantly strengthen the paper.
