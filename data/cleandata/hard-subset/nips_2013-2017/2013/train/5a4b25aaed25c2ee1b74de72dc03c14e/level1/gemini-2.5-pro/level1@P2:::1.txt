### Summary

This paper introduces a novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models. Instead of approximating an intractable high-treewidth model with a tractable low-treewidth one (as in mean-field or tree-reweighted belief propagation), the authors propose approximating a "slow-mixing" model with a "fast-mixing" one. A model is defined as fast-mixing if it satisfies a parameter-space condition—specifically, that the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths is less than one (`|| |β| ||_2 < 1`). This condition, based on prior work by Dyer et al. and Hayes, guarantees that Gibbs sampling converges rapidly.

The core of the paper is the idea of "projecting" an arbitrary set of Ising parameters `θ` onto the set of fast-mixing parameters `Ψ`. The authors explore four different notions of closeness, or divergence, for this projection:
1.  **Euclidean Distance:** The authors derive a dual algorithm (Theorem 7) to find the closest fast-mixing parameters in the L2 norm. This method involves iteratively thresholding singular values and is the main technical tool used for the other divergences.
2.  **"Zero-avoiding" KL-divergence (KL(θ||ψ)):** This is the probabilistically natural choice, but it is intractable to compute as it requires expectations with respect to the original, slow-mixing distribution `p(x; θ)`.
3.  **Piecewise KL-divergence:** A novel, tractable approximation to the KL divergence, computed over a collection of low-treewidth subgraphs of the original model.
4.  **"Zero-forcing" KL-divergence (KL(ψ||θ)):** This divergence is computationally feasible. Its gradient requires expectations with respect to the approximating distribution `p(x; ψ)`, which can be estimated via Gibbs sampling since `ψ` is constrained to be fast-mixing. The overall optimization is performed using projected stochastic gradient descent, where the projection step uses the Euclidean algorithm from (1).

Experimentally, the authors show that Gibbs sampling on the projected parameters yields more accurate marginals than standard variational methods like LBP, TRW, and Mean-Field, especially at high interaction strengths. Furthermore, when given a limited time budget, Gibbs sampling on the projected model is more accurate than running Gibbs on the original, slow-mixing model.

### Qualitative Evaluation

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation of the dual method for Euclidean projection is a solid and non-trivial contribution. The formulation of the reversed KL-divergence minimization as a projected stochastic gradient descent problem is sound and well-motivated. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against strong and relevant baselines across different graph structures and parameter regimes. The key claim—that for a fixed budget, Gibbs on the projected model is better than Gibbs on the original—is well-supported by the time-accuracy plots.

A significant strength of the paper is its intellectual honesty. The authors explicitly discuss the looseness of the `||R||_2 < 1` bound, citing the known critical threshold for the 2D Ising model as evidence. They are also transparent about using a looser, empirically chosen bound of 2.5 in their experiments to achieve better practical results. While this creates a gap between the theory and the implementation, acknowledging it is commendable and points to clear avenues for future work. The work is a complete and self-contained contribution.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It provides a clear motivation for defining a new type of "tractable" family for approximation. The necessary background on mixing times and dependency matrices is presented concisely. The four different projection methods are introduced in a logical progression, clearly outlining the trade-offs between probabilistic desirability and computational tractability. The experimental setup and results are described clearly, making them easy to interpret. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to understand the methods and potentially reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The central idea of this paper is highly original. The dominant paradigm in approximate inference is to leverage tractable graphical structures (trees, low-treewidth graphs, or fully factorized distributions). This work reframes "tractability" in terms of the dynamics of MCMC sampling. This is a fundamentally new and insightful perspective that opens up a new family of approximations. While the components build on existing work (mixing time bounds from Dyer et al., SVD thresholding, stochastic gradient descent), their combination to create this projection framework is novel. The piecewise KL-divergence is also a creative and new proposal. The work is well-situated within the literature, clearly distinguishing itself from prior work in variational inference.

**Significance:** The results are significant and likely to be influential. The paper presents a new class of algorithms that demonstrably outperforms standard variational methods in the difficult regime of strong interactions. The idea of trading asymptotic accuracy (which may be unattainable in practice) for guaranteed rapid convergence is of great practical importance. Researchers are likely to build on this work by exploring:
1.  Tighter or different fast-mixing conditions.
2.  Extensions to more general graphical models beyond the Ising model.
3.  Applications in learning, where MCMC-based gradient estimates are common and could benefit from being constrained to the fast-mixing set.

This work provides both a new theoretical viewpoint and a practical algorithmic tool for the machine learning and graphical models communities.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Pro:**
*   **Novelty:** Proposes a genuinely new paradigm for approximate inference based on fast-mixing dynamics rather than graphical structure.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The Euclidean projection algorithm is a solid technical contribution, and its use within a projected gradient framework is elegant.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The method outperforms widely used baselines like LBP, TRW, and Mean-Field, particularly in challenging, high-interaction settings.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The time-accuracy trade-off is a crucial practical problem, and this paper offers a principled solution.
*   **Clarity and Honesty:** The paper is very well-written and is transparent about the limitations of the theoretical bounds used.

**Con:**
*   **Gap between Theory and Practice:** The theoretical guarantee requires a spectral norm bound of 1.0, but a heuristic value of 2.5 is used in experiments for better performance. This weakens the connection between the practical algorithm and its theoretical justification.
*   **Looseness of the Bound:** As the authors note, the underlying fast-mixing condition itself can be quite conservative, which may limit the quality of the best possible approximation within this family.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. The originality of the core idea and the strong empirical results make this a standout paper. The identified weaknesses are more a reflection of the difficulty of the problem and serve as excellent starting points for future research. This paper is a clear accept.
