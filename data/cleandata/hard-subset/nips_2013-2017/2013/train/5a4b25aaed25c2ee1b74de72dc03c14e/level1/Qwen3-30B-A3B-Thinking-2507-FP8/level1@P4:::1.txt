

This paper proposes a novel approach to inference in Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set where Gibbs sampling converges rapidly to the stationary distribution. The authors define a sufficient condition for fast mixing (spectral norm of the interaction matrix < 1) and develop projection algorithms under four different divergences: Euclidean distance, "zero-avoiding" KL divergence, piecewise KL divergence, and "zero-forcing" KL divergence. The key insight is that for strong interactions, Gibbs sampling on original parameters may be slow to converge, while using projected parameters can provide more accurate marginals within limited sampling time.

The paper builds on established work on mixing times (Dyer et al. [4], Hayes [8]) and existing approximate inference methods (mean-field, loopy belief propagation). It differs by focusing on parameter-space conditions that guarantee fast mixing rather than restricting model structure to low-treewidth graphs. The authors relate their "fast-mixing" set to mean-field inference (a degenerate case where the spectral norm is constrained to zero), showing how their approach generalizes existing methods.

Strengths:
- The paper provides a technically sound theoretical foundation with clear proofs of projection algorithms (Theorem 7, Theorem 8).
- The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple standard inference methods (LBP, TRW, MF, Gibbs on original parameters) across different graph topologies and interaction strengths.
- The piecewise KL divergence approach is particularly novel and well-motivated by connections to tractable subgraphs and tree-based approximations.
- The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the spectral norm bound being conservative) and discuss future work extending to general MRFs.
- The paper is well-organized with clear explanations of mathematical concepts and consistent notation.

Weaknesses:
- The spectral norm bound for fast mixing (||R||₂ < 1) is conservative compared to known results for specific models (e.g., 2D Ising model threshold βc ≈ 0.4407 vs. spectral bound β = 0.25). While the authors use a looser bound (2.5) in practice, they don't provide theoretical justification for this relaxation.
- The experimental comparison doesn't fully explore computational trade-offs (e.g., how projection time compares to Gibbs sampling time).
- The paper could better emphasize how the projection quality (divergence measure) directly affects inference accuracy.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Introduces a novel and well-motivated approach to inference in Ising models.
- Theoretical contributions are sound and well-supported with clear proofs.
- Experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over standard variational methods in strong-interaction scenarios with limited sampling time.
- Addresses an important practical problem in graphical models with clear benefits for practitioners.
- Well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to the state of the art.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The conservative spectral bound is a significant limitation that isn't adequately addressed.
- Practical implementation details (e.g., parameter choices for stochastic gradient descent) lack thorough justification.
- The relationship between projection quality and inference accuracy could be more explicitly analyzed.

The paper meets all four criteria for quality:
- Quality: Technically sound with strong theoretical foundation and thorough experimental validation.
- Clarity: Well-structured with clear explanations of mathematical concepts and consistent notation.
- Originality: Introduces a new perspective on "tractability" and develops novel projection methods under different divergences.
- Significance: Addresses an important practical challenge in inference for high-treewidth models with clear benefits for real-world applications.

While the conservative spectral norm bound is a limitation, the authors acknowledge this and the method still provides valuable practical benefits. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to approximate inference in graphical models, advancing the state of the art by offering a new approach to handling strong interactions where standard methods fail. The experimental results robustly support the theoretical claims, showing consistent improvements over existing methods in relevant scenarios.
