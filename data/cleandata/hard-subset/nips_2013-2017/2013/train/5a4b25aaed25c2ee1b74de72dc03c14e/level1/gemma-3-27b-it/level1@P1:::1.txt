This paper proposes a method for projecting Ising model parameters onto a set guaranteed to be fast-mixing, aiming to improve Gibbs sampling accuracy, particularly with limited sampling time. The core idea is compelling: instead of directly sampling from potentially slow-mixing distributions, transform the parameters to ensure faster convergence, even if it introduces some approximation. The paper builds upon existing work in approximating intractable distributions via tractable families (mean field, tree-reweighted belief propagation) but frames tractability as fast mixing, a potentially fruitful perspective.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach. The theorems regarding projection, particularly Theorem 7, are well-defined and address the practical issues of maintaining graph structure. The discussion of the dependency matrix and its relation to mixing time is accurate and clearly links to established results (Dyer et al., Hayes). The experimental section demonstrates improvements over standard variational methods and highlights the trade-off between accuracy and sampling time. However, the discussion of the looseness of the spectral norm bound (Section 7) reveals a limitation—the theoretical guarantee isn’t as tight as known bounds for the 2D Ising model. While the authors acknowledge this, a deeper investigation into tightening the bound or exploring alternative fast-mixing criteria would strengthen the work. The experimental setup is well described and reasonably comprehensive, comparing against established baselines. The use of LBFGS-B for optimization is appropriate.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the progression from background material to the proposed method is logical. The mathematical notation is standard and consistent. However, some sections, particularly those detailing the different divergence measures (Section 5), could benefit from more intuitive explanations. For example, the probabilistic interpretation of the zero-forcing KL-divergence is briefly mentioned but could be expanded upon. The connection between the piecewise KL-divergence and existing methods like piecewise likelihood could also be elaborated. While the experiments are described well, the parameters used for stochastic gradient descent (step size, pool size, iterations) feel somewhat arbitrary and could be justified further.

**Originality:** The idea of projecting parameters onto a fast-mixing set, while building on the notion of approximating intractable distributions, presents a novel approach.  The application of different divergences to achieve this projection, particularly the piecewise KL-divergence, and the accompanying algorithms are original contributions. The paper effectively differentiates itself from prior work by focusing on mixing time guarantees rather than traditional approximation accuracy metrics like KL divergence minimization. It also offers a fresh perspective on mean-field inference, viewing it as a specific case within a broader framework. The authors adequately reference related work, though a more thorough comparison to recent advances in spectral methods for graphical models (e.g., those exploiting Lovasz local lemma) might be valuable.

**Significance:** The work addresses a practically relevant problem: accelerating MCMC sampling in Ising models, which are widely used in various fields like statistical physics, computer vision, and machine learning. While the performance gains demonstrated in the experiments are not overwhelming, they are consistent and suggest a potential benefit, especially in scenarios where sampling time is limited. The broader theoretical contribution—framing tractability in terms of fast mixing—could influence future research directions. The projection techniques developed here might be adaptable to other graphical models beyond the Ising model, although this would require extending the fast-mixing criteria. The potential for integrating this approach with learning algorithms (as briefly mentioned) also suggests a significant avenue for future work.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel framing of tractability as fast mixing.
*   Technically sound projection algorithms with theoretical guarantees.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements over standard variational methods.
*   Clear presentation and organization of the material.
*   Potential for broader applicability to other graphical models and learning algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The spectral norm bound for fast mixing appears to be conservative, as noted by the authors.
*   Experimental gains are not substantial.
*   Some sections (divergence measures) could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
*   Limited discussion of computational complexity of the proposed methods compared to alternatives.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While not groundbreaking, it presents a well-motivated, technically sound, and reasonably well-evaluated approach to accelerating MCMC sampling in Ising models. The novelty lies in the shift in perspective—focusing on fast mixing as a criterion for tractability—and the associated projection techniques. The weaknesses are minor and could be addressed in a revised version. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the clarity of the divergence measure explanations and providing a more thorough discussion of the spectral norm bound’s tightness.
