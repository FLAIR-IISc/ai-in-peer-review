Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes using “fast-mixing” parameter sets as a tractable family for approximate inference in Ising models. Rather than reducing graph complexity (e.g., mean-field, structured mean-field, LBP/TRW), the authors enforce parameter-space conditions that guarantee rapid mixing for single-site Gibbs sampling. Building on sufficient mixing time bounds that depend on the dependency matrix R, they use the fact that, for Ising models, Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij| and that Gibbs mixes rapidly when ||R||2 < 1 (Dyer-Goldberg-Jerrum; Hayes). The core algorithmic contribution is to project a given parameter vector θ to a nearby ψ within the fast-mixing set under different divergences: (i) Euclidean distance between parameters with an SVD-based projection adapted to preserve sparsity and constrain ||R||2; (ii) KL(θ||ψ), theoretically appealing but impractical at scale due to expectations under θ; (iii) a novel “piecewise” KL based on exact KL on low-treewidth subgraphs; and (iv) KL(ψ||θ), optimized via stochastic approximation using samples from ψ, which is constrained to be fast-mixing. The KL(ψ||θ) approach can be seen as generalizing mean-field by enlarging the tractable family from fully factorized to fast-mixing models.

Empirically, the authors evaluate on 8×8 grids and small random graphs, comparing Euclidean projection, piecewise KL, and KL(ψ||θ) against LBP, TRW, mean-field, and Gibbs on the original parameters. They assess marginal accuracy against exact marginals and also provide time–accuracy comparisons of Gibbs with original vs projected parameters. They observe that projection to fast-mixing frequently improves finite-time sampling accuracy in regimes with strong interactions.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The use of the spectral norm bound on the dependency matrix is standard and correct, with Lemma 5 (Rij ≤ tanh|βij|) matching known results. The Euclidean projection formulation is carefully designed to preserve sparsity via a mask Z and to constrain the spectral norm of |B| rather than B. The reduction to optimizing over D=|B| is appropriate: since the objective is ||A−B||F and constraints depend only on |B|, the optimal B for fixed D matches the sign of A, which justifies moving to R=|A|. The dual-based algorithm (Theorem 7) that repeatedly applies operator-norm projection Πc(·) is plausible; however, the paper relies on an appendix for the derivation, and the notation around Λ·Z and the Hadamard structure could be made clearer. The stochastic optimization for KL(ψ||θ) uses a pooled MCMC approach and references Ergodic Mirror Descent to justify convergence with appropriate schedules; the implementation uses fixed steps without guarantees, but this is a common practical compromise.
- Empirical methodology: Comparisons are standard and include strong baselines. Accuracy is measured against exact marginals, which is appropriate. However, the time–accuracy comparison does not include the cost of the projection procedure, which can be substantial, potentially biasing conclusions about wall-clock performance.
- A significant concern is that many experiments use a spectral norm bound of 2.5, exceeding the theoretical guarantee of <1. This undermines the “guaranteed fast mixing” claim and should either be removed or supplemented with diagnostics and theory to justify when larger bounds still mix rapidly.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clearly written, with a good review of mixing-time background and positioning relative to standard variational methods. Notational points that could be clarified:
- Define explicitly that “Λ·Z” denotes elementwise products where they appear (e.g., Λ ∘ Z).
- The triple-dot product is defined but sparsely used; consistent notation would help.
- There are minor typos (e.g., “divergnce”). The reference to Section 3.2 seems off by numbering.
- State explicitly that the optimal B shares the sign of A under the |B| constraint, which motivates working with R=|A|.

Originality:
The central idea—treating the fast-mixing set as a tractable family and projecting onto it—is novel and distinct from classical structure-based approximations. The Euclidean projection under operator-norm constraints while preserving sparsity is a technical contribution. The use of KL(ψ||θ) with a projection step is a principled extension of mean-field. The piecewise KL is a natural adaptation of piecewise training/mixtures-of-trees ideas to divergences. To my knowledge (circa NIPS 2013), this combination of mixing-time guarantees and projection-based approximation is new.

Significance:
The work opens a promising direction: enlarging the space of tractable approximations beyond trees/factors to parameter regimes with provable rapid mixing, potentially improving MCMC-based inference under tight time budgets. However, its practical significance depends on (1) how tight the mixing condition is (the current ||R||2<1 bound can be conservative, as the authors acknowledge), and (2) the computational overhead of projection versus alternatives. The empirical results on small graphs indicate clear gains over MF/LBP/TRW in difficult regimes, and improved finite-time sampling versus original Gibbs, but stronger evidence on larger problems and fair wall-clock comparisons would increase impact.

Pros:
- Conceptually novel use of fast-mixing parameter sets as a tractable family.
- Sound connection to dependency matrix bounds; clean Euclidean projection algorithm that preserves sparsity.
- Stochastic optimization for KL(ψ||θ) is practical and ties to mean-field as a special case.
- Empirical gains over standard variational baselines in strong-interaction regimes; sensible evaluation against exact marginals.

Cons:
- Main theoretical guarantee is not respected in many experiments (using spectral norm bound 2.5 > 1), weakening the central claim of guaranteed rapid mixing.
- Limited empirical scale; 8×8 grids and n=10 random graphs restrict generality.
- Time–accuracy comparisons omit projection cost; no ablation on projection overhead.
- Piecewise KL underperforms and its design space (choice of subgraphs) is not systematically explored.
- Some notational/clarity issues in the dual derivation and constraints.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report results strictly under the proven guarantee (||R||2<1), including mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, effective sample sizes) to validate the claim when using looser bounds.
- Include wall-clock time comparisons that account for projection/optimization cost.
- Explore tighter sufficient conditions (e.g., L1/Dobrushin bounds as alternatives, block dynamics conditions) and adaptive block updates that may enlarge the guaranteed region.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for KL(ψ||θ) (step sizes, sample pool) and ablations on piecewise subgraph choices.
- Clarify the dual derivation, notation, and the sign argument linking B to A.

Overall recommendation:
Promising and original direction with solid technical underpinnings for the Euclidean projection and a practical KL(ψ||θ) procedure, but the reliance on violating the guarantee in experiments and limited empirical scope temper the strength of the contribution. With revisions addressing the guarantee, fairness in time comparisons, and expanded experiments, this would be a strong submission.
