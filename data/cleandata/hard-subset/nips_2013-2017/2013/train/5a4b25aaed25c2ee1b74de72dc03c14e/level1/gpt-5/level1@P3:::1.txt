Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a new notion of “tractability” for approximate inference: instead of projecting onto distributions with low treewidth (mean-field, structured mean-field, TRW, planar), project an intractable Ising model’s parameters onto a parameter set that guarantees rapid mixing of single-site Gibbs. The fast-mixing set is characterized via a sufficient condition that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix R is less than one (building on Dobrushin-type criteria and spectral norm results; e.g., Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum; Hayes). For Ising models, the entries of R are upper bounded by tanh|βij|, allowing a sufficient constraint in terms of the spectral norm of |β|.

The main algorithmic contributions are: (i) a Euclidean projection of parameters onto this fast-mixing set via a dual formulation that preserves sparsity pattern (circumventing the dense fill-in of naive spectral clipping via SVD), solved with LBFGS-B, and (ii) projected optimization under other divergences between the target θ and approximation ψ, including KL(θ||ψ) (inclusive; “zero-avoiding,” impractical for large graphs), a piecewise KL computed on tractable subgraphs (trees/low-treewidth), and KL(ψ||θ) (“zero-forcing”), optimized by stochastic approximation with a pool of Gibbs samples from ψ. The KL(ψ||θ) gradient reduces to Covψ[f]·(ψ−θ), estimated via samples, and each gradient step is followed by Euclidean projection back to the fast-mixing set. Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs compare against LBP, TRW, MF, and Gibbs on the original parameters. The empirical claim is that when interactions are strong and one has limited sampling time, sampling ψ (fast-mixing) yields better marginals than sampling θ (slow-mixing).

Quality:
- Theory: The use of spectral-norm conditions for rapid mixing is sound and appropriately referenced. The bound Rij ≤ tanh|βij| is standard. Theorem 4 (mixing time bound via ||R||2) is correctly cited. The stochastic gradient for KL(ψ||θ) is consistent with the well-known form Covψ[f]·(ψ−θ).
- Projection algorithm: The dual-based Euclidean projection that enforces sparsity while bounding |||B|||2 is interesting. However, the paper does not discuss convexity: the feasible set {B: || |B| ||2 ≤ c, sparsity constraints} is not convex in B due to the entrywise absolute value, so uniqueness and global optimality of the “projection” are unclear. The derivation and duality gap properties should be stated more explicitly, and conditions for correctness given.
- Clarity/typos: There are a few issues that hinder verification. In Theorem 6, projection onto the spectral norm ball in Frobenius metric typically clamps singular values at c, not c^2 (the text appears to have “c 2”); please correct. In Theorem 7, the sign and constants in g(Λ,M) look suspicious (the objective seems to be missing the conventional negative 1/2||D−R||^2F term one expects in a dual), and notation overload (A as log-partition earlier versus A as a matrix in Section 4) makes it hard to follow. The role of Z (zeroing non-edges) and whether the projection (via Πc on R+M−Λ⊙Z) indeed respects sparsity in B (not only in D=|B|) needs clarification.
- Empirics: The setup is standard and targets regimes where Gibbs is slow. However, two choices weaken the strength of the conclusions: (1) in practice you use a spectral norm threshold of 2.5, not ≤1 as required by the theorem, which eliminates the theoretical mixing guarantee that motivates the projection; (2) there is no direct measurement of mixing time or coupling diagnostics to confirm “fast mixing” after projection. Also, computation budgets are not normalized: the overhead of the projection (including many Gibbs steps within the KL(ψ||θ) optimization) is excluded from the final sampling budget. Finally, block Gibbs, tree sampling, or Swendsen–Wang (especially for attractive models) are natural baselines for mixing that are absent.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written, but Section 4 requires careful revision for notation and correctness (see above). A brief complexity discussion of the projection (per-iteration cost dominated by SVD and how it scales with graph size; whether a power method suffices since only the top singular value is constrained) would help. The experimental section should clearly separate “final inference time” versus “projection optimization time,” or report end-to-end accuracy under matched wall-clock budgets.

Originality:
The idea of projecting onto a fast-mixing parameter set is novel and distinct from classical variational families (mean-field, TRW, planar). The dual construction to enforce spectral-norm constraints while preserving sparsity is an interesting technical contribution. Using KL(ψ||θ) with stochastic projection to a fast-mixing family generalizes mean-field (the limiting case ||R||2=0) in a principled way.

Significance:
If substantiated, the approach could provide a practical tool: in regimes of strong couplings where exact/variational methods perform poorly and Gibbs is slow, projecting to a guaranteed-fast-mixing surrogate may enable reliable sampling-based marginals under tight time budgets. However, the mismatch between the theoretical guarantee (||R||2<1) and the practical setting (using 2.5) and the lack of actual mixing diagnostics reduce the immediate impact. Expanding baselines to fast-mixing MCMC variants and normalizing compute would better establish practical significance.

Pros:
- Clear conceptual shift: tractability via fast mixing rather than low treewidth.
- Technically interesting dual projection with sparsity constraints.
- Sound gradient structure for KL(ψ||θ) and a reasonable stochastic optimization scheme.
- Empirical evidence that projected fast-mixing models can outperform naive Gibbs under time constraints.
- Connects and generalizes mean-field as a limiting case.

Cons:
- Feasible set likely nonconvex; no guarantees the projection solves the stated optimization globally.
- Typos/notation issues in Section 4 (Theorems 6–7) and missing details on dual derivation.
- Theory–practice gap: using thresholds >1 voids mixing guarantees; no mixing-time measurements.
- Limited baselines (no block Gibbs/SW/tree sampling); compute budgets not normalized.
- Piecewise KL objective defined as max over subgraphs may be brittle; no comparison to weighted/average variants.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Fix Theorem 6 and Theorem 7 statements, provide a self-contained, precise derivation, and discuss convexity/optimality of the projection problem. If nonconvex, describe what is guaranteed by your algorithm (e.g., KKT stationary points) and provide empirical sensitivity to initialization.
- Either adhere to the theoretical threshold (≤1) and report mixing-time diagnostics, or explicitly frame the relaxed threshold as a heuristic and quantitatively assess mixing (e.g., coupling bounds, auto-correlation times, Gelman–Rubin-like diagnostics).
- Normalize computational budgets: report accuracy versus wall-clock for all methods, including projection time; provide convergence curves for the stochastic projected optimization.
- Add strong MCMC baselines (block Gibbs/tree sampling/Swendsen–Wang for attractive models; parallel tempering) and report their mixing/accuracy under equal time.
- For piecewise KL, consider weighted averages over subgraphs (TRW-like weights) and compare to your “max” choice.
- Discuss scalability: cost per iteration, memory, and performance on larger grids/graphs.

Overall, this is a creative and potentially impactful direction. With the theoretical/notation fixes and stronger empirical validation (especially on mixing and compute), it would be a solid contribution. Pending those revisions, I lean toward weak accept.
