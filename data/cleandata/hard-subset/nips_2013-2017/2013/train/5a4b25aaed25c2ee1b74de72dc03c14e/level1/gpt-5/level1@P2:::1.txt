Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes treating “fast mixing” models as a tractable family for approximate inference: given an Ising model with potentially slow-mixing parameters, project those parameters onto a subset that guarantees rapid mixing of univariate Gibbs sampling. The fast-mixing subset is characterized via a sufficient condition on the dependency matrix R: for Ising models, Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|, and fast mixing is guaranteed when ||R||2 < 1, yielding a mixing-time bound (Hayes; Dyer–Goldberg–Jerrum). The paper develops:
- A Euclidean projection algorithm onto the fast-mixing set that preserves sparsity by dualizing a spectral-norm-constrained problem and solving it with LBFGS-B. This addresses the fact that naive spectral-norm projection on the interaction matrix densifies the graph.
- Projections under several divergences: Euclidean, “inclusive” KL(θ||ψ), a piecewise KL built from tractable subgraphs, and “exclusive” KL(ψ||θ). For KL(ψ||θ), they use stochastic approximation with samples from ψ, which is fast-mixing by construction, connecting to Ergodic Mirror Descent. They show the gradient form and propose a practical estimator.
- Experiments on 8×8 grids and small random graphs comparing LBP, TRW, mean-field, Gibbs on original parameters, and Gibbs on projected parameters.

This idea contrasts with classic tractable families (trees, planar, structured MF) used in NIPS and elsewhere (Yedidia et al., Wainwright & Jaakkola & Willsky, Globerson & Jaakkola, Saul & Jordan, Sutton & McCallum). It builds on mixing-time criteria (Dobrushin, spectral norm) and explicitly links projection to guaranteed MCMC tractability.

Quality
- Theory: The framing of sufficient conditions via ||R||2 < 1 and the Ising-specific bound Rij ≤ |βij| are standard and correct. The idea to project |B| (absolute interactions) under a spectral constraint while respecting sparsity is well-motivated. However:
  - Theorem 6 appears to have a typographical error: projecting onto a spectral-norm ball constrains singular values to ≤ c, not c^2; S′ii should be min(Sii, c).
  - The dual method (Theorem 7) is interesting, but the paper does not state convexity/duality-gap properties. Clarify whether the primal is convex in (B,D) with linear constraints and whether strong duality holds; otherwise, LBFGS-B may only find a stationary point of a nonconvex dual.
  - For KL(ψ||θ), the gradient expression and the plug-in estimator are reasonable; still, using the same set of MCMC samples both to estimate μ and to form the product introduces bias, and chains are correlated. The paper cites Ergodic Mirror Descent but does not provide convergence diagnostics or budgeted mixing per iteration.
- Claims vs guarantees: Empirically the authors relax the spectral threshold from 1.0 to 2.5, claiming rapid mixing “in practice.” This undermines the central “guarantee” and should be clearly separated: results with c ≤ 1 provide guarantees; results with c > 1 are heuristic. No empirical mixing diagnostics (autocorrelation, spectral gap surrogates) are reported.
- Experiments: The setup is appropriate for stress-testing inference under strong couplings. However:
  - Scale is modest (8×8 grids, n=10 random graphs).
  - There is a methodological ambiguity: early in Sec. 6 it says ground-truth marginals are computed by junction tree, but later it says they are estimated from Gibbs samples. Please clarify: use exact marginals for ground truth and use sampling only to approximate the projected model’s marginals.
  - Compute budgets are not equalized; the projection methods consume substantial extra computation. A fair wall-clock comparison (projection + sampling vs. simply running original Gibbs longer) and time–accuracy curves would strengthen claims.
  - The piecewise KL construction is plausible but underexplored: sensitivity to choice of subgraphs, coverage, and treewidth is not analyzed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear background on mixing times and the practical implications. Areas for improvement:
- Fix the spectral projection typo and precisely define all matrices (e.g., use consistent notation for the Hadamard product with Z).
- Provide a brief proof sketch or references for Lemma 5 and Theorem 7, and state convexity/duality assumptions explicitly.
- Clarify the experimental protocol (ground-truth computation, sampling details, stopping criteria), and report mixing diagnostics.
- A footnote is referenced in Sec. 5.3 but missing; include it or remove the marker.

Originality
The central idea—projecting to a fast-mixing set as a tractable family—is novel and conceptually unifies MCMC tractability with variational-like projection. The dual projection preserving sparsity is a neat technical contribution. The piecewise KL divergence is a fresh twist on piecewise/mixture-of-trees ideas. Positioning KL(ψ||θ) minimization as a generalization of mean-field under spectral constraints is insightful.

Significance
The work could influence approximate inference practice in regimes where Gibbs is slow and variational methods struggle, by giving a principled way to “regularize toward fast mixing” while staying in the original model class. That said, current impact is moderated by:
- Loose sufficient conditions (and the paper’s reliance on thresholds >1 without guarantees).
- Limited empirical scale and missing fair runtime comparisons.
- Focus on Ising models; extension to general MRFs is left open.

Pros
- Clear, novel perspective on tractability via mixing guarantees.
- Practical algorithms for Euclidean and KL-based projections; elegant dual projection respecting sparsity.
- Empirical gains over MF/LBP/TRW in strong-interaction regimes when time is limited.

Cons
- Theoretical guarantees weakened by using c > 1 in experiments; no mixing diagnostics.
- Possible error in Theorem 6; dual method lacks stated optimality conditions.
- Experimental scope and fairness (compute budgets) need strengthening; some ambiguity in evaluation protocol.
- Piecewise KL underperforms and design choices are not thoroughly analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct Theorem 6 and state convexity/duality properties for Theorem 7; provide conditions for global optimality.
- Report experiments with c ≤ 1 (guaranteed regime) and c > 1 (heuristic), including empirical mixing indicators (autocorrelation, effective sample size).
- Equalize wall-clock budgets; provide time–accuracy curves comparing: (i) original Gibbs, (ii) projected Gibbs (including projection time), (iii) LBP/TRW/MF.
- Clarify ground-truth computation and sampling details; include ablations on subgraph choices for piecewise KL.
- Discuss connections to other fast-mixing criteria (e.g., Dobrushin’s condition, correlation decay/Weitz) and whether your method can incorporate alternative bounds.

Overall evaluation
A thoughtful and original contribution with promising empirical evidence and a compelling conceptual shift. The paper would benefit from tightening theoretical statements and more rigorous, fair experimental evaluation. With these addressed, it would be a strong addition; as is, it’s borderline but leaning positive due to novelty and potential impact.
