### Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses the challenge of inference in Ising models, which is often intractable due to high treewidth or slow convergence of MCMC methods like Gibbs sampling when interactions are strong. The central and novel idea is to define a tractable family of models not by their graphical structure (e.g., being fully factorized or tree-structured, as in mean-field or TRW-BP), but by their parameters. Specifically, the authors define a set of "fast-mixing" models as those whose parameters satisfy a condition guaranteeing rapid convergence of Gibbs sampling.

Building on prior work by Dyer et al. [4] and Hayes [8], the authors use the condition that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix is less than one (`||R||_2 < 1`). For Ising models, this can be satisfied by ensuring the spectral norm of the matrix of absolute interaction strengths is less than one (`|| |β| ||_2 < 1`). The paper's primary contribution is a set of algorithms to project a given set of (potentially slow-mixing) Ising model parameters `θ` onto this fast-mixing set `Ψ`. The goal is to find the closest parameters `ψ ∈ Ψ` to the original `θ`.

The authors explore four different divergences for this projection:
1.  **Euclidean Distance:** The authors derive a dual algorithm based on iterative SVD thresholding (Theorem 7) to solve for the projection, which can also enforce sparsity.
2.  **"Zero-avoiding" KL-Divergence (KL(θ||ψ)):** This is probabilistically natural but computationally intractable for the target problem, as it requires expectations with respect to the original, slow-mixing distribution `p(x;θ)`.
3.  **Piecewise KL-Divergence:** A novel and tractable approximation to the KL-divergence, where the KL is maximized over a set of low-treewidth subgraphs of the original model.
4.  **"Zero-forcing" KL-Divergence (KL(ψ||θ)):** This is more tractable than the standard KL because it requires expectations with respect to the fast-mixing distribution `p(x;ψ)`, which can be approximated via Gibbs sampling. Minimizing this divergence is performed using stochastic projected gradient descent. The authors frame this as a generalization of mean-field, which corresponds to the extreme case where the spectral norm of the interaction matrix is constrained to zero.

Experiments on 2D grids and random graphs demonstrate that sampling from the projected parameters, particularly under the reversed KL-divergence, yields more accurate marginals than standard variational methods like LBP, TRW, and mean-field. Furthermore, for a limited time budget, Gibbs sampling on the projected parameters is shown to be more accurate than Gibbs sampling on the original, slow-mixing parameters.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. It rests on established theoretical results concerning Markov chain mixing times and correctly applies optimization techniques to derive projection algorithms. The derivation of the Euclidean projection via a dual formulation (Theorem 7) is solid. The use of stochastic gradient descent for the KL(ψ||θ) projection is well-motivated and appropriate for the problem. Claims are supported by a thorough experimental evaluation comparing the proposed methods against relevant and widely used baselines. The authors are also transparent about a key weakness of the work, namely the looseness of the theoretical mixing time bound.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, followed by a concise background on mixing time theory. The progression from the simple Euclidean projection to the more complex KL-based projections is logical. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, allowing for an easy comparison of the different methods. The provided algorithms and gradients are sufficient for an expert to understand the implementation.

**Originality:** The primary contribution of the paper is highly original. The conceptual shift from structure-based approximations to parameter-space approximations based on dynamic properties (i.e., mixing time) is a novel and valuable perspective in the field of approximate inference. While it builds on existing MCMC theory, its application to create a new class of "tractable" approximations is new. This approach differs significantly from prior work in NIPS and elsewhere, which has largely focused on variational methods over structurally simple distributions. The proposed piecewise KL-divergence is also a novel contribution.

**Significance:** The paper's results are significant. It presents a new, practical approach to approximate inference that is shown to outperform several standard methods in the difficult regime of strong interactions. The idea of projecting parameters to guarantee desirable sampler behavior has the potential to be influential. Researchers and practitioners using MCMC for inference or learning could use or build upon these ideas to improve sampler efficiency and reliability. The framing of mean-field as a special case of their KL(ψ||θ) projection provides a new and insightful connection between previously disparate techniques.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Pro:**
*   **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The paper introduces a genuinely new way to think about approximate inference, defining tractability by fast mixing rather than by graphical structure. This is a significant conceptual contribution.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed methods, particularly the KL(ψ||θ) projection, demonstrate superior accuracy over established baselines like LBP, TRW, and Mean Field, especially in challenging high-interaction settings.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The work addresses the very practical problem of slow MCMC convergence. The finding that sampling from projected parameters is more accurate than sampling from original parameters under a time limit is a compelling result for practitioners.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides concrete algorithms derived from sound theoretical principles.

**Con:**
*   **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** A central premise is the "guarantee" of fast mixing. However, the theoretical bound (`|| |β| ||_2 < 1`) is acknowledged to be conservative. The experiments achieve strong results using an empirically chosen, much looser bound (`|| |β| ||_2 < 2.5`), which lacks the same theoretical guarantee. This weakens the main argument of projecting onto a *provably* fast-mixing set.
*   **Computational Cost of Projection:** The projection step itself requires solving a non-trivial optimization problem, which in the case of KL(ψ||θ) involves iterative MCMC sampling. The experiments focus on the efficiency of sampling *after* projection, but a more complete analysis would account for the computational overhead of the projection step itself.
*   **Limited Scope:** The analysis and algorithms are developed specifically for Ising models with single-site Gibbs sampling. The extension to more general MRFs with larger state spaces or different samplers is mentioned as future work but presents significant technical challenges, potentially limiting the immediate applicability of the method.
