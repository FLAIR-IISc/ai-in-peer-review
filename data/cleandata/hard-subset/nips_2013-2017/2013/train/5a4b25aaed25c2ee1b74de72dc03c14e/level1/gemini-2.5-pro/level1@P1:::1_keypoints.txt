- Proposes projecting intractable Ising model parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set to enable efficient Gibbs sampling.  
- Formalizes "fast-mixing" using the MCMC result that the spectral norm of the dependency matrix < 1 guarantees rapid mixing.  
- Develops four projection methods: Euclidean (L2), zero-avoiding KL (KL(q||p)), piecewise KL, and zero-forcing KL (KL(p||q)).  
- Main technical result: Euclidean projection via dual algorithm and iterative SVD thresholding (Theorem 7) preserving graph sparsity.  
- KL(q||p) is probabilistically natural but intractable because it requires expectations under the original slow-mixing distribution.  
- Piecewise KL is a novel, tractable approximation using tractable subgraphs (e.g., spanning trees) but underperforms and feels underdeveloped.  
- KL(p||q) is tractable since expectations are under the projected model; optimized via projected SGD with Gibbs sampling and generalizes mean-field.  
- Experiments on grids and random graphs show projected models (especially with KL(p||q)) yield more accurate marginals than LBP, TRW, and naive mean-field.  
- For limited computation budgets, running a fast sampler on the projected model outperforms running Gibbs on the original slow-mixing model.  
- Paper quality is high: solid theory, sound derivations, and thorough experimental evaluation against strong baselines.  
- Writing and organization are clear; background and motivation are well explained for non-experts in MCMC mixing theory.  
- The approach is original: parameter projection onto a dynamic (mixing-based) set is a novel framing for approximate inference.  
- Significance: addresses the practical problem of slow MCMC mixing and suggests a general framework applicable beyond Ising models.  
- Main theoretical-practical gap: experiments use a spectral norm bound c = 2.5 ( >1 ), voiding the formal fast-mixing guarantee and needing justification.  
- Reviewer requests ablation/sensitivity analysis on the spectral-norm bound (e.g., c = 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5) to show its impact on accuracy.  
- Computational-cost evaluation is incomplete: projection overhead (notably KL(p||q) SGD with many samples) should be included in total wall-clock time comparisons.  
- Suggests plotting marginal error vs total wall-clock time (projection + sampling) for a fairer comparison to running longer Gibbs chains on the original model.  
- Recommends either strengthening or removing the piecewise KL section unless clearer use-cases or improvements are shown.  
- Arguments for acceptance: high originality, strong technical content, compelling empirical results, and clear, honest presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: reliance on c > 1 in practice undermines core theoretical claim, and omission of projection cost from time-accuracy trade-offs.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — paper is strong and its weaknesses are addressable and do not negate the contribution.
