- Proposes constraining Ising model parameters to a “fast-mixing” set (||R||2 < 1 via Rij ≤ tanh|βij| ≤ |βij|) and projecting arbitrary θ onto that set for tractable inference.
- Derives Euclidean projection onto an operator-norm ball with a sparsity mask via a dual algorithm (Theorem 7) using singular value thresholding.
- Considers multiple projection divergences: Euclidean, KL(θ||ψ) (impractical), piecewise-KL (heuristic), and KL(ψ||θ) (practical via projected stochastic gradient using samples from ψ).
- Empirically, projections often give more accurate marginals than MF/LBP/TRW and can outperform Gibbs on original slow-mixing models under finite sampling budgets.
- The use of ||R||2 < 1 and the Rij ≤ tanh|βij| bound is standard and appropriate as a sufficient condition for rapid mixing.
- Theorem 6 appears to have a typo in singular-value thresholding (should threshold at c, not c^2).
- Theorem 8 likely has a sign error; gradient of KL(ψ||θ) should be −Covψ[f,f](ψ−θ).
- The projection operator Πc is non-smooth at singular-value thresholds; using LBFGS-B requires justification or smoothing/subgradient treatment and convergence discussion.
- Experiments violate theoretical guarantees by using spectral-norm bounds >1 (e.g., 2.5); need empirical mixing-time verification or adherence to theory.
- Experimental evaluation lacks compute-normalized comparisons (wall-clock or total Gibbs updates), making performance claims incomplete.
- No sensitivity analysis or error bars for KL(ψ||θ) optimizer hyperparameters (step size, sample pool size, iterations); uncertainty estimates are missing.
- Piecewise-KL (max over subgraphs) is ad hoc; no ablation between max vs average or analysis of induced bias.
- Notational issues (triple-dot product, Z′, Πc) and the referenced theorem typos reduce clarity; short derivations would help.
- Novelty: using fast-mixing parameter sets as an approximation family and the dual Euclidean projection that enforces operator-norm and sparsity constraints are original contributions.
- Practical contribution: KL(ψ||θ) projected SGD is a natural, scalable method related to mean-field but sampling from a guaranteed-fast-mixing model.
- Significance: compelling idea to trade exactness for guaranteed rapid mixing with potential impact on finite-time inference and learning, but tempered by looseness of spectral bound and missing optimization/compute guarantees.
- Pros: novel conceptual approach, solid connection to mixing-time theory, elegant sparsity-preserving projection, and empirical gains in strong-coupling regimes.
- Cons: theoretical–practical mismatch in experiments, apparent theorem errors, non-smooth optimization without convergence justification, heuristic piecewise-KL, and incomplete experimental accounting.
- Key suggestions: enforce ||R||2 < 1 or measure mixing times empirically, fix Theorems 6/8 with derivations, address subgradient/non-smooth optimization, include compute-normalized comparisons and error bars, explore tighter fast-mixing criteria, and ablate piecewise-KL choices (max vs average).
