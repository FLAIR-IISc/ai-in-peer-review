

This paper introduces a novel framework for improving inference in high-treewidth Ising models by projecting parameters onto a "fast-mixing" set, where Gibbs sampling converges rapidly. The core idea extends beyond structural tractability (e.g., tree-based approximations like mean-field or loopy BP) to parameter-space constraints guaranteeing rapid mixing via spectral norm bounds on interaction matrices. The authors derive four projection methods: Euclidean (using SVD thresholding via Theorem 7), piecewise KL (on low-treewidth subgraphs), and two KL variants (zero-avoiding, impractical for slow-mixing θ; zero-forcing, tractable via stochastic gradient descent). Experiments on 2D grids and random graphs show projected parameters yield more accurate marginals than original Gibbs sampling within limited time, outperforming variational methods like LBP and MF.

**Strengths**: The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical foundations (Theorems 4, 7) and clear experimental validation. The Euclidean projection algorithm (Theorem 7) is a significant contribution, and the zero-forcing KL approach generalizes mean-field to a broader fast-mixing set. The work connects to prior NIPS literature on mixing times (Hayes, Dyer et al.) and variational inference (Hazan & Shashua), while offering a distinct "parameter-space" perspective on tractability. Experiments are well-designed, comparing against relevant baselines (LBP, TRW, MF) and demonstrating practical utility.

**Weaknesses**: The spectral norm bound (||R||₂ < 1) is overly conservative compared to known Ising thresholds (e.g., β_c ≈ 0.44 vs. their β=0.25 cutoff, as noted in Section 7). This limits the applicability of their guarantee, as they empirically use a looser bound (2.5) for better approximation. The piecewise KL divergence lacks theoretical justification for its subgraph selection, and the comparison to modern fast-mixing methods (e.g., Swendsen-Wang) is missing. The paper also does not address whether their projection preserves key properties like log-partition consistency.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:  
*Pro*: Novel framework for fast-mixing parameter constraints; technically rigorous; strong empirical results for limited-time inference.  
*Con*: Loose theoretical bound; limited comparison to recent mixing-time advances; piecewise KL lacks principled subgraph selection.

The work is well-motivated and addresses a meaningful challenge in approximate inference. While the spectral norm bound warrants further tightening, the projection framework is a valuable addition to the inference toolbox. The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, and significance, though the bound looseness slightly reduces its immediate impact. **Recommendation: Accept**.
