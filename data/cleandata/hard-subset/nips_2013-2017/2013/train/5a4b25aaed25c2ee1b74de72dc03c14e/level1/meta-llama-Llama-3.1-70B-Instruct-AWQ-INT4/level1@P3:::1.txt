The authors present an algorithm to project Ising model parameters onto a parameter set that is guaranteed to be fast mixing under several divergences. They demonstrate that Gibbs sampling using the projected parameters is more accurate than with the original parameters when interaction strengths are strong and when limited time is available for sampling.

The paper relates to previous work on mixing times in Markov chains, including a recent textbook [10]. The authors build upon the work of Dyer et al. [4] and Hayes [8] to establish a sufficient condition for rapid mixing in Ising models. They also draw inspiration from various approximate inference methods, including mean-field inference [14], structured mean-field [16, 20], loopy belief propagation [21], and tree-reweighted belief propagation [19].

The authors' contribution is a novel algorithm for projecting Ising model parameters onto a fast-mixing set. They consider four different divergences: Euclidean distance, "zero-avoiding" KL-divergence, "piecewise" KL-divergence, and "zero-forcing" KL-divergence. They derive a dual algorithm for the Euclidean distance and extend it to more probabilistic divergences.

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their work. The writing is concise, and the notation is well-defined. The authors adequately inform the reader about the necessary background and provide sufficient details about their algorithms and experiments.

The originality of the paper lies in the authors' novel approach to approximate inference in Ising models. They introduce a new notion of "tractability" based on parameter-space conditions that guarantee rapid mixing. Their algorithm for projecting onto the fast-mixing set is a significant contribution to the field.

The significance of the paper is evident in its potential to improve approximate inference in high-treewidth graphical models. The authors demonstrate that their method can outperform standard variational methods and Gibbs sampling on the original parameters in certain scenarios. The paper also opens up new avenues for research, including the extension of this approach to general Markov random fields and the use of fast-mixing distributions for learning.

Arguments for acceptance:

1. The paper presents a novel and technically sound algorithm for approximate inference in Ising models.
2. The authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their work.
3. The paper is original and makes a significant contribution to the field.
4. The experimental evaluation is thorough and demonstrates the potential of the proposed method.

Arguments against acceptance:

1. The paper assumes a relatively simple model (Ising model) and may not generalize to more complex models.
2. The authors do not provide a theoretical analysis of the convergence rate of their algorithm.
3. The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, including more comparison methods and a more detailed analysis of the results.

Suggestions for improvement:

1. Provide a more detailed analysis of the convergence rate of the algorithm.
2. Extend the experimental evaluation to more complex models and comparison methods.
3. Consider providing a more intuitive explanation of the "piecewise" KL-divergence and its relation to the "zero-avoiding" KL-divergence.
4. Clarify the relation between the proposed method and other approximate inference methods, such as mean-field inference and loopy belief propagation.
