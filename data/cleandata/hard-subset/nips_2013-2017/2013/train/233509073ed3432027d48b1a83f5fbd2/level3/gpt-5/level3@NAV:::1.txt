Summary and relation to prior work: This paper proposes LoSST, an online heuristic for tracking time-varying graphical structure by maintaining weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) and triggering structure relearning via pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances. The structure at each relearning step is estimated with PC. Simulations show parity with batch PC in stationary phases and faster adaptation after changes; a CPI volatility case study suggests responsiveness to macroeconomic regime shifts. While the manuscript argues most structure-learning assumes global stationarity, this claim overreaches: Siracusa & Fisher (AISTATS’09) already address time-varying directed structure in a Bayesian framework, and Talih & Hengartner (JRSS-B’05) treat time-varying undirected graphs. Moreover, many score-based BN methods do not require a single global model; their decomposable/local scoring can be used with sliding windows, CPD+relearning, or switching DBNs. BOCPD and related change-point approaches are also directly relevant.

Quality: The results appear plausible and internally consistent, but the evaluation is underpowered: the only baseline is batch PC. Missing comparisons include (a) sliding-window PC/score-based search, (b) BOCPD coupled with windowed structure relearning, and (c) Siracusa & Fisher. There is little sensitivity analysis for T, γ, ν or ablations of the p-value pooling. Section 6’s assertion that improved tracking offsets additional compute remains speculative and domain-dependent without runtime/accuracy tradeoff curves.

Clarity: The learning task is underspecified: is the goal change-point detection, timely recovery of the current structure, or both? Is LoSST a new model, an algorithmic wrapper on PC, or both? Presentation issues hinder readability: ambiguous definition and indexing for ρr+1 (Lipták pooling), unclear role of Sr in the T2 parameters, punctuation around equations, and small, under-labeled Figures 1–4 (fonts, color/legend).

Originality: The combination of pooled p-values with adaptive relearning is somewhat original, but claims of novelty over prior time-varying graphical models (e.g., Siracusa & Fisher) are overstated. The p-value construction is difficult to follow and appears largely heuristic.

Significance: Modest. The idea may inspire Bayesian or principled variants, but as presented, it reads as a heuristic that existing ML toolkits (windowed/CPD + structure learning, switching DBNs) could already address.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, implementable online heuristic with promising simulated behavior.
- Clear articulation of convergence–diligence trade-offs.

Cons for acceptance:
- Overstated novelty and incomplete related work.
- Missing strong baselines and sensitivity/ablation studies.
- Clarity/presentation issues (ρr+1, Sr, figures).
- Limited evidence of state-of-the-art improvement or domain impact.

Suggestions: Precisely define the task; add the above baselines and runtime–accuracy tradeoffs; provide sensitivity analyses and CIs; clarify ρr+1/Sr and fix notation; enlarge and annotate figures; temper novelty claims with appropriate citations.
