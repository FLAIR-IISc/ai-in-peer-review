- Addresses an important practical problem: learning graphical models in non-stationary, online settings where standard methods assuming stationarity fail.  
- Proposes LoSST, an intuitive single-pass heuristic that weights sufficient statistics and uses Mahalanobis distance to detect departures from the current model.  
- Uses pooled p-values (Liptak's method) to decide when to upweight recent data and probabilistically invoke PC for relearning, enabling adaptation to changepoints.  
- Empirical results on synthetic structural and parametric changepoints and a real price-index dataset show LoSST adapts where batch PC fails.  
- Claims of novelty are overstated; prior work (e.g., Siracusa & Fisher 2009) and adaptable score-based approaches contradict the paper's positioning.  
- Mischaracterizes related work by asserting other methods cannot handle changing structures, weakening the paper’s motivation.  
- The precise learning task is unclear—ambiguous whether the goal is changepoint detection, online structure estimation, or both.  
- The pooled p-value definition and the update rules for weights lack clarity and appear circular in presentation.  
- Presentation problems: grammatical issues, hard-to-read figures (small fonts, poor legend distinctions), and vague discussion points.  
- Technical contribution is largely a heuristic combination (Mahalanobis distance, p-value pooling, PC) with ad-hoc weight updates and noisy-OR relearning, lacking theoretical justification.  
- The p-value construction is complex and hard to follow, reducing accessibility and obscuring novelty.  
- For acceptance: tackles a significant problem, shows promising empirical performance, and could spur further research in online/non-stationary structure learning.  
- For rejection: originality and related-work claims are weak, clarity issues impede understanding, and the contribution is more engineering than a fundamental methodological advance.  
- Overall: interesting problem and promising empirical results, but important weaknesses in positioning, clarity, and novelty need to be addressed.
