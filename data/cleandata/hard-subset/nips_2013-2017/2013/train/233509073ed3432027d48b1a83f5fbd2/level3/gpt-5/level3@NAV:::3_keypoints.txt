- Proposes LoSST: online structure learning for recursive (linear-Gaussian) SEMs under locally i.i.d. time-varying data using online sufficient statistics with adaptive weights driven by pooled p‑values of Mahalanobis distances.  
- Uses a probabilistic relearning schedule to trigger constraint-based structure discovery (implemented with PC), emphasizing recent data when model fit degrades.  
- Positions LoSST relative to stationary structure learning, online changepoint detection, TD updates, and time-indexed graphical models, claiming unique real-time support for directed models.  
- Methodology is technically sound; the link between weighting, effective sample size, and pooled p‑values is well justified and convergence/diligence trade-offs are thoughtfully discussed.  
- Experiments show LoSST matches batch PC under stationarity and outperforms after structural changes; CPI case study shows real-data utility.  
- Claim of generality beyond PC is unsupported by experiments limited to PC; the statement that other learners would behave similarly is too strong.  
- Reviewer requests qualifying the generality claim or adding comparisons (e.g., sliding-window PC, score-based GES with windows, CPD+reset baselines).  
- Paper is well written; should state up front that method is designed for linear-Gaussian SEMs and that extensions to discrete BNs are nontrivial.  
- Recommend adding brief pseudocode/algorithm summary and a sensitivity analysis figure for hyperparameters T, γ, ν to aid reproducibility.  
- Novelty: combining pooled p‑value–driven reweighting, online sufficient statistics, and probabilistic relearning is original and tackles an under-studied problem.  
- Significance: simple, computationally attractive, and effective on PC—promising baseline for nonstationary learning—but broader impact needs demonstration across learners and model classes.  
- Pros: clear formulation of locally i.i.d. structure tracking, scalable mechanism, solid theory intuition, strong synthetic results, plausible real-data case study.  
- Cons/requests: overgeneral claims about other learners, explicit scope to SEMs, limited baselines, and need for sensitivity/ablation and computational cost breakdown.  
- Reviewer has read the authors’ feedback.
