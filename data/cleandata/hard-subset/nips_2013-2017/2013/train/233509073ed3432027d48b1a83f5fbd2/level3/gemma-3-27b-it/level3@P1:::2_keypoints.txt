- Addresses a novel and important problem: real-time tracking of changing graphical model structure beyond stationarity assumptions.
- Proposes LoSST, a heuristic algorithm combining online sufficient-statistic tracking with episodic batch re-learning (PC algorithm) triggered by detected changes.
- Core innovation: weighting data by “surprisingness” using Mahalanobis distance to adapt to shifting dependencies.
- Demonstrates effectiveness on synthetic experiments and US price-index real-world data, outperforming standard batch learning in non-stationary settings.
- Paper is well written with a comprehensive literature review that situates the work within graphical model learning and online changepoint detection.
- LoSST is practically useful and efficient, suitable for large datasets and real-time applications due to online updates plus occasional batch updates.
- Clear explanation of algorithm components: weighted means, covariance updates, effective sample size, Mahalanobis-triggered re-learning, and Liptak pooling.
- Extensive simulations cover both structural and parameter shifts, providing reasonable experimental validation.
- Major weakness: heavy reliance on heuristics with no theoretical analysis or guarantees (convergence rates, detection delays, false positive rates).
- Numerous ad-hoc design choices (reweighting scheme, effective sample-size updates, trigger criteria) lack thorough justification and need sensitivity analysis.
- Statistical concerns: Hotelling’s T2 p-values may be invalid with weighted/effective samples, and Liptak pooling assumes independent tests that may not hold in time series.
- Authors should explicitly state when methods are used beyond theoretical guarantees and discuss potential implications.
- Presentation issues: proofs in footnotes disrupt flow and should move to supplementary material.
- Figures are too small, poorly labeled, and captions lack detail; experiment descriptions (notably page 7) are unclear and need elaboration.
- Specific empirical explanation (initial poorer performance on edge removals) is unconvincing; consider decoupling parameter updates from structural updates.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel important problem, practical and efficient algorithm, adequate experimental validation, clear exposition.
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks theory, relies on heuristics without justification, potential misuse of statistical methods, and presentation flaws.
- Overall recommendation: reject but encourage revision — strengthen theoretical grounding, justify design choices, provide sensitivity analyses, and improve clarity/presentation.
