This paper presents LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a novel online algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from data where the underlying generative process may change over time. The key assumption is that the data is locally stationary, a relaxation of the standard i.i.d. assumption made by most structure learning algorithms. The LoSST algorithm works by tracking sufficient statistics—specifically the mean, covariance matrix, and an effective sample size for Structural Equation Models (SEMs)—in a weighted, online fashion. The weight assigned to new data points is dynamically adjusted based on their fit to the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. When a significant deviation is detected over a sequence of points, the weights of recent data are increased, effectively "forgetting" past data that may have been generated by a different structure. A standard structure learning algorithm is then periodically re-run on the current weighted statistics to produce an updated graph. This work is well-positioned relative to prior work on static structure learning and changepoint detection, addressing the important gap of real-time structure learning in dynamic environments.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses the important and highly practical problem of learning causal or dependency structures from non-stationary data. This is a critical challenge in many real-world domains where the standard i.i.d. assumption does not hold. The proposed LoSST framework is a novel and systematic method for tackling this problem in an online setting, which is a significant contribution.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The LoSST algorithm is described clearly, with detailed explanations for the online updates of sufficient statistics and the principled motivation for the adaptive weighting scheme. The discussion of the trade-off between "convergence" and "diligence" provides a valuable theoretical framing for the algorithm's behavior under different parameterizations.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed techniques are simple, intuitive, and experimentally effective. Using the Mahalanobis distance to detect distributional shifts and re-weighting data accordingly is an elegant solution for updating the sufficient statistics of an SEM. The experimental results, though limited, convincingly demonstrate that LoSST successfully tracks changes in the underlying graph structure and significantly outperforms a batch-mode algorithm that assumes stationarity.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Unsupported Generality Claims:** The claim in Section 4 that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly" is too strong and is not supported by the evidence provided. The experiments are conducted exclusively using the constraint-based PC algorithm. It is not obvious that score-based algorithms would behave identically, as their scoring functions depend on the effective sample size and covariance matrix in potentially complex ways. This claim should be softened to reflect the specific experimental setup or, alternatively, the authors should provide a more rigorous justification for this generalization.
*   **Clarity of Scope:** While the algorithm is presented as a general method for learning "graphical models," its implementation and the theory of sufficient statistics are specific to linear SEMs over continuous variables. The paper would be stronger if it stated its SEM-specific focus more clearly from the outset. Extending the approach to other model classes, such as Bayesian networks with discrete variables, is non-trivial. Tracking weighted sufficient statistics for discrete models (i.e., conditional count tables) in an online fashion would be significantly more complex and memory-intensive. Acknowledging this limitation and framing such extensions as a direction for future research would improve the manuscript's precision.
*   **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The experimental results are promising but the evaluation is narrow. The sole baseline is a standard batch-mode PC algorithm applied to the entire dataset, which is expected to perform poorly under non-stationarity. A more comprehensive evaluation would strengthen the paper's claims by comparing LoSST against other plausible adaptive baselines, such as a sliding-window PC approach or an online learner using a simple fixed exponential forgetting factor. This would help to better quantify the advantage of LoSST’s adaptive weighting scheme over simpler heuristics.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel and well-motivated solution to an important problem. The method is simple, elegant, and shows strong empirical performance. The primary weaknesses are related to overstated claims of generality and a limited experimental comparison, both of which can be addressed by carefully revising the text. The contribution is significant and likely to be of interest to researchers working on causal discovery and learning from time-series data.
