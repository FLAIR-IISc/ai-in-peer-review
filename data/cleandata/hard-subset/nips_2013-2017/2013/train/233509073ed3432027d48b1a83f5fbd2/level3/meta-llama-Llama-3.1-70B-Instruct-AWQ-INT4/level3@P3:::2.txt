This paper presents a novel algorithm, Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), for tracking changes in graphical model structure or parameters in a dynamic, real-time manner. The authors tackle an under-explored problem in the field of graphical models, where most standard algorithms assume a globally stationary generating process. The proposed algorithm is a significant contribution to the field, as it addresses the need for online learning in situations where the underlying structure can change.

The LoSST algorithm is based on tracking the locally stationary sufficient statistics, including means, covariances, and sample size, in an online fashion. The algorithm uses a weighted pooling of individual p-values to detect changes in the underlying generating structure. The authors provide a detailed explanation of the algorithm, including the update equations for the sufficient statistics and the weighted pooling of p-values.

The paper includes a thorough evaluation of the LoSST algorithm using synthetic data and a real-world dataset. The results show that the algorithm performs comparably to batch-mode learning when the generating graphical structure is globally stationary and significantly better when it is only locally stationary. The authors also demonstrate the ability of the algorithm to detect changes in the underlying structure, including changes in parameters and graph structure.

The strengths of the paper include the novel approach to online learning in graphical models, the thorough evaluation of the algorithm, and the clear explanation of the methodology. The authors also provide a good literature review and adequately reference related work.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. One major con is the lack of theoretical analysis or guarantees, such as convergence or detection under assumptions. The authors should at least discuss these limitations as future work. Additionally, the algorithm is heuristic-heavy, with many ad-hoc decisions, such as reweighting, effective sample size, and re-learn criteria, which need clearer documentation.

Another concern is the potential misuse of statistical methods, such as the application of Hotelling's T2 and Liptak pooling, which assumes conditions that may not hold. The authors should explicitly state when methods are used outside their theoretical guarantees to avoid misleading readers.

Other issues with the paper include the excessive use of footnotes for proofs, which should be moved to supplementary material. The figures are also too small, poorly labeled, and lack details, making it difficult to understand the results. The experiment descriptions, such as on page 7, are unclear and should be improved.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and practical first attempt at addressing the problem of online learning in graphical models. However, it needs clearer justification of heuristic choices, better presentation, and theoretical development to strengthen the results.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper tackles an important and under-explored problem in the field of graphical models.
* The proposed algorithm is novel and has the potential to perform well in challenging contexts.
* The evaluation of the algorithm is thorough and includes both synthetic and real-world data.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The lack of theoretical analysis or guarantees is a significant limitation of the paper.
* The algorithm is heuristic-heavy, and many ad-hoc decisions need clearer documentation.
* The potential misuse of statistical methods is a concern and should be addressed.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Provide a more detailed explanation of the heuristic choices and ad-hoc decisions in the algorithm.
* Include a discussion of the limitations of the algorithm and potential future work.
* Improve the presentation of the figures and experiment descriptions.
* Consider moving the proofs to supplementary material to improve the readability of the paper.
