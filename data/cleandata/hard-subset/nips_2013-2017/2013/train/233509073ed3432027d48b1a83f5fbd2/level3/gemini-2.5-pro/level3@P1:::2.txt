This paper addresses the important and under-explored problem of learning graphical model structure from a stream of data where the underlying generative process may change over time. Standard structure learning algorithms assume a stationary data-generating process, an assumption that fails in many real-world scenarios. The authors propose the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel heuristic algorithm designed to track changes in the structure and parameters of Structural Equation Models (SEMs) in an online fashion.

The core idea of LoSST is to maintain online, weighted estimates of the sufficient statistics (means, covariance matrix) and an "effective sample size." As each new data point arrives, its "surprisingness" is measured by its Mahalanobis distance from the current estimated distribution. This distance is converted into a p-value using Hotelling's T-squared distribution. The algorithm then uses Liptak's method to pool these p-values over time, creating a statistic, ρ, that reflects the goodness-of-fit of the recent data sequence to the current model. A low ρ value indicates a likely change, which in turn increases the weight assigned to new data points and increases the probability of triggering a full structure relearning step, performed by a standard batch algorithm (here, the PC algorithm). The authors demonstrate LoSST's performance on synthetic data, showing it performs comparably to a batch learner in stationary settings and significantly better when the underlying structure or parameters shift. An application to U.S. price index data shows the method can detect known macroeconomic shifts.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem of online structure learning in non-stationary environments is highly relevant and has received relatively little attention. This paper makes a valuable contribution by proposing a concrete, practical algorithm to tackle this challenge, moving beyond the standard i.i.d. assumption. The proposed approach is novel in its creative combination of online sufficient statistic tracking, Mahalanobis distance for anomaly detection, and weighted p-value pooling to dynamically trigger a structure learning procedure.

2.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** LoSST is designed to be computationally and memory efficient. It processes each data point only once and maintains a small state (sufficient statistics, weights, etc.), making it suitable for streaming data and large datasets where batch processing is infeasible.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is a clear strength. The authors test the algorithm on synthetic data with both parameter and structure shifts, across a range of graph sizes and densities. This provides a good sense of the algorithm's behavior and its advantages over a standard batch approach. The inclusion of a real-world dataset on economic indicators, where LoSST identifies known historical periods of change (the "Great Moderation"), adds credibility and demonstrates potential applicability.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the algorithm is described in sufficient detail, and the related work section effectively situates the contribution within the fields of structure learning, online learning, and changepoint detection.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary weakness of the paper is its highly heuristic nature and the corresponding lack of theoretical analysis. While the authors introduce an interesting discussion of "convergence" vs. "diligence," the supporting arguments are relegated to brief, informal "proof sketches" in footnotes. A NIPS paper should ideally provide a more rigorous analysis, even if under strong assumptions. For instance, under what conditions (e.g., rate of change, magnitude of change) can LoSST be guaranteed to detect a shift? What are the theoretical properties of the estimated covariance matrix? This should be a major focus of future work and its absence should be more clearly stated as a limitation of the current paper.

2.  **Potential Misuse of Statistical Methods:** Several core components of the algorithm appear to use statistical tools outside of their standard-use conditions without sufficient justification.
    *   Hotelling's T^2 test is typically applied to i.i.d. samples from a multivariate normal distribution. Here, it is applied using a covariance matrix and sample size derived from *weighted* data, which are not i.i.d. The validity of the p-values derived in this context is questionable.
    *   Liptak's method for pooling p-values assumes the individual tests are independent. In this sequential setting, the p-values are clearly dependent, as each is calculated based on a model that includes information from previous points.
    The authors should explicitly acknowledge that these methods are being used as heuristics and discuss the potential impact of these violated assumptions.

3.  **Ad-Hoc Design Choices:** Several key update equations and mechanisms feel arbitrary and lack clear justification. For example, the specific functional form for updating the data point weights (Eq. 4) and the effective sample size (Eq. 3) are presented without motivation. Similarly, the noisy-OR gate for probabilistic relearning is one of many possible choices. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of why these specific heuristics were chosen over alternatives.

4.  **Presentation and Clarity Issues:**
    *   The figures are too small and poorly labeled, making them very difficult to interpret. Axis labels, legends, and captions need to be significantly improved to make the results clear.
    *   The description of the experimental setup can be confusing. For example, the use of a "fixed 'graph relearning' schedule" for the comparison in Section 4 is not well-motivated and potentially confounds the comparison between LoSST's detection mechanism and PC's learning ability.
    *   The explanation for LoSST's worse performance on edge removal errors after a parameter-only change (p. 7) is unsatisfying. The claim that "PC initially responds by adding edges... while LoSST responds by being more cautious" is vague. A more detailed analysis of this failure mode is needed. Perhaps this suggests a need to decouple parameter adaptation from full structure relearning.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:** The paper tackles a novel, important, and difficult problem. The proposed LoSST algorithm is an original and practical first step, combining existing ideas in a clever way. The empirical results on both synthetic and real data are promising and clearly demonstrate the potential utility of the approach.
*   **Arguments against acceptance:** The work is largely heuristic and lacks theoretical grounding, which is a significant weakness. There are serious unanswered questions about the statistical validity of the core mechanisms. The presentation of results, particularly the figures, needs substantial improvement to meet the standards of a top-tier conference.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting and promising direction for research. However, the heavy reliance on unjustified heuristics and the potential statistical issues are significant concerns. While the problem's importance and the novelty of the approach are compelling, the paper would need to better acknowledge its limitations and strengthen the justification for its design choices to be a strong candidate for acceptance.
