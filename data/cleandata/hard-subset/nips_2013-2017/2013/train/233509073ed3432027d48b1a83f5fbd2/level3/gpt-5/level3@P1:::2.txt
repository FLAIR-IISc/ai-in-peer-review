This paper addresses online detection and tracking of changes in the dependency structure of multivariate data, focusing on linear-Gaussian SEMs with DAG structure. The proposed Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) maintains streaming sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) and uses these to periodically relearn graph structure via a constraint-based algorithm (PC). Novelty lies in how datapoints are differentially weighted over time using a measure of “surprisingness”: for each new sample, the Mahalanobis distance relative to the current estimates yields a Hotelling’s T^2 p-value; these p-values are pooled via Liptak’s method to produce a running significance statistic ρ. When ρ is small, LoSST increases weights for recent data and raises the probability of triggering structure relearning via a noisy-OR update. The authors analyze trade-offs between “convergence” and “diligence” as a function of the weight-scaling parameters (γ, T), provide synthetic experiments with abrupt changes in structure and/or parameters across a range of graph sizes/degrees, explore probabilistic relearning, and apply LoSST to U.S. price index data where known macroeconomic regime changes appear to be detected.

Relation to prior work: Compared to batch score-based and constraint-based structure learning (e.g., Chickering; Spirtes et al.), LoSST explicitly targets nonstationarity and online operation. It differs from temporal-difference style tracking (Sutton) by operating on multivariate sufficient statistics and from online changepoint detection (Adams & MacKay; frequentist CPD) by avoiding explicit storage of long histories and by accommodating gradual drift. It also differs from time-varying graph estimation in batch settings (Talih & Hengartner on undirected graphs; Siracusa & Fisher on Bayesian time-indexed DAGs) by being single-pass and frequentist.

Quality:
- Methodological soundness: The streaming updates for weighted means and covariances are correct and memory-efficient, and using PC for episodic structure discovery is reasonable. The paper offers an insightful discussion of convergence vs. diligence and sketches sufficient conditions depending on γ and T.
- However, key components are heuristic and rest on statistical approximations whose validity is unclear in this setting: Hotelling’s T^2 requires i.i.d. Gaussian samples with a well-defined sample size; here, the covariance is itself adaptively weighted and the “effective” N may not justify the reference distribution. Liptak pooling typically assumes independent tests; successive p-values from overlapping, adaptively updated estimates will be dependent. The paper should assess calibration (false alarm rates) under stationarity and quantify sensitivity to these violations.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments convincingly show parity with PC under stationarity and faster adaptation after change; the probabilistic relearning experiment indicates efficiency without loss of accuracy; and the CPI case study is illustrative. Missing are baselines closer to LoSST’s goal, such as: (a) sliding-window PC; (b) exponentially weighted covariance + PC on a window; (c) BOCPD/CPD feeding windowed PC. Ablations on γ, T, ν and sensitivity to dimensionality/invertibility would strengthen claims.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written with a thorough related work section. Some technical choices are under-explained: the effective sample size update, the exact mapping from pooled p-value to weight (motivation and alternatives), and numerical stabilization of C^{-1} (e.g., shrinkage, regularization when Sr is small).
- Figures are hard to read (small fonts/legends) and captions should specify settings (graph sizes, degrees, parameter values, ν, T, γ) and summarize what to look for. Moving footnote proof sketches to an appendix would improve flow; provide complete proofs or make the claims explicitly heuristic.

Originality:
- The problem framing—online structure learning under local stationarity—is timely and relatively under-explored for DAGs. The combination of streaming sufficient statistics, pooled p-values, and triggered constraint-based relearning is a novel and practical synthesis. The convergence–diligence viewpoint is interesting and could inspire further work.

Significance:
- If made robust and better calibrated, LoSST could be useful in domains with evolving causal/associational structure (finance, biology, monitoring). The single-pass memory efficiency is attractive. However, the current reliance on heuristics and limited baselines constrain confidence in broad applicability. Providing stronger theoretical or empirical calibration would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Tackles an important, under-studied problem (online DAG structure tracking under nonstationarity).
- Practical, memory-efficient algorithm; single-pass operation.
- Clear demonstration that LoSST matches batch PC under stationarity and adapts quickly after changes.
- Includes real-data case study and a useful discussion of convergence vs. diligence.

Cons for acceptance:
- Heavy reliance on heuristics without clear theoretical guarantees or calibration of error rates.
- Potential misuse/miscalibration of Hotelling’s T^2 and Liptak pooling under adaptive weighting and dependence.
- Missing comparisons to relevant streaming baselines (windowed/forgetting-factor approaches, CPD+PC).
- Presentation issues (figure readability, proof placement) and incomplete documentation of parameter choices and computational costs.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Calibrate ρ and the relearning trigger under stationarity: report false alarm rates and expected time-between-relearns; consider sequential multivariate SPC methods (CUSUM, MEWMA) or GLR tests as alternatives to T^2+Liptak, which better handle dependence and forgetting factors.
- Compare against: (i) fixed-size sliding window PC; (ii) EWMA covariance + PC; (iii) BOCPD or frequentist CPD coupled with windowed PC; (iv) Talih–Hengartner-style time-varying undirected methods (converted to skeleton) as a sanity check.
- Provide ablations on γ, T, ν and show robustness across ranges; justify the chosen mapping from ρ to ar and explore other monotone mappings.
- Address numerical stability: regularize covariance (e.g., shrinkage), specify how inverses are computed when Sr is small, and report computational complexity per step.
- Clarify the parameter-change experiment: the explanation for edge removal behavior could be strengthened; consider decoupling parameter updates from structure triggers, or using stability selection to avoid transient edge losses.
- Improve figures and captions; move proof sketches and additional derivations to supplementary material; state explicitly where methods are used outside their formal assumptions.

Overall assessment: A promising and practical first attempt at online graphical structure tracking with compelling empirical indications, but it would benefit from clearer justification of statistical components, stronger baselines, and improved presentation.
