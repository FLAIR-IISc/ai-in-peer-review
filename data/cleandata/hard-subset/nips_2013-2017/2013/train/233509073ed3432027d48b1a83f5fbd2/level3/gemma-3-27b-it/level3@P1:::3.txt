This paper addresses the critical and often overlooked problem of learning graphical model structure when the underlying generative process is not stationary, but rather *locally* stationary. Existing structure learning algorithms largely assume i.i.d. data, a restriction rarely met in real-world applications. The authors present LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm designed to track changes in graphical model structure and parameters in a dynamic, online manner. The approach is well-motivated, clearly articulated, and demonstrates promising results through simulation and a real-world application.

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of related work in graphical model learning (Chickering, 2002; Pearl, 2009), online learning (Sutton, 1988), and changepoint detection (Adams & MacKay, 2007; Desobry et al., 2003). It correctly identifies the limitations of existing techniques when applied to non-stationary environments.  The key contribution lies in adapting sufficient statistic tracking—specifically means, covariances, and sample size—to a dynamic context where datapoints require differential weighting based on their consistency with the current model. The integration of this tracking mechanism with the PC algorithm (Spirtes et al., 1991) provides a practical and experimentally validated approach.

The strengths of this work are numerous. The clarity of the exposition is particularly commendable. The authors present a complex algorithm in a readily understandable manner, providing sufficient detail for reproduction. The focus on both convergence and diligence (Kummerfeld & Danks, 2011) is insightful, and the exploration of the trade-offs between these properties adds nuance to the discussion. The simulation results are convincing, demonstrating LoSST's ability to perform competitively with batch-mode learning in stable environments and significantly outperform it when the generating structure changes. The application to U.S. price index volatility provides a compelling, albeit brief, demonstration of real-world relevance. 

However, there are also weaknesses.  The claim that “other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly” (specifically in comparison to PC) feels unsupported and potentially overstates the generality of the findings. While it's reasonable to expect similar trends, performance can vary significantly based on the algorithm’s inherent properties and implementation. It would be beneficial to either provide justification for this assertion or rephrase it to acknowledge the specific focus on the PC algorithm.  

Furthermore, the paper heavily centers on recursive Structural Equation Models (SEMs). While the authors acknowledge adaptability to other graphical model types, they should explicitly state the SEM-specific scope of the current work at the outset. The discussion of extending the approach to Bayesian networks highlights the challenges associated with tracking sufficient statistics for discrete variables. This suggests that a straightforward adaptation might not be possible, and acknowledging this upfront would manage expectations appropriately.

The experimental evaluation, while promising, is somewhat limited. The comparison is predominantly with the PC algorithm, and it would be valuable to explore the performance of LoSST with alternative structure learning methods.  Additionally, the simulations could be expanded to include more complex change scenarios, such as gradual drifts in parameters or more frequent structural changes.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem in graphical model learning.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated algorithm (LoSST) that effectively handles time-varying data.
*   Clear and concise writing with sufficient detail for reproduction.
*   Promising experimental results demonstrating LoSST's competitive performance.
*   Thoughtful discussion of the trade-offs between convergence and diligence.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The claim regarding the generalizability of results across structure learning algorithms requires more support.
*   The focus on SEMs limits the immediate applicability of the work to other graphical model types.
*   Experimental evaluation could be broadened to include more extensive comparisons and change scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The problem addressed is important, the proposed solution is well-designed and clearly explained, and the experimental results are encouraging. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above – specifically, clarifying the scope regarding SEMs and providing justification for the generalizability claim.
