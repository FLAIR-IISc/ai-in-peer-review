- Problem: Detecting and tracking changes in dependency structure over time in linear-Gaussian SEMs with an online method.
- Method (LoSST): Maintains streaming sufficient statistics and triggers batched PC re-learning when change is detected.
- Novel components: Mahalanobis-based weighting of incoming points by “surprisingness.”
- Novel components: Convert distances to p-values via Hotelling’s T2 and pool them with Liptak’s method to obtain a significance statistic ρ.
- Novel components: Noisy-OR rule combining current ρ and past relearn probability to decide when to re-run PC.
- Evaluation: Synthetic experiments with structural and parametric changes plus a CPI real-data case study showing plausible regime detection.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on PC and change-detection ideas; differs by real-time operation and online sufficient-statistic updates.
- Empirical support: Method is clearly specified and performs comparably to PC before changes and better after structural shifts.
- Empirical caveat: Brief performance dip after parameter-only changes with increased edge removal errors.
- Theoretical concern: Limited theoretical guarantees; only high-level sketches without formal detection error rates or conditions.
- Heuristic reliance: Several ad-hoc design choices (a-update, effective sample size recursion, noisy-OR) lack justification and ablation.
- Statistical validity: Potential misuse of Hotelling’s T2 with weighted covariance/effective N and violation of Liptak’s independence assumption for p-values.
- Experimental adequacy: Experiments are broad but baselines are limited; missing comparisons to sliding-window PC, CPD+PC hybrids, and EWMA triggers.
- Reporting issues: Need confidence intervals, calibration diagnostics for ρ, and clearer experimental details for reproducibility.
- Clarity: Generally well-written but key proofs and derivations are hidden in footnotes and figures are small/poorly labeled.
- Reproducibility: Centralize and document design choices, hyperparameters (T, γ, ν), CI test settings, and stopping criteria; provide code/supplement.
- Originality: Novel integration of known components into a practical online SEM-structure tracking pipeline.
- Significance: Potentially valuable for practitioners if robustness and justification are improved; real-data case suggests utility.
- Major strengths: Addresses important problem, computationally efficient (O(p^2) state, single pass), and has sensible empirical behavior.
- Major weaknesses: Lack of theory, heavy heuristic design, and questionable statistical test application without calibration.
- Suggested fixes: Move derivations to supplement, enlarge/clarify figures, add baselines and runtime/memory comparisons, and perform calibration checks.
- Suggested method improvements: Consider decoupling parameter updates (frequent) from structure updates (rarer) to reduce unnecessary edge pruning.
- Suggested statistical fixes: Use permutation/block bootstrap for calibration or robust p-value combination (e.g., Cauchy test) to handle dependence.
- Acceptance arguments: Timely, practical, simple and efficient algorithm with promising empirical gains and good literature grounding.
- Rejection arguments: Insufficient theoretical backing, heuristic-driven design, limited baselines, and presentation issues reduce confidence.
- Overall: Promising first attempt that would benefit from stronger theory, calibration, clearer documentation, and expanded baselines.
