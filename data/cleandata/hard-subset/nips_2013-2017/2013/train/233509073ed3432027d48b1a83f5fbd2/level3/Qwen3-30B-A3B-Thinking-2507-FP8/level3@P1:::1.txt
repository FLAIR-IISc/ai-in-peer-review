

The paper introduces LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical model structure under locally stationary assumptions. It addresses a gap in the literature by proposing a method to learn graphical models in real-time without requiring batch processing, contrasting with standard structure learning algorithms (e.g., PC algorithm) that assume globally stationary data. The authors demonstrate that LoSST performs comparably to batch methods in stable environments but significantly outperforms them when structures change, using simulations and a US price index case study.

**Originality** is the paper's weakest point. The authors claim novelty in handling "locally stationary" environments, but Siracusa and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) explicitly address time-varying graphical structures using Bayesian methods on time-series data, though they operate in batch mode. While LoSST's online nature is a technical contribution, the paper's related work section misleadingly states that "both types of structure learning algorithms assume that the data come from a single generating structure," which is inaccurate. Many score-based Bayesian network algorithms (e.g., [3, 12]) use decomposable scores that can be updated incrementally for non-iid data, making the novelty claim unsubstantiated. The p-value construction via Liptak’s method (Equation 4) is also not novel, and the algorithm’s core approach resembles online changepoint detection (e.g., [1, 4]) adapted to graphical models. The paper fails to clearly differentiate its contribution from existing work, undermining its originality.

**Quality** is partially supported but marred by methodological gaps. The simulation results (Figures 1–3) show LoSST outperforms batch learning after structural changes, but the experimental design is flawed. The "edge removal error" metric in Figure 2 exhibits counterintuitive behavior (LoSST performs worse than PC after parameter changes), which the authors attribute to "cautious inferences" but provide no theoretical justification. The real-world application (Section 5) is superficial: the US price index analysis detects the "Great Moderation" but offers no comparison to standard methods (e.g., sliding-window batch learning), making the significance claim unsubstantiated. The algorithm’s reliance on Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values lacks rigorous justification—e.g., why Liptak’s method is optimal, and how the threshold \(T = 0.05\) was chosen. The convergence/diligence trade-off (Section 3.1) is theoretically interesting but not empirically validated.

**Clarity** suffers from poor exposition. The definition of \(\rho_{r+1}\) (Equation 4) is confusingly written: the text states it "cannot include \(p_{r+1}\) without being circular," yet the equation for \(\rho_{r+1}\) depends on \(\rho_r\) (not \(p_{r+1}\)), creating ambiguity. Critical details—like how \(\eta_r\) and \(\tau_r\) are initialized—are omitted. The paper’s presentation is inconsistent: some equations end with punctuation, others do not, and Figure 1–4 suffer from unreadable fonts and unclear legends. The distinction between "detecting change points" and "inferring the current generating structure" is blurred throughout, leaving the core learning task ill-defined. Section 6’s claim that "improved tracking justifies extra computational cost" is vague and domain-dependent without empirical support.

**Significance** is modest. While the problem of online graphical model learning is relevant, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that LoSST solves a problem unaddressable by existing methods. For instance, sliding-window batch approaches (e.g., [14]) could be applied online with minimal modification, yet the paper dismisses them without comparison. The real-world case study adds little value: the detected "shifts" align with known economic events (e.g., the 2000 recession), but no analysis shows LoSST’s outputs are more actionable than those of simpler methods. The work appears to be a heuristic extension of online changepoint detection rather than a novel framework, limiting its potential to advance the field.

**Strengths** include the clear algorithmic description (Sections 3–4), the practical focus on online learning, and the thorough simulation setup (500 runs across multiple graph sizes). The convergence/diligence analysis (Section 3.1) is theoretically sound and provides useful insights for future work.

**Weaknesses** outweigh strengths. The novelty claim is unsupported by related work, the methodology lacks theoretical grounding for key choices, clarity is poor, and significance is overstated. The paper fails to address whether LoSST’s advantages hold for non-Gaussian data or complex structural changes (e.g., edge reorientations). The real-world analysis is underdeveloped, and the figures are unprofessional.

**Recommendation**: Reject. While the problem is relevant and the algorithm is technically sound, the paper does not make a sufficiently novel contribution. The related work is misrepresented, the significance is unsubstantiated, and clarity issues prevent the results from being trusted. The authors should either substantially revise to clarify novelty (e.g., by rigorously comparing to Siracusa & Fisher’s method) or abandon the "online" claim in favor of a more precise contribution. As written, it reads as a minor extension of existing online learning techniques without compelling evidence of advancement.
