## Review of “Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure”

This paper tackles the important and under-explored problem of detecting changes in dependency structure within streaming data for Structural Equation Models (SEMs). The authors propose LoSST, a novel and practical algorithm that efficiently learns graphical model structure by triggering batch PC structure learning based on tracked streaming statistics. The core idea – using weighted means, correlation matrices, effective sample size, and Mahalanobis distance-based “surprisingness” to dynamically adjust re-learning frequency – is a creative combination of established methods. 

LoSST’s strengths are significant. It addresses a real gap in the literature, where most structure learning algorithms assume stationarity. The algorithm itself is well-motivated, appearing computationally efficient, and the experimental evaluation, including parameter sensitivity analysis and a real-world application to US price index data, is adequate. The writing is clear and the literature review is thorough, demonstrating a strong understanding of the field. The application to the price index data is particularly compelling, showing detection of known economic shifts and robustness to noise. 

However, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses. The most significant is the lack of theoretical analysis. While the authors acknowledge the trade-off between convergence and diligence, the paper lacks any formal guarantees regarding convergence, detection rates, or the validity of the statistical methods employed under specific assumptions. This is a major omission, and a clear discussion of future work in this area is essential. 

Furthermore, LoSST relies heavily on heuristics with limited justification. The choices regarding reweighting schemes, effective sample size calculation, and the re-learning criterion are largely ad-hoc. The use of Hotelling’s T² and Liptak’s pooling, particularly when applied to effective/weighted sample sizes, raises concerns about potential misuse of statistical methods, as the underlying assumptions may not hold. Explicitly stating when methods are used outside their theoretical guarantees is crucial to avoid misleading readers.

Several presentation issues detract from the paper's impact. The figures are too small and lack sufficient detail in labels and captions. Proofs are excessively relegated to footnotes, hindering readability; they should be moved to supplementary material. Finally, the explanation for LoSST’s poorer performance on edge additions during parameter-only changes feels inadequate; a decoupling of parameter and structure updates could address this.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and relatively unexplored problem.
*   Novel and potentially impactful algorithm.
*   Adequate experimental evaluation, including real-world data.
*   Clear writing and strong literature review.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of theoretical analysis or guarantees.
*   Heuristic-heavy design lacking sufficient justification.
*   Potential misuse of statistical methods without proper caveats.
*   Presentation issues (figures, proofs).



**Overall:** LoSST represents an interesting and practical first attempt at tackling a challenging problem. However, it requires significant refinement, particularly in terms of theoretical grounding and justification of its heuristic choices, before it can be considered a substantial contribution to the field. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the major concerns outlined above.
