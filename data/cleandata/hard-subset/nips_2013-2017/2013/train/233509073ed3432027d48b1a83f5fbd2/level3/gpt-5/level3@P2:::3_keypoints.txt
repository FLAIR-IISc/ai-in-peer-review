- Introduces LoSST: online algorithm that tracks time-varying DAG structure in linear-Gaussian SEMs using weighted sufficient statistics and adaptive per-sample weights.
- Core mechanism: pooled p-value (Liptak’s method on Mahalanobis/Hotelling T^2 distances) increases weights when recent data misfit and probabilistically triggers PC relearning.
- Memory and efficiency: single-pass method using O(p^2) memory with occasional episodic PC calls.
- Analytical contribution: discusses trade-off between “convergence” and “diligence” as a function of weight-update parameters (T, γ, ν) with sketched proofs.
- Relation to prior work: distinguishes from batch time-varying estimators, online stationary Bayesian updates, and changepoint-detection methods requiring large storage.
- Technical soundness: recursions for weighted mean/covariance and effective sample size are standard; sampling-distribution justification under adaptive weighting is heuristic.
- Statistical caveat: use of Hotelling’s T^2 and pooled p-values assumes Gaussianity and independence, but serial dependence from streaming estimation violates these assumptions.
- Empirical support: simulations show parity with PC under stationarity and adaptation after change; probabilistic relearning experiment is informative.
- Real-data evaluation: CPI case study is illustrative but lacks ground truth and quantitative validation beyond qualitative narrative alignment.
- Over-claimed generality: some assertions (e.g., other structure learners would perform similarly) are too strong and need tempering or empirical support.
- Clarity: paper is well written; algorithm steps, parameter roles, and pooled p-value intuition are clear.
- Reproducibility suggestions: include pseudocode for LoSST and the relearning trigger, and clarify per-step computational complexity.
- Originality: novel combination of adaptive weighting of sufficient statistics, an online effective sample-size tied to CI tests, and probabilistic relearning for DAGs.
- Significance: addresses an important, under-explored streaming causal discovery problem with a simple, adoptable method likely to influence the area.
- Strength: single-pass, low-memory design suitable for locally stationary structure; modular and compatible with CI-based learners.
- Strength: clear exposition and useful convergence vs. diligence conceptual framing.
- Weakness — baselines: comparisons limited to batch PC; should add sliding-window PC, exponentially-weighted PC, and online CPD+reestimation or EWMA/CUSUM detectors.
- Weakness — robustness: need discussion/experiments on non-Gaussian noise, heteroskedasticity, and alternatives like robust/rank-based distances.
- Weakness — statistical validity: justify use of effective sample size in CI tests under adaptive weighting or provide empirical calibration of Type I error.
- Weakness — hyperparameters: provide principled guidance or sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν rather than “unsystematic” selection.
- Weakness — computational details: report runtime, number of PC calls, and methods for maintaining C^{-1} (e.g., Sherman–Morrison) to justify O(p^2) per-step claims.
- Weakness — scope and extensions: explicitly state linear-Gaussian SEM scope in abstract/introduction and note discrete BN extensions are nontrivial.
- Weakness — real-data validation: augment with quantitative checks (predictive likelihood, imputation error, bootstrap stability of adjacencies).
- Acceptance arguments: practical, simple, novel synthesis with strong synthetic results and a useful conceptual lens; easy to adopt.
- Rejection arguments: limited baselines, heuristic statistical pieces lacking theory, insufficient hyperparameter/robustness study, and illustrative (not rigorous) real-data validation.
- Overall recommendation: promising and well-presented; lean toward acceptance if claims are tempered and empirical evaluation (baselines, hyperparameter guidance) is strengthened, otherwise borderline.
