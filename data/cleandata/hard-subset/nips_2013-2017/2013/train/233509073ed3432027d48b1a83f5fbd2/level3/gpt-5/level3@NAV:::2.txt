This paper tackles an under-explored and important problem: detecting and adapting to changes in dependency structure for SEMs over a data stream. The proposed LoSST algorithm tracks weighted sufficient statistics (means, correlation/covariance, an “effective” sample size), where new points are weighted by “surprisingness” via Mahalanobis distance. Individual MD p-values are computed using Hotelling’s T2, pooled with Liptak’s method into a significance statistic ρ; ρ (and a noisy-OR accumulator) schedules when to trigger batch PC structure learning. Experiments compare LoSST to vanilla PC under synthetic parameter and structure shifts across graph sizes/degrees, and on a US price-index time series (529 monthly points, six variables). The paper positions itself against online changepoint detection (e.g., Adams & MacKay) and prior time-varying graphical models (Talih & Hengartner; Siracusa & Fisher), highlighting the need for real-time, general structure tracking in SEMs.

Quality: The empirical study is adequate (including parameter sensitivity) and the real-data case is informative. However, the design is heuristic-heavy, and several steps appear to apply statistical tools outside their standard guarantees: Hotelling’s T2 is used with a weighted/effective sample size, and Liptak pooling presumes independence that likely does not hold for serially dependent, adaptively weighted tests. The paper should explicitly state these departures, discuss potential biases/calibration issues, and, ideally, include checks (e.g., simulations assessing Type I/II error and ρ calibration). The “convergence vs. diligence” discussion is interesting but lacks rigorous conditions; proofs scattered in footnotes hamper readability.

Clarity: Writing is generally clear with a solid literature review. Conversely, figures are small/under-labeled and captions terse; some experiment descriptions (e.g., p.7) need more detail (data generation, PC settings, ν/T/γ choices). Move detailed proofs from footnotes to the supplement and better document heuristics (effective sample size update, weight transform, relearn noisy-OR).

Originality: A novel and pragmatic integration of known tools (MD, Hotelling’s T2, Liptak pooling, PC) into a streaming structure tracker. The problem framing for SEMs is timely and distinct from existing batch or CPD-only approaches.

Significance: Practical and potentially impactful; likely to spur follow-up on online structure learning.

Suggestions: (1) Provide theoretical discussion/assumptions where guarantees might be possible; otherwise clearly mark heuristic uses. (2) Add baselines (sliding-window PC; CPD+relearn wrappers; kernel CD). (3) Address why LoSST underperforms on edge additions after parameter-only changes; consider decoupling parameter updates from structure updates or two-tier triggers. (4) Improve figures and experimental detail.

Pros:
- Addresses an important, under-explored problem
- Novel, practical, memory/compute-efficient design
- Adequate experiments with sensitivity analysis and real data
- Clear writing and literature coverage

Cons:
- Lacks theoretical guarantees; heuristic choices under-justified
- Potential misuse/miscalibration of statistical tests; independence assumptions questionable
- Presentation issues (figures, footnotes), and some ambiguous experimental descriptions
