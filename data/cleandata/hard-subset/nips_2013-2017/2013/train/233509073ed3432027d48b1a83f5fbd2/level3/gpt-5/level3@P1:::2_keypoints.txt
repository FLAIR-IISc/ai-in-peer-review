- Problem & approach: Proposes LoSST, an online, single-pass tracker for changing DAG structure using streaming sufficient statistics and episodic PC relearning.  
- Novel mechanism: Weights recent data by “surprisingness” using Hotelling’s T^2 p-values pooled via Liptak to form a running significance ρ that triggers re-learning.  
- Relation to prior work: Targets online nonstationarity distinct from batch score/constraint methods, TD-style trackers, and batch time-varying graph estimators.  
- Methodological strengths: Streaming updates for weighted means/covariances are memory-efficient and the episodic use of PC is reasonable.  
- Theoretical discussion: Offers a useful convergence vs. diligence analysis relating behavior to weight-scaling parameters (γ, T).  
- Statistical concerns: Hotelling’s T^2 assumes i.i.d. Gaussian samples and a well-defined sample size, but adaptive weighting violates these assumptions.  
- Dependence issue: Liptak pooling assumes independent p-values; successive pooled p-values from overlapping estimates are dependent and validity is unclear.  
- Calibration missing: Paper should assess false alarm rates and sensitivity under stationarity to quantify miscalibration risks.  
- Empirical strengths: Synthetic experiments show parity with PC under stationarity and faster adaptation after change; probabilistic relearning appears efficient; CPI case study is illustrative.  
- Missing baselines: Lacks comparisons to sliding-window PC, EWMA covariance + PC, BOCPD/CPD feeding windowed PC, and Talih–Hengartner-style time-varying methods.  
- Ablation gaps: Needs ablations on γ, T, ν and sensitivity analyses for dimensionality and covariance invertibility.  
- Clarity issues: Several technical choices (effective sample size update, mapping from ρ to weights, covariance inverse stabilization) are under-explained.  
- Presentation problems: Figures have small fonts/unclear captions; proof sketches should move to appendix and formal proofs or clearer heuristics provided.  
- Originality: Novel and practical synthesis for online DAG tracking; pooling+triggering idea and convergence–diligence viewpoint are interesting.  
- Significance: Potentially useful in finance/biology/monitoring if robustness and calibration are improved; single-pass efficiency is attractive.  
- Pros for acceptance: Tackles important problem, memory-efficient algorithm, matches batch PC under stationarity, adapts quickly, and includes real-data case study.  
- Cons for acceptance: Relies on heuristics without guarantees, potential misuses of T^2/Liptak under adaptive dependence, missing streaming baselines, and presentation gaps.  
- Key suggestions: Calibrate ρ/false-alarm rates, consider SPC/GLR alternatives, add missing baselines, run ablations over parameters, regularize covariances and report computational cost, and improve figures/proofs.  
- Overall assessment: Promising practical first attempt with compelling empirical signs but needs stronger theoretical justification, calibration, baselines, and clearer presentation to increase confidence.
