

This paper addresses an important but under-explored problem in graphical model learning: tracking time-varying dependency structures in streaming data. While NIPS has seen significant work on batch-mode structure learning (e.g., Chickering's optimal search, Spirtes' constraint-based methods), the authors correctly identify that real-world data often exhibit local stationarity rather than global stationarity. The proposed LoSST algorithm fills this gap by combining online sufficient statistic tracking with a probabilistic relearning mechanism triggered by "surprisingness" in the data stream.

The algorithm's core innovation lies in its efficient tracking of weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) using Mahalanobis distances to detect structural shifts. The authors cleverly adapt Hotelling's T² statistic to weighted samples and use Liptak's method for pooling p-values to form the significance metric ρ. The probabilistic relearning mechanism using a noisy-OR gate provides a practical solution to the convergence-diligence tradeoff identified in the literature (Kummerfeld & Danks). The approach effectively combines several established techniques into a novel framework for online structure learning.

The experimental evaluation demonstrates strong performance: LoSST matches batch-mode PC algorithm performance in stable regimes while significantly outperforming it during structural changes. The synthetic experiments across varying variable counts and graph degrees provide convincing evidence of robustness. The real-world application to US price index volatility is particularly compelling, successfully identifying the Great Moderation period and demonstrating robustness to short-term fluctuations (unlike naive approaches that would react to single-month spikes). This practical application significantly enhances the paper's significance.

The paper excels in clarity and organization, with a thorough literature review that properly situates the work within NIPS' previous contributions (notably citing Talih & Hengartner, Siracusa & Fisher). The writing is precise and accessible to the machine learning community.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong candidate for NIPS. First, it lacks theoretical guarantees about convergence or detection performance. While the authors acknowledge the convergence-diligence tradeoff, they don't provide any formal analysis of the algorithm's performance under specific assumptions about the data-generating process. This is particularly concerning given the heuristic nature of several design choices.

Second, the statistical methodology requires more careful justification. Applying Hotelling's T² to weighted samples and using Liptak's pooling method for dependent p-values (as the authors acknowledge in their footnotes) violates key assumptions of these techniques. The authors should explicitly state when they're using methods outside their theoretical guarantees and discuss the potential implications for their results.

Third, the paper's presentation issues significantly hinder comprehension. The figures (particularly Figures 1-3) are too small and poorly labeled, making it difficult to interpret the results. The experimental details (e.g., the "fixed graph relearning schedule" for LoSST) are inadequately explained, and the explanation for LoSST's worse performance on edge removal errors during parameter changes is unsatisfactory.

The paper's major strength is its practical approach to an important real-world problem with clear applications. The algorithm is computationally efficient, requires minimal overhead in stable environments, and demonstrates clear advantages in dynamic settings. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field by addressing this under-explored problem.

However, the lack of theoretical analysis, insufficient methodological justification, and poor figure presentation represent significant weaknesses that prevent this from being a strong NIPS submission. The heuristic-heavy design choices require more careful documentation and justification. While the paper presents an interesting and practical first attempt at online graphical structure learning, it would benefit substantially from addressing these limitations.

For acceptance considerations: The problem is significant and well-motivated, the approach is novel in its combination of techniques, and the experiments are generally convincing. However, the lack of theoretical guarantees and methodological weaknesses outweigh these strengths. The paper would be better suited for a more applied venue where theoretical guarantees are less critical, or for a future revision with stronger theoretical analysis and improved presentation.

Pro: Addresses an important, under-explored problem with clear real-world relevance.
Pro: Algorithm is practical, efficient, and well-motivated.
Pro: Strong experimental evaluation with synthetic and real-world data.
Con: Lacks theoretical analysis or guarantees for key algorithmic properties.
Con: Heuristic design choices lack sufficient justification.
Con: Statistical methodology requires careful re-evaluation for applicability to weighted samples.
Con: Poor figure presentation and insufficient experimental detail.
