This paper addresses the important and under-explored problem of learning graphical model structure from a data stream where the underlying generative process can change over time. Traditional structure learning algorithms, such as PC or score-based methods, assume data is i.i.d. and generated from a globally stationary distribution, an assumption often violated in real-world scenarios. The authors propose a novel algorithm, Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), to handle such locally stationary environments.

The core of LoSST is an online, single-pass procedure that tracks sufficient statistics for a Structural Equation Model (SEM)—specifically, the weighted means, covariance matrix, and an "effective sample size." The algorithm detects potential changes in the data-generating process by monitoring the "surprisingness" of each new data point, quantified by its Mahalanobis distance from the current estimated model. This distance is converted to a p-value using Hotelling's T^2 distribution. These sequential p-values are then pooled using Liptak's weighted method to produce a single significance statistic, ρ. This statistic guides two main processes: (1) it dynamically adjusts the weight of new data points, giving more influence to recent, surprising observations, and (2) it probabilistically triggers a full re-learning of the graphical structure using a batch algorithm (PC) on the current weighted sufficient statistics.

The authors evaluate LoSST on synthetic data where both the structure and parameters of an SEM are changed mid-stream, comparing it favorably to a standard batch PC algorithm which fails to adapt. They also demonstrate its utility on a real-world dataset of U.S. price indices, showing that it detects known macroeconomic shifts while remaining robust to short-lived fluctuations.

### Strengths

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a highly significant problem. The assumption of stationarity is a major limitation of most structure learning methods, and developing algorithms that can operate in dynamic environments is crucial for practical applications. The proposed LoSST algorithm is a novel and resourceful combination of streaming statistics, change-point detection principles, and batch learning. It represents a creative first step toward a practical solution.
*   **Practicality and Efficiency:** The algorithm is designed to be computationally and memory efficient. By processing each data point only once and maintaining a compact set of sufficient statistics, it is well-suited for streaming data and large datasets where batch processing is infeasible.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-conceived and provide good evidence for the algorithm's utility. The use of synthetic data with known ground truth allows for a clear comparison against a standard baseline (PC) across different types of changes (structural vs. parametric). The application to the U.S. price index data is a strong point, grounding the method in a real-world problem and showing that its detected change points correspond to known historical economic events.

### Weaknesses

*   **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary weakness of this work is its almost entirely heuristic nature. While the authors briefly discuss the concepts of "convergence" and "diligence," the paper lacks any formal theoretical analysis. There are no guarantees on detection probability, false alarm rate, or convergence under specific assumptions about the nature of the change. This makes it difficult to understand the fundamental limits and behavior of the algorithm beyond the specific empirical settings tested. This should be explicitly framed as a critical direction for future work.
*   **Heuristic Design and Justification:** Many of the core components of LoSST are based on ad-hoc decisions that are not sufficiently justified. For example:
    *   The update rule for the "effective sample size" (Eq. 3) and the specific functional form for re-weighting new data points (Eq. 4) are presented without clear motivation for why these specific forms were chosen over other possibilities.
    *   The probabilistic re-learning trigger, based on a noisy-OR of the pooled p-value, is another heuristic. Its properties and sensitivity to the parameter ν are not deeply explored.
*   **Potential Misuse of Statistical Methods:** The paper applies standard statistical tests in non-standard ways, which could be misleading.
    *   Hotelling's T^2 test assumes a sample size from i.i.d. data. Its application here with a weighted, heuristically defined "effective sample size" (Sr) is unconventional, and its statistical validity is not established.
    *   Liptak's method for pooling p-values requires the underlying tests to be independent. The sequential Mahalanobis distance tests are unlikely to be independent, as they are based on incrementally updated statistics. The authors should explicitly state that these methods are being used as well-performing heuristics outside of their proven theoretical regimes.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The figures are a major issue. They are too small, and the labels, legends, and axis ticks are often illegible (especially Figures 1 and 2), which significantly hinders the reader's ability to interpret the results.
    *   The experimental descriptions can be dense and difficult to parse (e.g., the fixed relearning schedule on page 7).
    *   Placing proof sketches in footnotes is poor form; this content is better suited for an appendix where it can be properly detailed.
    *   The explanation for LoSST's worse performance on edge removal errors after a parameter-only change is somewhat superficial. It attributes the effect to PC being over-eager to add edges while LoSST becomes more conservative. A more detailed analysis would be beneficial.

### Overall Assessment and Suggestions

This paper presents an interesting, novel, and practical approach to a challenging and important problem. The empirical results are promising and suggest the method has real potential. However, the work is let down by its lack of theoretical grounding and reliance on insufficiently motivated heuristics. As it stands, it is a strong proof-of-concept but requires more rigor to be considered a complete scientific contribution.

I would recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the following in their revision:

1.  **Acknowledge Limitations:** Be more transparent about the heuristic nature of the algorithm. Explicitly state that statistical tools like Hotelling's T^2 and Liptak's method are used as components in a heuristic change-detection mechanism, rather than as formal statistical tests under their standard assumptions.
2.  **Improve Justifications:** Provide more intuition or justification for the specific design choices (e.g., the update equations for weights and effective sample size). Even a short discussion of alternatives that were considered and discarded would strengthen the paper.
3.  **Enhance Presentation:** The figures must be remade to be legible. This is critical for a conference paper. Move detailed proofs from footnotes to supplementary material.
4.  **Future Work:** The conclusion should more strongly highlight the need for theoretical analysis as the most important next step for this line of research.
