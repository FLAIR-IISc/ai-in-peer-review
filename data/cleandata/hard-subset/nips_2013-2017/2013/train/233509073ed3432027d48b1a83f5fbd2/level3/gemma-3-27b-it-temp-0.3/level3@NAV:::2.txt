## Review of “Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure”

This paper tackles the important and largely unexplored problem of detecting changes in dependency structure within data streams, specifically in the context of Structural Equation Models (SEMs). The authors propose LoSST, a novel algorithm that combines batch PC structure learning with online tracking of streaming statistics. This is a valuable contribution, as existing structure learning algorithms largely assume stationarity, a limitation frequently violated in real-world applications. 

LoSST’s approach – tracking weighted means, correlation matrices, effective sample size, and utilizing Mahalanobis distance for reweighting – is creative and appears practical. The algorithm’s efficiency is a significant strength, as is its ability to perform comparably to batch learning when the underlying structure is stable. The experimental evaluation, including synthetic data with induced shifts and a real-world US price-index dataset, provides reasonable evidence of LoSST’s effectiveness. The demonstration of detecting known economic changes in the price index data is particularly compelling. The writing is generally clear and the literature review is thorough.

However, the paper suffers from a significant lack of theoretical grounding. While the empirical results are promising, the absence of convergence guarantees or even a discussion of the conditions under which LoSST might fail is a major weakness. The algorithm relies heavily on heuristics – the reweighting scheme, effective sample size calculation, and re-learning criteria – which are not fully justified and lack a strong theoretical basis. The use of Hotelling’s T2 and Liptak’s pooling method, potentially outside their established theoretical limits (given the weighted and effective sample sizes), raises concerns about the validity of the statistical inferences. The authors should explicitly acknowledge these limitations and discuss potential remedies in future work. 

Several smaller issues detract from the presentation. Excessive use of footnotes hinders readability; detailed proofs should be moved to supplementary material. Figures are too small and lack sufficient labeling, and experiment descriptions are occasionally unclear. The explanation for LoSST’s poorer performance on edge additions during parameter-only changes is unconvincing and warrants further investigation, potentially by decoupling parameter and structure updates. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Addresses an important, under-explored problem; presents a novel and practical algorithm; demonstrates promising empirical results on both synthetic and real data.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** Lacks theoretical analysis and guarantees; relies heavily on unjustified heuristics; potential misuse of statistical methods.



**Overall:** This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable first attempt at online structure learning. However, the lack of theoretical justification and the reliance on heuristics necessitate significant revisions before it can be considered a strong contribution. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage resubmission after addressing the identified weaknesses.
