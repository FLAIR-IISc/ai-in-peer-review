This paper proposes LoSST, a systematic method for structure learning in recursive SEMs under locally i.i.d. (time-varying) data. The core idea is to maintain sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an effective sample size) online with adaptive weights driven by pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances, thereby emphasizing recent data when model fit deteriorates. A probabilistic relearning schedule triggers constraint-based structure discovery (instantiated with PC). The paper positions LoSST relative to prior work on stationary structure learning, online changepoint detection, TD updates, and time-indexed graphical models (Talih & Hengartner; Siracusa & Fisher), arguing LoSST uniquely supports real-time structure tracking with directed models.

Quality: The methodology is technically sound and clearly derived; the connection between weighting, effective sample size, and pooled p-values is well justified. The convergence/diligence discussion is thoughtful and frames practical trade-offs. Experiments convincingly show LoSST matches batch PC under stationarity and outperforms after structural changes; the CPI case study illustrates real-data utility. However, claims of generality are not fully supported by experiments limited to PC. The statement that “other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly” is too strong without evidence; please either qualify this claim and clarify that experiments focus on PC, or add comparisons (e.g., sliding-window PC, GES/score-based with windows, CPD+reset baselines).

Clarity: The paper is well written and easy to follow. I recommend stating up front that the method is designed for linear-Gaussian SEMs and that extension to Bayesian networks is nontrivial because sufficient statistics for discrete CPTs are harder to maintain online. A brief algorithmic summary/pseudocode and a sensitivity analysis figure for T, γ, ν would further aid reproducibility.

Originality: The synthesis of pooled p-value–driven reweighting with online sufficient statistics and probabilistic relearning is novel and addresses an under-studied problem in time-varying structure learning.

Significance: The approach is simple, computationally attractive, and experimentally effective on PC, making it a promising baseline for learning with nonstationary data. Broader impact depends on demonstrating robustness across learners and model classes.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-motivated formulation of locally i.i.d. structure tracking for SEMs
- Simple, scalable mechanism; solid theory intuition (convergence vs. diligence)
- Strong synthetic results; plausible real-data case study

Cons / requests:
- Overgeneral claim about other learners; restrict or justify
- Scope to SEMs should be explicit; BN extensions likely nontrivial
- Limited baselines; add sliding-window/CPD-reset and a score-based method
- Provide sensitivity/ablation and computational cost breakdown
