This paper addresses the important and under-explored problem of learning graphical model structure from a data stream where the underlying generative process may change over time. Standard structure learning algorithms assume stationary data, which is often not the case in real-world applications. The authors propose the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel heuristic algorithm designed to track changes in both the structure and parameters of a Structural Equation Model (SEM) in an online fashion.

The core idea of LoSST is to maintain a set of weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, effective sample size). As new data points arrive, they are weighted based on how "surprising" they are, as measured by their Mahalanobis distance from the currently estimated distribution. The p-values from these distances are pooled over time using Liptak's method to create a statistic `ρ` that reflects the goodness-of-fit of the current model to the recent data history. A drop in this value suggests a change in the underlying data generating process, which in turn increases the probability of triggering a full relearning of the graphical structure using a standard batch algorithm (in this case, the PC algorithm). The algorithm is evaluated on synthetic data where both structure and parameters are changed, and its performance is compared favorably to a batch-mode PC algorithm. A case study on U.S. price index data demonstrates its ability to detect known macroeconomic shifts.

### Strengths

*   **Significance & Originality:** The paper tackles a highly significant problem. Most structure learning research focuses on the i.i.d. setting, yet many real-world systems are dynamic. This work provides a practical first step towards online structure learning in non-stationary environments and opens up many avenues for future research.
*   **Novelty of Approach:** LoSST is a novel and practical algorithm. It creatively combines several existing statistical tools—Mahalanobis distance for anomaly detection, Hotelling's T-squared test, Liptak's method for p-value pooling, and a standard batch structure learner—into an efficient online framework. The design is computationally and memory efficient, processing each data point only once.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments, while based on simulations, are well-designed. They demonstrate that LoSST performs comparably to a batch learner in stationary settings (incurring little cost) and significantly outperforms it when the underlying structure changes. The inclusion of a real-world dataset, while illustrative, adds confidence in the method's practical utility.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, with a clear motivation and a good review of related work that effectively situates the contribution.

### Weaknesses

*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The primary weakness of the paper is its heavily heuristic nature and the lack of theoretical analysis. While the algorithm is intuitive, there are no guarantees on its performance (e.g., convergence in stationary periods, or rates of detection for changes of a certain magnitude). The discussion of "convergence vs. diligence" is interesting, but the proofs are relegated to footnotes and feel more like sketches than rigorous analysis. The authors should at least discuss these limitations and frame theoretical guarantees as a key direction for future work.
*   **Potential Misuse of Statistical Methods:** There are significant concerns about the statistical validity of how some components are used.
    1.  The p-value for the Mahalanobis distance is calculated using Hotelling's T-squared distribution, which assumes the covariance matrix is estimated from a sample of size N. Here, it is applied using a weighted "effective sample size" `Sr`. It is not clear that the T-squared distribution is appropriate under these conditions.
    2.  Liptak's method for pooling p-values assumes that the individual tests are independent. In this context, the sequence of Mahalanobis distances and their corresponding p-values are temporally correlated and dependent on the evolving estimated model, violating this core assumption.
    The authors should explicitly acknowledge that these methods are being used as heuristics outside of their established theoretical guarantees to avoid misleading readers.
*   **Ad-hoc Design Choices:** Several key components of the algorithm are defined by ad-hoc rules that lack clear justification. For example, the update rule for the effective sample size (Eq. 3), the specific functional form for updating the data point weights (Eq. 4), and the noisy-OR mechanism for triggering relearning all feel arbitrary. A more thorough explanation or ablation study justifying these specific choices would strengthen the paper.
*   **Presentation and Clarity Issues:**
    1.  The figures are too small and poorly labeled, making them difficult to interpret. For instance, in Figures 1 and 2, the legends are absent, and one must refer to the text to understand which line corresponds to which model complexity.
    2.  The experimental descriptions are sometimes imprecise. The fixed "graph relearning" schedule used for the first two simulations feels artificial and could be better explained.
    3.  The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on edge *removal* errors after a parameter-only change is not entirely convincing. The paper suggests this is because LoSST becomes more "cautious," but this warrants a deeper analysis. Perhaps decoupling parameter updates from structure updates could be a more robust approach.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and important problem in a practical way.
*   The proposed algorithm, LoSST, is an original and clever synthesis of existing methods.
*   The algorithm is computationally efficient and suitable for online applications.
*   Empirical results on both synthetic and real data are promising and demonstrate the utility of the approach.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Major concerns regarding the statistical soundness of the approach, particularly the application of Hotelling's T-squared and Liptak's method.
*   The algorithm is highly heuristic, with many ad-hoc design choices that are not well-justified.
*   The paper lacks any theoretical analysis or performance guarantees.
*   Significant presentation issues with figures and experimental clarity.

In conclusion, this paper presents an interesting and practical first attempt at a challenging and important problem. However, the significant concerns about the statistical validity of its core components and its heavily heuristic nature without theoretical backing detract from the contribution. While the empirical results are promising, the technical foundations need to be strengthened considerably.
