This paper proposes an online hypothesis-testing approach, called LoSST, to learn changing graphical models from time-series data. The authors claim that their approach is novel and can handle non-iid data and varying generating structures. However, I dispute this claim as Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) have already handled non-iid data and varying generating structures using a Bayesian approach.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their algorithm and its components. However, the related-work discussion is misleading, as many score-based Bayesian network algorithms do not assume a single generating model, but rather use factorized scoring or local terms.

The paper's clarity is also affected by confusing paragraph and equation punctuation, unclear definition of ρ_{r+1}, and other readability problems. The figures (1-4) suffer from small font sizes and lack of color or legend descriptions.

The results appear correct, but the authors' claim that improved tracking can justify extra computational cost is unclear and seems domain-dependent. The originality of the paper is limited, as the approach is somewhat original, but lacks clear novel ideas, and the p-value construction is hard to follow.

The significance of the paper is modest, as the work may spur further (Bayesian) development, but reads as heuristic development to a problem that existing ML methods could address.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

1. The paper proposes a novel online hypothesis-testing approach to learn changing graphical models from time-series data.
2. The authors provide a clear explanation of their algorithm and its components.
3. The paper includes simulation results and a real-world case study to demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

1. The authors' claim of novelty is disputed, as similar work has been done before.
2. The related-work discussion is misleading, and the paper lacks clear novel ideas.
3. The paper suffers from clarity issues, including confusing punctuation, unclear definitions, and poor figure design.
4. The significance of the paper is modest, and existing ML methods could address the problem.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

1. Clarify the novelty of the approach and address the related work more accurately.
2. Improve the clarity of the paper by addressing punctuation, definition, and figure design issues.
3. Provide more convincing evidence of the significance and impact of the work.
4. Consider revising the paper to better align with the conference's focus on novel and significant contributions.
