This paper proposes an online hypothesis-testing approach to learn changing graphical models from time-series data. The authors introduce the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm, which tracks changes in graphical model structure or parameters in a dynamic, real-time manner. The algorithm is evaluated on both simulated and real datasets.

The main idea of the paper is to develop an online learning algorithm that can detect and adapt to changes in the underlying generating structure of the data. The authors argue that current online learning algorithms are not capable of general, graphical model structure learning, and that their approach fills this gap. The LoSST algorithm assumes that the data are locally i.i.d. and learns graphical model structure in an online fashion.

The paper is related to previous work on graphical models, including Bayesian networks and recursive Structural Equation Models (SEMs). The authors discuss the limitations of standard graphical model structure learning algorithms, which assume that the data come from a single generating structure. They also mention previous papers that have aimed to learn time-indexed graph structures from time-series data, but note that these approaches require full datasets as input and cannot function in real-time.

The LoSST algorithm is based on the idea of tracking the locally stationary sufficient statistics for recursive SEMs, including the means, covariances, and sample size. The algorithm uses a weighted mean and covariance update equation to track the changing generating structure. The weights are based on the "fit" between the current estimated covariance matrix and the input data, with poor fit implying that a change in the underlying generating structure is more likely.

The authors evaluate the performance of LoSST using synthetic data and compare it to a standard batch-mode learning algorithm. The results show that LoSST performs comparably to batch-mode learning when the generating graphical structure is globally stationary, and significantly better when it is only locally stationary.

The paper also includes an application to US price index volatility data, which demonstrates the ability of LoSST to detect changes in the underlying generating structure.

However, the reviewer disputes the authors' claim of novelty, noting that Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) already handle non-iid data and varying generating structures. The reviewer also notes that the related-work discussion is misleading, as many score-based BN algorithms do not assume a single generating model, only factorized scoring/local terms.

The paper is also unclear about the precise learning task (detecting change points vs inferring the current generating structure) and whether it proposes a new model, algorithm, or both. Additionally, the presentation has several issues, including confusing paragraph/end-of-equation punctuation, unclear definition of ρ_{r+1}, and other readability problems.

The figures are also difficult to read due to small font sizes and lack of color/legend descriptions. Furthermore, the claim in Section 6 that improved tracking can justify extra computational cost is unclear and seems domain-dependent.

In terms of originality, the approach is somewhat original, but it lacks clear novel ideas and the p-value construction is hard to follow. The significance of the work is modest, as it may spur further (Bayesian) development but reads as heuristic development to a problem existing ML methods could address.

Overall, while the paper proposes an interesting approach to online learning of graphical models, it has several limitations and areas for improvement.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to online learning of graphical models.
* The LoSST algorithm is evaluated on both simulated and real datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness.
* The paper includes an application to US price index volatility data, which demonstrates the ability of LoSST to detect changes in the underlying generating structure.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's claim of novelty is disputed, as Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) already handle non-iid data and varying generating structures.
* The related-work discussion is misleading, as many score-based BN algorithms do not assume a single generating model, only factorized scoring/local terms.
* The paper is unclear about the precise learning task and whether it proposes a new model, algorithm, or both.
* The presentation has several issues, including confusing paragraph/end-of-equation punctuation, unclear definition of ρ_{r+1}, and other readability problems.
* The figures are difficult to read due to small font sizes and lack of color/legend descriptions.
* The claim in Section 6 that improved tracking can justify extra computational cost is unclear and seems domain-dependent.
* The originality of the approach is limited, and the p-value construction is hard to follow.
* The significance of the work is modest, as it may spur further (Bayesian) development but reads as heuristic development to a problem existing ML methods could address.

Rating: 6/10

Recommendation: The paper has some interesting ideas, but it needs significant revisions to address the issues mentioned above. The authors should clarify the precise learning task, improve the presentation, and provide more details on the p-value construction. Additionally, they should acknowledge the previous work of Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) and clarify the novelty of their approach.
