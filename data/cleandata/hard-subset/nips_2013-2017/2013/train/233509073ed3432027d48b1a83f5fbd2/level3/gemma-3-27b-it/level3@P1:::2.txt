This paper addresses the important and relatively unexplored problem of tracking changes in graphical model structure in a dynamic, real-time manner, moving beyond the assumption of stationarity common in traditional structure learning algorithms. The authors propose LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm that leverages online sufficient statistic tracking and periodically re-learns the graphical model structure using a batch-mode approach (specifically, the PC algorithm) triggered by detected changes. The core idea of weighting data points based on their “surprisingness” via Mahalanobis distance is a creative way to adapt to shifting dependencies. The authors demonstrate LoSST's performance through synthetic experiments and a real-world application to US price index data, showing its ability to detect structure changes and outperform standard batch learning in non-stationary environments. The paper is well-written and provides a good overview of related work.

LoSST represents a significant contribution due to its novelty and practicality. The algorithm's efficiency, stemming from the combination of online statistic tracking and episodic batch learning, makes it suitable for large datasets and real-time applications. The clear explanation of the algorithm's components – weighted means, covariance updates, effective sample size, and the re-learning mechanism based on Mahalanobis distance and Liptak’s pooling – demonstrates a thoughtful integration of existing techniques.  The extensive simulations, encompassing shifts in both structure and parameters, alongside the analysis of a real-world dataset, provide adequate experimental validation.  The literature review is comprehensive and accurately positions the work within the broader field of graphical model learning and online changepoint detection.

However, the paper’s reliance on heuristics and the lack of theoretical analysis are significant weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge the trade-off between convergence and diligence, they do not provide any theoretical guarantees on the algorithm’s performance, such as convergence rates, detection delays, or false positive rates. At a minimum, a discussion of the assumptions necessary for these properties to hold would greatly strengthen the work. The numerous ad-hoc decisions made in the algorithm’s design – the reweighting scheme based on Mahalanobis distance, the update rules for effective sample size, and the criteria for triggering re-learning – lack thorough justification and could benefit from sensitivity analysis beyond what is presented.  

Furthermore, the statistical validity of certain methods is questionable when applied in the context of LoSST. The use of Hotelling's T2 distribution to compute p-values for the Mahalanobis distance may be inappropriate given the use of weighted and effectively-sized samples, which violates assumptions of the test. Similarly, Liptak's weighted p-value pooling assumes independent tests, an assumption that may not hold when assessing the “surprisingness” of consecutive data points within a time series.  The authors should explicitly state when methods are used outside their theoretical guarantees and discuss the potential implications.  This transparency is crucial to prevent misleading readers about the robustness of the results.

Several aspects of the presentation could be improved. The extensive use of footnotes for proofs disrupts the flow of the text; detailed proofs should be moved to the supplementary material. The figures are too small, poorly labeled, and their captions lack sufficient detail to fully understand the results. The descriptions of the experiments, particularly on page 7, are unclear and need further elaboration. The explanation for LoSST's initial poorer performance on edge removal errors during parameter-only changes is unconvincing; exploring a decoupling of parameter updates from structural updates could provide a more insightful explanation. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a novel and important problem.
*   Proposes a practical and efficient algorithm.
*   Provides adequate experimental validation with synthetic and real-world data.
*   Well-written and clearly explains the key concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks theoretical analysis and guarantees.
*   Relies heavily on heuristics without sufficient justification.
*   Potential misuse of statistical methods with unclear implications.
*   Presentation issues (figures, experiment descriptions, footnotes).



**Overall:**  This paper presents an interesting and promising first attempt at addressing the problem of tracking time-varying graphical structures. However, the lack of theoretical grounding, the reliance on heuristics, and the presentation issues raise concerns. I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The authors should focus on providing a more rigorous justification for their design choices, conducting a more thorough theoretical analysis, and improving the clarity and presentation of the results. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
