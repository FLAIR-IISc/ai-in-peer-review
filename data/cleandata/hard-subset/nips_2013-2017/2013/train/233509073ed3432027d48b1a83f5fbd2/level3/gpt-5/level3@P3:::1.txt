Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LoSST, an online method for tracking changes in graphical model structure for continuous variables modeled as recursive SEMs (linear Gaussian DAGs). The core idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) with per-datapoint weights that increase when the incoming data look unlikely under the current model. Unlikeliness is quantified via a pooled p-value constructed from a running sequence of Mahalanobis-distance p-values (Hotelling’s T2), combined by Liptak’s method. A probabilistic trigger (increasing with 1 − pooled p-value) schedules when to re-run a standard structure learner (PC) on the current weighted sufficient statistics. The paper analyzes convergence/diligence trade-offs and evaluates on synthetic switching-SEM data (structure and parameter changes) and a CPI volatility case study.

The paper positions itself as addressing non-iid data and changing generating structures, arguing that standard BN learners assume a single generating model. This is misleading and undersells prior art. In particular, Siracusa & Fisher (AISTATS 2009) explicitly infer time-varying DAGs for nonstationary data in a Bayesian framework, and Talih & Hengartner (2005) learn time-varying undirected graphs. Moreover, many score-based BN methods only require decomposability/factorized scores; they can be applied with sliding windows or discounting/forgetting to accommodate nonstationarity. Online changepoint detection (e.g., Adams & MacKay) can be combined with BN learners to address the same problem class.

Quality
- Correctness and methodology: The online updates for weighted means/covariances are standard and appear correct; the effective-sample-size recursion is reasonable though heuristic. The p-value pooling and relearning trigger are also heuristic. The convergence/diligence discussion is interesting but based on sketches and parameter-specific arguments. Claims about “no extra cost” in stationary regimes are supported empirically in the simulations that impose a fixed relearning schedule.
- Baselines: The main comparison is against batch PC on the full dataset. More informative baselines would include (i) sliding-window PC, (ii) exponentially discounted sufficient statistics with fixed forget rates, and (iii) BOCPD + (re)learning, or (iv) time-varying/penalized Gaussian graphical models (for the linear-Gaussian case). Without these, it is hard to assess the incremental benefit of the pooled-p-value mechanism beyond standard forgetting.
- Statistical calibration: The Liptak pooling assumes some independence/known dependence among test statistics; here, Mahalanobis distances are computed from overlapping, adaptively weighted estimates, so dependence and adaptivity may affect calibration. The paper would benefit from discussion or empirical calibration (e.g., false alarm rates under stationarity).
- Ambiguity of task: It is not entirely clear whether the objective is (a) changepoint detection, (b) tracking/inferring the current structure at each time, or (c) both; the algorithm and evaluations lean toward (b), while parts of the text read as (a). Clarifying the learning objective and evaluation metrics accordingly would help.
- Minor technical issues: The Hotelling T2 parameterization appears inconsistent: p should be the dimensionality V, not N; m should be tied to the (effective) sample size S − 1. The definition and recursion for ρr+1 are difficult to follow; the non-circularity footnote helps, but the notation (using r vs r + 1) is easy to misread.

Clarity
- The paper is readable at a high level but suffers from notational confusions (e.g., ρr+1, T2 parameters), punctuation around equations, and occasional ambiguities (e.g., whether learning a “model” means a new probabilistic model, an algorithmic scheme, or both). Section 3 would benefit from a clean algorithm box specifying inputs/outputs/state and an explicit, index-consistent recursion for all tracked quantities.
- Figures 1–4 have small fonts and under-explained legends/colors; include colorblind-safe palettes, axis labels, and legend entries described in the captions.

Originality
- The problem setting (time-varying graphical models, non-iid, online tracking) is established in prior work. The proposed combination—adaptive per-datapoint weighting via pooled p-values driving occasional relearning with a standard constraint-based learner—is somewhat original as an engineering approach, but it does not introduce a clearly novel theoretical idea. The construction of the pooled p-value is the most distinctive piece, yet is hard to parse and not convincingly justified statistically.

Significance
- Practical significance is modest without stronger baselines. The method may stimulate follow-on work (e.g., Bayesian or likelihood-based versions with principled discounting), but as presented it reads as a heuristic that addresses a problem existing ML tools can also tackle. The real-data CPI example is suggestive but not decisive, as no external ground truth is available and alternative explanations/baselines are not explored. The claim in Section 6 that improved tracking can justify additional computational/memory costs seems domain-dependent and would benefit from empirical cost–benefit analysis.

Detailed comments and suggestions
- Clarify the precise learning task: Is the goal to output the current best-guess graph at each time, detect changes, or both? Align evaluation metrics accordingly (e.g., detection delay, false alarms, and structural Hamming distance over time).
- Fix the Hotelling T2 parameterization and clearly define how “effective” sample size enters; consider a small appendix deriving the exact distributional assumptions under adaptive weighting.
- Rework the ρr recursion to eliminate index confusion. Consider presenting it as maintaining a running z-score and a sum of squared weights; explain assumptions about dependence among p-values and provide calibration (e.g., via simulation) of false alarm rates.
- Add stronger baselines: sliding-window PC; exponential forgetting; BOCPD + relearning; time-varying graphical lasso (for Gaussian case). Include ablations: (i) simple exponential forgetting vs pooled-p-value weighting; (ii) fixed vs probabilistic relearning; (iii) sensitivity to T, γ, ν.
- Report computational costs (per step and cumulative) and the trade-off governed by ν; include wall-clock time and number of relearnings.
- Improve presentation: fix punctuation at end of equations, standardize notation (p vs V, N vs S), define all symbols on first use, and enlarge figure fonts with clear legends/captions.
- Temper novelty claims and properly acknowledge Siracusa & Fisher (AISTATS 2009) as handling non-iid and time-varying DAGs, and that many score-based BN approaches can be adapted to nonstationary data via windows/discounting.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and practical problem (online tracking of changing structures).
- Simple, single-pass implementation with broadly understandable components.
- Empirically performs well relative to batch PC in switching settings.
- Interesting discussion of convergence vs diligence that may inspire follow-up work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Overstates novelty; related-work discussion is misleading.
- Heuristic core (pooled p-values and triggers) lacks strong justification or calibration.
- Missing strong baselines; significance is unclear relative to standard forgetting/windowing or BOCPD+learning.
- Clarity issues (notation, ρr+1, figure readability) and some technical inconsistencies.

Overall assessment
Promising direction and sensible engineering, but given limited originality, missing baselines, and clarity/justification gaps, I lean toward rejection in its current form. Strengthening the empirical comparisons, clarifying/statistically grounding the weighting scheme, and improving presentation would substantially increase the paper’s impact.
