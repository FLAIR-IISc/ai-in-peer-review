This paper proposes LoSST, an online, heuristic framework for tracking time-varying graphical structure in linear-Gaussian SEMs. The core idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) where the per-datum weight ar adapts based on a pooled p-value of recent Mahalanobis distances; when those distances collectively indicate poor fit under the current model, weights increase to emphasize recent data and accelerate adaptation. Structure is relearned intermittently (using the PC algorithm) with a probability that increases when pooled p-values fall. The authors analyze convergence/diligence trade-offs as a function of parameters T and γ, provide simulations with both structural and parameter changes, and include a CPI case study that appears to flag known macroeconomic regime shifts.

Relation to prior work: The paper positions LoSST against batch score- and constraint-based learners, online changepoint detection (CPD), and previous time-varying graphical models (Talih & Hengartner, Siracusa & Fisher). However, the novelty claims are overstated. Siracusa & Fisher (AISTATS’09) explicitly infer time-varying DAG structure, albeit in batch Bayesian form. Dynamic and time-varying graphical models (e.g., DBNs, time-varying GMRFs) and windowed or forgetting-factor variants of standard scores provide natural comparators. Moreover, many score-based methods do not inherently assume a single global model; rather, they assume i.i.d. data within whatever window is scored. They can be paired with sliding windows/discounting or CPD to adapt to nonstationarity. The paper would benefit from a clearer, fairer positioning relative to these lines of work and stronger baseline comparisons.

Strengths
- Focuses on a practically important setting: locally stationary generative processes with changing structure/parameters.
- Simple, computationally lightweight sufficient-statistics updates (means, covariances, effective sample size) and an intuitive change-sensitivity mechanism based on pooled p-values.
- Clear articulation of a convergence–diligence trade-off and parameters that control it; useful conceptual lens rarely made explicit in the literature.
- Simulation results show LoSST matches batch PC when stationary and adapts faster after changes; the CPI case study is compelling and highlights the value of pooling p-values rather than reacting to single outliers.
- The probabilistic relearning schedule reduces compute without large accuracy penalties.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited; the method is a heuristic assembly of known components (EWMA-style weighting, Mahalanobis distances, Liptak’s p-value pool, PC relearning), and prior work already addresses time-varying structure (e.g., Siracusa & Fisher; time-varying GGMs/DBNs).
- Related-work discussion is misleading: it implies score-based BN learners “assume a single generating structure,” whereas in practice they assume local i.i.d. within a dataset/window and can be made adaptive via discounting or CPD+relearn.
- The precise learning objective is underspecified: is the goal online changepoint detection, online estimation of the current graph, or joint tracking? The algorithm couples both, but the evaluation and claims sometimes emphasize detection and sometimes current-structure accuracy.
- Clarity/presentation issues: confusing punctuation around equations; the definition of ρr+1 is hard to follow and index-specifics (which p-values, which weights) are not crisply stated despite a footnote about circularity; figure fonts and legends are too small/underspecified.
- Methodological gaps: no discussion of covariance regularization when Sr is small or p is large; no calibration of false-alarm/false-dismissal trade-offs; limited sensitivity analysis for T, γ, ν; reliance on Gaussianity/linearity without robustness checks; no runtime results.
- Baselines are weak: comparisons are primarily to batch PC on full data. Missing are sliding-window PC, CPD+relearn (Bayesian or frequentist), adaptive/forgetting-factor score-based search, and time-varying graphical lasso for Gaussian models, which are natural, competitive alternatives.

Quality
- The algorithmic components are standard and correctly assembled; the convergence/diligence observations are plausible and partially justified. However, theory is informal (proof sketches) and important practicalities (regularization, CI-test calibration under weighting, multiple testing) are not addressed.
- Simulations are sensible, but comparative evaluation is narrow, and hyperparameters are “unsystematically” chosen; ablations and sensitivity analyses are limited.
- The real-data study is interesting but qualitative; no external validation or ground-truth proxy is provided.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but several definitions (ρr, pr, Sr) and indexing choices need to be made precise; punctuation at line breaks around equations is distracting.
- Figures 1–4 require larger fonts, clear legends, and colorblind-safe palettes; axes and units should be labeled consistently.

Originality
- Moderate. The online pooling of p-values to modulate forgetting and trigger relearning is a reasonable twist, but the building blocks are well known, and prior work targets similar goals, including time-varying structures.

Significance
- Modest. The approach may encourage more principled online structure tracking, but as presented it feels heuristic and does not clearly advance state of the art over strong adaptive baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the task definition and evaluation objectives (detection vs current-structure accuracy) and report both metrics.
- Precisely define ρr (indexing, included p-values, weights) and provide a short derivation or appendix; discuss why Liptak vs Fisher/Stouffer and how this choice affects sensitivity.
- Add covariance regularization (e.g., ridge/shrinkage) and report behavior when p is large relative to Sr.
- Compare against: (a) CPD (Bayesian/frequentist) + relearn with PC; (b) sliding-window/forgetting-factor score-based search (e.g., BIC with exponential weights); (c) time-varying graphical lasso for Gaussian models; (d) kernel change detection (Desobry et al.) + relearn. Include runtime.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν and a principled tuning strategy (e.g., controlling false discovery rate of change).
- Improve figures (fonts, legends) and fix punctuation/notation issues.
- Temper novelty claims; better position relative to Siracusa & Fisher and time-varying graphical models.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored online setting with a simple, practical method.
- Empirically promising, with clear wins after changes and negligible cost under stationarity.
- The convergence–diligence framing is insightful and potentially influential.

Arguments against acceptance
- Overstated novelty and incomplete related-work treatment.
- Heuristic method with limited theory, unclear statistical calibration, and weak baselines.
- Clarity and presentation issues hinder reproducibility.

Recommendation: Borderline reject. The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the paper needs clearer positioning, stronger baselines, sharper definitions, and better empirical and statistical grounding to meet the bar.
