## Review of “Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure”

This paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic algorithm for online structure learning of graphical models in non-stationary environments. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics and dynamically relearn the graphical model structure as indicated by changes in data fit, assessed via a p-value-based weighting scheme. The authors evaluate LoSST on simulated data exhibiting structural changes and a real-world dataset of US price indices. While the results appear correct and the approach is a reasonable attempt at tackling a difficult problem, I have concerns regarding novelty, clarity, and significance.

A primary issue is the claim of novelty. The authors state previous work is insufficient, but fail to adequately acknowledge related work. Specifically, Siracusa and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) already address learning directed graphical models from time-series data with varying generating structures using a Bayesian approach. Furthermore, the authors’ characterization of existing score-based BN algorithms as assuming a single generating model is misleading; many rely on factorized scoring functions which inherently allow for local adaptation. 

The paper’s objective is also somewhat unclear. Is the goal detecting change points, inferring the *current* generating structure, or both? This ambiguity complicates understanding the contribution – is it a new model, a new algorithm, or a novel combination of existing techniques? This lack of clarity extends to the definition of ρ_{r+1} and some paragraph/equation punctuation which hinders readability. Figures 1-4 suffer from small font sizes and lack informative captions or legends.

The authors argue that improved tracking justifies potential computational costs (Section 6), but this justification feels domain-dependent and lacks strong support. While the simulation results demonstrate LoSST’s ability to track changes, the advantage over simpler adaptive methods isn’t convincingly demonstrated. The originality of the p-value construction feels limited, and its rationale is difficult to follow. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** The problem of online structure learning is important and relevant. The proposed approach offers a potentially useful heuristic for tracking changing graphical models. The simulations provide evidence of functionality.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Limited novelty due to overlooking relevant prior work. Unclear objective and contribution. Readability issues and poor figure presentation. Weak justification for computational cost. Modest significance – the work feels like a heuristic extension to existing methods rather than a significant breakthrough.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend **rejection**. While the work is not fundamentally flawed, the lack of demonstrable novelty, clarity issues, and modest significance do not warrant acceptance to a competitive conference like NIPS. I suggest the authors address the related work gaps, clarify the paper's contribution, improve readability and presentation, and provide a more compelling justification for the proposed approach's advantages.
