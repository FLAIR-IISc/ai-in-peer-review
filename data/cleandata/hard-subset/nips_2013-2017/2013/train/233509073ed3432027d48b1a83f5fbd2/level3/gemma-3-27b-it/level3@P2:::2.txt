This paper tackles the important and largely under-explored problem of detecting changes in dependency structure over a data stream, specifically within the context of Structural Equation Models (SEMs). The authors propose LoSST, a novel and practical algorithm that cleverly combines standard batch-mode PC structure learning with a mechanism for triggering re-learning based on tracked streaming statistics. The core idea – tracking weighted means, covariance matrices, effective sample size, and weighting new data points by their “surprisingness” as measured by the Mahalanobis distance – is well-motivated and presents a compelling approach to online structure learning.

The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of related work in graphical model structure learning, online change point detection, and time-series analysis. The literature review is thorough, properly situating LoSST within the existing landscape and highlighting its contributions. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, making the algorithm’s mechanics reasonably accessible. The authors also appropriately acknowledge limitations and suggest promising avenues for future research.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The problem addressed is genuinely important, as many real-world systems exhibit non-stationary dynamics. The proposed algorithm offers a practical solution where traditional batch methods fail.
*   **Algorithm Design:** LoSST is a creative and efficient combination of existing techniques. The use of the Mahalanobis distance for change detection, coupled with the Liptak p-value pooling method and a noisy-OR gate for triggering re-learning, is a well-considered design.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The simulations are adequate, covering scenarios with shifts in both parameters and structure, across varying numbers of variables and degrees. The inclusion of a real-world dataset (US price index data) adds further credibility.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the algorithm’s rationale and implementation details.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Theoretical Analysis:** The most significant weakness is the complete absence of theoretical analysis. While the algorithm’s performance is demonstrated empirically, there are no guarantees regarding its convergence, detection delay, or false positive rate under specific assumptions. Even a rudimentary analysis would significantly strengthen the paper. The authors acknowledge this in the discussion but should more explicitly state it as future work.
*   **Heuristic Design:** The algorithm relies heavily on heuristics, and many design decisions (reweighting scheme, effective sample size calculation, re-learning criteria) appear somewhat ad-hoc. The justification for these choices is not always clear, and a more systematic exploration of their impact would be beneficial.
*   **Statistical Validity:** The application of statistical methods, particularly Hotelling’s T<sup>2</sup> to weighted/effective sample sizes and Liptak’s pooling method, raises concerns. These methods have theoretical guarantees under specific conditions (e.g., independent tests), which may not hold in this context. The authors should explicitly acknowledge when methods are used outside their theoretical boundaries to avoid potentially misleading readers.
*   **Presentation of Results:** Figures are too small and poorly labeled, making it difficult to extract meaningful information. Captions and legends lack sufficient detail. Experiment descriptions (e.g., on page 7) are often unclear.
*   **Edge Removal Explanation:** The explanation for LoSST's poorer performance on edge removal errors during parameter-only changes is unconvincing. Decoupling parameter updates from structure updates might alleviate this issue.
*   **Footnote Overload:** The excessive use of footnotes for proofs detracts from the flow of the paper. Detailed proofs should be moved to supplementary material.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant gap in the literature on graphical model learning.
*   Presents a novel and practical algorithm with promising empirical results.
*   Demonstrates the algorithm's effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of theoretical analysis limits the paper’s contribution and raises concerns about its robustness.
*   Heavy reliance on heuristics and unclear justification for design choices.
*   Potential misuse of statistical methods without adequate justification.
*   Presentation of results and experiment descriptions need improvement.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable first attempt at addressing the challenging problem of online structure learning for SEMs. However, the lack of theoretical grounding, reliance on heuristics, and concerns regarding statistical validity prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance in its current form. I believe the paper has merit and could be a significant contribution with substantial revisions. I recommend **rejection** with a request for major revisions. The authors should prioritize providing theoretical analysis (even if limited), justifying their heuristic choices more rigorously, and clarifying the statistical assumptions underlying their methods. Improvements in presentation and experimental detail would also greatly enhance the paper’s impact.
