This paper introduces LoSST, a heuristic online algorithm to track time-varying graphical structure in linear-Gaussian SEMs. The core idea is to maintain, in a single pass, weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) that adapt to nonstationarity via a per-datum weight ar. Weights are increased when recent data look unlikely under the current model, using Mahalanobis distances combined across time via Liptak’s pooled p-value. When the pooled p-value drops, LoSST increases weights (and thus forgets older data faster), and triggers graph relearning with a probability that depends on how unlikely the recent data are. Structure is re-estimated with PC. The paper relates to (i) classical score- and constraint-based structure learning (which assume stationarity), (ii) TD learning and other methods with exponential forgetting, (iii) online change-point detection (BOCPD and frequentist variants), and (iv) time-indexed structure learning methods that operate in batch mode (Talih & Hengartner; Siracusa & Fisher). The authors provide simulation studies demonstrating parity with batch PC under stationarity and substantially better performance after changes in parameters or graph, as well as a case study on US price index volatility. They also discuss the tradeoff between convergence and diligence and provide conditions under which LoSST has each property.

Quality: The algorithmic components are simple, computationally lightweight (O(p^2) state for sufficient statistics), and clearly described. The derivations for online updates of means/covariances and the ar transformation are sensible; the use of Liptak pooling to detect sustained departures from stationarity is well motivated. The convergence/diligence discussion is valuable, though some proofs are sketches. The experimental design isolates key questions (before/after changepoints; structure vs parameter changes; stochastic relearning) and the results are consistent with the method’s intent. However, calibration of statistical tests under time-varying weighting is not fully justified: PC’s CI tests typically assume Wishart-distributed sample covariance (i.i.d., fixed N), whereas LoSST uses weighted covariances and an “effective” N. It would help to justify or empirically validate CI test calibration under weighted statistics (e.g., via coverage or Type I/II error under null/alternative). Sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν are limited; the paper reports unsystematic tuning. Computational cost per step and wall-clock comparisons are not reported.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The assumptions (linear-Gaussian SEMs; local i.i.d. within short windows) are mostly explicit, but the scope should be stated up front: the algorithm as implemented is for continuous linear SEMs with PC, and extensions to discrete BN structure learning are nontrivial because maintaining sufficient statistics and CI tests is harder. The “effective sample size” update deserves more intuition and justification. It would also help to detail exactly which CI tests and significance levels PC uses with weighted covariances, and any regularization for covariance inversion.

Originality: The combination of (i) pooled p-values on Mahalanobis distances to drive adaptive forgetting, (ii) an online sufficient-statistics tracker, and (iii) probabilistic graph relearning is novel in the graphical structure learning literature. While related ideas exist in TD/EWMA and CPD, LoSST provides a tailored mechanism to plug into constraint-based structure learning in real time.

Significance: Many application domains require structure learning under nonstationarity and streaming data. A practical, simple method that matches batch performance under stationarity and improves after changes is valuable. That said, the empirical scope is narrow (PC-only comparison) and baselines could be stronger: e.g., sliding-window PC, exponentially weighted covariances with fixed forgetting, or CPD+batch relearning pipelines. Stronger baselines and calibration checks would increase confidence in broader impact.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and under-studied problem: online structure learning under local stationarity.
- Simple, modular mechanism that can be combined with existing CI-based learners; single-pass updates.
- Clear articulation of convergence vs diligence tradeoffs with parameter regimes.
- Promising simulations and an illustrative real-world case study.
- Well written, easy to follow.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope and claims: The statement that “other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly” is too strong without evidence. Please rephrase to emphasize that experiments evaluate PC, or provide additional results (e.g., FCI for latent confounding or score-based GES with a streaming-friendly score).
- SEM specificity: State explicitly in the introduction that the implemented method targets linear-Gaussian SEMs with PC; extensions to Bayesian networks/discrete variables are nontrivial due to sufficient statistics and CI-test calibration.
- CI test calibration: Justify the use of weighted covariances and effective sample size within PC. Are Fisher z tests and partial correlations correctly calibrated under weighting? Consider empirical calibration (null-simulation Type I error) or alternative CI tests robust to weighting.
- Baselines: Add sliding-window PC and EWMA-PC with fixed forgetting factors; BOCPD (or frequentist CPD) followed by batch PC after detected changepoints; and a naive “relearn every K steps” baseline with tuned K. Report SHD, precision/recall, time-to-recovery after change, and runtime.
- Sensitivity/robustness: Provide systematic sweeps over T, γ, ν; analyze trade-offs between responsiveness and false alarms; study robustness to non-Gaussianity and outliers (e.g., using robust Mahalanobis or shrinkage covariance).
- Numerical stability: Discuss covariance regularization/shrinkage and matrix inversion stability when effective sample size shrinks; report any safeguards.
- Complexity: Report per-step and cumulative runtime, and how relearning frequency affects cost.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, practical contribution enabling online SEM structure learning with a clear mechanism to handle nonstationarity.
- Strong conceptual clarity and simple implementation; likely to be adopted/extended.
- Demonstrated parity with batch learning under stationarity and improvements when changes occur.

Arguments against acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is limited to PC; stronger baselines and calibration checks are missing.
- Some theoretical claims are only sketched; CI-test validity under weighting not fully justified.
- Parameter tuning and sensitivity analysis are limited.

Overall: I have read the authors’ feedback and my assessment incorporates their clarifications. With toned-down claims about generality, clearer statement of scope, and added empirical baselines/calibration, this would be a solid contribution. I lean toward weak accept.
