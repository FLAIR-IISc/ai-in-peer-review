## Review of “Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure”

This paper presents LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm for learning graphical model structure from locally i.i.d. (time-varying) data. The core idea – adapting standard structure learning techniques like the PC algorithm to an online setting by tracking sufficient statistics and dynamically relearning the graph – is a valuable contribution. The problem addressed is important and understudied, as most existing methods assume global stationarity, a limitation frequently encountered in real-world applications. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings, making it easy to follow. 

The authors effectively position their work within the existing literature on graphical model structure learning, Bayesian/score-based methods, and constraint-based approaches. They correctly identify the limitations of current online learning algorithms and changepoint detection methods for this specific task. The inclusion of a discussion on the trade-off between convergence and diligence is insightful and demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the challenges inherent in tracking dynamic systems. The simulation results are promising, demonstrating LoSST’s ability to track changes in both graph structure and parameters, and showing comparable performance to batch-mode learning when the underlying structure is stable. The application to US price index volatility provides a compelling, albeit preliminary, demonstration of the algorithm’s potential in a real-world setting.

However, the claim that “other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly” requires stronger justification. While plausible, it’s not universally true and the experiments primarily focus on the PC algorithm. A brief exploration with another algorithm (e.g., a score-based method) would strengthen this assertion. Furthermore, the paper acknowledges the difficulty of extending this approach to Bayesian networks due to the challenges of maintaining sufficient statistics. This limitation should be stated more prominently upfront, clarifying the SEM-specific scope of the work. While the authors mention this in the conclusion, emphasizing it earlier would manage expectations and prevent potential misinterpretations. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and under-explored problem in graphical model learning.
* Presents a clear, well-motivated, and technically sound algorithm.
* Demonstrates promising results in simulation and a preliminary real-world application.
* Provides a thoughtful discussion of the trade-offs inherent in online structure learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The claim of generalizability to other algorithms needs further support.
* The SEM-specific scope should be more clearly stated upfront.
* Experimental evaluation is limited to the PC algorithm.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further enhance its impact and make it a compelling addition to the conference proceedings. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
