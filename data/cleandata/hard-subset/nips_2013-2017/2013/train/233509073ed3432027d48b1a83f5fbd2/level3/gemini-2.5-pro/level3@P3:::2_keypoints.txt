- Tackles learning graphical model structure from nonstationary data streams via a novel online algorithm (LoSST).  
- LoSST maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariance, effective sample size) in a single pass.  
- Detects changes by Mahalanobis distance per point, converting to p-values using Hotelling’s T^2.  
- Pools sequential p-values with Liptak’s weighted method to produce a global significance ρ.  
- Uses ρ to reweight new data (favoring recent surprising points) and probabilistically trigger batch re-learning (PC).  
- Empirical evaluation on synthetic data shows LoSST adapts to mid-stream structural and parametric changes better than batch PC.  
- Real-world U.S. price index experiment shows detected shifts align with known macroeconomic events and ignore short-lived fluctuations.  
- Strength: addresses a significant, under-explored problem and proposes an original, resourceful solution.  
- Strength: computationally and memory efficient—single-pass, compact statistics suitable for streams.  
- Strength: well-designed experiments including synthetic ground truth and a real-world case study.  
- Weakness: largely heuristic with almost no formal theoretical guarantees (detection, false alarms, convergence).  
- Weakness: core design choices (effective sample size update, reweighting rule, probabilistic relearning trigger) lack strong justification.  
- Weakness: potential misuse of statistical methods—Hotelling’s T^2 with weighted “effective” samples and Liptak pooling despite likely dependent tests.  
- Weakness: clarity and presentation problems—small/illegible figures, dense experimental descriptions, proofs misplaced in footnotes.  
- Overall: promising proof-of-concept deserving acceptance but needs more rigor and clearer presentation to be a complete scientific contribution.  
- Revision suggestions: explicitly acknowledge heuristic limits, justify design choices, improve figure legibility and move proofs to appendix, and emphasize theoretical analysis as future work.
