- Paper proposes LoSST, an online algorithm that tracks sufficient statistics to learn time-varying graphical structures under local stationarity.  
- LoSST uses weighted updates (Mahalanobis distances, pooled p-values) and a probabilistic relearning trigger; evaluated on simulations and U.S. price-index data.  
- Strength: Algorithmically sound with rigorous weighted sufficient-statistic updates and reproducible simulations showing advantages over batch methods during changes.  
- Strength: Practically relevant — single-pass, low computational cost and validated on real-world non-stationary data.  
- Strength: Core mechanics and online update equations are clearly derived and correctly implemented for recursive SEMs.  
- Weakness: Novelty overstated — prior work (Siracusa III & Fisher 2009) already addresses online time-varying structure learning with Bayesian methods.  
- Weakness: Paper misrepresents related work and incorrectly implies many score-based methods assume global stationarity.  
- Weakness: Unclear learning task — conflates change-point detection with structure inference and does not clearly state whether contribution is a model, algorithm, or both.  
- Weakness: Circular definition in pooled p-value construction (ρ_{r+1} depends on p_{r+1} which depends on ρ_{r+1}) with opaque justification.  
- Weakness: Presentation/readability problems — ρ_{r+1} undefined in Eq.5, unreadable figures, missing legends/axis units, and grammatical issues.  
- Weakness: Modest significance — incremental heuristic adapting known techniques (Mahalanobis, PC) to online use without advancing theoretical state of the art.  
- Recommendation: Reject — contribution not novel, related-work mischaracterized, and ambiguities undermine claims.  
- Key rejection arguments: lack of originality (prior art), unclear definitions/notation, limited significance, and misrepresentation of related work.  
- Suggestions: Acknowledge and contrast Siracusa III & Fisher (2009); clearly define task (detection vs. inference) and contribution; fix Eq.5 circularity; improve figures/readability; provide a supported cost–benefit discussion for computational trade-offs.
