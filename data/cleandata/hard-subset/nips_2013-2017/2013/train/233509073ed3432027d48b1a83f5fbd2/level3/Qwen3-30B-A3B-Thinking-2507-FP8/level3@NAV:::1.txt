

This paper proposes LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical model structures in locally stationary environments. The authors claim novelty in handling non-iid data with changing structures, but the related work discussion is misleading. Siracusa and Fisher III (AISTATS 2009) already addressed time-varying graphical structures using Bayesian methods, though they did not operate in real-time. The paper incorrectly states that "none are capable of general, graphical model structure learning" when online methods like Bayesian CPD algorithms exist. The core algorithm uses Mahalanobis distances and Liptak's p-value pooling to weight data, with a probabilistic relearning mechanism based on ρ_{r+1}. While the simulation results appear correct (Figures 1-3), the paper fails to clearly distinguish whether it's proposing a new learning task (detecting change points vs. inferring current structure) or a new algorithm.

The paper suffers from significant clarity issues: ρ_{r+1} is poorly defined (Equation 5), the presentation contains confusing punctuation, and the figures (1-4) have unreadable fonts without sufficient legend descriptions. The claim in Section 6 that "improved tracking can justify extra computational cost" is domain-dependent and unsupported. Originality is limited—the p-value construction is hard to follow, and the approach resembles existing score-based BN methods that handle non-stationarity through local scoring terms, as noted in the literature (e.g., Chickering, 2002).

Quality is moderate: the algorithm is technically sound with correct simulations, but the authors overlook key related work. Clarity is poor due to presentation flaws. Originality is marginal, as the core idea resembles existing non-stationary learning approaches. Significance is modest—the work may spur further Bayesian development but reads as a heuristic adaptation rather than a substantial advance. The paper would benefit from a clearer problem statement, proper comparison to Siracusa and Fisher (2009), and addressing the ambiguous distinction between change-point detection and structure inference. While the real-world application (Section 5) demonstrates practical utility, the limited novelty and clarity issues make the contribution incremental rather than transformative for the field.
