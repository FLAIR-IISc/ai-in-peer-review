Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles an important, under-explored problem: detecting and tracking changes in dependency structure over time in linear-Gaussian SEMs. The authors propose LoSST, an online method that maintains streaming sufficient statistics (weighted means, covariance matrix, and an “effective” sample size) and triggers batch-style structure re-learning with PC when the stream exhibits evidence of change. Novel elements include: (i) weighting incoming points by their “surprisingness” via Mahalanobis distance; (ii) converting those distances to p-values using Hotelling’s T2, then pooling them using Liptak’s method to obtain a significance statistic ρ; and (iii) a noisy-OR rule combining current ρ with past relearn probability to decide when to re-run PC. The method is evaluated on synthetic data with both structural and parametric changes, and on a US price-index dataset (529 monthly points, six variables), where it reportedly detects known macroeconomic shifts and ignores short-lived spikes.

The work builds on constraint-based learning for DAGs (PC) and on change detection ideas (Mahalanobis distance, p-value pooling). It differs from prior time-varying graphical structure work (e.g., Talih & Hengartner, Siracusa & Fisher) by focusing on real-time operation and online sufficient-statistic updates. It also contrasts with BOCPD and related CPD techniques by avoiding storage of long histories and allowing gradual change sensitivity.

Quality
- Correctness and empirical support: The streaming-statistics machinery is clearly specified, and the simulation setup compares LoSST to vanilla PC under structural and parameter shifts across varying variable counts and degrees. Results are sensible: indistinguishable performance before the first change; superior post-change performance vs. PC when structure shifts; and a brief performance dip after parameter-only changes (LoSST exhibits more edge removal errors immediately after a shift). The real-data case study (CPI components) is a valuable sanity check and aligns with known economic regimes.
- Methodological concerns:
  - Theoretical guarantees are limited. Convergence/diligence tradeoffs are discussed, but only high-level sketches are given, and no conditions are provided for statistical detection performance (e.g., false alarm/miss rates under assumptions).
  - The design leans heavily on heuristics (choice of a-update function, effective sample size recursion, relearn noisy-OR), and several ad-hoc decisions are insufficiently justified or documented for replication.
  - Statistical validity is questionable in places. Hotelling’s T2 is applied using a weighted covariance and an “effective” sample size Sr that may not correspond to the sampling distribution assumed by T2, and temporal dependence among p-values violates the independence assumptions underlying Liptak pooling. The paper should explicitly acknowledge and empirically probe these violations (calibration, Type I error control), or provide robust alternatives.
- Experimental adequacy: Synthetic experiments are broad in graph sizes/degrees and include parameter sensitivity and a realistic dataset. However, baselines are limited. It would strengthen the case to compare against sliding-window PC, CPD+PC hybrids (e.g., BOCPD followed by PC on recent data), and simple EWMA-based triggers. Reporting confidence intervals and calibration diagnostics for ρ would also help.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written with a solid literature review and clear motivation. That said:
- Many proofs and crucial explanations are relegated to footnotes; please move derivations and proof details to the supplement and keep only essential intuition in the main text.
- Figures are small and insufficiently labeled; captions should explain axes, units, and parameters (e.g., ν, T, γ). The experimental description around p.7 needs more detail (data generation, CI test specifics in PC, significance levels, stopping criteria).
- Several design choices (effective sample size update; exact mapping from ρ to ar; noisy-OR parameters) should be centralized and precisely documented for reproducibility.

Originality
The problem setting is novel for NIPS: online tracking of SEM structure. The algorithm is a creative combination of pieces (streaming covariance with adaptive weights; T2-based surprisingness; Liptak pooling; stochastic retraining) into a practical pipeline. While none of the components are new individually, their integration for this task is original and useful.

Significance
If made robust and better justified, LoSST could be a valuable tool for practitioners who need to adapt graphical structures online. The work opens a space for further theory and improved methods (e.g., edge-specific reweighting, online scores, discrete-variable extensions). The real-data case study suggests potential utility beyond synthetic benchmarks.

Major strengths
- Addresses an important, under-explored problem with clear practical motivation.
- Novel, pragmatic algorithm that is computationally and memory efficient (O(p^2) state; single pass).
- Adequate experiments including sensitivity analyses and a real dataset; before-change parity with PC is encouraging.
- Good related work coverage and generally clear exposition.

Major weaknesses
- Lack of theoretical guarantees (convergence beyond sketches; change-detection properties; false alarm/miss tradeoffs).
- Heavy use of heuristics with limited justification and ablation; several ad-hoc choices should be made explicit and tested.
- Potential misuse of statistical tests: T2 with weighted/effective N and Liptak pooling under dependence. The paper should clearly state when methods are used outside their guarantees and assess empirical calibration.

Other issues and suggestions
- Presentation: reduce footnotes; enlarge and clarify figures; add detailed captions; clarify experiment setup and metrics.
- Parameter-only changes: the explanation for the short-term degradation (reported on edge removals) is thin. Consider decoupling parameter updates (frequent, via EWMA/recursive least squares) from structure updates (rarer, triggered by edge-specific CI drift), which may prevent unnecessary edge pruning.
- Baselines: add sliding-window PC, CPD+PC, and window-size ablations; report runtime and memory comparisons.
- Statistical calibration: validate ρ with permutation or block bootstrap; consider robust p-value combination (e.g., Cauchy combination test) that tolerates dependence; or bypass p-values and use likelihood ratios tailored to EW covariance.
- Documentation: clearly list all hyperparameters (T, γ, ν), CI test settings, and provide code/supplement to ensure reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Timely, practical contribution on a problem with real applications.
- Conceptually simple and efficient algorithm; promising empirical gains after shifts.
- Solid literature grounding; opens up a new track for online structure learning.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient theoretical backing and possible misapplication of statistical procedures.
- Heuristic-driven design with many undocumented choices.
- Presentation issues and limited baselines reduce confidence in generality.

Overall evaluation
An interesting and practical first attempt at a difficult problem. With clearer justification of heuristic choices, stronger baselines, improved presentation, and a frank discussion of theoretical limitations (plus calibration checks), this could be a strong contribution. As it stands, it is promising but would benefit from additional rigor and clarity.
