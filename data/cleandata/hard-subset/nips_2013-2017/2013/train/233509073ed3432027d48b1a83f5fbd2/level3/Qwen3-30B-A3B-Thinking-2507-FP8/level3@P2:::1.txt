

The paper introduces LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical model structures under local stationarity assumptions. The authors claim it addresses a gap in existing work by enabling real-time structure learning when the underlying generative model changes, contrasting with standard batch methods. The algorithm dynamically weights data points using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values to adaptively relearn structures, with simulations showing improved performance over batch learning (PC algorithm) during structural shifts.

**Quality**: The experimental design is sound, with thorough simulations comparing LoSST to PC on synthetic data with known changepoints. Results correctly show LoSST matches batch performance during stability and outperforms it during structural shifts. However, the related work section misrepresents score-based Bayesian network (BN) learning: many score-based methods (e.g., [3, 12]) use decomposable scores that *do* support online updates via sufficient statistics (e.g., incremental covariance tracking), contrary to the claim that "neither type of structure learning algorithm is directly usable" for changing structures. The authors fail to acknowledge Siracusa & Fisher (AISTATS 2009), which *does* handle non-i.i.d. data with a Bayesian approach to time-varying structures—albeit in batch mode. This undermines the novelty claim. The algorithm’s reliance on heuristic weight updates (Eq. 4) and the unclear justification for the ρ_{r+1} construction (Section 3) also weaken theoretical rigor.

**Clarity**: Significant issues impair readability. The definition of ρ_{r+1} is ambiguous (e.g., "ρ_{r+1} cannot include p_{r+1} without being circular"), and critical equations (e.g., Eq. 2) lack proper punctuation. Figures 1–4 suffer from unreadable fonts, missing color legends, and insufficient axis labels, making quantitative comparisons difficult. The paper also conflates two distinct tasks: detecting *when* structure changes (changepoint detection) versus *inferring the current structure* (structure learning). This ambiguity is never resolved, leaving readers unclear whether LoSST is a change-detection method, a structure-estimation method, or both.

**Originality**: The core idea—adapting structure learning to non-stationary data via weighted statistics—is not novel. Siracusa & Fisher (2009) predated this work with a Bayesian approach for time-varying graphs (though batch-mode). LoSST’s use of pooled p-values and Mahalanobis distances is a heuristic extension of existing changepoint detection techniques (e.g., [1, 4]), not a fundamentally new approach. The paper’s claim that "none [online learning algorithms] are capable of general graphical model structure learning" is incorrect due to the oversight of prior work. The probabilistic relearning schedule (Section 3.1) is an interesting practical refinement but does not constitute a novel contribution.

**Significance**: While the real-world application to US price index volatility (Section 5) demonstrates utility, the significance is modest. The algorithm’s performance gains are context-specific (only during structural shifts), and the computational cost of probabilistic relearning (Section 6) is unconvincingly justified ("improved tracking might compensate for extra cost" is speculative without empirical evidence). The work primarily refines existing heuristics rather than advancing the state of the art. It may spur follow-up work on Bayesian variants (as noted in Section 6), but does not provide a compelling new framework for the field.

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:  
*Pro*: Strong empirical validation on synthetic data; real-world application shows practical relevance; algorithm is implementable and performs well in target scenarios.  
*Con*: Overstated novelty (ignores Siracusa & Fisher 2009); misleading related work; unclear task definition; presentation flaws; limited theoretical contribution; significance is incremental.

**Recommendation**: Reject. While the paper presents a functional algorithm for a niche problem, its failure to properly contextualize prior work (especially Siracusa & Fisher 2009), combined with clarity issues and limited novelty, prevents it from meeting NIPS’s standards for original contribution. The authors should revise to: (1) fully address Siracusa & Fisher’s work and clarify how LoSST differs, (2) resolve the task definition ambiguity, (3) improve figure quality, and (4) strengthen theoretical grounding. As-is, the paper does not advance the field sufficiently to warrant publication.
