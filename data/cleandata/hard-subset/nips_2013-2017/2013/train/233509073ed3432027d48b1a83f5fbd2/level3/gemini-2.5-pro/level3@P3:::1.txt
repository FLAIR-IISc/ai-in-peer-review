This paper introduces LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a heuristic, online algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from time-series data where the underlying generative process is non-stationary. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics (mean, covariance) by differentially weighting incoming data points. A new data point's weight is determined by a hypothesis-testing framework that uses Mahalanobis distance to assess its fit to the current model. A low pooled p-value suggests a structural change, increasing the weight of recent data and the probability of re-running a structure learning algorithm (in this case, the PC algorithm). The authors evaluate LoSST on simulated data with changepoints and a real-world US price index dataset, arguing that it outperforms a standard batch-mode learner in non-stationary settings while performing comparably in stationary ones. The work positions itself as a novel online, frequentist approach to a problem previously addressed by batch-mode methods or Bayesian approaches for tracking time-series dependence structures (e.g., Siracusa and Fisher III, 2009).

**Strengths**

*   The paper addresses the important and practical problem of learning graphical models in dynamic, non-stationary environments, where traditional batch-mode algorithms assuming i.i.d. data are unsuitable.
*   The proposed online, hypothesis-testing framework for detecting change and re-weighting data is an interesting heuristic.
*   The experimental results, on both synthetic and real-world data, appear sound and demonstrate that the method can successfully track changes in the underlying graph structure, outperforming a batch-mode baseline in the tested scenarios.

**Weaknesses**

The paper has significant weaknesses regarding originality, clarity, and the overall framing of its contribution.

1.  **Originality and Related Work:** The central claim of novelty is questionable, and the discussion of related work is misleading.
    *   The authors' claim that their approach is novel in handling non-iid data and varying structures is disputed by prior work. For example, Siracusa and Fisher III (AISTATS 2009) present a Bayesian method that explicitly models time-varying dependency structures. The distinction of LoSST being a frequentist, real-time method does not, on its own, constitute a significant leap forward, particularly given its heuristic nature.
    *   The paper's motivation rests on an oversimplified characterization of existing structure learning methods. It claims that score-based algorithms assume a single generating model, which is inaccurate. Many such algorithms only require a decomposable score (i.e., local scoring), a property that is compatible with non-stationary data streams, for example, through the use of a sliding window or an exponential forgetting factor. This misrepresentation weakens the stated need for the proposed algorithm.
    *   Overall, the approach appears to be a combination of existing techniques (Mahalanobis distance, p-value pooling, PC algorithm) without a clear, foundational new idea. This limits the originality of the contribution.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from several clarity issues that impede understanding and reproducibility.
    *   The precise learning task is not clearly defined. It is ambiguous whether the primary goal is to detect change points, to infer the current generating structure at any given time, or both. The paper also fails to clearly state whether LoSST should be considered a new model, a new algorithm, or a framework for combining existing components.
    *   Key technical details are opaque. The construction of the pooled p-value, ρ_{r+1}, is difficult to follow. The paper notes in a footnote that *p*<sub>r+1</sub> is excluded from the calculation of ρ_{r+1} to avoid circularity, but this ad-hoc solution is not well-justified and the logic remains confusing.
    *   The manuscript's presentation needs substantial improvement. There are numerous instances of confusing paragraph structure and end-of-equation punctuation. The figures (1–4) are particularly problematic, with font sizes that are too small to be legible and a lack of clear legends or color descriptions, making them very difficult to interpret.

3.  **Quality and Significance:** While the experiments show the algorithm works as described, the paper's broader claims are not well-supported, and its significance appears modest.
    *   The claim in Section 6 that improved tracking could justify additional computational costs is speculative and unsubstantiated. Such a trade-off is highly domain-dependent and requires more rigorous analysis.
    *   The significance of the work is limited. LoSST is presented as a heuristic solution to a problem that could likely be addressed by other existing machine learning methods (e.g., adaptive Bayesian models or state-space models). While the work might spur further development in this area, it does not seem to demonstrably advance the state of the art. It reads more as a preliminary exploration of a specific heuristic than a complete and principled scientific contribution.

In summary, while the paper tackles a relevant problem and the proposed heuristic shows promise in experiments, major concerns about its originality, misleading framing of related work, and lack of clarity prevent a recommendation for acceptance.
