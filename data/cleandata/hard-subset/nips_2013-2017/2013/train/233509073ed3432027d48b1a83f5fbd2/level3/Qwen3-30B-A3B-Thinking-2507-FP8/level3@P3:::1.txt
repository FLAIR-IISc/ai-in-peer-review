

**Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"**  

**Summary**  
This paper proposes LoSST, an online algorithm for learning graphical model structures from time-series data under local stationarity (i.e., data are i.i.d. within intervals but may change between intervals). The approach tracks sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) using weighted updates based on Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values, dynamically relearning structure via a probabilistic trigger. It evaluates performance on simulated data (showing advantages over batch methods during structural shifts) and a real-world U.S. price index dataset. The authors claim novelty in handling *online* structure tracking, contrasting with batch methods and prior changepoint detection (CPD) techniques.  

**Strengths**  
- **Technical Soundness**: The algorithm’s core mechanics (weighted sufficient statistics, Mahalanobis-based weighting) are mathematically rigorous. Simulations correctly demonstrate LoSST’s advantage over batch learning (e.g., PC algorithm) during structural changes, with results reproducible given the described setup.  
- **Practical Relevance**: The U.S. price index case study validates the method’s applicability to real-world non-stationary data, and the algorithm’s low computational cost (single-pass processing) is well-motivated.  
- **Clarity in Core Mechanics**: The online update equations (e.g., Eqs. 1–3) are logically derived and correctly implemented for recursive SEMs.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Misrepresented Novelty (Originality)**:  
   The paper’s central claim of novelty is unsubstantiated. Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) explicitly address *online* learning of time-varying directed graphical structures using Bayesian inference over edge probabilities (e.g., tracking posterior uncertainty over edges across time). While LoSST uses frequentist methods (to avoid exponential complexity), the *problem space* (time-varying structure learning from streams) and *core approach* (sequential model adaptation) are directly anticipated by Siracusa III & Fisher. The related-work section misleadingly dismisses their work as "batch-mode," but their method processes data sequentially. The paper also incorrectly implies that "all" score-based BN algorithms assume global stationarity; many (e.g., dynamic Bayesian networks, Bayesian structure learning with time-varying priors) handle non-stationarity via local scoring or incremental updates.  

2. **Unclear Learning Task & Contribution**:  
   The paper conflates *change-point detection* (e.g., "when did the structure change?") with *structure inference* (e.g., "what is the current structure?"). It is unclear whether LoSST proposes a *new model*, *algorithm*, or *both*. The algorithm’s novelty hinges on the weighted p-value pooling (Section 3), but the construction of ρ<sub>r+1</sub> (Eq. 5) is circular (ρ<sub>r+1</sub> depends on p<sub>r+1</sub>, which depends on ρ<sub>r+1</sub>), and its justification is opaque. The "probabilistic relearning" mechanism (Section 3.1) further blurs the line between detection and inference.  

3. **Presentation & Readability Issues**:  
   - ρ<sub>r+1</sub> is undefined in Eq. 5; the description ("pooled p-value") is insufficient without context.  
   - Figures 1–4 suffer from unreadable fonts, missing color legends, and unclear axis labels (e.g., Figure 1(d-f) lacks units for error differences).  
   - Paragraphs end with commas (e.g., "data are locally i.i.d., and learn graphical model structure in an online fashion." → comma splice).  
   - Section 6’s claim that "improved tracking justifies extra computational cost" is domain-dependent and unsupported (e.g., no cost-benefit analysis for real-world deployment).  

4. **Modest Significance**:  
   While LoSST is a technically sound *heuristic* for a specific problem, it lacks conceptual novelty. The approach is incremental: it adapts standard techniques (Mahalanobis distance, PC algorithm) to online settings without addressing deeper challenges (e.g., rapid structural shifts, non-Gaussian data). The significance is limited to "filling a gap" that was already partially addressed by Siracusa III & Fisher (2009) and dynamic Bayesian networks. The paper does not advance the *state of the art* in structure learning but offers a practical implementation.  

**Recommendation**  
**Reject**. The paper’s core contribution is not novel, as it builds on existing work (Siracusa III & Fisher, 2009) without clear differentiation. The misleading related-work discussion and unresolved ambiguities in the learning task undermine the paper’s claims. While technically sound, the work is an incremental engineering improvement rather than a significant scientific contribution. The significance is modest, and the presentation issues hinder reproducibility. For NIPS, the threshold for acceptance requires a *clear* conceptual or methodological advance—this paper falls short.  

**Key Arguments for Rejection**  
- **Originality**: Novelty claim contradicted by Siracusa III & Fisher (AISTATS 2009), which solves the *same problem* (online time-varying structure learning) with a Bayesian approach.  
- **Clarity**: Critical definitions (ρ<sub>r+1</sub>) are unclear; figures are unreadable; presentation errors impede understanding.  
- **Significance**: No theoretical advance; the method is a heuristic adaptation of known techniques with no evidence of broader impact.  
- **Quality**: Simulations are correct but fail to address the core novelty dispute. The related-work section misrepresents the field.  

**Constructive Suggestions**  
1. Acknowledge Siracusa III & Fisher (2009) as the direct predecessor and clarify how LoSST *differs* (e.g., frequentist vs. Bayesian, computational efficiency).  
2. Explicitly define the learning task (change detection vs. structure inference) and state whether the contribution is algorithmic, model-based, or both.  
3. Revise figures for readability (larger fonts, clear legends) and fix Eq. 5’s circularity.  
4. Replace Section 6’s unsupported claim with a discussion of *when* LoSST’s trade-offs (convergence vs. diligence) are advantageous.  

This work has merit as a practical tool but lacks the novelty and significance expected at NIPS. Addressing the above issues would require substantial revision to qualify as a contribution to the field.
