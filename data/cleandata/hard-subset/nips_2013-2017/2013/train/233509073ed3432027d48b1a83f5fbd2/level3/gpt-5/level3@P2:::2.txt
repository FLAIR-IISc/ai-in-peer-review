Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the under-explored problem of tracking time-varying graphical structure in data streams. The setting is locally stationary data generated by recursive linear SEMs over a DAG that may change in both structure and parameters over time. The core contribution is LoSST, a practical online heuristic that maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) to reflect recent data more than distant data, where the weights adapt to how “surprising” new observations are relative to the current model. Surprise is measured by the Mahalanobis distance with Hotelling’s T^2 p-values, which are pooled over time using Liptak’s method into a statistic ρ. This ρ drives both the per-datapoint weight update (including growth factor γ and threshold T) and a probabilistic noisy-OR schedule for triggering structure relearning using the PC algorithm. The paper includes an analysis of “convergence vs. diligence” trade-offs for different settings of T and γ.

Related work is well covered: batch score-based and constraint-based methods [3, 12, 10] assume stationarity; online changepoint detection [1, 4] is not directly suited for general structure learning and typically stores substantial history; prior time-varying structure learning work [14, 11] is offline and either restricts the type of change or is Bayesian over edges. The paper’s combination of adaptive weighted statistics, pooled p-values, and probabilistic relearning is novel in this context and aims to be single-pass and memory efficient.

Empirically, LoSST matches batch PC when the process is globally stationary and outperforms PC when structure changes, across multiple variable counts and degrees, both when graph structure and parameters change and when only parameters change. A case study on US price indices (six variables, 1967–2011, differenced) suggests LoSST detects known macroeconomic transitions (e.g., the Great Moderation) and is robust to isolated spikes.

Quality
- Strengths: The algorithmic components are clearly described, with incremental update equations for weighted means and covariances, and a clean trigger mechanism for relearning. The simulations are sensible and include both abrupt structure and parameter shifts, as well as a long series with probabilistic relearning. The real-data case study is appropriate and illustrates the method’s behavior.
- Weaknesses: The methodology is heavily heuristic with limited theory. In particular, (a) the “effective sample size” update Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1)Sr + 1 is ad hoc and does not align with standard definitions for weighted effective sample size ((∑ w)^2/∑ w^2); (b) Hotelling’s T^2 p-values are used with a time-varying, weighted covariance and a nonstandard “effective N,” raising concerns about calibration; (c) Liptak’s pooling assumes independence or at least known dependence—serial dependence among p-values (and reuse of estimated covariance) is likely severe. These choices risk miscalibrated ρ and hence brittle trigger behavior. The “convergence vs. diligence” discussion is insightful but largely sketched; formal guarantees (assumptions, error probabilities, false alarm and detection delay) are missing. Baselines are limited to vanilla batch PC; strong practical baselines such as sliding-window PC, exponentially weighted PC, simple forgetting-factor CI tests, and online CPD+relearn hybrids are absent. There is no ablation of γ, T, ν or of design choices (e.g., Liptak vs. EWMA of distances), and little diagnostic analysis (e.g., false positives vs. detection delay).

Clarity
The paper is generally well written with a thorough related work section. However:
- Several important derivations and claims (e.g., convergence/diligence conditions) appear as brief footnotes; move proofs to an appendix/supplement and give clear statements in the main text.
- Figures are small and under-labeled; legends and captions should specify error metrics, averaging, and confidence intervals. The experiment schedule and hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) should be explicitly reported in captions or a dedicated experimental setup subsection.
- Provide algorithm pseudocode with all maintained state (µ, C, b, S, η, τ, ρ, Pr(relearn)) and a clear description of CI tests within PC under weighted statistics.

Originality
The problem focus (online structure learning under nonstationarity) is timely and under-served. The specific integration—streaming weighted sufficient statistics, pooled surprise via Liptak, and probabilistic relearning of PC—is, to my knowledge, novel relative to [14, 11] and online CPD [1, 4].

Significance
If robust, LoSST would be useful in domains that require online adaptation (finance, sensors, A/B experimentation, healthcare). The empirical results are promising: indistinguishable from batch PC under stationarity and better after change. However, the lack of calibration/theory and limited baselines currently temper confidence in broad adoption. Strengthening evaluation and clarifying statistical validity would substantially increase impact.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Statistical calibration: Justify the use of Hotelling’s T^2 with weighted covariances and “effective N,” or replace with a calibrated statistic. Consider permutation/bootstrap calibration of ρ under stationarity, or use self-normalized statistics. Address dependence among p-values in Liptak pooling (e.g., block bootstrap, downweighting, or alternative aggregators).
- Effective sample size: Either derive Sr from a principled definition (e.g., Kish’s ESS for weights) or avoid using N-dependent pivots; if you retain Sr, empirically validate coverage (Q-Q plots) of the resulting p-values.
- Baselines: Add sliding-window PC and EWMA-weighted PC; a CPD-triggered relearn baseline (Bayesian [1] or frequentist [4]); and Talih & Hengartner [14] or Siracusa & Fisher [11] offline methods applied in a retrospective manner for context.
- Ablations: Systematically vary T, γ, ν and report detection delay, false alarm rate, and post-change error. Compare Liptak pooling to simpler EWMAs of Mahalanobis distance.
- Parameter-only changes: The explanation for short-term edge removal behavior is plausible but incomplete. Consider decoupling parameter updates from structure updates (e.g., allow continuous parameter refitting with forgetting, and trigger PC only when edge-level tests consistently change).
- High-dimensional stability: Discuss covariance regularization (e.g., shrinkage) and invertibility for p ~ n; report runtime and memory profiles.
- Reproducibility: Provide code or pseudo-code and seeds; clarify graph generation process and CI test details (alpha, multiple testing control).

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and under-explored problem: online tracking of changing dependency structure for SEMs.
- Practical, memory- and compute-efficient single-pass algorithm; easy to implement atop existing PC code.
- Novel combination of ideas (adaptive weighting + pooled surprise + probabilistic relearning).
- Solid simulations across multiple regimes and a relevant real-world case study; matches batch PC under stationarity and improves after change.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on heuristics with limited theoretical support; potential miscalibration of p-values (Hotelling’s T^2 with weighted N; dependence in Liptak pooling).
- Sparse baselines and no ablation studies; unclear robustness to hyperparameters and design choices.
- Presentation issues: small figures, reliance on footnotes for key arguments; some experimental descriptions are opaque.
- Limited discussion of CI test validity under weighting within PC.

Overall recommendation
Interesting and promising first step toward online structure learning with nonstationarity. With strengthened calibration/theory, additional baselines and ablations, and improved presentation, this could be a strong contribution. As it stands, I lean toward weak reject/weak accept depending on how much revision is feasible pre-proceedings.
