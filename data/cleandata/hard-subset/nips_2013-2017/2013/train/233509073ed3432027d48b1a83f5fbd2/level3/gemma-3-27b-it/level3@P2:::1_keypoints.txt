- Proposes LoSST: a heuristic for real-time tracking of graphical model structure using dynamically weighted data and pooled p-values from Mahalanobis distance, with periodic relearning via the PC algorithm.  
- Empirical results show LoSST matches batch learning when structure is stable and outperforms it under local stationarity in simulations and a US price index case study.  
- Correctly motivates the need for non-iid, non-stationary structure learning and builds on prior graphical-model and online-learning literature.  
- Literature positioning is incomplete and sometimes misleading; the paper downplays relevant techniques and overstates novelty.  
- The claim about score-based Bayesian network methods universally assuming a single model is inaccurate; many allow incremental/local updates.  
- The paper dismisses Siracusa & Fisher (AISTATS 2009) despite its direct relevance as a Bayesian approach to non-iid, changing structures.  
- Strengths: pragmatic approach, technically sound simulations, and a concrete, implementable algorithm.  
- Key weakness: unclear contribution—uncertain if it proposes a new model, a new algorithm, or a heuristic adaptation.  
- Presentation issues: dense paragraphs, inconsistent punctuation, convoluted definition of ρ_{r+1}, and poorly designed figures (small fonts, unclear legends/colors).  
- The pooled p-value construction is ad-hoc, hard to follow, and lacks clear theoretical justification.  
- Section 6’s discussion of trade-offs between tracking and computation is speculative and domain-dependent without concrete evidence.  
- Originality is modest: a novel combination of ideas but not fundamentally groundbreaking; significance appears incremental.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, shows promising empirical performance, and provides an implementable method.  
- Arguments against acceptance: overstates novelty, downplays related work, poor presentation and reproducibility, weak theoretical grounding, and limited significance.  
- Overall recommendation: reject; substantial revisions needed to clarify positioning, improve presentation, and justify design/theoretical choices for reconsideration.
