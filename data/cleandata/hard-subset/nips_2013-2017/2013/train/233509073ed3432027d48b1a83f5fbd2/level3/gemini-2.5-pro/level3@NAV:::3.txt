This paper introduces LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of Structural Equation Models (SEMs) from time-varying data streams where the underlying generative process is only locally stationary. The core contribution is a systematic method for tracking sufficient statistics (means and covariances) using a dynamic weighting scheme. This scheme, based on the Mahalanobis distance of incoming data points, allows the algorithm to detect distributional shifts and adaptively re-weight the data history, triggering a standard structure learning algorithm when a significant change is inferred. This work is well-positioned relative to previous literature, addressing a critical gap between traditional structure learning methods that assume globally i.i.d. data and online changepoint detection algorithms not designed for graphical model discovery.

The paper’s primary strengths lie in its originality and clarity. It addresses the important and practical, yet under-studied, problem of structure learning from non-stationary data. The proposed approach is elegant, the technical details are presented clearly, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The techniques for tracking statistics are simple but appear experimentally effective when coupled with the PC algorithm. The results are promising, demonstrating that LoSST can outperform a standard batch-mode baseline in dynamic environments while performing comparably in stable ones.

However, there are areas for improvement. First, the claim that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly" is unsubstantiated, as the experiments are conducted exclusively with the PC algorithm. This assertion should be softened to reflect the specific empirical context or be supported with further justification. Second, the paper’s scope is specific to SEMs, where sufficient statistics are compact. The authors should state this limitation more explicitly upfront and acknowledge that extending the approach to other models, such as Bayesian networks, is a non-trivial challenge due to the difficulty of maintaining sufficient statistics for discrete variables. Finally, the experimental evaluation, while insightful, is limited to a comparison against a single batch-mode algorithm.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and significant contribution to the field. With minor revisions to moderate its claims and clarify its scope, it would be a strong addition to the conference.
