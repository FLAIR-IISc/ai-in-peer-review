This paper proposes a novel algorithm, LoSST, for tracking changes in graphical model structure or parameters in a dynamic, real-time manner. The algorithm is designed to address the problem of detecting changes in dependency structure over a data stream for Structural Equation Models (SEMs). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of LoSST through simulations and a real-world application to US price index volatility.

The main ideas of the paper are well-motivated and related to previous work in the field. The authors provide a clear overview of the limitations of existing structure learning algorithms and the need for an online approach that can handle non-stationary data. The LoSST algorithm is a creative combination of several methods, including tracking weighted means, correlation matrices, and effective sample sizes, and using Mahalanobis distance to weight new points by "surprisingness."

The strengths of the paper include its novel approach to addressing an important and under-explored problem, its practical and computationally efficient algorithm, and its adequate experiments, including parameter sensitivity and a real-world dataset. The literature review is also good, and the writing is clear.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. The most significant concern is the lack of theoretical analysis or guarantees for the LoSST algorithm. The authors do not provide any proofs or discussions of convergence or detection under assumptions, which is a major limitation of the paper. Additionally, the design of the algorithm is heuristic-heavy, with many ad-hoc decisions, such as reweighting, effective sample size, and re-learn criteria, that need clearer documentation.

Another issue is the potential misuse of statistical methods, such as Hotelling's T2 and Liptak pooling, which assume conditions that may not hold in the context of the algorithm. The authors should explicitly state when methods are used outside their theoretical guarantees to avoid misleading readers.

Some minor issues with the paper include excessive use of footnotes for proofs, which should be moved to supplementary material. The figures are also too small, poorly labeled, and lack details in their captions and legends. The experiment descriptions could be clearer, and the explanation for LoSST performing worse on edge additions under parameter-only changes is unsatisfactory.

Overall, the paper is an interesting and practical first attempt at addressing the problem of detecting changes in dependency structure over a data stream for SEMs. However, it needs clearer justification of heuristic choices, better presentation, and theoretical development to be considered a significant contribution to the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper addresses an important and under-explored problem in the field.
* The LoSST algorithm is novel, practical, and computationally efficient.
* The experiments are adequate and include parameter sensitivity and a real-world dataset.
* The literature review is good, and the writing is clear.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper lacks theoretical analysis or guarantees for the LoSST algorithm.
* The design of the algorithm is heuristic-heavy, with many ad-hoc decisions.
* The potential misuse of statistical methods is a concern.
* The presentation could be improved, including the use of footnotes and figure quality.

Recommendation:

* Accept the paper with minor revisions to address the presentation issues and provide clearer documentation of the algorithm's design choices.
* Encourage the authors to provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of the LoSST algorithm in future work.
