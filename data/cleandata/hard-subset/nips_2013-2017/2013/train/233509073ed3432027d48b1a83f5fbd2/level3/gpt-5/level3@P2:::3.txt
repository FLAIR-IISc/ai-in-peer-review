Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces LoSST, an online algorithm for learning and tracking time-varying graphical structure in linear-Gaussian SEMs (DAGs). The key idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an effective sample size) in a single pass, where the per-sample weights adapt to evidence of non-stationarity. The evidence signal is a pooled p-value based on Mahalanobis distances (via Liptak’s method), which increases weights when recent observations fit poorly under the current estimates and also governs a probabilistic trigger to re-run a constraint-based structure learner (PC) on the current sufficient statistics. The paper analyzes the trade-off between “convergence” and “diligence” as a function of the weight update parameters and provides simulations and a real-data case study.

Relative to prior work, LoSST distinguishes itself from (i) batch time-varying graphical model estimators that require full datasets and/or place strong structural constraints (e.g., Talih & Hengartner, 2005; Siracusa & Fisher, 2009), (ii) online Bayesian updating that presumes stationarity, and (iii) changepoint detection methods that emphasize abrupt shifts and often require substantial storage (Adams & MacKay, 2007; Desobry et al., 2005). The approach of tracking decomposable sufficient statistics aligns well with the constraint-based PC learner for linear-Gaussian DAGs. The paper’s conceptual contribution is a simple, practical recipe for online, locally stationary structure learning using only O(p^2) memory and occasional relearning.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The weighted mean and covariance recursions are standard and clearly derived; the effective sample-size update is reasonable for interpreting CI tests, though the sampling distribution under adaptive weighting is only heuristically justified. The use of Hotelling’s T^2 for Mahalanobis distances presumes Gaussianity and independence assumptions that are not fully satisfied (distances are serially dependent because parameters are estimated from the same stream), but the paper positions these choices as heuristics. The convergence/diligence discussion is helpful; proofs are sketched rather than fully developed.
- Empirical support: Simulations convincingly show that LoSST matches PC when the process is stationary and adapts well after changes, with an informative probabilistic relearning analysis. The real-data CPI example is illustrative but lacks ground truth and quantitative validation beyond qualitative alignment with macroeconomic narratives.
- Scope of claims: Some claims are too strong (e.g., that “any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly”) and should be circumscribed or empirically supported.

Clarity
The paper is well written and easy to follow. The algorithmic steps, parameter roles (T, γ, ν), and intuition behind pooled p-values are clearly explained. It would help to:
- State the SEM/linear-Gaussian scope up front (abstract/introduction) and that extensions to discrete BNs are nontrivial due to the difficulty of maintaining sufficient statistics for CI tests.
- Provide pseudocode for LoSST and its relearning trigger for reproducibility.
- Clarify computational complexity per step (e.g., maintaining C−1 via rank-one updates) and the cost of episodic PC calls.

Originality
While related to time-varying graphical models and changepoint detection, the combination of: (i) adaptive weighting of sufficient statistics via pooled p-values, (ii) an online “effective sample size” notion tied to CI testing, and (iii) a probabilistic relearning trigger for DAG structure, appears novel and practically motivated. The convergence/diligence framing is also a useful conceptual lens.

Significance
Online structure learning for nonstationary processes is important and under-explored. The method is simple, practical, and likely to influence work on streaming causal discovery and adaptive modeling. Demonstrated gains over batch PC after changes are meaningful. A broader empirical evaluation would further strengthen impact.

Strengths
- Addresses an important, realistic setting (locally stationary structure) with a single-pass, low-memory algorithm.
- Simple, modular design; compatible with existing CI-based learners.
- Clear exposition and helpful analysis of convergence vs. diligence.
- Solid simulations that show performance parity under stationarity and advantages after change.
- Probabilistic relearning experiment demonstrates computational practicality.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Baselines: Comparisons are limited to batch PC. Please add or discuss:
  - Sliding-window PC (various window sizes).
  - Exponentially weighted PC with fixed forgetting factors.
  - Online CPD coupled with re-estimation (even if limited memory), and EWMA/CUSUM-style detectors as lightweight alternatives to pooled p-values.
- Assumptions and robustness: The T^2-based p-values presume Gaussianity and independence; discuss robustness to non-Gaussian noise and heteroskedasticity. Consider robust distances or rank-based tests as variants.
- Statistical validity under adaptive weighting: The CI tests in PC rely on known sampling distributions; justify more explicitly why using Sr in Fisher’s z-tests/partial correlations is appropriate under adaptive weights, or provide empirical calibration (e.g., type-I error under stationarity).
- Hyperparameter selection: T, γ, ν are chosen via “unsystematic investigation.” Provide guidance (e.g., cross-validation on a holdout, stability-based criteria), ablations, or sensitivity analyses.
- Computational details: Report runtime/number of PC calls, and discuss maintaining inverse covariance (e.g., Sherman–Morrison) to keep per-step updates near O(p^2).
- Scope of claims: Rephrase the assertion that “other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly” unless supported by experiments. Emphasize that results concern the PC algorithm.
- Extensions: Clearly state in the abstract/introduction that the method as presented targets linear-Gaussian SEMs; extensions to discrete BNs are nontrivial and should be couched as future work.
- Real-data study: Augment qualitative interpretation with quantitative checks (e.g., predictive likelihood/imputation error before vs. after detected regimes; stability of learned adjacencies across bootstraps).

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical, simple algorithm for an important streaming problem; easy to adopt.
- Novel synthesis of adaptive weighting and online structure relearning with clear intuition.
- Strong synthetic results showing the right qualitative behavior.
- Useful conceptual contribution (convergence vs. diligence) with partial guarantees.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited baselines; unclear advantage over simpler sliding-window/forgetting-factor approaches.
- Heuristic statistical components (pooled p-values, effective sample size) not fully justified theoretically.
- Hyperparameter selection and robustness not systematically studied.
- Real-data validation is illustrative rather than rigorous.

Overall evaluation
Promising and well-presented work on an important problem, with a simple and effective approach. I lean toward acceptance provided the claims are tempered and the empirical section is strengthened with stronger baselines and guidance on hyperparameters; otherwise, a borderline accept.
