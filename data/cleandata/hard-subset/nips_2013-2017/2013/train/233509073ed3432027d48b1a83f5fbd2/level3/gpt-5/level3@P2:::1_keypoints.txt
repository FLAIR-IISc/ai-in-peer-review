- Summary: Proposes LoSST, an online heuristic that tracks weighted sufficient statistics and pools Mahalanobis p-values to trigger adaptive forgetting and probabilistic PC-based structure relearning for time-varying linear Gaussian SEMs.

- Method detail: Maintains running means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size, updates per sample, pools p-values with Liptak’s method to form ρr, and increases forgetting or relearning when ρr is small.

- Theoretical contribution: Provides proof sketches about a convergence vs diligence trade-off controlled by parameters (γ, T), but theory is limited and relies on strong assumptions.

- Empirical evaluation: Shows parity with batch PC in stationary regimes and improved recovery after changes on synthetic data; includes a CPI volatility case study.

- Relation to prior work: Positions as filling a gap in online DAG structure learning but understates closely related works like Siracusa & Fisher (2009) and other time-varying graph methods.

- Misleading claims: Some related-work statements overclaim novelty—many BN methods are not designed for nonstationarity but do not strictly assume a single generating model.

- Strength: Addresses an under-explored practical setting (online structural and parametric change tracking in DAGs/SEMs).

- Strength: Simple, modular, and implementable design combining online sufficient statistics with periodic constraint-based relearning.

- Strength: Pooled p-value approach intuitively accumulates evidence for change beyond single outliers.

- Strength: Probabilistic relearning reduces computation with little accuracy loss in experiments.

- Weakness: Novelty is limited given prior time-varying and Bayesian dynamic-structure literature.

- Weakness: The precise learning objective is unclear—change-point detection, instantaneous recovery, or both.

- Weakness: Key components are heuristic (effective sample size update, forgetting schedule) with limited derivation or justification.

- Weakness: Limited theoretical guarantees; convergence/diligence results are sketches relying on strong assumptions.

- Weakness: Evaluation omits stronger baselines (sliding-window PC, exponential forgetting, CPD + per-segment learning, time-varying GGM/DAG methods).

- Weakness: No systematic parameter sensitivity study (T, γ, ν) or robustness analysis to outliers.

- Weakness: Clarity issues—confusing notation/indices for ρr and pr, punctuation around equations, and poor figure readability.

- Weakness: Numerical stability not discussed (covariance inversion with small effective sample sizes, need for shrinkage).

- Quality: Algorithm is coherent and simulations align with claims, but methodology is largely heuristic and some statistical steps need justification; empirical evaluation is incomplete.

- Clarity: Readable in parts but suffers from notational ambiguities and unclear task statement; scope and assumptions should be stated up front.

- Originality: Moderate—pragmatic assembly of known ideas (pooled p-values, forgetting, relearning) rather than a novel conceptual advance.

- Significance: Modest—practically useful but not a convincing advance due to heuristics, limited theory, and incomplete comparisons.

- Requested experiments/questions: Provide sensitivity analyses, comparisons to sliding-window/exponential forgetting/CPD baselines, regularization for Cr, T2 calibration diagnostics, and compute/time costs vs batch PC.

- Suggested improvements: Revise related work, clarify timing/indexing of statistics, add ablations and stronger baselines, improve figures, discuss numerical stabilization, and strengthen theoretical or calibration results.

- Arguments for acceptance: Practical, implementable approach with encouraging empirical results and an interesting convergence vs diligence discussion.

- Arguments against acceptance: Overstated novelty, heuristic core, missing strong baselines and sensitivity analyses, and presentation/clarity problems.

- Overall recommendation: Weak reject—promising but needs stronger positioning, clearer exposition, better baselines, and more rigorous validation.

- Reviewer confidence: High on methods and related work; medium on CPI interpretation.
