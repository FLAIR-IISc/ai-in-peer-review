## Review of “Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure”

This paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic algorithm for online learning of changing graphical model structures from time-series data. The core idea is to dynamically adjust data weights based on a p-value derived from Mahalanobis distance, triggering model re-learning when a significant deviation from the current structure is detected. The algorithm is evaluated on simulated data exhibiting structural changes and a real-world dataset of US price indices. While the results appear correct and the approach is potentially useful, the paper suffers from issues of novelty, clarity, and presentation.

The authors’ claim of novelty is questionable. The paper overlooks prior work by Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) which already addresses non-iid data and varying generating structures in a Bayesian framework. Furthermore, the discussion of related work is misleading; many score-based Bayesian Network algorithms do *not* assume a single generating model, but rather rely on factorized scoring functions and local terms. The paper also lacks clarity regarding its contribution: is it a new model, an algorithm, or both? The connection between the p-value construction and existing changepoint detection methods is also difficult to follow.

The strengths of the paper lie in its attempt to tackle a practically relevant problem – online structure learning in dynamic environments – and the demonstration of LoSST’s performance in simulations. The algorithm appears to perform comparably to batch methods when the structure is stable and outperforms them when changes occur. The application to US price index data provides a glimpse of real-world applicability.

However, several weaknesses detract from the overall quality. The presentation is marred by minor issues like inconsistent punctuation and an unclear definition of ρ_{r+1}. Figures 1-4 are difficult to interpret due to small font sizes and a lack of clear labeling. The justification for increased computational cost based on improved tracking (Section 6) is weak and domain-dependent. The originality is limited; while the combination of techniques is somewhat novel, the core ideas lack significant innovation. Finally, the significance is modest; the work feels more like heuristic development than a substantial advancement, as existing ML methods could potentially address similar problems. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.
*   Demonstrates promising results in simulated and real-world settings.
*   May spur further research, particularly in Bayesian approaches.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty, with relevant prior work overlooked.
*   Misleading discussion of related work.
*   Lack of clarity regarding the core contribution.
*   Presentation issues hinder readability and understanding.
*   Modest significance and reliance on heuristics.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend **rejection** due to the limited novelty, clarity issues, and modest significance. While the work has potential, it requires substantial revision to address the identified weaknesses and convincingly demonstrate its contribution to the field.
