- Proposal: LoSST — online tracking of changing linear-Gaussian SEM structure using weighted sufficient statistics and a pooled p-value trigger to occasionally relearn with PC.
- Prior work undersold: time-varying DAGs and nonstationary graphical models exist (e.g., Siracusa & Fisher 2009; Talih & Hengartner 2005) and sliding-window/discounting approaches are standard.
- Method correctness: weighted mean/covariance updates look standard and correct; effective sample-size recursion is heuristic.
- Heuristic components: p-value pooling and probabilistic relearning trigger are heuristic and lack strong statistical justification.
- Convergence analysis: discussion of convergence vs diligence is interesting but largely sketchy and parameter-specific.
- Missing baselines: should compare to sliding-window PC, exponential forgetting, BOCPD+relearning, and time-varying/penalized Gaussian graphical models.
- Calibration concern: Liptak pooling assumes independence/dependence structures that don’t hold here; pooled p-values may be miscalibrated under adaptive, overlapping estimates.
- Ambiguous objective: unclear whether the goal is changepoint detection, per-time structure tracking, or both — evaluation and metrics should be aligned accordingly.
- Technical errors: Hotelling T2 parameterization looks incorrect (p vs V, m vs effective sample size); notation around ρr+1 is confusing.
- Notation and presentation: paper has notational confusions and punctuation issues around equations; needs standardized symbols and clearer recursion.
- Algorithm clarity: Section 3 needs a clear algorithm box specifying inputs, outputs, state, and consistent indexing.
- Figures: figures use small fonts, unclear legends/colors; recommend colorblind-safe palettes and clearer captions/labels.
- Originality: problem setting is established; the per-datapoint pooled p-value driving relearning is an engineering novelty but lacks principled theory.
- Significance: practical impact modest without stronger baselines and cost–benefit analysis; CPI case study is suggestive but not conclusive.
- Suggested experiments: add ablations (exponential forgetting vs pooled-p-value, fixed vs probabilistic relearning), sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν, and false-alarm calibration under stationarity.
- Complexity reporting: report per-step and cumulative computational costs, number of relearnings, and wall-clock times to justify trade-offs.
- Presentation fixes: define all symbols on first use, fix equation punctuation, standardize p/V and N/S notation, and clarify “model” vs “algorithmic scheme.”
- Citation correction: temper novelty claims and explicitly acknowledge prior work (Siracusa & Fisher 2009) and adaptability of score-based BN methods to nonstationarity.
- Acceptance arguments: addresses an important practical problem, simple single-pass implementation, shows empirical gains vs batch PC, and discusses convergence/diligence trade-offs.
- Rejection arguments: overstates novelty, core heuristics lack justification and calibration, missing key baselines, and suffers clarity/technical inconsistencies.
- Overall assessment: promising engineering direction but currently insufficient novelty, baselines, statistical grounding, and clarity — lean toward rejection unless substantially strengthened.
