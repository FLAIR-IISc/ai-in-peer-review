Summary
This paper introduces LoSST, a heuristic online method for tracking time-varying graphical structure under the assumption of local stationarity: at any time r, data are i.i.d. from a linear-Gaussian SEM Gr, but Gr may change over time in both structure and parameters. The method maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) with per-datum weights ar adapted via a pooled p-value over Mahalanobis distances (using Lipták’s method). Low pooled p-values increase weights and trigger more frequent relearning of structure; high values preserve weights and reduce relearning frequency. Structure is relearned with PC on the current weighted sufficient statistics, with both fixed and probabilistic relearning schedules. The paper sketches conditions under which LoSST trades off diligence (rapid response to change) and convergence (stability under stationarity). Experiments on synthetic SEMs show LoSST matches batch PC before a changepoint and outperforms batch PC after latent changes; a CPI case study illustrates qualitative changepoint detection.

Relative to prior work, LoSST differs from online changepoint detection, sliding windows, and batch time-indexed network models by offering a single-pass, light-memory approach that integrates change detection and structure relearning. It situates itself alongside constraint-based SEM learners (e.g., PC), with related work on time-varying undirected models (Talih & Hengartner) and a Bayesian time-varying DAG approach (Siracusa & Fisher), and argues for frequentist, online, decomposable updates for scalability.

Quality
- Soundness: The sufficient-statistic updates for weighted means and covariances are standard and appropriate; the ar update via pooled p-values is well-motivated albeit heuristic. The use of Hotelling’s T^2 and Lipták pooling is reasonable for Gaussian SEMs, but the serial dependence among p-values and adaptive weights means calibration is approximate; some care is needed interpreting ρ as a p-value. The convergence/diligence discussion is clear about trade-offs, with proofs sketched.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments convincingly show no loss under stationarity and substantial gains after change relative to batch PC. Results under parameter-only changes are nuanced and informative. The CPI application is illustrative, though lacking ground truth; quantitative predictive validation would strengthen it. The empirical scope is limited to PC; the claim that other structure learners would behave similarly is not substantiated experimentally.
- Completeness: This is a complete piece of work for SEMs+PC under Gaussian assumptions. Hyperparameter sensitivity, calibration, and broader baselines could be expanded.

Clarity
- The paper is well written and easy to follow. The algorithmic components (stat updates, pooled p-values, relearning schedule) are clearly described. I suggest:
  - State the SEM-specific scope and linear-Gaussian assumptions up front, and note that extension to discrete Bayesian networks is nontrivial due to challenges in maintaining (conditional) sufficient statistics and decomposable scores online.
  - Rephrase or justify the statement that “other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly,” and make explicit that experiments focus on PC.
  - Provide pseudocode for LoSST with complexity annotations, and detail how you update the inverse covariance efficiently (e.g., Sherman–Morrison) to avoid O(p^3) per step.

Originality
- The combination of (i) pooled p-value driven, adaptive forgetting; (ii) online sufficient-statistic tracking for SEMs; and (iii) probabilistic triggering of constraint-based relearning is a neat and simple recipe. While components (Hotelling’s T^2, Lipták pooling, forgetting factors) are known, their integration for time-varying SEM structure learning appears novel and practical.

Significance
- Learning graphical structure from time-dependent data is important and under-studied. The demonstrated improvements after changepoints and the single-pass nature make this relevant to practitioners. Broader impact would be clearer with stronger baselines and additional model families.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Claims and scope:
  - Soften “other standard algorithms would perform similarly,” or support with targeted experiments (e.g., GES/BIC with a forgetting factor, MMHC, FCI for latent confounding). At minimum, clarify that the empirical evidence is limited to PC.
  - Explicitly position LoSST as SEM-specific; for Bayesian networks, weighted conditional counts and decomposable scores are harder to maintain online, and independence tests under adaptive weighting may not be valid without rederivation.
- Statistical calibration:
  - Discuss the dependence among sequential p-values in the Lipták pool and the effect on false-alarm rates; consider block bootstrap/permutation to calibrate thresholds T, or provide empirical calibration curves.
  - Clarify the derivation and interpretation of the effective sample size update S_{r+1} = (a_r/a_{r+1}) S_r + 1; a short derivation or appendix would help.
- Hyperparameters:
  - Provide sensitivity analyses for T, γ, and ν, including time-to-recovery, false-alarm rate, and steady-state error under stationarity.
- Baselines:
  - Include sliding-window PC, exponentially weighted PC with a fixed forgetting factor, and CPD+PC (relearn structure only upon detected changepoints). For Gaussian undirected models, compare to time-varying GGM methods (if feasible) to contextualize gains.
- Metrics and reporting:
  - Report SHD/F1 across edge presence and orientations; provide time-to-stabilization after change.
  - For the CPI study, add out-of-sample predictive log-likelihood or one-step-ahead error before/after detected shifts, and discuss economic interpretability more rigorously.
- Scalability:
  - Detail the per-step time and memory complexity; indicate whether you use rank-one updates for the inverse covariance and how you handle numerical stability (regularization, shrinkage).
- Extensions:
  - Note the practical difficulty of per-edge weights (as you mention), but consider a middle ground: per-variable or per-clique weights to capture localized changes.

Pros
- Addresses an important, under-explored problem; conceptually simple and practical.
- Clear exposition; thoughtful discussion of convergence vs. diligence.
- Strong synthetic results demonstrating advantages under nonstationarity; minimal cost under stationarity.
- Single-pass, low-memory framework suitable for streaming.

Cons
- Evaluation limited to PC; generalization claims not empirically supported.
- Statistical calibration of pooled p-values under adaptivity not fully addressed.
- Limited baselines; no sliding-window/forgetting-factor comparisons.
- Real-data validation is qualitative.

Recommendation
Weak Accept. The paper is clear, original in its integration of simple ideas for an important problem, and empirically promising. With modest revisions to clarify scope, temper claims, and strengthen evaluation, it would make a useful contribution.

I have read the authors’ feedback and appreciate the clarifications; my main requests remain to (i) state the SEM-specific focus and difficulty of BN extensions up front, and (ii) tone down or justify the generalization beyond PC.
