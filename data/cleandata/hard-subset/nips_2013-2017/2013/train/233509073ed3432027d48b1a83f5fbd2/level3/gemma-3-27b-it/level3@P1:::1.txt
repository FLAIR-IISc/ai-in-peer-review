This paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic algorithm for tracking changes in graphical model structure in non-stationary environments. The core idea is to dynamically weight data points based on their fit to the current model, enabling online structure learning. The authors evaluate LoSST on simulated data and a real-world dataset of US price indices, demonstrating its ability to adapt to changing relationships. While the results appear sound, several weaknesses regarding novelty, clarity, and presentation detract from the overall contribution.

The paper positions itself as filling a gap in existing structure learning algorithms which predominantly assume i.i.d. data and stable generative processes. However, the related work section presents a somewhat selective and misleading view of the literature. The authors claim existing algorithms cannot handle changing structures, but Siracusa & Fisher III (AISTATS 2009) already address this problem with a Bayesian approach for time-series data, a crucial omission given the focus on non-iid data and evolving structures. Furthermore, the assertion that many score-based Bayesian Network algorithms require a single generating model is inaccurate; many utilize factorized scores and local terms, allowing for incremental updates without assuming a static underlying distribution. This casts doubt on the claimed novelty of the approach.

The central contribution of the paper is not entirely clear. Is it a new model, a novel algorithm for structure learning, or simply an application of existing techniques to a specific problem? The paper oscillates between these interpretations, hindering understanding. The algorithm itself blends elements from time-series analysis (TD(0) learning) and constraint-based structure learning (PC algorithm) with a novel weighting scheme based on Mahalanobis distance and pooled p-values. However, the pooling method, particularly the definition of ρ_{r+1}, is presented unclearly and requires careful scrutiny to follow. 

The technical soundness of LoSST appears reasonable, and the simulation results support the claim that it performs comparably to batch-mode learning in stable environments and outperforms it in non-stationary scenarios. However, the presentation of these results is hampered by poor visual design. Figures 1-4 employ small font sizes, lack clear color coding, and omit essential legend descriptions. This makes it difficult to interpret the results effectively. The discussion around the convergence vs. diligence trade-off (Section 3.1) is intriguing but requires further elaboration, and the claim in Section 6 about justifying increased computational cost with improved tracking feels unsubstantiated and domain-dependent.

The writing quality also suffers from minor but distracting issues, including inconsistent punctuation (particularly at the end of equations) and occasional ambiguity. These stylistic choices detract from the paper’s overall clarity.

**Strengths:**

*   The algorithm appears technically sound and demonstrates promising performance in simulations.
*   The problem addressed – online structure learning in non-stationary environments – is practically relevant.
*   The combination of concepts from time-series analysis and graphical model learning is potentially fruitful.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The related work section is incomplete and overlooks relevant contributions (e.g., Siracusa & Fisher III, 2009).
*   **Lack of Clarity:** The precise contribution of the paper (model vs. algorithm) is unclear, and the description of ρ_{r+1} is ambiguous.
*   **Presentation Issues:** Figures are poorly designed and difficult to interpret. Minor writing inconsistencies detract from clarity.
*   **Modest Significance:** While potentially stimulating future work, the proposed approach appears heuristic and may not represent a significant advancement over existing methods (particularly Bayesian alternatives).



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem is relevant and the proposed approach shows promising empirical results.
*   The algorithm could be useful in applications where online structure learning is crucial.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The claimed novelty is overstated and the related work section is incomplete.
*   The lack of clarity and poor presentation hinder understanding and evaluation.
*   The significance of the contribution is modest and the approach lacks a strong theoretical foundation.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper tackles an interesting problem and presents a potentially useful algorithm, the limited novelty, clarity issues, and presentation flaws outweigh its strengths. The authors need to more thoroughly address the existing literature, clarify their contribution, and improve the presentation of their results to make this work a compelling addition to NIPS. Addressing the omissions in related work is crucial.
