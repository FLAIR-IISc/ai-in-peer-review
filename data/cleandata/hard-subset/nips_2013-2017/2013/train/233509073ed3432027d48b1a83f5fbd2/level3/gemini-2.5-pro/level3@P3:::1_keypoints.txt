- Paper proposes LoSST: an online heuristic that differentially weights incoming time-series data, uses Mahalanobis-distance-based hypothesis tests to detect change, and reruns PC structure learning when change is detected.  
- Evaluated on simulated changepoints and a US price index dataset; outperforms a batch baseline in non-stationary settings and is comparable in stationary ones.

Strengths:
- Addresses the practical problem of learning graphical models in non-stationary environments.  
- Introduces an online hypothesis-testing and reweighting heuristic for change detection.  
- Experiments are reasonable and show the method can track structural changes.

Weaknesses — Originality & related work:
- Novelty is overstated; prior Bayesian/time-varying dependency methods (e.g., Siracusa & Fisher III 2009) already address similar problems.  
- Mischaracterizes existing score-based methods by implying they require a single generating model, ignoring sliding-window/forgetting-factor adaptations.  
- The method appears to combine standard techniques (Mahalanobis distance, p-value pooling, PC algorithm) without a clear foundational advance.

Weaknesses — Clarity & presentation:
- The precise learning objective is ambiguous (change-point detection vs. current-structure inference vs. framework).  
- The pooled p-value construction and the exclusion of p_{r+1} are opaque and insufficiently justified.  
- Manuscript quality is poor: confusing writing, illegible figures, missing legends, and formatting issues hinder reproducibility.

Weaknesses — Quality & significance:
- Claim that better tracking justifies extra computation is speculative and unsupported.  
- Overall significance is modest; the heuristic does not clearly advance the state of the art and could likely be matched by existing adaptive models.

Overall conclusion:
- Promising experimental behavior but major concerns about originality, misleading framing of related work, and lack of clarity lead to a recommendation against acceptance.
