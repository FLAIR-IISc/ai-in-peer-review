- Tackles learning graphical model structure from non-stationary data and proposes LoSST, a heuristic online structure-tracking algorithm.  
- Core mechanism: maintain weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance), score new points with Mahalanobis distance → p-value, pool recent p-values to update weights so poor fits down-weight old data, and probabilistically trigger PC on weighted stats.  
- Problem is important and relevant to many real-world domains where stationarity is unrealistic.  
- Relates to batch structure learners, online parameter methods, and changepoint detection, but the related-work discussion is misleading and overstates novelty (e.g., ignores Siracusa & Fisher 2009).  
- Strength — empirical performance: matches batch PC in stationary settings and outperforms it under changing structure; includes a compelling real-world US price index example.  
- Strength — algorithm is online, single-pass, and memory-light, suitable for streaming/large-scale data.  
- Weakness — clarity/presentation: confusing writing, awkward notation, unclear pooled p-value definition, ambiguous primary task (changepoint detection vs current-structure estimation), and unreadable figures without clear legends.  
- Weakness — originality: approach is a heuristic assembly of existing ideas (weighted averaging, Mahalanobis test, Liptak pooling, PC) rather than a fundamentally new concept.  
- Weakness — technical soundness: weight updates and relearning triggers lack a formal objective or rigorous justification; theoretical claims are sketched and some assertions (e.g., variable-specific weights) are unsubstantiated.  
- Acceptance arguments: addresses a significant problem, offers a fully online single-pass method, and shows strong empirical results in non-stationary settings.  
- Rejection arguments: presentation issues, misleading related work/overstated novelty, heuristic nature without strong theory, and overall modest significance.  
- Recommendation: require substantial revisions — clarify presentation and notation, provide a balanced discussion of prior work, and better motivate/formalize the heuristic choices.
