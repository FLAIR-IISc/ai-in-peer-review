- Paper introduces LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical model structure under local stationarity and shows it outperforms batch methods when structures change.  
- Originality is weak: prior work (e.g., Siracusa & Fisher 2009) and incrementally updatable score-based methods already address time-varying structures, so the novelty claim is unsubstantiated.  
- Related work is misrepresented by claiming existing methods assume a single generating structure, which is inaccurate for several Bayesian and score-based algorithms.  
- The p-value pooling via Liptak’s method and the core approach resemble known online changepoint detection techniques and are not novel.  
- Experimental design has flaws: the edge-removal error behaves counterintuitively after parameter changes with no theoretical explanation provided.  
- Real-world case study (US price index) is superficial and lacks comparisons to reasonable baselines like sliding-window batch learning.  
- Key methodological choices (Mahalanobis distances, pooled p-values, threshold T=0.05) lack rigorous justification or theoretical grounding.  
- The convergence/diligence trade-off is interesting theoretically but not empirically validated.  
- Clarity is poor: ambiguous definitions (e.g., ρ_{r+1}), omitted initialization details (η_r, τ_r), inconsistent presentation, and unreadable/unclear figures.  
- The distinction between detecting change points and inferring the current structure is blurred, leaving the core task ill-defined.  
- Significance is modest: problem relevance is clear but the paper doesn’t convincingly solve issues unaddressed by simpler methods (e.g., sliding windows).  
- Strengths: clear algorithmic description, practical online focus, and extensive simulation setup (500 runs across graph sizes).  
- Weaknesses outweigh strengths: novelty unsupported, methodological gaps, poor clarity, limited applicability to non-Gaussian or complex structural changes, and unprofessional figures.  
- Recommendation: Reject — authors should clarify novelty, compare rigorously to prior work (e.g., Siracusa & Fisher), justify methodological choices, improve clarity, and provide stronger empirical baselines.
