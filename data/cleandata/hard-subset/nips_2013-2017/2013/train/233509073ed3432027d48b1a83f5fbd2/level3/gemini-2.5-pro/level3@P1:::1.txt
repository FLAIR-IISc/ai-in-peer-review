This paper presents LoSST, a heuristic, online algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from data streams where the underlying generating process may be non-stationary. The core idea is to maintain a weighted estimate of the sufficient statistics (mean and covariance for SEMs) required for structure learning. At each timestep, a new datapoint is evaluated against the current estimated model using the Mahalanobis distance. This distance is converted to a p-value, and a sequence of these p-values is pooled using Liptak's method to determine if the data is becoming unlikely under the current model. If a change is suspected (i.e., the pooled p-value is low), the algorithm increases the weight of new datapoints, effectively down-weighting historical data and allowing the sufficient statistics to adapt. A standard structure learning algorithm (in this case, the PC algorithm) is then invoked probabilistically to learn a new graph, with relearning being more frequent after a suspected change. The authors evaluate LoSST on synthetic data with both structural and parametric changepoints and apply it to a real-world dataset of U.S. price indices.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses the important and practical problem of learning graphical models in non-stationary environments. The assumption of a single, stable data-generating process is a major limitation of most standard structure learning algorithms, and developing methods that can operate in an online, time-varying setting is a valuable research direction.

2.  **Algorithm Design:** The proposed LoSST algorithm is an intuitive and practical heuristic for this problem. The use of Mahalanobis distance to detect departures from the current model and the subsequent re-weighting of datapoints is a sensible approach. The algorithm's ability to function as a single-pass learner is also a notable strength for applications involving very large datasets or streaming data.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The simulation results appear sound and effectively demonstrate the algorithm's utility. The comparison against a batch-mode PC algorithm clearly shows that LoSST successfully adapts to changepoints where the batch method fails due to mixing data from different distributions. The demonstration of its performance on both structural and parameter changes is thorough.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Originality and Related Work:** The paper's claims of novelty are overstated, and the discussion of related work is misleading. The authors claim that previous methods are insufficient for this task, but Siracusa and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) explicitly present a Bayesian approach for inferring time-indexed graph structures from non-i.i.d. data. While the authors' method is frequentist and online, their characterization of prior work as being unable to handle changing structures is inaccurate. Furthermore, the claim that score-based methods assume a single generating structure is a mischaracterization. These methods assume the data used to compute a score is i.i.d., but they do not preclude adaptations like sliding windows or other weighting schemes that would make them applicable to non-stationary data. This weakens the paper's positioning and motivation.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from several clarity issues that impede understanding.
    *   The precise learning task is not clearly defined. It is ambiguous whether LoSST is primarily a changepoint detection algorithm that triggers a separate learner, or an integrated algorithm for inferring the current generating structure. The paper blends these two goals without a formal problem statement.
    *   The definition of the pooled p-value, ρ_r+1, is confusing. Footnote 1 correctly notes the circularity of including p_r+1 in its own calculation, but the main text and update equations for η and τ could be presented more clearly to show how ρ_r+1 is calculated based on historical p-values *before* being used to determine the new weight a_r+1.
    *   The overall presentation could be improved. There are minor but distracting grammatical issues, such as placing sentence-ending punctuation on a new line after an equation. The figures are difficult to read due to extremely small font sizes, and the lack of distinct colors or line styles in the legends makes them hard to interpret, especially in black and white.
    *   The discussion in Section 6 contains vague statements. For example, the claim that improved tracking could compensate for additional computational cost is self-evident and domain-dependent; it does not offer a concrete research direction.

3.  **Technical Contribution:** The proposed method is a heuristic combination of several existing statistical tools (Mahalanobis distance, p-value pooling, PC algorithm) rather than a contribution with novel theoretical underpinnings. The specific functional form for updating the weights (Equation 4) and the noisy-OR mechanism for triggering relearning feel ad-hoc. While heuristic methods are valuable, the paper would be stronger if it provided more justification for these specific design choices over other alternatives. The construction of the p-value feels complex and is hard to follow, limiting the work's accessibility and perceived novelty.

**Arguments for Acceptance**

*   The paper tackles a significant and practical problem in machine learning.
*   The proposed heuristic appears to perform well in the conducted experiments, outperforming a standard batch-mode baseline in non-stationary settings.
*   The work may encourage further research into online and non-stationary structure learning.

**Arguments for Rejection**

*   The claims of originality are not well-supported, and the characterization of related work is inaccurate, particularly regarding score-based methods and the work of Siracusa and Fisher (2009).
*   The paper suffers from significant clarity issues in its problem definition, technical descriptions, and figure presentation.
*   The contribution is primarily a heuristic assembly of existing methods, and its significance is modest. The approach feels more like an engineering solution than a fundamental advance in machine learning methodology.

In summary, while the problem is interesting and the empirical results are promising, the paper's weaknesses in its positioning relative to prior work, overall clarity, and limited technical novelty are significant.
