This paper tackles the important and largely unexplored problem of detecting changes in dependency structure within streaming data for Structural Equation Models (SEMs). The authors propose LoSST, a novel algorithm combining several techniques – batch PC structure learning triggered by tracked streaming statistics, weighted means/covariances, Mahalanobis distance-based reweighting, and a noisy-OR gate for relearning – to achieve this goal. The work is generally well-written and provides a solid literature review. Experimental evaluation demonstrates LoSST’s effectiveness on synthetic data with parameter and structural shifts, and on a real-world US price-index dataset, detecting known economic changes.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature; most structure learning algorithms assume stationarity, a restrictive assumption in many real-world applications. LoSST is a creative and potentially practical solution, leveraging existing methods in a novel way. The algorithm’s computational and memory efficiency is a definite strength, crucial for online learning scenarios. The experiments are adequate, incorporating parameter sensitivity analysis and a relevant real-world dataset. The writing is clear and the paper is generally well-organized.

**Weaknesses:** The most significant drawback is the lack of theoretical analysis. While the authors acknowledge this, a discussion of potential convergence or detection guarantees, even under simplifying assumptions, would substantially strengthen the paper. The algorithm’s design is heavily heuristic, with several *ad hoc* decisions – reweighting scheme, effective sample size calculation, and relearning criteria – that require more justification. The authors should provide a clearer rationale for these choices. Furthermore, the use of statistical methods, particularly Hotelling’s T<sup>2</sup> and Liptak’s pooling method, outside their standard theoretical guarantees is concerning. The paper should explicitly state these deviations and acknowledge the potential implications.

**Specific Concerns & Suggestions:**

* **Statistical Validity:** The application of Hotelling's T<sup>2</sup> to effective/weighted sample sizes, and the use of Liptak’s pooling under potentially non-independent test assumptions, are questionable. The authors should explicitly discuss the limitations of these approaches in their context.
* **Presentation:** The excessive reliance on footnotes for proofs detracts from readability. Detailed proofs should be moved to supplementary material. Figures are too small, poorly labeled, and lack informative captions. Experiment descriptions, particularly on p.7, are unclear and require greater detail.
* **Edge Removal Anomaly:** The explanation for LoSST’s poorer performance on edge additions during parameter-only changes is unconvincing. Decoupling parameter updates from structure updates might alleviate this issue and warrants further investigation.
* **Convergence vs. Diligence:** The discussion regarding convergence and diligence (Section 3.1) is insightful, but could be more concise and integrated into the broader discussion of algorithm parameters.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its implementation, but lacks formal guarantees. Claims are supported by experimental results, though a theoretical grounding would significantly enhance confidence. It’s a complete *first attempt* at tackling a challenging problem, rather than a fully polished, definitive solution. The authors are careful in their presentation, acknowledging limitations.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, but suffers from minor presentation issues (footnotes, figure quality). Expanding on the details of the experiments would improve clarity.

**Originality:** The problem is relatively novel, and LoSST represents a unique combination of existing techniques. It’s a clear departure from traditional static structure learning algorithms. The related work section is comprehensive.

**Significance:** The results are potentially important. Online structure learning has broad applicability in dynamic systems. If robustified with theoretical analysis and improved justification of heuristics, LoSST could become a valuable tool for practitioners and researchers. It advances the state of the art by providing a practical, albeit preliminary, solution to a difficult problem.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important, under-explored problem.
* Proposes a novel and potentially practical algorithm.
* Demonstrates promising results in both synthetic and real-world data.
* Good literature review and clear writing.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Lack of theoretical analysis and guarantees.
* Heavy reliance on heuristics with insufficient justification.
* Potential misuse of statistical methods.
* Presentation issues (figures, footnotes).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is an interesting and promising first attempt. While the lack of theoretical foundation and the reliance on heuristics are significant weaknesses, the problem addressed is important and the proposed algorithm demonstrates initial success. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the clarity and statistical validity concerns. The authors should prioritize a discussion of potential theoretical guarantees as future work.
