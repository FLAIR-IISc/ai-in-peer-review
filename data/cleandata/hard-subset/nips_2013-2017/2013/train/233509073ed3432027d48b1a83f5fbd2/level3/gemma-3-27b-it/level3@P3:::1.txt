This paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic algorithm for online learning of changing graphical model structures from time-series data. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics online and dynamically relearn the graphical model when evidence suggests a change in the underlying generating process. The approach is evaluated on simulated data and a real-world dataset of US price indices.

The paper attempts to address a relevant problem: existing structure learning algorithms largely assume stationarity, a condition often violated in real-world applications. The proposal to learn online and adapt to changing structures is a natural and valuable direction. However, the paper falls short of making a compelling contribution due to limited novelty, clarity issues, and a lack of thorough engagement with related work.

**Quality:** The results presented appear correct, and the simulation setup seems reasonable. However, the heuristic nature of the algorithm, combined with the lack of theoretical guarantees, raises concerns about its robustness and generalizability. The claim of being a "novel" algorithm feels overstated given the prior work discussed (and insufficiently addressed, see below). The application to the US price index data is a positive, providing some evidence of real-world applicability, but lacks a detailed comparative analysis against established time-series modeling techniques. The presentation of the application’s results is descriptive and doesn’t quantify the improvements offered by LoSST.

**Clarity:** The paper suffers from several clarity issues. The punctuation, particularly around paragraph breaks and equations, is inconsistent and distracting. The definition of ρ<sub>r+1</sub> is unclear and requires careful parsing. The interplay between the various parameters (γ, T, ν) and their impact on convergence vs. diligence is discussed, but remains difficult to grasp without significant effort. The overall flow is choppy, and several concepts could benefit from more detailed explanations. The relationship between the algorithm's components – sufficient statistic tracking, change detection (Mahalanobis distance, p-values, ρ<sub>r+1</sub>), and graph relearning – isn't always clearly articulated. 

**Originality:** The originality of this work is limited. The authors acknowledge, but downplay, the relevance of Siracusa & Fisher III (AISTATS 2009), which already addresses the problem of learning time-varying graphical models using a Bayesian approach. While LoSST utilizes a frequentist approach, the fundamental problem and the need to account for non-iid data are already established in that work. Furthermore, the authors’ assertion that most score-based BN algorithms assume a single generating model is misleading. Many utilize decomposable scores based on local terms and don’t inherently require this assumption. The p-value construction, while a pragmatic choice, doesn’t present a novel statistical technique and is difficult to follow in the paper’s presentation. The combination of existing techniques, while potentially useful, doesn’t constitute a substantial breakthrough.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is modest. While LoSST might spur further development in Bayesian or frequentist online structure learning, it currently reads as a heuristic implementation of ideas already present in the literature. The problem addressed *is* important, but the proposed solution doesn’t represent a significant advance over existing approaches. The claim that improved tracking justifies extra computational cost (Section 6) is unclear and seems highly domain-dependent. The lack of a strong theoretical foundation or compelling empirical advantage limits the potential impact of this work. It addresses a challenging problem but doesn't demonstrably outperform existing methods in a convincing way.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and challenging problem (online structure learning in non-stationary environments).
* Provides a functional algorithm (LoSST) that demonstrates reasonable performance on simulated data.
* Includes a real-world application, demonstrating potential applicability.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Limited novelty; significant related work (Siracusa & Fisher III, 2009) is downplayed.
* Clarity issues: poor writing, inconsistent notation, and difficult-to-follow explanations.
* Lack of strong theoretical grounding or guarantees.
* Modest significance; the algorithm doesn't offer a clear advantage over existing methods.
* Misleading claims about related work (score-based BN algorithms).



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper addresses an important problem, the limited novelty, clarity issues, and modest significance outweigh the strengths. The authors need to more thoroughly address the related work, particularly Siracusa & Fisher III (2009), and provide a clearer justification for the proposed approach. Substantial revisions to improve clarity and rigor are required before this paper would be suitable for NIPS. A more convincing demonstration of LoSST’s advantages over existing techniques, ideally with a more thorough analysis of the real-world application, is also necessary.
