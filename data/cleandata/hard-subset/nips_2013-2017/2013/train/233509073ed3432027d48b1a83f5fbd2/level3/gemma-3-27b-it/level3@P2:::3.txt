This paper introduces LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm for tracking changes in graphical model structure in dynamic, real-time environments. The core idea is to adapt structure learning to scenarios where data are only locally i.i.d., a crucial departure from the typical assumption of global stationarity in graphical model learning. The authors demonstrate through simulation and a real-world application that LoSST performs comparably to batch-mode learning when the underlying structure is stable, and significantly better when it is changing.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains a challenging problem and a practical, intuitive solution. It effectively situates the work within the existing literature on graphical model learning, online learning, and changepoint detection, demonstrating a strong understanding of related work. The motivation for tackling locally stationary data is compelling, as many real-world applications inherently involve non-stationary processes. The algorithm itself builds upon established techniques like the PC algorithm and temporal difference learning, making it accessible and readily understandable. The inclusion of a discussion on the trade-off between convergence and diligence, and how LoSST navigates that space, is a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Importance:** The problem of learning graphical models from non-stationary data is largely unexplored, and this paper provides a significant contribution by proposing a practical approach.
* **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making complex concepts easy to grasp. The organization is logical, and the explanations are thorough.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithm is based on sound statistical principles and effectively leverages existing techniques. The updates to sufficient statistics are clearly explained and justified.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation results convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of LoSST in tracking changing graphical structures, particularly when compared to a standard batch-mode approach. The application to US price index data provides further evidence of its practical utility.
* **Discussion of Trade-offs:** The insightful discussion of convergence vs. diligence highlights the inherent challenges in online learning and clarifies the design choices made in LoSST.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scope and Generalizability:** While the authors acknowledge the focus on recursive Structural Equation Models (SEMs), the claim that "other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly" feels overly strong. The performance of LoSST is specifically evaluated with the PC algorithm. The authors should either provide justification for this claim – perhaps by discussing why the core principles of LoSST are broadly applicable – or rephrase it to more accurately reflect the scope of the experimental evaluation. Furthermore, the paper should more explicitly state its SEM-specific scope upfront, and acknowledge that extending the approach to, for example, Bayesian networks may not be straightforward due to the computational challenges of maintaining sufficient statistics for discrete variables.
* **Limited Comparison:** The experimental evaluation primarily focuses on comparing LoSST to the PC algorithm. While this comparison is valuable, exploring the performance of LoSST in conjunction with other structure learning algorithms (e.g., constraint-based methods beyond PC, or score-based methods with suitable adaptations for online learning) would strengthen the findings.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of LoSST is likely sensitive to the choice of parameters like γ, T, and ν. The paper briefly mentions a somewhat unsystematic investigation of these parameters, but a more thorough sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and largely unexplored problem in graphical model learning.
* The proposed algorithm is technically sound, well-explained, and empirically validated.
* The results demonstrate a clear advantage of LoSST over batch-mode learning in dynamic environments.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and contributes to a deeper understanding of the trade-offs inherent in online learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The claim of generalizability to other structure learning algorithms requires further justification.
* The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope, focusing primarily on a comparison to the PC algorithm.
* A more thorough sensitivity analysis of the algorithm's parameters would be desirable.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed algorithm is innovative, well-motivated, and effectively demonstrated through simulations and a real-world application. While the limitations regarding generalizability and experimental scope are worth noting, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the aforementioned weaknesses, specifically refining the claim about algorithm generalizability and providing a clearer delineation of the paper's SEM-specific scope.
