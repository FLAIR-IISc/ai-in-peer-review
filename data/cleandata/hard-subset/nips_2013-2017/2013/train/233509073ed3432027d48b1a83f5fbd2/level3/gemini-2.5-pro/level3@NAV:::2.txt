This paper addresses the important and under-explored problem of detecting and adapting to changes in dependency structures, specifically for Structural Equation Models (SEMs), from a continuous data stream. The authors propose a novel algorithm, LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), which operates by tracking weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariance) in an online fashion. It creatively uses the Mahalanobis distance of incoming data points to measure "surprisingness," which in turn updates the weights of past data. This feeds into a change detection mechanism that pools p-values using Liptak's method to decide when to trigger a full, batch-based structure relearning step using the PC algorithm. The approach is a novel synthesis of ideas from online parameter tracking, changepoint detection, and classical constraint-based structure learning.

The paper’s main strengths are its originality and significance. It tackles a practical problem relevant to many real-world domains where stationarity cannot be assumed, and the proposed algorithm is an elegant and computationally efficient first attempt at a solution. The experiments are reasonably thorough, demonstrating LoSST’s ability to outperform a vanilla batch PC algorithm on synthetic data with both parameter and structural shifts, and its application to a real-world US price-index dataset shows plausible results that align with known macroeconomic events. The paper is also well-written and provides a good overview of related literature.

However, the work has several major weaknesses in its current form. The most significant is the lack of theoretical analysis. LoSST is a complex system of heuristics, and the paper provides no formal guarantees on its detection capabilities or convergence properties. This is compounded by a potential misuse of statistical methods; for instance, applying Hotelling’s T2 test to a heuristically-defined "effective" sample size and pooling p-values with Liptak’s method (which assumes independent tests) are strong assumptions that are not justified and likely violated. The justification for many ad-hoc design choices, such as the specific reweighting function and the noisy-OR relearning trigger, is also insufficient.

Minor issues include poor presentation choices, such as placing proofs in footnotes, and figures that are too small and poorly labeled, hindering reproducibility. The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on edge additions under parameter-only changes is also unconvincing.

In summary, this is a promising paper on an important topic, but its technical contribution is undermined by a heavy reliance on unjustified heuristics and questionable statistical applications. The work is more of a proof-of-concept than a complete piece of research.
