This paper proposes LoSST, a novel heuristic for online structure learning of Structural Equation Models (SEMs) in locally stationary environments. The core idea—tracking sufficient statistics and dynamically relearning the graphical model—is a valuable contribution, addressing a largely unstudied problem of learning from time-dependent data. The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and provides a clear explanation of the algorithm and its theoretical underpinnings. The simulations demonstrate the efficacy of LoSST, particularly its advantage over batch learning in non-stationary settings.

The work builds upon the established foundation of graphical model structure learning (Chickering, 2002; Pearl, 2009) and online learning techniques (Sutton, 1988; Desobry et al., 2005), specifically recognizing the limitations of existing methods when applied to time-varying data. Previous work addressing time-series graphical models (Siracusa & Fisher III, 2009; Talih & Hengartner, 2005) relies on full datasets, precluding real-time operation, a key motivation for LoSST. The paper appropriately situates itself within this landscape, highlighting its unique contribution of an online, heuristic approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Learning from non-stationary data is a critical challenge in many real-world applications. This paper directly tackles this issue with a practical, online algorithm.
* **Clear and well-organized:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. The algorithm is explained in a step-by-step manner, making it easy to understand and potentially implement.
* **Effective technique:** The simulation results convincingly demonstrate LoSST's ability to track changing structures, outperforming standard batch methods in dynamic environments. The inclusion of both parameter and structure changes in the simulations strengthens the evaluation.
* **Theoretical considerations:** The discussion of convergence vs. diligence (Kummerfeld & Danks, 2013) adds depth to the work, acknowledging the inherent trade-offs in adapting to changing environments.
* **Real-world application:** The application to US price index volatility provides a compelling demonstration of LoSST’s potential utility in a real-world context.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Overly strong claim of generalizability:** The claim that “any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly” requires more justification. While plausible, it’s an assertion made without empirical evidence beyond the PC algorithm. The authors should rephrase this to acknowledge the dependence of results on the chosen structure learning algorithm (PC) or provide experiments with other algorithms to support the claim. The paper heavily focuses on PC and its performance impacts the overall conclusions.
* **SEM-specific scope:** The paper's focus on Structural Equation Models is not immediately apparent. While the algorithm might be adaptable to other graphical models, the details related to sufficient statistics tracking are clearly SEM-specific. The paper should explicitly state this scope upfront, and acknowledge the potential non-triviality of extending the approach to Bayesian networks or other model classes. Maintaining sufficient statistics for discrete variables in Bayesian networks, for example, presents significant challenges.
* **Limited experimental evaluation:** While the simulations are well-designed, they are limited to a comparison against the PC algorithm. Investigating performance against alternative structure learning algorithms (e.g., constraint-based or score-based methods) would strengthen the conclusions and provide a broader assessment of LoSST’s applicability. Further, exploring different types of structural changes (e.g., more complex changes than single edge additions/deletions) would increase confidence in the algorithm’s robustness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and understudied problem in graphical model learning.
* The proposed algorithm is well-motivated, clearly explained, and demonstrates promising empirical performance.
* The theoretical discussion adds depth and highlights important trade-offs.
* The real-world application provides a compelling example of LoSST's potential.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The claim of generalizability needs to be qualified or supported with additional experiments.
* The SEM-specific scope should be explicitly stated.
* The experimental evaluation is limited to a single comparison algorithm.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed with further experimentation and clarification. The strengths of the paper – its clear presentation, novel approach, and promising results – outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points regarding the generalizability claim, clarifying the SEM-specific scope, and potentially including additional experimental comparisons.
