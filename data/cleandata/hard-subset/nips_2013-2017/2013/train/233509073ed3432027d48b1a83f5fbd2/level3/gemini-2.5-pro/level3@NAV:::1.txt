This paper presents LoSST, an online algorithm for learning graphical model structures from time-series data where the underlying generative process may be non-stationary. The method tracks weighted sufficient statistics, using a pooled p-value derived from Mahalanobis distances to detect distributional shifts. Upon detecting a change, the algorithm up-weights recent data points and probabilistically retriggers a standard structure learning algorithm to infer the new model. This approach is evaluated against a batch-mode baseline on both synthetic and real-world financial data.

The main strength of this work lies in addressing the important and practical problem of learning from dynamic, non-stationary data streams. The experimental results appear sound and effectively demonstrate the algorithm's ability to adapt to abrupt changes in the data-generating process, outperforming a batch learner that assumes stationarity.

Despite this, the paper has significant weaknesses in originality, clarity, and its framing of related work. The authors' claim to novelty is questionable, as prior work such as Siracusa and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) already presents a Bayesian approach for inferring time-varying graphical structures from non-iid data. The related work section is also misleading in its characterization of score-based methods; many of these algorithms do not assume a single global model but rely on locally decomposable scores, making them potentially adaptable to online settings.

Clarity is a major issue throughout the manuscript. The paper fails to precisely define its core learning task—whether it is primarily a change-point detection method or a structure inference algorithm—and whether it proposes a new model or simply a new algorithmic wrapper. The presentation suffers from confusing punctuation, an unclear definition of the pooled p-value ρ_{r+1}, and poor-quality figures with unreadably small fonts and inadequate legends.

The contribution's significance appears modest. The algorithm is presented as a collection of heuristics, with a p-value construction that is difficult to follow and justify. The claim that improved tracking can offset computational costs is unsubstantiated and domain-dependent. While the problem is compelling, the work reads as a heuristic solution to a problem that existing, more principled machine learning methods could likely address. Due to the limited originality and significant clarity issues, I cannot recommend this paper for acceptance.
