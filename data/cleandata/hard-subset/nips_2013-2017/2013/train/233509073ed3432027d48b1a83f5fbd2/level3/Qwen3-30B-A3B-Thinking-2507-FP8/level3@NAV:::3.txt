

This paper proposes LoSST, a novel algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical structure in locally stationary environments, addressing an important gap in graphical model learning where existing methods assume globally stationary data. The authors present a systematic approach that tracks sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) online using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values, dynamically triggering structure learning via the PC algorithm when changes are detected. The method is well-motivated, clearly written, and effectively tackles the under-studied problem of online structure learning from time-dependent data.

The paper's strengths are substantial: it provides a clear theoretical foundation for handling locally stationary data, demonstrates strong experimental results showing LoSST performs comparably to batch learning in stable settings and significantly better in time-varying scenarios, and offers a thoughtful analysis of the convergence-diligence tradeoff. The simulation results are convincing, showing LoSST's ability to detect structural changes within approximately 250 datapoints after a shift, while the real-world application to US price index volatility demonstrates practical relevance.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The claim that "other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly" is overly strong without justification; the experiments only compare against PC, and the paper should explicitly limit its scope to SEMs (noting that extensions to Bayesian networks may be nontrivial due to difficulties maintaining sufficient statistics). The experimental evaluation is limited to PC comparisons, which weakens the generalizability claim. Additionally, while the method is elegant for SEMs, the paper doesn't sufficiently acknowledge the limitations of applying this approach to other graphical model types.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by providing a practical solution to an important problem, though it should temper its generalization claims and clarify its specific scope. The writing is clear and well-organized, meeting high standards of presentation. Overall, this is a technically sound, well-written contribution with significant potential impact, though it would benefit from more precise scope definition and broader experimental validation. The work advances the state-of-the-art in online graphical model learning for time-varying structures.
