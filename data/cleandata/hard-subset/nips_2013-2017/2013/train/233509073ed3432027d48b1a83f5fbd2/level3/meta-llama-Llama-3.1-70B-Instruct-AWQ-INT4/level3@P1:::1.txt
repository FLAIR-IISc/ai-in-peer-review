This paper proposes an online hypothesis-testing approach to learn changing graphical models from time-series data. The authors present a novel algorithm, LoSST, which tracks changes in graphical model structure or parameters in a dynamic, real-time manner. They evaluate their approach on simulated and real datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in detecting changes in the generating structure.

The paper builds upon existing work in online learning and changepoint detection, but the authors' claim of novelty is disputed. Siracusa and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) already handle non-iid data and varying generating structures, which diminishes the originality of the proposed approach.

The results presented in the paper appear correct, but the related-work discussion is misleading. Many score-based Bayesian network algorithms do not assume a single generating model, only factorized scoring/local terms. This oversight might indicate a lack of thoroughness in the authors' review of existing literature.

Furthermore, the paper is unclear about the precise learning task it addresses. Is it detecting change points or inferring the current generating structure? Additionally, it is unclear whether the paper proposes a new model, algorithm, or both. This ambiguity makes it challenging to assess the contributions of the work.

Presentation issues also detract from the paper's overall quality. Confusing paragraph and end-of-equation punctuation, unclear definition of ρ_{r+1}, and other readability problems make it difficult to follow the authors' arguments. The figures (1-4) suffer from small font sizes and lack of color/legend descriptions, which hinders their interpretability.

The authors' claim in Section 6 that improved tracking can justify extra computational cost is unclear and seems domain-dependent. This statement requires further clarification and supporting evidence to be convincing.

In terms of originality, while the approach is somewhat original, it lacks clear novel ideas. The p-value construction, in particular, is hard to follow, and its contribution to the field is not immediately apparent.

The significance of the work is modest. While it may spur further development in Bayesian methods, it reads as heuristic development to a problem that existing machine learning methods could address. The authors could strengthen their argument by providing more compelling evidence of the superiority of their approach over existing methods.

Overall, this paper presents an online hypothesis-testing approach to learn changing graphical models from time-series data. While the results appear correct, the paper suffers from presentation issues, unclear contributions, and limited originality. The authors should address these concerns to improve the overall quality and impact of their work.
