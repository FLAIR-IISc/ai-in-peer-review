- Introduces LoSST, a novel heuristic for tracking locally stationary sufficient statistics and triggering batch PC structure learning on data streams for SEMs.  
- Tackles the under-explored problem of detecting dependency-structure changes over streaming data.  
- Efficiently tracks weighted means, correlation matrices, and effective sample size with low computational and memory cost.  
- Evaluated against vanilla PC on synthetic parameter/structure shifts and varying graph sizes/degrees.  
- Performs comparably to batch learning under global stationarity and substantially better when only local stationarity holds.  
- Demonstrated on a real US price-index dataset, detecting known economic changes and robust to short-lived fluctuations.  
- Good literature review, clear writing, and practical, creative combination of methods.  
- Experiments include parameter sensitivity analysis and a real-world case study.  
- Lacks theoretical analysis or guarantees (e.g., convergence or detection under assumptions); theory should be discussed or developed.  
- Heuristic-heavy design with many ad-hoc choices (reweighting, effective sample size, re-learn criteria) needing clearer documentation and justification.  
- Potential misuse of statistical methods (e.g., Hotelling’s T2 on weighted/effective samples, Liptak pooling assuming independence) that may violate method assumptions.  
- Authors should explicitly state when methods are used beyond theoretical guarantees to avoid misleading readers.  
- Recommend moving detailed proofs to supplementary material and improving figure size/labels and experiment descriptions.  
- Explanation for worse performance on edge additions during parameter-only changes is unsatisfactory; suggest decoupling parameter updates from structure updates.  
- Overall: interesting and practical first attempt with promise, but requires clearer justification of heuristics, better presentation, and theoretical development.  
- Recommendation: major revision before acceptance; rating 6/10, confidence 7/10.
