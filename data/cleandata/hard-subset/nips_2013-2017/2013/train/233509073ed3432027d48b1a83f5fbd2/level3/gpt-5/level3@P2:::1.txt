Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, an online heuristic for tracking time-varying graphical structures in linear, Gaussian SEMs (DAGs). The method maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) updated per sample. It monitors model fit via Mahalanobis distances and pools their p-values over time using Liptak’s method to form a statistic ρr. When ρr is small, LoSST increases the data weight ar+1 (i.e., forgets the past faster) and increases the probability of triggering a full structure relearning using the PC algorithm. The authors analyze the trade-off between “convergence” and “diligence” via parameter choices (γ, T), provide proof sketches, and evaluate on synthetic data with structural and parametric changepoints and a CPI volatility case study.

Relation to prior work
The problem of time-varying dependency structure has been studied in both Bayesian and frequentist settings, including Talih & Hengartner (2005) for undirected graphs and Siracusa & Fisher (AISTATS 2009) for time-varying directed dependencies in a Bayesian framework. Change-point detection methods (e.g., Adams & MacKay, Desobry et al.) address abrupt regime shifts; sliding windows/forgetting factors are common in online estimation and time-varying GGMs. The paper positions LoSST as filling a gap in online DAG structure learning, but some related-work claims are misleading: many score-based BN methods rely on decomposable local scores and i.i.d. assumptions for estimation, but do not “assume a single generating model” in a hard sense; rather, they are not designed to adapt to nonstationarity. The most closely related prior work (Siracusa & Fisher) already handles non-i.i.d., time-varying dependencies, albeit in batch mode; this should be acknowledged more directly, and contrasts (online, frequentist, heuristic vs Bayesian/posterior) clarified.

Strengths
- Addresses a practical and under-explored setting: online tracking of structural and parametric changes in DAGs/SEMs.
- Simple, modular approach: online sufficient-statistics tracking plus periodic constraint-based relearning.
- Pooled p-value mechanism provides an intuitive way to accumulate evidence for change beyond single outliers.
- Empirical results on synthetic data show parity with batch PC in stationary regimes and improved recovery after changes; probabilistic relearning reduces computation without large accuracy losses.
- Useful discussion of convergence vs diligence and the role of parameters.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited and claims of novelty are overstated given Siracusa & Fisher (2009) and time-varying graphical models literature; related work section is misleading in places.
- The precise learning objective is not crisply stated: is the goal change-point detection, instantaneous structure recovery, or both?
- Key components are heuristic with limited theory: the effective sample size update Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1)Sr + 1 is chosen to satisfy two constraints but not derived; the forgetting schedule ar+1 via Eq. (4) is ad hoc; “convergence/diligence” results are only sketches and rely on strong assumptions.
- Evaluation uses a weak baseline (batch PC on mixed regimes). Missing comparisons to sliding-window PC, exponential forgetting, CPD + per-segment learning, time-varying GGMs/DAGs, or simple adaptive baselines.
- Parameter sensitivity (T, γ, ν) and robustness to outliers are not systematically studied; the paper admits “unsystematic investigation.”
- Clarity issues: confusing notation/indices for ρr+1 and pr, punctuation around equations, and figure readability (small fonts, limited legends).
- Numerical stability is not discussed (e.g., covariance inversion with small effective sample sizes; need for shrinkage/regularization).

Quality
The algorithm is coherent and the simulations are consistent with the claims, but the methodology is largely heuristic and some statistical steps (e.g., use of Hotelling’s T2 with weighted effective N) need justification. The empirical evaluation omits stronger baselines and ablations. Real-data analysis is qualitative; no predictive or stability metrics are reported. Overall soundness is moderate.

Clarity
The paper is readable in parts but suffers from notational ambiguities (definition and timing of ρr and pr+1), punctuation around equations, and figure legibility. The learning task and scope (online SEMs under local stationarity) should be stated up front, along with explicit assumptions.

Originality
Moderate. The combination of pooled p-values with adaptive forgetting and probabilistic relearning for online DAG structure is interesting, but closely related ideas exist (time-varying graph learning, Bayesian dynamic structures, sliding-window/forgetting approaches). The paper’s main contribution is a pragmatic assembly suitable for online use rather than a conceptually novel algorithm.

Significance
Modest. The approach could be useful in practice and may inspire more principled (e.g., Bayesian) online methods, but the current form does not convincingly advance the state of the art due to heuristic design, limited theory, and incomplete comparisons.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to T, γ, ν? Please include ablations and guidance for setting them.
- Can you compare to sliding-window PC, exponentially weighted sufficient statistics without pooled p-values, and CPD + per-segment PC?
- How do you regularize (or shrink) Cr when Sr is small to ensure stable inversion?
- Is the Hotelling T2 approximation valid with weighted samples and time-varying Cr? Any calibration diagnostics?
- What is the computational overhead vs batch PC under stationarity? Provide wall-clock and memory use.

Suggestions for improvement
- Revise related work to accurately position against Siracusa & Fisher (2009), Talih & Hengartner (2005), and CPD methods; clarify what is new (online, frequentist, heuristic) and what is not.
- Clarify the timing and indexing of pr and ρr; define when a sample contributes to the pool and avoid circularity cleanly.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν; include ablations (no pooling, fixed forgetting, deterministic vs probabilistic relearning).
- Add stronger baselines (sliding windows, forgetting factors, CPD + PC).
- Improve figures (larger fonts, clear legends, color explanations).
- Discuss numerical stabilization (diagonal loading/shrinkage).
- If possible, provide more formal guarantees or at least conditions under which the T2 and pooling are well-calibrated.

Arguments for acceptance
- Practical online method with encouraging empirical results.
- Simple, implementable approach that may be useful to practitioners.
- Interesting discussion of convergence vs diligence.

Arguments against acceptance
- Overstated novelty and incomplete related work.
- Heuristic core with limited theoretical support.
- Missing strong baselines and parameter sensitivity analyses.
- Clarity and presentation issues.

Overall recommendation
Weak reject. The problem is important and the approach is promising, but the paper needs stronger positioning, clearer exposition, better baselines, and more rigorous validation.

Reviewer confidence
High on methodological assessment and related work; medium on domain-specific CPI interpretation.
