- Tackles online structure learning for graphical models under non-stationary data via a novel LoSST algorithm.  
- LoSST maintains online weighted sufficient statistics and an effective sample size, using Mahalanobis distance and Hotelling's T^2 to assess surprisingness.  
- Pools sequential p-values with Liptak's method to produce a change statistic ρ that increases weight on new data and probabilistically triggers PC-based structure relearning.  
- Empirically, LoSST matches batch learners in stationary settings and outperforms them when parameters or structure shift; detects known shifts in real economic data.  
- Significance and originality: addresses an important, under-explored problem and combines ideas (online stats, Mahalanobis anomaly detection, p-value pooling) creatively.  
- Practicality and efficiency: single-pass processing, small memory footprint, suitable for streaming/large datasets.  
- Empirical evaluation: thorough synthetic experiments across graph sizes/densities and a convincing real-world economics application.  
- Clarity: generally well-written with clear motivation, algorithm description, and related work placement.  
- Main weakness: highly heuristic with little theoretical analysis or guarantees on detection conditions, convergence, or covariance estimates.  
- Statistical validity concerns: Hotelling's T^2 is applied to weighted, non-i.i.d. data and pooled p-values violate Liptak's independence assumption — these uses need to be acknowledged as heuristics.  
- Ad-hoc design choices: update equations, effective sample size, and noisy-OR relearning gate lack justification or comparison to alternatives.  
- Presentation issues: figures are small/poorly labeled; experimental setup descriptions (e.g., fixed relearning schedule) are confusing and may confound comparisons.  
- Insufficient analysis of failure modes: vague explanation for worse edge-removal performance after parameter-only changes; suggests need to decouple parameter adaptation from structure relearning.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel, important problem; practical and promising method with supportive empirical results.  
- Rejection arguments: reliance on unjustified heuristics, statistical issues, and weak theoretical grounding; presentation needs improvement.
