This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm, a novel heuristic structure learning algorithm designed to track changes in graphical model structure or parameters in a dynamic, real-time manner. The authors tackle the under-explored problem of detecting changes in dependency structure over a data stream for Structural Equation Models (SEMs). The proposed algorithm efficiently tracks the locally stationary sufficient statistics, including weighted means, correlation matrix, and effective sample size, and uses batch PC structure learning triggered by these tracked streaming statistics.

The LoSST algorithm is evaluated against the vanilla PC algorithm on synthetic shifts in parameters and structure across varying variables and degrees. The results show that LoSST performs comparably to batch-mode learning when the generating graphical structure is globally stationary and significantly better when it is only locally stationary. The algorithm is also tested on a real US price-index dataset, demonstrating its ability to detect known economic changes and robustness to short-lived fluctuations.

The paper provides a good literature review and overall clear writing. The algorithm is novel, practical, and computationally/memory efficient, combining several methods creatively. The experiments are adequate and include parameter sensitivity and a real-world dataset.

However, there are several major concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the paper lacks theoretical analysis or guarantees (convergence/detection under assumptions), which should at least be discussed as future work. Secondly, the heuristic-heavy design of the algorithm, including many ad-hoc decisions (reweighting, effective sample size, re-learn criteria), needs clearer documentation. Additionally, there is a potential misuse of statistical methods, such as the application of Hotelling's T2 to effective/weighted sample sizes and Liptak pooling, which assumes conditions (e.g., independent tests) that may not hold.

To improve the paper, the authors should explicitly state when methods are used outside their theoretical guarantees to avoid misleading readers. Furthermore, detailed proofs should be moved to supplementary material, and figures should be enlarged and better labeled, with captions/legends providing more details. Experiment descriptions should also be clarified.

Moreover, the explanation for LoSST performing worse on edge additions under parameter-only changes is unsatisfactory, and the authors should consider decoupling parameter updates from structure updates.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting and practical first attempt at addressing the problem of detecting changes in dependency structure over a data stream for SEMs, it needs clearer justification of heuristic choices, better presentation, and theoretical development.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper tackles an important and under-explored problem.
* The algorithm is novel, practical, and computationally/memory efficient.
* The experiments are adequate and include parameter sensitivity and a real-world dataset.
* The paper provides a good literature review and overall clear writing.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Lack of theoretical analysis or guarantees (convergence/detection under assumptions).
* Heuristic-heavy design with many ad-hoc decisions.
* Potential misuse of statistical methods.
* Poor presentation of figures and experiment descriptions.

**Rating:** 6/10

**Confidence:** 7/10

**Recommendation:** The paper has potential, but it needs significant revisions to address the concerns mentioned above. I recommend a major revision before considering acceptance.
