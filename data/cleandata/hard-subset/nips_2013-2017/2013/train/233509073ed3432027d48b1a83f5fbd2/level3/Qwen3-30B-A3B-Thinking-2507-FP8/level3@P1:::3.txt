

This paper introduces LoSST, a novel algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical model structures in real-time under locally stationary assumptions. The work addresses a critical gap in the literature: existing structure learning methods assume globally stationary data, but real-world applications often involve dynamic environments where structures change without warning. LoSST efficiently tracks these changes by dynamically weighting data points using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values, updating sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) online, and triggering probabilistic graph relearning. The approach is rigorously grounded in the theoretical tension between convergence and diligence, offering a tunable trade-off between these desiderata.

**Strengths:**  
The paper makes a compelling contribution to an under-studied problem with high practical relevance. The core idea—leveraging locally stationary sufficient statistics tracking for real-time structure learning—is elegant, technically sound, and well-motivated. The algorithm’s simplicity (e.g., using Mahalanobis distance for change detection and Liptak’s pooling for p-values) ensures computational efficiency, avoiding prohibitive overhead. The writing is exceptionally clear, with well-structured explanations of the sufficient statistics update rules (Equations 1–4) and the convergence/diligence trade-off analysis. Experimental validation is thorough: simulations demonstrate LoSST’s parity with batch learning under global stationarity and significant superiority under local stationarity (Figures 1–3), with the US price index case study (Figure 4) providing convincing real-world relevance. The discussion of limitations (e.g., performance degradation under rapid changes) and future directions (extending to Bayesian networks, edge-specific weights) adds scholarly depth.

**Weaknesses:**  
The primary weakness lies in overgeneralizing the algorithm’s applicability. The claim that "any standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly" (Section 4) is unsupported; experiments only use the PC algorithm, while score-based methods (e.g., BDeu) cannot decompose for online learning (Section 6). This should be explicitly qualified—LoSST is SEM-specific, and extensions to discrete Bayesian networks require nontrivial modifications (e.g., maintaining sufficient statistics for conditional probability tables). The paper should clarify this scope upfront to avoid misinterpretation. Additionally, while the PC comparison is valid, broader benchmarking against other online methods (e.g., incremental Bayesian structure learning) would strengthen the significance claim. The real-world application, though insightful, is limited to a single economic dataset; more diverse case studies would better demonstrate generalizability.

**Originality and Significance:**  
The work is highly original in its focus on *online* structure learning for locally stationary data, distinct from batch-mode methods (e.g., Talih & Hengartner, 2005) or change-point detection (e.g., Adams & MacKay, 2007). It advances the state of the art by providing a practical, real-time solution to a pervasive problem in time-series graphical models, with clear implications for finance, healthcare, and systems monitoring. The significance is amplified by the algorithm’s minimal overhead in stable environments—LoSST incurs no cost when structures are globally stationary (Figure 1d–f), making it broadly deployable.

**Quality and Clarity:**  
The technical quality is strong: theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) rigorously establishes convergence/diligence trade-offs, and experiments are well-designed with sufficient statistical power (500 runs per condition). Clarity is exemplary—the paper’s organization, mathematical notation, and figures (e.g., Figure 3’s relearning dynamics) enable full reproducibility. Minor gaps include brief mentions of parameter sensitivity (e.g., T=0.05, γ=3) without justification and the lack of runtime complexity analysis for large graphs, but these are not critical to the core contribution.

**Recommendation:**  
This is a strong paper that makes a significant, well-executed contribution to time-varying graphical models. The core idea is novel, technically robust, and practically valuable. While the scope should be more precisely defined (emphasizing SEM-specificity), and additional experiments would further bolster significance, these are minor concerns. The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is of high quality (sound theory/experiments), clear, original (addressing a gap in prior work), and significant (with clear real-world impact). I recommend **acceptance** with the following minor revision: explicitly state that LoSST is designed for SEMs (not general graphical models) in the abstract and introduction, and clarify that experimental comparisons are limited to PC due to methodological constraints. The authors’ response to this point would be straightforward and enhance precision. This work is a valuable addition to NIPS 2013.
