This paper proposes a novel online hypothesis-testing approach to learn changing graphical models from time-series data, which is a significant contribution to the field of graphical models. The authors present the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm, which tracks changes in graphical model structure or parameters in a dynamic, real-time manner. The algorithm is designed to handle non-iid data and varying generating structures, which is a common problem in real-world applications.

The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work, including Bayesian and frequentist online changepoint detection algorithms, as well as previous papers that have aimed to learn time-indexed graph structures from time-series data. However, the reviewer notes that the authors' claim of novelty is disputed, as Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) already handle non-iid data and varying generating structures.

The LoSST algorithm is described in detail, including its components, such as the weighted mean, covariance, and sample size updates. The authors also provide a discussion on the convergence and diligence of the algorithm, which is an important aspect of online learning. The simulation results show that LoSST performs comparably to batch-mode learning when the generating graphical structure is globally stationary, and significantly better when it is only locally stationary.

However, the reviewer notes that the presentation of the paper has some issues, including confusing paragraph and end-of-equation punctuation, unclear definition of ρ_{r+1}, and other readability problems. The figures also suffer from small font sizes and lack of color/legend descriptions.

The reviewer also questions the originality of the approach, stating that while it is somewhat original, it lacks clear novel ideas, and the p-value construction is hard to follow. The significance of the work is also modest, as it may spur further (Bayesian) development, but reads as heuristic development to a problem existing ML methods could address.

The reviewer suggests that the authors should clarify the precise learning task, whether it proposes a new model, algorithm, or both, and provide more details on the related-work discussion. The authors should also address the presentation issues and improve the readability of the paper.

Overall, the reviewer concludes that the paper is a good scientific contribution, but it needs some revisions to improve its clarity, originality, and significance.
