This paper introduces LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a novel heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from data where the underlying generative process may change over time. The key assumption is that the data is locally stationary, rather than globally stationary as assumed by most standard structure learning algorithms. The LoSST algorithm works in an online fashion, processing one data point at a time. It maintains a weighted estimate of the sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and effective sample size for the presented Structural Equation Model case). The weight of each new data point is determined by how well it fits the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. A pooled p-value method is used to aggregate these fit scores over time to robustly detect changes. Based on the detection of a change, the algorithm probabilistically triggers a relearning of the graphical model structure. The authors position this work as filling a critical gap between batch-mode structure learning (which assumes stationarity) and online parameter learning or changepoint detection (which do not typically handle structure learning). The paper provides an analysis of the algorithm's properties, distinguishing between "convergent" and "diligent" parameter regimes, and demonstrates its performance through simulations and on a real-world economic time-series dataset.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a very important and practical problem that has been largely overlooked by the graphical model structure learning community. The assumption of i.i.d. data is a major limitation of standard algorithms when applied to real-world systems, which are often dynamic. This work proposes a novel and principled-though-heuristic approach to tackle this challenge, which is a significant contribution. The combination of online sufficient statistic tracking, a robust change detection mechanism based on Mahalanobis distance, and a probabilistic relearning schedule is a novel approach to this problem.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated, the LoSST algorithm is described in sufficient detail, and the mathematical derivations for the online updates are presented clearly. The distinction between convergence and diligence is insightful and adds theoretical depth.

3.  **Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The use of weighted sufficient statistics is a natural way to handle non-stationarity, and the choice of Mahalanobis distance is well-justified for the multivariate Gaussian case. The experiments are well-designed, demonstrating that LoSST performs comparably to a standard batch algorithm (PC) in stationary environments (incurring no cost for its added flexibility) and substantially outperforms it when the underlying model changes. The application to US price index data provides a compelling case study of its real-world applicability.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Scope and Generality:** The paper's implementation and experiments are focused on learning Structural Equation Models (SEMs) over continuous variables. The abstract and introduction, however, frame the problem in the more general context of "graphical models." While the authors mention in the discussion that extending the method to other models like Bayesian networks over discrete variables is a topic for future work, the challenges associated with this are significant. Maintaining weighted sufficient statistics for discrete variables (i.e., counts for conditional probability tables) is substantially more complex from a memory and computational standpoint. It would strengthen the paper to state its specific focus on SEMs more explicitly in the introduction and to be more cautious about the direct applicability of the proposed techniques to other model classes.

2.  **Unsupported Claims:** The paper claims that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly" to the PC algorithm used in the experiments. This is a very strong claim that is not substantiated. The behavior of score-based methods, for instance, on data from mixed distributions could be quite different from constraint-based methods like PC. This statement should be softened to reflect that the experiments were conducted with the PC algorithm, and the results may not generalize to all other learning algorithms without further investigation.

3.  **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the superiority of LoSST over a naive batch-mode application of the PC algorithm. However, this is a somewhat weak baseline for a non-stationary setting. A more comprehensive evaluation could include comparisons against other plausible heuristics, such as applying the PC algorithm on a sliding window of recent data. Including such a baseline would provide a better sense of how much of the performance gain is due to the specific weighting and change-detection scheme of LoSST versus simply forgetting old data.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution by proposing a novel, effective, and clearly-described algorithm for an important and under-studied problem. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly relate to the framing of the contributions and the scope of the experimental evaluation. They can be addressed by toning down some of the general claims and being more explicit about the specific scope of the method. The paper is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community and to inspire further research in learning from non-stationary data.
