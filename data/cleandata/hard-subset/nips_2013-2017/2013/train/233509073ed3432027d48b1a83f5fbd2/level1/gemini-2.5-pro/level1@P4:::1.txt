### Review

**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper introduces LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a novel, online algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from data streams where the underlying generative process may be non-stationary. The central problem addressed is that most existing structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data from a single, stable generative model, an assumption often violated in real-world applications. LoSST aims to track changes in both the parameters and the directed acyclic graph (DAG) structure of a Structural Equation Model (SEM) in real-time.

The core of the algorithm involves tracking weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an "effective" sample size). Each new data point is assigned a weight, and these weights are updated dynamically. The algorithm detects potential changes in the underlying model by measuring the fit of new data points to the current estimated model using the Mahalanobis distance. To avoid overreacting to single outliers, it uses Liptak's method to compute a weighted pooled p-value over the sequence of Mahalanobis distances. A low pooled p-value, indicating a persistent mismatch between the data and the model, causes the algorithm to increase the weights of subsequent data points, effectively "forgetting" older data and adapting to the new regime. Structure re-learning, using a standard algorithm like PC, is triggered probabilistically, with the probability of re-learning increasing when a change is detected.

The paper contrasts this approach with previous work. Unlike standard batch-mode learners (e.g., PC, score-based methods), LoSST does not assume stationarity. Unlike simple online parameter trackers (e.g., TD(0)), it can learn changes in graph structure. Compared to online changepoint detection (CPD) algorithms, LoSST aims to handle both abrupt and gradual changes without storing a large history of data points. Finally, unlike prior batch-mode methods for time-varying graphs (e.g., Talih & Hengartner, 2005; Siracusa & Fisher, 2009), LoSST operates online in a single pass. A theoretical analysis is presented, suggesting that LoSST's parameters allow it to be configured as either "convergent" (stabilizing on a fixed structure in the long run) or "diligent" (quickly reacting to change), or to strike a balance between these competing virtues.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The update equations for the weighted sufficient statistics are well-defined. The use of Mahalanobis distance as a measure of fit for multivariate Gaussian data is standard and appropriate. The key innovation of using a pooled p-value to provide a more robust signal of change is well-motivated and appears effective. The experimental evaluation is a significant strength, providing a clear comparison against a standard batch-mode PC algorithm on synthetic data. The simulations convincingly demonstrate that LoSST performs comparably to the batch learner in a stationary environment and substantially better after a changepoint. The design of the simulations, which separately test structure+parameter changes and parameter-only changes, adds valuable insight. The inclusion of a real-world application, while qualitative, helps demonstrate the potential utility of the method.

However, there are some areas where the technical details could be strengthened.
1.  The algorithm relies on inverting the covariance matrix `Cr` to compute the Mahalanobis distance. In high-dimensional settings or when the effective sample size `Sr` is small (e.g., immediately after a detected change), this matrix may become ill-conditioned or singular. The paper does not discuss how this potential issue is handled (e.g., via regularization).
2.  The theoretical claims about convergence vs. diligence are intriguing but are presented only as "proof sketches" in footnotes. A more formal and complete treatment, perhaps in an appendix, would be necessary to fully substantiate these claims.
3.  The algorithm contains several heuristic components. For instance, the specific linear function for updating the weight `ar+1` (Equation 4) and the noisy-OR gate for the probabilistic re-learning schedule are presented without strong theoretical justification. While they appear to work well in practice, their principled derivation is unclear.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is established effectively in the introduction. The algorithm's components are explained logically and sequentially, from statistic tracking to change detection and re-learning. The figures in the results section are clear and effectively communicate the performance of the algorithm over time. The distinction between the algorithm's different operating regimes (convergent, diligent, balanced) is explained conceptually, which aids understanding.

One point of slight ambiguity is the interaction between the effective sample size `Sr` and the underlying constraint-based learning algorithm (PC). The PC algorithm's conditional independence tests are sensitive to sample size. A sudden drop in `Sr` after a detected change would reduce the statistical power of these tests, potentially leading to an overly sparse graph. This is hinted at in the discussion of Figure 2 but could be made more explicit.

**Originality:**
The paper's contribution is original and significant. While its components are based on existing ideas (online updates, Mahalanobis distance, pooled p-values, PC algorithm), their combination into an end-to-end system for online graphical model structure learning is novel. It addresses a critical gap between traditional structure learning, which assumes stationarity, and real-world data streams, which often do not. The explicit framing of the trade-off between convergence and diligence is also a valuable conceptual contribution to the literature on online learning in changing environments. The work is well-situated within the related literature and clearly articulates its points of departure from previous methods.

**Significance:**
The problem of learning from non-stationary data is of high importance to the machine learning community, with applications in finance, sensor networks, neuroscience, and many other domains where systems evolve over time. This paper provides a practical, well-motivated, and empirically validated algorithm for a challenging version of this problem. The finding that LoSST incurs little to no performance cost in stationary settings makes it an attractive alternative to traditional batch methods even when stationarity is uncertain. The ideas presented—particularly the use of weighted statistics driven by a robust change detection signal—are likely to be influential and could be adapted for other online learning tasks beyond graphical models. Researchers and practitioners working with streaming data are likely to find this work useful and build upon it.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a highly significant and practical problem: learning graphical model structure from non-stationary data streams.
*   Presents a novel and coherent algorithm (LoSST) that combines several sound ideas in an original way.
*   The core mechanism for change detection using pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances is robust and well-motivated.
*   Empirical results are strong, showing clear benefits over a standard batch-mode baseline in non-stationary settings with no cost in stationary ones.
*   The paper is clearly written and the contributions are easy to understand.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Several key design choices are heuristic, and their theoretical justification is limited.
*   The sensitivity of the algorithm to its hyperparameters (`T`, `γ`, `ν`) is not analyzed, and their selection is described as "unsystematic."
*   The theoretical claims regarding convergence and diligence are not fully proven, being presented only as sketches.
*   The experimental comparison could be stronger by including other plausible online baselines, such as a sliding-window approach or a combination of a standard CPD algorithm with a batch learner.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—its novelty, significance, and strong empirical results for an important problem—outweigh its weaknesses. The weaknesses are primarily areas for future development rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed approach.
