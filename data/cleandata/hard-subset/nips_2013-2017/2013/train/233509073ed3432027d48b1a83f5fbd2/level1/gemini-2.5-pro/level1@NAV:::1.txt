This paper presents LoSST, a novel online algorithm for learning graphical model structure from data streams where the underlying data-generating process is only locally stationary. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, effective sample size) using a dynamic weighting scheme. Incoming data points are weighted based on their fit to the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. A poor fit, indicated by a low pooled p-value over recent data, increases the weight of new data, effectively "forgetting" the past and triggering a relearning of the graph structure. This work addresses a critical gap between traditional batch-mode structure learning algorithms, which assume i.i.d. data, and online changepoint detection methods, which typically do not perform general structure learning. The authors position their work effectively against prior art like Talih & Hengartner (2005) and Siracusa & Fisher (2009), highlighting LoSST’s ability to operate in real-time on directed acyclic graphs.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its originality and significance. It tackles the important and practical problem of learning in non-stationary environments, a common challenge in real-world systems. The proposed method is an elegant combination of established statistical techniques, and the theoretical discussion of the trade-off between "convergence" (stability in the long run) and "diligence" (rapid detection of change) is particularly insightful. The quality of the empirical evaluation is high; the simulations convincingly demonstrate that LoSST performs comparably to a batch-mode algorithm (PC) in stationary settings and significantly outperforms it after a structural or parametric shift. The application to U.S. price index data further validates the method's practical utility.

Despite these strengths, the work has some minor weaknesses. The algorithm relies on several heuristic components and hyperparameters (e.g., the weight update function in Eq. 4, the relearning probability parameter ν), whose settings were determined through "unsystematic investigation." A more systematic sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims of robustness. Furthermore, the computational complexity is not fully addressed. Calculating the Mahalanobis distance requires inverting the covariance matrix at each timestep, which could be a bottleneck for high-dimensional problems, limiting scalability beyond the tested 20 variables.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and practical limitation of existing structure learning methods.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated online algorithm.
*   Provides a strong theoretical framing of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off.
*   Includes a thorough empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Relies on heuristic parameter choices without a full sensitivity analysis.
*   Scalability to high-dimensional data is a potential concern and is not discussed in detail.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant contribution. The strengths clearly outweigh the weaknesses, which could be addressed in future work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
