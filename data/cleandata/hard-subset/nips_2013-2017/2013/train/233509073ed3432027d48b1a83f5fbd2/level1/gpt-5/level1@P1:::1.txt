Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LoSST, an online heuristic for learning and tracking the structure of directed acyclic graph (DAG) models (specifically linear-Gaussian SEMs) when the data-generating process may change over time. The core idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) updated in a single pass with time-varying weights ar that increase when recent data are poorly explained by the current model. Poor fit is quantified via Mahalanobis distances combined across time by Liptak’s pooled p-value. The algorithm periodically (probabilistically) triggers a structure-learning step (here, PC [12]) using the current weighted sufficient statistics. The authors analyze when LoSST tends toward convergence versus “diligence” (rapid responsiveness to changes), and show simulations where LoSST matches batch PC on stationary segments and outperforms it after changepoints. A small real-data case study on U.S. CPI components suggests LoSST detects macroeconomic volatility shifts.

This work connects to: (i) batch DAG structure learning (score-based [3], constraint-based [10,12]), (ii) online change-point detection (frequentist [4], Bayesian [1]), and (iii) time-varying graphical models (Talih & Hengartner [14] for undirected graphs; Bayesian time-indexed edge posteriors [11]). Unlike CPD, LoSST emphasizes continuous adaptation (no explicit run-length posterior) and unlike prior time-varying graph work, it operates online and targets DAG SEMs. Related concurrent literature not cited includes time-varying undirected Gaussian graphical models (e.g., Kolar & Xing, Zhou et al., fused/weighted graphical lasso) and sliding-window/forgetting-factor CI testing; while not DAG-focused, they constitute competitive baselines for structure tracking in continuous domains.

Quality
- Methodological soundness: The sufficient-statistics updates for weighted means/covariances are correct, and the single-pass memory footprint is attractive (O(p^2)). However, several key steps are heuristic:
  - The use of Hotelling’s T^2 to turn Mahalanobis distances into p-values assumes a specific sampling distribution that does not strictly hold when using adaptively weighted covariances and when the test point influenced the estimated covariance (even though you try to avoid circularity in ρr+1). The effective sample size Sr derivation (Eq. 3) is plausible but not justified as yielding correct null distributions for CI tests or T^2.
  - Liptak pooling assumes independence of p-values; here they are serially dependent. It is reasonable as a smooth detector but undermines probabilistic calibration.
  - The relearning trigger depends on ρr and a noisy-OR schedule; hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) are tuned informally with limited sensitivity analysis.
  - PC on weighted sufficient statistics: Using Fisher’s Z/partial correlations requires an effective dof; substituting Sr is reasonable but unvalidated. No discussion of Type I/II error control under adaptive weighting.
- Theory: The “convergence vs diligence” discussion is insightful. The provided proof sketches indicate parameter regimes where LoSST converges (γ=1 or specific Tr schedules) or is “diligent” (T>1, γ>1), but the conditions rest on heuristic quantities (ρ, Sr) and omit formal guarantees for the induced CI tests/search. More rigorous statements (e.g., bounds on detection delay, false alarm rates, or consistency under local stationarity) would improve confidence.
- Empirics: Simulations convincingly show that with abrupt changepoints LoSST can recover new structures faster than batch PC on the full mixture. However:
  - Baselines are limited. Using batch PC on all data is an unfavorable baseline after a change; more appropriate comparators include sliding-window PC, exponential-forgetting PC without pooled p-values, CPD+restart PC, or dynamic GGMs (even if undirected).
  - Sensitivity to T, γ, ν, and the relearning schedule is not systematically explored; an ablation of the components (pooled p-values vs simple EWMA; fixed vs probabilistic relearning) would clarify what drives gains.
  - Real data example is interesting but qualitative; it detects the Great Moderation-like behavior, yet no external validation, predictive improvement, or robustness checks (e.g., different differencing schemes, lagged variables) are provided.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written with clear motivation and algorithm description. The update equations are helpful. Some details are missing or could be clarified:
- Explicitly state the CI test used by PC and how Sr and Cr feed into it.
- Discuss numerical stability (matrix inversion of Cr when Sr is small and p relatively large) and any regularization.
- Provide pseudocode and a short complexity discussion (cost per step and per relearning).
- Minor typos/footnote artifacts (e.g., “graph2 relearning”) should be fixed.

Originality
The combination of online weighted sufficient-statistics tracking, pooled p-values to modulate forgetting, and periodic constraint-based structure relearning for DAG SEMs appears novel. While components (forgetting factors, Mahalanobis-based detectors, sliding windows) are known, their integration to enable real-time DAG structure tracking is a meaningful contribution. The theory on convergence/diligence trade-offs, though informal, is a nice conceptual addition.

Significance
The problem—tracking changing causal/graphical structure online—is important and underexplored for DAGs. The method is simple, scalable, and practical. That said, the current evaluation and theoretical guarantees are modest; broader baselines and more rigorous calibration would be needed to influence practice widely. Still, as a first step toward online DAG structure tracking with low memory, it has potential to spur follow-on work.

Pros
- Addresses an important, timely problem (nonstationary structure learning) squarely within NIPS scope.
- Simple, memory-efficient, single-pass updates; adaptable to probabilistic relearning schedules.
- Good simulation evidence that LoSST behaves like batch PC when stationary and better after changes.
- Clear exposition; thoughtful discussion of convergence vs diligence.

Cons
- Heuristic statistical foundations (p-value pooling, effective sample size, use of T^2) without calibration or guarantees.
- Limited baselines; lacks comparisons to sliding-window/exponential-forgetting PC, CPD+restart, or time-varying GGMs.
- Sparse sensitivity analysis and ablations; hyperparameter selection is ad hoc.
- Real-data study is qualitative and small-scale; no quantitative validation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: sliding-window PC with window sizes; EWMA-only forgetting; CPD-triggered restarts; dynamic/TV GGMs (even if undirected) to test detection/tracking speed and errors.
- Provide ablation/sensitivity studies over T, γ, ν and relearning frequency; report stability of results.
- Calibrate statistics: examine empirical null distributions of Mahalanobis distances with your weighted covariance; assess false alarm rates; consider robust alternatives (e.g., robust covariance, EWMA charts) and assess ρ’s calibration.
- Explain how CI tests in PC are adapted to weighted stats; discuss degrees-of-freedom choices and multiple testing impacts; consider regularized covariance estimates when Sr is small.
- Expand real-data evaluation: external events, predictive checks, alternative preprocessing, lagged variables to separate volatility shifts from structural changes.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper tackles an important problem with an appealing and practical approach and shows promising empirical behavior. However, the reliance on heuristic statistical machinery without calibration, limited baselines, and lack of systematic sensitivity analysis undermine confidence in the generality of the results. With stronger empirical comparisons and clearer statistical justification, this work could make a solid contribution.
