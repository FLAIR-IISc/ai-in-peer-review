Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces LoSST, an online, heuristic algorithm for learning and tracking time-varying graphical structures in linear Gaussian SEMs (DAGs). The key idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) that adapt to suspected changes in the data-generating process. Adaptation is driven by a pooled p-value ρr based on Mahalanobis distances of incoming observations relative to the current covariance estimate, aggregated with Liptak’s method using the same weights used in the sufficient statistics. When ρr falls below a threshold, the per-sample weight is increased up to a factor γ, implementing adaptive forgetting. Structure is relearned intermittently using a constraint-based method (PC), with a probabilistic relearning trigger increasing with 1 − ρr. The paper analyzes convergence vs. “diligence” (fast change detection) as a function of parameters (γ, T), and evaluates LoSST on synthetic SEMs with structural and parametric changes, as well as a real dataset of US price indices.

Compared to prior work, batch structure learning (e.g., PC, score-based BN learning) assumes global stationarity; online changepoint detection (Bayesian and frequentist) detects abrupt changes but typically requires storing substantial history and assumes piecewise constant regimes. Prior time-varying structure work is cited for undirected graphs (Talih & Hengartner, 2005) and Bayesian posterior edge trajectories (Siracusa & Fisher, 2009), both batch. LoSST differs by combining online tracking of weighted sufficient statistics with adaptive relearning for directed structures, aiming to work in real-time without storing historical data.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important gap: online tracking of time-varying DAG structure, with a single-pass, memory-efficient procedure (O(p^2) for p variables).
- Adaptively balances stability and responsiveness via a plausible, interpretable mechanism (pooled p-values from Mahalanobis distances controlling forgetting and relearning rates).
- Clear separation of statistic tracking and structure inference allows swapping in other learners.
- Convergence/diligence discussion clarifies behavioral regimes of the algorithm and conditions under which LoSST stabilizes vs. remains reactive.
- Synthetic experiments are reasonably thorough across graph sizes/degrees, with multiple change scenarios, and show that LoSST matches batch PC under stationarity and outperforms it when the distribution changes.
- Real-data case study detects known macroeconomic regime shifts in volatility, lending face validity.

Weaknesses
- Statistical assumptions underlying ρr are not fully justified. The Mahalanobis distance distribution (Hotelling’s T^2) is used with a dynamically weighted covariance and an “effective” sample size Sr; the validity of the T^2 approximation under adaptive weighting and time-varying parameters is unclear. Sequential dependence among distances also violates the independence assumption of Liptak pooling. These approximations should be assessed empirically (calibration plots) or theoretically bounded.
- Baselines are limited. Comparing LoSST only to global batch PC is favorable in nonstationary settings. Missing baselines include sliding-window or exponentially-weighted PC with fixed forgetting, CPD+restart PC, and time-varying undirected methods (e.g., TV-GLasso) on moralized graphs to gauge edge recovery, as well as dynamic BN or switching models where applicable.
- Hyperparameter selection (γ, T, ν) is ad hoc. No sensitivity analysis or guidance is provided beyond “unsystematic investigation,” despite these parameters governing core trade-offs. Reporting detection delays, false alarm rates, and error as functions of these parameters would improve interpretability.
- Some derivations and notation need clarification/correction. The sample-size update (Eq. 3) appears to require Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1) Sr + 1 (a fraction); the text has typographical ambiguity. The weight update formula and bounds could be stated more cleanly, with explicit behavior at ρ=T and ρ> T. Early-sample regularization of Cr (invertibility/shrinkage) is not discussed.
- Theoretical analysis is limited. Beyond qualitative convergence/diligence claims (with sketches), there are no guarantees on detection delay or error under local stationarity, nor consistency of the learned structure within regimes.
- Scope limited to linear Gaussian SEMs with PC for learning. Extension claims to undirected models and discrete BNs are plausible but untested.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The online updates for weighted means/covariances are standard. The use of Hotelling’s T^2 with an “effective” N and pooled p-values is heuristic; this is the main soundness concern. The convergence/diligence arguments are plausible but informal. Experiments are well-designed for the chosen baseline but do not cover stronger alternatives.
- Completeness: The method is implementable from the description, but more detail on numerical stability and early-phase regularization would help reproducibility.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized, with intuitive motivation and algorithmic flow. Some algebraic clarity and notation fixes are needed (Eq. 3; indexing in pooling; guidance on T>1 regime which is unusual since ρ∈(0,1]).

Originality
- The combination of adaptive, pooled goodness-of-fit to drive forgetting and online DAG structure relearning appears novel. However, individual components (exponentially weighted statistics, Mahalanobis monitoring, p-value pooling, PC) are known; the contribution is chiefly in their integration for online structure learning.

Significance
- Moderate. If validated against stronger baselines and with more rigorous statistical justification, LoSST could be a practical tool for tracking changing causal/associational structures in streaming data. As is, the impact is limited by heuristic elements and missing comparisons.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: sliding-window PC with tuned window size; fixed-forgetting EW-PC; BOCPD+restart PC; and a time-varying undirected method (e.g., varying graphical lasso) as a proxy.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for γ, T, ν; report detection delay and false alarm metrics; ablate pooled p-values vs. raw distances.
- Assess calibration of ρr under stationarity (QQ plots of distances; empirical Type I error of triggers).
- Clarify Eq. (3), invertibility/regularization of Cr, and early-sample behavior; discuss robustness to outliers (e.g., robust covariance).
- Consider theoretical bounds under piecewise-stationary models (e.g., detection delay vs. jump size; error within stable regimes).

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem: online learning of time-varying DAG structure.
- Simple, memory-efficient algorithm with promising empirical results on synthetic and real data.
- Clear, modular design enabling adoption and extension.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heuristic statistical foundations (T^2 with adaptive weighting; p-value pooling with dependence) not justified or validated.
- Missing strong baselines; gains over batch PC do not establish state-of-the-art performance.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity and selection are not analyzed; theory is limited to sketches.
