- Introduces LoSST, a novel heuristic for tracking graphical model structure changes in real-time.  
- Core idea: maintain sufficient statistics online and dynamically relearn graph structure for locally stationary data.  
- Provides simulations showing LoSST matches batch learning under stationarity and outperforms it under changing structures.  
- Includes a real-world application to US price index volatility demonstrating practical potential.  
- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and effectively motivates the problem.  
- Algorithm description is step-by-step with sufficient detail for reproduction.  
- Simulation setup and results are explained and presented clearly.  
- Related work overview is comprehensive and highlights limitations of existing online/structure-learning methods.  
- Discussion acknowledges limitations and suggests promising future research directions.  
- Novelty lies in combining online sufficient-statistics tracking with dynamic graph relearning for real-time structure learning.  
- Strong empirical claim: comparable to batch-mode under stationary conditions and superior in non-stationary environments.  
- Authors thoughtfully discuss the convergence vs. diligence trade-off.  
- Technical components (update equations, weighting scheme, Hotelling’s T^2, Liptak pooling) are sound and appropriate.  
- Limited theoretical justification for parameter choices (e.g., γ and T); relies mainly on empirical tuning.  
- Computational complexity concerns: PC algorithm cost noted but overall LoSST runtime/scalability analysis is lacking.  
- Empirical evaluation could be expanded (vary change speeds, graph complexity) to strengthen claims.  
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, novel combination of techniques, clear presentation, promising simulations, and real-world demo.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited theory on parameters/convergence, scalability concerns, need for broader simulations, and heuristic nature may limit generalizability.  
- Overall recommendation: solid contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to justify parameter choices and address computational scalability.
