This paper tackles online structure learning for nonstationary graphical models. The proposed LoSST algorithm maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) for linear-Gaussian SEMs, where weights adapt based on pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances computed against the current covariance estimate. When the pooled evidence indicates model misfit, weights increase (effectively forgetting older data) and the probability of relearning the graph structure rises; structure is learned via the PC algorithm. The authors show by simulation that LoSST matches batch-mode PC under global stationarity and adapts more quickly after structural or parametric changes. They analyze parameter regimes trading off convergence and “diligence” (rapid response to change), giving sufficient conditions for each. A small real-data case study on CPI components suggests LoSST identifies macroeconomic regime shifts. The work connects to online TD learning, Bayesian/frequentist changepoint detection (e.g., Adams & MacKay), and time-varying graphical structures (Talih & Hengartner; Siracusa & Fisher). It also relates to exponential forgetting/EWMA covariance tracking and dynamic/DBN models (e.g., Murphy), and to time-varying GGMs (e.g., Kolar et al., NIPS 2009), which are not cited.

Quality: The algorithm is technically plausible and well-motivated; the sufficient-statistic updates are correct for weighted samples, and the relearning scheme is simple and efficient. However, several elements are heuristic without rigorous guarantees: (i) using Hotelling’s T2 with an “effective” sample size for weighted covariances; (ii) Lipták pooling assumes independence that likely fails in time series; (iii) no formal bounds on detection delay, false alarm, or structural recovery under drift. Comparisons lack strong baselines (e.g., sliding-window PC, exponentially weighted PC, BOCPD + local reestimation, time-varying GGM methods).

Clarity: The manuscript is generally clear and well-organized, with helpful update equations and parameter discussion. Some proofs are sketched; full statements would improve credibility. Practical details (regularization/inversion stability, computational cost vs. relearning frequency, hyperparameter tuning) need elaboration.

Originality: Combining adaptive weighting via pooled p-values with online sufficient statistics and probabilistic relearning for general DAG structure is novel relative to batch time-varying methods and CPD. Relation to time-varying GGMs/DBNs should be clarified.

Significance: Online structure tracking is important for many applications; the single-pass design is appealing. With stronger theory and baselines, impact could be high.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important, underexplored online structure-learning problem.
- Simple, general framework with single-pass statistics and adaptive relearning.
- Solid simulations; no loss under stationarity, clear gains under change.

Cons for acceptance:
- Heuristic statistical tests (pooled p-values, effective df) lack justification.
- Missing comparisons to natural baselines and related time-varying network literature.
- Limited theoretical guarantees and ablation/sensitivity analyses.
- Real-data evaluation is suggestive but small.
