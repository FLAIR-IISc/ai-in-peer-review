## Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

This paper introduces LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm for online structure learning in graphical models, addressing a significant gap in the field which largely assumes static underlying generative processes. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics online and dynamically relearn the graphical model structure when changes are detected via a Mahalanobis distance-based change point detection scheme. This is a valuable contribution as real-world data often violates the i.i.d. assumption central to traditional methods. The paper clearly positions LoSST within the existing literature, contrasting it with Bayesian/score-based and constraint-based methods, as well as online changepoint detection algorithms and prior work on time-indexed graphs. 

The strengths of the paper lie in its practical focus and clear presentation. The algorithm is well-motivated, and the authors carefully consider the trade-off between convergence and diligence, providing a nuanced discussion of how the parameters influence the algorithm’s behavior. The simulation results demonstrate LoSST’s ability to track changes in both graph structure and parameters, and to perform competitively with batch-mode learning when the structure is stable. The application to US price index volatility offers a compelling illustration of the algorithm's potential in a real-world setting. The inclusion of detailed mathematical formulations for updating sufficient statistics and determining weights contributes to the paper's rigor.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the simulations provide compelling evidence, they are somewhat limited in scope. Exploring more complex dynamic scenarios – for example, scenarios with gradual rather than abrupt changes or with multiple interacting variables undergoing change – would strengthen the claims. The reliance on the PC algorithm for structure learning is a potential bottleneck and limiting factor. The performance gains claimed in the simulations are also tied to the specific settings of the probabilistic relearning schedule, and sensitivity to these parameters requires further discussion. Additionally, the choice of Mahalanobis distance as the sole indicator of change may be sensitive to outliers and dimensionality, warranting consideration of alternative change detection methods. Finally, the discussion of related work, while thorough, doesn't fully engage with recent advancements in online non-parametric graphical model learning.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a crucial gap in the literature by providing an algorithm for online structure learning.
*   Clear and well-motivated approach with a sound theoretical basis.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance in simulation and a promising application to real-world data.
*   Thoughtful discussion of the trade-off between convergence and diligence.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Simulations are somewhat limited in scope and complexity.
*   Reliance on the PC algorithm introduces a potential bottleneck.
*   Sensitivity analysis of the probabilistic relearning schedule could be more comprehensive.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the topic is highly relevant to current research trends in machine learning and causal inference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting more detailed sensitivity analysis and expansion of the simulation studies.
