Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces LoSST, an online heuristic for tracking time-varying graphical models, specifically linear-Gaussian SEMs (DAGs). The core idea is to maintain, in a single pass, exponentially reweighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) with adaptive weights ar driven by a pooled p-value of recent Mahalanobis distances. When pooled evidence suggests poor fit (low pooled p-value), the algorithm increases the weight assigned to incoming data to “forget” the past more quickly; it periodically re-learns the DAG using a constraint-based structure learner (PC). The paper analyzes a trade-off between “convergence” and “diligence” (responsiveness to changes), arguing these properties are incompatible and showing how algorithm parameters T and γ interpolate between them. Simulations show LoSST matches batch PC when the process is stationary and outperforms batch PC after changes in structure or parameters. A brief case study on U.S. CPI components suggests LoSST detects volatility regime shifts (e.g., Great Moderation).

This work sits at the intersection of online learning, change-point detection (e.g., Adams & MacKay 2007), and structure learning (e.g., PC; Spirtes et al.; Chickering). It contrasts with batch time-varying structure estimation in undirected models (e.g., Kolar, Song, and Xing on time-varying Gaussian graphical models; Talih & Hengartner 2005) and posterior-tracking approaches for temporal dependence (Siracusa & Fisher 2009). The novelty is an online, memory-light procedure for DAG structure tracking that avoids maintaining distributions over model spaces.

Quality:
- Methodological soundness: The sufficient-statistics updates for weighted means and covariances are standard; the pooled p-value (Lipták) as a compact accumulated statistic is reasonable. The ar update (piecewise linear map from pooled p-value; Eq. 4) and the effective sample size update (Eq. 3) are heuristic. The convergence/diligence statements are supported by sketches, but full proofs (and exact conditions) are deferred or depend on a tech report; the claimed incompatibility of convergence vs. diligence is plausible but not verified here. The approach assumes multivariate Gaussianity for Mahalanobis T2 and for PC’s partial-correlation tests; the impact of deviations from Gaussianity is not studied.
- Empirical support: Synthetic studies are carefully controlled (varying #vars and max degree), demonstrate parity with batch PC in stationary regimes and clear gains post-change in structure. The parameter-change-only setting reveals a short-lived disadvantage on edge removals, which is informative. The probabilistic relearning study shows fewer relearnings without significant error inflation. However, baselines are limited to batch PC on all data. Missing comparisons include (a) simple sliding window + PC, (b) exponentially weighted covariance + PC with fixed forgetting factor, (c) online/batch CPD coupled with reset, and (d) time-varying undirected methods (e.g., TV-GL) applied to moralized graphs as a proxy. The CPI case study is illustrative but anecdotal; there’s no external validation or quantitative metric.
- Computational aspects: Online covariance maintenance is O(p^2) per step, but computing Mahalanobis distance requires inverting Cr. The paper does not discuss numerical stability/regularization (e.g., when Sr < p or near-singularity) or use of rank-one inverse updates. Complexity and memory profiles under large p are not analyzed.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The rationale for Eq. 3 (effective sample size) is explained but remains ad hoc; a more principled derivation or sensitivity check would help. The mapping from pooled p-value to ar (Eq. 4) is intuitive but arbitrary; alternatives (e.g., EWMA/CUSUM-style score dynamics) could be discussed. The role and tuning of T, γ, ν are under-explained; the chosen values come from “unsystematic investigation.” Notation is mostly consistent, though the use of T for both Hotelling’s T2 and a threshold could be clarified.

Originality:
The problem—online tracking of DAG structure under local stationarity—is important and underexplored. The particular combination of pooled p-values for adaptively forgetting sufficient statistics and periodic constraint-based structure relearning appears novel. That said, related literatures on time-varying undirected graphs (kernel-smoothing/fused lasso penalties, e.g., Kolar et al., Zhou et al.) and classic sequential detection (CUSUM/Shiryaev–Roberts) are not fully engaged, and simple competing baselines are absent. The theoretical lens of diligence vs. convergence is interesting and differentiating.

Significance:
If robust and efficient, LoSST could be impactful for domains requiring real-time structure adaptation (finance, sensor networks, A/B testing with nonstationarity). The memory-light design is attractive. However, the current evaluation leaves open how LoSST compares to strong, simple baselines, how sensitive it is to hyperparameters and model violations, and how it scales computationally. As a result, practical significance is promising but not yet demonstrated.

Pros:
- Addresses an important, realistic setting (time-varying structure) with an online, single-pass method.
- Clever use of pooled p-values to control adaptive forgetting with O(1) memory overhead.
- Maintains parity with batch methods in stationary regimes; clear gains after structural changes on synthetic data.
- Thoughtful discussion of convergence vs. diligence, with parameterization to trade off.

Cons:
- Heuristic components (Eq. 3 and Eq. 4, relearning policy) lack principled justification and ablation.
- Missing strong baselines (sliding window, fixed EWMA, CPD+reset, time-varying undirected methods).
- Assumes Gaussianity; no robustness analysis or regularization for covariance inversion.
- Limited real-world validation; no quantitative external benchmarks.
- Sparse details on complexity, numerical stability, and hyperparameter selection.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Compare against sliding windows and fixed-forgetting-factor EWMA + PC, and a CPD+reset baseline; where feasible, include a time-varying graphical lasso baseline on the undirected skeleton.
- Add sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν and an ablation showing the benefit of pooled p-values versus simple EWMA/CUSUM statistics.
- Provide numerical stabilization (shrinkage/diagonal loading) and report computational cost; consider rank-one updates for the inverse covariance.
- Explore robustness to non-Gaussian noise and outliers (e.g., robust covariance, Huberized distances).
- Strengthen theory: formalize conditions for diligence/convergence and provide proofs in the paper or appendix.
- Expand real-data evaluation with multiple datasets and external event annotations; quantify detection accuracy and structural tracking.

Overall recommendation:
Promising and potentially significant, with a clear contribution to online structure learning. However, the current submission relies on heuristics without strong baselines or robustness/complexity analyses. With additional empirical comparisons, robustness checks, and tighter theory, this could be a strong NIPS paper; as is, it is borderline.
