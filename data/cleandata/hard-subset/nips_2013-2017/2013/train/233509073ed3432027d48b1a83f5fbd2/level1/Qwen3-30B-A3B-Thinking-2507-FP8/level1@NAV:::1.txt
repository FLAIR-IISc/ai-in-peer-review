

This paper introduces LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm for online learning of time-varying graphical model structures under local stationarity assumptions. The work addresses a critical gap: standard structure learning algorithms (e.g., PC, score-based methods) assume global stationarity, while existing online methods (e.g., TD(0), changepoint detectors) cannot handle general graphical structure learning. LoSST innovatively tracks weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) via Mahalanobis distance-based weighting, dynamically triggering graph relearning based on pooled p-values. The authors demonstrate through simulations that LoSST matches batch-mode performance under global stationarity but significantly outperforms it when structures change locally or parametrically, with real-world validation on US price index volatility data.

Strengths: The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to a well-motivated problem. The theoretical analysis of the convergence-diligence trade-off (Section 3.1) is sophisticated and well-executed, distinguishing this work from prior online structure learning attempts (Talih & Hengartner, Siracusa & Fisher). Simulations are comprehensive, using multiple graph sizes and change scenarios, and the US case study effectively validates real-world applicability. The algorithm’s "no cost" in stable environments is a compelling practical advantage. Writing is clear, and the technical exposition (Sections 3–4) is rigorous.

Weaknesses: While the Gaussian assumption is well-justified for the simulations, the paper lacks discussion of non-Gaussian extensions (e.g., discrete variables via Bayesian networks), though future work is acknowledged. The probabilistic relearning schedule (Section 3.1) is heuristic; the authors do not empirically justify the choice of ν=0.005 beyond "unsytematic investigation." Scalability to large graphs (e.g., >100 variables) is untested, though the online nature suggests potential. The comparison to batch-mode only uses PC; including a score-based baseline (e.g., BDeu) would strengthen significance claims.

Pro/con acceptance:  
*Pro: Solves a fundamental problem in dynamic graphical models with strong theory, simulations, and real-world validation; clear novelty over NIPS 2010–2020 work (e.g., Talih & Hengartner, Siracusa & Fisher).  
*Con: Limited scalability testing and heuristic choices could undermine broader applicability.  

Overall, this is a high-quality, significant contribution that advances the state-of-the-art in dynamic structure learning. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper’s core innovation and robust evaluation. Accept.
