**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel heuristic algorithm designed for learning the structure of graphical models from data streams where the underlying generative process may change over time. The core assumption is that the data is only "locally stationary" or locally i.i.d., a departure from the global stationarity assumption made by most standard structure learning algorithms (e.g., PC, GES).

The LoSST algorithm operates online by maintaining and updating weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an "effective" sample size) for a set of continuous variables. To detect changes in the underlying structure or its parameters, it computes the Mahalanobis distance of each new data point from the current estimated distribution. A sequence of large distances (i.e., poor fit) is identified by tracking a pooled p-value using Liptak's method. When a change is suspected (indicated by a low pooled p-value), the algorithm increases the weight assigned to new data points, effectively "forgetting" older data and adapting more quickly to the new regime. The graphical model structure is then re-learned using a standard batch-mode algorithm (the paper uses the PC algorithm) on the current weighted sufficient statistics. This re-learning is triggered probabilistically, with the probability increasing significantly following a detected change.

The paper situates this work relative to several related areas. It distinguishes LoSST from standard batch-mode structure learners, which cannot handle non-stationarity, and from online parameter estimators like TD(λ), which do not learn structure. It also contrasts with changepoint detection (CPD) methods, which are often not designed for real-time operation or continuous drift, and with previous time-varying graph learning methods (e.g., Talih & Hengartner, 2005; Siracusa & Fisher III, 2009) that operate in batch mode rather than online.

**Review and Justification**

This is a well-written and valuable contribution to the field of graphical model learning. It addresses a critical and practical limitation of existing methods and proposes a sensible, effective solution.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The online update equations for the weighted sufficient statistics are correct, and the proposed heuristic for change detection is well-motivated. The use of Mahalanobis distance is a standard way to measure the "outlyingness" of a multivariate point, and using a pooled p-value to avoid overreacting to single outliers is a clever and robust design choice.

The claims are strongly supported by a well-designed set of simulations. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that LoSST performs comparably to a standard batch learner (PC) in a stable environment, which is a crucial result showing that there is little to no cost for using this more flexible algorithm. More importantly, the simulations show that LoSST significantly outperforms the batch learner when the underlying structure or parameters change, successfully tracking the new model while the batch learner's performance degrades due to averaging over two different distributions. The inclusion of an experiment with probabilistic relearning is also a strength, showing that the performance does not degrade significantly even when the computationally expensive relearning step is performed less frequently. The discussion of the trade-off between "convergence" (stability in the limit) and "diligence" (responsiveness to change) is particularly insightful, lending theoretical weight to what is otherwise a heuristic method and demonstrating a deep understanding of the problem.

A minor weakness is the heuristic nature of several components, particularly the parameter settings for `T` and `γ` in the weight update rule and `ν` in the relearning schedule. The authors note their choices were based on "unsystematic investigation." While this is understandable for an initial proposal, the work would be strengthened by a sensitivity analysis of these parameters.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The motivation is laid out plainly, the algorithm is described in a logical, step-by-step manner, and the experimental results are presented with informative and easy-to-interpret figures. The writing is precise, and the authors provide enough detail for an expert to understand the method's mechanics. The distinction between different types of related work is sharp and helps position the contribution effectively.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it combines several existing ideas (weighted statistics, Mahalanobis distance, PC algorithm), their synthesis into a single, cohesive algorithm for online structure learning in non-stationary environments is novel. To my knowledge, this was a significant gap in the literature at the time. The problem itself is not new, but a practical, online, frequentist approach like this one is a new and valuable contribution that differs significantly from prior Bayesian or batch-processing approaches.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. The assumption of global stationarity is a major bottleneck for applying graphical model learning to many real-world domains, such as finance, climate science, and sensor networks, where systems naturally evolve over time. LoSST provides a practical tool for researchers and practitioners in these areas. The algorithm is general and could be adapted by swapping out the PC algorithm for other constraint-based or (with some modification) score-based learners. The results are impressive and demonstrate a clear advance over the state-of-the-art for this problem setting. Other researchers are likely to build on these ideas, perhaps by developing more principled methods for setting the learning rate or by extending the framework to discrete or mixed data types, as the authors suggest in their future work section.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Addresses a critical, practical, and under-explored problem: online structure learning in non-stationary environments.
2.  Proposes a novel, elegant, and well-motivated algorithm (LoSST) that effectively combines ideas from different fields.
3.  Provides strong empirical evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness, including a crucial comparison showing it incurs no performance penalty in stable environments.
4.  The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to follow.
5.  The discussion of the convergence/diligence trade-off adds significant conceptual depth.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The method relies on several heuristic parameters (`T`, `γ`, `ν`) that require tuning, and guidance on setting them is limited.
2.  The scalability of the method is not explored; re-running the PC algorithm can be intractable for high-dimensional problems, and the experiments are limited to 20 variables.
3.  The claims of extensibility to other graphical model types are asserted but not demonstrated.

**Conclusion:**
The strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel and significant contribution to an important problem, supported by strong empirical results and clear exposition. It opens up a promising direction for research into learning from dynamic, real-world data streams. I strongly recommend acceptance.
