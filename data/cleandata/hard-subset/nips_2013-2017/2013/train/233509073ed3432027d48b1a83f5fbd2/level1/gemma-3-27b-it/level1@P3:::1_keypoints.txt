- Introduces LoSST, a heuristic algorithm for online tracking of graphical model structure in non-stationary environments.  
- Core idea: track sufficient statistics online and dynamically relearn the graph to handle locally stationary data.  
- Empirical finding: LoSST matches batch-mode learning when structure is stable and outperforms it when changes occur.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written, with an effective introduction motivating the problem.  
- Related work section is comprehensive and correctly positions LoSST relative to Bayesian methods, TDL, and CPD.  
- Algorithmic description is detailed and logical, with clear update equations for sufficient statistics and weight adjustments.  
- Convergence vs. diligence discussion is a strength, showing thoughtful consideration of theoretical properties.  
- Simulations are well-designed, covering both structural and parameter change scenarios.  
- Real-world application to US price index data provides a useful demonstration of practical relevance.  
- Major weakness: algorithm is heuristic with seemingly arbitrary choices (Mahalanobis detector, weight update eq.4, relearning schedule).  
- Insufficient justification or rigorous/sensitivity analysis for parameter choices (e.g., γ=3, T=0.05 chosen via unsystematic investigation).  
- Heavy reliance on synthetic simulations; limited real-world evaluations reduce claims of generalizability.  
- Simulation setup lacks reproducibility details (how SEMs were generated, error term distributions).  
- Scalability concerns: computational complexity of PC algorithm and overall LoSST scaling with variable count not thoroughly addressed.  
- The pooled p-value → weight update mapping feels ad hoc and needs clearer rationale.  
- Convergence/diligence explanation is dense and at times counterintuitive (e.g., statement about convergence with T < 0 needs clarification).  
- Inconsistency in simulation discussion regarding edge removal errors vs. PC producing a superset of edges requires clarification.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty, sound algorithmic description, empirical support, real-world relevance, and theoretical insight.  
- Arguments against acceptance: heuristic nature, limited real-world evaluation, scalability concerns, and ad-hoc weighting scheme.  
- Overall recommendation: promising work; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to justify heuristics, expand experiments, address scalability, and clarify theoretical/results issues.
