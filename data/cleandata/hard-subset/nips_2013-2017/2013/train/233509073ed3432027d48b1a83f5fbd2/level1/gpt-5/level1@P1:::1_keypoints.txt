- Proposal: LoSST — an online heuristic that maintains weighted sufficient statistics and uses pooled Mahalanobis p-values to trigger periodic PC-based DAG relearning for time-varying linear-Gaussian SEMs.

- Novelty: the integration of online weighted stats, Liptak pooled p-values, and periodic constraint-based relearning for DAG tracking appears new and meaningful.

- Update correctness and footprint: weighted mean/covariance updates are correct and single-pass memory is O(p^2), making the method scalable.

- Heuristic foundations: key steps are heuristic — Hotelling’s T^2, the effective sample size Sr, and Liptak pooling lack formal calibration under adaptive weighting and serial dependence.

- T^2 validity issue: using Hotelling’s T^2 with adaptively weighted covariances and influence from test points violates strict null-distribution assumptions.

- P-value pooling issue: Liptak pooling assumes independent p-values but p-values here are serially dependent, undermining probabilistic calibration even if detector is practically smooth.

- Relearning trigger tuning: the relearning mechanism (ρr, noisy-OR schedule) depends on hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) tuned informally without systematic sensitivity analysis.

- PC on weighted stats: substituting Sr for degrees-of-freedom in Fisher Z/partial-correlation tests is reasonable but unvalidated; no discussion of Type I/II error control under weighting.

- Theory: the convergence vs diligence analysis is insightful but relies on heuristic quantities and lacks formal guarantees (e.g., detection delay, false alarm bounds, consistency under local stationarity).

- Simulations: show LoSST recovers structure like batch PC in stationary segments and adapts faster after abrupt changepoints.

- Limited baselines: evaluations compare mainly to batch PC on all data — need stronger comparators (sliding-window PC, EWMA-only forgetting, CPD+restart, dynamic/TV GGMs).

- Missing ablations: no systematic sensitivity or ablation studies over T, γ, ν, pooled p-values vs EWMA, or fixed vs probabilistic relearning to identify what drives gains.

- Real-data study: interesting qualitative detection of macro volatility shifts but small-scale, lacking external validation, predictive checks, and robustness to preprocessing/lag choices.

- Clarity gaps: generally well written, but missing explicit statement of the CI test used, how Sr/Cr feed into it, numerical-stability/regularization discussion, pseudocode, and complexity per step/relearning.

- Originality assessment: components are known but their integration for online DAG tracking is a novel contribution; the conceptual trade-off theory is a useful addition.

- Significance: addresses an important, underexplored problem with a practical, low-memory approach, but limited evaluation and theoretical guarantees reduce immediate practical impact.

- Pros: tackles nonstationary structure learning; simple, memory-efficient, single-pass updates; promising simulation results; clear exposition and conceptual theory.

- Cons: heuristic statistical machinery without calibration, limited baselines, sparse sensitivity analyses, and a small qualitative real-data evaluation.

- Suggested improvements: add stronger baselines (sliding-window, EWMA, CPD+restart, TV GGMs), perform ablation/sensitivity studies, empirically calibrate Mahalanobis/T^2 nulls and false-alarm rates, adapt CI tests for weighted stats and regularize when Sr is small, and expand/quantify real-data validation.

- Recommendation: weak reject — promising and practical first step, but needs stronger empirical comparisons and clearer statistical justification before acceptance.
