Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying structure in linear-Gaussian DAG models (recursive SEMs). The method maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariance, effective sample size) with per-sample weights adapted from a pooled measure of model misfit. Misfit is quantified by the Mahalanobis distance under the current covariance; per-sample p-values (from Hotelling’s T^2) are combined via Liptak’s method to obtain a pooled p-value ρr; ρr modulates both the forgetting factor (through ar+1) and the probability of triggering a structure relearn event via the PC algorithm. The paper discusses convergence vs. diligence trade-offs as a function of thresholds and a gain parameter, and evaluates on synthetic data with changepoints (both structure and parameter changes), plus a CPI case study.

Relative to prior art: batch score-based and constraint-based algorithms (e.g., PC, BDeu-based search) assume global stationarity; online Bayesian updates coincide with batch under i.i.d.; CPD methods (Adams & MacKay 2007; Desobry et al. 2005) detect changepoints but do not learn graphical structure. Time-varying graphical models have been studied in batch settings (Talih & Hengartner 2005 for undirected graphs; Siracusa & Fisher 2009 for time-indexed posteriors over edges). Important related work omitted includes: time-varying GGMs via fused lasso/smoothness penalties and sparsistency results (e.g., Kolar, Song, Xing, 2010; Zhou et al., 2010), UAI work on changing dependency structure (Xuan & Murphy, 2007), and dynamic Bayesian networks (Murphy, 2002) as a modeling alternative to nonstationarity. Also relevant are sequential change detection (CUSUM/GLR, Page-Hinkley) and online covariance shrinkage literature (Ledoit-Wolf) for stability at small effective sample sizes.

Quality:
- Strengths: The sufficient-statistics tracker is simple, memory O(d^2), and can be combined with off-the-shelf structure learners. The idea of using a pooled misfit statistic to adapt forgetting and to gate relearning is appealing and practically motivated. The simulations convincingly show that when the full data are a mixture from multiple regimes, batch PC on the full set can be misled while LoSST recovers the current regime faster. The discussion of convergence vs. diligence is thoughtful and aligns with classical no-free-lunch trade-offs in change detection.

- Weaknesses and questions of statistical validity:
  - The use of Hotelling’s T^2 with a weighted covariance and an “effective” sample size Sr lacks justification. The T^2 reference distribution assumes unweighted i.i.d. samples and an unbiased sample covariance; with exponential/variable weights the null distribution is not T^2, so the individual p-values may be miscalibrated. Similarly, Liptak’s pooling assumes independence or known dependence structure of p-values; here the p-values are temporally dependent. Using a pooled ρr as if it were a calibrated probability risks uncontrolled false alarm/false negative rates.
  - The independence tests in PC typically rely on Fisher’s z-transform and accurate estimation of partial correlations and sample size. It is unclear how the authors modify the test to account for weighting and for the variability in Sr; if they simply substitute Sr as sample size, the null distribution is again unclear. This is critical, because LoSST’s claimed ability to retain batch-mode performance under stationarity depends on the tests being valid under constant weights.
  - Numerical stability: When the effective sample size rapidly decreases around changepoints, the covariance inverse can be ill-conditioned, especially in higher dimensions. No shrinkage/regularization is used, which is standard in time-varying covariance estimation (e.g., Ledoit-Wolf, graphical lasso). The simulations seem limited to modest d; behavior for d comparable to Sr is not assessed.
  - Baselines: Comparing LoSST only to batch PC on the full dataset is weak. Stronger baselines include: (i) sliding-window PC, (ii) CPD (Bayesian or frequentist) coupled with restart of PC, (iii) time-varying GGM estimators (fused-lasso) and DBN baselines where appropriate, (iv) adaptive forgetting-factor covariance with fixed retraining schedule. Without these, the performance gains may reflect poor baseline choice rather than superiority of LoSST.
  - Hyperparameters: T, γ, ν are set “unsystematically”; no sensitivity analysis is provided, nor a principled way to choose them. The convergence/diligence statements are sketched but do not provide guarantees on false alarm rates or detection delay as a function of parameters.
  - Real data: The CPI case study is anecdotal: no external validation, no comparison to known economic regime-shift detectors, and no baseline methods.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The online update formulas are clear. However, important implementation details are missing: how independence tests are conducted under weighting; how partial correlations are computed and regularized; how the relearning probability is reset/tuned; and computational complexity in practice. Some references are incomplete, and a few typos remain.

Originality:
The combination of online weighted sufficient statistics, pooled misfit to adapt forgetting, and probabilistic triggers for constraint-based structure relearning is a novel synthesis. However, the individual components (adaptive forgetting, change detection, time-varying networks) have substantial prior art, some not cited. The contribution is best viewed as a practical heuristic framework rather than a theoretically grounded new estimator.

Significance:
If validated against stronger baselines and with better-calibrated tests, LoSST could be a useful tool for practitioners needing single-pass, adaptive structure learning in linear-Gaussian settings. As it stands, the lack of calibration, baselines, and guidance on hyperparameters limits immediate impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and underexplored problem: online structure learning under nonstationarity.
- Simple, computationally light updates; compatible with existing structure learners.
- Empirical gains in scenarios where batch learning on mixed regimes fails.
- Thoughtful framing of convergence vs. diligence trade-offs.

Cons for acceptance:
- Questionable statistical calibration of the misfit p-values and PC tests under weighted data.
- Missing strong baselines (sliding-window PC, CPD+restart, time-varying GGMs/DBNs).
- No regularization for covariance inversion at small effective sample sizes.
- Hyperparameters chosen ad hoc; no sensitivity or selection strategy.
- Real-data evaluation lacks validation; claims of regime detection are speculative.
- Important related work on time-varying graphical models is not cited.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a statistically justified test for misfit under weighted covariance (e.g., derive or bootstrap the null for Dr, or use robust distances with shrinkage) and adjust independence tests for weighting and small Sr.
- Include baselines: sliding-window PC with tuned window, BOCPD+restart, time-varying GGM with fused penalties, and DBN where applicable; report SHD/precision-recall and runtime.
- Add covariance regularization (e.g., Ledoit-Wolf shrinkage) and report high-dimensional performance.
- Conduct sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν; consider adaptive thresholding (e.g., control false alarm via sequential testing/FDR).
- Expand discussion and citations to the time-varying network literature (Kolar et al. 2010; Xuan & Murphy 2007; related fused-lasso approaches).
- Release code and detailed settings to enhance reproducibility.

Overall, this is a promising heuristic with practical appeal, but requires stronger empirical and statistical grounding before it meets the bar for NIPS.
