- Paper proposes LoSST: an online, single-pass, memory-efficient algorithm that tracks time-varying DAG structure by maintaining weighted sufficient statistics and using pooled Mahalanobis-based p-values to drive adaptive forgetting and intermittent PC relearning.

- Key novelty is integrating exponentially-weighted statistics, Mahalanobis monitoring with Liptak pooling, adaptive forgetting (factor γ), and probabilistic relearning to enable real-time DAG updates without storing history.

- Strength: memory- and compute-efficient (O(p^2)), suitable for streaming settings.

- Strength: interpretable adaptive mechanism (pooled p-values) that balances stability and responsiveness.

- Strength: modular separation of statistic tracking and structure learner allows swapping in other learners.

- Strength: discussion of convergence vs. diligence clarifies algorithmic regimes and behavior under parameter choices.

- Strength: synthetic experiments show LoSST matches batch PC under stationarity and outperforms it with distributional changes; real-data case study finds plausible macroeconomic regime shifts.

- Weakness: statistical foundations of the pooled p-value ρr are heuristic — Hotelling T^2 validity under adaptive weighting and time-varying parameters is unclear, and Liptak pooling assumes independence that sequential distances violate.

- Weakness: baselines are limited — missing sliding-window PC, fixed-EW PC, CPD+restart PC, time-varying undirected methods (e.g., TV-GLasso on moralized graphs), and relevant dynamic BN/switching models.

- Weakness: hyperparameter selection for γ, T, ν is ad hoc with no systematic sensitivity analysis or reporting of detection delay/false alarm trade-offs.

- Weakness: some derivations and notation need correction/clarification (sample-size update Eq.3, weight updates, behavior at ρ = T, early-sample regularization and Cr invertibility).

- Weakness: theoretical analysis is informal and lacks formal guarantees on detection delay, error rates, or within-regime structure consistency.

- Weakness: scope limited to linear Gaussian SEMs with PC; claimed extensions to other models are untested.

- Quality: implementation is feasible from description, but numeric-stability and early-phase regularization details are missing for reproducibility.

- Clarity: generally well-written and organized but needs algebraic/notation fixes and clearer guidance for T>1 regime.

- Suggestions: add stronger baselines (sliding-window, EW-PC, BOCPD+restart PC, TV-GLasso), and compare edge recovery on moralized graphs.

- Suggestions: provide sensitivity analyses for γ, T, ν; report detection delay, false alarm rates, and error vs. parameters; ablate pooled p-values vs. raw distances.

- Suggestions: empirically assess calibration of ρr under stationarity (QQ plots, Type I error rates) and clarify Eq.3, Cr regularization, early-sample handling, and robustness to outliers.

- Suggestions: pursue theoretical bounds under piecewise-stationary models (detection delay vs. jump size; error within stable regimes).

- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important, underexplored problem with a simple, modular, memory-efficient method and promising empirical results.

- Arguments against acceptance: heuristic statistical basis unvalidated, insufficient baselines, and limited theory and hyperparameter analysis limit the paper’s current impact.
