- Introduces LoSST, an online algorithm for graphical model structure learning in locally stationary (time-varying) environments.  
- Uses weighted sufficient statistics and a pooled p-value test to detect and track structural shifts in real time.  
- Shows via simulations that LoSST matches batch-mode performance in stationary settings and outperforms it when structures change.  
- Validated on US price index volatility data, detecting meaningful economic shifts (e.g., the Great Moderation).  
- Situates work relative to PC algorithm, online learning, changepoint detection, and time-varying graphical-model literature, clearly distinguishing contributions.  
- Technically sound: provides mathematical derivations for online sufficient-statistics tracking and analyzes convergence vs. diligence trade-offs.  
- Simulation design is comprehensive, testing graph-structure and parameter changes across multiple variable counts and degrees.  
- Paper is very clear and well-organized; figures and the convergence-vs-diligence explanation make complex points accessible.  
- Novelty: fills a genuine gap by enabling online structure tracking without large data storage; pooled p-value approach is particularly novel.  
- Significance: relevant to finance, social networks, biology; no penalty in stationary settings and clear benefits for non-stationary data.  
- Theoretical limitations: lacks convergence rates and error bounds in the non-convergent/non-diligent regime used in experiments.  
- Computational complexity: acknowledges expensive graph relearning but provides no detailed cost/scale analysis for large applications.  
- Parameter sensitivity: key parameters (T=0.05, γ=3) were chosen unsystematically; needs a systematic sensitivity study.  
- Pro/Con acceptance summary: pro—important problem, novel and practical solution, strong experiments and clarity; con—limited theoretical guarantees, unclear computational scaling, minimal parameter analysis.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution with practical utility; recommends acceptance despite theoretical and computational caveats.
