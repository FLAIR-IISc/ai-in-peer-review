- Proposes LoSST: an online algorithm that tracks time-varying structure in linear-Gaussian DAGs using weighted sufficient statistics and pooled misfit to adapt forgetting and trigger relearning.  
- Misfit computed via Mahalanobis distance and per-sample Hotelling’s T^2 p-values pooled by Liptak’s method to form ρr, which modulates forgetting and relearning probability.  
- Paper analyses a convergence vs. diligence trade-off (thresholds and gain) and evaluates on synthetic changepoint data plus a CPI case study.  
- Novelty lies in combining online weighted sufficient statistics, pooled misfit-driven forgetting, and probabilistic triggers for constraint-based relearning as a practical heuristic.  
- Strength: simple sufficient-statistics tracker with O(d^2) memory that plugs into off-the-shelf structure learners.  
- Strength: pooled misfit gating for relearning is intuitively appealing and practically motivated.  
- Strength: simulations show LoSST can recover current regimes faster than batch PC when data mix regimes.  
- Main statistical validity concern: Hotelling’s T^2 assumes unweighted i.i.d. and unbiased covariance; using it with exponential/variable weights and effective sample size Sr is unjustified and may miscalibrate p-values.  
- Main statistical validity concern: Liptak’s pooling assumes independence or known dependence among p-values; temporally dependent p-values here invalidate that assumption and risk uncontrolled false alarms/negatives.  
- Important methodological gap: independence tests in PC (partial correlations/Fisher z) are not adapted or justified for weighted data and variable Sr.  
- Numerical stability issue: no covariance shrinkage/regularization is used, risking ill-conditioned inverses when Sr is small or d is large.  
- Baselines are weak: only compared to batch PC; missing stronger baselines like sliding-window PC, CPD+restart, time-varying GGMs (fused-lasso), DBNs, and adaptive forgetting schedules.  
- Hyperparameter selection is ad hoc (T, γ, ν) with no sensitivity analysis or principled selection strategy and no guarantees on false alarm/detection delay.  
- Real-data evaluation (CPI) is anecdotal with no external validation or comparison to established regime-detection baselines.  
- Clarity is generally good and updates are clear, but implementation details are missing (how tests are adapted for weights, partial correlation computation/regularization, relearning probability tuning, practical complexity).  
- Missing related work citations: time-varying GGM/fused-lasso (Kolar et al. 2010; Zhou et al. 2010), changing dependency structure (Xuan & Murphy 2007), DBNs (Murphy 2002), sequential change detection (CUSUM/GLR) and covariance shrinkage (Ledoit-Wolf).  
- Originality assessment: useful and novel synthesis of ideas, but components have substantial prior art; best viewed as a practical heuristic rather than a theoretically grounded estimator.  
- Significance: potentially useful if statistical calibration, stronger baselines, regularization, and hyperparameter guidance are provided; currently limited by those gaps.  
- Pros for acceptance: addresses an important online structure-learning problem, is computationally light, and shows empirical gains in mixed-regime simulations.  
- Cons for acceptance: questionable p-value/test calibration under weighting, missing strong baselines, lack of covariance regularization, ad hoc hyperparameters, and weak real-data validation.  
- Key suggestions: derive or bootstrap a valid misfit test under weighting, adapt independence tests for weighted Sr, add covariance regularization (e.g., Ledoit-Wolf/graphical lasso), and run stronger baselines (sliding-window PC, BOCPD+restart, fused-lasso GGMs, DBNs).  
- Key suggestions: perform hyperparameter sensitivity analysis and consider adaptive thresholding/control of false alarms, expand citations to time-varying network literature, and release code/settings for reproducibility.  
- Overall verdict: promising, practically appealing heuristic that requires stronger empirical validation and better statistical grounding before top-conference acceptance.
