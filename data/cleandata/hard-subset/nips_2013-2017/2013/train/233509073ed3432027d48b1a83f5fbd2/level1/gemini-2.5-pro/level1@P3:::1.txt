This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of learning graphical model structure from data streams where the underlying generating process may change over time. Standard structure learning algorithms assume stationarity (i.e., i.i.d. data), making them unsuitable for such dynamic environments. The authors propose a novel heuristic algorithm, the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), which operates in an online, single-pass fashion to track changes in both the parameters and the structure of a graphical model.

The core idea of LoSST is to maintain online estimates of weighted sufficient statistics (for linear SEMs, this is the mean, covariance, and an "effective" sample size). The key innovation lies in the dynamic weighting scheme. At each time step, the fit of the new data point to the current model is measured by its Mahalanobis distance. To distinguish a genuine structural change from a single outlier, the algorithm uses Liptak's method to pool the p-values of these distances over a weighted history. A consistently poor fit (a low pooled p-value) signals a likely change in the generating process. This triggers an increase in the weight assigned to new data points, effectively down-weighting the influence of past data. The algorithm then probabilistically triggers a full structure relearning step (using the PC algorithm in this implementation), with the probability of relearning increasing when a change is detected. The paper also provides a nice theoretical discussion of how the algorithm's parameters allow it to be tuned between being "convergent" (stable in the long run on a fixed structure) and "diligent" (quickly responsive to change), two desirable but provably incompatible properties.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The problem of learning in non-stationary environments is of great practical importance, yet it is often overlooked by the graphical model structure learning community. This paper provides a novel and well-motivated heuristic solution. The combination of online weighted statistics, Mahalanobis distance, robust p-value pooling for change detection, and an adaptive relearning trigger is an original and compelling approach.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The algorithm is presented logically, and the motivation for each component is clearly explained. The connections to and distinctions from related work in online learning and changepoint detection are well-articulated.
*   **Quality of Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is a strong point. The simulations systematically test LoSST in scenarios with both structure and parameter changes, demonstrating that it adapts quickly while performing comparably to a batch learner in stationary periods. The inclusion of an experiment with probabilistic relearning validates the trigger mechanism, and the application to US price index data provides a compelling real-world case study, showing that the algorithm identifies historically significant economic shifts.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Baselines for Comparison:** The primary baseline is the PC algorithm run in batch mode on all data accumulated so far. While this highlights the failure of stationary assumptions, it is not a strong baseline for a non-stationary setting. More appropriate and commonly used heuristics should be compared against, such as:
    *   A sliding window approach, where learning is performed only on the N most recent data points.
    *   A simpler exponential forgetting scheme, where sufficient statistics are updated with a fixed forgetting factor (which the authors note is a special case of their model).
    Comparing against these would more clearly isolate the benefit of LoSST's adaptive, data-driven weighting scheme.

2.  **Computational Complexity:** The algorithm requires computing the Mahalanobis distance at every time step, which involves inverting the current covariance matrix. This is an O(V^3) operation, where V is the number of variables. This cost is incurred for *every single data point*, which could make the algorithm prohibitively slow for even moderately high-dimensional problems (V > 100). This significant computational aspect is not discussed. The experiments are limited to V <= 20. The authors should address this limitation and perhaps suggest approximations for scalability.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm introduces several crucial hyperparameters, namely the p-value threshold `T`, the weight increase factor `gamma`, and the relearning rate `nu`. The paper mentions that these were set via "unsystematic investigation." A sensitivity analysis is needed to understand how robust the algorithm's performance is to the choice of these parameters and to provide guidance for practitioners on how to set them.

4.  **Justification of Update Rules:** While the updates for the weighted mean and covariance are standard, the update for the effective sample size (Eq. 3) is presented as a heuristic justified by two constraints. Given that this quantity is used to determine the degrees of freedom for the Hotelling's T-squared distribution (which is central to the change detection mechanism), a more rigorous statistical justification or discussion of its properties would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to an important problem. It proposes an elegant and effective algorithm for an online structure learning task that has been largely neglected. The methodology is well-explained, and the experiments are convincing. While there are weaknesses—most notably the lack of stronger baselines, the unaddressed computational complexity for higher dimensions, and the need for a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis—these do not undermine the core value of the work. They represent important directions for future research that this paper opens up. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and is likely to inspire follow-up work. I recommend acceptance.
