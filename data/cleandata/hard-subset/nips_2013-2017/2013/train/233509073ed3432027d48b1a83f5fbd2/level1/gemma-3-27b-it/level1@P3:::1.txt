This paper introduces LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm for tracking changes in graphical model structure in a dynamic, real-time manner, addressing a significant gap in existing structure learning algorithms that primarily assume stationary environments. The core idea – tracking sufficient statistics online and dynamically relearning the graph – is a compelling approach to handling locally stationary data. The authors demonstrate through simulations that LoSST performs comparably to batch-mode learning when the underlying structure is stable, and significantly better when changes occur.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions LoSST relative to existing work. The related work section is comprehensive, accurately summarizing limitations of existing techniques (Bayesian methods, TDL, CPD) and highlighting the novelty of their approach. The algorithmic description is detailed and logical, with clear explanations of the update equations for the sufficient statistics and the weight adjustments. The inclusion of convergence vs. diligence discussion is a strength, demonstrating a thoughtful consideration of the algorithm’s theoretical properties.  The simulations are well-designed, covering scenarios with both structural and parameter changes. The application to US price index data provides a valuable real-world demonstration.

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, the heuristic nature of the algorithm is a significant limitation. While acknowledged, the justification for specific choices – the Mahalanobis distance as a change detector, the update rules for weights (eq. 4), the probabilistic relearning schedule – feels somewhat arbitrary. A more rigorous analysis, or at least a more thorough sensitivity analysis, of these choices would strengthen the work. The authors state that γ=3 and T=0.05 “seemed to appropriately trade off convergence vs. diligence,” but this is based on “unsystematic investigation.” 

Second, the simulations, while comprehensive, focus heavily on synthetic data. While providing a controlled environment for evaluation, they may not fully capture the complexities of real-world data. The application to US price index data is helpful, but a more extensive evaluation on diverse datasets would bolster the claims of generalizability. The simulation setup is also not fully explained. Specifically, how were the SEMs randomly generated? What distributions were used for the error terms?  More details are needed to allow for reproducibility.

Third, the computational complexity of the PC algorithm used for structure learning is mentioned as a concern but not thoroughly addressed. The probabilistic relearning strategy helps, but a discussion of scalability to higher-dimensional datasets and potentially faster structure learning algorithms would be beneficial. It’s not clear how the computational cost of LoSST scales with the number of variables.

Fourth, the connection between the “pooled p-value” and the weight update (eq. 4) feels somewhat ad hoc. The authors acknowledge this is one way to convert the p-value to a weight, but a clearer rationale for this choice is needed. Furthermore, the explanation regarding the theoretical properties of convergence and diligence, while insightful, is somewhat dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.  Specifically, the statement regarding convergence with T < 0 is counterintuitive and requires clarification.

Finally, there is a minor inconsistency in the discussion of simulation results. The authors state that LoSST performs *worse* on edge removal errors when only parameters change, yet they also state that PC's output is a superset of the original edges, which seems to imply LoSST *should* have fewer false positives. This needs clarification.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by proposing an algorithm for online graphical model structure learning in non-stationary environments.
*   Soundness: The algorithm is logically sound and well-described, with clear update equations for sufficient statistics.
*   Empirical Support: The simulations demonstrate the effectiveness of LoSST compared to batch-mode learning, particularly in dynamic environments.
*   Real-world relevance: The application to US price index data highlights the potential of LoSST for real-world applications.
*   Theoretical Insight: The discussion on convergence and diligence demonstrates a careful consideration of the algorithm’s theoretical properties.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Heuristic Nature: The algorithm relies on several heuristic choices without sufficient justification.
*   Limited Real-World Evaluation: The evaluation is primarily based on synthetic data.
*   Scalability Concerns: The computational complexity of the underlying structure learning algorithm is not adequately addressed.
*   Ad-hoc Weighting Scheme: The connection between the pooled p-value and the weight update feels arbitrary.



**Overall Assessment:**

LoSST is a promising approach to a challenging problem. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated algorithm with compelling empirical results. However, the heuristic nature of the algorithm and the limited real-world evaluation raise concerns. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing more justification for the heuristic choices, conducting additional experiments on diverse datasets, and discussing scalability concerns. A clearer explanation of the convergence/diligence properties and clarification of the edge removal results would also be beneficial. Addressing these points will significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
