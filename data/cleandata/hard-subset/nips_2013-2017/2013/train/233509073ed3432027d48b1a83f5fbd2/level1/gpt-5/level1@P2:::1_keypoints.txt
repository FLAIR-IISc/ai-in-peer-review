- Introduces LoSST: an online, single-pass heuristic to track time-varying DAGs (linear-Gaussian SEMs) using exponentially reweighted sufficient statistics and adaptive forgetting driven by a pooled p-value of Mahalanobis distances, with periodic PC-based relearning.  
- Analyzes a trade-off between “convergence” and “diligence” and argues parameters T and γ interpolate between these behaviors.  
- Empirically, LoSST matches batch PC in stationary regimes and outperforms after structural or parameter changes; brief CPI case study suggests detection of volatility regime shifts.  
- Novelty: a memory-light online DAG-tracking procedure that avoids maintaining distributions over model spaces, positioned between online learning, change-point detection, and structure learning.  
- Methodological: sufficient-stat updates are standard and pooled p-value use is reasonable, but ar update (Eq.4) and effective sample size update (Eq.3) are heuristic and lack principled justification.  
- Theoretical claims about convergence vs. diligence are sketched but not fully proven or rigorously conditioned in the paper.  
- Assumes multivariate Gaussianity for Mahalanobis T2 and PC’s partial-correlation tests; robustness to non-Gaussianity or outliers is not studied.  
- Synthetic experiments are well-controlled, showing parity in stationary settings and gains after change, but baselines are limited to batch PC only.  
- Missing empirical baselines: sliding-window + PC, fixed-forgetting-factor EWMA + PC, CPD + reset, and time-varying undirected methods (e.g., TV-GL on moralized graphs).  
- CPI case study is illustrative but anecdotal—no external validation or quantitative performance metrics provided.  
- Computationally, online covariance maintenance is O(p^2) per step and Mahalanobis requires inverting Cr; paper omits discussion of numerical stability, regularization, rank-one inverse updates, and scaling to large p.  
- Clarity: generally clear and well organized, but Eq.3/Eq.4 rationale is ad hoc, tuning of T, γ, ν is under-explained, and notation reuses T ambiguously.  
- Originality: problem is important and underexplored; pooled p-value + periodic relearning appears novel, but connections to time-varying undirected graph methods and classic sequential detection are under-engaged.  
- Significance: potentially impactful for real-time structure tracking with low memory, but current evaluation leaves open sensitivity to hyperparameters, robustness, and computational scalability.  
- Pros: addresses realistic time-varying structure setting with an online, single-pass method; clever pooled p-value control with O(1) memory; strong synthetic performance post-change; conceptual diligence vs. convergence framing.  
- Cons: relies on heuristics without principled ablation; lacks strong comparative baselines; assumes Gaussianity without robustness checks; sparse details on numerical stability, complexity, and hyperparameter selection; limited real-data validation.  
- Suggested improvements: add sliding-window/EWMA/CPD+reset and TV-GL baselines; perform sensitivity analyses and ablations for pooled p-value vs. EWMA/CUSUM; implement numerical stabilization (shrinkage/diagonal loading, rank-one updates) and report runtimes; test robustness to non-Gaussian noise/outliers; formalize and prove diligence/convergence conditions; expand quantitative real-data evaluations.  
- Overall recommendation: promising and potentially significant but currently borderline—would be much stronger with additional baselines, robustness/complexity analyses, ablations, and tighter theoretical support.
