- Tackles online structure learning for nonstationary graphical models via the LoSST algorithm.
- LoSST maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) with weights adapted by pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances.
- When pooled evidence indicates misfit, weights shift toward recent data and the probability of relearning the graph via the PC algorithm increases.
- Simulations show LoSST matches batch PC under global stationarity and adapts faster after structural or parametric changes.
- Paper analyzes parameter regimes trading off convergence versus rapid response (“diligence”) and gives sufficient conditions for each.
- Small real-data case study on CPI components suggests LoSST can identify macroeconomic regime shifts.
- Connects to online TD learning, Bayesian/frequentist changepoint detection, exponential forgetting/EWMA covariance tracking, DBNs, and time-varying GGMs, though some relevant prior work (e.g., Kolar et al. NIPS 2009) is not cited.
- Algorithm is technically plausible and well-motivated; weighted sufficient-statistic updates are correct and relearning is simple and efficient.
- Several elements are heuristic without rigorous guarantees, notably Hotelling’s T2 with an “effective” sample size and Lipták pooling that assumes independence in time series.
- No formal bounds provided on detection delay, false alarm rate, or structural recovery under drift.
- Empirical comparisons lack strong baselines (sliding-window PC, exponentially weighted PC, BOCPD + local reestimation, time-varying GGM methods).
- Manuscript is generally clear and organized, with helpful update equations, but some proofs are only sketched.
- Practical implementation details need more elaboration (regularization/inversion stability, computational cost vs. relearning frequency, hyperparameter tuning).
- Originality: the adaptive-weighting + online sufficient-statistics + probabilistic relearning combination is novel, but relations to DBNs/time-varying GGMs should be clarified.
- Significance: single-pass online structure tracking is important and appealing, but impact would rise with stronger theory and more comprehensive baselines.
- Pros for acceptance: important problem, simple general framework, single-pass design, and solid simulation results.
- Cons for acceptance: heuristic statistical tests lack justification, missing comparisons to natural baselines and literature, limited theoretical guarantees and sensitivity analyses, and small real-data evaluation.
