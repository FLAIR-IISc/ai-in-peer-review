This paper introduces LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm for tracking changes in graphical model structure in dynamic, real-time environments. The core idea is to maintain sufficient statistics online and dynamically relearn the graphical model structure as needed, adapting to locally stationary data. The paper provides simulation results demonstrating LoSST’s performance, comparing it favorably to batch-mode learning under stable and changing structures, and includes a real-world application to US price index volatility.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem of learning in non-stationary environments and positions LoSST within the existing literature. The algorithm itself is described in a step-by-step manner, with sufficient detail for reproduction. The simulation setup is explained adequately, and the results are presented clearly. The related work section provides a good overview of existing methods and highlights their limitations in the context of online structure learning. The discussion section correctly points out the limitations of the approach and suggests promising avenues for future research.

The novelty of the work lies in its combination of online sufficient statistics tracking with dynamic graph relearning. While online learning and changepoint detection are individually well-studied, applying them specifically to the problem of graphical model structure learning in a real-time fashion is a significant contribution. The algorithm’s ability to perform comparably to batch-mode learning under stationary conditions, while simultaneously excelling in non-stationary environments, is a particularly strong aspect. The authors acknowledge the trade-off between convergence and diligence, which is a thoughtful consideration.

The technical soundness of the paper is generally good. The update equations for the sufficient statistics appear correct, and the rationale behind the weighting scheme is well-explained. The application of Hotelling’s T^2 distribution and Liptak’s pooling method is appropriate. However, the mathematical justification for the choice of parameters (e.g., γ and T) is somewhat limited, relying more on empirical investigation.  The discussion of convergence vs. diligence, while insightful, could benefit from more rigorous theoretical analysis. Additionally, the computational complexity of the PC algorithm used for structure learning is mentioned as a concern, but a more detailed analysis of LoSST's overall computational cost would be helpful.  The simulations are reasonably comprehensive, covering both parameter and structure changes. However, more experiments varying the speed of change or the complexity of the graph would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem: online graphical model structure learning.
*   Novel combination of existing techniques tailored for the problem at hand.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Promising simulation results demonstrating performance benefits over batch-mode learning in dynamic environments.
*   Real-world application illustrating the algorithm’s potential.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited theoretical analysis of parameter choices and convergence properties.
*   Computational complexity of the underlying structure learning algorithm (PC) is not fully addressed.
*   Simulations could be more comprehensive, exploring a wider range of scenarios and parameters.
*   The heuristic nature of the algorithm might limit its generalizability to complex datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem, proposes a novel and well-motivated algorithm, and provides compelling evidence of its effectiveness. While some aspects of the technical analysis could be strengthened, the overall quality of the work is high. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should consider providing more justification for the choice of algorithm parameters and discussing the computational scalability of the proposed approach.
