- Introduces LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking both parameters and DAG structure of SEMs in non-stationary data streams.  
- Tracks weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, effective sample size) and updates weights per incoming point.  
- Detects model mismatch using per-point Mahalanobis distances and pools them with Liptak’s method to avoid overreacting to outliers.  
- Low pooled p-values increase weights on recent data to forget old regimes; probabilistic re-learning (e.g., PC) is triggered with rising change probability.  
- Operates in a single pass and aims to handle both abrupt and gradual changes without storing large histories.  
- Theoretical sketch frames a trade-off between “convergent” (stable) and “diligent” (fast-reacting) operating regimes.  
- Update equations and use of Mahalanobis distance are technically sound; pooled p-value as change signal is a clear innovation.  
- Experiments show LoSST matches batch PC in stationary settings and outperforms after changepoints; simulations test both structure+parameter and parameter-only changes.  
- Includes a qualitative real-world application to illustrate potential utility.  
- No discussion of regularization or handling when covariance Cr is ill-conditioned or Sr is small (matrix inversion issues).  
- Convergence vs. diligence claims are only proof sketches in footnotes; full formal proofs are missing.  
- Key algorithmic choices (linear weight update, noisy-OR re-learning schedule) are heuristic with limited theoretical justification.  
- Interaction between effective sample size Sr and PC’s conditional-independence tests is unclear; small Sr could reduce test power and yield overly sparse graphs.  
- Sensitivity to hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) is not analyzed and their selection is described as unsystematic.  
- Experimental baselines are limited; missing comparisons to plausible online methods (sliding-window, CPD+batch).  
- Overall contribution is original, significant, well-situated in literature, and likely useful for streaming data applications.  
- Verdict: strengths (novelty, empirical performance, clarity) outweigh weaknesses, which are mainly areas for further development rather than fatal flaws.
