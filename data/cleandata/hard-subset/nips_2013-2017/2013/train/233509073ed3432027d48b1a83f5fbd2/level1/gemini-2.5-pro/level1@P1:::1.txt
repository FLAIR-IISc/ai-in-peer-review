### Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of directed graphical models from streaming data where the underlying data-generating process may change over time. The core assumption is that the data is "locally stationary" or "locally i.i.d.", meaning the generating model is stable for periods of time but can undergo abrupt or gradual changes.

The main idea of LoSST is to maintain and update a weighted set of sufficient statistics (for linear SEMs, this is the mean, covariance, and effective sample size) in an online fashion. Crucially, the weight assigned to each new data point is determined dynamically. The algorithm assesses the "fit" of a new data point to the current estimated model using the Mahalanobis distance. This distance is converted to a p-value, and a sequence of recent p-values is combined using Liptak's weighted pooling method. If the resulting pooled p-value is low, it suggests a systematic deviation and thus a likely change in the underlying structure. In response, the algorithm increases the weights of recent data points, effectively "forgetting" older, potentially irrelevant data, and shrinking the effective sample size.

To manage the high computational cost of structure learning, LoSST does not relearn the graph at every time step. Instead, it uses a probabilistic mechanism where the probability of triggering a relearning step increases when a change is detected (i.e., when the pooled p-value is low). The authors provide a compelling theoretical discussion framing the algorithm's behavior in terms of a trade-off between "diligence" (rapidly adapting to change) and "convergence" (stabilizing on a correct model in a stationary environment), showing that different parameter settings can favor one over the other.

This work is situated in contrast to standard batch-mode structure learning algorithms (e.g., PC, GES) which assume a single, stationary data-generating process. It also differentiates itself from simpler online methods like TD-learning which only track parameters, and from change-point detection algorithms which often require storing significant data history and assume abrupt, well-separated changes. While previous work has explored time-varying graphs [14, 11], those methods typically operate in batch mode, making them unsuitable for the real-time, streaming setting addressed here.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is well-motivated and technically sound. The online update equations for the weighted sufficient statistics are standard but correctly derived. The core novelty—using a pooled p-value of Mahalanobis distances to dynamically control data weighting—is a very clever solution for balancing sensitivity to genuine changes against robustness to individual outliers. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 regarding the diligence vs. convergence trade-off is a significant strength, providing principled insight into the algorithm's behavior and elevating it beyond a purely ad-hoc heuristic. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The simulations clearly demonstrate that LoSST performs comparably to a batch learner (PC) in a stationary environment and significantly outperforms it when the structure or parameters change. The analysis of different error types and the investigation into the probabilistic relearning mechanism are insightful.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the algorithm is described in sufficient detail, and the results are presented logically. The authors do a great job of explaining the intuition behind each component of LoSST, making a complex algorithm easy to follow. The figures are informative and support the paper's claims effectively.

**Originality:** The paper addresses a problem of high practical importance that has been relatively under-explored in the mainstream graphical models literature. While the components used (online covariance updates, Mahalanobis distance, p-value pooling) are not new in themselves, their synthesis into a coherent framework for online *structure learning* is highly novel. The approach of dynamically re-weighting sufficient statistics to track a changing model structure appears to be a new contribution to the field.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is high. The assumption of stationarity is a major limitation of most current structure learning methods, and this paper offers a practical, well-founded solution for relaxing that assumption. The algorithm could be readily applied in numerous domains where dynamic systems are studied, such as finance, economics, climate science, and systems biology. The finding that LoSST incurs little to no performance cost in stationary settings makes it an attractive "drop-in" replacement for batch learners in situations where stationarity cannot be guaranteed. The ideas presented are likely to inspire significant follow-up research.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The key parameters (`T`, `gamma`, `nu`) are chosen based on "unsystematic investigation." While the theoretical analysis provides some guidance, the paper would be strengthened by a more systematic sensitivity analysis. How robust is the algorithm to the choice of these parameters? How does performance degrade if they are chosen poorly? This is important for practical application.

2.  **Choice of Base Learner:** The experiments rely exclusively on the PC algorithm, a constraint-based method. The authors briefly mention that score-based methods are not suitable for online learning, but this claim could be elaborated upon. The sufficient statistics tracked by LoSST (covariance matrix and effective sample size) are precisely what is needed for many decomposable scores (e.g., BIC for Gaussian models). The main challenge would be adapting the score's penalty term to the dynamic effective sample size, which seems tractable. A discussion of this, or an experiment with a score-based method, would broaden the paper's applicability.

3.  **Scope of Models:** The work is currently focused on linear Structural Equation Models over continuous variables. While the authors acknowledge this and point to extensions for discrete models (Bayesian Networks) as future work, the challenges there are substantial (e.g., tracking a large number of conditional counts with dynamic weighting). This limitation should be clearly stated.

4.  **Proof Sketches:** The proofs for the diligence and convergence claims are presented as very brief "sketches." While understandable due to space constraints, providing more detailed derivations in an appendix would enhance the paper's technical completeness and value as a reference.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant, practical, and under-explored problem.
*   Presents a novel, elegant, and well-motivated algorithm.
*   Includes strong theoretical analysis that provides deep insight into the algorithm's behavior.
*   Empirical results are comprehensive and convincingly demonstrate the method's superiority over standard batch approaches in non-stationary settings.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The method's performance relies on several key parameters whose selection is not systematically explored.
*   The experimental validation is limited to a single class of structure learning algorithm (constraint-based) and data type (continuous).

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly point toward valuable avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current contribution. The paper is well-executed, clearly presented, and addresses a problem of high significance. I strongly recommend acceptance.
