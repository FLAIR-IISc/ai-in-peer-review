Reaction to the author feedback:
I disagree with point (3). Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) do not assume iid data and allow the underlying generating structure to change (albeit within a restricted family of graphical models). While the present method may be more flexible in some respects, it is misleading to imply it is the first to address structure learning under changing generative processes.

Summary:
The paper proposes a hypothesis-testing–based method for online learning of graphical models from time series, targeting settings where the generating graphical structure may change relatively frequently. The approach is evaluated on both simulated and real data.

Quality:
- The results appear technically sound, and the empirical demonstrations are informative.
- The related work discussion (Section 2) is, however, misleading. Standard score-based structure learning algorithms for Bayesian networks do not require that the data arise from a single, fixed generating model; rather, they rely on decomposability of the scoring function into local terms. It is true that commonly used probabilistic models for those algorithms often assume exchangeability (iid or, more generally, stationarity), but that is a modeling assumption, not a fundamental algorithmic requirement. Please revise the positioning to reflect this distinction and to acknowledge prior work that explicitly handles non-iid or time-varying structures (e.g., Siracusa III and Fisher, 2009).

Clarity:
- The paper needs a more precise statement of the learning task. Is the primary goal (a) detecting and localizing change points under specific assumptions; (b) tracking the current (most recent) generating structure; (c) maintaining a posterior or set of plausible structures over time; or (d) some combination of these? Please define the target output, evaluation metrics, and assumptions about the data-generating process up front.
- It is also unclear whether the main contribution is a new model (assumptions and objectives), a new algorithm (a computational procedure given a well-specified objective), or both. An explicit Contributions section separating modeling assumptions from algorithmic innovations would help.
- The paragraph at the end of page 3 is hard to follow. In particular, the equation for ρ_{r+1}: does it constitute the definition, or does it invoke a previously defined quantity? If it is a new definition, state this explicitly; if not, add a forward/backward reference for clarity. Also, please punctuate sentences that end with displayed equations (use a period).
- Figures 1–4: the font size is too small to read comfortably; doubling it would improve readability. Figures 1 and 2 would benefit from a legend explaining the color coding.
- Section 6: the claim that improved tracking can compensate for additional computational cost seems domain dependent. Please justify this with a clear utility trade-off, sensitivity analysis, or application-specific metrics. Otherwise, the statement reads as speculative.

Originality:
- The work appears original in overall framing and in combining hypothesis tests with online structure updates. However, the incremental novelty is not convincingly articulated. If the construction of the p-values or the sequential testing procedure is the key innovation, please make this explicit, provide intuition, and clarify why existing sequential testing or change-detection frameworks are insufficient.
- Given prior art on nonstationary or dynamic graphical models, the paper should better delineate what is genuinely new: e.g., types of structural changes supported, computational guarantees, error control, or robustness properties.

Significance:
- The problem—adapting graphical structure to evolving time series—is important. However, the paper’s current impact is limited by an imprecise problem statement and by positioning that underplays relevant prior work.
- The approach reads heuristic in places, and the connection between the testing machinery and a well-defined optimality criterion is not fully developed. Strengthening the theoretical framing (e.g., error-rate control over time, consistency under specified change models, or regret-style guarantees) would substantially increase significance.
- To support claims about practical value, consider comparisons with stronger baselines, including Bayesian or penalized likelihood methods that explicitly model change points or time-varying structures, and ablations that isolate the benefit of each component of your pipeline.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the task and assumptions: specify the change process (frequency, magnitude, affected edges/nodes), the exact objective (e.g., minimize detection delay at fixed false alarm rate; maximize accuracy of the current graph), and the evaluation metrics aligned to that objective.
- Reposition relative to prior work: accurately characterize what standard score-based methods assume, and acknowledge prior work handling non-iid or changing structures (including Siracusa III and Fisher, 2009). Explain concretely how your method differs and why those differences matter.
- Elaborate the testing framework: detail how p-values are constructed, how multiple testing over time and edges is handled, and what guarantees (if any) are provided on Type I/II errors, false discovery rates, or detection delays.
- Improve presentation: fix the ambiguous definition of ρ_{r+1}, punctuate equations, enlarge figure fonts, and add legends for colors. Consider moving technical derivations to an appendix while ensuring the main text remains self-contained.
- Quantify computation–accuracy trade-offs: provide wall-clock runtimes, scaling with nodes/edges/stream length, and empirical analysis showing when the added cost yields meaningful tracking gains.

Overall assessment:
This paper tackles a timely and meaningful problem and shows promising empirical results. However, it currently overstates novelty relative to existing work on changing graphical structures, and it lacks a sufficiently precise statement of the task and guarantees. With clearer positioning, a sharper articulation of the core contribution (particularly the testing mechanism), stronger theoretical or empirical support for error control and trade-offs, and improved exposition, the paper would be considerably strengthened.
