**Reaction to Author Feedback**

I disagree with point (3) of the author's feedback. The work of Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) does not assume the data are independent and identically distributed (iid), and it allows the "generating structure" to vary, albeit within a small set of graphical models. While the proposed method is more flexible in some aspects, I find it misleading to claim that it is the first to address the problem.

**Summary**

The paper proposes a hypothesis-testing-based approach to learn graphical models in an online fashion from time series data. The method is specifically designed for scenarios where the "data generating" graphical model may change frequently. The authors demonstrate the performance of their method using both simulated and real data.

**Quality**

The results presented in the paper appear to be correct. However, I find the description of related work in Section 2 to be somewhat misleading. Typical score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks do not assume that the data come from a single generating model; instead, they assume that the scoring function factorizes into local terms. While it is true that typically studied models assume exchangeability (or iid data), this assumption concerns the model, not the algorithm.

**Clarity**

The presentation of the paper is mostly clear, but there is room for improvement. A major point of confusion is the actual learning task being addressed: is it inferring change points (under well-formulated assumptions about the existence and nature of change points), inferring the generating structure of the last data point seen, or something else? Additionally, it is unclear whether the work proposes a new model (formulations of assumptions and goals of learning) or a new algorithm (computational means to achieve the learning goals), or both.

The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow, particularly the equation for \(\rho_{r+1}\). It is unclear whether this equation defines \(\rho_{r+1}\) or if the definition has been given previously. I suggest using periods "." when ending a sentence with a displayed equation. Furthermore, the font size in Figures 1-4 is too small and could be doubled. Adding descriptions for the different colors in Figures 1 and 2 would also improve clarity. In Section 6, I found the idea that improved tracking could compensate for the additional computational cost confusing, as it seems to depend on domain-specific valuation of tracking accuracy against computational cost.

**Originality**

The work appears to be original, but it lacks real innovation or novel ideas. Perhaps there is something in the construction of the p-values that I am not fully understanding.

**Significance**

The potential significance of this work, if published, lies in its potential to lead to the development of superior, presumably Bayesian, methods for the problem. However, this should be viewed as development rather than research. The approach presented is heuristic and addresses a somewhat unclearly defined learning problem that existing machine learning machinery should already have solved.
