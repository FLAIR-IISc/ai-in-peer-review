Paper Summary:
The paper addresses an interesting and underexplored problem: detecting changes in the dependency structure among variables in a data stream (e.g., time series). The authors focus on an efficient (computationally and memory-wise) solution for simpler SEMs and propose a new algorithm, LoSST. LoSST uses a standard batch structure-learning algorithm (PC; Spirtes et al., 2000) and maintains several streaming statistics to decide when to trigger new structure learning. The tracked statistics include each variable’s deviation from its weighted mean, the correlation matrix (Eqs. 1–2), and a weighted sample size. Each incoming point is assigned a weight based on how “surprising” it is (Eqs. 3–4). For each point, the Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed; the associated p-values, estimated using a Hotelling’s T2 distribution, are then combined via Liptak’s weighted pooling of p-values to produce an overall “significance” statistic rho. This statistic is combined with the previous step in a noisy-OR fashion to decide whether to initiate structure relearning. The authors compare LoSST with vanilla batch PC on synthetic datasets in which, after a fixed number of observations, either parameters or structure change, varying the number of variables and maximum degree. They also analyze a real US price index dataset with 529 monthly observations starting in 1967 and six variables, showing that the algorithm detects several known changes/trends in the US economy while ignoring short-lived fluctuations.

Pros:
1. The paper tackles an important, underexplored problem. As the authors note, there is substantial room for further practical methods for these and related models, as well as matching theory and guarantees.
2. The algorithm is novel, practical, and efficient, creatively integrating several techniques.
3. The experiments are generally adequate, exploring parameter effects and including an interesting real-world dataset.
4. The paper provides a solid review of prior work on this problem.
5. Overall, the writing is clear.

Cons:
1. The main weakness is the lack of theory or guarantees under reasonable assumptions. As the authors point out (p. 4), “convergence and diligence are opposing forces,” but it seems plausible to obtain at least some guarantees under simplifying conditions. At minimum, this should be explicitly identified as a direction for future work.
2. Relatedly, LoSST relies heavily on heuristics. That is acceptable and enables a practical method, but the design choices should be made more explicit and easier to follow. Tracking the mean and correlation matrix is straightforward; the key decisions concern reweighting, effective sample size, and when/how to relearn. Several components appear to be used outside the conditions under which their guarantees typically hold. For example, MD p-values are estimated using Hotelling’s T2, with N set to the effective sample size S^r. However, Hotelling’s T2 assumes complete counts from a fixed distribution, not partially reweighted counts. If one were to sample from these structures with partial weights, would the distribution still follow Hotelling’s T2? Similarly, Liptak’s weighted pooling was developed for meta-analysis of independent tests with different sample sizes. If these tools are used outside their standard context, the paper should clearly acknowledge this so readers are not misled about the validity of the resulting significance measures.

Other comments:
- The paper uses footnotes to outline proofs, likely beyond what the conference intended. Please move these and other missing details to the supplementary material.
- Figures are too small, with excessive white space, missing labels, and insufficient detail in captions and legends. The description of the experiments is also unclear in places (e.g., the top paragraph on p. 7 is cryptic).
- The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on edge addition when only parameters change is not convincing.
- In line with the above, it seems desirable to decouple parameter updates from structure updates.

Overall:
A genuinely interesting research direction, with a practical—though heuristic—first attempt at it.
