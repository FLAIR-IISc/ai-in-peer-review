- Results are technically sound and well-supported by simulations and real-data experiments.
- Related work is potentially mischaracterized: score-based BN algorithms don't inherently assume a single generating model; they use factorized scoring and often assume exchangeability for the model, not the algorithm.
- The novelty claim is overstated given prior work (e.g., Siracusa and Fisher III, AISTATS 2009) addressing non-iid data and changing structures.
- The paper needs a clearer statement of the precise learning task: change-point detection, recent-structure inference, or something else.
- It is unclear whether the contribution is a new model (assumptions and goals), a new algorithm (computational method), or both.
- Mathematical exposition has confusing parts (notably the end of page 3 and definition of ρ_{r+1}) and stylistic oddities (missing periods after displayed equations).
- Figures 1–4 have impractically small font sizes and lack color legends/descriptions, reducing interpretability.
- The claim in Section 6 that better tracking could justify extra computation is domain-dependent and needs more justification.
- The combination of techniques appears original but incremental rather than a transformative advance.
- The p-value construction may be a novel element, but it requires deeper scrutiny to assess its contribution.
- The method lacks a strong theoretical foundation, relying on heuristics rather than formal guarantees.
- The work is valuable as a development towards more sophisticated (possibly Bayesian) approaches but stops short of a major research breakthrough.
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an important online non-stationary structure-learning problem, shows promising simulation results, and demonstrates practical relevance on real data.
- Arguments against acceptance: novelty claims are overstated, presentation and mathematical clarity need improvement, theoretical grounding is weak, and overall significance is uncertain.
