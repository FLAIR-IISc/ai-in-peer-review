

Paper Summary:  
This paper addresses the underexplored challenge of tracking time-varying graphical structures in streaming data, where the underlying dependency relationships between variables may change dynamically. The authors propose LoSST, a heuristic algorithm designed for real-time structure learning under locally stationary assumptions (i.e., data are i.i.d. within short intervals but may change across intervals). LoSST tracks weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values derived from Hotelling’s T² distribution, with relearning decisions governed by a probabilistic noisy-OR mechanism. It leverages the PC algorithm for structure learning and is evaluated via synthetic data (with controlled structural/parameter shifts) and a real-world application to U.S. price index volatility (6 variables, 529 monthly observations from 1967–2011). The work builds on related literature concerning online structure learning, changepoint detection, and time-varying graphical models, while distinguishing itself by operating in real-time without batch processing.  

Strengths:  
1. **Originality**: The problem of real-time graphical structure tracking in locally stationary environments is novel and addresses a critical gap in existing literature (which focuses on batch learning or abrupt changepoints). The algorithmic approach creatively combines Mahalanobis distances, Liptak’s pooled p-values, and probabilistic relearning to achieve practical online learning.  
2. **Significance**: The method has clear applicability in dynamic domains (e.g., financial time series, causal discovery in evolving systems), demonstrated by the compelling U.S. price index case study that detects macroeconomic shifts (e.g., the Great Moderation) while ignoring short-term volatility spikes.  
3. **Quality**: Experimental design is thorough, with systematic testing across variable counts, graph densities, and structural/parameter change scenarios. Results show LoSST matches batch learning (PC algorithm) under stationarity and significantly outperforms it during structural shifts.  
4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-written, with clear exposition of the algorithm, sufficient technical detail for reproducibility, and effective use of figures to illustrate performance.  

Weaknesses:  
1. **Quality**: The lack of theoretical guarantees (e.g., convergence/diligence trade-offs under assumptions) is a notable gap. While the paper acknowledges the tension between convergence and diligence, it does not provide formal analysis or bounds—critical for assessing the algorithm’s reliability in scientific contexts.  
2. **Quality**: Key heuristic choices (e.g., using Hotelling’s T² with effective sample size for p-values, Liptak’s pooling for meta-analysis) operate outside their typical theoretical guarantees. The authors should explicitly clarify these limitations to avoid misinterpretation (e.g., "The p-value approximation assumes complete i.i.d. data; effective sample size may affect distributional validity").  
3. **Clarity**: Figure presentation requires improvement (e.g., small font, missing labels, inconsistent legends). The explanation for LoSST’s higher edge removal error during parameter-only changes (Section 4.2) is insufficient; decoupling parameter updates from structural relearning would strengthen the argument.  
4. **Significance**: Future work on theoretical guarantees and handling rapidly changing structures should be emphasized as a priority, not merely noted as "another direction."  

Arguments for acceptance:  
- Novel problem addressing a clear gap in dynamic graphical models.  
- Practical algorithm with strong empirical validation on synthetic and real data.  
- Well-structured, technically sound experiments demonstrating clear advantages over batch learning during structural shifts.  

Arguments against acceptance:  
- Theoretical foundations are underdeveloped, weakening the contribution’s scientific rigor.  
- Heuristic decisions lack justification within their theoretical context, potentially misleading readers.  
- Figure quality and certain explanations (e.g., edge removal error) detract from reproducibility.  

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to dynamic structure learning. With minor revisions to address theoretical gaps and clarify methodological choices, it would be a strong fit for NIPS.
