Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical structures in linear Gaussian SEMs (DAGs with linear equations and Gaussian noise). The method maintains streaming estimates of sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) using adaptive per-sample weights ar. Weights are increased when incoming data fit poorly under the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance; since single outliers are not decisive, the authors pool p-values over time using Lipták’s method to obtain a running pooled p-value ρr, which in turn controls both the weight update and the probability of “relearning” the graph. Structure is relearned by invoking the PC algorithm on the current weighted sufficient statistics. The authors analyze trade-offs between “convergence” and “diligence” as a function of parameters T and γ, and present simulations under structural and parametric change, along with a CPI volatility case study.

The paper is positioned relative to score-based and constraint-based static learning (Chickering; Pearl; Spirtes et al.), online change-point detection (Adams & MacKay; frequentist CPD), and time-varying graph learning (Talih & Hengartner, undirected GGMs; Siracusa & Fisher, time-indexed directed models). It argues that existing methods either assume stationarity or require batch data. After reading the rebuttal, I disagree with the claim that Siracusa & Fisher (AISTATS 2009) assumes i.i.d. data or cannot accommodate changing structure: they explicitly model time-varying dependency structure (albeit within a restricted family and in batch). LoSST is more flexible in some respects (streaming, generic SEMs), but it is not the first to address time-varying graphical structure.

Strengths
- Clear and practically motivated goal: online tracking of changing graph structure without storing full data.
- Simple, modular algorithmic design: streaming sufficient statistics + adaptive forgetting + probabilistic triggers for relearning; can be paired with various structure learners beyond PC.
- Useful discussion of convergence vs. diligence trade-offs, with intuitions tied to parameter ranges.
- Empirical results show that LoSST matches batch PC when the process is stationary and outperforms PC under structural and parametric shifts, including a long-horizon run with probabilistic relearning.
- Real-data illustration (US price indices) aligns with known macro phenomena and shows the value of pooling over multiple moderate deviations rather than reacting to isolated spikes.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Statistical grounding of the pooled p-values is unclear. Lipták’s pooling assumes some form of independence/weak dependence across tests; here the p-values are computed from overlapping, adaptively weighted, estimated covariance matrices, so the dependence is nontrivial. Please justify the use of Hotelling’s T^2 with an estimated, time-varying covariance and explain its calibration with the “effective” N. A small simulation to assess Type I error of the pooled test under stationarity would help.
- The transformation from pooled p-value ρr to weight ar+1 (linear ramp up to γar) is heuristic. An ablation comparing this to standard exponential forgetting, sliding windows, and CPD-triggered resets would clarify how much is gained by pooled p-value weighting vs. simpler schemes.
- Independence testing within PC with weighted sufficient statistics is not fully specified. How are significance thresholds adjusted for the effective sample size? Are partial correlations computed with the weighted covariance, and are test statistics calibrated accordingly?
- Computational aspects need clarification: maintaining and inverting Cr online (for Mahalanobis distance) can be numerically delicate with adaptive weights. Please discuss numerical stability, regularization, and complexity (e.g., rank-one updates vs. recomputation).
- The convergence/diligence statements are presented as sketches. Formal statements of assumptions (distributional, dependence of ar on ρr, relearning policy) and precise theorems would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
- Please define the learning objective explicitly: is the target the current generating graph Gr at time r, the most recent stable regime, or detection of change points plus recovery of post-change structure? Different objectives imply different evaluation protocols.
- Section 3: make the definition of ρr self-contained, state assumptions behind the T^2 approximation, and use punctuation after displayed equations for readability. The paragraph introducing ρr is hard to follow; define ηr and τr before using them.
- Figures 1–4: increase font sizes, add legends mapping colors to variable/degree settings, and use consistent axis labels. Briefly explain “graph relearning schedule” in the captions.
- Section 6: the sentence suggesting that “improved tracking could compensate for additional computational cost” needs context; this depends on application-specific accuracy/cost trade-offs.

Originality
- The combination of streaming sufficient statistics with p-value pooling to adapt forgetting and trigger relearning is a novel and practical twist on online tracking. That said, elements such as exponential forgetting, sliding windows, and relearning triggers have precedents in online estimation and CPD. Position the contribution more precisely relative to: (i) windowed/forgetful covariance estimation plus PC; (ii) CPD + reset + batch learner; (iii) time-varying GGMs (Talih & Hengartner) and time-indexed BN structures (Siracusa & Fisher). The claim that standard score-based algorithms “assume a single generating model” is misleading; it is the statistical model (i.i.d./exchangeability) that implies that, not the optimization machinery per se.

Significance
- The problem is important, and the proposed approach could be useful in practice due to its single-pass, low-memory nature. However, the empirical evaluation is limited to comparisons with batch PC. To establish significance, include baselines that reflect common practice for nonstationary data:
  - PC on sliding windows of various widths;
  - PC with exponential forgetting (fixed α);
  - CPD (Bayesian or frequentist) to reset sufficient statistics and relearn;
  - For Gaussian data, time-varying GGMs with edge-change penalties.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν and show how results vary; the current parameter choices are ad hoc.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely, practically relevant problem with a simple, implementable method.
- Demonstrates clear gains over a naive batch learner under distribution shift while maintaining parity under stationarity.
- Provides thoughtful discussion of online trade-offs (convergence vs. diligence).

Cons for acceptance
- Heuristic statistical elements (pooled p-values, effective N, test calibration) are insufficiently justified.
- Ambiguity about the precise learning objective and evaluation criteria.
- Limited baselines; unclear whether simpler window/forgetting or CPD+reset strategies would perform similarly or better.
- Some claims about related work overstate novelty.

Recommendations to improve the paper
- Clarify the objective and evaluation metrics; report time-to-detection and time-to-recovery along with structural Hamming distance.
- Add baselines (sliding windows, exponential forgetting, CPD+reset) and an ablation on the pooling/weighting and relearning components.
- Calibrate and validate the pooled p-value mechanism under stationarity (false alarm rates) and discuss dependence between tests.
- Detail how weighted covariances feed into PC’s tests and adjust significance levels for effective N.
- Expand computational details and stability measures; provide complexity per step and per relearning event.
- Improve figures and notation; add legends, larger fonts, and consistent punctuation.

Overall assessment
Promising and practically oriented, but currently too heuristic and under-evaluated relative to the nonstationary baselines that practitioners would consider. I recommend rejection in its current form, with the expectation that the above clarifications, baselines, and statistical validations would lead to a much stronger submission.
