Paper summary:
The paper addresses an interesting and under-explored problem: detecting changes in the dependency structure among variables in a data stream (e.g., time series). The authors focus on an efficient (computationally and memory-wise) solution for simpler SEM models and propose a new algorithm, LoSST. LoSST leverages a standard batch structure-learning algorithm (PC by Spirtes et al., 2000) and maintains several statistics over the data stream to decide when to trigger new structure learning. The tracked statistics include the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix (Eqs. 1, 2), and the weighted sample size. A weighting scheme assigns sample weights to each new point based on how “surprising” it is (Eqs. 3, 4). For each point, a Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed, and p-values for the MDs are estimated using a Hotelling’s T2 distribution. These p-values are then combined using Lipták’s weighted pooling to yield an overall “significance” statistic ρ. This statistic is used in a noisy-OR configuration with the previous step to decide whether to initiate new structure learning. The authors compare LoSST to vanilla batch PC on synthetic datasets where either a parameter change or a structural change is introduced after a fixed number of datapoints, across different numbers of variables and maximum degrees. They also analyze a real dataset of U.S. price indices with 529 monthly observations (starting in 1967) over six variables, showing that the algorithm detects some known economic shifts while ignoring short-lived changes.

Pros:
1. The paper tackles an important and under-explored problem. As the authors note, there is ample room for further development of practical solutions for these and related models, as well as for aligning practice with theory and providing theoretical guarantees.
2. The algorithm is novel, practical, and efficient, and it makes creative use of a range of methods to achieve this.
3. The experiments are generally adequate, exploring the impact of various parameters and including an interesting real-world dataset.
4. The related-work review is thorough and informative.
5. The paper is overall well written.

Cons:
1. The main shortcoming, given the importance of the problem, is the absence of theory or guarantees under plausible assumptions. As the authors point out (p. 4), convergence and diligence can operate at cross purposes, but it seems feasible to offer some form of guarantees under simplifying conditions. At minimum, please discuss this more explicitly as a direction for future work.
2. The proposed solution, LoSST, leans heavily on heuristics. This is acceptable and enables a practical solution, but the decision points should be more transparently documented. Since tracking the mean and correlation matrix is straightforward, the key choices concern reweighting, effective sample size, and when/how to relearn structure. The paper makes creative use of methods under conditions that likely void those methods’ guarantees. For instance, you estimate MD p-values via Hotelling’s T2 distribution and set the sample size N to the effective sample size Sr. However, Hotelling’s T2 is derived for complete samples from a common distribution, not for partially weighted samples. If one were to sample from these networks/structures with partial weights, would the distribution still follow this form? Similarly, Lipták’s weighted pooling of p-values was developed for meta-analysis of independent tests with differing sample sizes. If these methods are used outside their stated assumptions, the paper should clearly acknowledge this so readers are not misled about the validity of the resulting statistics.

Other comments:
- The paper uses footnotes to sketch proofs in a way that likely exceeds what the conference format intends. Please consider moving these and other missing technical details to a supplement.
- Figures are too small, contain excessive whitespace, and often lack labels, detailed captions, and clear legends. The experimental description is sometimes unclear (e.g., the top paragraph on p. 7 is cryptic).
- The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on edge addition when only parameters change is not convincing.
- Related to the above, it would be desirable to decouple parameter-update triggers from structure-update triggers.

Overall:
A compelling direction for future research, with a practical—though heuristic—first implementation. Clarifying the heuristic choices, explicitly stating when methods are used outside their guarantees, improving experimental clarity/figures, and discussing possible theoretical guarantees (even under simplified assumptions) would substantially strengthen the paper.
