

The paper proposes an online hypothesis-testing approach for learning graphical models from time series with potentially changing structures. While the problem setting is relevant, several critical issues undermine the contribution's novelty and clarity.

**Related Work & Claim of Novelty**  
The claim that the method is "the first to address non-iid data" (Section 1) is inaccurate. Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) explicitly handle non-iid data by allowing the generating structure to vary across a small set of graphical models, though not for time series. The proposed method is indeed more flexible, but misrepresenting prior work as assuming iid data (which they do not) misleads readers. The authors should clarify that their contribution lies in *online* adaptation for time series, not in addressing non-iid data generally.

**Clarity & Problem Definition**  
The core learning task is ambiguously defined. Is the goal: (a) detecting change points in the generating structure, (b) inferring the *current* structure (last observed state), or (c) simultaneously tracking changes and learning the latest model? The paper conflates these, particularly in Section 3. For instance, Equation (3) describes a *change-point detection* framework, yet the experiments (Section 5) focus on predicting the *final* structure. This lack of precision makes it impossible to evaluate the method’s intended contribution. The authors must explicitly state the learning objective and distinguish it from related change-point detection or online learning literature.

**Technical Presentation**  
- **Equations & Formatting:** The definition of $\rho_{r+1}$ (end of p.3) is unclear—does it rely on prior definitions or is it self-contained? Displayed equations should end with a period.  
- **Figures:** Font sizes in Figures 1–4 are too small (suggested doubling). Figure 2 lacks a color legend, making it difficult to interpret.  
- **Section 6:** The claim that "improved tracking compensates for computational cost" is domain-dependent and unsupported. Without a cost-benefit framework, this assertion lacks rigor.

**Originality & Significance**  
The method appears technically sound but lacks substantive innovation. The hypothesis-testing framework for structure learning is not novel (e.g., see Liu et al., *JMLR* 2010 for related p-value constructions in static settings). The core novelty—if any—lies in the *online* adaptation of p-values for time series, but this is not clearly articulated or justified. The paper fails to demonstrate *why* the proposed p-value construction is superior to existing alternatives (e.g., sequential hypothesis testing with sliding windows).  

The significance is overstated. The claim that this "would quickly lead to superior Bayesian methods" is speculative. The approach is heuristic and lacks theoretical guarantees (e.g., regret bounds for change-point detection or structure estimation). Without a clear problem definition or novel algorithmic insight, this work appears more like an *engineering adaptation* of existing techniques than a research contribution. It does not address fundamental challenges in online graphical model learning (e.g., non-stationarity, model selection under drift), and standard machine learning tools (e.g., online Bayesian networks) could likely solve the stated problem with less complexity.

**Quality & Correctness**  
The experimental results (simulated and real data) are plausible, but the evaluation metrics are insufficient. For instance, the "tracking" accuracy in Figure 3 is measured only on the final structure, not on change-point detection performance. The comparison to baselines (e.g., static BN learning) is superficial—no ablation on the hypothesis-testing component is provided. The lack of statistical significance tests for performance gains further weakens the claims.

**Recommendation**  
This paper requires substantial revision to address the ambiguities in problem formulation, clarify the true contribution relative to prior work, and provide stronger justification for the method’s novelty. The current version misrepresents related work, conflates distinct learning objectives, and fails to demonstrate theoretical or empirical advantages over simpler approaches. As submitted, it lacks the rigor expected at NIPS.  

**Summary of Key Revisions Needed:**  
1. **Correct the claim about non-iid data** by properly citing Siracusa & Fisher (2009) and clarifying the scope (online time series, not general non-iid).  
2. **Explicitly define the learning task** (e.g., "We aim to detect structural changes and maintain a current structure estimate").  
3. **Clarify the role of p-values**—is the novelty in the construction or the online adaptation?  
4. **Improve figures** (font sizes, color legends) and **restructure Section 6** to justify computational trade-offs.  
5. **Strengthen significance** by linking to open theoretical challenges (e.g., "Our method provides a first step toward regret-minimizing online structure learning under drift").  

Without these changes, the paper risks being perceived as incremental rather than innovative. The current draft does not advance the field beyond existing heuristics.
