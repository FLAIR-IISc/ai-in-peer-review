Paper Summary:

The paper addresses the challenging problem of tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a stream of data points. The authors focus on developing an efficient solution for Structural Equation Models (SEMs) and propose a novel algorithm, Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST). LoSST utilizes a standard batch learning algorithm for underlying structure learning and tracks several statistics from the data stream to determine whether to initiate new structure learning. The tracked statistics include the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix, and the weighted sample size. A weighting scheme is employed for each new point based on how "surprising" the new data point is, using the Mahalanobis distance and Liptak's weighted pooling of p-values to compute an overall "significance" statistic. This statistic is then used in a noisy OR configuration with the previous step to decide whether to start a new structure learning. The authors compare LoSST to vanilla PC batch learning on synthetic data sets with changes in parameters or structure, testing different variable and max degree configurations. They also perform a short analysis of real-life data from the US price index, demonstrating the algorithm's ability to detect known changes and trends while ignoring short-lived changes.

Strengths:

1. The paper tackles an interesting and under-explored problem, providing a novel and practical solution.
2. The algorithm is efficient and makes creative use of various methods to address the problem.
3. The experiments seem adequate, exploring the effects of different parameters and including an interesting real-life data set.
4. The authors provide a good review of previous work on this problem.
5. The paper is well-written.

Weaknesses:

1. The paper lacks theoretical guarantees or convergence analysis under simplifying conditions. While the authors mention the opposing forces of convergence and diligence, they do not provide any guarantees or discuss this as a direction for future work.
2. The LoSST algorithm relies heavily on heuristics, which, although practical, may not provide the desired guarantees. The authors should clearly note the decisions involved in reweighting, effective sample size, and relearning. Additionally, they should acknowledge that some methods are used outside their original context, such as estimating the Mahalanobis distance p-value using Hotelling's T2 distribution and employing Liptak's weighted p-values.

Other Comments:

- The authors' use of footnotes for proof outlines is unconventional and may be better suited for supplementary materials.
- The figures are too small, with unnecessary space between them, missing labels, and inadequate captions.
- The experiment descriptions, such as the top paragraph on page 7, are unclear and require more detail.
- The explanation for LoSST's poor performance on edge addition when only parameters are changed is unsatisfactory.
- Decoupling parameter updates and structure updates may be a desirable direction for future research.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper addresses a challenging and under-explored problem in the field.
* The proposed algorithm is novel, practical, and efficient.
* The experiments are adequate and provide insight into the algorithm's performance.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper lacks theoretical guarantees or convergence analysis.
* The algorithm relies heavily on heuristics, which may not provide the desired guarantees.
* The presentation and clarity of the paper can be improved.

Recommendation:

The paper has potential, but it requires revisions to address the mentioned weaknesses. The authors should provide more theoretical analysis, clarify the decisions involved in the algorithm, and improve the presentation and clarity of the paper. With these revisions, the paper can make a more significant contribution to the field.
