Reaction to the author feedback:
I cannot agree with point (3). Siracusa and Fisher III (AISTATS 2009) do not assume that the data are i.i.d., and they allow the “generating structure” to vary (even if within a restricted family of graphical models). While the present method is arguably more flexible, it is misleading to claim it is the first to address this problem.

Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic, online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical structure in multivariate time series, assuming local stationarity. At each time step, it maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size), where weights adapt to the fit between new data and the current model via Mahalanobis distances pooled into a running p-value (Lipták’s method). These statistics drive both the update of effective sample size and the probability of triggering structure relearning (implemented via the PC algorithm). The authors argue LoSST matches batch learning when the structure is globally stationary and outperforms batch methods when the structure changes, based on simulations and a CPI case study.

The work is positioned against: (a) standard score-based and constraint-based structure learning methods that presume a single generating model; (b) online changepoint detection (Bayesian and frequentist), which typically assumes abrupt changes and requires storing substantial data; and (c) prior time-varying structure approaches that operate in batch mode (e.g., Talih & Hengartner, 2005; Siracusa & Fisher III, 2009). The paper’s main contribution is a practical, single-pass mechanism to adapt weights and relearning frequency based on pooled goodness-of-fit signals, enabling online structure updates without maintaining distributions over models.

Quality
- The methodology appears technically sound, and the simulation results are consistent with the claims. The mechanism for updating weighted sufficient statistics and the link to Mahalanobis distances are reasonable, and the probabilistic relearning schedule is clearly described.
- However, the related work discussion is partly misleading. Typical score-based Bayesian network structure learners do not assume data come from a single generating model at the algorithmic level; rather, they rely on decomposable scores and, in most commonly studied models, on exchangeability/i.i.d. assumptions in the data model. The limitation is thus in the modeling assumptions, not strictly in the search machinery.
- The experimental setup is sensible: comparisons to a batch baseline (PC), scenarios with structure and parameter changes, and a real-data illustration. The interpretation of where LoSST gains (post-change) and where batch methods can temporarily look better (edge removals after parameter-only changes) is helpful.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly well written, but key aspects of the learning objective are insufficiently specified. Is the primary task (i) changepoint detection under a stated generative model for changes, (ii) estimation of the current-time generating structure, or (iii) both? Clarifying the formal learning goal and assumptions would help readers assess guarantees and compare to CPD and time-varying graph literature.
- It is also somewhat unclear whether the contribution is a new model (assumptions/objective) or a new algorithmic framework for a known objective (online structure tracking under local stationarity). As written, the approach reads as an algorithmic heuristic layered on standard SEM/PC assumptions.
- The paragraph at the end of page 3 is hard to follow. For example, does the displayed expression for ρr+1 constitute its definition, or was ρr+1 defined earlier? Please make definitions explicit when first introduced. Also, end sentences with periods even when they conclude with a displayed equation.
- Figures 1–4: the font sizes are difficult to read; doubling the font size would greatly improve readability. Figures 1 and 2 would benefit from legends or explicit descriptions of the color coding.
- Section 6 states that improved tracking could compensate for added computational cost. Whether this is worthwhile depends on domain-specific trade-offs between tracking accuracy and computational budget; a more nuanced discussion would be appropriate.

Originality
- The problem setting is important, and the combination of pooled p-values, adaptive weighting of sufficient statistics, and probabilistic relearning is, to my knowledge, novel in this specific form. That said, the paper’s innovations feel incremental and heuristic rather than conceptually groundbreaking. If there is a deeper insight—perhaps in the pooled p-value construction or the convergence/diligence trade-off—please articulate it more explicitly and connect it to prior theory.

Significance
- The work addresses a relevant practical gap: online tracking of changing graphical structure without storing large windows of data. The simulations and case study suggest the approach is useful.
- However, the problem formulation remains somewhat vague, and the method is heuristic. I anticipate that this paper, if published, would primarily stimulate more principled (likely Bayesian) approaches to the same problem class, rather than stand as the definitive solution. Stronger theoretical guarantees or clearer problem formalization would substantially increase its impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Tackles an important, under-explored practical problem: online structure learning under local nonstationarity.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with reasonable performance on simulations and a real dataset.
- Clearly describes a useful weighting/update mechanism and a pragmatic relearning policy.

Cons for acceptance
- Overstates novelty relative to prior time-varying structure learning (e.g., Siracusa & Fisher III, 2009).
- Learning objective and modeling assumptions are not crisply specified; unclear whether this is a model, an algorithm, or both.
- Largely heuristic; limited theoretical justification beyond qualitative convergence/diligence discussion.
- Clarity issues (definitions around ρr+1, equation punctuation, small figure fonts, missing legends).
- Claims about cost–benefit trade-offs in Section 6 need more careful, domain-aware framing.
