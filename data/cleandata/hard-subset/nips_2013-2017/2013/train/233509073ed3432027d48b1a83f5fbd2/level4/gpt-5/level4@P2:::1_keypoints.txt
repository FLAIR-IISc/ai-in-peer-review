- Proposal: LoSST — an online method that tracks time-varying DAG structure by maintaining weighted sufficient statistics and an effective sample size.  
- Adaptation mechanism: uses pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances (Liptak’s method) to downweight unlikely recent data and trigger relearning.  
- Structure update: relearning via the PC algorithm on a fixed schedule or probabilistically; shown to match batch PC under stationarity and recover better after changes; demonstrated on CPI volatility.  
- Positioning: compared to batch structure learning, changepoint detection, and prior time-varying-graph work (e.g., Talih & Hengartner; Siracusa & Fisher).  
- Prior-work caveat: Siracusa & Fisher (AISTATS 2009) already model time-varying edges without iid assumption, so claims of being first are overstated.  
- Reviewer reaction: authors should acknowledge Siracusa & Fisher as closely related; LoSST remains distinct (frequentist, heuristic, lightweight) but novelty claims need moderation.  
- Strength — problem importance: tackles online tracking of nonstationary graphical structures, a relevant practical problem.  
- Strength — simplicity: single-pass sufficient-statistic updates with modest memory.  
- Strength — intuition: clear link between goodness-of-fit (Mahalanobis distances) and adaptation via pooled p-values.  
- Strength — empirical performance: parity with batch PC in stationary periods and improved recovery after changes.  
- Strength — real-data robustness: CPI case shows responsiveness to volatility shifts and robustness to isolated outliers.  
- Quality concern: methodology is largely heuristic with limited theoretical guarantees on edge-level error under adaptation.  
- Sensitivity concern: limited sensitivity analyses; performance likely depends on T, γ, ν, and relearning policy but these are underexplored.  
- Baseline concern: comparisons limited to batch PC; missing key baselines (sliding-window PC, CPD+per-segment learning, Siracusa & Fisher or approximation, score-based forgetting methods).  
- Complexity concern: computational cost of probabilistic relearning not quantified, problematic for high-dimensional settings.  
- Clarity issue: learning objective is underspecified — unclear whether goal is change-point detection, current-graph estimation, or full time-indexed sequence.  
- Model vs algorithm: unclear whether LoSST introduces a new statistical model (local stationarity assumptions) or is an algorithmic scheme — needs explicit statement.  
- Notational clarity: definition of ρ_{r+1} is not self-contained; displayed equations need clearer introductions and punctuation.  
- Figure/exposition issues: small fonts, missing legends, lack of reported parameter settings (T, γ, ν), and unclear preprocessing hinder reproducibility.  
- Trade-off framing: section 6’s claim that tracking can “compensate” for higher cost should be framed as application-dependent cost–benefit.  
- Originality assessment: the engineering combination is useful, but conceptual novelty is incremental given prior time-varying structure and streaming/forgetting literature.  
- Significance assessment: practical relevance is high for streaming use, but unclear impact due to limited baselines and vague problem definition.  
- Suggested improvements: clarify task and evaluation metrics; run ablations/sensitivity analyses and report relearning frequency and compute footprint.  
- Suggested improvements: add baselines (sliding-window PC, CPD+learning, score-based forgetting, Siracusa & Fisher variant) and tighten pooled p-value/effective-sample-size exposition with proofs or appendices.  
- Suggested improvements: improve figures/captions, include legends and parameter settings.  
- Acceptance arguments: addresses a timely problem with a simple, scalable method; pooled p-value mechanism is a transferable idea; experiments are promising and reproducible in principle.  
- Rejection arguments: overstates novelty, lacks crisp problem definition, limited theory and baselines, and presentation issues impede impact.  
- Overall recommendation: leaning toward rejection in current form; could be acceptable after clearer problem definition, moderated claims, stronger baselines, and improved exposition.
