- Problem: learning graphical model structure online from non-stationary data streams where the generative process can change over time.  
- Method: proposes LoSST, an online algorithm for SEMs that tracks locally weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, effective sample size).  
- Surprise weighting: weights new points by "surprise" measured via Mahalanobis distance and its Hotelling's T²–based p-value.  
- Change detection: pools recent p-values with Liptak's method to produce a statistic ρ that probabilistically triggers structure relearning.  
- Relearning: when triggered, runs a batch structure learner (PC algorithm) on current weighted sufficient statistics.  
- Experiments: synthetic tests show LoSST matches batch PC in stationary settings and outperforms it under changing structure; also applied to US price indices.  
- Real-data result: LoSST identifies known macroeconomic change periods while being robust to short-term volatility.  
- Strength — originality: addresses an important, under-explored real-world problem of online structure tracking.  
- Strength — algorithm: creatively combines Mahalanobis distances, Hotelling's T², and Liptak pooling into a pragmatic, efficient online framework.  
- Strength — evaluation and writing: thorough synthetic experiments, real-data application, and clear motivation with useful discussion on convergence vs. diligence.  
- Weakness — theory: method is largely heuristic and lacks formal guarantees (e.g., detection guarantees or time-to-detect bounds).  
- Weakness — statistical assumptions: uses Hotelling's T² with an effective sample size and Liptak pooling despite violated assumptions (re-weighting and dependent tests), needing explicit acknowledgement and discussion of impacts.  
- Weakness — presentation: some explanations (e.g., edge removal error behavior) are unconvincing, figures are small/cluttered, and proof sketches in footnotes are inappropriate.  
- Suggestions: discuss decoupling parameter drift detection from structural relearning, explicitly frame the work as a strong empirical/heuristic contribution, and move detailed proofs to supplementary material.
