## Review:

The paper addresses a compelling and relatively unexplored problem: tracking evolving dependency structures within data streams. The authors focus on developing a computationally and memory-efficient solution tailored for Structural Equation Models (SEMs), introducing a novel algorithm called LoSST. LoSST leverages a standard batch learning approach (PC algorithm by Spirtes et al., 2000) for structure learning, coupled with a mechanism for triggering re-learning based on statistics monitored from the incoming data stream. These statistics include distances from weighted means, the correlation matrix (eq. 1,2), and a weighted sample size. The weighting scheme assigns importance to new data points based on their “surprisingness” (Eq. 3,4), quantified using Mahalanobis distance (MD) and Hotelling’s T2 distribution to estimate p-values. Liptak’s weighted pooling of p-values then combines these into an overall significance statistic, ρ, used in conjunction with prior information to determine when to initiate a new structure learning phase. The algorithm’s performance is evaluated on synthetic datasets featuring both parameter and structural changes, varying the number of variables and maximum degree. A brief analysis is also presented using US price index data (529 monthly observations, six variables), demonstrating the algorithm’s ability to detect known economic shifts while filtering out transient fluctuations.

**Strengths:**

1.  The paper investigates a significant and under-addressed problem. The authors correctly identify the need for practical solutions and further theoretical development in this area.
2.  The proposed algorithm, LoSST, is novel and appears to be a pragmatic and efficient approach. It creatively integrates several existing methods.
3.  The experimental setup appears reasonable, systematically exploring the impact of various parameters and incorporating a relevant real-world dataset.
4.  The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work.
5.  The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  A key limitation is the lack of theoretical analysis or formal guarantees. While the authors acknowledge the tension between convergence and diligence (p. 4), exploring potential guarantees under simplifying assumptions – and discussing this as future work – would significantly strengthen the paper.
2.  LoSST relies heavily on heuristics. While this enables a practical solution, the rationale behind key decisions should be more transparent. Specifically, the choices regarding re-weighting, effective sample size determination, and re-learning triggers warrant further clarification. The authors employ established methods, such as Hotelling’s T2 distribution and Liptak’s weighted p-values, in contexts that potentially invalidate their underlying assumptions. For instance, using Hotelling’s T2 with a re-weighted, effective sample size raises questions about the validity of the resulting p-value distribution. Similarly, Liptak’s method assumes independent tests, which may not hold here. A clear acknowledgement of these deviations from standard usage is crucial to avoid misinterpretation.

**Other Comments:**

*   The extensive use of footnotes for proofs is unconventional and potentially disruptive. Utilizing supplementary materials would be a more appropriate approach for detailed proofs and additional information.
*   Figures are inadequately sized, lack clear labels and detailed captions, and are spaced inefficiently. The description of the experiments, particularly the initial paragraph on p. 7, is unclear.
*   The explanation for LoSST’s diminished performance on edge addition when only parameters change is unconvincing.
*   Decoupling parameter updates from structural updates appears desirable and could improve the algorithm’s performance. 

Overall, this paper presents an interesting and promising direction for research. The practical implementation, while heuristic, provides a valuable first step towards addressing a challenging problem.
