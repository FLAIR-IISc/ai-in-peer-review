Reaction to the author feedback:

I disagree with point (3) of the author's response. The work of Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) does not assume that the data are independently and identically distributed (iid) and allows the "generating structure" to vary, albeit within a limited set of graphical models. Although the proposed method offers more flexibility in this regard, I find it misleading to claim that it is the first to address this problem.

The paper proposes a hypothesis-testing-based approach for learning graphical models in an online setting from time series data. The method is specifically designed for scenarios where the "data generating" graphical model may change relatively frequently. The authors demonstrate the performance of their method using both simulated and real data.

Quality:

The results presented in the paper appear to be correct. However, I find the description of related work in Section 2 to be somewhat misleading. Typical score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks do not assume that the data come from a single generating model; instead, they assume that the scoring function can be factorized into local terms. While it is true that many commonly studied models assume exchangeability (or iid data), this assumption pertains to the model rather than the algorithm itself.

Clarity:

The presentation of the paper is mostly clear, but there is room for improvement. A major point of confusion is the lack of clarity regarding the actual learning task being addressed. Specifically, it is unclear whether the goal is to infer change points (under well-formulated assumptions about the existence and nature of these change points), infer the generating structure of the most recent data point seen, or something else. Additionally, it is difficult to discern whether the work proposes a new model (i.e., a formulation of assumptions and learning goals) or a new algorithm (i.e., computational means to achieve the learning goals), or both.

The paragraph at the end of page 3 is challenging to follow, particularly with regard to the equation for \rho_{r+1}; it is unclear whether this equation defines \rho_{r+1} or if the definition has been provided earlier. I suggest that the authors use periods (.) to end sentences that include displayed equations. Furthermore, the font size in Figures 1-4 could be increased, and including descriptions for the different colors used in Figures 1 and 2 would be helpful. In Section 6, I found the idea that improved tracking could compensate for the additional computational cost to be confusing, as this seems to depend on domain-specific valuations of tracking accuracy versus computational cost.

Originality:

The work appears to be original, but it seems to lack any significant innovation or novel ideas. The construction of the p-values may be an exception, although I was unable to fully follow this aspect of the paper.

Significance:

The potential significance of this work, if published, lies in its potential to lead to the development of superior, presumably Bayesian, methods for addressing the problem. However, this should be viewed as a development rather than a research contribution. The paper presents a heuristic approach to a somewhat unclearly defined learning problem, which existing machine learning machinery should already be able to solve.
