This manuscript presents a systematic approach to structure learning for Structural Equation Models (SEMs) that can accommodate data sets that are only locally independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.), meaning the data may be generated from distinct underlying models over time. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, addressing the important problem of learning from time-dependent data, which has been neglected by many commonly used algorithms for structure learning. The proposed techniques for extending learning to time-dependent data are simple yet effective, as demonstrated by the experiments with the PC method.

There are two suggestions that could clarify the paper's contributions. Firstly, in the experiments, the authors state that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly, ..." This assertion may be too strong, as differences among methods for structure learning can be considerable. It would be better to rephrase this statement to make it clearer that the experiments focus on the PC method for time-independent and time-dependent data, which is highly relevant, and then speculate that similar results might be obtained with other methods. Alternatively, the authors could keep the original assertion and provide a detailed explanation of why this would be the case.

Secondly, in the conclusions, the authors discuss that applying the same ideas to learning the structure of Bayesian networks may not be straightforward due to difficulties in maintaining sufficient statistics. Although this issue is not central to the proposal in this paper, it highlights that extending the learning procedure to Bayesian networks and other models may require more than a few modifications. Therefore, I believe the paper would benefit from disclosing this fact from the beginning, presenting the paper in more direct terms about its goal of applying the approach to SEM models, while still pointing out that extension to other models is possible but not necessarily straightforward. In short, the paper could be more precise about its scope.

This is a well-written paper about learning the graph structure for SEMs under the assumption that data are only locally i.i.d. The formulation is simple to understand and implement, and the experimental results look promising, even if focused primarily on a comparison using the PC method. I acknowledge having read the feedback from the authors.
