

The paper introduces LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm for online structure learning of graphical models under locally stationary conditions—where the generating structure may change over time but remains stable within short intervals. This addresses a critical gap in existing work, as standard structure learning methods (e.g., PC algorithm [12]) assume global stationarity, while prior online approaches either lack generality for structure learning (e.g., TD(0) [13]) or require batch processing (e.g., Talih & Hengartner [14]). The work builds directly on NIPS 2005 (Talih & Hengartner) and recent NIPS advances in online learning [1, 4], but extends them by enabling real-time tracking of both structural and parametric changes without retrospective data.  

**Strengths:**  
- *Originality & Significance:* The problem of locally stationary structure learning is well-motivated and underexplored in NIPS (e.g., no NIPS paper prior to 2020 has addressed this for general graphical models). LoSST’s design—using weighted sufficient statistics and pooled p-values to dynamically adjust learning weights—is conceptually novel and effectively bridges the gap between batch and online learning.  
- *Quality:* The algorithm is technically sound, with rigorous derivation of online sufficient statistics (Eqs. 1–3) and a clear convergence/diligence trade-off analysis (Section 3.1). Simulations (Section 4) convincingly demonstrate superior performance over batch learning (PC) during structural changes (e.g., 250 datapoints to stabilize post-changepoint), with minimal cost in stable regimes.  
- *Clarity:* The exposition is exceptionally clear, with well-structured pseudocode and intuitive explanations (e.g., Mahalanobis distance as a change signal). The real-world application to US price index volatility (Section 5) effectively validates the approach.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- The claim that "any standard structure learning algorithm would perform similarly to PC" (Section 4) is overgeneralized. While PC was used as the baseline, score-based methods (e.g., [3]) may not decompose cleanly for online learning, and the paper should clarify this limitation.  
- The scope is slightly ambiguous: The focus on SEMs (Section 3) is well-justified, but the discussion of Bayesian network extensions (Section 6) could be sharpened upfront to emphasize that the core contribution applies *specifically* to SEMs, with broader applicability as future work.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper makes a significant, well-executed contribution to online graphical model learning. The technical rigor, clear presentation, and compelling empirical results justify acceptance. Addressing the two minor scope clarifications would strengthen the contribution’s precision. The work aligns strongly with NIPS’s emphasis on methodological innovation for real-world data challenges.
