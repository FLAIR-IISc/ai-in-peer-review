

This paper introduces LoSST, a novel algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical structures in data streams where the underlying generative model is only locally stationary rather than globally stationary. The authors address an important gap in graphical model structure learning, as most existing methods assume a fixed structure over time and process data in batch mode. The paper makes a clear contribution by developing a heuristic online algorithm that can detect structural changes and adapt to new models in real-time while maintaining comparable performance to batch methods when structures are stable.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough literature review that carefully distinguishes the authors' approach from related work on online learning, changepoint detection, and time-varying graphical models. The authors effectively demonstrate that their approach fills a gap in the literature by providing the first algorithm capable of general graphical model structure learning in an online setting. The technical development of the algorithm is sound, with clear descriptions of how sufficient statistics are tracked online and how the algorithm balances between convergence and diligence through its parameterization.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors conduct three sets of simulations comparing LoSST to the PC algorithm (used as a batch-mode baseline) under different scenarios: when both graph structure and parameters change, when only parameters change, and when using probabilistic relearning. The results consistently show that LoSST performs as well as batch methods during stable periods and significantly outperforms them after structural changes occur. The real-world application to US price index volatility provides a compelling case study demonstrating the algorithm's practical utility.

The paper's main strengths are its clear problem formulation, thoughtful algorithm design, and thorough empirical evaluation. The authors successfully demonstrate that their method can track structural changes in real-time while maintaining good performance in stable environments. The connection between Mahalanobis distances, pooled p-values, and the weighting scheme for data points is particularly elegant.

However, the paper contains two significant weaknesses that need addressing. First, the authors make an unsubstantiated claim that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly" to the PC algorithm in their experiments. This is a strong assertion that lacks justification. Different structure learning algorithms have different properties (e.g., score-based vs. constraint-based methods) that could interact differently with the online framework. The authors should either provide evidence supporting this claim or rephrase it to clarify that their experiments specifically evaluate the PC algorithm, with a more cautious statement about potential generalizability.

Second, the paper's scope is somewhat unclear. While the authors briefly acknowledge that extending their method to Bayesian networks would be challenging due to difficulties in maintaining sufficient statistics, this limitation is not emphasized early enough. The paper focuses primarily on SEMs but implies broader applicability. To strengthen the paper, the authors should clarify that their method is specifically designed for SEMs (with continuous variables and linear relationships) and explicitly state that extensions to other graphical model types require additional work. This would make the paper's contribution more precise and avoid potentially misleading readers about the algorithm's generalizability.

The discussion of convergence versus diligence is insightful and well-argued, though the mathematical details might be challenging for some readers. The authors' exploration of the trade-offs between these two desirable properties provides valuable theoretical grounding for their algorithm.

The real-world application to US price index volatility is compelling, though the interpretation of the detected structural changes could be strengthened with additional economic context. The authors correctly note that the algorithm correctly identifies the "Great Moderation" period but could provide more detail on how the detected structural changes align with known economic events.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the literature on graphical model structure learning. The technical approach is sound, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive, and the problem addressed is important. With minor revisions to clarify the scope and temper the generalization claims, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important gap in the literature (online structure learning for time-varying models)
- Clear, well-organized presentation with thorough literature review
- Sound technical development of the algorithm
- Comprehensive and well-designed empirical evaluation
- Compelling real-world application

Weaknesses:
- Overstated claim about generalizability to other structure learning algorithms
- Scope not clearly defined early in the paper (focus on SEMs vs. broader applicability)
- Limited economic interpretation of the real-world results

Overall, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the scope and generalization claims. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical model structure learning, particularly for applications involving time-varying structures, and the proposed algorithm is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated.
