This paper presents LoSST, a novel algorithm for learning the structure of Structural Equation Models (SEMs) from data that is only locally i.i.d. The core idea is to address non-stationary environments where the underlying data-generating model may change over time by tracking sufficient statistics with a dynamic weighting scheme. The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and addresses an important and often-neglected problem in the structure learning literature. The proposed techniques for tracking changes are simple yet appear effective, as demonstrated through experiments comparing an adapted PC algorithm to its standard batch-mode counterpart.

While the contribution is valuable, two suggestions could help clarify and strengthen the paper.

First, the experimental validation could be framed more precisely. The authors claim that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly" to the PC algorithm used in the simulations. This assertion may be too strong, as different structure learning methods can exhibit considerable differences in performance and behavior. The paper would be improved by either rephrasing this claim to clearly state that the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed online adaptation for the PC algorithm (while perhaps speculating that similar benefits could extend to other methods), or by providing a more detailed justification for why these results are expected to generalize across different classes of learning algorithms.

Second, a minor adjustment could sharpen the paper's scope. The discussion section rightly points out that extending these ideas to other model classes, such as Bayesian networks over discrete variables, is non-trivial due to the difficulty of efficiently tracking the necessary sufficient statistics. While this is not central to the paper's main contribution, acknowledging this limitation earlier would be beneficial. By presenting the work more explicitly as a solution tailored for SEMs from the outset—while noting that extensions to other models are possible but not necessarily straightforward—the paper could better manage reader expectations about the method's generality.

In summary, this is a well-written paper on an important topic. The proposed formulation is clear and appears straightforward to implement, and the experimental results are promising, making it a valuable contribution to learning graphical models in dynamic environments.
