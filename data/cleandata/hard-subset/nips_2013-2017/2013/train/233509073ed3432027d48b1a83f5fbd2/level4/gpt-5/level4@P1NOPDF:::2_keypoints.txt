Addresses detecting changes in dependency structure in data streams and proposes LoSST for efficient structure change detection.  
LoSST integrates batch PC structure learning with streaming statistics: deviations from weighted means, a weighted correlation matrix, and an effective sample size.  
Incoming points are weighted by "surprise"; Mahalanobis distances yield p-values via Hotelling’s T2, combined with Liptak pooling into a rho significance, and a noisy-OR with the prior step decides relearning.  
Evaluated against vanilla batch PC on synthetic parameter/structure-change datasets (varying variables and max degree) and on a 6-variable US price index real dataset, detecting known economic changes.  
Pros: tackles an underexplored problem, presents a novel/practical/efficient algorithm, provides adequate experiments including real data, offers a solid prior-work review, and is clearly written.  
Main con: lacks theoretical guarantees or convergence analysis under reasonable assumptions; this should be explicitly stated as future work.  
Relies heavily on heuristics with design choices (reweighting, effective sample size, relearning criteria) that need clearer justification and exposition.  
Uses Hotelling’s T2 and Liptak pooling outside their typical assumptions (partially reweighted counts, dependent tests), so the validity of p-values/significance should be acknowledged and discussed.  
Move proofs and extended technical details from footnotes into the supplementary material.  
Figures are too small, have excessive whitespace, missing labels, and weak captions/legends; some experiment descriptions (e.g., top paragraph p.7) are unclear.  
The explanation for worse LoSST performance on edge addition when only parameters change is not convincing.  
Recommend decoupling parameter updates from structure updates for better clarity and possibly improved performance.  
Overall: a genuinely interesting and practical first attempt on a promising research direction despite its heuristic nature.
