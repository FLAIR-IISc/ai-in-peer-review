This manuscript presents a novel and systematic approach to structure learning for Structural Equation Models (SEMs) in non-stationary environments, specifically addressing scenarios where data are only locally i.i.d. – meaning the generative model may change over time. The paper is well-written and clearly explains a significant problem often overlooked by standard structure learning algorithms: adapting to time-dependent data. The proposed techniques for extending learning to these dynamic scenarios are both conceptually simple and, according to the experimental results using the PC algorithm, demonstrably effective.

The paper effectively situates its contribution within the existing literature on graphical model structure learning, contrasting it with both Bayesian/score-based and constraint-based methods that assume data stationarity. The discussion of Temporal Difference Learning (TDL) and online changepoint detection (CPD) algorithms provides valuable context, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches when applied to general graphical model structure learning. The algorithm, LoSST, is presented with sufficient detail to allow for implementation and understanding.

There are two suggestions that could refine the paper's contribution and clarity. First, the statement in the experimental section that “any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly” feels overly strong. The performance of structure learning algorithms can vary considerably based on underlying assumptions and computational strategies. It would be more accurate to rephrase this, clarifying that the experiments focus specifically on the PC method and its adaptation to time-dependent data – a valuable contribution in itself – and then speculating on the likely outcomes with other methods, justifying that speculation with relevant algorithmic comparisons. Alternatively, retaining the assertion would require a more detailed explanation of why similar performance is expected across different algorithms.

Second, the discussion in the conclusions regarding the challenges of applying the same ideas to Bayesian network structure learning is insightful. The difficulty in efficiently tracking sufficient statistics for discrete variables is a valid concern. While not central to the paper's primary contribution, acknowledging this complexity from the outset would sharpen the paper’s scope. Presenting the work more directly as focusing on SEMs, while still acknowledging the potential (though not necessarily straightforward) extension to other models, would strengthen the overall narrative.

In summary, this is a well-written and technically sound paper addressing an important problem in graphical model learning. The proposed algorithm, LoSST, offers a practical and effective solution for learning graph structure when data are only locally i.i.d. The formulation is readily understandable and implementable, and the experimental results, while focused on the PC method, are promising.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Addresses a previously under-explored problem in graphical model learning – structure learning in non-stationary environments.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The LoSST algorithm is presented with clear theoretical justification and practical implementation details.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Simulation results demonstrate the effectiveness of LoSST compared to a standard batch-mode algorithm in dynamic settings.
*   **Significance:** The ability to learn from time-varying data has broad applicability in real-world scenarios where underlying relationships change over time.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Scope:** The paper could benefit from a tighter focus on its contributions to SEMs, acknowledging the challenges of extending the approach to other graphical model types (e.g., Bayesian networks) earlier in the manuscript.
*   **Generalizability:** The experimental evaluation is currently limited to the PC algorithm; further investigation with other structure learning algorithms would strengthen the conclusions.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper. While the suggestions for improvement are valid, they do not detract from the significant contribution of this work. The paper presents a novel, technically sound, and potentially impactful approach to a challenging problem in graphical model learning.
