- Method (LoSST) is technically sound and evaluated on simulated and real data.  
- Related work discussion is potentially misleading: score-based BN algorithms don't require a single generating model; they assume factorized scoring and often exchangeability—authors should clarify.  
- Claiming LoSST is the first to handle varying generating structures is inaccurate; Siracusa and Fisher III (AISTATS 2009) allows varying structure within a set.  
- Core learning objective is unclear: is the goal change-point detection, inferring the current generating structure, or something else?  
- Unclear whether the contribution is a new model (assumptions/goals), a new algorithm (computational method), or both.  
- Some equations (e.g., ρ_{r+1}) and notation are introduced without definition; punctuation for displayed equations is inconsistent.  
- Presentation could be tightened—dense sections (e.g., p-value construction) obscure potential novelty.  
- Figures 1–4 have unreadably small font and lack color/legend explanations; readability must improve.  
- Statement in Section 6 about trading improved tracking for extra computation is context-dependent and needs qualification.  
- Originality: the combination of techniques is novel but not groundbreaking; p-value construction might be the main novel element but is not clearly presented.  
- Significance: work is a useful stepping stone towards more principled (e.g., Bayesian) methods rather than a major advance.  
- Pros for acceptance: addresses a practical non-stationary online learning problem, shows promising simulation results (outperforms batch after change), and includes a real economic time-series case study.  
- Cons for acceptance: incremental originality, unclear presentation/definitions, misleading related-work claims/overlooked prior work, and a heuristic approach that may be outperformed by more principled methods.
