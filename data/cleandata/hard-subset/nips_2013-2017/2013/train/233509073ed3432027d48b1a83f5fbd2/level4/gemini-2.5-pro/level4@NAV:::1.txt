I must dissent on point (3). The AISTATS 2009 paper by Siracusa III and Fisher does not presuppose iid data and explicitly allows the "generating structure" to change, albeit within a constrained set of graphical models. While the current method is arguably more flexible, claiming it is the first to address this problem is misleading.
*
This paper proposes an approach based on hypothesis testing for the sequential learning of graphical models from time series data. The methodology is specifically designed for scenarios where the underlying data-generating graphical model may change with relative frequency. The authors validate the method's performance through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

Quality
The presented results appear to be sound. However, the characterization of prior work in Section 2 is potentially misleading. Standard score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks do not require the data to originate from a single generating model; rather, their core assumption is that the scoring function can be decomposed into local terms. While it is common for the models themselves to assume exchangeability (or iid data), this is a property of the model, not an inherent limitation of the learning algorithm.

Clarity
The paper's presentation is generally adequate, but there are opportunities for enhancement. Critically, the precise learning objective remains ambiguous: is the goal to infer change points (under a formal set of assumptions), to determine the generating structure of the most recent data point, or something else? It is also difficult to discern whether the primary contribution is a novel probabilistic model, a new computational algorithm, or both.

The paragraph at the end of page 3 is challenging to parse; for example, it is unclear if the equation for ρ_r+1 serves as its definition or if it has been defined previously. I also note the absence of periods at the end of sentences concluding with a displayed equation. A significant practical issue is the font size in Figures 1-4, which would benefit from being doubled for legibility. Furthermore, adding legends to describe the color-coding in Figures 1 and 2 would be helpful. In Section 6, the argument that improved tracking can justify increased computational expense seems questionable, as this trade-off is highly dependent on the specific valuation of accuracy versus cost in a given application domain.

Originality
While the work appears to be original, it is not immediately clear what the central innovative idea is. A potential source of novelty might lie in the construction of the p-values, though I was unable to fully grasp the details of this component.

Significance
The main significance of this work, if published, may be to stimulate the creation of more advanced, likely Bayesian, methods to address this problem. This contribution feels more akin to engineering development than fundamental research. The paper proposes a heuristic-style solution for a learning problem that is not rigorously defined and for which solutions might already be achievable using existing machine learning frameworks.
