This paper addresses the important and under-explored problem of learning graphical model structure from non-stationary data, where the underlying generative process can change over time. Most existing structure learning algorithms assume data is globally stationary. To overcome this limitation, the authors propose a novel, online heuristic algorithm named Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST). LoSST operates on a stream of data from a Structural Equation Model (SEM) and works by tracking sufficient statistics (weighted mean, covariance, and an "effective" sample size) in an online manner. To detect changes, the algorithm weights incoming data points based on their "surprise," which is quantified by the Mahalanobis distance from the current model. The p-value of this distance, estimated using Hotelling's T^2 distribution, is pooled over recent data points using Liptak's method to produce a statistic, ρ, that indicates the goodness-of-fit. When ρ suggests a poor fit, the algorithm probabilistically triggers a relearning of the graphical structure using a standard batch algorithm (in this case, the PC algorithm). The authors provide an analysis of the trade-off between convergence to a stable structure and diligence in detecting changes. The algorithm's performance is evaluated on synthetic data, comparing it to a batch-mode PC algorithm in scenarios involving both structural and parametric shifts. Results show that LoSST performs comparably to the batch algorithm in stable environments and significantly better after a change point. A case study on U.S. price index data demonstrates the method's ability to detect known macroeconomic shifts.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem. The assumption of stationarity is often violated in real-world data streams, and developing methods that can adapt to such changes is a significant contribution. The LoSST algorithm is a novel approach that cleverly integrates several existing statistical techniques into a practical framework for online structure learning.
*   **Methodological Contribution:** The proposed algorithm is computationally efficient and well-suited for online settings. The design, which uses weighted sufficient statistics and a principled (though heuristic) mechanism for triggering updates, is creative and provides a complete, end-to-end solution to the problem. The discussion of the diligence vs. convergence trade-off is insightful.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is thorough. The synthetic experiments are well-designed to test the algorithm's performance against a standard baseline under various conditions (different graph sizes, degrees, and types of change). The inclusion of a real-world application on economic time-series data compellingly demonstrates the potential utility of the approach.
*   **Clarity and Context:** The paper is generally well-written and provides a good overview of related work, effectively situating its contribution within the literature on structure learning and online learning.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** While the algorithm is intuitively appealing and performs well empirically, its primary weakness is the lack of formal theoretical guarantees. The discussion on convergence and diligence is valuable, but it would significantly strengthen the paper to provide a more rigorous analysis of the algorithm's properties, even under simplified assumptions. At minimum, this limitation should be more explicitly discussed as a primary direction for future work.
*   **Heuristic Nature of Statistical Methods:** The LoSST algorithm is largely heuristic, and the paper could be more transparent about the potential violation of assumptions for the statistical tools it employs. For instance:
    *   Hotelling's T^2 distribution is used with an "effective sample size" S_r, which is derived from weighted data. The validity of the distribution under this weighting scheme is not established. The authors should either justify this choice or clearly state it is an approximation.
    *   Liptak's method for pooling p-values typically assumes independent tests. It is unclear whether the sequentially generated p-values in this framework meet this assumption.
    Acknowledging that these are well-motivated heuristics and discussing the potential implications would improve the paper's scientific rigor.
*   **Presentation and Clarity:** The presentation could be improved in several areas to aid reader understanding and reproducibility.
    *   The use of footnotes for proof sketches is unconventional; moving these to a supplementary document would allow for a more complete and appropriate presentation.
    *   The figures are too small, making them difficult to read. Larger figures with more detailed captions and clearer legends would be beneficial.
    *   The description of the experimental setup could be more detailed to ensure the work is reproducible.
*   **Analysis of Results:** The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on edge removal errors immediately following a parameter-only change is not fully convincing. A more detailed analysis of this phenomenon—perhaps exploring the interplay between the shrinking effective sample size and the nature of constraint-based tests—would be valuable.
*   **Coupling of Updates:** The current algorithm triggers a full structure relearning for any detected change. It seems plausible that some changes might be purely parametric. Decoupling parameter updates from structural updates could lead to a more robust and efficient system. This could be mentioned as an interesting avenue for future research.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and practical algorithm for an important and challenging problem in machine learning. While the approach is heuristic and lacks theoretical backing, it represents a valuable contribution that performs well empirically and is likely to inspire significant follow-up research.
