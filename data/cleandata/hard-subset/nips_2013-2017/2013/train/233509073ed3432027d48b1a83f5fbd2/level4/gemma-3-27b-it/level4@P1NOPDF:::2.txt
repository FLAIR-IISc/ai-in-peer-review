Paper Summary:
The paper tackles an interesting and underexplored problem: tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a stream of data (e.g., time-based). The authors focus on an efficient (computationally and memory-wise) solution for simpler Structural Equation Models (SEMs). They develop a new algorithm for this task, named LoSST. LoSST utilizes a standard batch learning algorithm for underlying structure learning (PC by Spirtes et al., 2000) and tracks several statistics from the data stream to decide when to initiate new structure learning. These tracked statistics include the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix (eq. 1, 2), and the weighted sample size. The sample weighting scheme assigns weights to each new data point based on how “surprising” it is (Eq. 3, 4). Each point’s Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed, and estimated p-values for the MDs, using Hotelling's T<sup>2</sup> distribution, are combined using Liptak's weighted pooling of p-values to compute an overall “significance” statistic, ρ. This statistic is then used in a noisy OR configuration with the previous step to determine whether to begin a new structure learning phase. The authors compare LoSST to vanilla PC batch learning on synthetic datasets where either a change in parameters or a change in structure is introduced after a fixed number of data points, testing for different numbers of variables and maximum degree configurations. Finally, they perform a short analysis of real-world data from the US price index, consisting of 529 data points (monthly, starting in 1967) and six variables. They demonstrate that the algorithm detects some known changes/trends in the US economy while ignoring short-lived fluctuations.

Pros:
1. The paper addresses an interesting and underexplored problem. As the authors point out, there is ample room for further development of practical solutions for these and other models, as well as aligning theory and theoretical guarantees.
2. The algorithm is novel and practical/efficient, creatively leveraging a range of different methods.
3. The experiments appear adequate, tracking the effects of various parameters and including an interesting real-world dataset.
4. The authors provide a good review of previous work on this problem.
5. The paper is generally well-written.

Cons:
1. The most significant shortcoming of the paper, given the interesting problem it addresses, is the lack of theoretical analysis or guarantees under any assumptions. Convergence and diligence appear as opposing forces, as the authors note (p. 4), but some form of guarantees under simplifying conditions seems plausible. At the very least, this should be mentioned as a direction for future work.
2. The solution proposed by the authors, LoSST, relies heavily on heuristics. While this enables a practical solution, the decisions involved should be more clearly articulated. Tracking the mean and correlation matrix is relatively straightforward; the key decisions relate to reweighing, effective sample size, and when/how to re-learn. The authors creatively apply several methods under conditions that likely invalidate their original guarantees. For example, they estimate the MD p-value using Hotelling's T<sup>2</sup> distribution, setting the sample size *N* to the effective sample size *S<sup>r</sup>*. However, that distribution was developed for complete counts from a given distribution, not for partial or re-weighted counts. Would the distribution still hold this form if samples were actually drawn from these networks/structures with these partial weights? Similarly, Liptak's weighted p-values were developed for meta-analysis using independent tests with different sample sizes. At a minimum, if methods are used outside their guaranteed context, the authors should explicitly state this, preventing potential misinterpretation.

Other comments:
- The authors make creative use of footnotes to outline proofs, possibly exceeding the intended scope of Leslie Lamport or the conference organizers. Consider using the supplementary material for this and other missing details.
- Figures are too small, with unnecessary space between them, missing labels, and insufficient detail in the captions and legends. The description of the experiments is not clear enough (e.g., the top paragraph on p. 7 is cryptic).
- The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on edge addition when only the parameters are changed is unconvincing.
- Decoupling parameter updates and structure updates appears desirable.
- This is a really interesting research direction, with a practical, though heuristic, implementation representing a good first step.
