

This manuscript addresses an important gap in structure learning for Structural Equation Models (SEMs) by proposing a method that accommodates data generated from locally i.i.d. processes—where the underlying model may change over time. The problem is well-motivated, as most standard structure-learning algorithms neglect time-dependence. The approach is clearly presented, technically sound, and experimentally validated using the PC algorithm, demonstrating that the proposed adaptation effectively handles time-dependent data. The core idea is both simple and promising, with results that appear robust for the PC method.

Two minor points warrant clarification to strengthen the paper’s contributions. First, the claim that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly" is overly strong. The performance differences between structure-learning methods (e.g., PC vs. GES vs. MMPC) can be substantial, particularly under time-dependence. The authors should either rephrase to clarify that the experiments specifically compare the PC method (time-independent vs. adapted time-dependent versions) and note that similar results *may* extend to other methods, or provide a detailed justification for why this generalization holds. As stated, the assertion lacks sufficient support.

Second, the paper’s conclusion notes that extending the approach to Bayesian networks (BNs) may be non-trivial due to challenges in preserving sufficient statistics. While this limitation is not central to the SEM-focused contribution, it subtly undermines the paper’s broader implications. To sharpen the scope, the authors should explicitly frame the work as targeting SEMs (not general graphical models) from the outset, while still acknowledging that extensions to BNs or other models could be possible—though not straightforward. This would prevent potential overstatement of the method’s generality and align the paper’s claims with its actual focus.

Overall, this is a well-written, technically solid paper that makes a meaningful contribution to learning SEM structures from non-stationary data. The experiments are compelling, the methodology is accessible, and the problem is clearly articulated. With minor refinements to the scope and the overgeneralized claim, the paper would be significantly stronger. The authors’ response to prior feedback appears adequate, and the revisions requested are focused and reasonable.
