- Disagreement with authors: Siracusa & Fisher III (AISTATS 2009) did not assume i.i.d. data and allowed varying generating structure, so claiming novelty as the first to address the problem is misleading.  
- Paper proposes LoSST: a single-pass, online heuristic to track time-varying graphical structure under local stationarity using weighted sufficient statistics and pooled p-values.  
- LoSST updates weights via Mahalanobis-distance-based fit signals and triggers probabilistic structure relearning using the PC algorithm.  
- Positioning: contrasts with standard single-model structure learners, online changepoint detection (which assumes abrupt changes), and batch time-varying structure methods.  
- Main contribution: practical mechanism combining pooled p-values, adaptive weighting, and probabilistic relearning for online updates without maintaining full model distributions.  
- Methodology: technically sound; update mechanism and link to Mahalanobis distances are reasonable and simulations support claims.  
- Related-work discussion is misleading: typical score-based learners’ limitations arise from modeling assumptions (exchangeability/i.i.d.) not the search machinery.  
- Experiments: sensible setup comparing to batch PC, include structure/parameter-change scenarios and a real CPI case study; interpretations of results are useful.  
- Clarity: primary learning objective is insufficiently specified—unclear if task is changepoint detection, current-structure estimation, or both.  
- Clarity: unclear whether contribution is a new formal model or an algorithmic heuristic for an existing objective; reads like a heuristic on standard assumptions.  
- Notation/definition issues: some definitions (e.g., ρr+1) and equation punctuation are unclear and should be clarified where first introduced.  
- Figures: small font sizes and missing legends/color descriptions hinder readability.  
- Section 6: claims that improved tracking offsets computational cost need a more nuanced, domain-aware discussion of trade-offs.  
- Originality: combination of techniques appears novel in form but is incremental and heuristic rather than conceptually groundbreaking.  
- Significance: addresses an important practical gap and is useful in practice, but vague problem formulation and heuristic nature limit theoretical impact.  
- Pros for acceptance: tackles an under-explored practical problem, offers a simple implementable algorithm, and presents reasonable empirical performance.  
- Cons for acceptance: overstates novelty relative to prior work, unclear objectives/assumptions, largely heuristic with limited theory, clarity and presentation issues, and under-justified cost–benefit claims.
