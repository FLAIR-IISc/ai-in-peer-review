Paper Summary:
This paper addresses an interesting and underexplored problem: detecting changes in the dependency structure among variables in a data stream. The authors focus on an efficient solution for SEMs and propose a new algorithm, LoSST. LoSST relies on a standard batch structure learner (the PC algorithm; Spirtes et al., 2000) and maintains streaming statistics to decide when to trigger relearning. Tracked statistics include deviations from the weighted mean for each variable, the correlation matrix (Eqs. 1–2), and the weighted sample size. New data points are reweighted based on how “surprising” they are (Eqs. 3–4): each point’s Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed, MD p-values are estimated using Hotelling’s T2 distribution, and these p-values are combined via Lipták’s weighted pooling to yield an overall significance score ρ. This score is then combined via a noisy-OR with the previous step’s signal to determine whether to relearn the structure.

The authors compare LoSST against vanilla batch PC on synthetic datasets where either parameters or structure change after a fixed number of observations, varying the number of variables and maximum degree. They also analyze a real dataset of US price indices comprising 529 monthly observations (starting in 1967) with six variables, showing that the algorithm detects known economic shifts while ignoring short-lived fluctuations.

Pros:
1. The paper tackles a timely and underexplored problem. As noted, there is ample room for practical advances and theoretical development.
2. The algorithm is novel, practical, and efficient, creatively integrating multiple ideas.
3. The experimental evaluation is generally adequate, exploring parameter effects and including a real-world dataset.
4. The related work section is thorough and well-situated.
5. Overall, the paper is clearly written.

Cons:
1. The main weakness is the lack of theory or guarantees under reasonable assumptions. While there is a trade-off between convergence and responsiveness (as the authors note), some form of guarantee under simplifying conditions seems plausible; at minimum, this should be identified as future work.
2. LoSST relies heavily on heuristics. This is acceptable for a practical first attempt, but the design decisions should be stated more transparently. Tracking means and correlations is straightforward; the critical choices concern reweighting, effective sample size, and relearning triggers. Several tools are applied outside their usual assumptions. For example, MD p-values are estimated via Hotelling’s T2 with N set to the effective sample size S^r, but T2 is derived for i.i.d. complete samples, not reweighted or partially weighted counts; it is unclear that the resulting distribution remains valid under this weighting. Similarly, Lipták’s method assumes independent tests with varying sample sizes; independence and the sampling model may be violated here. If these methods are used beyond their standard regimes, the paper should state this explicitly to avoid giving readers a misleading sense of validity.

Other comments:
- Footnotes are used to sketch proofs beyond typical use; these details should be moved to the supplement.
- Figures are too small, with excessive whitespace, missing labels/legends, and sparse captions. The experimental description is sometimes unclear (e.g., the top paragraph of p. 7).
- The explanation for LoSST’s poorer performance on edge addition when only parameters change is unconvincing.
- It seems desirable to decouple parameter updates from structure updates.

Overall, this is a promising direction with a practical, albeit heuristic, first implementation.
