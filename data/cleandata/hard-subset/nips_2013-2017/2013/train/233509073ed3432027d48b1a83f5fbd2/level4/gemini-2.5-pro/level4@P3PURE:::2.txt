**Paper Summary**

The paper addresses the interesting and under-explored problem of tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure of variables from a stream of data points (e.g., a time series). The authors focus on an efficient (in terms of computation and memory) solution for Structural Equation Models (SEMs). They develop a new algorithm for this task, named LoSST, which uses a standard batch learning algorithm (the PC algorithm by Spirtes et al., 2000) for the underlying structure learning. LoSST tracks several statistics from the data stream to decide when to initiate a new structure learning phase. The tracked statistics include the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix (Eqs. 1, 2), and the weighted sample size.

The algorithm employs a weighting scheme where the weight of each new data point is determined by how "surprising" it is (Eqs. 3, 4). The Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed for each point, and the corresponding p-value is estimated using a Hotelling's T-squared distribution. These p-values are then combined using Liptak's weighted pooling method to compute an overall significance statistic, ρ. This statistic is then used in a noisy-OR configuration with its value from the previous step to decide whether to initiate a new structure learning process.

The authors compare LoSST to a standard batch PC learning algorithm on synthetic datasets. These experiments involve introducing either a parameter change or a structural change after a fixed number of data points, and they test various configurations of variable counts and maximum degrees. Finally, they present an analysis of a real-world dataset from the US price index, comprising 529 monthly data points (starting from 1967) across six variables. They demonstrate that the algorithm detects several known changes and trends in the US economy while ignoring short-lived fluctuations.

**Pros**

1.  The paper addresses an important and under-explored problem. As the authors point out, there is significant room for developing practical solutions for these and other models, as well as for establishing corresponding theory and theoretical guarantees.
2.  The proposed algorithm is novel, practical, and efficient. It creatively combines a variety of methods to achieve its goal.
3.  The experiments appear adequate, exploring the effects of various parameters and including an interesting analysis of a real-world dataset.
4.  The authors provide a thorough review of previous work on this problem.
5.  The paper is generally well-written.

**Cons**

1.  A major weakness of the paper is the lack of a theoretical analysis or any performance guarantees, even under simplifying assumptions. While the authors correctly note the trade-off between convergence and diligence (p. 4), it seems plausible that some form of guarantees could be derived under simplified conditions. This should, at a minimum, be mentioned as a direction for future work.
2.  The proposed solution, LoSST, relies heavily on heuristics. While this approach leads to a practical solution, the heuristic design choices should be more explicitly acknowledged and justified. The core heuristic elements relate to the re-weighting scheme, the calculation of the effective sample size, and the criteria for triggering a relearning phase.
3.  The authors apply several statistical methods in contexts that may violate the assumptions under which their theoretical guarantees were derived. When using methods outside of their original context, the authors should explicitly state this to avoid misleading readers. For example:
    *   The p-value of the Mahalanobis distance is estimated using a Hotelling's T-squared distribution, where the sample size N is set to the effective sample size S^r. However, this distribution is traditionally defined for integer sample counts from a specific distribution, not for re-weighted, non-integer effective sample sizes. It is unclear whether data sampled according to these partial weights would still follow the assumed distribution.
    *   Similarly, Liptak's method for pooling p-values was developed for meta-analysis of independent tests, an assumption which may not hold in this streaming context.

**Other Comments**

*   The authors place proof outlines in footnotes, which seems to be an unconventional use of this feature. These details, along with other omitted information, would be better placed in supplementary material.
*   The figures are too small and poorly formatted. There is excessive white space between them, and they are missing axis labels, detailed captions, and clear legends.
*   The description of the experiments is not sufficiently clear. For example, the first paragraph on page 7 is difficult to understand.
*   The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on the edge addition metric when only parameters have changed is unconvincing.
*   Related to the previous point, it seems that decoupling the triggers for parameter updates and structure updates would be a desirable feature.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting direction for future research and offers a practical, albeit heuristic, first implementation.
