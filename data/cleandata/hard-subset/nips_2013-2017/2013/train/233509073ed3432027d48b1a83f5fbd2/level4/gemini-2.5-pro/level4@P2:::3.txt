### **Summary**

This paper introduces LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a novel heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from data that is only locally stationary. The key problem addressed is that most existing structure learning algorithms assume a globally stationary data-generating process, which is often not the case in real-world scenarios where the underlying causal structure or parameters can change over time. The proposed method tackles this by tracking sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and effective sample size for Structural Equation Models) in an online manner. It adaptively weights new data points based on their fit to the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance and a pooled p-value. This dynamic weighting allows the algorithm to rapidly adapt to changes. The authors provide simulation results showing that LoSST performs comparably to a standard batch-mode algorithm (the PC algorithm) in stationary environments and significantly outperforms it when the underlying structure or parameters change.

### **Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses an important and practical limitation of most graphical model structure learning algorithms. The problem of learning from non-stationary data is highly relevant for many real-world applications, and this work presents a novel and coherent approach to solving it.
2.  **Clarity and Quality of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The LoSST algorithm is presented logically, from the online updates of sufficient statistics to the mechanism for triggering model relearning. The distinction between convergence and diligence is insightful and adds theoretical depth to the discussion of the algorithm's properties.
3.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is elegant and technically sound. The use of weighted sufficient statistics, driven by a principled measure of data fit (Mahalanobis distance), is an effective way to balance stability with adaptability. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the method's ability to track changes and its clear advantage over a batch-mode approach in non-stationary settings. Importantly, the experiments also show that this capability comes at little to no performance cost in a stable environment.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Overstated Generalization of Experimental Results:** The authors state in the experiments section that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly." This is a very strong and unsubstantiated claim. Different structure learning algorithms (e.g., score-based vs. constraint-based, or different algorithms within each class) can have considerably different performance characteristics, sensitivities to sample size, and error profiles. The paper would be more rigorous if this assertion were either rephrased as a well-reasoned conjecture or removed. The current framing could be seen as over-generalizing from experiments based solely on the PC algorithm. The core contribution—the online wrapper for tracking statistics—is strong enough on its own, and its value could be framed as a successful demonstration with PC, with the potential for extension to other methods.
2.  **Lack of Clarity on Scope:** The paper focuses exclusively on Structural Equation Models (SEMs) over continuous variables, where tracking the covariance matrix is sufficient. However, the discussion section reveals that extending this approach to other popular models, such as Bayesian networks over discrete variables, is a non-trivial challenge due to the difficulty of tractably tracking their sufficient statistics online. This is a significant limitation on the method's generalizability. The paper would benefit from being more upfront about its scope. Stating clearly in the introduction or methodology that the proposed method is specifically designed for SEMs (or models where covariance is sufficient) would provide a sharper focus and manage reader expectations. The potential for extension to other models could still be discussed as an avenue for future work, but the challenges should be acknowledged earlier.

### **Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a simple, effective, and well-motivated solution to the important problem of structure learning from non-stationary data. The formulation is clear, the experiments are promising, and the work represents a solid contribution to the field. The main weaknesses relate to the framing of the contributions rather than fundamental flaws in the methodology. By clarifying the scope of the method and tempering the claims about its applicability to all structure learning algorithms, the authors could further strengthen an already compelling paper. I recommend acceptance.
