This paper introduces LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm for learning graphical model structure in non-stationary environments, where the data-generating process is assumed to be only locally independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.). The core contribution is an online method for tracking sufficient statistics—specifically, the means and covariance matrix for Structural Equation Models (SEMs)—by dynamically weighting incoming data. The weighting scheme is based on the fit of new data points to the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. This allows the algorithm to detect and adapt to changes in the underlying graph structure or its parameters. This work effectively addresses a significant gap between traditional structure learning algorithms, which presuppose globally stationary data, and online learning or changepoint detection methods, which generally do not perform full graphical model structure learning.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem. Many real-world systems are dynamic, and the assumption of stationary data required by most structure learning algorithms is often violated. This work provides a principled approach to address this limitation.
*   **Quality and Originality:** The proposed LoSST algorithm is an elegant and well-motivated solution. The method for updating statistics based on a pooled p-value of Mahalanobis distances is clever and appears effective. The theoretical discussion of the trade-off between "convergence" in stable environments and "diligence" in changing ones is insightful and adds considerable depth to the paper.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The algorithm, its motivations, and its parameters are explained in sufficient detail for understanding and potential replication.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The simulation results are compelling and effectively demonstrate the algorithm's capabilities. The experiments rightly show that LoSST performs comparably to a standard batch-mode algorithm (PC) in a stationary setting while significantly outperforming it when the data-generating model changes. The evaluation considers both structural and parametric shifts, which is a thorough approach.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is strong, but two points could be addressed to further enhance its contribution:

1.  The assertion that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly" to the PC algorithm used in the experiments is a very strong claim that is not substantiated. While the core of LoSST is to provide an adaptive estimate of sufficient statistics (the covariance matrix), different structure learning algorithms may have varying sensitivities to estimation errors. The authors should consider either softening this claim to a conjecture or providing a more detailed argument for why this generalization is expected to hold for other algorithms that rely on the same sufficient statistics.

2.  The discussion section correctly notes that extending this framework to other model types, like Bayesian networks with discrete variables, is a non-trivial challenge. Given that the proposed method is tightly integrated with the sufficient statistics of continuous SEMs, the paper's scope could be framed more explicitly from the beginning. Clarifying in the introduction that the primary focus and demonstrated success are for SEMs would help manage reader expectations about the method's immediate generalizability. This would make the paper's excellent contribution even sharper.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, well-explained, and empirically validated algorithm for an important problem in machine learning. The methodology is sound, and the results are promising. The minor weaknesses identified are primarily related to the framing of the claims and scope, which can be easily addressed in a final version.
