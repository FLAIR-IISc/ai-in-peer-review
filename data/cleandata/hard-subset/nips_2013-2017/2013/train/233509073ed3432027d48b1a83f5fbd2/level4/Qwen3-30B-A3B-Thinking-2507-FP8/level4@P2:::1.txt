

This paper proposes LoSST, a novel algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical model structure in real-time. The authors address a significant gap in the literature by focusing on locally stationary environments rather than the commonly assumed globally stationary settings. LoSST tracks locally stationary sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and effective sample size) online using a weighted approach based on Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values. The algorithm demonstrates comparable performance to batch-mode learning in stable environments and significantly better performance when structure changes occur, as shown through simulations and a real-world application to U.S. price index data.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-designed experiments that properly evaluate the algorithm's performance under different conditions. The theoretical justification for the weighted statistics and the convergence/diligence trade-off are well-developed. However, the related work section (Section 2) misrepresents standard score-based structure learning algorithms, incorrectly stating they assume data comes from a single generating model. While these algorithms do typically assume exchangeability (i.i.d. data), the scoring functions themselves factorize into local terms, which is a different concern. The authors should clarify this distinction. The real-world application to U.S. price index volatility is well-motivated and demonstrates the algorithm's practical utility.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, but several aspects require clarification. The core learning task is not precisely defined: is the goal to infer change points, the current generating structure, or both? This ambiguity affects the evaluation. The paragraph at the end of page 3 (defining ρr+1) is particularly difficult to follow, and the paper should explicitly state whether this definition is new or references prior work. Figure formatting needs improvement—font sizes in Figures 1-4 should be doubled for readability, and color descriptions should be included. The discussion in Section 6 about improved tracking compensating for computational cost is overly speculative without domain-specific valuation of accuracy versus cost.

Originality: The problem of tracking time-varying graphical structure is novel and addresses a real gap in the literature. However, the specific contributions lack clear innovation. The authors claim their method is the first to address this problem, but Siracusa and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) already considered a similar setting with time-indexed graph structures, though they required full datasets rather than online processing. The paper should acknowledge this work more fairly. The construction of the pooled p-value using Liptak's method is not sufficiently novel, and the algorithm's heuristic nature (e.g., the weight update rule) doesn't represent a significant theoretical advance.

Significance: The work addresses an important practical problem—real-world data often comes from non-stationary environments where batch learning is inappropriate. The algorithm's ability to operate in real-time with minimal overhead in stable environments makes it potentially valuable for applications like financial market analysis (as demonstrated with the U.S. price index data). However, the paper overstates the significance by suggesting this work "would quickly lead to the development of superior Bayesian methods." The approach is heuristic and doesn't fundamentally advance the theoretical understanding of graphical model learning. The real significance lies in its practical utility for specific applications where real-time structure tracking is required, rather than as a theoretical breakthrough.

The authors' response to my review question about Siracusa and Fisher is insufficient. While LoSST does allow for more flexible structure changes, Siracusa and Fisher's work does consider varying generating structures (though with a limited set of possible changes). Claiming LoSST is "the first" to address this problem is misleading.

Overall, the paper presents a useful algorithm for a practical problem, but the theoretical contribution is limited. The writing would benefit from greater precision in defining the learning task and clarifying the relationship to prior work. The experiments are well-designed and provide convincing evidence of the algorithm's utility in non-stationary settings. The paper would be strengthened by more careful distinction between the problem formulation and algorithmic contributions, and a more accurate assessment of related work.

I recommend acceptance, as the work addresses a meaningful problem with practical utility, though the theoretical novelty is limited. The authors should revise the related work section to properly acknowledge Siracusa and Fisher, clarify the learning task, and improve the presentation of technical details. These revisions would significantly strengthen an otherwise promising contribution.
