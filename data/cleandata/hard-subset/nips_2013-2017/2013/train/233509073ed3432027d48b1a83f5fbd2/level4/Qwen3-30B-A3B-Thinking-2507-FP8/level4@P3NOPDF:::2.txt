

**Review of "LoSST: Tracking Structural Changes in Streaming Data"**

This paper addresses a significant yet underexplored challenge: detecting shifts in dependency structures within time-series data streams. The authors propose LoSST, an efficient algorithm for tracking structural changes in semi-Markovian (SEM) models. LoSST leverages batch structure learning (via the PC algorithm) but adapts it to streaming data by monitoring key statistics: weighted variable means, correlation matrices (Eqs. 1–2), and an effective sample size. A novel weighting scheme (Eqs. 3–4) assigns higher weights to "surprising" data points based on Mahalanobis distance (MD), with p-values aggregated via Liptak’s weighted pooling to form a global significance statistic ρ. This ρ, combined with a noisy OR mechanism, triggers structure relearning. Experiments include synthetic data with controlled structural/parametric shifts (varying variables and maximum degree) and a real-world analysis of US price index data (529 monthly observations, 6 variables), demonstrating LoSST’s ability to identify meaningful economic trends while ignoring transient fluctuations.

**Strengths**  
1. **Problem significance**: The paper targets a critical gap in streaming data analysis, where structural changes are often overlooked despite their practical importance. The authors compellingly argue for the need for efficient, practical solutions and theoretical foundations.  
2. **Algorithmic novelty and efficiency**: LoSST’s design is pragmatic and resource-conscious. Its integration of weighted statistics, adaptive reweighting, and Liptak’s pooling demonstrates creative problem-solving, avoiding heavy computational overhead.  
3. **Empirical rigor**: Experiments systematically evaluate parameter sensitivity and include a meaningful real-world case study, effectively validating the approach’s utility.  
4. **Literature review**: The survey of prior work is thorough and well-contextualized, highlighting the paper’s contribution within the field.  
5. **Clarity**: The writing is generally accessible and well-organized, making complex ideas approachable.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of theoretical guarantees**: The most significant omission is the absence of theoretical analysis. The paper acknowledges the tension between model convergence and computational diligence (p. 4) but fails to provide even minimal guarantees under simplifying assumptions (e.g., bounded drift, smooth weight decay). For instance, the use of Hotelling’s T² distribution to compute MD p-values assumes i.i.d. samples from a multivariate normal—a condition violated by the weighted, non-i.i.d. stream data. Similarly, Liptak’s pooling was designed for independent meta-analysis with fixed sample sizes, not for adaptive weighting in streaming contexts. The authors must explicitly state these methodological limitations or explore theoretical bounds in future work. Without such analysis, the algorithm’s reliability remains questionable.  
2. **Over-reliance on heuristics**: While heuristics are acceptable for a first practical step, the paper does not sufficiently justify critical design choices. For example:  
   - Why use the effective sample size \(S^r\) (Eq. 4) as \(N\) in Hotelling’s T²? This neglects the non-i.i.d. nature of weighted samples, potentially invalidating the p-value approximation.  
   - The noisy OR mechanism for relearning lacks a principled basis; its parameters (e.g., threshold for ρ) are not derived from theory but appear arbitrary.  
   The authors should clarify these trade-offs and explicitly note where heuristics deviate from established statistical assumptions.  

**Other Comments**  
- **Footnotes**: Proof sketches in footnotes are unconventional for NIPS. Move all technical details (e.g., proof of Lemma 1) to supplementary material.  
- **Figures**: Poorly scaled with excessive whitespace, missing axis labels, and vague captions (e.g., "Fig. 3: Results"). Revise for clarity and consistency.  
- **Experimental analysis**: The explanation for LoSST’s poor performance on *edge additions* (when only parameters change) is unsatisfactory. The authors should decouple parameter updates from structure updates (e.g., via separate thresholds) to improve interpretability.  
- **Real-world analysis**: The US price index case study is promising but lacks depth. More concrete links between detected changes and known economic events (e.g., oil crises, recessions) would strengthen the argument.  

**Overall Assessment**  
This work presents a timely, well-motivated approach to a challenging problem. LoSST’s practical efficiency and real-world applicability are commendable, but the theoretical gaps and unexamined heuristic choices undermine its scientific contribution. The authors have laid a solid foundation for future work, but the current submission requires substantial revision to address the methodological concerns.  

**Recommendation**: **Reject** (with major revisions). The core idea is promising, but the lack of theoretical grounding and insufficient justification of heuristics prevent the paper from meeting NIPS’s standards for technical rigor. If the authors provide a clear theoretical analysis (even under restrictive assumptions) and rigorously justify their heuristic choices, the paper could be reconsidered. As-is, it risks misleading readers about the method’s reliability.
