Paper Summary:
The paper tackles an interesting and largely unexplored problem: tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a stream of data points (e.g., time-based). The authors focus on an efficient (computationally and memory-wise) solution for recursive Structural Equation Models (SEMs). They develop a novel algorithm for this task, named LoSST, which utilizes a standard batch learning algorithm (PC by Spirtes et al., 2000) for underlying structure learning and tracks several statistics from the data stream to determine when to initiate new structure learning. These tracked statistics include the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix (equations 1 & 2), and a weighted sample size. A weighting scheme assigns weights to each new data point based on how “surprising” it is (equations 3 & 4). Each point’s Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed, and the estimated p-values for the MDs, obtained using a Hotelling’s T<sup>2</sup> distribution, are combined using Liptak’s weighted pooling of p-values to compute an overall “significance” statistic, ρ. This statistic is then used in a noisy-OR configuration with the previous step to decide whether to start a new structure learning process. The authors compare LoSST to vanilla PC batch learning on synthetic datasets where either a change in parameters or a change in structure is introduced after a fixed number of data points, testing for different numbers of variables and maximum degree configurations. Finally, they perform an analysis of real-world data from the US price index with 529 data points (monthly from 1967) and six variables, showing the algorithm detects some known changes/trends in the US economy while ignoring short-lived fluctuations.

Pros:
1. The paper addresses an interesting and relatively unexplored problem. As the authors point out, there is significant potential for further development of practical solutions for these and other models, along with opportunities for theoretical advancements and guarantees.
2. The algorithm is novel and appears practical and efficient. It creatively combines several existing methods.
3. The experiments seem adequate and attempt to track the effect of the various parameters, including an interesting real-world dataset.
4. The authors provide a good review of previous work in this area.
5. The paper is generally well-written.

Cons:
1. A significant limitation, given the interesting problem addressed, is the lack of theoretical analysis or guarantees under any assumptions. While the authors acknowledge the opposing forces of convergence and diligence (p. 4), some form of guarantees under simplifying conditions seems plausible and should be mentioned as a direction for future work.
2. The LoSST solution relies heavily on heuristics. While this allows the authors to achieve a practical solution, the decisions involved could be more clearly explained. Tracking the mean and correlation matrix is relatively straightforward, but the reweighing scheme, effective sample size calculation, and the decision-making process for relearning are less transparent. The authors make creative use of existing methods, potentially outside the conditions for which their theoretical guarantees hold. For example, they estimate the MD p-value using Hotelling’s T<sup>2</sup> distribution, setting the sample size N to the effective sample size S<sup>r</sup>. However, that distribution was developed for complete counts from a given distribution, not for partial or reweighted counts. If data were actually sampled from these networks/structures with these partial weights, would the distribution still follow this form? Similarly, Liptak’s weighted p-values were developed for meta-analysis using independent tests with different sample sizes. At a minimum, if these methods are used outside of their guaranteed context, the authors should explicitly state this to avoid misinterpretations.

Other comments:
- The authors use footnotes to outline proofs, which may be outside the intended use of the conference formatting guidelines. Consider using supplementary material for that and other missing details.
- Figures are too small and lack clarity, with unnecessary space between them, missing labels, and insufficient detail in the captions and legends. The description of the experiments is not clear enough (e.g., the top paragraph on p. 7 is cryptic).
- The explanation of why LoSST performs worse on edge removal when only the parameters change is not entirely convincing.
- Decoupling parameter updates and structure updates appears desirable.

Overall, this is a really interesting direction for future research, with a practical, though heuristic, implementation as a first step.
