This paper introduces a hypothesis-testing based approach for learning graphical models online from time series data, specifically addressing scenarios where the underlying data-generating model changes frequently. The method’s performance is evaluated using both simulated and real-world datasets.

**Quality:**

The reported results appear technically sound. However, the discussion of related work in Section 2 contains a potentially misleading characterization. While many score-based algorithms for Bayesian network learning assume exchangeability or i.i.d. data *within* the model, the algorithms themselves do not necessarily require a single, unchanging generating model; they often rely on scoring functions that factorize locally. I disagree with the authors’ assertion regarding prior work (specifically point 3 in their response to review); the work of Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) does not assume i.i.d. data and permits variations in the underlying graphical structure, albeit within a defined set of models. The presented method may offer increased flexibility, but it is inaccurate to claim it is the first to tackle this problem.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written, but clarity could be improved. The core learning task remains somewhat ambiguous. Is the goal to identify change points, given specific assumptions about their existence and characteristics? Or is the objective to infer the structure of the most recent data-generating model? Or something else entirely? Further clarification is needed. Similarly, it's unclear whether the paper primarily presents a novel *model* (with defined assumptions and learning objectives) or a new *algorithm* (providing computational methods to achieve established learning goals), or a combination of both. 

Specifically, the paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to parse. The equation for ρ<sub>r+1</sub> is presented without clear indication of whether it’s a definition or a previously established relationship. Furthermore, the omission of periods at the end of sentences containing displayed equations is a stylistic concern. The font size in Figures 1-4 is noticeably small and should be increased for better readability. Adding descriptive labels for the colors used in Figures 1 and 2 would also enhance understanding. In Section 6, the claim that improved tracking can offset increased computational cost feels contingent on domain-specific trade-offs between tracking accuracy and computational resources; this connection requires further elaboration.

**Originality:**

The work appears original in its combination of techniques. However, the core ideas do not represent a substantial conceptual breakthrough. While the p-value construction may contain novel elements (though I admit to struggling to follow the details), the overall approach feels incremental.

**Significance:**

If published, this work could potentially stimulate the development of more advanced, particularly Bayesian, methods for learning graphical models in dynamic environments. However, the current work reads more like a pragmatic development effort than a fundamental research contribution. The proposed approach appears to be a heuristic solution to a learning problem that existing machine learning techniques should, in principle, be capable of addressing.
