

Paper Summary:  
This paper addresses an interesting and underexplored problem: tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a streaming (e.g., time-based) dataset. The authors focus on an efficient (computationally and memory-wise) solution for simpler structural equation models (SEMs). They propose a new algorithm, LoSST, which builds on a standard batch structure-learning method (PC algorithm by Spirtes et al., 2000) and tracks key statistics from the data stream to determine when to initiate new structure learning. These statistics include the weighted mean deviation for each variable, the correlation matrix (Equations 1–2), and an effective sample size. Data points are weighted based on their "surprise" level (Equations 3–4), and each point’s Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed. The authors combine p-values for these MDs using Liptak’s weighted pooling to derive an overall significance statistic, ρ. This statistic, in conjunction with a noisy OR configuration with prior steps, determines whether to trigger new structure learning. Experiments compare LoSST to vanilla batch PC learning on synthetic datasets with controlled structural or parameter changes after fixed data segments, varying numbers of variables and maximum degrees. A brief real-world analysis uses US price index data (529 monthly observations from 1967, six variables), showing LoSST detects meaningful economic trends while ignoring transient fluctuations.  

Pros:  
1. The problem is well-motivated and underexplored; the authors rightly note significant room for practical solutions and theoretical development.  
2. LoSST is novel and practical, creatively leveraging diverse techniques for an efficient implementation.  
3. Experiments are adequate, systematically probing parameter effects and including a compelling real-world case study.  
4. The literature review is thorough and well-contextualized.  
5. The paper is well-written overall.  

Cons:  
1. The most significant gap is the lack of theoretical guarantees under any assumptions. As the authors note (page 4), convergence and theoretical rigor are opposing forces, but even simplified conditions should admit some guarantees. At minimum, this should be explicitly flagged as future work.  
2. LoSST relies heavily on heuristics, which is acceptable for a practical first step but warrants clearer justification. Key decisions—reweighting schemes, effective sample size, and relearning triggers—lack transparency. Crucially, the authors apply methods outside their intended contexts without warning. For instance:  
   - They estimate MD p-values using Hotelling’s T² distribution, but this assumes complete, unweighted samples from a known distribution. Here, the effective sample size S^r (Equation 4) is used for N, which invalidates the distribution’s validity.  
   - Liptak’s pooling was designed for meta-analysis of independent tests with varying sample sizes, not for sequential streaming data. The authors should explicitly state where these methods are applied outside their guarantees to avoid misleading readers.  

Other Comments:  
- Creative footnote use for proofs (beyond standard conventions) should be moved to supplementary material.  
- Figures are too small, with excessive spacing, missing labels, and unclear captions. Experimental descriptions (e.g., top paragraph on page 7) require greater clarity.  
- The explanation for LoSST’s poorer performance on edge additions when only parameters change is unsatisfactory.  
- Decoupling parameter updates from structure updates would be desirable to isolate effects.  

This work presents a promising direction for streaming structure learning with a practical, heuristic-first implementation. The core idea is compelling, and the real-world validation adds significant value. However, the absence of theoretical grounding and insufficient contextualization of methodological choices weaken the contribution. Addressing these gaps would substantially strengthen future iterations.
