- Proposes LoSST, an online heuristic that reweights sufficient statistics using Mahalanobis distance and a pooled p-value to adapt graphical-structure learning to non-stationary streams.  
- Demonstrates empirical gains over a standard batch learner on simulated and real economic data when the underlying structure changes.  
- Addresses an important practical problem where traditional i.i.d. structure-learning methods fail.  
- Main novelty is the specific heuristic (pooled p-value weighting) rather than a core theoretical advance.  
- Claim of being the first method for non-stationary structure learning is overstated; prior work (e.g., Siracusa & Fisher 2009) handles time-varying structures.  
- Mischaracterizes score-based algorithms by conflating scoring-factorization properties with the i.i.d. assumption; needs clarification.  
- Framing and relation to prior art are imprecise and should be strengthened.  
- Lacks a clear, formal problem definition—unclear whether goal is changepoint detection, current-structure recovery, or something else.  
- Unclear whether the contribution is a new probabilistic model for time-varying graphs or a heuristic algorithm for an existing problem.  
- Explanation of the pooled p-value (ρ_{r+1}) is confusing and possibly improperly presented.  
- The argument that better tracking compensates for extra computational cost (Section 6) is underdeveloped and domain-specific.  
- Several presentation issues: missing punctuation after displayed equations, figures with too-small fonts, and missing legends in Figures 1–2.  
- Overall impact is limited by heuristic nature and ambiguous task definition; reads more like an engineering solution than foundational research.  
- For acceptance: tackles a significant problem and presents an intuitive, empirically effective online method.  
- Against acceptance: ambiguous problem statement, heuristic and unprincipled methodology, overstated originality, and clarity/presentation weaknesses.
