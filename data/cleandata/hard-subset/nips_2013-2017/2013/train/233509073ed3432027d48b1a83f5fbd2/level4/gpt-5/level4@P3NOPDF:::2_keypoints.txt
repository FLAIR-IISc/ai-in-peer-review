- Paper tackles online detection of dependency-structure changes in streaming linear SEMs and proposes LoSST, a computationally and memory-efficient detector.  
- LoSST tracks deviations from weighted means, a weighted correlation matrix, and an effective weighted sample size between relearning events.  
- Incoming points are weighted by Mahalanobis distance; per-variable MD p-values use Hotelling’s T^2, pooled via Lipták, and a noisy-OR over recent pooled statistics triggers PC-based relearning.  
- Empirical evaluation compares LoSST to repeated batch PC on synthetic data with controlled parameter/structure changes and applies LoSST to a real U.S. price-index dataset.  
- Strength: addresses an important, underexplored problem with broad applicability for streaming causal discovery and model monitoring.  
- Strength: proposes a practical, efficient method that combines ideas from causal discovery, change detection, and meta-analysis.  
- Strength: experiments explore varying numbers of variables, graph degrees, parameter vs. structure changes, and include a real-data case.  
- Strength: related work is well surveyed and the paper is generally clear and readable.  
- Weakness: lacks theoretical guarantees (false-alarm control, detection delay bounds, consistency) or even partial results under simplifying assumptions.  
- Weakness: heavy reliance on heuristics whose statistical validity is unclear in the nonstationary, reweighted setting.  
- Concern: Hotelling’s T^2 p-values may be miscalibrated because they assume i.i.d. samples, not reweighted/nonstationary data.  
- Concern: Lipták’s pooling assumes independent tests and fixed sample sizes; correlations and data-dependent weights likely violate these assumptions.  
- Concern: noisy-OR accumulation is ad hoc and its impact on false alarms and detection delay is unanalyzed.  
- Presentation issue: several figures are hard to read, with excessive whitespace, small size, missing labels/legends, and weak captions.  
- Experimental clarity issue: synthetic setup, timing/magnitude of changes, hyperparameter settings, and metric computation are insufficiently described.  
- Interpretation issue: explanation for worse performance on edge addition under parameter-only changes is unconvincing; need ablations (e.g., disable structure updates) and deeper analysis.  
- Suggestion: provide partial theoretical guarantees or detectability limits under simplifying assumptions (e.g., Gaussian SEM, bounded degree, stationary epochs).  
- Suggestion: empirically validate/calibrate per-variable p-values and the pooled statistic ρ under the null via simulation to assess false-alarm rates.  
- Suggestion: analyze noisy-OR’s impact on detection delay/false positives and consider alternatives (e.g., CUSUM-style accumulation) as ablations.  
- Suggestion: document all heuristic choices (weighting, S^r, thresholds, relearning policy) and explicitly state where classical assumptions are violated.  
- Suggestion: decouple parameter adaptation from structure relearning with separate triggers and report their separate effects.  
- Suggestion: improve figures, clarify synthetic protocol (runs, seeds, change schedules, metrics with CIs), add ablations on design choices, and compare to standard change-point baselines.  
- Suggestion: move lengthy technical digressions to supplement and restate PC assumptions, discussing how nonstationarity affects conditional-independence tests.  
- Overall: a timely, practical, and promising first step with efficient methodology and encouraging results, but needs stronger theory, calibration, ablations, and improved presentation.
