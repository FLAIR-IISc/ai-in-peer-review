

# Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

This paper addresses an important gap in graphical model structure learning: the need for algorithms that can track changes in dependency structure in real-time data streams, rather than assuming globally stationary data. The authors introduce LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a heuristic algorithm that processes data incrementally to detect and adapt to changes in graphical model structure or parameters. The paper effectively situates its contribution within the literature, noting that while standard structure learning algorithms (e.g., PC algorithm) assume i.i.d. data, and online methods like TDL or changepoint detection (CPD) algorithms exist, none provide general graphical model structure learning in an online manner. The authors convincingly argue that real-world data often exhibit local stationarity rather than global stationarity, making their approach both novel and practically relevant.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a compelling case for the problem's significance, with strong motivation from real-world applications where batch processing is infeasible. The algorithm is well-motivated and creatively combines several techniques: using Mahalanobis distances for novelty detection, Liptak's p-value pooling for robust change detection, and a probabilistic relearning mechanism. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, testing the algorithm on synthetic data with varying numbers of variables and graph structures, and demonstrating its advantage over batch learning when structures change. The real-world application to US price index volatility is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates the algorithm's ability to detect meaningful economic shifts while ignoring short-term noise.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough review of related work that establishes the novelty of their approach. The authors provide sufficient detail on the algorithmic implementation, including the weighted sufficient statistics tracking and the decision rules for relearning. The comparison to batch-mode learning is appropriately designed, showing that LoSST incurs no performance cost in stable environments while significantly outperforming batch methods during structural changes.

**Weaknesses:**
The most significant weakness is the lack of theoretical guarantees regarding the algorithm's behavior. While the authors acknowledge the tension between convergence and diligence (p. 4), they fail to provide any formal analysis under specific assumptions. The use of Hotelling's T² distribution with weighted data and Liptak's p-value pooling in contexts where these methods weren't originally developed raises concerns about the validity of their statistical approach. The paper should more explicitly acknowledge these methodological limitations and discuss their implications.

The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on edge removal errors when only parameters change (rather than structure) is insufficient and requires deeper analysis. The paper would benefit from decoupling parameter updates from structure updates, which would clarify this behavior. Additionally, the figures are poorly presented (too small, lacking clear labels), and the experimental description (e.g., "500 datasets were generated for each of a range of 〈#variables,MaxDegree〉 pairs") lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**
*Pro:* Novel problem with clear practical relevance, well-designed experiments demonstrating clear advantages over batch methods during structural changes, compelling real-world application, and a well-structured paper.
*Con:* Lack of theoretical guarantees, methodological concerns regarding statistical assumptions, insufficient explanation of certain experimental results, and poor figure presentation.

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The problem addressed is significant, the algorithm is well-designed, and the experimental results are convincing. However, the lack of theoretical grounding and certain methodological concerns prevent this from being a strong candidate for acceptance. With revisions addressing the theoretical limitations, clarifying the statistical assumptions, and improving the presentation of results, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

The authors should explicitly acknowledge the limitations of their statistical approach (Hotelling's T² with weighted data, Liptak's pooling in this context), provide more detailed experimental descriptions, and consider decoupling parameter and structure updates. The real-world application is promising but would benefit from more extensive validation. With these improvements, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
