*

The paper presents a hypothesis-testing based approach to learning graphical models online from time series data. The method is specifically designed for scenarios where the data-generating graphical model may change frequently. The performance is demonstrated using both simulated and real data. 

Quality 
The results appear correct. However, I find the discussion of related work in Section 2 somewhat misleading. Typical score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks do not assume a single generating model; rather, they assume the scoring function can be factored into local terms. While commonly studied models assume exchangeability (or iid data), this concerns the model specification, not the algorithm itself. 

Clarity 
The presentation is generally sound but could be improved. The primary issue is a lack of clarity regarding the precise learning task. Is the goal to infer change points (under specific assumptions about their existence and nature), to infer the structure generating the most recent data point, or something else entirely? Furthermore, it’s unclear whether the work proposes a novel model (i.e., a new formulation of assumptions and learning goals) or a new algorithm (i.e., novel computational methods to achieve established learning goals), or both.

The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow; for example, it is unclear whether the equation for ρ<sub>r+1</sub> defines the variable or if it has been defined previously. I also note the authors’ choice to omit periods when ending a sentence with a displayed equation. An almost equally important issue is the font size in Figures 1-4, which should be doubled for readability. Additionally, providing descriptions for the different colors in Figures 1 and 2 would be helpful. In Section 6, I found the claim that improved tracking could compensate for increased computational cost confusing; such a trade-off is highly dependent on domain-specific valuations of tracking accuracy versus computational resources. 

Originality 
The work appears original, but lacks substantial innovation. The potential novelty may lie in the construction of the p-values (although I am unable to fully follow the details). 

Significance 
The potential significance of this work, should it be published, lies in its potential to rapidly facilitate the development of improved, likely Bayesian, methods for this problem. This should be viewed more as development than fundamental research. The approach appears heuristic and addresses a somewhat unclearly defined learning problem that existing machine learning techniques may already be capable of solving.
