This paper introduces LoSST, a novel, heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from data streams where the underlying generative process may be non-stationary. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics (mean and covariance) in an online fashion, using a weighting scheme that dynamically adapts based on how well new data points fit the current model. A poor fit, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance and a pooled p-value, suggests a change in the data-generating structure. This triggers an increase in the weights of recent data points, effectively "forgetting" older data and allowing a standard structure learning algorithm (in this case, the PC algorithm) to be re-run on the updated statistics to find the new structure. The authors demonstrate through simulations that LoSST performs comparably to a batch-mode learner in stationary environments and significantly better when the structure or parameters change over time. An application to US price index data is also presented to illustrate its use on real-world time-series data.

The problem of learning graphical models in dynamic environments is significant and practical, and this paper presents a sensible approach. However, the contribution's framing and its relationship to prior work could be clarified.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles the important and challenging problem of structure learning in non-stationary environments, a common scenario in many real-world domains where batch-mode learning is inappropriate or infeasible.
2.  **Methodological Approach:** The proposed online, single-pass method is well-suited for streaming data and very large datasets. The mechanism of using Mahalanobis distance to detect change and re-weighting data is intuitive and clever.
3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The simulation results are compelling. They clearly demonstrate the algorithm's ability to adapt to both abrupt structural changes and subtler parameter shifts, outperforming a standard batch algorithm in these dynamic settings while incurring little to no performance cost in a stable environment.
4.  **Real-World Application:** The analysis of US price index volatility provides a nice demonstration of the algorithm's potential utility on a real-world problem, correctly identifying a known period of macroeconomic change.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of the Contribution:** The paper is somewhat ambiguous about the precise nature of its contribution. Is it proposing a new statistical model for time-varying graphical structures, or is it a novel algorithm for a known learning task? The current presentation seems to focus on the latter, presenting a collection of well-chosen heuristics. A more formal definition of the learning task—whether it is changepoint detection, inference of the most recent graph, or tracking the full history of graphs—would help clarify the goals and position the work more precisely.

2.  **Positioning Relative to Prior Work:** The characterization of related work could be more accurate.
    *   The claim that standard score-based algorithms "assume that the data come from a single generating structure" is slightly imprecise. These algorithms typically assume that the scoring function factorizes according to the graph structure, while the statistical model (e.g., assuming i.i.d. data) is what imposes the stationarity assumption. This is a subtle but important distinction between the algorithm and the model.
    *   The paper could better differentiate itself from existing work on time-varying graphs. For example, Siracusa & Fisher (AISTATS 2009) also address time-varying dependence structures without assuming i.i.d. data. While the LoSST algorithm is distinct in being frequentist and fully online, the authors should more accurately acknowledge prior work that has relaxed the stationarity assumption.

3.  **Originality:** The primary contribution appears to be a novel and effective integration of several existing statistical techniques (online covariance updates, Mahalanobis distance, Liptak's method for p-value pooling, and the PC algorithm) rather than a fundamentally new theoretical insight. While this engineering contribution is valuable, the paper would be stronger if it acknowledged this more directly. The innovation lies in the specific combination and application of these tools to the problem of online structure learning.

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** Several aspects of the paper could be improved for clarity:
    *   The paragraph at the end of Section 3 describing the calculation of ρ_{r+1} is difficult to follow. It is not immediately clear if the equation is a definition or a result, and the surrounding text could better explain the logic behind the update equations for η and τ.
    *   The figures, particularly Figures 1-3, are difficult to read due to the small font size of the axes and legends. The lines in Figures 1 and 2 are not labeled, making it impossible to distinguish between the different experimental conditions (e.g., <4, 3> vs. <20, 12>). Increasing the font size and adding a clear legend would significantly improve readability.
    *   The argument in the discussion (Section 6) that "improved tracking would compensate for the additional computational and memory cost" seems speculative. This trade-off is highly dependent on the specific application's requirements for accuracy versus computational resources.
    *   A minor formatting point: Sentences ending with a displayed equation should be properly punctuated with a period.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and promising heuristic approach to an important problem. The core ideas are sound, and the experimental results are strong. The main weaknesses lie in the clarity of the problem formulation and the positioning of the work within the literature. Addressing these issues, along with the suggested presentation improvements, would substantially strengthen the paper.
