This manuscript introduces a novel approach to structure learning for Structural Equation Models (SEMs) designed to handle non-stationary data – specifically, datasets generated from potentially changing underlying models over time (locally i.i.d. data). The paper is clearly written and well-organized, addressing an important and often overlooked problem: learning from time-dependent data, a scenario common in real-world applications but often implicitly assumed away by standard structure learning algorithms. The proposed technique, LoSST, extends existing methods in a conceptually simple yet effective way, as demonstrated through experiments utilizing the PC algorithm.

The core contribution lies in adapting structure learning to environments where the generative process isn't globally stationary. This is achieved by dynamically weighting data points based on their consistency with the current model, allowing the algorithm to adapt to changes in the underlying structure. The authors present a compelling argument for the utility of this approach, particularly in online learning settings where batch processing is impractical.

Two points could enhance the clarity and precision of the paper. First, the statement that “any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly” requires nuance. While the authors’ intuition may be correct, this claim feels overly strong without further justification. It would be more accurate to frame the experiments specifically as a comparison of the PC algorithm in both time-independent and time-dependent settings (with the proposed adaptation). Alternatively, if the authors wish to maintain the broader assertion, they should elaborate on *why* other structure learning methods are likely to exhibit similar behavior in this context – perhaps detailing shared sensitivities or common assumptions.

Second, the discussion regarding the extension of these ideas to Bayesian network structure learning is insightful. Highlighting the challenges associated with tracking sufficient statistics for discrete variables from the outset would sharpen the paper’s focus. Presenting the work more explicitly as targeting SEM models, while acknowledging the potential (but non-trivial) extension to other model classes, would clarify the scope of the contribution. This could also frame the limitations of the approach more effectively, guiding future research directions. 

In summary, this is a well-written and promising paper addressing the important problem of structure learning under local stationarity. The proposed LoSST algorithm is conceptually straightforward and appears effective, based on the presented experimental results focused on the PC method. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem in graphical model learning.
*   Presents a clear and intuitive algorithm for adapting structure learning to non-stationary data.
*   Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in simulated environments.
*   The algorithm is relatively simple to implement.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The claim regarding the generalizability of the results to other structure learning algorithms requires further justification.
*   The scope of the paper could be more clearly defined by explicitly focusing on SEMs and acknowledging the challenges of extending the approach to other model classes (e.g., Bayesian networks).
