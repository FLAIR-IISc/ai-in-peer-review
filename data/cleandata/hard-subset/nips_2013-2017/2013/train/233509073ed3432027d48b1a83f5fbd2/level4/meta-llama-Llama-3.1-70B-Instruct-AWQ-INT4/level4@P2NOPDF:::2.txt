Paper Summary:

This paper addresses an intriguing and under-explored problem of tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a stream of data points, such as time-based data. The authors focus on developing an efficient solution for simpler Structural Equation Models (SEMs). They propose a novel algorithm, LoSST, which utilizes a standard batch learning algorithm for structure learning (PC by Spirtes et al., 2000) and tracks several statistics from the data stream to determine when to initiate new structure learning. These statistics include the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix, and the weighted sample size. The authors employ a weighting scheme for each new point based on its "surprisingness" (Eq. 3, 4). They compute the Mahalanobis distance (MD) for each point and estimate the p-values for the MDs using Hotelling's T2 distribution, which are then combined using Liptak's weighted pooling of p-values to compute an overall "significance" statistic. This statistic is used in a noisy OR configuration with the previous step to decide whether to start a new structure learning. The authors evaluate LoSST on synthetic datasets with changes in parameters or structure and perform a short analysis on real-life data from the US price index.

Pros:

1. The paper tackles an interesting and under-explored problem, providing a valuable contribution to the field.
2. The proposed algorithm, LoSST, is novel and practical, making creative use of various methods to address the problem.
3. The experiments are adequate, exploring the effects of various parameters and including an interesting real-life dataset.
4. The authors provide a good review of previous work on this problem.
5. The paper is well-written.

Cons:

1. A significant limitation of the paper is the lack of theoretical guarantees or convergence analysis under certain assumptions. While the authors acknowledge the trade-off between convergence and diligence, it would be beneficial to discuss potential future work in this direction.
2. The LoSST algorithm relies heavily on heuristics, which, although enabling a practical solution, may not provide a clear understanding of the decisions involved. The authors should explicitly note these decisions, particularly regarding reweighting, effective sample size, and when to relearn. Furthermore, the authors use methods outside their original context, such as estimating MD p-values using Hotelling's T2 distribution and applying Liptak's weighted p-values. It is essential to clearly state these deviations from the original guarantees to avoid misinterpretation.

Other comments:

- The authors' use of footnotes to outline proofs is unconventional and may be better suited for supplementary materials.
- The figures are too small, with unnecessary space between them, missing labels, and inadequate captions. The experiment descriptions, such as the top paragraph on page 7, are unclear.
- The explanation for LoSST's poor performance on edge addition when only parameters are changed is unsatisfactory.
- It would be desirable to decouple parameter updates and structure updates in future work.
Overall, this paper presents an interesting direction for future research, with a practical, albeit heuristic, implementation as a first attempt at addressing this problem.
