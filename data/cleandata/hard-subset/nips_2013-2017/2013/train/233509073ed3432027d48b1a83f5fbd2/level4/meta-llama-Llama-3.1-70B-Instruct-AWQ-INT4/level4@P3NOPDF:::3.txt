This manuscript presents a systematic approach to adapting structure learning for Structural Equation Models (SEMs) to accommodate data sets that are only locally independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.), meaning the data may be generated from distinct underlying models over time. The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and addresses a crucial problem often neglected by many widely used algorithms for structure learning: learning from time-dependent data. The techniques proposed to extend learning to time-dependent data are straightforward yet effective, as demonstrated by the experiments using the PC method.

The paper's contributions could be clarified with two suggestions. Firstly, in the experimental section, the assertion that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly" seems overly broad. The differences among methods for structure learning can be significant. It would be more accurate to rephrase this statement to emphasize that the experiments focus on the PC method for both time-independent and time-dependent data, with an adaptation for the latter, and then speculate that similar results might be obtained with other methods. Alternatively, the authors could retain the original statement and provide a detailed explanation for why they believe other methods would perform similarly.

Secondly, in the conclusions, the authors discuss the potential challenges of applying the same ideas to learning the structure of Bayesian networks, primarily due to difficulties in maintaining sufficient statistics. Although this issue is not central to the proposal in this paper, it highlights that extending the learning procedure to Bayesian networks and other models may require more than minor modifications. Therefore, I suggest that the paper could benefit from explicitly stating its focus on SEM models from the outset, while also acknowledging the possibility of extending the approach to other models, albeit with potential complexities. In short, the paper could be more precise about its scope.

Overall, this is a well-written paper about learning the graph structure for SEMs under the assumption that data are only locally i.i.d. The formulation is simple to understand and implement, and the experimental results appear promising, even if focused primarily on a comparison using the PC method. I acknowledge having read the authors' feedback.
