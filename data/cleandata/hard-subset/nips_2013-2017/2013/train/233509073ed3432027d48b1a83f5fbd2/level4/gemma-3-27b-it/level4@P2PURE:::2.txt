Paper Summary:
This paper addresses the compelling and relatively unexplored problem of tracking changes in the dependency structure between variables within a data stream (e.g., time series). The authors focus on developing a computationally and memory-efficient solution tailored for simpler Structural Equation Models (SEM). Their novel algorithm, LoSST, leverages a standard batch learning approach for structure learning (PC algorithm by Spirtes et al., 2000) and monitors statistics from the data stream to determine when to trigger a new round of structure learning. These tracked statistics include the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix (eq. 1,2), and the weighted sample size. The weighting scheme assigns weights to new data points based on their “surprisingness” (Eq. 3,4), calculated via each point's Mahalanobis distance (MD) and estimated p-values obtained using Hotelling's T² distribution. These p-values are then combined using Liptak's weighted pooling method to compute an overall “significance” statistic, ρ. This statistic, in conjunction with the previous step, informs a noisy OR gate to decide whether to initiate a new structure learning phase. The authors evaluate LoSST against vanilla PC batch learning on synthetic datasets featuring changes in either parameters or structure after a fixed number of data points, varying the number of variables and maximum degree configurations. They also present an analysis of US price index data, comprising 529 monthly data points (starting in 1967) across six variables. Their results demonstrate the algorithm's ability to detect known economic shifts while filtering out short-term fluctuations.

Pros:
1. The paper tackles an interesting and under-explored problem. As the authors note, substantial opportunity exists for developing practical solutions and accompanying theoretical foundations for this and related models.
2. The proposed algorithm is novel and demonstrates practicality and efficiency, creatively integrating a variety of existing methods.
3. The experimental setup appears adequate for assessing the effects of various parameters and includes a relevant real-world dataset.
4. The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work in this area.
5. The paper is generally well-written.

Cons:
1. A significant limitation is the lack of theoretical analysis or guarantees under any assumptions. While the authors acknowledge the trade-off between convergence and diligence (p. 4), exploring potential guarantees even under simplified conditions would be valuable. At minimum, this direction should be mentioned as future work.
2. LoSST relies heavily on heuristics. While this enables a practical solution, the rationale behind key decisions should be more clearly articulated. Tracking the mean and correlation matrix is straightforward; the critical choices pertain to reweighing, effective sample size, and the criteria for re-learning the structure. The authors employ several methods outside their established guarantees. For example, the Mahalanobis distance p-value is estimated using Hotelling's T² distribution, applied to an effective sample size S^r, despite this distribution being designed for complete counts from a given distribution, not partial or re-weighted counts. The validity of applying this distribution given the weighting scheme is questionable. Similarly, Liptak’s weighted p-values are intended for meta-analysis with independent tests, each with a different sample size. The authors should explicitly acknowledge when methods are used outside their guaranteed context to avoid potential misinterpretations.

Other comments:
- The authors extensively utilize footnotes for proofs, potentially exceeding the intended scope of footnote usage. Consider moving this material to the supplementary material along with other missing details.
- Figures are excessively small, with wasted space, missing labels, and incomplete captions and legends. The experimental description lacks clarity (e.g., the top paragraph on p. 7 is unclear).
- The explanation for LoSST’s poorer performance on edge addition when only parameters change is unconvincing.
- Decoupling parameter updates from structure updates appears desirable, given the observed behavior.
- This work represents a promising research direction, offering a practical, though heuristic, first implementation.
