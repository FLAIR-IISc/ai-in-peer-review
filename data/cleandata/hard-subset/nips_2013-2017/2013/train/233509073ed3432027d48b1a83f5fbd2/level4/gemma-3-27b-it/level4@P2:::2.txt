This paper addresses the important and relatively unexplored problem of tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a data stream. The authors focus on an efficient solution for recursive Structural Equation Models (SEMs), developing a novel algorithm called LoSST. LoSST leverages a standard batch learning algorithm (PC algorithm [Spirtes et al., 2000]) for structure learning and tracks statistics from the data stream to determine when to initiate re-learning. These tracked statistics include weighted means, the covariance matrix, and a weighted sample size. A key component is a weighting scheme for new data points based on their “surprisingness” (Equations 3 & 4), calculated using Mahalanobis distance (MD) and a pooled p-value (via Liptak’s method) to assess the fit of the new data to the current model. This p-value is incorporated into a noisy-OR gate with the previous state to trigger re-learning. The algorithm’s performance is evaluated on synthetic datasets with induced changes in parameters or structure, varying the number of variables and maximum degree, and on a real-world dataset of US price indices.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Importance:** The paper tackles a genuinely interesting and under-explored problem with significant practical relevance. Online structure learning is crucial in many real-world applications where data distributions are non-stationary.
2.  **Practical Efficiency:** LoSST appears to be a computationally and memory-efficient approach, essential for online learning scenarios. The creative combination of existing methods demonstrates ingenuity.
3.  **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experimental setup appears reasonable, including synthetic data to isolate specific effects and a real-world case study to demonstrate applicability.
4.  **Thorough Related Work:** The authors provide a good overview of existing work and clearly position their contributions within the field.
5.  **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and the algorithm is explained in a reasonably accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** The most significant weakness is the absence of theoretical analysis or guarantees. While the authors acknowledge the trade-off between convergence and diligence (p. 4), some theoretical exploration, even under simplifying assumptions, would significantly strengthen the paper. At least outlining potential avenues for future theoretical work would be beneficial.
2.  **Heuristic Nature & Assumptions:** LoSST relies heavily on heuristics. While understandable for a practical solution, the rationale behind key design choices, particularly the weighting scheme, effective sample size calculation, and re-learning criteria, could be more explicitly justified. The authors utilize statistical methods (Hotelling’s T<sup>2</sup>, Liptak’s pooling) potentially outside their original contexts, and this should be clearly acknowledged. For instance, using the effective sample size *S<sup>r</sup>* as *N* in the Hotelling’s T<sup>2</sup> distribution assumes that the distribution remains valid under weighted sampling, which needs justification. Similarly, Liptak's method assumes independent tests, an assumption potentially violated here.
3.  **Presentation of Results:** The figures are small and lack sufficient detail. Captions are often incomplete, and labels are missing. The description of the experimental setup (e.g., the first paragraph on p. 7) is somewhat cryptic.
4.  **Explanation of Results:** The explanation for LoSST’s decreased performance on edge removal when only parameters change is not entirely convincing. Decoupling parameter and structure updates may be desirable.

**Other Comments:**

*   The extensive use of footnotes for proofs disrupts the flow of the main text. Consider relocating this material to a supplementary document.
*   The discussion of convergence vs. diligence is insightful but could be further developed.
*   The statement that LoSST detects changes “while ignoring short-lived changes” requires more justification based on the experimental results.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising paper that addresses an important problem with a practical and potentially valuable solution. While the lack of theoretical guarantees and the reliance on heuristics are weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, the comprehensive experiments, and the clear writing make it a worthwhile contribution. The authors successfully demonstrate the feasibility of online structure learning in a dynamic environment.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem.
*   Presents a novel and potentially useful algorithm.
*   Demonstrates promising empirical results.
*   Clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lacks theoretical guarantees.
*   Relies heavily on heuristics without sufficient justification.
*   Presentation of results could be improved.



**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss potential avenues for theoretical analysis, even if preliminary.
2.  Explicitly acknowledge the assumptions underlying the use of Hotelling’s T<sup>2</sup> and Liptak’s pooling method.
3.  Improve the clarity and completeness of the figures and captions.
4.  Provide a more convincing explanation for the observed results in the parameter-only change scenario.
