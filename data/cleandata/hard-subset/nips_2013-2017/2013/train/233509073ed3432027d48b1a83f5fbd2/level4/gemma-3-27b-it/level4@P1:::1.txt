The paper presents a hypothesis-testing based approach to learn graphical models in an online fashion from time series data. The proposed method, LoSST, is specifically targeted to scenarios where the underlying generative graphical model may change relatively frequently. The performance of the method is demonstrated using both simulated and real data.

**Quality**

The results appear technically sound. However, the description of related work (in Section 2) contains a potential misleading statement: typical score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks do not assume the data come from a single generating model—rather, they assume the scoring function factorizes into local terms. While these algorithms typically assume exchangeability (or i.i.d. data), this concerns the model assumption, not the algorithm itself. The authors should clarify this distinction. I also note a disagreement with the authors’ claim regarding prior work; Siracusa and Fisher III (AISTATS 2009) does not assume the data are i.i.d. and allows the generating structure to vary, albeit within a defined set of graphical models. While LoSST is potentially more flexible, claiming it is the *first* to address this problem is inaccurate.

**Clarity**

The presentation is generally good, but could be improved. The core learning task is somewhat unclear: is the goal to infer change points under specific assumptions, to infer the generating structure of the most recent data point, or something else? It would be helpful to explicitly state the learning objective. Furthermore, it’s unclear whether the work presents a new model (defining assumptions and learning goals), a new algorithm (computational means to achieve those goals), or both. 

The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow; for example, the equation for ρ<sub>r+1</sub> appears without prior definition. The authors should ensure all equations are properly introduced. Additionally, sentences ending with displayed equations should be punctuated with periods. The font size in Figures 1-4 is too small and should be doubled for readability. Descriptions for the different colors used in Figures 1 and 2 would also be beneficial. Finally, in Section 6, the statement that improved tracking could compensate for additional computational cost is context-dependent and requires further clarification—this will vary based on specific domain valuations of accuracy versus computational resources.

**Originality**

The work appears original in its specific combination of techniques. However, it lacks a truly groundbreaking or novel idea. The construction of the p-values might contain a novel element, but it wasn’t immediately clear to me due to the density of the presentation.

**Significance**

The potential significance of this work lies in paving the way for the development of more sophisticated, potentially Bayesian, methods for this problem. However, the current work feels more like a stepping stone toward further development than a major research contribution. The heuristic approach to a somewhat unclearly defined learning problem suggests that existing machine learning tools might already offer solutions or near-solutions.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practically relevant problem: online learning of graphical models in non-stationary environments.
*   Demonstrates promising performance on simulated data, outperforming a batch-mode approach after a change in the underlying structure.
*   Provides a real-world case study showing potential applicability to economic time series data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The originality is incremental, rather than revolutionary.
*   The presentation is somewhat unclear and could be improved with more precise definitions and explanations.
*   The related work discussion contains a potentially misleading statement and overlooks relevant prior work.
*   The overall approach feels somewhat heuristic and may be surpassed by more principled Bayesian methods.
