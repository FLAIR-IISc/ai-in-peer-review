- Paper addresses detecting shifts in dependency structure in streaming time-series and proposes LoSST, an efficient method for simple SEMs.  
- LoSST uses batch PC for structure learning and monitors weighted means, correlation matrices, and effective sample size to trigger relearning.  
- Data points are weighted by novelty via Mahalanobis distance p-values (Hotelling’s T²), combined with Liptak’s pooling into a significance ρ used in a noisy-OR trigger.  
- Evaluation compares LoSST to batch PC on synthetic shifts (parameter/structural) across dimensions and degrees, and on a 6-variable US price-index real dataset.  
- Strength: tackles a compelling, underexplored problem with significant practical/theoretical interest.  
- Strength: LoSST is a novel, practical algorithm that efficiently integrates multiple methodological components.  
- Strength: Experiments are well-designed, exploring parameter effects and including a relevant real-world case.  
- Strength: Literature review is thorough and situates the work within prior art.  
- Strength: Paper is clearly and professionally written.  
- Weakness: Lacks theoretical guarantees or analysis under simplifying assumptions, which is critical for this problem.  
- Weakness: Heavy reliance on heuristics (reweighting, effective sample size, triggers) without sufficient justification; methodological mismatches (using Hotelling’s T² on reweighted data; Liptak pooling assumptions vs. sequential dependence) need clarification.  
- Presentation issues: proofs overused in footnotes should go to supplementary material.  
- Presentation issues: figures are poorly formatted (size, spacing, missing labels/caption details) and some experimental descriptions lack clarity.  
- Methodological/analysis gaps: explanation for poorer performance on edge additions during parameter shifts is inadequate; decoupling parameter and structure updates is recommended.  
- Overall: a promising, practical heuristic and a valuable first step toward more robust solutions.
