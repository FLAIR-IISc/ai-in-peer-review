

The paper introduces LoSST, a heuristic algorithm for online graphical model structure learning under locally stationary conditions—where the generating structure may change over time without requiring batch processing. This addresses a gap in prior work, which either assumes global stationarity (e.g., standard score-based or constraint-based methods [3, 10, 12]) or handles only specific change patterns (e.g., Talih & Hengartner 2005 on single-edge changes in undirected graphs; Siracusa & Fisher 2009 on Bayesian edge uncertainty in batch mode). Unlike online changepoint detection methods [1, 4], LoSST tracks *graphical structure* (not just environmental features) in real-time using weighted sufficient statistics, Mahalanobis distances, and pooled p-values. Simulations show it matches batch-mode performance under stability and outperforms it during structural shifts, with a real-world application to U.S. price index volatility (1967–2011) demonstrating detection of the "Great Moderation" (1980–2007).

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The technical approach is sound. The online sufficient statistics update (Equations 1–3) and weighted Mahalanobis distance/pooled p-value framework (Equation 4) are well-motivated, and simulations rigorously compare LoSST to the PC algorithm [12] across 500 datasets. The convergence/diligence trade-off analysis (Section 3.1) is a notable theoretical contribution.  
*Clarity*: The core algorithm is clearly described, and the real-world application (Section 5) effectively contextualizes the method. The simulation results (Figures 1–3) are well-organized, though minor improvements would help: (1) Explicitly define *ρ*<sub>r+1</sub> *before* Equation 4 (it’s currently derived in the text); (2) Add figure legends for color coding in Figures 1–2; (3) Increase font size in Figures 1–4 for readability.  
*Originality*: The paper correctly identifies the gap in handling *time-varying graphical structure* in online settings. While inspired by TD(0) [13] and changepoint detection [1, 4], the integration of pooled p-values for *structure* tracking (not just parameter shifts) is novel for graphical models.  
*Significance*: The method enables real-time structure learning in domains like financial time-series analysis (Section 5), where batch processing is infeasible. It provides a practical foundation for future work on dynamic graphical models, though the current heuristic nature means it *enables* rather than *is* the solution (e.g., paving the way for principled Bayesian extensions).

**Weaknesses**  
*Related Work*: The claim that "no prior work handles general graphical model structure learning online" (Section 2) misrepresents Siracusa & Fisher (2009), which *does* allow structure to vary (though via Bayesian edge uncertainty and batch processing). The paper should explicitly acknowledge this and clarify how LoSST’s *online, frequentist* approach differs.  
*Clarity*: The learning task is ambiguously framed. The paper focuses on tracking *changes* (e.g., "detect and learn the new structure"), but it’s unclear whether the goal is to infer the *current* structure (after changes) or *all* intermediate structures. The end-of-page-3 paragraph on *ρ*<sub>r+1</sub> is confusing; the definition should precede the equation.  
*Originality/Significance*: The core innovation is a *heuristic* (e.g., the weight update in Equation 4) rather than a novel statistical framework. While the approach is practical, the paper overstates its "novelty" by not sufficiently contrasting with Siracusa & Fisher. The significance would be stronger if the authors more clearly articulated *why* existing methods (e.g., batch PC on sliding windows) fail for *rapidly evolving* structures (Section 6 mentions this but doesn’t quantify it).

**Recommendation**  
**Weak Accept**. The paper makes a valuable contribution to dynamic graphical models, with strong technical execution and compelling simulations. However, the mischaracterization of Siracusa & Fisher (2009) undermines the novelty claim, and the learning task framing needs clarification. The method is clearly applicable to NIPS (e.g., time-series analysis, causality [Spirtes et al., 2000]), and the real-world example adds weight.  

**Pro/Con Summary**:  
*Pro*: Novel online structure-tracking framework; rigorous simulations; practical real-world validation; clear convergence/diligence analysis.  
*Con*: Misattribution in related work; ambiguous learning task definition; heuristic approach lacks principled innovation (though it’s a useful step toward it).  

**Suggestions for Revision**:  
1. Correct the Siracusa & Fisher comparison in Section 2 (e.g., "While Siracusa & Fisher (2009) allow structure variation, their Bayesian batch method cannot handle real-time updates").  
2. Clarify the learning objective (e.g., "LoSST tracks the *current* generating structure at each timestep, responding to changes in real-time").  
3. Define *ρ*<sub>r+1</sub> explicitly before Equation 4.  
4. Add figure legends and increase font sizes in Figures 1–4.  

This work advances the state of the art in dynamic graphical models and deserves publication, provided the related work and clarity issues are addressed. The heuristic nature is acceptable for NIPS, as long as the contribution is accurately positioned as a *practical algorithmic step* rather than a fundamental theoretical breakthrough.
