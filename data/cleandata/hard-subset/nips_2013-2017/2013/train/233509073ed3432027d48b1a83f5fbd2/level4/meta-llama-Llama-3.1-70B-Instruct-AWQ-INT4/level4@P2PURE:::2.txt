Paper Summary: 
The paper addresses an interesting and under-explored problem of tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a stream of data points, such as time-based data. The authors focus on developing an efficient solution, in terms of computation and memory, for simpler Structural Equation Models (SEMs). They propose a novel algorithm, LoSST, which utilizes a standard batch learning algorithm for structure learning (PC by Spirtes et al., 2000) and tracks various statistics from the data stream to determine whether to initiate new structure learning. The tracked statistics include the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix (Equations 1 and 2), and the weighted sample size. A weighting scheme is used for each new data point based on its "surprisingness" (Equations 3 and 4). The Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed for each point, and the estimated p-values for the MDs are combined using Liptak's weighted pooling of p-values to compute an overall "significance" statistic, ρ. This statistic is then used in a noisy OR configuration with the previous step to decide whether to start a new structure learning process. The authors evaluate their algorithm by comparing it to vanilla PC batch learning on synthetic datasets with changes in parameters or structure introduced after a fixed number of data points, testing different numbers of variables and maximum degree configurations. They also perform a short analysis on real-life data from the US price index, comprising 529 data points (monthly, starting from 1967) and six variables, demonstrating the algorithm's ability to detect known changes and trends in the US economy while ignoring short-lived changes.

Pros:

1. The paper tackles an interesting and under-explored problem, as acknowledged by the authors, leaving room for additional development of practical solutions and theoretical guarantees.
2. The proposed algorithm is novel and practical, making creative use of various methods to achieve efficiency.
3. The experiments seem adequate, exploring the effects of various parameters and including an interesting real-life dataset.
4. The authors provide a good review of previous work on this problem.
5. The paper is overall well-written.

Cons:

1. A significant limitation of the paper is the lack of theoretical guarantees or convergence analysis under certain assumptions. While the authors acknowledge the trade-off between convergence and diligence (p. 4), it would be beneficial to discuss potential guarantees under simplifying conditions or mention it as a direction for future work.
2. The LoSST algorithm relies heavily on heuristics, which, although enabling a practical solution, should be clearly noted and justified. The decisions involved in tracking the mean and correlation matrix are straightforward, but the reweighting, effective sample size, and relearning processes require more explicit explanations. The authors creatively apply existing methods, such as estimating MD p-values using Hotelling's T2 distribution and employing Liptak's weighted p-values, under conditions that may void their original guarantees. For instance, the Hotelling's T2 distribution was developed for complete counts from a given distribution, not partial or reweighted counts. Similarly, Liptak's weighted p-values were designed for meta-analysis using independent tests with different sample sizes. The authors should clearly state when methods are used outside their original context to avoid misinterpretation.

Other comments:

- The authors' use of footnotes to outline proofs is unconventional and may be better suited for supplementary materials, along with other missing details.
- The figures are too small, with unnecessary space between them, and lack labels, details in captions, and legends. The experiment descriptions, such as the top paragraph on page 7, are unclear.
- The explanation for LoSST's poor performance on edge addition when only parameters are changed is unsatisfactory.
- Decoupling parameter updates and structure updates seems desirable.
- The paper presents an interesting direction for future research, with a practical, albeit heuristic, implementation as a starting point.
