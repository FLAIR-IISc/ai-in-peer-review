- Disagreement with authors: Siracusa and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) already handle non-i.i.d., changing-generating-structure settings, so claiming novelty on that front is misleading.  
- Summary: Proposes an online, hypothesis-testing-based method to learn graphical models from time series where the generating graph may change frequently, evaluated on simulated and real data.  
- Results/quality: Empirical results seem correct, but related-work framing is misleading about score-based algorithms inherently assuming a single generating model.  
- Technical nuance: Score-based methods typically rely on score factorization into local terms; exchangeability/i.i.d. is a model assumption, not a limitation of the algorithm.  
- Clarity—goal ambiguity: The paper is unclear whether it targets change-point detection, inferring the most recent structure, or another learning objective.  
- Clarity—model vs algorithm: Unclear whether the contribution is a new model, a new algorithm, or both.  
- Clarity—notation/exposition: Paragraph at end of page 3 is hard to follow (e.g., unclear definition/status of ρ_{r+1}).  
- Clarity—stylistic fixes: Minor issues such as missing periods after displayed equations reduce readability.  
- Figures: Figures 1–4 use too-small fonts; Figures 1–2 lack legends explaining colors.  
- Section 6 claim: The assertion that improved tracking can justify extra computation is questionable and domain-dependent.  
- Originality: Work appears original but lacks a clear, significant conceptual innovation; novelty may be limited to the specific p-value construction.  
- Derivation clarity: The p-value derivation is hard to follow, preventing assessment of its novelty.  
- Significance: Tackles an important problem but reads as a heuristic for an unclearly defined task and likely an incremental contribution that may stimulate more principled (e.g., Bayesian) methods.
