### Summary

This paper introduces LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a novel, heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from data streams where the underlying generative process may change over time. The core problem addressed is that standard structure learning algorithms assume globally stationary (i.i.d.) data, an assumption often violated in real-world settings. LoSST tackles this by processing data in an online, single-pass manner, assuming only local stationarity.

The algorithm works by tracking sufficient statistics—specifically the weighted mean and covariance matrix for Structural Equation Models (SEMs)—using a dynamic weighting scheme. New data points that fit poorly with the current estimated model (as measured by the Mahalanobis distance) are given higher weight, effectively allowing the algorithm to "forget" older, irrelevant data and adapt to changes. The algorithm uses a pooled p-value approach to make this change detection robust to single outliers. Structure relearning is triggered probabilistically, with the probability increasing when a change is detected. The authors also provide a compelling theoretical discussion on the trade-off between diligence (quickly detecting change) and convergence (stabilizing on a fixed structure), showing how different parameter settings for LoSST navigate this trade-off.

Empirical evaluation on synthetic data shows that LoSST performs comparably to a standard batch-mode algorithm (the PC algorithm) in stationary environments and significantly outperforms it when the underlying graph structure or parameters change. A real-world application to U.S. price index data further demonstrates the algorithm's potential to identify meaningful shifts in real-world systems.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses a critical and practical limitation of most existing graphical model structure learning algorithms. The problem of learning in non-stationary environments is highly relevant, and this work presents a well-reasoned, principled approach to solving it. The ability to operate online and in a single pass is a significant advantage for applications involving large-scale data streams.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly established, the LoSST algorithm is described in sufficient detail with well-explained update equations, and the theoretical discussion on the convergence-diligence trade-off adds significant depth. The experiments are well-designed and the results are presented clearly.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The use of Mahalanobis distance to measure fit and a pooled p-value to trigger updates is an intelligent way to balance sensitivity to change with robustness to noise. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that the algorithm works as intended, adapting to changes while incurring little to no performance cost in stable environments.

4.  **Completeness:** The paper presents a complete piece of work, including the algorithmic formulation, a theoretical analysis of its properties, comprehensive synthetic experiments, and a real-world case study.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, there are a few areas where its claims could be clarified or refined to strengthen the contribution further.

1.  **Generalization of Experimental Results:** In the simulation results section, the authors state, "we conjecture that any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly." This is a strong claim that is not fully substantiated. While the core of LoSST is the adaptive tracking of sufficient statistics, the performance of the overall system is still dependent on the downstream structure learning algorithm (here, PC). Different algorithms have different sensitivities to sample size and statistical errors, which could interact with LoSST's effective sample size in complex ways.
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper would be strengthened by softening this claim. It would be more accurate to state that the proposed *framework for tracking sufficient statistics* demonstrates significant benefits when paired with the PC algorithm, and that it is hypothesized to offer similar advantages for other constraint-based or even score-based methods that rely on the same statistics.

2.  **Clarity of Scope (SEMs vs. General Graphical Models):** The algorithm as presented is tailored for SEMs over continuous variables, as it fundamentally relies on tracking the covariance matrix. The discussion section rightly points out that extending this approach to other models, like Bayesian networks over discrete variables, is a non-trivial challenge due to the difficulty of tracking their sufficient statistics online.
    *   **Suggestion:** The paper would benefit from being more explicit about its primary focus on SEMs from the outset. While the abstract and introduction frame the problem in terms of general graphical models, the contribution is more precisely an online structure learning method for linear SEMs. Framing the paper more directly in this context, while still noting that the general *idea* could potentially be extended, would sharpen the presentation and manage reader expectations about the method's immediate applicability.

### Overall Assessment

This is a well-written, clear, and high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical model learning. It addresses an important, practical problem with a novel and effective solution. The experiments are convincing and the theoretical analysis of the diligence-convergence trade-off is a valuable addition. The minor weaknesses related to the scope of experimental claims and the framing of the contribution can be easily addressed with minor revisions. The work is likely to be of high interest to researchers and practitioners working with time-series data and dynamic systems. I recommend acceptance.
