- Introduces LoSST: an online, single-pass algorithm for learning time-varying graphical structures under local stationarity for linear SEMs.
- Maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) updated online.
- Adapts weights using Mahalanobis distance and pooled p-values (Liptak) so recent poor fit increases influence of new data.
- Relearns structure probabilistically using the PC algorithm, more often when pooled p-value indicates poor fit.
- Analyzes trade-off between diligence (fast change response) and convergence (stability), with conditions for each behavior.
- Differs from prior work by being online and handling gradual/adaptive changes without storing large buffers or assuming abrupt changepoints.
- Update rules and use of Mahalanobis/Hotelling’s T2 and Liptak pooling are plausible and experiments show sensible behavior.
- Proofs are mostly sketched rather than fully formalized.
- Experiments show LoSST matches batch PC under stationarity and outperforms after changes, with a limited real-data CPI case study.
- Needs justification for using Hotelling’s T2 with a non-integer, time-varying “effective” sample size.
- Lacks discussion of dependence assumptions in Liptak pooling given overlapping, adaptively weighted statistics.
- Computational details absent: how inverse covariance is maintained/regularized, complexity, and numerical stability.
- Missing specifics on implementing CI tests in PC with weighted covariances and whether thresholds are adjusted.
- Baselines are weak: should include sliding-window PC, exponential-forgetting PC, or CPD+relearn comparisons.
- Scope/clarity issue: must state early that methods and guarantees are tailored to linear SEMs with Gaussian noise.
- Overgeneral claim that other structure learners would perform similarly is too strong and should be tempered or justified.
- Novelty: the combination of online sufficient-statistics tracking, pooled p-value weighting, and probabilistic relearning is original and practical.
- Significance: single-pass, memory-light approach is valuable for streaming nonstationary settings but needs broader empirical validation (domains, larger graphs, drifts).
- Strengths: addresses an important problem, simple implementable updates, strong synthetic results, and insightful diligence-vs-convergence framing.
- Weaknesses/suggestions: limit generality claims, discuss statistical assumptions, add stronger baselines, analyze hyperparameter sensitivity, provide computational/test details, and consider robustness to outliers/heavy tails.
- Pro-accept: practical, novel approach with empirical support and attractive streaming properties.
- Con-accept: empirical evaluation and methodological details need strengthening for full validity and reproducibility.
- Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying scope, adding methodological/numerical details, and including at least simple stronger baselines.
