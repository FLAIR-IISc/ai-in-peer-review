This paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic online structure learning algorithm for SEMs that assumes only local stationarity: data at any time are i.i.d. from a current generative model, which may change over time. LoSST tracks weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an effective sample size) in a single pass, using Mahalanobis distances combined via Liptak’s pooled p-values to adapt weights and trigger probabilistic “relearning” of structure. The implementation uses the PC algorithm to recover graph structure at selected times. Simulations show LoSST matches batch PC when the process is stationary and outperforms it when structure or parameters change, and a real-data case on U.S. price indices illustrates plausible change detection. The paper addresses an important gap: most structure learning assumes global stationarity, while prior online methods (e.g., TD learning or changepoint detection) do not support general graphical structure learning or require storage and abrupt-change assumptions. The convergence–diligence trade-off is usefully articulated.

Two suggestions to clarify and sharpen the contribution:

1) Strength of generalization across structure learners. The paper states that “any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly.” This seems too strong without empirical support, as different learners (score-based, constraint-based, hybrid) can behave quite differently under nonstationarity, finite samples, and varying assumptions (e.g., Gaussianity). I recommend rephrasing to emphasize that the experiments evaluate LoSST coupled with PC (both in batch and onlineized forms), and that extending LoSST to other learners is plausible but untested here. Alternatively, provide justification for why similar behavior is expected (e.g., decomposition properties, reliance on the same sufficient statistics) or a small-scale comparison.

2) Scope with respect to Bayesian networks and other models. The conclusion notes that extending the approach to Bayesian networks may be nontrivial due to sufficient-statistic and decomposition issues. This is important and argues for stating the scope more explicitly up front: the paper’s primary target is SEMs (linear Gaussian DAG models), with extensions to other model classes as future work rather than straightforward drop-ins. Making this scope explicit in the introduction would better set expectations while still highlighting paths to generalization.

Overall assessment: This is a clear, well-organized paper that tackles a significant problem. The proposed updates are simple to implement, single-pass, and empirically effective in the tested settings. Tightening the claims about generality and clarifying scope would strengthen the paper without detracting from its core contributions.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an underexplored but practically important problem: online structure learning under local stationarity.
- Simple, computationally lightweight updates that integrate naturally with existing constraint-based learning.
- Strong empirical evidence that LoSST matches batch performance under stationarity and adapts quickly after changes.
- Helpful conceptual discussion of convergence vs. diligence.

Cons for acceptance:
- Claims about applicability across structure learners are not substantiated empirically.
- Scope beyond SEMs is discussed but appears nontrivial; setting expectations earlier would improve clarity.
