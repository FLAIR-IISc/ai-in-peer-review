

Reaction to author feedback:  
I remain unconvinced by point (3). The work of Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) does not assume iid data and explicitly allows the "data-generating structure" to vary (even within a constrained set of graphical models). While the proposed method is more flexible in some respects, it is misleading to claim it is the first to address this problem.  

Quality:  
The results appear technically sound. However, the related work discussion (Section 2) is misleading. Typical score-based Bayesian network learning algorithms do not assume data arise from a single generating model; rather, they rely on scoring functions that factorize into local terms. The assumption of exchangeability (or iid data) pertains to the model itself, not the algorithm.  

Clarity:  
The paper’s presentation is generally clear but requires refinement. Crucially, the core learning task is ambiguous: is the goal to infer change points (under well-defined assumptions about their existence and nature), to recover the generating structure of the most recent data point, or something else? Additionally, it is unclear whether the work proposes a new *model* (formalizing assumptions and learning objectives) or a new *algorithm* (computational methods to achieve these goals), or both. The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow (e.g., the definition of $\rho_{r+1}$ is unclear—does the equation define it, or was it defined earlier?). The absence of periods after displayed equations is inconsistent. Figure 1–4 require larger font sizes (should be doubled), and Figures 1–2 need color legends. In Section 6, the claim that improved tracking compensates for computational cost depends on domain-specific trade-offs between accuracy and cost, which is not adequately justified.  

Originality:  
The work appears original but lacks substantive innovation. The novelty, if any, may lie in the construction of p-values (which I was unable to fully follow).  

Significance:  
The potential impact of this work—if published—would be limited to accelerating the development of superior Bayesian methods for this problem. This constitutes *development* rather than *research*. The approach is heuristic, targets an ill-defined learning problem, and appears to address a challenge that existing machine learning frameworks should already have resolved.
