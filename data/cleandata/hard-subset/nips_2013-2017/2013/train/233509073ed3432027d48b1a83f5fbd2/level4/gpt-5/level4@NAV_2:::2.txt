Paper Summary:
This paper addresses the important and underexplored problem of tracking shifts in the dependency structure among variables in a streaming (e.g., time-indexed) setting. The authors target an efficient solution for linear SEMs and introduce a new algorithm, LoSST, that learns structure online. LoSST relies on a standard batch learner for structure (PC; Spirtes et al., 2000) and continuously maintains statistics from the data stream to decide when to trigger re-learning. The tracked quantities include the weighted means, the covariance matrix (eq. (1), (2)), and an effective sample size. The per-sample weight is determined by how surprising the new observation is (eq. (3), (4)). Specifically, each observation’s Mahalanobis distance is computed, p-values are estimated using Hotelling’s T^2 with the effective sample size, and these p-values are combined using Liptak’s weighted pooling to produce a pooled significance measure ρ. This statistic is then used in a noisy-OR update with the previous step to decide whether to initiate structure re-learning. The authors compare LoSST to vanilla batch PC on synthetic datasets in which either parameters or structure change after a fixed number of samples, across varying numbers of variables and maximum degrees. They also analyze a real dataset of six U.S. price index series (monthly, starting in 1967; 529 observations), showing that the method detects known macroeconomic shifts while ignoring short-lived anomalies.

Pros:
1. The paper tackles a timely and insufficiently explored problem. As the authors note, there is ample room for additional practical methods and matching theory for these and related models.
2. The algorithm is novel, computationally and memory efficient, and creatively integrates several techniques to achieve practicality.
3. The experiments are sensible, explore parameter effects, and include a relevant real-world case study.
4. The related work discussion is thorough and situates the contribution well.
5. The paper is generally well written.

Cons:
1. The main shortcoming is the absence of theoretical guarantees under explicit assumptions. While the paper discusses the tension between convergence and diligence (p. 4), some form of guarantees under simplifying conditions seems plausible. At a minimum, clearly flag this as a direction for future work.
2. The proposed method, LoSST, is heavily heuristic/ad hoc. That is reasonable for a first practical solution, but the design choices should be made more explicit. Beyond the straightforward tracking of means and covariances, the key judgments concern reweighting, effective sample size, and relearning triggers. Some components appear to be used outside their standard conditions. For instance, p-values for Mahalanobis distances are obtained via Hotelling’s T^2 by substituting the effective sample size S^r, although Hotelling’s T^2 is derived for unweighted samples from a fixed distribution. Would the same reference distribution hold if one were to sample according to these partial weights? Similarly, Liptak’s weighted pooling is intended for meta-analysis of independent tests with differing sample sizes. If these methods are used beyond their usual validity domains, please state this explicitly to avoid misinterpretation by readers.

Other comments:
- The paper uses footnotes to sketch proofs in ways that likely exceed what the venue intended; consider moving such material (and other missing details) to a supplement.
- Figures are too small, with excessive whitespace, missing axes/labels, and underspecified captions/legends. The experimental description is also unclear in places (e.g., the top paragraph on p. 7).
- The rationale for why LoSST performs worse on edge addition when only parameters change is unconvincing.
- Relatedly, it seems desirable to decouple parameter-update triggers from structure-update triggers.

Overall, this is a compelling direction with a practical, albeit heuristic, first step.
