This manuscript presents a systematic approach to structure learning for Structural Equation Models (SEMs) using datasets that are only locally independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.). This addresses the important and often-neglected problem of learning from time-dependent data, where the underlying generating model may change over time. The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The proposed techniques for extending structure learning to this setting are simple yet appear effective, as demonstrated by the experiments conducted with the PC algorithm.

Overall, this is a strong contribution. The proposed formulation is straightforward to understand and implement, and the experimental results are promising. I offer two suggestions to further clarify and strengthen the paper's contributions.

First, regarding the experimental evaluation, the manuscript contains the assertion that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly." I believe this claim is too strong, as performance differences between various structure learning algorithms can be considerable due to their differing assumptions and search strategies. To improve the paper, I suggest one of two revisions:
1.  Rephrase the statement to more accurately reflect the scope of the experiments. The authors could frame the results as a successful proof-of-concept demonstrating the benefits of their adaptation for the PC algorithm. They could then speculate that similar improvements might be achievable with other methods, positioning it as a promising direction for future work.
2.  Alternatively, if the authors maintain that the conclusion generalizes, they should provide a detailed justification for why this is the case. This would require a more thorough theoretical or empirical argument explaining why the observed performance gains are algorithm-agnostic.

Second, the paper's scope could be defined more sharply from the outset. In the conclusion, the authors astutely discuss the potential difficulties of applying these ideas to learn the structure of Bayesian networks, particularly concerning the maintenance of sufficient statistics. This insight reveals that extending the proposed framework to other model classes is a non-trivial task. The paper would benefit from disclosing this nuance earlier. I recommend framing the contribution more directly in the introduction as a method tailored for SEMs. While the authors should still mention that the core ideas might be extensible to other models, they should also state that such extensions are not necessarily straightforward and represent avenues for future research. This would provide the reader with a more precise understanding of the paper's primary contribution and context.

In summary, this is a well-written paper that addresses an important problem with a clear and effective solution. The experiments, though focused on a single base algorithm, convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed method. Addressing the points above would further enhance the clarity and impact of this work.

I acknowledge to have read the feedback from the authors.
