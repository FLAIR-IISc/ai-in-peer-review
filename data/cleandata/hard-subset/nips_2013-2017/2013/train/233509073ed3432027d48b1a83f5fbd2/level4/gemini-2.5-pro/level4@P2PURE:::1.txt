**Reaction to author feedback:**

I disagree with the authors' response to point (3). The work of Siracusa and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) does not assume i.i.d. data and allows the data-generating structure to vary, even if only within a small set of graphical models. While the proposed method may offer more flexibility, it is misleading to claim it is the first to address this problem.

***

This paper presents a hypothesis-testing approach for learning graphical models online from time-series data. The method is designed for scenarios where the underlying data-generating graphical model changes frequently. The performance is demonstrated on both simulated and real-world data.

**Quality**

The technical results appear to be correct. However, the description of related work in Section 2 is somewhat misleading. Score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks do not inherently assume that data originate from a single generating model; rather, they assume the scoring function factorizes into local terms. While many commonly studied *models* assume exchangeability (or i.i.d. data), this is a property of the model, not a prerequisite of the learning algorithm itself.

**Clarity**

The paper is mostly well-written, but its clarity could be improved in several areas.

Most importantly, the paper does not clearly define the learning task. Is the goal to infer change points (given a formal model of their existence and nature), to infer the generating structure of the most recent data point, or something else entirely? Furthermore, it is difficult to discern whether the primary contribution is a new model (i.e., a formulation of assumptions and learning goals) or a new algorithm (i.e., a computational method to achieve those goals), or both.

Specific presentation issues also hinder readability:
*   The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow. For instance, it is unclear whether the equation for ρ_{r+1} is its definition or if it has been defined previously.
*   Sentences ending with a displayed equation are missing periods.
*   The font size in Figures 1-4 is too small and should be increased for legibility.
*   Figures 1 and 2 lack a legend or description for the different colors used.
*   The argument in Section 6, suggesting that improved tracking performance justifies the additional computational cost, is questionable. This trade-off between accuracy and cost is highly domain-specific and cannot be asserted universally.

**Originality**

The work appears original in its approach. However, it seems to lack a significant conceptual innovation or novel idea. The primary novelty may lie in the construction of the p-values, but I was unable to fully assess this due to the presentation.

**Significance**

The main significance of this work, if published, might be in stimulating the development of superior methods for this problem, presumably more principled Bayesian approaches. In this sense, the contribution feels more like engineering development than fundamental research. It proposes a heuristic-based solution to a vaguely defined learning problem that seems solvable with existing, more established machine learning techniques.
