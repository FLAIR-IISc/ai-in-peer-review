### **Summary**

This paper introduces an online, hypothesis-testing-based method for learning graphical models from time series data. The proposed method is specifically designed for non-stationary environments where the underlying data-generating graphical model can change over time. The authors validate their approach through experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

### **Evaluation**

While the paper addresses an important problem and the proposed approach appears original, there are significant concerns regarding its positioning, clarity, and overall contribution that prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form.

#### **Strengths**

*   **Problem Relevance:** The paper tackles the important and challenging problem of learning dynamic graphical models from streaming data, which is highly relevant to the community.
*   **Originality of Approach:** The specific hypothesis-testing framework developed for this task appears to be novel.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results presented seem internally consistent and demonstrate the method's intended behavior on the chosen tasks.

#### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Misleading Positioning and Discussion of Related Work:** The paper's positioning with respect to prior work is a major concern.
    *   The authors' claim that this is the first method to address this problem is inaccurate. For instance, the work of Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) also handles non-IID data and allows for a varying generating structure, albeit within a finite set of models. While the proposed method may offer greater flexibility, the claim of being the "first" is misleading and overlooks relevant literature.
    *   Furthermore, the characterization of score-based learning methods in Section 2 is imprecise. These algorithms do not inherently assume a single generating model for the entire dataset; rather, their key assumption is the factorization of the scoring function into local terms. While many models used with these algorithms assume exchangeability, this is a property of the model, not a fundamental limitation of the score-based learning paradigm itself.

2.  **Lack of a Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper lacks a precise formulation of the learning task it aims to solve. It is unclear whether the primary goal is (a) to detect change points in the data-generating process, (b) to accurately infer the graphical model structure at the most recent time step, or (c) some combination of these objectives. This ambiguity makes it difficult to evaluate the method's contributions and performance rigorously. Relatedly, the paper does not clearly distinguish whether its primary contribution is a new probabilistic model for dynamic graphs or a novel algorithm for a pre-existing problem formulation.

3.  **Apparent Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** While the specific combination of techniques appears original, the method comes across as somewhat heuristic and lacks a clear theoretical or probabilistic foundation. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more formal justification of its design choices. As it stands, the contribution feels more like an engineering solution than a fundamental advance in machine learning. The innovation appears to lie in the construction of the p-values, but this part of the paper was not clear enough for me to fully assess its novelty.

4.  **Limited Significance:** The significance of the work is difficult to assess. The approach seems tailored to a problem that could likely be addressed by more principled, existing frameworks (e.g., Bayesian non-parametrics or state-space models). The paper's main value may be in inspiring the development of superior, perhaps Bayesian, methods for this problem. In its current form, it reads more like a report on development rather than fundamental research, presenting a heuristic-style approach to a somewhat unclearly defined problem.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The overall presentation could be improved.
    *   The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow. For instance, is the equation for ρ_{r+1} a definition, or is it derived from a previous one? This needs clarification.
    *   The manuscript consistently omits periods at the end of sentences that conclude with a displayed equation. Standard typesetting conventions should be followed for readability.
*   **Figure Readability:** The font size in Figures 1-4 is far too small, making them nearly unreadable. The font size should be increased substantially. Additionally, Figures 1 and 2 would be much clearer with a legend explaining the color coding.
*   **Justification of Trade-offs:** In Section 6, the claim that improved tracking can compensate for higher computational cost is subjective. This trade-off is highly application-dependent and should not be presented as a general justification without further context or a defined loss function that explicitly balances both aspects.
