## Review of “Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure”

This paper tackles the important and relatively underexplored problem of tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a data stream. The authors focus on an efficient solution for recursive Structural Equation Models (SEMs), developing a novel algorithm, LoSST, which combines a standard batch learning algorithm (PC algorithm [Spirtes et al., 2000]) with online tracking of statistics to trigger re-learning of the structure. LoSST dynamically weights data points based on their “surprisingness” – quantified using Mahalanobis distance and a pooled p-value – to adjust the effective sample size and inform re-learning decisions. The algorithm’s performance is evaluated on synthetic datasets with varying structure and parameter changes, and demonstrated on a real-world US price index dataset.

The core idea of adaptively weighting data and triggering structure re-learning is compelling and addresses a significant limitation of traditional graphical model learning methods, which assume stationarity. The authors provide a comprehensive overview of related work, demonstrating awareness of existing approaches in online learning and changepoint detection and clearly articulating why these are insufficient for the problem at hand. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, effectively explaining the algorithm's components and rationale.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Relevance:** The paper addresses an important problem with limited existing solutions. Online structure learning is crucial for many real-world applications where data arrives sequentially and the underlying generative process is non-stationary.
* **Practicality and Efficiency:** LoSST appears to be a computationally feasible approach, leveraging a well-established batch learning algorithm and efficient online statistic tracking.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors present a good range of simulation experiments, varying the number of variables, maximum degree, and type of change (structure vs. parameters). The application to the US price index data provides a valuable, albeit brief, demonstration of real-world applicability.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a well-structured and informative review of relevant literature, properly situating the contribution within the broader field.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** A major limitation is the absence of theoretical analysis or guarantees on the algorithm’s performance. While the authors acknowledge the trade-off between convergence and diligence (p. 4), even simplified theoretical results regarding convergence rates or conditions for successful change detection would significantly strengthen the paper.
* **Heuristic Nature & Justification:** The algorithm relies heavily on heuristics, particularly in the weighting scheme and the decision of when to re-learn the structure. While this is understandable for a practical approach, the rationale behind these choices could be more clearly articulated and the potential impact of deviating from these choices explored.  The use of Hotelling’s T<sup>2</sup> distribution to estimate p-values for the Mahalanobis distance, given the non-standard effective sample size, requires further justification. The validity of applying this distribution when the data are weighted and not a truly i.i.d. sample should be discussed. Similarly, the application of Liptak’s weighted p-value pooling method, designed for independent tests, in this context warrants more detailed consideration.
* **Presentation of Results:** The figures are small and lack sufficient detail (labels, captions, clear axis descriptions). The description of the experimental setup (e.g., the first paragraph on p. 7) is unclear and requires elaboration.  The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on edge removal when only parameters change is not entirely convincing.
* **Decoupling Parameter and Structure Updates:** The current implementation interweaves parameter and structure updates. Exploring a decoupled approach, where parameter updates occur more frequently than structure updates, could potentially improve performance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel and important problem in a practical way.
* The algorithm appears to perform well in simulations and shows promising results on a real-world dataset.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its rationale.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of theoretical guarantees weakens the scientific rigor of the contribution.
* The reliance on heuristics and the insufficient justification for certain design choices raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the algorithm.
* Issues with the presentation of results and experimental details hinder clear understanding and evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of dynamic graphical model learning. While the lack of theoretical analysis and the reliance on heuristics are weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, the promising experimental results, and the clear writing make it a worthwhile submission. I believe the paper is currently borderline. 

**Recommendation:**  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Address the theoretical limitations:**  Even a preliminary discussion of convergence properties or conditions for successful change detection would significantly improve the paper.
2.  **Provide more justification for the heuristics:**  Explain the rationale behind key design choices (weighting scheme, re-learning criteria) and discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm to these choices.
3.  **Improve the presentation of results:**  Enlarge figures, add missing labels and captions, and provide more detailed descriptions of the experimental setup.
4. **Acknowledge limitations of statistical methods:** Explicitly state when methods are used outside their guaranteed conditions, and acknowledge potential impacts.
