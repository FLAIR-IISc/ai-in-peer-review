- Claim of novelty is inaccurate: prior work (Siracusa & Fisher 2009) handles non-iid data; the paper should claim novelty in online time-series adaptation, not non-iid data generally.  
- Core learning task is ambiguous: unclear whether goal is change-point detection, inferring the current structure, or both, and the paper conflates these objectives.  
- Equation and formatting issues: definitions (e.g., ρ_{r+1}) are unclear and displayed equations lack proper punctuation.  
- Figures are hard to read: fonts too small and Figure 2 lacks a color legend, impairing interpretation.  
- Unsupported computational claim: statement that improved tracking compensates for computational cost is domain-dependent and unsubstantiated without a cost-benefit analysis.  
- Limited novelty: hypothesis-testing framework for structure learning is not new; claimed innovation in online p-value adaptation is not clearly articulated or justified.  
- Unclear advantage of proposed p-value construction: no argument or comparison showing superiority to alternatives like sliding-window sequential tests.  
- Lack of theoretical guarantees: no regret bounds or formal analysis for change-point detection or online structure estimation.  
- Weak empirical evaluation: metrics focus only on final-structure tracking, omit change-point performance, lack ablation studies, and provide no statistical significance tests.  
- Recommendation: substantial revision needed to clarify problem formulation, correct related-work claims, justify novelty, improve technical presentation, and strengthen theory and experiments.  
- Required revisions summarized: correct non-iid claim and cite Siracusa & Fisher (2009); explicitly define the learning objective; clarify the role/novelty of p-values; improve figures and Section 6; and strengthen theoretical and empirical significance.
