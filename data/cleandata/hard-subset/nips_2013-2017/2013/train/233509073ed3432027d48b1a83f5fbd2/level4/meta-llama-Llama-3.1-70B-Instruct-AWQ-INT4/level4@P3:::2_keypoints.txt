- Paper addresses tracking changing dependency structure in data streams and proposes LoSST, which combines batch SEM learning with streaming “surprise” statistics (Mahalanobis distance + Liptak pooling) to trigger relearning.
- LoSST is presented as an efficient, practical algorithm that creatively integrates multiple methods for the problem.
- Experiments on synthetic data (varying variables and max degree) and a US price-index real dataset are adequate and demonstrate detection of sustained changes while ignoring short-lived ones.
- The paper provides a solid literature review and is generally well-written.
- Main weakness: no theoretical guarantees or convergence analysis are provided, nor is future-theory work discussed in depth.
- Main weakness: heavy reliance on heuristics (reweighting, effective sample size, relearning rules) and use of methods outside original contexts (Hotelling’s T2 for p-values, Liptak pooling) without clear justification.
- Presentation issues: footnote proofs are unconventional and should be moved to supplementary material.
- Presentation issues: figures are too small, have poor spacing/labels/captions, and some experiment descriptions (e.g., top paragraph p.7) lack clarity and detail.
- Method explanation issues: unsatisfactory account of LoSST’s poor performance for edge additions under parameter-only changes and lack of discussion on decoupling parameter vs. structure updates.
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles an interesting, under-explored problem with a novel, practical, and efficient algorithm and reasonable experiments.
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks theoretical guarantees, depends on heuristics, and needs clearer presentation and explanations.
- Recommendation: paper has potential but requires revisions—add theoretical analysis, clarify algorithmic decisions, and improve presentation for stronger contribution.
