

Reaction to author feedback:  
I disagree with point (3). Siracusa and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) explicitly do not assume iid data and allow the generating structure to vary (within a constrained set of graphical models). While the proposed method offers greater flexibility, claiming it is the first to address this problem is misleading.  

The paper introduces a hypothesis-testing framework for learning graphical models in an online setting from time series data, targeting scenarios where the generating model changes relatively frequently. Performance is validated on both simulated and real datasets.  

**Quality**  
Results appear correct. However, Section 2’s related work mischaracterizes score-based Bayesian network learning. Such algorithms do not require iid data; they assume scoring functions factorize locally. Exchangeability (or iid assumptions) pertains to the *model*, not the *algorithm*.  

**Clarity**  
The paper’s presentation is generally sound but suffers from critical ambiguities:  
1.  **Learning task definition:** It remains unclear whether the goal is (a) inferring change points (under specific assumptions), (b) recovering the generating structure of the final observed data point, or (c) something else. This ambiguity undermines the problem formulation.  
2.  **Model vs. algorithm contribution:** The work’s novelty is obscured by uncertainty over whether it proposes a *new model* (redefining assumptions/goals) or a *new algorithm* (computational approach), or both.  
3.  **Technical presentation:** The equation defining $\rho_{r+1}$ (end of p. 3) is confusing—does it provide the definition, or is it derived from prior definitions? Displayed equations should end with periods when concluding sentences.  
4.  **Figures:** Font sizes in Figures 1–4 are too small (recommend doubling); Figures 1–2 require color legends for clarity.  
5.  **Section 6:** The claim that "improved tracking compensates for computational cost" is problematic. Compensation depends on domain-specific trade-offs between tracking accuracy and computational expense, which are not justified.  

**Originality**  
The work appears original but lacks substantive novelty. While the p-value construction may contain technical insight (which the reviewer could not fully follow), the core approach resembles existing hypothesis-testing frameworks. The paper does not convincingly establish a meaningful conceptual advance over prior work addressing non-stationary graphical models.  

**Significance**  
The potential impact is limited. If published, this work would likely spur *development* of heuristic, application-specific methods (e.g., Bayesian extensions) rather than *fundamental research*. The approach addresses a problem that standard machine learning tools (e.g., online Bayesian methods, changepoint detection) could arguably resolve with more rigorous modeling. The learning task itself remains poorly defined, and the method’s heuristic nature diminishes its broader scientific contribution.  

**Overall Recommendation**  
The paper presents an interesting application of hypothesis testing to non-stationary graphical model learning but suffers from significant flaws in clarity, problem definition, and novelty justification. Key issues include misrepresenting prior work, failing to clarify the learning task, and lacking a compelling argument for the method’s conceptual advance. While technically sound, the contribution does not meet NIPS’s bar for substantial theoretical or methodological innovation. The work leans toward engineering development rather than research.  

**Summary of Major Concerns**  
- Mischaracterization of Siracusa & Fisher (2009) and related work on iid assumptions.  
- Unclear problem statement (change-point inference vs. final structure recovery).  
- Ambiguity about whether the contribution is model or algorithmic.  
- Poorly defined technical details (equation $\rho_{r+1}$) and figure presentation.  
- Overstated novelty without clear conceptual differentiation from existing frameworks.  
- Limited significance due to heuristic approach and underdeveloped problem context.
