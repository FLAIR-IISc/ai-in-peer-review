**Paper Summary**

This paper addresses the interesting and under-explored problem of tracking changes in the dependency structure between variables within a data stream. The authors focus on developing a computationally and memory-efficient solution for Structural Equation Models (SEMs). They propose a new algorithm, LoSST, which employs a standard batch algorithm (the PC algorithm) for structure learning. LoSST monitors several statistics from the data stream to determine when to re-learn the structure. The monitored statistics include the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix (Eqs. 1, 2), and the weighted sample size.

A weighting scheme assigns a weight to each new data point based on its "surprisingness" (Eqs. 3, 4). This is quantified by computing the Mahalanobis distance (MD) for each point. The corresponding p-values, estimated using a Hotelling's T^2 distribution, are then aggregated using Liptak's method for weighted p-value pooling to produce an overall significance statistic, ρ. This statistic is used in a noisy-OR formulation with its value from the previous time step to decide whether to trigger a new structure learning phase.

The authors evaluate LoSST on synthetic datasets, comparing it against a standard batch PC algorithm. The experiments introduce either parameter changes or structural changes after a fixed number of data points and test various configurations of variable counts and maximum graph degrees. The paper concludes with an analysis of a real-world dataset: the US price index, comprising 529 monthly data points from 1967 onwards across six variables. The results show that LoSST identifies several known long-term changes in the US economy while ignoring short-lived fluctuations.

**Pros:**

1.  The paper addresses an important and under-explored problem. As the authors note, there is significant scope for developing practical solutions for SEMs and other models, as well as establishing corresponding theoretical guarantees.
2.  The proposed algorithm is novel, practical, and efficient. It creatively combines a variety of existing methods to achieve its goal.
3.  The experimental evaluation appears adequate, assessing the impact of various parameters and including a compelling real-world dataset.
4.  The authors provide a thorough review of related work.
5.  The paper is well-written overall.

**Cons:**

1.  A significant weakness is the lack of theoretical analysis or guarantees, even under simplifying assumptions. While the authors correctly identify a trade-off between "convergence and diligence" (p. 4), it seems that some formal guarantees under simplified conditions could be established. This should be highlighted as a key area for future work.
2.  The proposed LoSST algorithm relies heavily on heuristics. While this is acceptable for achieving a practical solution, the heuristic nature of the design choices should be more explicitly acknowledged and justified. The core heuristics relate not to tracking the mean and correlation matrix, which is straightforward, but to the schemes for re-weighting samples, calculating the effective sample size, and triggering the re-learning process. The authors creatively apply several statistical methods in contexts that likely violate the assumptions under which their theoretical guarantees hold.
    *   For instance, p-values for the Mahalanobis distance are estimated using Hotelling's T^2 distribution, where the sample size N is substituted with the effective sample size S^r. However, the T^2 distribution is derived for i.i.d. samples, and it is unclear if it remains valid for weighted samples as used here.
    *   Likewise, Liptak's method for pooling p-values assumes independent tests, a condition which may not be met in this streaming context. When using methods outside of their established contexts, the authors should clearly state the potential violation of assumptions to prevent misleading readers.

**Other Comments:**

*   Proofs and other details are outlined in footnotes. This content would be more appropriate for supplementary material.
*   The presentation of the results could be improved. The figures are too small, poorly labeled, and contain insufficient detail in captions and legends. Furthermore, the experimental description is at times unclear (e.g., the first paragraph on p. 7 is difficult to follow).
*   The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on the edge addition metric when only parameters are changed is unconvincing.
*   Related to the previous point, it seems desirable that the triggers for parameter updates and structure updates should be decoupled.

In summary, this paper presents a practical, albeit heuristic, implementation as a first attempt at solving an important problem, opening up an interesting direction for future research.
