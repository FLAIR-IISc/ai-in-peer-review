This manuscript presents a systematic approach to structural equation modeling (SEM) structure learning in non-stationary environments, specifically those where data are only locally independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.). This is an important and often overlooked problem, as many widely-used structure learning algorithms assume global i.i.d. conditions. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed techniques, which offer a simple yet effective extension of established methods – demonstrated here using the PC algorithm – to handle time-dependent data.

The primary strength of this work lies in its practical relevance and straightforward implementation. However, I have two suggestions that would strengthen the paper’s contribution and clarity. 

First, the statement in the experiments section that “any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly” appears overly strong. While the core idea of adapting algorithms to local i.i.d. assumptions is likely broadly applicable, significant differences often exist between various structure learning methods. To support this claim, the authors should either provide a more detailed justification for why similar performance is expected across different algorithms – explaining the underlying principles that lead to this expectation – or rephrase the statement to specifically focus on the results obtained with the PC algorithm and *speculate* on the potential for similar results with other methods. For example, the authors could state that the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of adapting the PC algorithm for time-dependent data and hypothesize that similar adaptations would benefit other algorithms, citing the common reliance on conditional independence tests.

Second, the discussion in the conclusion regarding the challenges of applying the same approach to Bayesian network structure learning – specifically the difficulty in maintaining sufficient statistics – is insightful. While not central to the paper's core contribution regarding SEMs, this observation highlights the potential complexities of generalizing the proposed methodology beyond SEMs. I believe the paper would benefit from acknowledging this limitation earlier in the manuscript, perhaps by framing the work more explicitly as addressing the problem of non-stationary data within the context of SEMs. The authors could then briefly mention the potential, but not necessarily straightforward, extension to other graphical models like Bayesian networks. This would sharpen the paper’s scope and provide a more accurate representation of the contribution.

In summary, this is a well-written and promising paper addressing a crucial issue in structure learning for SEMs under local i.i.d. assumptions. The formulation is accessible, and the experimental results, while focused on the PC method, are encouraging. Addressing the suggestions outlined above would further enhance the clarity and impact of the work.
