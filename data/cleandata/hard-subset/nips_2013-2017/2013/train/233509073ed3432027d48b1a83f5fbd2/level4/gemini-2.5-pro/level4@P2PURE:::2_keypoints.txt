- Tackles tracking changes in dependency structure in data streams for SEMs, proposing a computationally and memory-efficient solution (LoSST).
- LoSST uses the PC algorithm for batch structure learning and monitors weighted means, correlation matrix, and effective sample size to decide re-learning.
- New points are weighted by "surprisingness" via Mahalanobis distance; p-values are estimated with Hotelling’s T^2 and pooled using Liptak’s method to form a trigger statistic ρ.
- A noisy-OR combining current and previous ρ values determines when to trigger structure re-learning.
- Experiments on synthetic data (parameter and structural changes) and a real US price-index dataset show LoSST detects long-term structural changes while ignoring short fluctuations.
- Pro: Addresses an important, under-explored problem with scope for theoretical and practical advances.
- Pro: The algorithm is novel, practical, efficient, and creatively combines existing methods.
- Pro: Experimental evaluation is adequate and includes a compelling real-world dataset.
- Pro: Thorough related-work review and generally well-written paper.
- Con: Lacks theoretical analysis or guarantees even under simplifying assumptions; formal guarantees should be future work.
- Con: Heavy reliance on heuristics (re-weighting, effective sample size, trigger rules) that need clearer acknowledgment and justification.
- Con: Statistical assumptions may be violated—Hotelling’s T^2 applied to weighted samples and Liptak’s method assumes independent tests, which may not hold.
- Other: Proofs/details belong in supplementary material rather than footnotes.
- Other: Figures are small/poorly labeled and experimental descriptions are sometimes unclear; explanation for LoSST’s worse edge-addition performance is unconvincing.
- Other: Desirable to decouple triggers for parameter updates and structure updates.
