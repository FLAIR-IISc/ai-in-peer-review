Reaction to the author feedback:
I disagree with point (3): Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) do not assume iid data and allow the “generating structure” to change (albeit within a limited family of graphical models). While the present method may be more flexible in some respects, it is misleading to claim it is the first to address this problem.

The paper proposes a hypothesis-testing approach to learn graphical models online from time-series data, targeting scenarios where the data-generating graphical model may change relatively frequently. Performance is demonstrated on both simulated and real data.

Quality
The results appear correct. However, the discussion of related work (Section 2) is somewhat misleading: typical score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks do not assume that the data arise from a single generating model; rather, they assume the scoring function decomposes into local terms. It is true that the commonly studied models assume exchangeability (iid data), but that is an assumption about the model, not about the algorithm.

Clarity
The presentation is mostly adequate, but there is room for improvement. Most importantly, the paper is unclear about the precise learning task: is the goal to infer change points (under explicit assumptions about their existence and nature), to infer the generating structure at the most recent time point, or something else? It is also difficult to tell whether the contribution is a new model (assumptions and learning objectives), a new algorithm (computational procedures), or both.
The paragraph at the end of page 3 is hard to follow; for example, does the given equation for ρ_{r+1} define it, or was the definition provided earlier? Also, why do the authors omit periods at the end of sentences that conclude with displayed equations?
Figure readability should be improved: the font size in Figures 1–4 could be doubled, and a legend describing the colors in Figures 1 and 2 would help. In Section 6, the claim that improved tracking can compensate for additional computational cost is confusing—surely this trade-off depends on domain-specific valuations of tracking accuracy versus computational cost.

Originality
The work appears original, but it seems to lack a clear innovation or genuinely novel idea. Perhaps there is novelty in the construction of the p-values, but I was not able to fully follow that part.

Significance
If published, the likely significance would be to spur the rapid development of superior—presumably Bayesian—methods for this problem. That outcome would be better characterized as development rather than research. The current approach looks heuristic and targets a somewhat ill-defined learning problem that existing machine-learning methods should arguably already address.
