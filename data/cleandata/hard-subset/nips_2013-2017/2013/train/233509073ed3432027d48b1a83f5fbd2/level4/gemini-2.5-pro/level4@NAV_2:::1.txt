This paper introduces a heuristic-based method for learning graphical models from time-series data in an online fashion. The proposed algorithm, LoSST, is designed for scenarios where the underlying data-generating graphical model is subject to change over time. The method's efficacy is demonstrated through experiments on both simulated and real-world datasets, comparing its performance against a standard batch-mode learning algorithm.

**Quality**
The empirical results presented appear to be correct and support the paper's claims. However, the characterization of related work in Section 2 could be more precise. For instance, the claim that typical score-based learning algorithms assume data comes from a single generating model is not entirely accurate. These algorithms more fundamentally assume that the scoring function factorizes into local components. While commonly used models often assume exchangeable (i.i.d.) data, this is a property of the model being scored, not an inherent limitation of the scoring algorithm itself.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally well-written, but several areas could be improved for clarity.
- The precise learning task needs a clearer definition. Is the primary goal to infer changepoints, to learn the structure of the most recent data segment, or something else? A more formal problem statement would strengthen the paper.
- It is not always clear whether the contribution is a new statistical model for time-varying structures or a new computational algorithm for an existing model. Clarifying this distinction would be beneficial.
- The paragraph at the end of page 3, particularly the introduction of the equation for ρr+1, is difficult to parse. It is unclear if this is a definition or a derived result.
- Minor formatting suggestions: Sentences ending in a displayed equation should include a period for grammatical correctness. The font size in Figures 1-4 is too small to be easily legible and should be increased. Figures 1 and 2 would also benefit from a legend explaining the different colored lines.
- In Section 6, the suggestion that "improved tracking would compensate for the additional computational and memory cost" seems to depend on a domain-specific cost-benefit analysis. This point could be clarified by framing it as a trade-off that would need to be evaluated in the context of a particular application.

**Originality**
The work appears to be original. The specific combination of weighted sufficient statistics, p-value pooling, and a probabilistic relearning schedule for online structure learning is novel. The primary innovation seems to lie in the engineering of this specific heuristic framework rather than a new theoretical principle. The method for constructing the pooled p-values may contain a novel technical contribution, but the current description makes it difficult to fully assess.

**Significance**
The paper addresses the important and practical problem of learning graphical structures in dynamic, non-stationary environments. The proposed heuristic approach offers a pragmatic solution that performs well empirically. A limitation is that, as a heuristic, it lacks the formal guarantees that a more principled, model-based approach might provide. Nevertheless, this work makes a useful contribution by tackling a challenging problem and presenting a functional solution. It could serve as a valuable benchmark and inspire future research into more formal frameworks, such as Bayesian methods, for this problem domain.
