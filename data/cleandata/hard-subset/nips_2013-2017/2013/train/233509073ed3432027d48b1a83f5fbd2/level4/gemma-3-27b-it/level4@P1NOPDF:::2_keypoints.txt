- Paper tackles tracking changes in dependency structure in data streams and proposes LoSST for simpler SEMs.  
- LoSST combines the PC algorithm with streaming statistics: weighted means, correlation matrix, effective sample size, and surprise-based weighting.  
- Each point is scored by Mahalanobis distance, p-values (via Hotelling’s T²), pooled with Liptak’s method to produce a significance ρ, and a noisy-OR with the previous step triggers relearning.  
- Experiments evaluate LoSST on synthetic shocks to parameters/structure across variable counts and degrees, and on a 529-point US price-index real dataset.  
- Strength: addresses an interesting, underexplored problem with ample room for future theory and methods.  
- Strength: algorithm is novel, practical, and computationally/memory efficient, creatively combining multiple methods.  
- Strength: experiments are adequate and include a relevant real-world dataset.  
- Strength: paper provides a good literature review and is generally well written.  
- Major concern: no theoretical analysis or guarantees—convergence vs. diligence tradeoffs are noted but not formalized.  
- Major concern: heavy reliance on heuristics; key design choices (reweighting, effective sample size, relearn criteria) are not fully justified.  
- Methodological concern: use of Hotelling’s T² with effective sample size and Liptak pooling may violate the original assumptions (weighted/dependent samples), and this misuse should be explicitly acknowledged.  
- Presentation issues: proofs in footnotes are inappropriate—move details to supplementary material.  
- Presentation issues: figures are too small, sparsely labeled, and captions/legends lack detail; experimental description is sometimes unclear.  
- Additional critique: explanation for poorer performance on edge addition under parameter-only changes is unconvincing; decoupling parameter and structure updates seems desirable.  
- Overall: promising research direction and a practical heuristic first step, but needs clearer articulation of assumptions, limitations, and future theoretical work.
