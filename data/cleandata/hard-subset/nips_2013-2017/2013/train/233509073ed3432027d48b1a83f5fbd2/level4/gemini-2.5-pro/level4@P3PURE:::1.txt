**Reaction to the author feedback:**

I cannot agree with the authors' response regarding point (3). The work of Siracusa and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) does not assume i.i.d. data and explicitly allows the generating structure to vary, albeit within a small set of graphical models. While the proposed method may offer greater flexibility, it is misleading to claim it is the first to address this problem.

**Summary**

The paper presents a hypothesis-testing-based approach for learning graphical models from time series data in an online fashion. The proposed method is specifically targeted to scenarios where the data-generating graphical model may change frequently. The performance of the method is demonstrated using both simulated and real-world data.

**Quality**

The results appear to be correct. However, I find the description of related work in Section 2 somewhat misleading. The paper states that typical score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks assume that the data come from a single generating model. This is imprecise; rather, these algorithms typically assume the scoring function factorizes into local terms. While many commonly studied statistical models assume exchangeability (or i.i.d. data), this is a property of the model, not an inherent limitation of the score-based learning algorithm itself.

**Clarity**

While the paper is mostly well-written, there is significant room for improvement in clarity.

*   Most importantly, the paper is unclear about the precise learning task it aims to solve. Is the primary goal to infer change points (under well-formulated assumptions about their existence and nature), to infer the generating structure of the most recent data point, or something else?
*   It is also hard to tell whether the work is proposing a new model (i.e., new formulations, assumptions, and learning goals), a new algorithm (i.e., computational means to achieve these goals), or both.
*   The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow. For example, does the given equation for ρ_{r+1} serve as its definition, or has it been defined previously?
*   Minor stylistic issues detract from readability. For instance, the authors should use periods to end sentences that conclude with a displayed equation.
*   The figures are difficult to interpret. The font size in Figures 1-4 is too small and should be increased. Furthermore, Figures 1 and 2 need legends to describe the different colors used.
*   In Section 6, the claim that improved tracking can compensate for additional computational cost is questionable. This trade-off is highly domain-specific and depends on the relative valuation of tracking accuracy versus computational cost in a particular application.

**Originality**

The work appears original. However, it seems to lack a significant conceptual innovation or a core novel idea. The novelty may lie in the specific construction of the p-values, but I was unfortunately unable to follow this part of the derivation completely due to the presentation.

**Significance**

The paper addresses an important problem. However, the proposed solution appears to be a heuristic approach to a somewhat unclearly defined learning problem, for which existing machine learning machinery could likely be adapted. The potential significance of this work, if published, may be in stimulating the development of superior, perhaps Bayesian, methods for this problem. As it stands, the contribution feels more like an incremental development than fundamental research.
