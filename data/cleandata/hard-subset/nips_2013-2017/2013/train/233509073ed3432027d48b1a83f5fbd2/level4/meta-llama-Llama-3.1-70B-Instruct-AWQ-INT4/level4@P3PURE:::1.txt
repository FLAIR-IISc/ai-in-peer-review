Reaction to the author feedback:

I must respectfully disagree with point (3) of the author feedback. The work of Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) does not assume the data are independently and identically distributed (iid), and it allows the "generating structure" to vary, albeit within a small set of graphical models. Although the proposed method is more flexible in some aspects, I find it misleading to claim that it is the first to address this problem.

The paper presents a hypothesis-testing-based approach to learning graphical models in an online fashion from time series data. The proposed method is specifically designed for scenarios where the "data generating" graphical model may change relatively frequently. The performance of the method is demonstrated using both simulated and real data.

Quality:

The results presented in the paper appear to be correct. However, I find the description of related work in Section 2 to be somewhat misleading. Typical score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks do not assume that the data come from a single generating model; instead, they assume the scoring function factorizes into local terms. While it is true that typically studied models assume exchangeability (or iid data), this assumption concerns the model, not the algorithm.

Clarity:

The presentation of the paper is mostly clear, but there is room for improvement. A significant issue is that the paper is unclear about the actual learning task in question. Specifically, it is unclear whether the task is to infer change points (under well-formulated assumptions about the existence and nature of change points), infer the generating structure of the very last data point seen, or something else. Additionally, it is difficult to determine whether the work proposes a new model (i.e., formulations of assumptions and goals of learning) or a new algorithm (i.e., computational means to achieve the learning goals) or both.

The paragraph at the end of page 3 is challenging to follow, particularly the equation for \rho_{r+1}. It is unclear whether this equation defines \rho_{r+1} or if the definition has been provided earlier. I suggest that the authors use periods (.) to end sentences with displayed equations for clarity. Furthermore, the font size in Figures 1-4 is too small and could be doubled. Including descriptions for the different colors in Figures 1 and 2 would also improve clarity. In Section 6, I found the idea that improved tracking could compensate for the additional computational cost confusing, as this seems to depend on domain-specific valuations of tracking accuracy against computational cost.

Originality:

The work appears to be original, but it lacks real innovation or novel ideas. The construction of the p-values may contain some innovative elements, but I was unable to fully follow this aspect.

Significance:

The potential significance of this work, if published, lies in its potential to lead to the development of superior, presumably Bayesian, methods for the problem. However, this should be viewed as development rather than research. The approach presented in the paper appears to be heuristic and addresses a somewhat unclearly defined learning problem that existing machine learning machinery should already be able to solve.
