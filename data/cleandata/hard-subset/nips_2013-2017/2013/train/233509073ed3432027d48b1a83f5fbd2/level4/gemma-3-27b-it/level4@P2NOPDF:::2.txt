This paper addresses the compelling and relatively unexplored problem of tracking changes in the dependency structure between variables within a data stream. The authors focus on developing a computationally and memory-efficient solution for Structural Equation Models (SEM), introducing a novel algorithm called LoSST. LoSST leverages a standard batch learning algorithm – the PC algorithm (Spirtes et al., 2000) – for underlying structure learning and employs tracked statistics from the data stream to trigger re-learning. These statistics include the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix (Equations 1 & 2), and a weighted sample size. A key component is a weighting scheme for new data points based on their “surprisingness” (Equations 3 & 4), quantified using Mahalanobis distance (MD) and p-values estimated via Hotelling’s T<sup>2</sup> distribution, combined with Liptak’s weighted pooling of p-values to generate an overall significance statistic, ρ. This statistic, in conjunction with prior state, determines whether to initiate a new structure learning phase. The algorithm's performance is evaluated on synthetic datasets featuring both parameter and structural changes, varying the number of variables and maximum degree configurations. A limited analysis on a real-world dataset of US price indices (529 monthly data points from 1967 with six variables) demonstrates the algorithm's ability to detect known economic shifts while filtering out short-term fluctuations.

**Strengths:**

1.  The paper tackles a significant and under-investigated problem with considerable practical relevance. The authors rightly emphasize the need for further development in this area, including stronger theoretical foundations.
2.  LoSST is a novel and pragmatic algorithm, effectively integrating diverse methodologies.
3.  The experimental setup appears adequate for exploring the influence of various parameters and includes a valuable real-world case study.
4.  The authors provide a comprehensive review of related work.
5.  The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  A major limitation is the lack of theoretical guarantees. While the authors acknowledge the tension between convergence and diligence (p. 4), providing even limited guarantees under simplifying assumptions would significantly strengthen the work. This should be explicitly identified as a direction for future research.
2.  LoSST relies heavily on heuristics. While this allows for a practical solution, the rationale behind key decisions—particularly those regarding re-weighting, effective sample size determination, and re-learning triggers—should be more clearly articulated. The authors leverage established methods, like Hotelling’s T<sup>2</sup> and Liptak’s p-value pooling, in contexts that potentially violate their underlying assumptions. For example, applying Hotelling’s T<sup>2</sup> to a re-weighted, non-complete sample raises questions about the validity of the resulting p-values. Similarly, Liptak’s method assumes independent tests with varying sample sizes, an assumption that may not hold here. The authors should explicitly state when methods are used outside their established guarantees to prevent misinterpretation.
 
**Other Comments:**

*   The extensive use of footnotes for proofs is unconventional and detracts from readability. Utilizing supplementary materials would be more appropriate for detailed proofs and supporting information.
*   Figures are poorly presented: they are too small, unnecessarily spaced, lack clear labels, and have insufficient detail in their captions and legends. The description of the experiments, particularly the opening paragraph on page 7, is unclear and requires substantial improvement.
*   The explanation for LoSST’s poorer performance on edge addition when only parameters change is unconvincing.
*   Decoupling parameter updates from structural updates appears desirable and could potentially improve performance.

In conclusion, this is an interesting and promising initial exploration of a challenging problem. While the current implementation is largely heuristic, it represents a valuable first step and opens up exciting avenues for future research. The paper would benefit significantly from a discussion of theoretical limitations and a more transparent justification of the heuristic choices made within the algorithm.
