- Addresses an important problem: SEM structure learning under local i.i.d./time-varying data.  
- Method adapts constraint-based learning (PC) using local segments to keep CI tests valid in time-dependent settings.  
- Conceptually simple and easy to implement with standard CI testing machinery.  
- Empirical PC-based results show meaningful gains over time-agnostic baselines.  
- Paper is clearly written and easy to follow.  

- Claim of generality across algorithms is too strong; different methods may behave differently under shift and finite samples.  
- Recommend rephrasing generality claims or providing theoretical/empirical support (e.g., experiments with at least one alternative algorithm or argument about algorithmic properties).  
- State scope explicitly in the introduction: focus on SEMs and PC-style constraint-based learning under local i.i.d. assumptions.  
- Specify which components rely on linear-Gaussian SEMs or chosen CI tests versus what generalizes to other models.  
- Explain why extending to Bayesian networks is nontrivial (e.g., maintaining sufficient statistics, score decomposability issues).  

- Experimental clarity: detail how nonstationarity is introduced, segmentation/bandwidth, and hyperparameter selection.  
- Report sensitivity to segment length/window size and to misspecified change points.  
- Provide variability measures (confidence intervals/variance) and standard metrics (precision/recall, structural Hamming distance).  
- If feasible, add a small-scale comparison with a score-based method to contextualize generality claims.  
- Consider demonstrating on a real-world time-varying dataset.  
- Situate work relative to time-varying graphical models, dynamic causal discovery, and distribution-shift-aware CI testing.  
- Discuss computational overhead vs. standard PC (runtime, number of CI tests) and trade-offs between localization and statistical power.  
- Be consistent with terminology ("locally i.i.d." vs "piecewise stationary") and clarify assumption of abrupt vs gradual changes.  

- Soften or justify strong generalization language and fix minor phrasing/typos (e.g., “not straightforward”).  
- Clarify whether reported improvements are statistically significant.  

- Overall: a well-executed, timely contribution with clear methodology and promising results, but would be stronger with clearer scope, tempered generality claims, and additional experimental detail and robustness checks.
