Paper Summary:

The paper addresses an intriguing and under-explored problem of tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a stream of data points. The authors focus on developing an efficient solution for simpler Structural Equation Models (SEMs). They propose a novel algorithm, LoSST, which utilizes a standard batch learning algorithm for underlying structure learning (PC by Spirtes et al., 2000) and tracks several statistics from the data stream to determine whether to initiate new structure learning. These tracked statistics include the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix (Equations 1 and 2), and the weighted sample size. A weighting scheme is employed for each new point based on how "surprising" the new data point is (Equations 3 and 4). Each point's Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed, and the estimated p-values for the MDs by a Hotelling's T2 distribution are combined using Liptak's weighted pooling of p-values to compute an overall "significance" statistic ρ. This statistic is then used in a noisy OR configuration with the previous step to decide whether to start a new structure learning. The authors compare LoSST to vanilla PC batch learning on synthetic data sets where either a change in parameters or a change of structure is introduced after a fixed set of data points, testing for different numbers of variables and max degree configurations. They also perform a short analysis of real-life data from the US price index with 529 data points (every month, starting at 1967) and six variables, demonstrating that the algorithm detects some known changes/trends in the US economy while ignoring short-lived changes.

Pros:

1. The paper tackles an interesting and under-explored problem, as acknowledged by the authors, leaving room for additional development of practical solutions for these and other models, as well as matching theory and theoretical guarantees.
2. The LoSST algorithm is novel and practical/efficient, making creative use of various methods for this task.
3. The experiments seem adequate, attempting to track the effect of various parameters and including an interesting real-life data set.
4. The authors provide a good review of previous work on this problem.
5. The paper is overall well-written.

Cons:

1. The most significant limitation of the paper is the lack of theory or guarantees under some assumptions. While the authors acknowledge the trade-off between convergence and diligence, it seems plausible that some form of guarantees under simplifying conditions could be established. This should be mentioned as a direction for future work.
2. The LoSST solution relies heavily on heuristics, which, although enabling the authors to obtain a practical solution, should be more clearly noted. The decisions involved, such as reweighting, effective sample size, and when/how to relearn, should be explicitly stated. The authors creatively employ methods outside their original context, such as estimating MD p-values using Hotelling's T2 distribution with an effective sample size. It is essential to clearly state when methods are used outside their guarantees, ensuring readers understand the limitations.

Other Comments:

- The authors' use of footnotes to outline proofs is excessive and may be better suited for supplementary materials, along with other missing details.
- Figures are too small, with unnecessary space between them, missing labels, and missing details in the captions and legends. The description of experiments, such as the top paragraph on page 7, is not clear enough.
- The explanation for LoSST's poor performance on edge addition when only parameters are changed is unsatisfactory.
- It seems desirable to decouple sparking parameter updates and structure updates, which could be an interesting direction for future research.
- The paper presents a practical, albeit heuristic, implementation as a first attempt at addressing this problem, making it a promising starting point for further exploration.
