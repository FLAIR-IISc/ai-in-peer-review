Reaction to the author feedback:
I cannot agree with point (3). The work of Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) does not assume iid data, and it allows the “generating structure” to vary (albeit within a small set of graphical models). While the method proposed here is, in some sense, even more flexible, it is misleading to claim it is the first to address this problem.

The paper presents a hypothesis-testing-based approach to learning graphical models online from time-series data. The method targets scenarios where the data-generating graphical model may change relatively frequently. Its performance is demonstrated on both simulated and real data.

Quality
The results seem correct. However, I find the description of related work (Section 2) somewhat misleading: typical score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks do not assume the data come from a single generating model; rather, they assume the scoring function factorizes into local terms. It is true that commonly studied models assume exchangeability (iid data), but that concerns the model, not the algorithm.

Clarity
The presentation is mostly fine, but there is room for improvement. Most importantly, the paper is unclear about the precise learning task: Is it to infer change points (under well-formulated assumptions about their existence and nature), to infer the generating structure at the most recent time point, or something else? It is also hard to tell whether the contribution is a new model (i.e., a formulation of assumptions and learning goals), a new algorithm (i.e., computational means to achieve those goals), or both.

The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow; for example, does the displayed equation for ρ_{r+1} define it, or was the definition given earlier? The practice of omitting periods at the end of sentences that conclude with displayed equations further hurts readability. The font size in Figures 1–4 should be at least doubled, and I would appreciate legends describing the different colors in Figures 1 and 2. In Section 6, I am confused by the suggestion that improved tracking could compensate for the additional computational cost—doesn’t that depend on a domain-specific valuation of tracking accuracy versus computational cost?

Originality
The work appears original, yet it seems to lack a genuinely novel idea. Perhaps there is novelty in the construction of the p-values (which I was unfortunately not able to fully follow).

Significance
If published, the main impact of this work may be to prompt the development of superior, presumably Bayesian, methods for the problem. That should indeed be viewed more as development than research. Overall, this is a heuristic-looking approach to a somewhat unclearly defined learning problem that existing machine learning methods should already have solved.
