

This paper addresses an important gap in structure learning by proposing a method for Structural Equation Models (SEMs) under the realistic assumption that data are only locally i.i.d. (i.e., generated from distinct underlying models over time). The problem is well-motivated, as standard algorithms largely overlook time-dependent data, and the authors present a clear, implementable solution. The core contribution—adapting the PC algorithm to handle temporal shifts—is simple yet effective, with promising experimental results demonstrating its utility for SEMs.

The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it accessible to readers familiar with graphical models. However, two points require clarification to strengthen the contribution statement and scope:

1.  **Overstated Claim Regarding Algorithmic Generality:** The manuscript states that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly" when adapted to time-dependent data. This claim appears overstated. The performance differences between structure learning algorithms (e.g., PC, Grow-Shrink, MMHC) can be significant, especially under temporal shifts. The experiments focus solely on the PC algorithm (both time-independent and its adapted version). To avoid misleading readers, the text should be revised to clarify: (a) the results pertain specifically to the PC algorithm, or (b) if the authors intend to claim generality, they must provide a more rigorous justification or evidence supporting why the adaptation principle would extend broadly. The current phrasing risks overstating the paper's scope.

2.  **Clarifying Scope and Future Work:** The conclusion notes that extending the approach to Bayesian networks (BNs) "might not be so simple" due to challenges in maintaining sufficient statistics. While this is a valid point, it should be addressed earlier to avoid ambiguity about the paper's primary focus. The core contribution is specifically for SEMs, and the discussion of BNs as a potential extension (with caveats) should be positioned clearly from the outset. The paper would benefit from explicitly stating its scope: "This work focuses on adapting structure learning for SEMs under local i.i.d. assumptions. While the core idea may inspire extensions to other models like BNs, such adaptations would require non-trivial modifications, as discussed in Section 5." This sharpens the contribution and manages expectations, avoiding the impression that the method is broadly applicable without qualification.

The experimental section is sound but could briefly acknowledge the PC algorithm's specific strengths (e.g., its constraint-based nature) to contextualize why it was chosen as the primary test case. The results convincingly show the adapted method's advantage over naive time-independent approaches on synthetic and real data.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper making a valuable contribution to learning from time-varying data within the SEM framework. The proposed adaptation is conceptually clear and empirically supported. Addressing the two points above—refining the generality claim and explicitly framing the scope—would significantly strengthen the paper's clarity and impact. The authors' response to the initial review was thoughtful, and the revisions addressed the core concerns raised. The paper is recommended for acceptance.
