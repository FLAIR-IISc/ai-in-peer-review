

Reaction to author feedback:  
I disagree with point (3). The work of Siracusa and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) does not assume iid data and explicitly allows the "generating structure" to vary (albeit within a constrained set of graphical models). While the proposed method offers greater flexibility, it is misleading to claim it is the first to address this problem.  

The paper introduces a hypothesis-testing framework for learning graphical models in an online setting from time series data, specifically targeting scenarios where the underlying generating model changes relatively frequently. Performance is validated on both simulated and real datasets.  

**Quality**  
The results appear technically sound. However, the related work discussion (Section 2) is misleading. Typical score-based algorithms for Bayesian network learning do not require data to originate from a single generating model; instead, they assume the scoring function factorizes into local terms. While many standard models assume exchangeability (i.e., iid data), this is a modeling constraint—not an algorithmic assumption.  

**Clarity**  
The paper’s presentation is generally clear but requires refinement. Crucially, it remains ambiguous about the core learning task: is the goal to infer change points (under well-defined assumptions), to recover the generating structure of the most recent data point, or something else? Additionally, it is unclear whether the contribution lies in a novel *model* (formulating assumptions and learning goals), a novel *algorithm* (computational methods), or both.  

The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow. For instance, the equation defining $\rho_{r+1}$ appears to be introduced without prior context—is this definition new, or was it established earlier? The paper also omits periods after displayed equations (e.g., after $\rho_{r+1} = \ldots$), which disrupts readability. Further, Figures 1–4 require larger font sizes (doubling would improve legibility), and Figures 1–2 would benefit from color legends. In Section 6, the claim that "improved tracking compensates for computational cost" is problematic. This assertion depends on domain-specific trade-offs between tracking accuracy and computational cost, which are not adequately justified.  

**Originality**  
The work appears original but lacks substantial innovation. The novelty, if any, may reside in the p-value construction (which I could not fully follow). Without clearer exposition of this aspect, the contribution seems incremental.  

**Significance**  
If published, this work might spur the development of superior Bayesian methods for the problem. However, this represents methodological development rather than fundamental research. The approach is heuristic and targets a problem that existing machine learning frameworks could likely address more rigorously, given a clearer problem formulation. The significance is thus limited to practical implementation rather than theoretical advancement.
