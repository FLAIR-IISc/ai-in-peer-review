

This paper introduces LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a novel algorithm for learning graphical model structure in environments where the underlying generating process may change over time. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: most structure learning algorithms assume globally stationary data (i.i.d.), whereas real-world data often follows locally stationary processes where structure changes gradually or abruptly. The paper makes a clear contribution by extending structure learning to dynamic environments while maintaining comparable performance to batch methods in stable conditions.

The work builds upon previous NIPS contributions in graphical model learning (e.g., Chickering 2002, Spirtes et al. 2000) while addressing their fundamental limitation of assuming stationary data. The authors demonstrate how LoSST tracks changes in graphical structure through weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and effective sample size) and uses Mahalanobis distances to detect structural shifts. The probabilistic relearning mechanism balances convergence and diligence, addressing a fundamental tension in online learning algorithms (Kummerfeld & Danks, 2013).

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The algorithm description is precise, with well-explained update equations and intuitive justification for the weighted statistics approach. The authors effectively demonstrate how LoSST differs from both standard structure learning methods (which assume stationarity) and online changepoint detection approaches (which typically track parameters, not structure). The simulation framework is well-designed, comparing LoSST to batch-mode PC algorithm across multiple scenarios (structure changes, parameter changes, and combined changes) with appropriate statistical measures (edge addition/removal/orientation errors).

The quality of the work is strong, with thorough theoretical analysis of the algorithm's properties (convergence vs. diligence) and comprehensive experimental validation. The real-world application to US price index volatility demonstrates practical relevance, with LoSST correctly identifying the "Great Moderation" period without being misled by single-month volatility spikes. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations (e.g., performance degradation with very rapid structure changes) and future research directions.

The paper's main strength is its practical significance: it solves a genuine problem in real-world applications where batch learning is impossible. The algorithm's computational efficiency (single-pass processing) and minimal performance cost in stable environments make it particularly valuable for large-scale or streaming data applications. The authors convincingly argue that many real-world domains (financial markets, sensor networks, social systems) require exactly this type of online structure learning.

Two minor weaknesses warrant attention. First, the claim that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly" to PC is overgeneralized. While the PC algorithm is well-justified as the baseline, the paper would benefit from either more explicit justification for this claim or a brief discussion of how the algorithm might adapt to different learning methods (e.g., score-based approaches). Second, while the paper focuses on SEMs, the discussion of potential extensions to Bayesian networks (Section 6) could be more prominently featured in the introduction to better define the paper's scope. The authors should clarify whether the approach is specifically designed for SEMs or could be more broadly applicable with certain modifications.

The paper makes a clear and novel contribution to the NIPS literature. It advances the state of the art in structure learning by addressing the critical, underexplored problem of time-varying graphical models. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and has clear practical implications. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, with clear descriptions of both the algorithm and evaluation methodology.

Strengths:
- Solves a significant, underaddressed problem in graphical model learning
- Theoretical foundation addressing convergence/diligence trade-offs
- Comprehensive simulations across multiple change scenarios
- Practical real-world application demonstrating utility
- Clear, well-organized writing with precise algorithmic descriptions
- Minimal performance cost in stable environments

Weaknesses:
- Overgeneralization regarding applicability to other structure learning algorithms
- Scope could be more clearly defined in the introduction (SEM-specific vs. broader applicability)
- Limited discussion of computational complexity compared to batch methods

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound with strong theoretical and empirical support; it is exceptionally clear and well-organized; it makes a novel contribution by addressing a critical gap in the literature; and it has significant potential impact for real-world applications. The work represents a meaningful advancement in graphical model learning that will likely influence future research in dynamic structure learning.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's contribution is substantial, well-executed, and addresses a genuine limitation in current methodology. The minor weaknesses noted above are easily addressable in the camera-ready version and do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance. The work is particularly timely given the increasing prevalence of streaming data applications in machine learning.
