This paper introduces LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a novel heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from data streams where the underlying generative process may change over time. The core problem addressed is that most existing structure learning algorithms assume stationary data (i.e., data are i.i.d.), which is often not true in real-world settings. LoSST operates in an online fashion, processing each data point once. It works by tracking weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and effective sample size). The weight assigned to each new data point is dynamically adjusted based on how well it fits the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. A poor fit, indicated by a low pooled p-value over recent data, increases the weight of new data, allowing the algorithm to adapt to changes. Structure relearning is triggered probabilistically, with a higher probability of relearning after a suspected change. The authors contrast their approach with batch-mode learners, which fail in non-stationary environments, and with online changepoint detection algorithms, which often require storing significant data history. The method is evaluated on synthetic data, showing it performs comparably to a batch-mode algorithm (the PC algorithm) in stationary settings and significantly better when the underlying graph structure or parameters change. An application to U.S. price index data is also presented to demonstrate its real-world utility.

### Strengths

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem. The assumption of stationarity is a major limitation of many machine learning models, and developing robust online methods for non-stationary environments is an important research direction. This work directly addresses the need for structure learning algorithms that can operate in dynamic, real-time settings.

2.  **Novelty and Intuition:** The proposed LoSST algorithm is a novel synthesis of several well-known statistical concepts (online updates for sufficient statistics, Mahalanobis distance for outlier/change detection, and p-value pooling) into a coherent system for tracking graphical structure. The core idea of using data fit to dynamically re-weight data points is intuitive and clever.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The simulation results are thorough and effectively demonstrate the algorithm's key advantages. The comparison against a standard batch-mode learner clearly shows that LoSST successfully adapts to both abrupt structural and parametric changes, while incurring minimal performance cost in a stable environment. The inclusion of an experiment with probabilistic relearning (Figure 3) is a nice touch, showing that the triggering mechanism works as intended.

4.  **Conceptual Contribution:** The discussion of the trade-off between "convergence" (stability in the long run) and "diligence" (rapid response to change) is insightful. Framing the algorithm's behavior in terms of these competing desiderata and linking them to parameter settings (γ, T) adds depth to the contribution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of the Learning Task and Contribution:** The paper is somewhat imprecise about the formal learning objective. Is the goal to accurately identify the changepoints, to provide the best possible estimate of the graph G_r at the most recent time step *r*, or to maintain an accurate estimate over the entire time series? Without a formal problem definition, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed algorithm is a principled solution or a well-engineered heuristic. This also makes it unclear whether the primary contribution is a new statistical model for time-varying graphs or a novel algorithm for a loosely defined task.

2.  **Positioning with Respect to Related Work:** The claim that previous online methods are not "capable of general, graphical model structure learning" seems too strong. For instance, the work of Siracusa and Fisher (2009) presents a Bayesian approach for inferring time-varying directed graph structures. While LoSST differs in its frequentist approach and real-time capability, the problem it addresses is not entirely new. A more nuanced comparison highlighting the specific advantages of LoSST (e.g., computational efficiency, not needing to maintain a posterior over a super-exponential space) would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Heuristic Nature of the Algorithm:** Several key components of LoSST are based on heuristics. For example, the specific functional form for updating the weights `a_r+1` (Equation 4) and the noisy-OR gate for triggering relearning are choices that lack strong theoretical justification. While they appear to work well empirically, the paper would be stronger if it included an analysis of the algorithm's sensitivity to the crucial hyperparameters (T, γ, and ν). The values T=0.05 and γ=3 are described as being chosen via "unsystematic investigation," which undermines the robustness of the results.

4.  **Minor Clarity and Presentation Issues:**
    *   The presentation of the pooled p-value calculation (ρr+1) at the end of Section 3 is difficult to follow. The equation for ρr+1 appears without sufficient context or explanation of how it follows from Liptak's method.
    *   The font size in all figures is far too small, making them very difficult to read. Furthermore, Figures 1 and 2 are missing a legend to clarify which line corresponds to which 〈#variables, MaxDegree〉 pair.
    *   In Section 6, the statement that "improved tracking would compensate for the additional computational and memory cost" is vague. This trade-off is entirely dependent on the specific application's utility function, which is not discussed.

### Summary of Arguments

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper addresses an important, practical problem with a novel and intuitive online algorithm. The empirical evidence is strong and clearly demonstrates the method's effectiveness in its target scenario (non-stationary environments) without sacrificing performance in stationary ones. The conceptual framework of convergence vs. diligence is a valuable contribution.

*   **Con-acceptance:** The work's contribution is somewhat diminished by an imprecise problem definition and its heuristic nature. The claims regarding originality could be moderated and more carefully compared with existing literature. The presentation also has several clarity issues that need to be addressed, particularly regarding the mathematical details and figure quality.

Overall, I believe the strengths of this paper outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a useful and novel algorithm for an important problem. While the approach is heuristic, it is well-motivated and shown to be effective. I recommend acceptance, but strongly encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above, especially by clarifying the learning task, providing a more nuanced discussion of related work, and improving the clarity of the mathematical presentation and figures.
