*

The paper presents a hypothesis-testing-based approach to learning graphical models online from time series data. The method is specifically designed for scenarios where the underlying “data generating” graphical model may change frequently. The authors demonstrate the method’s performance using both simulated and real data.

Quality 
The results appear correct. However, I find the description of related work (in Section 2) somewhat misleading. Typical score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks do not assume the data originate from a single generating model; rather, they assume the scoring function factorizes into local terms. It is true that many standard models assume exchangeability (or iid data), but this applies to the model itself, not the algorithm. 

Clarity 
The presentation is generally good, but could be improved. The paper’s primary ambiguity lies in its definition of the learning task. Is the goal to infer change points (under well-defined assumptions about their existence and nature), to infer the generating structure of the most recent data point, or something else? Additionally, it’s unclear whether the work proposes a (new) model – that is, novel formulations of assumptions and learning goals – or a (new) algorithm – novel computational methods to achieve existing learning goals – or both.

The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow; for example, does the equation for ρ<sub>r+1</sub> define the variable, or has it been defined previously? I also note the authors’ omission of periods at the end of sentences containing displayed equations. An equally important issue is the font size in Figures 1-4, which is too small and should be doubled. Finally, providing descriptions for the different colors in Figures 1 and 2 would be helpful. In Section 6, I was confused by the suggestion that improved tracking could compensate for increased computational cost—doesn’t this depend on a domain-specific evaluation of tracking accuracy versus computational expense? 

Originality 
The work appears original, though it lacks substantial innovation or a truly novel idea. Perhaps the construction of the p-values represents a novel contribution, but I wasn’t able to follow the details completely. 

Significance 
The potential significance of this work (if published) lies in its potential to quickly stimulate the development of superior, likely Bayesian, methods for this problem. However, this feels more like development than fundamental research. The paper presents a heuristic approach to a somewhat unclearly defined learning problem that existing machine learning techniques should, in principle, already be capable of solving.
