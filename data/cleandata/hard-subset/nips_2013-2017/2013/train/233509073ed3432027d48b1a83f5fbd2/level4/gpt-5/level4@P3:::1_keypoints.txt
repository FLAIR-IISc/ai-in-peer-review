- Paper proposes LoSST: an online heuristic for tracking time-varying DAGs in linear Gaussian SEMs using streaming sufficient statistics, adaptive per-sample weights, pooled p-values (Lipták) and PC-based relearning.

- Claimed novelty: streaming, generic SEM support and pooled-p-value-driven adaptive forgetting/relearning; positioned against static structure learning, CPD, and time-varying graph work.

- Reviewer notes Siracusa & Fisher (2009) does model time-varying structure in batch, so the claim of being first is overstated.

- Strength: clear, practically motivated goal of online graph tracking without storing full data.

- Strength: simple, modular design—streaming statistics + adaptive forgetting + probabilistic relearning—that can pair with different structure learners.

- Strength: useful discussion of convergence vs. diligence trade-offs with parameter intuitions.

- Strength: empirical results show parity with batch PC under stationarity and improvements under structural/parametric shifts, including long-horizon experiments.

- Strength: real-data CPI case study aligns with macro phenomena and shows benefit of pooling moderate deviations.

- Major statistical concern: pooled p-value validity is unclear because Lipták’s method assumes weak independence, but p-values here are from overlapping, adaptively weighted estimates with complex dependence.

- Request to justify Hotelling’s T^2 use with estimated, time-varying covariance and the calibration of “effective” sample size; suggest simulation of Type I error under stationarity.

- Heuristic mapping from pooled p-value ρr to weight ar+1 (linear ramp to γar) lacks justification; ablation vs. exponential forgetting, sliding windows, and CPD-triggered resets is needed.

- Unclear specification of independence tests within PC using weighted sufficient statistics: how are thresholds adjusted for effective N and are partial correlations/test statistics calibrated?

- Computational/numerical concerns: online maintenance and inversion of Cr may be unstable with adaptive weights; need discussion of regularization, stability, and complexity (e.g., rank-one updates).

- Theoretical claims are sketchy; formal assumptions and precise theorems about convergence/diligence, dependence on ar and relearning policy would strengthen the paper.

- Clarity issues: learning objective not explicitly defined (current Gr vs. stable regime vs. CPD+recovery), affecting evaluation protocol.

- Exposition problems: ρr definition is hard to follow—need self-contained definition, T^2 assumptions, and clearer notation (define ηr and τr first).

- Figures need improvements: larger fonts, legends, consistent axes, and captions explaining the graph-relearning schedule.

- Originality: combination of streaming statistics with p-value pooling is a useful twist, but many components have precedents (exponential forgetting, sliding windows, CPD+reset, time-varying GGMs).

- Significance concern: evaluation limited to batch PC baseline; recommend adding realistic nonstationary baselines (sliding-window PC, PC with exponential forgetting, CPD+reset, time-varying GGMs).

- Recommend sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν and ablations on pooling/weighting and relearning components.

- Practical recommendations: calibrate pooled p-value false alarm rates under stationarity, detail how weighted covariances feed PC and adjust significance for effective N, and expand computational/stability details and per-step complexity.

- Pros for acceptance: timely problem, simple implementable method, demonstrated gains over naive batch learner, thoughtful trade-off discussion.

- Cons for acceptance: heuristic statistical elements insufficiently justified, ambiguous objective/evaluation, limited baselines, and overstated novelty.

- Overall assessment: promising and practical but currently too heuristic and under-evaluated; recommend rejection pending clarifications, stronger baselines, formal validation, and statistical justification.
