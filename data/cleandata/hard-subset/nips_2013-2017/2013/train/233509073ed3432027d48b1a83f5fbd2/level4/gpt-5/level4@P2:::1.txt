Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LoSST, an online method for learning and tracking time-varying graphical structure in linear SEMs (DAGs with Gaussian noise). The core idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an effective sample size) that adapt to potential changes in the data-generating process. Adaptation is driven by pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances (via Liptak’s method): unlikely recent data increase weights, shrink the effective sample size, and raise the probability of relearning the graph. Structure is relearned with the PC algorithm, either on a fixed schedule or probabilistically. Simulations show LoSST matches batch PC under stationarity and recovers more accurate graphs after changes; a CPI volatility case study illustrates behavior on real data.

The paper positions LoSST relative to batch structure learning (e.g., PC, score-based BN learning), changepoint detection, and prior work on time-varying graphs (Talih & Hengartner, 2005; Siracusa & Fisher, 2009). Importantly, Siracusa & Fisher (AISTATS 2009) do not assume iid data and allow the generating structure to vary across a restricted set; while LoSST is more flexible and single-pass, claims of being the first to address changing graphical structure are overstated. Also, typical score-based methods assume decomposability and are often applied under an iid model, but the single-model assumption is a property of the statistical model, not strictly of the algorithm.

Reaction to author feedback
I disagree with the assertion that Siracusa & Fisher assume iid data or a fixed structure. Their Bayesian approach explicitly models time-varying edges across a small set of candidate structures; thus, the paper should acknowledge this as closely related prior art. LoSST remains distinct in being frequentist, heuristic, and computationally light, but novelty claims should be moderated.

Strengths
- Addresses an important problem: online tracking of nonstationary graphical structures.
- Simple, single-pass updates for sufficient statistics; memory usage is modest.
- Intuitive mechanism linking goodness-of-fit (Mahalanobis distances) to adaptation via pooled p-values.
- Empirical results show parity with batch PC in stationary periods and improvement after changes.
- Real-data application demonstrates responsiveness to extended volatility shifts and robustness to isolated outliers.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- The methodology is largely heuristic. While the convergence/diligence discussion is helpful, theoretical guarantees about statistical error (false positives/negatives in edges) under adaptation are limited.
- Sensitivity analyses are thin. Performance likely depends on T, γ, ν and on relearning policy; more systematic exploration would strengthen claims.
- Baselines are limited. Comparisons only to batch PC (over mixed data) are favorable by construction post-changepoint. Additional baselines are needed: (a) sliding-window PC; (b) CPD plus per-segment learning; (c) Siracusa & Fisher (or a tractable approximation); (d) score-based methods with forgetting.
- Computational complexity is not quantified. Expected cost of probabilistic relearning matters in high-dimensional settings.

Clarity
- The learning objective is underspecified. Please clarify whether the goal is (i) change-point detection, (ii) estimating the current generating graph at time r, (iii) maintaining a time-indexed sequence of graphs, or some combination, and state the loss/utility implicitly optimized.
- It is unclear whether LoSST is a new model (assumptions about local stationarity and change dynamics) or a new algorithmic scheme for existing models; make this explicit.
- The definition of ρr+1 needs to be self-contained. As written, it is hard to tell whether the displayed equation introduces ρr+1 or depends on an earlier definition. Use clear, numbered definitions and punctuation after displayed equations.
- Figures 1–4: fonts are too small; consider doubling. Add legends explaining colors/lines in Figures 1–2. Report parameter settings (T, γ, ν) and any preprocessing.
- Section 6 suggests that better tracking might “compensate” for higher cost; this trade-off is domain dependent. Consider framing it as an application-specific cost–benefit decision.

Originality
- The combination of online sufficient-statistic tracking, pooled p-value weighting, and probabilistic relearning is a useful engineering contribution. However, related work on time-varying structure (Siracusa & Fisher; Talih & Hengartner) and standard streaming/forgetting strategies suggest that the conceptual novelty is incremental rather than fundamental.

Significance
- Practical significance could be high for streaming applications needing lightweight adaptation. However, without broader baselines and clearer problem definition, it is difficult to assess how much LoSST advances the state of the art versus providing a reasonable baseline that may motivate more principled Bayesian or penalized-likelihood approaches.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the task and evaluation metrics: what constitutes success at time r?
- Provide ablations and sensitivity analyses over T, γ, ν; report how often relearning occurs and the computational footprint.
- Add baselines: sliding-window PC; CPD+learning; score-based with forgetting; and, if feasible, a comparison to a simplified Siracusa & Fisher variant.
- Tighten the exposition around the pooled p-value construction and effective sample size update; add proofs or appendices as appropriate.
- Improve figures and captions; include legends and parameter settings.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a timely, practical problem with a simple, scalable method.
- Empirical results are promising and easy to reproduce in principle.
- The pooled p-value mechanism is a neat idea that could transfer to other online learners.

Arguments against acceptance
- Overstates novelty relative to prior work on time-varying structures.
- Problem statement and learning objective are not crisply defined.
- Limited theoretical guarantees and limited baselines leave impact uncertain.
- Presentation issues hinder reproducibility and understanding.

Overall recommendation
Leaning toward rejection in the current form. With clearer problem definition, moderated claims, stronger baselines, and improved exposition, this could become a valuable contribution as a practical baseline for online structure learning under local nonstationarity.
