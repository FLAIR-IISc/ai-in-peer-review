

The paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical structure in an online manner. The authors address the gap in existing methods that assume globally stationary environments, which is unrealistic for many real-world applications. They position their work as filling a critical need for algorithms that can learn graphical model structure in a dynamic, real-time fashion without requiring batch processing.

Quality: The paper presents a technically sound approach with thorough simulation studies demonstrating that LoSST performs comparably to batch-mode learning when the structure is globally stationary, and significantly better when it is only locally stationary. The algorithm correctly tracks changes in both structure and parameters of recursive SEMs. However, the related work section (Section 2) misrepresents standard score-based structure learning algorithms as assuming a single generating model, when in fact these algorithms assume the scoring function factorizes into local terms while typically assuming exchangeability (i.i.d. data) as part of the model, not the algorithm. The authors' claim that Siracusa and Fisher (2009) assumes data are i.i.d. is inaccurate; their method does allow the generating structure to vary, though it operates in batch mode. The theoretical analysis of convergence vs. diligence is solid, though the proof sketches could be more complete.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, but contains several points of confusion. The actual learning task is not clearly defined: is the goal to infer change points, the structure of the most recent data point, or something else? The distinction between proposing a new model (formulation of assumptions and learning goals) versus a new algorithm (computational means) is blurred. The equation for ρ_{r+1} on page 3 is particularly confusing without clear context, and the paper fails to use periods after displayed equations. The font sizes in Figures 1-4 are too small, and the color coding lacks descriptions, making it difficult to interpret the results. The discussion in Section 6 about improved tracking compensating for computational costs is overly speculative without domain-specific valuation criteria.

Originality: The core approach of using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values to weight data points for online structure learning appears novel. However, the paper lacks sufficient innovation in the technical details. While the authors claim their method is the first to address this problem, the work by Siracusa and Fisher (2009) already considered time-varying structures (though in batch mode), and the authors' claim that no prior work handles this problem is misleading. The method represents a heuristic extension rather than a fundamentally new approach.

Significance: The potential significance lies in enabling online graphical model learning for applications where batch processing is infeasible. However, the paper's claim that this work "would quickly lead to the development of superior, presumably Bayesian, methods" is overstated. The heuristic nature of LoSST, combined with the lack of theoretical guarantees beyond the convergence/diligence tradeoff, suggests this is more of a practical engineering contribution than a significant scientific advancement. The real-world application to US price index volatility is interesting but limited in scope.

Strengths:
- Addresses a genuine gap in online graphical model learning
- Strong simulation results demonstrating performance advantages over batch methods during structural changes
- Clear theoretical analysis of the convergence vs. diligence tradeoff
- Practical real-world application to economic data
- Well-structured algorithm with efficient sufficient statistics tracking

Weaknesses:
- Mischaracterization of related work (particularly Siracusa and Fisher 2009)
- Ambiguous statement of the learning task
- Insufficient novelty compared to existing work
- Theoretical claims not fully supported by detailed proofs
- Poorly presented figures with insufficient detail
- Overstated significance claims

Pro: The paper identifies an important problem in graphical model learning, presents a well-motivated algorithm, and demonstrates its practical utility with convincing simulations and a real-world application.

Con: The work lacks sufficient novelty to justify publication at NIPS. The core approach is heuristic rather than principled, the related work is misrepresented, and the theoretical contributions are limited. The significance is overstated given that the method is a practical extension rather than a fundamental advance.

Overall, while the paper addresses a relevant problem and demonstrates practical utility, it lacks the novelty and theoretical depth expected for NIPS. The mischaracterization of prior work is particularly concerning. I recommend rejection, as the paper would be better suited for a more application-focused venue.
