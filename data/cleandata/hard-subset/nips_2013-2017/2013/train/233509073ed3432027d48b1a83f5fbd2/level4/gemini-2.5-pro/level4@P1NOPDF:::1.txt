I disagree with the author's response regarding point (3). The work of Siracusa and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) does not assume that the data are i.i.d. and allows the generating structure to vary, albeit within a small set of graphical models. ---
The paper presents a hypothesis-testing approach for learning graphical models from time series data in an online fashion. The method is designed for scenarios where the data-generating graphical model may change frequently. The performance of the algorithm is demonstrated on both simulated and real-world data.

**Quality**

The technical results appear to be correct. However, I find the description of related work in Section 2 to be somewhat misleading. Score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks do not typically assume that the data originate from a single generating model; rather, the key assumption is that the scoring function factorizes into local terms. While many commonly studied models assume exchangeability (i.e., i.i.d. data), this is an assumption of the model, not a requirement of the algorithm itself.

**Clarity**

While the paper is generally well-written, there are several areas that could be improved. Most importantly, the paper needs to more clearly define the core learning objective. Is the goal to infer change points (under well-formulated assumptions about their existence and nature), to learn the structure corresponding to the most recent data point, or something else entirely? Furthermore, it is difficult to determine whether the primary contribution is a new probabilistic model for changing structures, a new algorithm for an existing task, or both.

The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow. For instance, is the equation for ρ_{r+1} a definition, or has it been defined previously? On a minor note, the authors should use periods to end sentences that conclude with a displayed equation.

The presentation of the figures also needs improvement. The font size in Figures 1-4 is too small to be legible and should be significantly increased. Additionally, Figures 1 and 2 are missing a legend or description to explain the color coding.

Finally, the argument in Section 6—that improved tracking can compensate for the additional computational cost—is confusing. This trade-off between tracking accuracy and computational cost is highly application-dependent and cannot be asserted universally.

**Originality**

The proposed approach appears to be original. However, the work seems to lack a core conceptual innovation or novel idea. The novelty may lie in the specific construction of the p-values, though I was unable to fully follow the details from the description provided.

**Significance**

The primary significance of this work, if published, may be in inspiring the development of more principled and superior methods for this problem, perhaps from a Bayesian perspective. In this sense, the contribution feels more like an incremental development than fundamental research. The paper presents a seemingly heuristic approach to a vaguely defined learning problem that appears to be solvable with existing machine learning frameworks.
