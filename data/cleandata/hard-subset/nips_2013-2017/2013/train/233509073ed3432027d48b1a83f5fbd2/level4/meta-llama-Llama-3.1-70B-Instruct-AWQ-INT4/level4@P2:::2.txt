**Paper Summary**

The paper addresses the problem of tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a stream of data points, focusing on efficient solutions for simpler Structural Equation Models (SEMs). The authors develop a novel algorithm, LoSST, which uses a standard batch learning algorithm (PC) for underlying structure learning and tracks several statistics from the data stream to decide whether to initiate new structure learning. These tracked statistics include the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix, and the weighted sample size. The sample weight is determined by a weighting scheme based on how "surprising" the new data point is. Each point's Mahalanobis distance is computed, and the estimated p-values for the Mahalanobis distances are combined using Liptak's weighted pooling of p-values to compute an overall "significance" statistic. This statistic is then used in a noisy OR configuration with the previous step to decide whether to start a new structure learning.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The authors provide a good review of previous work on this problem, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches. They note that standard graphical model structure learning algorithms assume a single generating structure and are not directly usable for learning when structure change is possible. They also discuss online learning methods that track environmental features, such as the TDL algorithm, and online changepoint detection algorithms, which are effective but have limitations.

**Strengths**

1. The paper tackles an interesting and under-explored problem, providing a novel and practical solution.
2. The algorithm makes creative use of various methods, demonstrating a good understanding of the problem and the relevant techniques.
3. The experiments are adequate, trying to track the effect of various parameters and including an interesting real-life data set.
4. The authors provide a good review of previous work on this problem.
5. The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper lacks theoretical guarantees or analysis under simplifying conditions, which would strengthen the results and provide a clearer understanding of the algorithm's behavior.
2. The solution is heavy on heuristics, which, while enabling a practical solution, may not provide the most robust or generalizable results. The decisions involved in the algorithm, such as reweighting, effective sample size, and relearning, could be more clearly noted and justified.
3. The authors use methods outside their original context or guarantees, such as estimating the Mahalanobis distance p-value using Hotelling's T2 distribution and using Liptak's weighted p-values. While creative, these choices should be clearly stated and justified.

**Other Comments**

* The use of footnotes to outline proofs is excessive and could be replaced with supplementary materials.
* The figures are too small, with unnecessary space between them, and lack clear labels, details in captions, and legends.
* The explanation of the experiments could be clearer, and the description of the results could be more detailed.
* The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on edge addition when only parameters are changed is not satisfactory.
* Decoupling parameter updates and structure updates could be a desirable direction for future research.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper addresses an interesting and under-explored problem, providing a novel and practical solution.
* The algorithm demonstrates good performance on synthetic and real-life data sets.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper lacks theoretical guarantees or analysis under simplifying conditions.
* The solution is heavy on heuristics, which may not provide the most robust or generalizable results.
* The use of methods outside their original context or guarantees could be problematic.

Overall, while the paper has some weaknesses, it provides a good starting point for addressing the problem of tracking changes in underlying dependency structures. With some revisions to address the weaknesses, the paper could be even stronger.
