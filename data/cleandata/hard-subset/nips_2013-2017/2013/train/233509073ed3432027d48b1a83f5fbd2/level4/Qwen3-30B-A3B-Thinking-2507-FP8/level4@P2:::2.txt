

This paper addresses an important gap in graphical model structure learning: the need for algorithms that can track changes in dependency structures over time in a streaming, real-time setting. While most existing methods assume globally stationary data generating processes, the authors correctly identify that real-world applications often involve locally stationary environments where structure changes can occur without warning. The paper introduces LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a novel heuristic algorithm designed to track graphical model structure changes in a dynamic, online fashion. The authors build upon the PC algorithm for structure learning and develop a sophisticated mechanism to track sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) while dynamically deciding when to relearn the graphical model based on Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values. The paper is well-motivated by a thorough review of related work, including online learning methods, changepoint detection, and previous approaches to time-varying graphical structures.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through comprehensive simulation studies. The authors evaluate LoSST under two distinct scenarios: when the underlying graph structure changes (with parameters fixed) and when only parameters change (with graph structure fixed). In both cases, LoSST significantly outperforms batch-mode learning after structure changes, while maintaining comparable performance during stable periods. The real-world application to US price index volatility is particularly compelling, as LoSST successfully identifies known economic shifts (e.g., the Great Moderation) while ignoring short-term fluctuations. The experimental design is thorough, testing multiple variable counts and graph complexities, and the results are clearly presented with appropriate error metrics.

The paper's originality is strong, as it tackles a problem that has received little attention in the literature despite its practical importance. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from previous work on online learning (which typically handles parameter changes but not structure changes) and batch-mode time-varying structure learning (which cannot operate in real-time). The algorithm's clever use of Mahalanobis distances, Liptak's weighted p-value pooling, and probabilistic relearning represents a novel combination of established techniques to address this specific challenge. The authors also provide a thoughtful discussion of the convergence-diligence tradeoff, which is a fundamental theoretical limitation of online structure learning algorithms.

The significance of this work is substantial. Many real-world applications—such as financial market analysis, sensor network monitoring, and biological systems—require tracking dynamic relationships between variables in real-time. The authors' algorithm provides a practical solution to this problem with minimal computational overhead compared to batch methods. The paper's careful treatment of the theoretical limitations (convergence vs. diligence) and clear exposition of the algorithm's operation make it valuable to both practitioners and researchers.

Strengths:
1. The problem is well-motivated and addresses a significant gap in the literature.
2. The algorithm is practical, efficient, and well-justified through comprehensive experiments.
3. The real-world application to US economic data demonstrates clear utility.
4. The paper provides a thorough review of related work and clearly positions its contribution.
5. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, with appropriate technical detail.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks theoretical guarantees for the algorithm's behavior. While the convergence-diligence tradeoff is discussed, the authors do not provide any formal analysis of performance under specific assumptions. A brief discussion of what guarantees might be possible under simplifying assumptions would strengthen the theoretical foundation.
2. Several heuristic choices are not well-justified. The use of Hotelling's T² distribution with effective sample size (rather than true sample size) and Liptak's p-value pooling (originally designed for independent meta-analyses) requires more careful justification. The authors should explicitly acknowledge these methodological departures from standard practice and discuss their potential impacts on performance.
3. Some experimental details are insufficiently explained. For instance, the explanation for why LoSST performs worse than batch learning on edge removal errors during parameter changes is unclear and seems to contradict the main intuition of the algorithm.
4. The presentation of figures needs improvement. The figures are small, lack clear labels, and have insufficient detail in captions, making it difficult to fully interpret the results.

The paper would benefit from addressing these weaknesses, particularly by adding a brief theoretical discussion and clarifying the methodological choices. The authors' suggestion to decouple parameter updates from structure updates is a promising direction for future work that could address some of the current limitations.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses an important practical problem with a novel and well-executed algorithm. While the theoretical foundation could be strengthened, the paper's practical utility, comprehensive evaluation, and clear presentation make it a valuable addition to the literature. The authors have made a significant step toward enabling real-time graphical model structure learning in dynamic environments, which has substantial potential for applications across multiple domains. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical and methodological clarifications.
