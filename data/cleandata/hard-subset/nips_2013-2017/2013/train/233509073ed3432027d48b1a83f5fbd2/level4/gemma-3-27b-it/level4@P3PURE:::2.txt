## Review of NIPS 2013-2017/2013 Submission

**Paper Summary:**

This paper addresses an interesting and relatively unexplored problem: tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a data stream (e.g., time-based). The authors focus on developing a computationally and memory-efficient solution for Structural Equation Models (SEMs). They introduce a novel algorithm, LoSST, which leverages a standard batch learning approach for structure learning (PC algorithm by Spirtes et al., 2000) and monitors statistics from the data stream to trigger re-learning of the structure. These tracked statistics include the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix (eq. 1, 2), and a weighted sample size. The weighting scheme assigns weights to new data points based on their “surprisingness” (Eq. 3, 4). Specifically, each point’s Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed, and estimated p-values for the MDs—derived using Hotelling’s T<sup>2</sup> distribution—are combined via Liptak’s weighted pooling of p-values to compute an overall “significance” statistic, ρ. This statistic, in conjunction with previous steps, determines whether to initiate a new structure learning phase. The authors evaluate LoSST against vanilla PC on synthetic datasets where either parameter changes or structural changes are introduced after a fixed number of data points, varying the number of variables and maximum degree. They also present a brief analysis of a real-world dataset comprising US price indices with 529 monthly data points (starting in 1967) across six variables. Their results demonstrate that the algorithm detects known economic changes while filtering out short-lived fluctuations.

**Pros:**

1. The paper tackles an interesting and under-explored problem. As the authors highlight, there is significant potential for further development of practical solutions for these and related models, along with the need for stronger theoretical foundations and guarantees.
2. The proposed algorithm, LoSST, is novel and offers a practical and efficient approach. It creatively integrates a variety of methods.
3. The experimental setup appears adequate for investigating the impact of various parameters and includes a relevant real-world dataset.
4. The authors provide a thorough review of existing work in this area.
5. The paper is generally well-written and clear.

**Cons:**

1. A significant limitation of the paper, given the complexity of the problem, is the lack of theoretical analysis or guarantees under specific assumptions. While the authors acknowledge the tension between convergence and diligence (p. 4), exploring potential guarantees even under simplifying conditions would be valuable. At least mentioning this as a direction for future work would strengthen the paper.
2. The LoSST solution relies heavily on heuristics. While this approach enables a practical implementation, the rationale behind key decisions should be more clearly articulated. Tracking the mean and correlation matrix is relatively straightforward; the critical choices concern reweighing, effective sample size determination, and the triggers for re-learning. The authors employ established methods in contexts that likely invalidate their original guarantees. For example, the Mahalanobis distance p-value is estimated using Hotelling’s T<sup>2</sup> distribution, which is intended for complete counts from a given distribution, not partial or re-weighted counts. Would the distribution still hold if sampling occurred from the networks/structures using these partial weights? Similarly, Liptak’s weighted p-values are designed for meta-analysis with independent tests and varying sample sizes. If these methods are applied outside of their guaranteed context, the authors should explicitly state this limitation to avoid misinterpretation.

**Other Comments:**

- The authors make extensive use of footnotes to present proofs, perhaps exceeding the intended scope of these features or conference guidelines. Consider utilizing supplementary material for this and other detailed information.
- Figures are too small and suffer from poor presentation—excessive spacing, missing labels, and insufficient detail in captions and legends. The description of the experiments lacks clarity (e.g., the top paragraph on p. 7 is difficult to interpret).
- The explanation for LoSST’s diminished performance on edge addition when only parameters change is unconvincing.
- Decoupling parameter updates from structure updates appears desirable, given the observations presented.

Overall, this is an interesting and promising direction for future research, offering a practical, albeit heuristic, initial implementation.
