- Tackles tracking changes in dependency structure of streaming data for SEMs with a focus on computational and memory efficiency.  
- Proposes LoSST: uses batch PC algorithm for structure learning and monitors stream statistics to decide when to relearn.  
- Tracks weighted means, correlation matrix, and weighted sample size to detect changes.  
- Weights new points by "surprisingness" via Mahalanobis distance and Hotelling's T2 p-values.  
- Combines p-values across variables using Liptak's weighted pooling to compute an overall significance ρ.  
- Uses a noisy-OR with the previous ρ to trigger structure relearning.  
- Evaluated on synthetic data with parameter/structure changes and varying graph sizes and degrees.  
- Applied to a 6-variable US price-index dataset and detected known long-term economic changes.  
- Strength: Addresses an interesting, underexplored problem with practical relevance.  
- Strength: LoSST is novel, practical, and computationally efficient, creatively combining statistical methods.  
- Strength: Experiments are adequate and include a real-world analysis; literature review is good and writing is clear.  
- Major weakness: No theoretical analysis or performance guarantees; needs at least discussion or future work.  
- Major weakness: Heavy reliance on heuristics (reweighting, effective sample size, trigger design) without clear justification.  
- Concern: Statistical methods are applied outside their assumptions (Hotelling's T2 for reweighted data; Liptak assumes independent tests), requiring explicit acknowledgement.  
- Presentation issues: Proofs in footnotes should be in supplementary material; figures are small/poorly formatted; experiment descriptions unclear.  
- Methodological issues: Unconvincing explanation for poorer performance on edge additions after parameter changes; triggers for parameter vs. structure updates should be decoupled.
