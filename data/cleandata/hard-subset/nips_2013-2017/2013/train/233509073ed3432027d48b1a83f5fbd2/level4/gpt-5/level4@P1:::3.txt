Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces LoSST, an online, heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of linear SEMs when the data are only locally stationary. Rather than assuming a single, globally stationary data-generating process, LoSST tracks sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an effective sample size) using adaptive, time-varying weights. The weights are driven by pooled p-values computed from Mahalanobis distances, which indicate lack of fit to the current model. Structure is relearned probabilistically using a standard constraint-based method (PC), with relearning frequency tied to the pooled p-values. The authors further analyze the trade-off between convergence and responsiveness (“diligence”) via parameters that control the weighting scheme (γ, T) and the relearning frequency (ν).

The paper positions itself relative to batch structure learning (both score-based and constraint-based) that assumes stationarity, online change-point detection (which often stores substantial history and assumes abrupt changes), and prior work on time-varying graphical structures that operates in batch mode (e.g., Talih & Hengartner; Siracusa & Fisher). Within this landscape, LoSST fills a clear gap: online learning of time-varying DAG/SEM structure via sufficient-statistic tracking and adaptive relearning.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important and understudied setting: structure learning under local stationarity.
- Methodologically simple, practical, and easy to implement; single-pass updates are appealing for streaming/large-scale data.
- Clear exposition; the derivations for online updates of weighted means, covariances, and effective sample size are helpful.
- Theoretical discussion of convergence vs. diligence provides valuable intuition about algorithmic behavior and parameter regimes.
- Experiments on synthetic data show LoSST matches batch PC under stationarity and outperforms PC after structural changes; probabilistic relearning substantially reduces computation without sacrificing accuracy. A real-data case study (U.S. price indices) is informative.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
1) Generality beyond PC: The manuscript states that “any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly.” This claim seems too strong without evidence, as different algorithms (e.g., alternative constraint-based procedures, score-based searches with different regularizers, or methods tailored to continuous vs. discrete variables) can behave quite differently, especially with finite samples and under nonstationarity. I suggest either (a) rephrasing to emphasize that the experiments are specifically with PC (time-independent PC versus LoSST+PC), and that similar behavior is plausible but unverified for other learners; or (b) providing arguments/experiments to justify why comparable performance would hold more broadly.

2) Scope and extensions: The conclusions note that extending the approach to Bayesian networks over discrete variables is nontrivial due to sufficient-statistics tracking. This is an important point. To set expectations appropriately, it would help to state clearly at the outset that the paper’s target is linear Gaussian SEMs/DAGs, with extensions to other graphical models being possible but not straightforward. Making this scope explicit earlier would strengthen the presentation.

Additional comments for clarity and completeness:
- Hyperparameters and sensitivity: The choices of T, γ, and ν materially affect convergence and responsiveness. Some guidance on selection (e.g., sensitivity analyses, heuristics, or rules of thumb) would aid practitioners.
- Statistical assumptions: The pooled p-value via Liptak’s method implicitly assumes certain independence/weighting properties. A brief discussion of when this pooling is reliable, given dependence in sequential statistics, would clarify applicability.
- Computational considerations: Since structure learning via PC can be expensive and is invoked repeatedly, a short discussion (or empirical report) of runtime, memory, and the impact of probabilistic relearning on computational cost would be valuable.
- Real data: The CPI application is compelling; adding a qualitative interpretation of the structural differences before/after detected shifts (even briefly) would increase the value of the case study.

Qualitative evaluation:
- Quality: Generally sound and well-supported by simulations and a real-data example. Theoretical discussion of convergence/diligence is a plus. Some claims (generality beyond PC) should be softened or justified.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. Clarifying the scope early and moderating the generality claim would improve it further.
- Originality: The combination of adaptive sufficient-statistic tracking with pooled p-values and probabilistic relearning for time-varying SEM structure appears novel and addresses an unmet need.
- Significance: Likely to be useful for practitioners dealing with streaming or nonstationary data and for researchers studying dynamic causal/graphical models.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Tackles a relevant and challenging problem neglected by standard methods.
- Simple, implementable approach with clear benefits under nonstationarity and no apparent loss under stationarity.
- Solid experimental evidence and useful theoretical insights.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is limited to PC; the claim of broader applicability is not substantiated.
- Scope regarding non-SEM models (e.g., discrete Bayesian networks) is acknowledged late; earlier clarity would help.
- Limited guidance on hyperparameter selection and computational trade-offs.
