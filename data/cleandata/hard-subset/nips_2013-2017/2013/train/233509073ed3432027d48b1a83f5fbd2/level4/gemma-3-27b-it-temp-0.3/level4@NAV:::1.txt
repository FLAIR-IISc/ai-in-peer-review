The paper introduces a hypothesis-testing framework for online learning of graphical models from time series data, specifically designed for scenarios with frequently changing underlying graphical structures. The method’s performance is evaluated through simulations and real-world data analysis.

**Quality**

The reported results appear to be accurate. However, the discussion of related work (Section 2) contains a potentially misleading characterization. Standard score-based algorithms for Bayesian network learning do not necessarily require the data to originate from a single generative model; they typically rely on scoring functions that decompose into local components. While many established models assume exchangeability or i.i.d. data, this is a property of the model itself, not the learning algorithm. Furthermore, I disagree with the authors’ assertion regarding novelty (point 3 in their response). The work of Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) does not necessitate i.i.d. data and accommodates variations in the underlying graphical structure, albeit within a defined set of graphical models. While the current method offers increased flexibility, it is inaccurate to claim it is the first to address this problem.

**Clarity**

The paper’s presentation is generally acceptable, but could benefit from improvements. A key area needing clarification is the precise learning task being addressed: is the goal to identify change points based on specific assumptions about their existence and nature, to infer the structure generating the most recent data, or something else entirely? It is also unclear whether the work primarily presents a novel model (defining assumptions and learning objectives) or a novel algorithm (computational methods to achieve those objectives), or a combination of both. 

The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to understand; for instance, it is unclear whether the equation for ρ<sub>r+1</sub> is being defined or referenced. The omission of periods at the end of sentences containing displayed equations is also a stylistic concern. Additionally, the font size in Figures 1-4 is too small and should be increased. Providing color descriptions for Figures 1 and 2 would also enhance readability. In Section 6, the claim that improved tracking can offset increased computational cost seems overly general and dependent on domain-specific trade-offs between tracking accuracy and computational resources.

**Originality**

The work appears original, but lacks a substantial breakthrough or novel idea. The construction of the p-values may contain a novel element, though I was unable to fully follow the details. 

**Significance**

If published, this work could potentially stimulate the development of more advanced, likely Bayesian, methods for this problem. However, this impact should be viewed as incremental development rather than fundamental research. The paper presents a heuristic approach to a learning problem that may already be addressable with existing machine learning techniques.
