The paper introduces a hypothesis-testing framework for online learning of graphical models from time series data, specifically designed for scenarios with frequently changing underlying graphical structures. The method’s performance is evaluated through simulations and analysis of real-world datasets.

**Quality**

The reported results appear to be accurate. However, the discussion of related work (Section 2) contains a potentially misleading characterization. Existing score-based algorithms for Bayesian network learning generally assume a factorization of the scoring function into local terms, rather than requiring data to originate from a single generative model. While many established models leverage exchangeability or the iid assumption, this applies to the model itself, not necessarily the learning algorithm.  Regarding a previous comment, I maintain that the work of Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) does not necessitate iid data and accommodates variations in the generating structure through a set of graphical models. While the current method offers increased flexibility, it is inaccurate to claim it is the first to address this problem.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written, but clarity could be enhanced. The core learning task remains somewhat ambiguous: is the goal to identify change points based on specific assumptions about their existence and nature, to infer the structure generating the most recent data, or something else? Furthermore, it is unclear whether the contribution lies in a novel model formulation (defining assumptions and learning goals) or a new algorithm (computational methods to achieve these goals), or both.

The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to parse; for instance, the equation for  \rho_{r+1}  is not clearly defined – is it being defined here, or has it been previously? The omission of periods at the end of sentences containing displayed equations is also a stylistic concern. The font size in Figures 1-4 is too small and should be increased.  Adding descriptions for the colors used in Figures 1 and 2 would also improve understanding. In Section 6, the assertion that improved tracking can offset increased computational cost seems contingent on domain-specific trade-offs between accuracy and computation. 

**Originality**

The work appears to be original, but the level of innovation is incremental. The novelty might reside in the construction of the p-values, although a full assessment is hindered by my difficulty following the details. 

**Significance**

If published, this work could stimulate the development of more advanced, likely Bayesian, methods for this problem.  However, this represents more of a developmental step than a fundamental research contribution. The presented approach—a heuristic applied to a learning problem that established machine learning techniques might already address—lacks a clearly defined objective.
