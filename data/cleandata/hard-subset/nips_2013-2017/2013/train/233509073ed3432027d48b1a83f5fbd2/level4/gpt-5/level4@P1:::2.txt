Paper summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses an important and under-explored problem: tracking changes in the dependency structure among variables in a data stream (e.g., over time). The authors propose LoSST, an efficient online algorithm for linear SEMs that detects and adapts to changes in either graph structure or parameters. LoSST maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an effective sample size) via online updates (Eqs. 1–3) and uses a data-driven weighting scheme (Eq. 4) that increases the influence of recent data when the current model poorly explains new observations.

Specifically, each new observation’s Mahalanobis distance under the current weighted mean and covariance is translated to a p-value using Hotelling’s T^2 (with the effective sample size substituted for N). These p-values are then pooled over time using Liptak’s weighted method to obtain a global “fit” statistic ρ. A probabilistic relearning trigger updates the probability of structure relearning via a noisy-OR that combines the previous probability with a term proportional to (1 − ρ). When triggered, LoSST relearns the graph using the PC algorithm.

The paper positions LoSST relative to (i) batch and constraint-based structure learning methods that assume stationarity; (ii) online learning approaches such as TD(0) that track slowly varying quantities but do not handle abrupt structure changes; (iii) Bayesian and frequentist online changepoint detection, which are effective for abrupt changes but require storing substantial history and assume piecewise stationarity; and (iv) prior work on time-varying graphs (e.g., Talih & Hengartner; Siracusa & Fisher) that relies on batch processing. LoSST offers a single-pass, real-time alternative tailored to locally stationary processes.

Experiments compare LoSST against batch PC on synthetic data with injected changepoints (in parameters only or in both structure and parameters) over varying numbers of variables and maximum degrees. A case study on U.S. price index data (monthly, 1967–2011) shows LoSST detecting known macroeconomic shifts while ignoring isolated spikes.

Strengths:
- Timely problem and clear motivation: handling structure drift is crucial in many real-world applications where batch learning is infeasible.
- Practical and efficient: the algorithm maintains only lightweight sufficient statistics and integrates with standard structure learners (PC here).
- Novel integration: creative combination of weighted statistics, Mahalanobis distances, pooled p-values, and a probabilistic relearning mechanism.
- Empirical evaluation: synthetic studies explore several regimes (structure vs. parameter changes; probabilistic vs. scheduled relearning), and the real-world example is compelling.
- Related work: solid and fair coverage of adjacent literatures.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Limited theory/guarantees: although Section 3.1 discusses a convergence–diligence trade-off and gives some conditions, formal guarantees are minimal. Given the importance of reliable online behavior, even under simplifying assumptions, stronger results would add significant value; at minimum, this should be emphasized as a key direction for future work.
- Heuristic components and assumptions: several design choices are heuristic, and some statistical tools are used outside their standard conditions. In particular:
  - Hotelling’s T^2 p-values are computed with an “effective” sample size from reweighted data, whereas T^2 is derived for i.i.d. samples with fixed N. It is not clear whether the stated distributional assumptions hold under the proposed weighting scheme.
  - Liptak’s weighted pooling assumes independent tests (and standard sampling assumptions). Here, p-values arise from overlapping, adaptively reweighted data streams, likely violating independence and calibration.
  These choices may well work in practice, but their limitations should be explicitly acknowledged to avoid giving readers a false sense of validity.
- Presentation and clarity: figures are too small, with excessive whitespace, missing labels, and sparse captions/legends; some experimental details (e.g., the setup in the top paragraph of p. 7) are hard to parse. The paper would benefit from clearer descriptions of datasets, parameter settings, relearning schedules, and metrics.
- Explanation of behavior under parameter-only changes: the provided rationale for the error profile when only parameters change is not fully convincing, particularly regarding why LoSST performs worse on edge addition in that regime. A more careful analysis would help disentangle the effects of shrinking effective sample size, hypothesis test thresholds, and PC’s sensitivity to transient correlations.
- Coupling of parameter and structure updates: it seems desirable to decouple parameter adaptation from structure relearning to avoid overreacting (or underreacting) when only parameters shift. This could reduce unnecessary relearning while still tracking changing coefficients.

Detailed suggestions:
- Theory: formalize conditions under which the Hotelling T^2 approximation with effective sample sizes is acceptable (or provide a calibration/correction), and characterize the impact of dependence among pooled p-values. Alternatively, consider surrogate fit metrics with clearer online guarantees.
- Diagnostics: report calibration of ρ (e.g., Q–Q plots under stationarity), sensitivity to γ, T, and ν, and ablations of the weighting scheme and pooling choice.
- Decoupling updates: evaluate a variant that distinguishes parameter drift from structural drift (e.g., via stability tests on adjacencies or intervention on PC’s conditioning sets) before triggering full relearning.
- Presentation: enlarge figures, add informative captions and legends, and move proof sketches from footnotes to a clearly marked supplement with missing details and pseudocode.

Overall assessment:
This is a compelling first step toward online structure learning for locally stationary processes. The approach is practical and shows promising empirical behavior in both synthetic and real-world settings. However, the paper currently leans heavily on heuristics and uses classical statistical tools outside their standard assumptions without sufficient caveats or validation. Strengthening the theoretical underpinnings (or, at least, explicitly acknowledging the limits of the approximations), improving clarity and figure quality, and analyzing the parameter-only change regime more carefully would considerably enhance the contribution.
