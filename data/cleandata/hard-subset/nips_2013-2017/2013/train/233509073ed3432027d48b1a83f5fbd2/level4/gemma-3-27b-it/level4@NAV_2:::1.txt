The paper presents a heuristic approach to learning graphical models in an online fashion from time series data, specifically targeting scenarios where the underlying generative graphical model may change. The performance of the method is demonstrated using both simulated and real data.

**Quality**
The results appear technically sound and well-supported by simulations. However, the discussion of related work (Section 2) contains a potential mischaracterization: standard score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks do not necessarily assume data originate from a single generating model; rather, the scoring function is typically factorized into local terms. While these algorithms often assume exchangeability or i.i.d. data, this applies to the model itself, not the algorithm's functionality. The claim of novelty should be carefully considered in light of Siracusa and Fisher III (AISTATS 2009), which also addresses non-iid data and varying generative structures, even if within a different framework.

**Clarity**
The paper's presentation is generally clear, but could benefit from refinement. A key area for improvement is a more precise articulation of the learning task: is the goal to detect change points under specific assumptions, to infer the structure generating the most recent data, or something else? Further clarification is needed regarding whether the work presents a novel model (defining assumptions and learning goals) or a novel algorithm (computational methods to achieve these goals), or both.

The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow, specifically regarding the definition of ρ<sub>r+1</sub>. The absence of periods at the end of sentences containing displayed equations is also an unusual stylistic choice. Crucially, the font size in Figures 1-4 is impractically small and should be increased significantly. Adding descriptions for the different colors used in Figures 1 and 2 would also enhance clarity. In Section 6, the statement that improved tracking could potentially offset additional computational cost seems overly dependent on domain-specific valuations and warrants further explanation.

**Originality**
The work appears original in its specific combination of techniques. However, the degree of innovation feels incremental rather than transformative. The construction of the p-values may represent a novel aspect, though a deeper understanding requires further scrutiny.

**Significance**
The potential significance of this work lies in its potential to spur the development of more sophisticated, potentially Bayesian, methods for this problem. However, the current approach – a heuristic applied to a relatively well-defined problem where existing machine learning tools might already offer solutions – positions this work more as development than fundamental research. The proposed method addresses an important problem, and the demonstration of its performance is valuable, but the leap to a demonstrably significant advance feels limited.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem – online structure learning in non-stationary environments.
*   The proposed heuristic demonstrates promising performance in simulations.
*   The real-data application provides some evidence of practical relevance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The claim of novelty is overstated, particularly regarding related work.
*   The clarity of the presentation could be improved, particularly regarding the precise learning task and the mathematical derivations.
*   The method lacks a strong theoretical foundation and feels incremental in its innovation.
*   The significance of the work, while potentially present, remains somewhat unclear.
