Paper Summary:
The paper addresses an interesting and underexplored problem: detecting changes in the dependency structure among variables in a data stream (e.g., time series). The authors focus on an efficient (computationally and memory-wise) solution for simpler SEMs and propose a new algorithm, LoSST. LoSST relies on a standard batch structure-learning method (PC; Spirtes et al., 2000) and maintains several streaming statistics to decide when to trigger a new round of structure learning. The tracked statistics include each variable’s distance from its weighted mean, the correlation matrix (Eqs. 1 and 2), and the weighted sample size. New samples are weighted based on how “surprising” they are (Eqs. 3 and 4). For each point, a Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed; the corresponding p-values, estimated via Hotelling’s T2 distribution, are then combined using Liptak’s weighted pooling of p-values to produce an overall “significance” statistic, rho. This statistic is then combined with the previous step via a noisy-OR to decide whether to initiate new structure learning. The authors compare LoSST to vanilla batch PC on synthetic datasets in which either parameters or structure change after a fixed number of observations, evaluating different numbers of variables and maximum-degree configurations. They also analyze a real-world US price index dataset with 529 monthly observations (starting in 1967) and six variables, showing that the algorithm flags some known economic shifts while ignoring short-lived fluctuations.

Pros:
1. The paper tackles an interesting, underexplored problem. As the authors note, there is substantial room for developing practical methods for these and related models, as well as for advancing theory and guarantees.
2. The algorithm is novel, practical, and efficient, making creative use of a range of techniques.
3. The experiments are adequate, exploring parameter effects and including an interesting real dataset.
4. The paper provides a solid review of prior work on the problem.
5. Overall, the paper is well written.

Cons:
1. The main shortcoming is the absence of theory or guarantees under stated assumptions. Convergence and diligence appear to be opposing forces, as the authors note (p. 4), but some form of guarantees under simplifying conditions seems plausible. At minimum, this should be highlighted as a direction for future work.
2. LoSST is heavily heuristic. That is acceptable for a practical solution, but the decision points should be stated more transparently. Since tracking the mean and correlation matrix is straightforward, the core choices concern reweighting, effective sample size, and when/how to relearn. The paper creatively applies methods under conditions that likely invalidate their standard guarantees. For example, MD p-values are estimated using Hotelling’s T2 distribution, with the sample size N set to the effective sample size S^r. However, Hotelling’s T2 is derived for complete (unweighted) samples from a distribution, not partial or reweighted counts. If one were to sample from these networks/structures with partial weights, would the resulting distribution still follow this form? Similarly, Liptak’s weighted p-values were designed for meta-analysis aggregating independent tests with differing sample sizes. If these tools are used outside their intended conditions, the paper should make that explicit so readers are not misled about their validity in this context.

Other comments:
- The paper uses footnotes to sketch proofs in a way that likely exceeds what the format intends. Consider moving these and other missing details to the supplementary material.
- Figures are too small, with excessive whitespace, missing labels, and insufficient caption and legend detail. The experimental description is not clear enough (e.g., the top paragraph on p. 7 is cryptic).
- The explanation for why LoSST underperforms on edge additions when only parameters change is not satisfactory.
- Relatedly, it seems desirable to decouple the triggering of parameter updates from structure updates.

Overall, this is a genuinely interesting research direction, with a practical—though heuristic—first attempt at a solution.
