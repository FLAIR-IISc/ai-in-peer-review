### Paper Summary

This paper tackles the interesting and under-explored problem of tracking changes in the dependency structure between variables within a data stream. The authors focus on developing an efficient (in terms of computation and memory) online solution for Structural Equation Models (SEMs), which they name LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker). The core idea is to use a standard batch-mode structure learning algorithm (the PC algorithm) but to trigger it dynamically. LoSST tracks several statistics from the data stream to decide when to re-learn the structure. These statistics include the weighted mean of each variable, the covariance matrix, and an effective sample size, all updated online.

A key component is the data weighting scheme, where each new data point's weight is determined by how "surprising" it is. The p-value for each point's Mahalanobis distance is estimated using a Hotelling's T-squared distribution. These p-values are then combined using Liptak's weighted pooling method to compute an overall significance statistic, ρ. This statistic is used in a noisy-OR configuration to probabilistically decide whether to initiate a new structure learning phase.

The authors compare LoSST to a standard batch-mode PC algorithm on synthetic datasets where either the model parameters or the full graph structure are changed after a fixed number of data points. The comparison is run across various configurations of variable counts and maximum node degrees. Finally, the authors apply their method to a real-world dataset of the US price index (1967-2011), showing that the algorithm detects known macroeconomic shifts while ignoring short-lived fluctuations.

### Strengths

1.  **Important Problem:** The paper addresses an interesting and important, yet under-explored, problem. As the authors point out, there is significant room for developing practical solutions for SEMs and other models, as well as for establishing corresponding theoretical guarantees.
2.  **Novel and Practical Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm is novel, practical, and efficient. It creatively combines several existing methods to solve the target problem.
3.  **Adequate Experiments:** The experiments are well-designed, exploring the effects of various parameters and including an interesting application to a real-world dataset that demonstrates the method's potential.
4.  **Good Context:** The paper includes a thorough review of related work, effectively positioning its contribution.
5.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary weakness is the lack of theoretical analysis or performance guarantees, even under simplifying assumptions. While the authors correctly identify the tension between convergence and diligence, it seems plausible that some form of guarantees could be derived under simplified conditions. This would be a valuable direction for future work.
2.  **Heuristic Nature:** The proposed solution, LoSST, is largely heuristic. While this is acceptable for a practical solution, the heuristic choices should be more clearly acknowledged and justified. The core novelties lie in the decisions about data re-weighting, the definition of effective sample size, and the trigger for re-learning. These components are based on a collection of methods used in ways that may not satisfy their original assumptions.
3.  **Use of Statistical Tools Outside of Assumed Context:** The authors make creative use of statistical methods, but potentially under conditions that violate their underlying assumptions, which could void their guarantees.
    *   For instance, the p-value of the Mahalanobis distance is estimated using Hotelling's T-squared distribution, with the sample size N set to the *effective* sample size S_r. However, this distribution is derived for i.i.d. samples with integer counts, not for the weighted data that results in a non-integer effective sample size.
    *   Similarly, Liptak's method for pooling p-values was developed for the meta-analysis of independent tests. This condition of independence may not hold here, as the p-values are derived from a sequential, dependent process.
    When using statistical tools outside of their original, guaranteed context, the authors should explicitly state this limitation so as not to mislead the reader.

### Other Comments

*   **Presentation of Proofs:** The use of footnotes to present proof sketches is unconventional. These details, along with other omitted information, would be better placed in a supplementary appendix.
*   **Figure Quality:** The figures are too small and poorly formatted. They have excessive white space between them and suffer from missing axis labels, incomplete captions, and unclear legends.
*   **Clarity of Experiments:** The description of the experimental setup could be improved. For example, the first paragraph on page 7, which describes the fixed relearning schedule used for the comparison, is difficult to understand.
*   **Unsatisfactory Explanation:** The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on edge removal errors immediately after a parameter-only change is not entirely convincing.
*   **Decoupling Updates:** Related to the previous point, it seems that decoupling the triggers for parameter updates and full structure updates could be a beneficial extension worth discussing.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a practical, albeit heuristic, first step in a very interesting and important research direction. The proposed method is novel and the experimental results are promising. However, the work would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the limitations imposed by its heuristic nature and the non-standard application of its statistical components.
