

Reaction to author feedback:  
I cannot concur with point (3). The work of Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) does not assume iid data and permits variation in the "data-generating structure" (even within a limited set of graphical models). While the proposed method offers greater flexibility, claiming it is the first to address this problem is misleading.  

Quality:  
Results appear correct. However, Section 2’s related work description is inaccurate: standard score-based Bayesian network learning algorithms do not assume data originate from a single generating model; they rely on factorized scoring functions. The assumption of exchangeability (or iid data) pertains to the model, not the algorithm.  

Clarity:  
The paper’s presentation is generally sound but requires refinement. Crucially, the core learning task is ambiguous: is it inferring change points (under defined assumptions), the structure of the final observed data point, or another objective? Additionally, it is unclear whether the contribution lies in a novel model (formulating assumptions/goals) or a novel algorithm (computational approach), or both. The paragraph on page 3 is difficult to follow (e.g., does the equation for $\rho_{r+1}$ define it or reference prior definition?). Displayed equations should end with periods. Figures 1–4 require doubled font sizes and color key descriptions (Figures 1–2). Section 6’s claim that improved tracking compensates for computational cost is domain-specific and lacks justification.  

Originality:  
The work appears original but lacks substantive innovation or novel concepts. The construction of p-values may contain an insight (though its details are unclear to me).  

Significance:  
The work’s potential impact is limited: it may spur heuristic development of Bayesian methods for this problem, but this constitutes applied development rather than research. The approach is heuristic, targets an ambiguously defined learning problem, and does not advance beyond existing machine learning frameworks.
