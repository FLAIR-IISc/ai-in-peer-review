This paper introduces a systematic approach for learning the structure of Structural Equation Models (SEMs) from data that are only locally independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.). The proposed method addresses the important and often-neglected challenge of learning from non-stationary data, where the underlying generative model may change over time. The manuscript is well-written and the core ideas are easy to follow. The techniques used to extend structure learning to time-dependent data are simple yet, according to the experimental results with the PC algorithm, effective.

While the paper is strong, two suggestions could further clarify and strengthen its contributions:

1.  **Generalizability of Experimental Results:** The paper asserts that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly." This claim seems too strong, as the performance differences between various structure learning algorithms can be considerable depending on the data characteristics. To improve the paper, I would recommend one of the following revisions:
    *   Rephrase the statement to be more specific to the experiments conducted. The authors could clarify that their results demonstrate the value of their adaptation for the PC algorithm and then speculate that similar performance gains might be achievable when applying the framework to other algorithms.
    *   Alternatively, if the authors wish to retain this strong claim, they should provide a more detailed theoretical or empirical justification for why the observed improvements are expected to be largely independent of the specific base algorithm used.

2.  **Clarity of Scope:** The conclusion discusses the difficulty of applying this framework to Bayesian Networks, noting challenges with maintaining sufficient statistics. This is an important and insightful point, but its placement at the end of the paper comes as a surprise. The paper would benefit from disclosing this limitation earlier to provide a clearer picture of its scope. I suggest framing the paper more directly in the introduction as a solution specifically for SEMs. While the authors can still mention that the core idea might be extensible to other model classes, they should also state upfront that such extensions are non-trivial and may require significant modifications. This would better manage reader expectations and sharpen the focus on the paper's core contribution to SEM structure learning.

In summary, this is a well-written paper presenting a valuable method for learning SEM structures from non-stationary data. The formulation is straightforward to understand and implement, and the experimental results are promising. Addressing the two points above—by tempering the claim about generalizability and clarifying the paper's scope from the beginning—would significantly enhance the manuscript's impact.
