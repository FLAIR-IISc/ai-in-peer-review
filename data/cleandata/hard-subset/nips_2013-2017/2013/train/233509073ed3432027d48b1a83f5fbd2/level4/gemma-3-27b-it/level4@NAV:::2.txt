## Review:

This paper addresses the compelling and relatively unexplored problem of tracking evolving dependencies between variables in data streams. The authors propose LoSST, a computationally and memory-efficient algorithm designed for Structural Equation Models (SEM). LoSST leverages a standard batch learning approach (PC algorithm by Spirtes et al., 2000) for structure learning, intelligently triggering re-learning based on monitoring statistics derived from the incoming data stream. These statistics include distances from weighted means, the correlation matrix (eq. 1,2), and a weighted sample size. A key component is a weighting scheme (Eq. 3,4) assigning weights to new data points based on their “surprisingness,” quantified via Mahalanobis distance (MD) and Hotelling’s T2 distribution-based p-values, combined using Liptak’s weighted pooling to produce a significance statistic ρ. This ρ value, used in conjunction with prior assessments, determines when to initiate a new structure learning phase. The algorithm’s performance is evaluated on synthetic datasets simulating parameter and structural changes, varying the number of variables and maximum degree, and on a real-world US price index dataset (529 monthly observations, six variables). Results demonstrate LoSST's ability to detect known economic shifts while filtering out transient fluctuations.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper investigates a significant and relatively untouched problem. The authors correctly highlight the need for practical solutions and theoretical advancement in this area.
2. LoSST presents a novel and pragmatic algorithm, creatively integrating diverse statistical techniques to address the challenge of dynamic structure learning.
3. The experimental setup appears reasonable, systematically exploring the influence of key parameters and featuring a relevant real-world application.
4. The authors provide a comprehensive overview of existing work related to the problem.
5. The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

1. A significant limitation is the lack of theoretical analysis or formal guarantees regarding the algorithm’s behavior. While acknowledging the trade-off between convergence and diligence (p. 4), exploring potential guarantees under simplifying assumptions—even as a direction for future work—would significantly strengthen the contribution.
2. LoSST relies heavily on heuristic choices. While this enables a practical solution, the rationale behind these decisions deserves greater transparency. Specifically, the weighting scheme, effective sample size calculations, and re-learning triggers require clearer justification. The authors employ statistical methods (e.g., Hotelling’s T2, Liptak’s pooling) potentially outside their established validity contexts. For instance, applying Hotelling’s T2 distribution to weighted samples raises questions about distributional assumptions. Similarly, Liptak’s method assumes independent tests.  A clear acknowledgement of these deviations from standard conditions is crucial to avoid misinterpretations.

**Other Comments:**

- The extensive use of footnotes for proofs is unconventional and may exceed conference formatting guidelines. Consider relocating this material to a supplementary document along with other omitted details.
- Figures are currently too small and lack clarity. Improve readability by increasing size, eliminating excess whitespace, adding comprehensive labels, and providing detailed captions and legends. The experimental descriptions, particularly the top paragraph on p. 7, require further clarification.
- The explanation for LoSST’s diminished performance on edge addition when only parameters change is insufficient. 
- Decoupling parameter updates from structural updates appears desirable and could improve performance.

In conclusion, this work presents an interesting and potentially impactful direction for research. The proposed LoSST algorithm offers a practical, though largely heuristic, first step towards addressing the challenging problem of tracking dynamic dependencies in data streams.
