

Paper Summary:
This paper addresses an important yet underexplored problem: tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a stream of data points, such as time series. The authors propose LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a novel heuristic algorithm for online graphical model structure learning that operates efficiently in terms of both computational and memory requirements. LoSST builds upon standard batch structure learning algorithms (specifically the PC algorithm by Spirtes et al., 2000) while tracking key sufficient statistics (weighted means, covariances, and effective sample size) from the data stream. The algorithm employs a clever weighting scheme where newer data points receive higher weights when they are "surprising" based on Mahalanobis distances, which are then combined using Liptak's weighted pooling of p-values to form a significance statistic ρ. This statistic determines when to initiate new structure learning through a probabilistic relearning mechanism. The authors evaluate LoSST through comprehensive simulations on synthetic data where either the graph structure or parameters change after a fixed number of data points, testing various configurations of variables and maximum degrees. Finally, they apply LoSST to real-world U.S. price index data (1967-2011, 6 variables, 529 monthly observations), demonstrating its ability to detect meaningful economic shifts while ignoring short-lived anomalies.

Strengths:
1. The paper tackles a genuinely important and underexplored problem with clear practical relevance. The authors convincingly argue that most existing structure learning algorithms assume global stationarity, which is rarely met in real-world applications.
2. The LoSST algorithm is both novel and practical. It successfully balances the need for real-time learning with the ability to maintain performance in stable environments (matching batch learning when no changes occur).
3. The experimental design is thorough, systematically varying key parameters and providing clear comparisons to batch learning. The real-world application to U.S. price index data is particularly compelling.
4. The literature review is comprehensive and well-structured, clearly establishing how LoSST differs from previous work on online learning, changepoint detection, and time-varying graphical models.
5. The paper is generally well-written, with clear explanations of the algorithm's components and rationale.

Weaknesses:
1. The most significant limitation is the lack of theoretical guarantees. While the authors acknowledge the tension between convergence and diligence (Section 3.1), they do not provide any formal analysis under simplifying assumptions. At minimum, they should discuss what theoretical guarantees might be achievable under specific conditions, as this would strengthen their methodological contribution.
2. The algorithm relies heavily on heuristics, which is acceptable for a practical solution, but the authors should more clearly document the rationale for their specific implementation choices. For instance, using Hotelling's T² distribution to estimate p-values for Mahalanobis distances with effective sample size Sʳ is problematic: this distribution assumes complete data from a single distribution, not weighted samples. Similarly, Liptak's method for pooling p-values was designed for meta-analysis with independent tests of different sample sizes, not for sequential data streams. The authors should explicitly acknowledge these methodological departures from standard assumptions.
3. The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on edge removal errors when only parameters change (rather than structure) is insufficient. The authors note that PC initially adds edges while LoSST becomes more cautious, but do not fully explain why this leads to higher edge removal errors for LoSST in the short term.

Other Comments:
- The authors' use of footnotes to outline proofs is unconventional for NIPS and would be better suited for supplementary material.
- The figures are poorly formatted (too small, with excessive spacing, missing labels, and unclear legends), which hinders interpretation of the results.
- The description of the third simulation (Section 4.3) is confusing in the manuscript, particularly the top paragraph on page 7.
- It would be valuable to decouple parameter updates from structure updates, as the current implementation ties these processes together, potentially contributing to the edge removal error issue.

Overall, this is an interesting and well-executed paper that addresses a significant gap in the literature. While the theoretical foundation is somewhat weak, the practical approach and compelling empirical results make this a strong candidate for publication. The authors have made a meaningful contribution to the growing area of time-varying graphical models, and their algorithm could be valuable for practitioners working with streaming data. With minor revisions to address the theoretical limitations and clarify certain implementation details, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS.
