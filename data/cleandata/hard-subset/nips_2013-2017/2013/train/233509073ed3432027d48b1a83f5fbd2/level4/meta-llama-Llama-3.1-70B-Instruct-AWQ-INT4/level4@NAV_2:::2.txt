The paper tackles an interesting and under-explored problem of tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a stream of data points. The authors focus on a computationally and memory-efficient solution for simpler Structural Equation Models (SEMs). They develop a new algorithm for this task, named Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST). LoSST uses a standard batch learning algorithm for the underlying structure learning (PC by Spirtes et al, 2000) and tracks several statistics from the stream of data to decide whether to initiate new structure learning.

The tracked statistics involve the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix, and the weighted sample size. For the sample weight, there is a weighting scheme for each new point based on how "surprising" the new data point is. Each point's Mahalanobis distance is computed, and the estimated p-values for the Mahalanobis distances by a Hotelling's T2 distribution are combined using Liptak's weighted pooling of p-values to compute an overall "significance" statistic. This statistic is then used in a noisy OR configuration with the previous step to decide whether to start a new structure learning.

The authors compare LoSST to vanilla PC batch learning on synthetic data sets where either a change in parameters or a change of structure is introduced after a fixed set of datapoints, testing for different number of variables and max degree configurations. They also perform a short analysis of real-life data from the US price index with 529 data points (every month, starting at 1967) and six variables. They show the algorithm detects some known changes/trends in the US economy while ignoring short-lived changes.

The paper relates to previous work on graphical models, online learning, and changepoint detection. The authors provide a good review of previous work on this problem, highlighting the limitations of existing methods and the need for a new approach. LoSST is a novel and practical algorithm that makes creative use of various methods to address the problem of tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure.

Strengths of the paper include its tackling of an interesting and under-explored problem, the development of a novel and practical algorithm, and the inclusion of adequate experiments to test the algorithm's performance. The authors also provide a good review of previous work on this problem. The paper is well-written, and the authors make a clear case for the importance of addressing this problem.

However, there are some weaknesses in the paper. One of the biggest lacks in the paper is the absence of theory or guarantees under some assumptions. Convergence and diligence are opposing forces, as the authors point out, but it seems like some form of guarantees under simplifying conditions should be plausible. At least, this could be mentioned as another direction for future work.

Another concern is that the solution suggested by the authors, LoSST, is heavy on the heuristic/hack side. While this enables the authors to get a practical solution, the decisions involved should be more clearly noted. For example, the authors estimate the Mahalanobis distance p-value using Hotelling's T2 distribution, but they set the sample size N to be the effective sample size S^r. However, this distribution was developed for complete counts from a given distribution, not for partial/reweighted counts. Similarly, Liptak's weighted p-values were developed for meta-analysis using independent tests with different sample sizes. At least, if the methods are used outside their guarantees/context, the authors should clearly state that, so readers don't get the wrong impression/understanding.

Other comments include the use of footnotes to outline proofs, which could be better presented in supplementary materials. The figures are too small, with unnecessary space between them, missing labels, missing details in the captions, legends, etc. The description of the experiments is not clear enough, and the explanation of why LoSST does worse on edge addition when only the parameters are changed is not satisfactory. It seems desirable that sparking parameters update and structure updates should be decoupled.

In conclusion, the paper presents a novel and practical algorithm for tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a stream of data points. While there are some weaknesses in the paper, including the lack of theory or guarantees and the heavy reliance on heuristics, the authors provide a good review of previous work and adequate experiments to test the algorithm's performance. The paper is well-written, and the authors make a clear case for the importance of addressing this problem. The algorithm has great potential to perform well in a range of challenging contexts, and at little cost in "traditional" settings.
