Summary
The paper introduces LoSST, an online algorithm for learning and tracking time-varying graphical structures, with a focus on linear SEMs over continuous variables. Unlike most structure learning methods that assume global stationarity, LoSST assumes only local stationarity: within short intervals data are i.i.d., but the generating SEM (both graph and parameters) may change over time. The core idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an effective sample size) updated in a single pass. Weights adapt based on model fit measured via the Mahalanobis distance and a pooled p-value (Liptak’s method) over recent discrepancies; poor fit increases weights so that recent data influence the model more strongly. Structure is relearned probabilistically (more frequently when the pooled p-value is low) using the PC algorithm. The paper discusses trade-offs between convergence and diligence (rapid change responsiveness), providing conditions under which LoSST exhibits each behavior.

Related to prior work, LoSST differs from: (i) batch constraint- and score-based learning [e.g., Spirtes et al. (2000); Chickering (2002)], which assume stationarity; (ii) temporal-difference-style trackers [Sutton (1988)] that adapt scalar expectations but not graph structure; (iii) online changepoint detection [Adams & MacKay (2007)], which typically assumes abrupt shifts and requires storing many data; and (iv) batch time-varying graphical model approaches [Talih & Hengartner (2005); Siracusa & Fisher (2009)] that are not online and either restrict change types or rely on Bayesian posteriors over edges. LoSST’s contribution is a practical, heuristic, single-pass procedure that combines adaptive forgetting with standard structure learning.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The update rules for weighted means, covariances, and effective sample size are clearly derived, and the use of Mahalanobis distance with Hotelling’s T2 and Liptak pooling is reasonable. The diligence–convergence discussion is thoughtful, though proofs are mostly sketched. The experimental design is sensible and demonstrates that LoSST matches batch PC under stationarity and outperforms it after changes.
- Areas needing clarification:
  - Validity of the Hotelling’s T2 approximation with an “effective” sample size that may be non-integer and time-varying; please justify this approximation or discuss its limits.
  - Independence assumptions in Liptak’s pooling: pooled p-values typically assume independence or known dependence; here, p-values are derived from overlapping, adaptively weighted statistics. A brief discussion of robustness to dependence would strengthen the approach.
  - Computational aspects: Mahalanobis distance requires the inverse covariance; do you maintain (and update) the inverse online (e.g., via Sherman–Morrison) and handle regularization when covariances are near singular? A short note on complexity and numerical stability would be helpful.
  - CI testing within PC: please specify how conditional independence tests are implemented with weighted covariances/effective sample sizes and whether test thresholds are adjusted.
  - Baselines: the main comparison is against batch PC on the entire dataset, which is a tough setting for PC post-change. Stronger baselines would include sliding-window PC, PC with exponential forgetting, or CPD plus re-estimation. Even a brief comparison or discussion would contextualize gains.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. Two presentation edits would improve precision:
- Scope: State early and explicitly that the main contributions target linear SEMs with Gaussian noise. While extensions to other graphical models may be feasible, the paper’s methods and guarantees are tailored to this setting; deferring broader claims to future work will sharpen the scope.
- Claims about generality across structure learners: The text asserts that “any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly.” This is too strong without evidence; please restrict claims to PC (as implemented) and frame extension to other learners as a plausible conjecture, with rationale (e.g., reliance on the same sufficient statistics) and caveats.

Originality
The combination of online sufficient-statistics tracking, pooled p-value–based adaptive weighting, and probabilistic relearning for structure discovery in nonstationary settings is, to my knowledge, novel and practical. The explicit framing of diligence versus convergence is also insightful.

Significance
Handling nonstationarity is essential for real-world applications. LoSST’s single-pass nature, ability to recover batch performance under stationarity, and improved performance after changes make it potentially impactful. The real-data CPI case study is illustrative, though limited in scope. Broader empirical validation (different domains, larger graphs, gradual drifts) and stronger baselines would further establish significance.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and under-explored problem: online structure learning under nonstationarity.
- Simple, implementable updates with clear intuition (adaptive forgetting via pooled p-values).
- Strong performance under stationarity and after changes in synthetic experiments; reasonable real-data demonstration.
- Thoughtful discussion of convergence vs. diligence and parameter regimes.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Overgeneral claim about applicability to other structure learners; rephrase and justify.
- Scope should be clearly limited to SEMs with Gaussian noise; temper claims about Bayesian networks and discrete models.
- Statistical assumptions behind pooled p-values and Hotelling’s T2 under adaptive weighting need discussion.
- Missing stronger baselines (sliding windows, forgetting PC, CPD+relearn).
- Limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (T, γ, ν); consider an ablation and guidance for selection.
- Computational details (matrix inverses, regularization, complexity) and CI test specifications should be added.
- Robustness to outliers and heavy-tailed noise: since Mahalanobis distances drive adaptation, consider robust alternatives or diagnostics.

Pro acceptance
- Clear and practical method tackling a significant problem.
- Conceptually novel combination of ideas with empirical support.
- Matches batch performance when stationary; improves markedly after changes.
- Single-pass, memory-light design is attractive for streaming settings.

Con acceptance
- Empirical evaluation would benefit from stronger baselines and sensitivity analyses.
- Some statistical and computational details require clarification to assess validity and reproducibility.
- Scope and generality claims should be sharpened.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a useful and well-motivated approach to online structure learning under local stationarity, with promising results and clear exposition. Addressing the clarity points (scope and claims), adding methodological detail (testing, numerics), and, if possible, including a simple sliding-window or forgetting baseline would substantially strengthen the submission.
