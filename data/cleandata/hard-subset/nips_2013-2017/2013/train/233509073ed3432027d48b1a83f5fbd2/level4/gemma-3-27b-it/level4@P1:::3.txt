This manuscript presents a systematic approach to adapting structure learning for Structural Equation Models (SEMs) to handle datasets that are only locally i.i.d., meaning the data may be generated from distinct underlying models over time. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, and addresses an important problem: learning from time-dependent data, a scenario often neglected by standard algorithms for structure learning. The techniques proposed to extend learning to time-dependent data are simple yet effective, as demonstrated by the experiments using the PC algorithm.

There are two suggestions that could clarify the paper’s contributions. First, in the experiments, the statement that “any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly” feels too strong. Differences among structure learning methods can be considerable. It would be better to rephrase this, clarifying that the experiments focus on the PC method in both time-independent and time-dependent adaptations – which is a valuable contribution – and then speculate on the likely behavior of other methods. Alternatively, one could retain the assertion but provide a detailed explanation justifying why that would be the case.

Second, the discussion in the conclusions notes that applying the same ideas to learn the structure of Bayesian networks might be more challenging due to difficulties in maintaining sufficient statistics. While this problem isn’t central to the paper’s proposal, it highlights that extending the learning procedure to Bayesian networks and other models might require significant modifications. Therefore, I believe the paper would benefit from explicitly stating this from the outset, perhaps framing the paper more directly around its goal for SEM models while acknowledging the possibility – but not guaranteed straightforwardness – of extensions to other models. In short, the paper could be slightly more focused in its scope.

This is a well-written paper concerning learning graph structure for SEMs under the assumption that data are only locally i.i.d. The formulation is straightforward to understand and implement, and the experimental results are promising, even if focused primarily on a comparison using the PC method. I acknowledge having read the feedback from the authors. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and previously under-addressed problem in graphical model learning – adapting to non-stationary environments.
*   Proposes a simple and intuitive algorithm (LoSST) that appears to be effective in tracking changes in graphical structure.
*   Provides convincing simulation results demonstrating the algorithm’s performance, both in stationary and non-stationary settings.
*   Offers insights into the trade-offs between convergence and diligence in the algorithm’s behavior.
*   Demonstrates applicability to real-world data with a compelling case study on U.S. price index volatility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The claim of generalizability to other structure learning algorithms (beyond PC) could be overstated and requires further justification.
*   The paper's scope could be more clearly defined, acknowledging the challenges of extending the approach to other model types (e.g., Bayesian networks) upfront.
*   The discussion of convergence vs. diligence, while interesting, could be further developed with more rigorous theoretical analysis.
