Review- Paper Summary:
The paper addresses a compelling and relatively understudied problem: monitoring changes in the latent dependency structure among variables in a data stream (e.g., time-indexed observations). The authors target a computationally and memory-efficient approach for simpler SEMs and introduce a new algorithm, LoSST. LoSST relies on a standard batch structure learner (the PC algorithm; Spirtes et al., 2000) and maintains several streaming statistics to decide when to trigger fresh structure learning. Tracked quantities include deviations from the weighted mean for each variable, the correlation matrix (Eqs. 1–2), and the weighted sample size. Incoming samples are weighted according to how “surprising” they are (Eqs. 3–4). Each point’s Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed; p-values for these MDs are estimated via a Hotelling’s T2 distribution and then combined using Liptak’s weighted pooling of p-values to yield an overall “significance” statistic rho. This statistic is then combined with the previous step via a noisy OR to determine whether to initiate new structure learning. The authors compare against vanilla batch PC on synthetic datasets where either parameters or structure are altered after a fixed number of observations, exploring different numbers of variables and maximum-degree settings. They also analyze a real U.S. price index dataset with 529 monthly observations (starting in 1967) across six variables, showing that the algorithm identifies known macroeconomic shifts while ignoring short-lived perturbations.

Pros:
1. The paper tackles a timely and underexplored problem. As the authors note, there is substantial room for further practical methods for these and related models, as well as for matching theory and guarantees.
2. The proposed algorithm is novel and practical/efficient, creatively integrating multiple techniques to achieve this.
3. The experiments are generally appropriate, probing the impact of various parameters and including an interesting real-world dataset.
4. The related work section provides a solid overview of prior efforts on this problem.
5. The manuscript is overall clearly written.

Cons:
1. The most significant shortcoming, given the importance of the problem, is the absence of theory or guarantees under stated assumptions. As the authors point out (p. 4), convergence and vigilance are in tension, but some form of guarantees under simplifying conditions seems plausible; at minimum, this should be highlighted as a direction for future work.
2. The proposed approach, LoSST, leans heavily on heuristics. This is acceptable and enables a practical solution, but the decision points should be made more transparent. Since tracking means and a correlation matrix is straightforward, the key choices concern reweighting, effective sample size, and when/how to relearn. Several methods are used in ways that likely fall outside their standard guarantees. For instance, MD p-values are estimated using a Hotelling’s T2 distribution, with the sample size N set to the effective sample size S^r, yet that distribution assumes complete counts from a given distribution rather than partial/reweighted counts. If one were to sample from the networks/structures according to these partial weights, would the distribution still follow this form? Likewise, Liptak’s weighted p-values were designed for meta-analysis with independent tests and differing sample sizes. If these tools are applied outside their intended context, the paper should explicitly state this so readers do not infer guarantees that do not hold.

Other comments:
- The use of footnotes to sketch proofs is inventive but likely beyond what Lamport or the conference guidelines envisioned; consider moving these and other missing details to the supplementary material.
- Figures are far too small, with excessive spacing, missing labels, and underspecified captions/legends; the experimental description is also unclear (e.g., the top paragraph on p. 7 is cryptic).
- The explanation for why LoSST underperforms on edge additions when only parameters change is not convincing.
- Relatedly, it seems preferable to decouple parameter-update triggers from structure-update triggers.

A very promising research direction, with a practical—albeit heuristic—first implementation.
