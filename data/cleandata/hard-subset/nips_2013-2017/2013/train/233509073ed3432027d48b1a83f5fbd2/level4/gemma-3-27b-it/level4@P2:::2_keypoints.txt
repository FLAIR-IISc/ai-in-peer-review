- Tackles the important problem of tracking changes in dependency structure in data streams and proposes LoSST for recursive SEMs.
- LoSST combines the PC algorithm with stream-tracked statistics (weighted means, covariance, effective sample size) to trigger re-learning.
- Introduces a “surprisingness” weighting scheme using Mahalanobis distance and a pooled p-value (Liptak) incorporated via a noisy-OR to decide when to relearn.
- Evaluated on synthetic data with induced parameter/structure changes and a real-world US price-index dataset.
- Strength: novel and practically important contribution to online structure learning in non-stationary environments.
- Strength: computationally and memory-efficient approach suitable for online scenarios.
- Strength: experiments are comprehensive, using synthetic cases to isolate effects and a real-world case study.
- Strength: related work is thoroughly reviewed and the paper is generally clearly written.
- Weakness: lacks theoretical analysis or guarantees; needs at least preliminary theoretical exploration or discussion.
- Weakness: heavy reliance on heuristics; key design choices (weighting, effective sample size, re-learning criteria) need stronger justification.
- Weakness: statistical methods (Hotelling’s T2, Liptak) may be used outside original assumptions and these assumptions must be acknowledged and justified.
- Weakness: presentation issues—figures are small, captions incomplete, and some experimental descriptions are cryptic.
- Weakness: explanation for reduced performance on edge removal in parameter-only changes is unconvincing; decoupling parameter vs structure updates may help.
- Other: proofs in extensive footnotes disrupt flow and should be moved to supplementary material.
- Other: discussion of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off is insightful but should be further developed.
- Other: claim that LoSST “ignores short-lived changes” requires stronger empirical justification.
- Overall: promising, practical contribution with clear writing and good experiments but weakened by lack of theory and heavy heuristics.
- For acceptance: addresses an under-explored problem, presents a novel algorithm with promising empirical results, and is well organized.
- Against acceptance: no theoretical guarantees, heavy heuristic reliance, and presentation issues.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—add theoretical discussion, acknowledge statistical assumptions, improve figures/captions, and better explain parameter-only change behavior.
