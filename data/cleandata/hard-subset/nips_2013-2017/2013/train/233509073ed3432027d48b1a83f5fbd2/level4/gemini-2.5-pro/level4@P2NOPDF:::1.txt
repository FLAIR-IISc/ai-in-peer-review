**Reaction to Author Feedback**

Regarding point (3) in the author feedback, I maintain my concern about the claim of novelty. The work of Siracusa and Fisher (AISTATS 2009), for instance, addresses non-i.i.d. data and allows the generating structure to vary within a defined set of graphical models. While the proposed method may offer greater flexibility, describing it as the first to address this problem overlooks relevant prior work.

**Review**

This paper introduces a hypothesis-testing framework for learning graphical models from time series data in an online setting. The method is designed for scenarios where the underlying data-generating graphical model may change over time. The authors demonstrate the approach's performance on both simulated and real-world datasets.

**Quality**

The empirical results presented in the paper appear to be sound. However, the discussion of related work in Section 2 could be refined for greater accuracy. The claim that typical score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks assume a single generating model is an oversimplification. While many standard *models* assume exchangeability (i.e., i.i.d. data), the score-based *algorithms* themselves are often more flexible, requiring only that the scoring function factorizes into local terms. This distinction between the assumptions of the model and the capabilities of the algorithm is important and should be clarified.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written, but several aspects could be clarified to strengthen the presentation. The most significant points are:

1.  **Unclear Learning Task:** A primary point of confusion is the precise definition of the learning task. It is unclear whether the main goal is to identify change points, to infer the graphical structure at the most recent time step, or another objective. A more explicit framing of the problem would be beneficial.
2.  **Ambiguous Contribution:** It is difficult to discern whether the paper's core contribution is a new probabilistic model for changing structures, a novel algorithm to perform inference in such a setting, or both. Clarifying the nature of the contribution would help readers better situate the work.

Additionally, there are several specific areas for improvement:

*   The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to parse. For example, it is not clear if the equation for ρ_{r+1} is a definition or a derivation from previous statements.
*   For improved readability and grammatical correctness, sentences ending with a displayed equation should be properly punctuated.
*   The font size in Figures 1-4 is too small, making them very difficult to read. Increasing the font size would significantly improve legibility.
*   Figures 1 and 2 lack a legend or description for the different colors used, which hinders their interpretation.
*   The argument in Section 6, suggesting that improved tracking performance justifies the additional computational cost, is unsubstantiated. This trade-off is highly domain-specific and depends on an external valuation of accuracy versus computational resources, which is not discussed.

**Originality**

The proposed approach appears to be a novel combination of existing techniques. However, the paper could do more to highlight a core, innovative concept that distinguishes it from prior art. The main conceptual novelty might lie in the construction of the p-values, but this section was difficult to follow in its current form, making it challenging to fully assess its contribution.

**Significance**

The problem of learning changing graphical models from time series is important, and this paper serves as a valuable exploration of a non-Bayesian, hypothesis-testing approach. However, the paper's impact could be limited by the somewhat heuristic nature of the proposed method and the lack of a formal problem definition, as noted under 'Clarity'. A more rigorous, model-based formulation, perhaps within a Bayesian framework, might offer a more principled solution to this problem. As it stands, the primary contribution may be in stimulating further research and the development of more sophisticated methods for this challenging problem, rather than providing a definitive solution itself.
