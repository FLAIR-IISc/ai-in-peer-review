

Paper Summary:  
The paper addresses an interesting and underexplored problem: tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a streaming (e.g., time-based) data context. The authors focus on an efficient (computationally and memory-wise) solution for simpler structural equation models (SEMs). They propose a new algorithm, LoSST, which leverages a standard batch structure-learning method (PC algorithm by Spirtes et al., 2000) and tracks key statistics from the data stream to decide when to initiate new structure learning. These statistics include the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix (Equations 1–2), and the weighted sample size. Data points are assigned weights based on their "surprise" level (Equations 3–4), with each point’s Mahalanobis distance (MD) computed and its p-value estimated via Hotelling’s T² distribution. These p-values are combined using Liptak’s weighted pooling to form an overall significance statistic, ρ. This statistic is then used in a noisy OR configuration with prior state to determine whether to trigger new structure learning. The authors evaluate LoSST against vanilla batch PC learning on synthetic datasets where either parameters or structure change after a fixed number of data points, varying the number of variables and maximum degree. They also apply the algorithm to real-world U.S. price index data (529 monthly observations from 1967 onward, six variables), demonstrating its ability to detect known economic trends while ignoring transient fluctuations.  

Pros:  
1. The paper tackles an important and underexplored problem. As the authors note, there is significant room for further development of practical solutions for this class of models, alongside theoretical guarantees.  
2. The algorithm is novel, practical, and efficient, creatively integrating multiple established methods.  
3. Experiments are adequate, systematically examining parameter effects and including an insightful real-world dataset.  
4. The literature review of prior work on this problem is thorough and well-executed.  
5. The paper is well-written overall.  

Cons:  
1. The most significant gap is the absence of theoretical guarantees under any assumptions. While the authors acknowledge the tension between convergence and diligence (p. 4), some form of guarantees under simplified conditions should be feasible. This should at least be highlighted as a direction for future work.  
2. LoSST relies heavily on heuristic design choices, which is acceptable for a practical solution but requires clearer justification. Key decisions involve reweighting, effective sample size estimation, and relearning triggers. The authors employ methods outside their intended contexts without explicit caveats. For instance, they estimate MD p-values using Hotelling’s T² distribution, setting the sample size N to the effective sample size S^r. However, this distribution assumes complete, unweighted samples from a fixed distribution—not partial or reweighted counts. It is unclear whether the distribution would hold if samples were drawn from the weighted structures. Similarly, Liptak’s weighted p-value pooling was designed for meta-analysis with independent tests of varying sample sizes. The authors should explicitly state when methods are applied outside their validated contexts to avoid misleading readers.  

Other Comments:  
- Creative use of footnotes for proofs exceeds typical conventions (e.g., Leslie Lamport’s guidelines); these details should be moved to the supplementary material.  
- Figures are too small, contain excessive spacing, lack labels, and have insufficient caption details. The experimental description (e.g., top paragraph, p. 7) is unclear.  
- The explanation for LoSST’s poorer performance on edge addition when only parameters change is unsatisfactory.  
- Decoupling parameter updates from structure updates would be desirable.  

This represents a promising direction for future research, with LoSST offering a practical, heuristic-first implementation.
