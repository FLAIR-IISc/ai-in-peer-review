Paper summary:
This paper addresses the underexplored problem of tracking time-varying dependency structures in streaming data. The authors propose LoSST, a computationally and memory-efficient algorithm for online structure learning in linear SEMs (DAGs with linear equations and Gaussian noise). LoSST maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an effective sample size) and uses them to decide when to relearn the graph using a standard batch structure learner (PC; Spirtes et al., 2000).

At each timestep, LoSST computes the Mahalanobis distance of the new observation under the current weighted covariance estimate, converts that to a p-value via Hotelling’s T2 (using the effective sample size), and pools p-values over time using Lipták’s weighted method to obtain a running “fit” statistic ρ. This statistic drives two mechanisms: (a) adaptive reweighting of new samples (larger weights when recent data look unlikely under the current model) and (b) probabilistic triggers for relearning the structure via a noisy-OR update. The authors compare LoSST to batch PC on synthetic data with either structural or parametric changes at a known time, across multiple graph sizes and degrees, and present a brief case study on U.S. price index data (monthly, 1967–2011) using differences. They report that LoSST matches batch performance in stationary regimes and adapts substantially better when changes occur; on the real data it appears to detect the Great Moderation and avoids overreacting to transient spikes.

Relation to prior work:
Most existing structure learning assumes stationarity (score-based and constraint-based methods), while online methods such as TD learning or changepoint detection do not directly address general graphical structure learning, or assume specific change dynamics (abrupt changepoints). Prior work on time-indexed graphs typically operates in batch mode and/or uses undirected models. LoSST fills a gap by offering online, directed-graph structure tracking with lightweight statistics and a trigger mechanism.

Strengths:
- Timely and important problem with clear relevance to streaming and nonstationary settings.
- A practical, novel algorithmic synthesis: maintains only simple sufficient statistics, leverages pooled p-values, and integrates with an off-the-shelf structure learner.
- Empirical evaluation is sensible: stationary vs. changing regimes, structural vs. parametric changes, multiple sizes/degrees, and a real-world case study.
- Literature review is appropriate and situates the contribution.
- Writing is generally clear, with an informative discussion of convergence vs. diligence trade-offs.

Weaknesses:
- Limited theoretical guarantees. While the paper discusses convergence vs. diligence and provides some conditions, more formal guarantees (even under simplifying assumptions) would strengthen the work. At minimum, clearly delineate what is proved vs. conjectured and outline concrete directions for theory.
- Heavy reliance on heuristics and use of methods outside their standard regimes. In particular:
  - Hotelling’s T2 p-values are computed using an “effective” sample size under reweighting; this departs from standard assumptions of i.i.d. samples from a single distribution with true sample size N.
  - Lipták’s method assumes independent tests; pooled p-values from temporally correlated, adaptively weighted data may violate this assumption.
  These choices are understandable for practicality, but the manuscript should explicitly acknowledge the departures from classical assumptions and discuss their implications.
- Presentation issues hinder reproducibility and interpretability:
  - Figures are too small, with substantial white space, missing labels/legends, and terse captions.
  - Some experimental descriptions are cryptic (e.g., top paragraph on p. 7). Clarify datasets, parameter settings (T, γ, ν), relearning schedules, and evaluation metrics.
- The explanation for LoSST’s behavior when only parameters change (worse edge-removal performance shortly after change) is brief; provide a more convincing analysis or diagnostic plots.
- The paper suggests that parameter adaptation and structure relearning are coupled through the same trigger. It may be beneficial to decouple parameter updates from structural updates or to use different thresholds, and at least discuss this design choice and its trade-offs.
- Footnotes are used to sketch proofs; these would be better in an appendix or supplementary material.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important gap with a practical, efficient method that can be readily used.
- Empirical results demonstrate clear benefits in nonstationary settings with no evident cost in stationary ones.
- The framework is broadly extensible and opens a promising line of research.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Theoretical underpinnings are limited relative to the scope of claims, and some guarantees are only sketched.
- The statistical components (T2, Lipták pooling) are applied outside their standard assumptions; the implications are not fully analyzed.
- Presentation (figures, experimental clarity) needs improvement for the work to be easily evaluated and reproduced.

Quality: Solid empirical work and a thoughtful algorithmic design, but theory is light and some statistical assumptions are stretched; clearer caveats and analysis are needed.

Clarity: Generally well written, but figures and experimental descriptions require significant revision. Move proof sketches to supplementary material.

Originality: The combination of online sufficient-statistics tracking with pooled p-values to trigger DAG relearning appears novel and distinct from prior time-varying graph work.

Significance: High practical potential for streaming settings where structure evolves; likely to spur further research on principled, online structure learning.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clearly state the assumptions and limitations of Hotelling’s T2 and Lipták pooling in this context; consider sensitivity analyses or alternatives (e.g., robust distances, calibration via simulation).
- Expand the analysis of the parameter-change scenario; include diagnostics explaining error patterns.
- Discuss the possibility of decoupled triggers for parameter vs. structure updates.
- Improve figure quality, captions, and experimental detail; provide an appendix/supplement with proofs and algorithmic pseudocode.
