

This paper addresses an important gap in structure learning for Structural Equation Models (SEMs) by proposing a method to handle datasets that are only locally i.i.d.—where the underlying generative model may change over time. The problem is well-motivated, as standard algorithms largely overlook temporal dependencies in data, and the proposed approach is conceptually simple, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. The manuscript is clearly written, logically structured, and makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical model learning under non-stationary conditions. The experiments, while focused on adapting the PC algorithm, demonstrate promising results and effectively illustrate the core idea.

The primary contribution lies in formalizing a practical framework for extending structure learning to locally i.i.d. data, which avoids the need for complex temporal modeling. The method’s elegance—leveraging sliding windows and standard conditional independence tests—makes it accessible for implementation and integration into existing pipelines. The empirical evaluation convincingly shows that the adaptation significantly outperforms naive applications of stationary methods on time-dependent data, particularly in scenarios with gradual model shifts.

Two points warrant clarification to strengthen the paper’s impact and precision:

First, the claim that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly" (Section 5) is overstated. While the PC adaptation is thoroughly evaluated, the assertion that *all* methods would behave identically under the same adaptation is unsupported. The differences between algorithms (e.g., constraint-based vs. score-based methods) in handling non-stationarity could be substantial. I recommend rephrasing to explicitly state that the experiments focus on the PC method’s adaptation, and that the extension to other methods is plausible but requires separate validation. For instance: "The PC adaptation demonstrates clear gains, and it is plausible that similar adaptations would extend to other constraint-based methods; however, this remains an open question for future work." This avoids overgeneralization while preserving the paper’s core insight.

Second, the discussion in the conclusions about extending the approach to Bayesian networks (BNs) due to challenges in maintaining sufficient statistics is insightful but should be addressed earlier. The paper currently frames its scope broadly as applicable to "graphical models," yet the technical focus is squarely on SEMs. The BN extension difficulty highlights a key limitation: the proposed method relies on the linear Gaussian structure of SEMs to compute sufficient statistics efficiently. For BNs (which often use discrete or non-linear components), this property does not hold, making direct adaptation non-trivial. The paper would benefit from explicitly stating its primary contribution as being for SEMs *from the outset*, with a clear caveat that extension to other models (like BNs) requires additional, non-trivial modifications. For example: "This work focuses on SEMs, where the linear-Gaussian structure enables efficient sufficient statistic computation. While the core idea of locally i.i.d. adaptation may inspire extensions to other models, we emphasize that direct application to BNs faces significant challenges, as discussed in Section 6." This sharpens the scope and manages reader expectations without diminishing the paper’s contribution.

The manuscript is well-written and technically sound, but these refinements would enhance its rigor and clarity. The current wording risks conflating the paper’s specific contribution (SEM adaptation) with broader claims about graphical models. Clarifying the scope upfront prevents potential misinterpretation and aligns the narrative with the empirical focus. The experiments are appropriately limited to the PC method, which is reasonable given the paper’s specific technical contribution; however, the text should avoid implying universal applicability to all structure learning algorithms.

I acknowledge having read the authors’ response to prior feedback. The authors’ clarifications on the scope and algorithmic claims address my concerns effectively, and the revised text now more accurately reflects the paper’s targeted contribution. The technical content remains strong, and the proposed method is a meaningful step toward handling non-stationary data in causal discovery. The simplicity of the approach, combined with its demonstrated effectiveness, makes it a valuable addition to the literature.

In summary, this is a solid contribution that tackles a relevant problem with a clear, implementable solution. With minor revisions to clarify the scope and temper the generalization claims, it will be a strong candidate for publication. The paper’s focus on SEMs is well-justified, and the proposed method offers a practical pathway for real-world applications where data distributions evolve over time. I recommend acceptance.
