## Review of NIPS 2013 Submission

This paper presents a hypothesis-testing based approach for learning graphical models in an online fashion from time series data, specifically addressing scenarios where the underlying data generating graphical model may change frequently. The method’s performance is demonstrated through both simulated and real data experiments.

**Quality:**

The presented results appear technically sound. However, the discussion of related work in Section 2 contains a potentially misleading characterization of existing methods. While many score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks *typically* assume exchangeability or i.i.d. data within the model itself, the algorithms themselves do not necessitate a single, unchanging generative model. They often rely on scoring functions that factorize into local terms, allowing them to operate even when the underlying distribution shifts. Furthermore, I disagree with the authors’ response to my previous feedback regarding the novelty of their approach. The work of Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) does not assume i.i.d. data and allows for variation in the generating structure – albeit within a defined set of graphical models. While the proposed method offers increased flexibility, claiming it is the *first* to address this problem is inaccurate.

**Clarity:**

The paper's presentation is generally acceptable, but clarity could be improved. A central ambiguity lies in the precise definition of the learning task. Is the goal to infer change points, assuming some structure to their occurrence? Or to estimate the structure of the most recent data point? Or something else entirely? The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of its objective. Similarly, it's unclear whether the contribution lies primarily in a new model formulation (i.e., revised assumptions and learning goals) or a novel algorithm for achieving existing learning goals, or a combination of both. 

Specific sections require further attention. The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow. For instance, the equation defining ρ<sub>r+1</sub> lacks context; it is unclear whether this is its initial definition or a reiteration of a previously stated one. A minor, but noticeable issue is the absence of periods at the end of sentences containing displayed equations. More significantly, the font size in Figures 1-4 is too small and should be increased substantially for readability.  Adding descriptions for the different colors used in Figures 1 and 2 would also enhance understanding. Finally, the claim in Section 6 that improved tracking can compensate for increased computational cost feels overly broad and domain-dependent. Such a trade-off hinges on a specific valuation of tracking accuracy versus computational expense, which is not justified within the paper's context.

**Originality:**

The work appears original in its specific implementation. However, it lacks a demonstrably novel core idea or significant innovation. The potential originality may reside in the construction of the p-values, but the explanation provided is insufficient for a full assessment.

**Significance:**

The potential impact of this work, if published, is primarily incremental. It could spur further development of more sophisticated, potentially Bayesian, methods for online graphical model learning. However, this feels more like engineering development than fundamental research. The presented approach appears to be a heuristic solution to a problem that existing machine learning techniques should, in principle, already be capable of addressing.
