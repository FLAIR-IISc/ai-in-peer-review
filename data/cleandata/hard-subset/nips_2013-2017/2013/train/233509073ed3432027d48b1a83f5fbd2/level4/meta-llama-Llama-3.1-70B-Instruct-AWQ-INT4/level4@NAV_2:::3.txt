This manuscript presents a systematic idea to make structure learning for SEMs work with data sets that are only locally i.i.d., that is, the data might be generated from distinct underlying models as time goes by. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, and addresses an important problem, namely learning from time-dependent data, which has been neglected by many of the most used algorithms for structure learning.

The main contribution of this paper is the introduction of LoSST, a novel, heuristic structure learning algorithm that tracks changes in graphical model structure or parameters in a dynamic, real-time manner. The algorithm is designed to handle data that is only locally stationary, meaning that the generating process is i.i.d. at each time step, but the underlying structure can change over time.

The paper provides a clear and concise overview of related work in the field, including standard graphical model structure learning algorithms and online learning methods. The authors also discuss the limitations of existing methods and how LoSST addresses these limitations.

The LoSST algorithm is described in detail, including the update equations for the sufficient statistics and the mechanism for detecting changes in the underlying structure. The authors also provide a discussion on the convergence and diligence properties of LoSST, and how these properties can be controlled by adjusting the algorithm's parameters.

The paper includes a thorough evaluation of LoSST using synthetic data and real-world data from the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics. The results show that LoSST performs comparably to batch-mode learning when the generating graphical structure is globally stationary, and significantly better when it is only locally stationary.

Strengths of the paper include its clear and concise writing style, its thorough evaluation of the proposed algorithm, and its discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of the work.

One potential weakness of the paper is its assertion that other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly to the PC method. While this may be true, it is not entirely clear why this is the case, and the authors could provide more explanation or evidence to support this claim.

Another potential weakness is the paper's discussion of the extension of LoSST to other types of graphical models, such as Bayesian networks. While the authors acknowledge that this extension may not be straightforward, they could provide more discussion on the challenges and potential solutions for this extension.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that presents a novel and effective algorithm for learning the graph structure of SEMs under the assumption that data are only locally i.i.d. The experimental results look promising, and the paper provides a good discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of the work.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and effective algorithm for learning the graph structure of SEMs under the assumption that data are only locally i.i.d.
* The algorithm is well-motivated and addresses an important problem in the field.
* The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed algorithm using synthetic and real-world data.
* The writing style is clear and concise, making the paper easy to follow.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper's assertion that other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly to the PC method could be better supported.
* The discussion of the extension of LoSST to other types of graphical models could be more thorough.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of the work.

Recommendation:

* Accept the paper with minor revisions to address the potential weaknesses mentioned above.
