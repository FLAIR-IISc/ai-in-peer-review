**Paper Summary**

This paper addresses the interesting and underexplored problem of tracking changes in the dependency structure between variables within a data stream. The authors focus on developing a computationally and memory-efficient solution for Structural Equation Models (SEMs). They propose a new algorithm for this task, named LoSST, which uses a standard batch algorithm (the PC algorithm) for the core structure learning. LoSST monitors several statistics from the data stream to decide when to re-learn the structure. The tracked statistics include the weighted mean for each variable, the correlation matrix (Eqs. 1, 2), and the weighted sample size.

The algorithm employs a weighting scheme where each new data point is weighted based on its "surprisingness" (Eqs. 3, 4). For each new point, the Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed, and its p-value is estimated using a Hotelling's T2 distribution. These p-values are then combined using Liptak's weighted pooling method to compute an overall significance statistic, ρ. This statistic is used in a noisy-OR configuration with its value from the previous time step to decide whether to trigger a new structure learning phase.

The authors compare LoSST to a standard batch PC algorithm on synthetic datasets. In these experiments, a change in either the model parameters or the underlying structure is introduced after a fixed number of data points, and performance is evaluated across different numbers of variables and maximum graph degrees. Finally, they apply their method to a real-world dataset from the US price index, comprising 529 monthly data points (starting from 1967) across six variables. They demonstrate that the algorithm successfully detects several known long-term changes in the US economy while correctly ignoring short-lived fluctuations.

**Pros**

1.  The paper addresses an interesting and underexplored problem. As the authors correctly point out, there is significant room for developing further practical solutions for these and other models, as well as establishing the corresponding theoretical guarantees.
2.  The proposed algorithm is novel, practical, and efficient. It creatively combines a variety of statistical methods to achieve its goal.
3.  The experiments appear adequate, evaluating the effect of various parameters and including an interesting analysis on a real-world dataset.
4.  The authors provide a good review of previous work on this topic.
5.  The paper is well-written overall.

**Cons**

1.  The paper's most significant shortcoming is the lack of theoretical analysis or performance guarantees, even under simplifying assumptions. The authors acknowledge a trade-off between rapid convergence and thoroughness (p. 4), but it seems plausible that some form of guarantee could be established under simplified conditions. This should at least be mentioned as a direction for future work.

2.  The proposed solution, LoSST, relies heavily on heuristics. While this approach is understandable for achieving a practical solution, the heuristic design choices should be more clearly justified. The core statistical tracking (mean and correlation matrix) is straightforward; the main heuristic components concern the re-weighting scheme, the calculation of the effective sample size, and the trigger mechanism for re-learning the structure.

    The authors creatively apply several statistical methods, but in a context that likely violates their underlying assumptions and invalidates their theoretical guarantees. For example:
    *   They estimate the p-value of the Mahalanobis distance using Hotelling's T2 distribution, substituting the effective sample size for the true sample size. However, the T2 distribution is derived for samples drawn i.i.d. from a population, not for re-weighted data points. It is unclear whether data sampled according to these weights would still follow this distribution.
    *   Similarly, Liptak's method for combining p-values is intended for the meta-analysis of independent statistical tests, an assumption which may not hold here.

    When methods are used outside of their intended context, the authors should explicitly state this and acknowledge the potential implications, so as not to mislead the reader.

**Other Comments**

*   The authors use footnotes to outline proofs, which is an unconventional choice. These details, along with other missing information, would be better placed in the supplementary material.
*   The figures are too small and poorly formatted, with excessive whitespace, missing labels, and insufficient detail in captions and legends.
*   The description of the experiments lacks clarity. For instance, the paragraph at the top of page 7 is difficult to understand.
*   The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on edge addition when only parameters have changed is not convincing.
*   Related to the point above, it seems desirable to decouple the triggers for parameter updates from those for structure updates.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting research direction and offers a practical, albeit heuristic, initial approach to a challenging problem.
