### **Review**

**Summary**

This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of learning graphical model structure from data streams where the underlying generative process is non-stationary. Standard structure learning algorithms typically assume i.i.d. data from a single, stable generative model. In contrast, the authors propose an online algorithm, the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), designed to detect and adapt to changes in both the parameters and the structure of the underlying model.

The core of the LoSST algorithm is a mechanism for tracking sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an "effective" sample size) by differentially weighting incoming data points. The weight assigned to a new data point is determined by its "surprise," quantified by its Mahalanobis distance from the currently estimated distribution. The individual p-values from these distances are pooled over time using Liptak's method to produce a statistic, ρ, that indicates the overall fit of the recent data to the current model. This statistic governs both the data point weighting and the probability of triggering a full relearning of the graphical structure, which is performed by a standard batch-mode algorithm (in this case, the PC algorithm).

The authors demonstrate LoSST's performance on synthetic data, showing that it performs comparably to a batch-mode learner in stationary environments and significantly better in non-stationary ones where the structure or parameters change. They also provide a brief case study on U.S. price index data, showing the algorithm identifies known periods of macroeconomic change.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a crucial and relatively under-explored problem. The assumption of stationarity in most graphical model learning literature is a major limitation for many real-world applications (e.g., finance, climate science, systems biology). This work provides a novel and practical approach to relax this assumption. The proposed LoSST algorithm is an original contribution that creatively integrates several existing statistical tools (Mahalanobis distance, Hotelling's T-squared test, Liptak's p-value pooling) into a cohesive online learning framework.

2.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm is computationally and memory efficient. By updating sufficient statistics online and only triggering the expensive structure learning step when a change is suspected, LoSST is suitable for real-time applications and for processing very large datasets in a single pass. This makes it a valuable tool for practitioners.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The simulation studies are well-designed. They convincingly demonstrate the algorithm's primary advantage over batch-mode learning in non-stationary settings while showing it incurs little to no performance cost in stationary ones. The inclusion of experiments that separately test structural and parametric changes provides valuable insight into the algorithm's behavior. The real-world data application, while brief, effectively illustrates the potential utility of the method.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a good overview of related work and effectively motivate the need for their approach. The discussion of the trade-off between "convergence" and "diligence" is insightful and provides an interesting theoretical framing for the algorithm's parameterization.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Heuristic Nature and Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary weakness of the work is its highly heuristic nature, which stems from applying statistical methods outside of their original theoretical contexts. While this is acknowledged as pragmatic, the paper would be stronger if these limitations were more explicitly discussed. For instance:
    *   The p-value for the Mahalanobis distance is estimated using Hotelling's T-squared distribution with the "effective sample size" S_r. This distribution, however, is derived under the assumption of i.i.d. samples from a single multivariate normal distribution. The re-weighting scheme fundamentally violates this assumption. It is unclear how this affects the validity of the computed p-values.
    *   Similarly, Liptak's method for pooling p-values assumes independent tests. Here, the tests (p-values from sequential Mahalanobis distances) are not independent, as they are all compared against a slowly evolving covariance matrix.
    The authors should clearly state that these methods are used as well-motivated heuristics and that their statistical guarantees do not hold, to avoid misleading the reader. While a full theoretical analysis may be beyond the scope of this paper, acknowledging these gaps and framing them as directions for future work would strengthen the manuscript.

2.  **Clarity of Presentation and Experiments:**
    *   The figures are too small and difficult to read. There is significant empty space between plots that could be used to enlarge them. Key details are missing, such as axis labels on some plots and more descriptive captions that explain the experimental setup without requiring the reader to hunt through the text. The top paragraph of Section 4 describing the first simulation is particularly dense and could be clarified.
    *   The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on edge *removal* errors immediately after a parameter-only change (p. 7) is not fully convincing. The authors state LoSST becomes "more cautious," but a deeper analysis of why this caution manifests as a failure to remove edges (which seems counter-intuitive) would be beneficial.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

*   The use of footnotes to present proof sketches is unconventional. This material, along with other implementation details that would aid reproducibility, would be better placed in a supplementary document.
*   The authors might consider discussing the possibility of decoupling parameter updates from structure updates. It seems plausible that the sufficient statistics could be updated continuously, while the more expensive structural relearning is triggered only by stronger evidence of change. This could be a fruitful direction for future work.
*   Overall, this is a very interesting paper that presents a practical solution to an important problem. The approach is novel and the results are promising. The main concerns relate to the lack of theoretical grounding for its heuristic components and the clarity of the presentation. Addressing these weaknesses, particularly by being more explicit about the heuristic nature of the statistical tests, would significantly improve the quality of this contribution.
