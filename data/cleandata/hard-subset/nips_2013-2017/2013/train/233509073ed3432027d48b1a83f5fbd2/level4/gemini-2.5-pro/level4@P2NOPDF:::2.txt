### Paper Summary

This paper addresses the important and relatively under-explored problem of tracking changes in the dependency structure between variables within a data stream. The authors focus on developing a computationally and memory-efficient solution for Structural Equation Models (SEMs). They propose a new algorithm, LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), which builds upon a standard batch structure learning algorithm (the PC algorithm) but intelligently decides when to re-run it.

LoSST monitors several statistics from the data stream, including the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix, and an effective sample size. A key component of the algorithm is a weighting scheme that assigns a weight to each new data point based on how "surprising" it is. This surprise is quantified by computing the Mahalanobis distance (MD) for each point, converting it to a p-value using a Hotelling's T-squared distribution, and then combining these p-values over a window using Liptak's method to derive an overall significance statistic, ρ. This statistic is then used in a noisy-OR model to probabilistically decide whether to trigger a new structure learning phase.

The authors evaluate LoSST on synthetic datasets, introducing either parameter changes or structural changes after a fixed number of data points, and test across different variable counts and maximum degree configurations. They compare its performance against a vanilla batch PC algorithm. Finally, they apply the algorithm to a real-world dataset of the US price index, spanning 529 months from 1967, showing that LoSST identifies known economic shifts while ignoring short-lived fluctuations.

### Strengths

1.  **Problem Novelty and Importance:** The paper tackles a significant and challenging problem. As the authors correctly point out, there is substantial room for developing practical solutions for dynamic structure learning, as well as the accompanying theory and guarantees.
2.  **Novel and Practical Algorithm:** The proposed LoSST algorithm is novel, practical, and efficient. It creatively combines a host of different statistical methods to achieve its goal.
3.  **Adequate Experiments:** The experimental evaluation appears sound, systematically exploring the effects of various parameters on synthetic data. The inclusion of an interesting real-world dataset demonstrates the potential applicability of the method.
4.  **Thorough Literature Review:** The authors provide a comprehensive review of previous work on this and related problems.
5.  **Overall Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** A significant limitation of the paper is the absence of theoretical analysis or performance guarantees, even under simplifying assumptions. The authors rightly note the tension between convergence (stability) and diligence (reactivity), but it seems plausible that some form of guarantees could be derived for simplified scenarios. At a minimum, this should be explicitly mentioned as a crucial direction for future work.

2.  **Heuristic Nature and Use of Statistical Tools:** The LoSST algorithm is composed of a series of well-motivated but ultimately heuristic steps. While this is acceptable for achieving a practical solution, the heuristic nature of the design choices should be more explicitly acknowledged. More importantly, the paper applies several statistical methods under conditions that likely violate their underlying assumptions, potentially voiding their guarantees.
    *   For example, the p-value for the Mahalanobis distance is estimated using a Hotelling's T-squared distribution. The authors use the effective sample size S^r as the sample size parameter N for this distribution. However, this distribution was derived for i.i.d. samples, and it is not clear that it remains valid for a re-weighted effective sample size.
    *   Similarly, Liptak's method for pooling p-values was developed for meta-analysis of independent statistical tests. The sequential p-values derived in this paper are unlikely to be independent.
    *   When statistical tools are used outside of their established contexts, it is crucial for the authors to state this clearly. This transparency would prevent readers from making incorrect assumptions about the statistical validity of the intermediate steps.

3.  **Presentation and Experimental Clarity:**
    *   **Figures:** The figures are difficult to interpret due to their small size, excessive white space between them, and a lack of clear labels, detailed captions, and legends. This significantly hinders the reader's ability to understand the experimental results.
    *   **Experimental Description:** The description of the experiments is sometimes unclear. For example, the first paragraph on page 7 is cryptic and requires significant effort to parse.
    *   **Proofs in Footnotes:** The authors make extensive use of footnotes to outline proofs. This is unconventional and makes the paper harder to read. Such details are better suited for a supplementary appendix.

4.  **Analysis of Results:**
    *   The explanation for why LoSST performs worse on edge addition tasks when only parameters are changed is unconvincing and requires a more thorough analysis.
    *   Given the above point, it seems desirable that the triggers for parameter updates and structure updates be decoupled. The current model conflates parameter drift and structural change into a single trigger, which may not be optimal. Exploring a two-tiered system could be a valuable line of future research.

### Conclusion

This paper presents an interesting and promising direction for future research. The authors propose a practical, albeit heuristic, implementation as a first attempt at solving the difficult problem of tracking structural changes in data streams. While the lack of theoretical grounding and the unconventional application of some statistical methods are notable weaknesses, the paper's novelty and the importance of the problem it addresses make it a valuable contribution. With improvements to the experimental presentation and a more transparent discussion of the algorithm's heuristic nature, this could be a strong paper.
