This manuscript proposes a systematic approach to structure learning for SEMs when data are only locally i.i.d.—that is, when the generative model may change over time. The paper is well written and easy to follow, and it tackles an important and often overlooked problem: learning from time-dependent data, which many commonly used structure-learning algorithms do not handle. The proposed techniques for extending learning to time-dependent data are simple yet, based on the experiments with the PC method, appear effective.

I have two suggestions to clarify the contributions:

1) Experimental claims about generality. The paper states that “any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly.” This claim seems too strong. Performance can vary substantially across structure-learning methods. It would be better to (a) rephrase to make clear that the experiments focus on the PC method in the time-independent setting and on its adaptation for time-dependent data, and then note that similar behavior is conjectured for other methods; or (b) keep the assertion but provide a detailed justification for why other standard algorithms would behave similarly under the proposed framework.

2) Scope with respect to Bayesian networks and other models. The conclusion notes that applying the same ideas to Bayesian networks may be nontrivial due to difficulties in maintaining sufficient statistics. Although this issue is not central to the present proposal, it indicates that extending the procedure to Bayesian networks and other models may require more than a few simple modifications. I suggest disclosing this limitation earlier and framing the paper more explicitly around SEMs, while acknowledging that extensions to other models may be possible but are not necessarily straightforward. In short, the paper would benefit from sharper articulation of its scope.

Overall, this is a well-written paper on learning the graph structure of SEMs under the assumption that data are only locally i.i.d. The formulation is straightforward to understand and implement, and the experimental results are promising, even though they focus on comparisons using the PC method. I acknowledge having read the authors’ feedback.
