

This paper addresses a significant gap in graphical model structure learning by introducing LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical structures under the locally stationary assumption. The authors convincingly argue that while most structure learning algorithms assume globally stationary data, real-world applications often involve environments where the generating model changes over time. The paper's contribution is particularly valuable as it fills a critical void in the literature: existing online learning methods either cannot handle graphical model structure learning (e.g., TD(0) algorithms) or require batch processing (e.g., changepoint detection methods and previous time-varying graph structure approaches like Talih & Hengartner, Siracusa & Fisher).

The paper demonstrates strong quality through both theoretical justification and empirical validation. The authors provide a rigorous mathematical foundation for their approach, including convergence and diligence properties of LoSST under different parameter settings. The simulation results are comprehensive, showing that LoSST performs comparably to batch-mode learning when structures are globally stationary and significantly better when structures are only locally stationary. The real-world application to US price index volatility further demonstrates the practical significance of the approach. The paper adequately acknowledges limitations, noting that LoSST may perform poorly with very rapid structural changes or random-order mixtures of multiple structures.

The clarity of the paper is excellent. The problem statement, algorithm description, and experimental setup are well-organized and accessible. The authors effectively use equations to explain the online sufficient statistics tracking and the Mahalanobis distance-based weighting scheme. The simulation results are clearly presented through well-designed figures that effectively communicate the algorithm's performance across different metrics. The writing is precise and avoids unnecessary jargon, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.

The originality of the work is substantial. While there have been previous attempts to learn time-varying graph structures, they either required batch processing or focused on specific types of changes (e.g., single edge modifications). LoSST's key innovation is its ability to track structural changes in real-time while maintaining comparable performance to batch methods in stable environments. The authors effectively differentiate their approach from related work, particularly in section 2, where they systematically analyze the limitations of existing methods.

The significance of this work is high. Many real-world applications, particularly in economics, finance, and other domains with evolving relationships between variables, require structure learning in non-stationary environments. The paper's demonstration of LoSST's effectiveness in analyzing US price index volatility provides a compelling real-world use case. The approach could be valuable for practitioners dealing with streaming data where structural changes occur without warning.

The paper has two main areas for improvement. First, the authors claim that "any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly" to the PC algorithm in their experiments. This assertion is too strong given the known differences between structure learning methods. The paper would be strengthened by either clarifying that the experiments specifically focus on PC (and noting that other methods might require different adaptations) or providing more detailed justification for why other methods would perform similarly. A more nuanced statement would improve the paper's scientific rigor.

Second, while the paper acknowledges the challenges of extending LoSST to other graphical models (such as Bayesian networks) in the discussion, this limitation is not clearly stated in the introduction or scope of the paper. The authors should explicitly frame the paper as being specifically about SEMs, noting that the extension to other graphical models would require additional work. This would make the paper's contribution more precise and help readers understand the scope of the proposed method.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with appropriate citations to relevant literature. The algorithm is presented with sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the experiments are thorough and well-designed. The authors have effectively addressed the four key criteria: the work is technically sound (quality), clearly presented (clarity), introduces a novel approach to a significant problem (originality), and addresses an important practical challenge (significance).

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature. The paper's focus on real-world applicability through both simulations and a real-world case study significantly enhances its impact. With minor revisions to clarify the scope and the generality of the experimental results, this paper would make an excellent contribution to NIPS.

I recommend acceptance. The work is technically sound, well-presented, and addresses a meaningful problem with significant potential impact. The minor suggestions for clarification would strengthen the paper without requiring substantial additional work.
