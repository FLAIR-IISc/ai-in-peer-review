**Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work**

The paper proposes a novel algorithm, LoSST, for tracking time-varying graphical structures in real-time. The algorithm is designed to handle scenarios where the underlying generating model changes frequently, and it assumes only local stationarity of the data. The authors relate their work to previous methods, including Bayesian and frequentist online changepoint detection algorithms, as well as other graphical model structure learning algorithms. However, I disagree with the authors' claim that their method is the first to address the problem of non-iid data, as the work of Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) also allows for varying generating structures.

**Quality**

The results presented in the paper seem correct, but I have some concerns regarding the description of related work. Typical score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks do not assume a single generating model; instead, they assume the scoring function factorizes into local terms. The assumption of exchangeability (or iid data) is a property of the model, not the algorithm.

**Clarity**

The presentation of the paper is mostly clear, but there are some areas for improvement. The paper could benefit from a clearer definition of the learning task, whether it is inferring change points, the generating structure of the last data point, or something else. Additionally, it is unclear whether the work proposes a new model, algorithm, or both. Some paragraphs, such as the one at the end of page 3, are difficult to follow, and the font size in Figures 1-4 could be increased. I would also appreciate descriptions for the different colors used in Figures 1 and 2.

**Originality**

The work appears original, but it seems to lack a novel idea or innovation. The construction of the p-values might be a unique aspect, but I am not entirely sure.

**Significance**

The potential significance of this work lies in its potential to lead to the development of superior, presumably Bayesian, methods for the problem. However, I view this as a development rather than a research contribution. The paper presents a heuristic-looking approach to a somewhat unclearly defined learning problem that existing machine learning machinery should already have solved.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical structures.
* The algorithm is designed to handle scenarios where the underlying generating model changes frequently.
* The paper includes a thorough evaluation of the algorithm using both simulated and real data.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper's description of related work is somewhat misleading.
* The learning task and goals are not clearly defined.
* The paper lacks a novel idea or innovation.
* The significance of the work is limited to potentially leading to the development of superior methods.

**Recommendations for Improvement**

* Clarify the description of related work, particularly with regards to score-based algorithms for learning Bayesian networks.
* Clearly define the learning task and goals of the paper.
* Consider adding more details about the construction of the p-values and how they contribute to the novelty of the work.
* Increase the font size in Figures 1-4 and add descriptions for the different colors used.
* Address the potential limitations and significance of the work in a more nuanced manner.
