- Paper tackles monitoring changes in latent dependency structure in data streams and introduces LoSST, a memory- and compute-efficient algorithm that uses PC plus streaming statistics and reweighting by surprise.
- LoSST tracks deviations, a weighted correlation matrix, effective sample size, computes Mahalanobis distances, converts them to p-values via Hotelling’s T2, pools p-values with Liptak’s method, and uses a noisy-OR to decide when to relearn structure.
- Experiments compare LoSST to batch PC on synthetic parameter/structure changes and analyze a real U.S. price-index dataset, showing detection of major shifts while ignoring short perturbations.
- Strength: addresses a timely, underexplored problem with room for further methods and theory.
- Strength: novel, practical, and efficient algorithm that integrates multiple techniques creatively.
- Strength: experiments are appropriate and include parameter sweeps plus a real-world dataset.
- Strength: related work section gives a solid overview.
- Strength: manuscript is generally clearly written.
- Weakness: lacks theoretical guarantees or convergence/vigilance analysis under stated assumptions; should be noted as future work.
- Weakness: heavy reliance on heuristics with opaque decision points (reweighting, effective sample size, relearning trigger) and potential misuse of statistical tools outside their standard guarantees (Hotelling’s T2 with reweighted counts, Liptak pooling with dependent tests).
- Other: footnote-proof sketches should be moved to supplementary material rather than footnotes.
- Other: figures are too small, poorly labeled, and captions/legends and experimental descriptions are underspecified or unclear.
- Other: explanation for LoSST’s underperformance on edge additions when only parameters change is unconvincing.
- Other: recommend decoupling parameter-update triggers from structure-update triggers.
- Overall: promising research direction with a practical but heuristic initial implementation.
