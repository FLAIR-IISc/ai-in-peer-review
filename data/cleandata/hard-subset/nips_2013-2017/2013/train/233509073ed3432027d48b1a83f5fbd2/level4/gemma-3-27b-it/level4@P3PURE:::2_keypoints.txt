- Tackles tracking changes in dependency structure over data streams for SEMs, aiming for computationally and memory-efficient methods.
- Proposes LoSST: uses PC for batch structure learning, tracks mean/correlation/weighted sample size, weights points by “surprisingness” (Mahalanobis distance), computes p-values via Hotelling’s T2 and pools them with Liptak to get a significance ρ that triggers re-learning.
- Evaluated on synthetic scenarios (parameter vs structural changes, varying variables/degree) and a real US price-index dataset; detects known economic shifts and filters short-lived fluctuations.

Pros:
- Addresses an interesting, under-explored problem with clear potential for further development and theory.
- LoSST is novel, practical, and computationally efficient, creatively integrating multiple methods.
- Experimental setup is adequate and includes a relevant real-world dataset.
- Provides a thorough review of related work.
- Paper is generally well-written and clear.

Cons:
- Lacks theoretical analysis or guarantees; should explore or at least discuss possible guarantees under simplifying assumptions.
- Relies heavily on heuristics with insufficient rationale for key design choices (reweighting, effective sample size, re-learning triggers); uses Hotelling’s T2 and Liptak pooling outside their standard assumptions without explicit caveats.

Other comments:
- Overuse of footnotes for proofs; supplementary material would be more appropriate.
- Figures suffer from poor presentation (too small, bad spacing, missing labels, weak captions); experimental descriptions lack clarity.
- Explanation for LoSST’s worse performance on edge addition under parameter-only changes is unconvincing.
- Recommends decoupling parameter updates from structure updates.
