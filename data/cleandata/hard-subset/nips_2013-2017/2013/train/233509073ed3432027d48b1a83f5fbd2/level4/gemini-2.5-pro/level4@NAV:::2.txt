### Paper Summary

The paper addresses the important and relatively unexplored problem of detecting changes in the dependency structure among variables within a data stream. The authors propose an efficient solution, in terms of both computation and memory, tailored for Structural Equation Models (SEMs). They introduce a novel algorithm, LoSST, for this purpose. LoSST leverages a standard batch structure learning algorithm (the PC algorithm) and monitors several statistics from the data stream to trigger a new learning phase. These monitored statistics include the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix (Eq. 1,2), and the weighted sample size.

A weighting scheme is employed where the influence of each new data point is determined by its "surprisingness." This is quantified by computing the Mahalanobis distance (MD) for each point. The corresponding p-values, estimated using a Hotelling's T² distribution, are then aggregated via Liptak's weighted pooling method to produce an overall significance statistic, ρ. This statistic, ρ, is incorporated into a noisy-OR function with the previous state to decide if a new structure learning process should be initiated.

The authors evaluate their method against a standard batch PC algorithm on synthetic datasets. These experiments involve introducing either parameter changes or structural changes after a predetermined number of data points, across various configurations of variable counts and maximum degrees. Furthermore, the paper includes an analysis of a real-world dataset from the US price index, comprising 529 monthly data points and six variables since 1967. The results indicate that the algorithm successfully identifies known long-term trends in the US economy while filtering out transient fluctuations.

### Pros

1.  The paper addresses an important and relatively nascent research area. As the authors correctly note, there is significant potential for further development of practical algorithms and corresponding theoretical guarantees for this class of models.
2.  The proposed algorithm is both novel and designed for practical efficiency. It creatively integrates a diverse set of statistical methods to achieve its goal.
3.  The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, systematically investigating the impact of various parameters and including a compelling real-world case study.
4.  The paper provides a thorough overview of related work in the field.
5.  The manuscript is well-written and clearly presented.

### Cons

1.  A significant limitation of the work is the absence of theoretical analysis or performance guarantees under specified assumptions. While the authors acknowledge the tension between convergence and diligence (p. 4), it seems feasible that some form of guarantee could be derived under simplified conditions. This should be highlighted as a key avenue for future work.
2.  The proposed LoSST algorithm is largely heuristic in nature. While this approach facilitates a practical solution, the underlying design choices—particularly concerning data re-weighting, the use of an effective sample size, and the re-learning triggers—could be articulated more clearly and justified more rigorously. Specifically, several statistical tools are employed in contexts that may violate their underlying assumptions. For instance, p-values for the Mahalanobis distance are estimated using Hotelling's T² distribution, with the sample size N set to the *effective* sample size S^r. This distribution, however, is derived for complete i.i.d. samples, not for re-weighted or effective sample sizes. Similarly, Liptak's method for pooling p-values is intended for combining independent statistical tests, a condition not met by the sequentially dependent data points in a stream. When using methods outside their established theoretical frameworks, it is crucial for the authors to explicitly acknowledge this to prevent potential misinterpretation by the reader.

### Other Comments

*   The use of footnotes for outlining proofs is unconventional. This material, along with other detailed derivations, would be more appropriately placed in a supplementary document.
*   The figures are insufficiently sized, leading to poor readability. They also suffer from excessive white space, missing axis labels, and inadequate detail in captions and legends.
*   The description of the experimental setup lacks clarity (e.g., the first paragraph on page 7 is difficult to follow).
*   The provided justification for LoSST's poorer performance on edge addition tasks during parameter-only changes is unconvincing.
*   Related to the previous point, a potential improvement would be to decouple the triggers for parameter updates from those for full structure relearning.

This work represents a promising research direction, offering a practical, albeit heuristic, initial solution to a challenging problem.
