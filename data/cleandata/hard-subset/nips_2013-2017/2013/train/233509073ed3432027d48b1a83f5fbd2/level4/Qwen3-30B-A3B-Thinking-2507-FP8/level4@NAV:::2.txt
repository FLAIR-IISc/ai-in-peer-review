

Paper Summary:  
The paper addresses an intriguing and underexplored challenge: tracking shifts in the underlying dependency structure between variables within streaming data (e.g., time-series). The authors propose an efficient (computationally and memory-wise) solution for simpler SEM models, introducing the LoSST algorithm. LoSST leverages the standard batch PC algorithm (Spirtes et al., 2000) for structure learning and monitors key stream statistics—weighted variable means, correlation matrices (Eqs. 1–2), and weighted sample size—to determine when to trigger new structure learning. Data points are weighted based on novelty (Eqs. 3–4), with each point’s Mahalanobis distance (MD) computed and its p-value estimated via Hotelling’s T² distribution. These p-values are combined using Liptak’s weighted pooling to yield an overall significance statistic ρ, which, in a noisy OR configuration with prior state, dictates whether to initiate structure learning. The authors benchmark LoSST against vanilla batch PC on synthetic datasets featuring either parameter or structural shifts after fixed data segments, varying dimensions and maximum degrees. They also analyze real-world US price index data (529 monthly points from 1967, six variables), demonstrating LoSST detects meaningful economic trends while filtering transient fluctuations.  

Pros:  
1. The problem tackled is compelling and underexplored, with significant potential for future theoretical and practical advancements.  
2. The LoSST algorithm is novel, practical, and efficiently integrates diverse methodological components.  
3. Experiments are well-designed, probing parameter effects and incorporating a relevant real-world dataset.  
4. The literature review thoroughly contextualizes prior work on this problem.  
5. The paper is clearly and professionally written.  

Cons:  
1. The paper lacks theoretical guarantees or analysis under simplifying assumptions—a critical omission given the problem’s significance. While convergence and diligence are acknowledged as opposing forces (p. 4), basic guarantees under plausible conditions should be addressed, at minimum as a future direction.  
2. LoSST relies heavily on heuristic decisions (e.g., reweighting, effective sample size definition, relearning triggers), which are not sufficiently justified. Key methodological mismatches require explicit acknowledgment: Hotelling’s T² distribution assumes complete, unweighted samples, yet it is applied to reweighted statistics (using effective size S^r); Liptak’s pooling was designed for independent meta-analyses with distinct sample sizes, not for sequential stream processing. The authors must clarify these methodological deviations to prevent misinterpretation.  

Other Comments:  
- Overuse of footnotes for proofs (beyond intended scope) should be moved to supplementary material.  
- Figures are poorly formatted (insufficient size, excessive spacing, missing labels/details in captions/legends); experimental descriptions (e.g., top of p. 7) lack clarity.  
- The explanation for LoSST’s poorer performance on edge additions during parameter shifts is inadequate.  
- Decoupling parameter updates from structure updates would strengthen the framework.  
This work presents a promising, practical heuristic for an important problem, serving as a valuable first step toward robust solutions.
