- Reviewer disagrees with the authors’ claim of novelty: Siracusa III and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) already handle non-iid, changing graphical structures.  
- Paper proposes a hypothesis-testing–based online method to learn graphical models from time series with frequently changing structure.  
- Results are technically sound and empirical demonstrations are informative.  
- Related-work section is misleading: score-based structure learning does not fundamentally require a single fixed model, and prior nonstationary methods must be acknowledged.  
- The learning task is not precisely stated—unclear whether goal is change-point detection, tracking current graph, maintaining posterior over graphs, or a combination.  
- Unclear whether the main contribution is a new model, a new algorithm, or both; an explicit Contributions section is needed.  
- The definition/usage of ρ_{r+1} is ambiguous and displayed equations need proper punctuation.  
- Figures 1–4 have unreadably small fonts and Figures 1–2 lack a legend explaining color coding.  
- The claim that improved tracking can justify extra computation is speculative and needs a utility trade-off, sensitivity analysis, or application-specific metrics.  
- Novelty is plausible in framing and combining tests with online updates, but incremental contributions (e.g., p-value construction, sequential testing) are not convincingly articulated.  
- The paper should clearly delineate what is genuinely new relative to prior dynamic graphical-model work (types of changes, guarantees, robustness).  
- Impact is limited by imprecise problem statement, underplayed prior work, and heuristic-seeming methods lacking a solid optimality criterion.  
- Theoretical strengthening is needed (error-rate control over time, consistency, or regret-style guarantees) to raise significance.  
- Empirical evaluation should include stronger baselines (Bayesian/penalized likelihood change-point or time-varying methods) and ablation studies.  
- Concrete suggestions: clarify task and assumptions, reposition relative to prior work, detail p-value and multiple-testing handling and guarantees, fix presentation issues, and quantify computation–accuracy trade-offs.  
- Overall: promising and timely work but currently overstates novelty and needs clearer task definition, better positioning, stronger theoretical/empirical support, and improved exposition.
