- Problem: tracks changes in dependency structure in data streams for recursive SEMs.  
- Method: proposes LoSST, combining batch PC structure learning with streaming statistics (weighted means, correlation matrix, effective sample size).  
- Weighting: assigns weights by point “surprisingness” using Mahalanobis distance; estimates p-values via Hotelling’s T² and pools them with Liptak’s method to get a significance ρ.  
- Trigger: uses a noisy-OR with the previous step’s signal to decide when to relearn structure.  
- Experiments: synthetic tests with parameter/structure changes across variable counts/degrees and a real-world US price-index dataset.  
- Pro: addresses an interesting, underexplored problem with room for practical and theoretical follow-up.  
- Pro: algorithm is novel, practical, and computationally efficient, creatively combining existing methods.  
- Pro: experiments are adequate, include parameter sensitivity analysis and a real dataset.  
- Pro: provides a good review of related work.  
- Pro: paper is generally well-written.  
- Con: lacks theoretical analysis or guarantees; should offer conditional guarantees or note as future work.  
- Con: solution relies heavily on heuristics; reweighting, effective sample size, and relearning decisions need clearer justification.  
- Con: potential misuse of statistical tools—Hotelling’s T² for reweighted counts and Liptak pooling assumptions (independence/different sample sizes) may not hold and should be explicitly qualified.  
- Comment: proofs in footnotes may violate formatting norms—move to supplementary material.  
- Comment: figures are too small/unclear, have poor labels/legends, and experiment descriptions lack clarity.  
- Comment: explanation for worse performance on edge removal under parameter-only changes is unconvincing.  
- Comment: recommend decoupling parameter updates from structure updates.  
- Overall: promising, practical heuristic approach that opens an interesting direction for future research.
