This paper proposes a principled approach to structure learning for SEMs when the data are only locally i.i.d.—that is, the data-generating process can change over time. The method adapts constraint-based learning (demonstrated with PC) to time-dependent settings by leveraging local segments to maintain conditional independence testing validity. The paper is clearly written, the idea is simple to implement, and the empirical results suggest meaningful gains over time-agnostic baselines.

Strengths
- Important problem: Many structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data, which is often violated in temporal settings. Addressing local stationarity fills a practical gap.
- Conceptual simplicity: The adaptation is straightforward and appears easy to apply with standard CI testing machinery.
- Empirical evidence: The PC-based experiments are clean and indicate that the proposed approach can recover changing structures more reliably than i.i.d.-based methods.
- Clarity: The exposition is generally easy to follow.

Main suggestions for improvement
1) Scope of claims about generality across algorithms. The manuscript states that “any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly.” This claim is too strong without justification. Different methods (e.g., score-based vs. constraint-based, or ones with different CI tests/regularization) can behave quite differently under distribution shift and with finite samples. I recommend either:
- Rephrasing to make clear that the experiments evaluate a PC-style approach in both time-independent and time-adapted forms, and offering this as a hypothesis for other methods; or
- Providing theoretical or empirical support (e.g., brief experiments with at least one alternative algorithm, or an argument identifying algorithmic properties that guarantee similar behavior).

2) Be explicit about the paper’s scope from the outset. The conclusion notes that directly extending the approach to Bayesian networks may be nontrivial, in part because maintaining sufficient statistics across changing segments is difficult. This is a substantive caveat that affects the generality of the contribution. The paper would benefit from:
- Stating clearly in the introduction that the focus is on SEMs and PC-style, constraint-based learning under local i.i.d. assumptions.
- Outlining which parts of the method rely on properties specific to linear-Gaussian SEMs (or the chosen CI tests) and which might transfer to other models.
- Briefly summarizing what would make BN extensions challenging (e.g., recomputation and reuse of counts/sufficient statistics under changing graphs/parameters, complications with score decomposability) so readers can calibrate expectations.

Additional recommendations to strengthen the paper
- Experimental clarity and robustness:
  - Clearly describe how nonstationarity is introduced (e.g., number/location/magnitude of changes), the segmentation strategy or bandwidth, and any hyperparameter selection procedures.
  - Report sensitivity to segment length/window size and to misspecified change points.
  - Include confidence intervals or variance across runs, and standard metrics such as precision/recall and structural Hamming distance.
  - If feasible, add a small-scale comparison with a different learning paradigm (e.g., a score-based method) to contextualize the claim of generality.
  - Consider a real-world time-varying dataset to demonstrate practical impact.

- Relation to prior work: Situate the contribution relative to time-varying graphical models, dynamic causal discovery, and methods that handle distribution shift or covariate shift in CI testing. Even a brief comparison will help readers understand novelty.

- Practical considerations: Discuss computational overhead versus standard PC (e.g., runtime and number of CI tests as a function of segments) and any trade-offs between localization and statistical power.

- Terminology: Be consistent about “locally i.i.d.” and “piecewise stationary” if used interchangeably, and clarify whether the method assumes abrupt changes (change points) or can accommodate gradual drift.

Minor writing suggestions
- Soften or justify strong generalization claims as noted above.
- Replace “not so simple of a task” with “not straightforward.”
- Fix minor spacing/typos (e.g., “under the assumption,” “to understand,” “even if focused”).
- Where applicable, clarify whether improvements are statistically significant.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed and timely contribution that addresses an underexplored but practically relevant setting: learning SEM structure from time-dependent data under local i.i.d. assumptions. The methodology is clear and appears effective in the presented experiments. The paper would be stronger with a more precise statement of scope, tempered claims about generality across algorithms, and additional experimental details and robustness checks. With these adjustments, the work should be of interest to researchers and practitioners dealing with nonstationary data.

I have read the authors’ feedback and appreciate the clarifications; addressing the above points in the final version would further improve the clarity and impact of the paper.
