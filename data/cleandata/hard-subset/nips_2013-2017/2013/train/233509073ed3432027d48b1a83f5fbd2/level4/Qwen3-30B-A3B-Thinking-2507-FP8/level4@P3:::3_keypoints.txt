- Introduces LoSST, an algorithm for learning graphical model structure in locally stationary (time-varying) environments.
- Addresses a key gap: most structure learning assumes global stationarity, whereas real data can change gradually or abruptly.
- Uses weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) and Mahalanobis distances to detect structural shifts.
- Employs a probabilistic relearning mechanism that balances convergence versus diligence in online learning.
- Builds on prior graphical model work (e.g., Chickering, Spirtes) while removing the stationarity assumption.
- Clear, precise algorithm description with well-explained update equations and intuitive justification.
- Distinguishes LoSST from batch structure learning and from online changepoint methods that track parameters only.
- Evaluation: comprehensive simulations comparing LoSST to batch PC across structure, parameter, and combined change scenarios using edge addition/removal/orientation errors.
- Theoretical analysis rigorously addresses convergence/diligence trade-offs.
- Real-world application to US price index volatility demonstrates practical relevance and correctly identifies the Great Moderation.
- Computationally efficient (single-pass) with minimal performance cost in stable environments, suitable for streaming or large-scale data.
- Strengths: addresses an underaddressed problem, strong theory, broad simulations, practical application, clear writing, and reproducibility.
- Weaknesses: overgeneralized claim that other standard algorithms would perform similarly to PC; needs justification or discussion of score-based methods.
- Scope ambiguity: paper focuses on SEMs but should more clearly state applicability/extensions to Bayesian networks earlier.
- Limited discussion of computational complexity compared to batch methods.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel, well-executed, high practical impact; recommendation for acceptance.
