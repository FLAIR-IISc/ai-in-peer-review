This manuscript presents a systematic approach to make structure learning for structural equation models (SEMs) work with data sets that are only locally i.i.d.—that is, data that may be generated by distinct underlying models over time. The paper is well written and easy to follow, and it addresses an important problem: learning from time-dependent data, which many widely used structure-learning algorithms have neglected. The techniques proposed to extend learning to time-dependent data are simple yet effective, as demonstrated by experiments with the PC method.

I have two suggestions to clarify the paper’s contributions:

1) In the experiments, the paper states that “any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly, ….” This assertion seems too strong, as differences among structure-learning methods can be substantial. It would be better to rephrase and make clear that the experiments concern the PC method in the time-independent setting and an adaptation for time-dependent data (which is very relevant), and then explicitly label as speculation the expectation that other methods would behave similarly; alternatively, if the claim is retained, the paper should explain in detail why it would hold across methods.

2) The conclusion notes that applying the same ideas to learning the structure of Bayesian networks may not be straightforward because of difficulties in maintaining sufficient statistics. Even if this issue is not central to the current proposal, it indicates that extending the same learning procedure to Bayesian networks and other models may require more than a few technical adjustments. The paper would benefit from disclosing this limitation upfront, presenting the work more directly in terms of its focus on SEMs while still noting that extensions to other models may be possible but are not necessarily straightforward. In short, the paper could be sharper about its scope.

Overall, this is a well-written paper about learning graph structure for SEMs under the assumption that data are only locally i.i.d. The formulation is simple to understand and implement, and the experimental results are promising, even though they focus on comparisons using the PC method. I acknowledge having read the authors’ feedback.
