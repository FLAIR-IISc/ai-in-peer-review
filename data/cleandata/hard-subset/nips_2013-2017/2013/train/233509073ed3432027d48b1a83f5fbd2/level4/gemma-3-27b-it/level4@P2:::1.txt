This paper introduces LoSST, a heuristic algorithm for online learning of graphical model structure in non-stationary environments. The method aims to track changes in the underlying generative process, demonstrated through simulations and application to US price index data.

**Quality:** The presented results appear technically sound and well-supported by the simulation results. However, the discussion of related work (Section 2) contains a potentially misleading characterization of score-based algorithms for Bayesian networks. These algorithms typically assume a factorization of the scoring function, rather than requiring data to originate from a single generating model. While LoSST offers flexibility, claiming it’s the *first* to address non-i.i.d. structure learning is inaccurate, as the work of Siracusa and Fisher III (AISTATS 2009) already explores this area, though with a different approach. 

**Clarity:** The paper's presentation is generally good, but improvements are needed to clarify the core learning task. Is the goal to detect change points, infer the structure of the most recent data, or something else?  The distinction between proposing a new model (defining assumptions and learning goals) versus a new algorithm (computational methods) is unclear. The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow, particularly the explanation of ρ<sub>r+1</sub> and its prior definition. Minor stylistic points include consistently using periods at the end of sentences containing displayed equations.  Figures 1-4 would significantly benefit from increased font size and color legends to improve readability. The discussion in Section 6 regarding trading off tracking accuracy against computational cost feels context-dependent and lacks broader justification.

**Originality:**  The work demonstrates originality in its specific approach to tracking time-varying graphical structures. However, the core ideas don't represent a substantial innovation. The potential novelty may lie in the construction of the p-values used for change detection, though a deeper understanding would require further clarification.

**Significance:**  If published, this work could stimulate the development of more advanced, potentially Bayesian, methods for this problem. However, the current approach feels more like an engineering development than fundamental research.  The proposed heuristic method addresses a somewhat ill-defined learning problem, which existing machine learning techniques might already handle effectively.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem of learning in non-stationary environments.
*   Demonstrates promising performance through simulations and a real-world application.
*   Could spur further research in online graphical model learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The claim of novelty is overstated, particularly concerning prior work by Siracusa and Fisher III.
*   The paper lacks a clear articulation of the learning problem and the contribution's placement within existing literature.
*   The heuristic nature of the approach and lack of theoretical guarantees raise concerns about robustness.
*   Clarity issues detract from the overall impact of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a functional algorithm and demonstrates its application, the limitations in clarity, overstated claims of originality, and lack of strong theoretical grounding outweigh the potential benefits. Addressing the clarity issues and more accurately positioning the work within the existing literature could significantly improve the paper’s prospects.
