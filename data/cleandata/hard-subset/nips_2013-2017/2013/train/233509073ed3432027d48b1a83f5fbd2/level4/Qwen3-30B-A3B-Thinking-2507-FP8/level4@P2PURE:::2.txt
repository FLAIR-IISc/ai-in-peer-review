

Paper Summary:  
The paper addresses an interesting and underexplored problem: tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a stream of data points (e.g., time-series data). The authors focus on an efficient (computationally and memory-wise) solution for simpler Structural Equation Models (SEMs). They propose a new algorithm, LoSST, which leverages a standard batch structure-learning algorithm (PC by Spirtes et al., 2000) and tracks key statistics from the data stream to determine when to initiate new structure learning. These statistics include the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix (Equations 1–2), and the weighted sample size. Data points are assigned weights based on their "surprise" level (Equations 3–4), and each point’s Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed. The authors combine the estimated p-values for these MDs using Liptak’s weighted pooling of p-values to derive an overall "significance" statistic, ρ. This statistic, in conjunction with a noisy OR configuration with prior steps, determines whether to trigger new structure learning. The authors evaluate LoSST against vanilla PC batch learning on synthetic datasets where either parameter values or the underlying structure change after a fixed number of data points, varying the number of variables and maximum degree. They also apply the method to a real-world dataset of U.S. price index data (529 monthly observations from 1967 onward, six variables), demonstrating that LoSST detects known economic trends while ignoring transient fluctuations.  

Pros:  
1. The problem tackled is interesting and underexplored, with significant potential for future theoretical and practical development.  
2. The algorithm is novel, practical, and efficient, creatively integrating multiple methods.  
3. Experiments are adequate, examining parameter effects and including a meaningful real-world dataset.  
4. The literature review thoroughly covers prior work on this problem.  
5. The paper is well-written overall.  

Cons:  
1. The most significant omission is the lack of theoretical guarantees under any assumptions. While the authors note the tension between convergence and diligence (p. 4), some form of guarantees under simplifying conditions should be feasible and should be mentioned as a future direction.  
2. LoSST relies heavily on heuristics, which is acceptable for a practical solution but requires clearer documentation of key decisions. The approach involves reweighting, effective sample size estimation, and relearning triggers—decisions that may violate assumptions of the underlying methods. For instance, the authors estimate MD p-values using Hotelling’s T² distribution, which assumes complete, unweighted samples from a distribution, not partially reweighted counts. Similarly, Liptak’s weighted p-value pooling was designed for independent meta-analytic tests with varying sample sizes, not for this context. The authors should explicitly state when methods are used outside their intended guarantees to avoid misleading readers.  

Other Comments:  
- Creative use of footnotes for proofs exceeds typical conventions; supplementary material should be used instead.  
- Figures are too small, contain excessive spacing, lack labels/details in captions, and have missing legends. Experiment descriptions (e.g., top paragraph on p. 7) are unclear.  
- The explanation for LoSST’s poorer performance on edge additions when only parameters change is unsatisfactory.  
- Decoupling parameter updates from structure updates would be desirable.  

This represents a promising direction for future research, with a practical heuristic implementation as a first step.
