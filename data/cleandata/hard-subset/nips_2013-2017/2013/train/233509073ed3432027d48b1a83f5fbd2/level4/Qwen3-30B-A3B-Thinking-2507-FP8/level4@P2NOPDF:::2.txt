

Paper Summary:  
This paper addresses an underexplored problem: tracking evolving dependency structures in streaming data (e.g., time-series). The authors propose LoSST, an efficient algorithm for structural equation models (SEMs), leveraging batch structure learning (PC algorithm, Spirtes et al., 2000) while tracking key statistics from the stream. LoSST monitors weighted variable means, correlation matrices (Eqs. 1–2), and effective sample size. Data points are weighted based on "surprise" (Eq. 3–4), and Mahalanobis distances (MDs) are computed. P-values for MDs are combined via Liptak’s weighted pooling to form a global significance statistic ρ. This ρ, combined with a noisy OR model, triggers structure relearning. Experiments compare LoSST against baseline PC on synthetic data with structural or parametric changes, varying variables and maximum degrees. A real-world analysis of monthly U.S. price index data (529 points, 6 variables, 1967–present) demonstrates LoSST’s ability to detect meaningful economic shifts while filtering transient fluctuations.  

Pros:  
1. **Novel problem focus**: The paper tackles an important yet underdeveloped area, highlighting significant potential for future theoretical and practical advances.  
2. **Practical novelty**: LoSST’s design creatively integrates existing methods (e.g., weighted statistics, p-value pooling) into an efficient streaming solution.  
3. **Adequate experiments**: Synthetic tests systematically evaluate parameter impacts, and the real-world case study adds tangible relevance.  
4. **Comprehensive literature review**: Previous work on streaming structure learning is well-summarized and contextualized.  
5. **Clear writing**: The paper is well-structured and accessible.  

Cons:  
1. **Lack of theoretical guarantees**: The absence of convergence or detection guarantees is a critical gap. While the authors note the tension between convergence and detection sensitivity (p. 4), even simplified theoretical assurances under specific assumptions would strengthen the contribution. This should be explicitly acknowledged as future work.  
2. **Heuristic dependencies without caveats**: LoSST’s reliance on heuristics (e.g., Hotelling’s T² for MD p-values, Liptak’s pooling) lacks proper contextualization. Hotelling’s T² assumes unweighted, complete samples—not reweighted or partial data. Similarly, Liptak’s method is designed for independent meta-analyses with varying sample sizes, not streaming statistics. The authors should explicitly state where their approach operates outside established methodological guarantees to prevent misinterpretation.  

Other Comments:  
- **Footnotes for proofs**: The unconventional use of footnotes for technical derivations is inappropriate; supplementary material should house these details.  
- **Figures and presentation**: Figures are poorly scaled, lack labels/legends, and have excessive spacing. Experimental descriptions (e.g., top of p. 7) are unclear and require revision.  
- **Edge addition performance**: The explanation for LoSST’s worse performance on edge additions (when only parameters change) is insufficient. Decoupling parameter updates from structure updates would likely improve robustness.  
- **Real-world analysis**: The U.S. price index case study is promising but needs clearer linkage between detected changes and known economic events.  

Overall, this work presents a valuable, pragmatic approach to a challenging problem. While LoSST’s heuristic design is justified for immediate applicability, the lack of theoretical grounding and insufficient contextualization of methodological departures from established practices are notable limitations. Future work should prioritize theoretical guarantees and clearer documentation of heuristic choices. The real-world validation is a strong asset, but presentation issues (figures, clarity) need addressing.  

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions to address theoretical gaps and presentation flaws).
