To the author's response: I cannot agree with point (3): The work of Siracusa III and Fischer (AISTATS 2009) does not assume that the data are iid, and it allows for the "generated structure" to change (even if only in a small set of graphical models). While the currently proposed method is, in some senses, even more flexible, I would find it misleading to claim that the proposed method is the first method to solve the problem.

*   The paper presents a hypothesis-test based approach for learning graphical models from time series data in the online fashion. The proposed method is specifically targeted for scenarios where the "generated data" graphical model may change relatively frequently. The performance of the method has been demonstrated using both simulated and real data.

**Quality**

The results appear to be correct. However, I find the description of the related work (in section 2) somewhat misleading: the specific score-based algorithm for learning Bayesian networks does not assume that the data come from a single generated model – rather, they assume that the scoring function is a factor in local terms. It is true that the models typically studied assume exchangeability (or, iid data), but this is related to the model, not the algorithm.

**Clarity**

While the presentation of the paper is mostly fine, there is room for improvement. Most importantly, the paper is somewhat unclear about what the actual learning task in question is: estimating the change points (under some well-established assumptions about the existence and nature of the change points) or estimating the generated structure of the observed final data point, or something else? Furthermore, it is difficult to tell whether the work is proposing a (new) model (i.e., the construction of assumptions and learning goals) or a (new) algorithm (i.e., the computational means to achieve the learning goals) or both.

The paragraph at the end of page 3 is difficult to follow; for example, does the equation given for \rho_{r+1} define it, or is the definition given first? I wonder why the author does not use the period "." when concluding the sentence with the equation displayed. Almost equally important is the font size in Figure 1-4: can it be doubled? Furthermore, I would not mind having a description for the different colors in Figures 1 and 2. In section 6, I was confused by the idea that better tracking could compensate for the additional computational cost – does this tracking not depend on some very domain-specific evaluation of accuracy versus computational cost?

**Originality**

The work looks original. Yet it seems that there is no real innovation or novel idea in it. Perhaps there is something in the construction of the p-values (which I, unfortunately, do not fully understand).

**Importance**

The potential importance of the current work (if published) is that it will quickly lead to the development of better, possibly Bayesian, methods for the problem. It should really be seen as a reverse development of research. A somewhat vaguely defined approach to a learning problem that has already been solved by existing machine learning machinery.
