Summary
The paper addresses the problem of learning and tracking time-varying graphical structure in streaming data, where the generative model may change over time. The authors propose LoSST, a heuristic online method that maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an effective sample size) and triggers occasional relearning of DAG structure using a standard constraint-based method (PC). Weights for new data are adapted based on how surprising the observations are under the current Gaussian approximation: each point’s Mahalanobis distance is converted to a p-value (via Hotelling’s T^2 with “effective” sample size), and p-values are pooled over time using Lipták’s method to produce a statistic ρ. This ρ both adjusts the weights (higher weights when recent data fit poorly) and governs a probabilistic “noisy-OR” trigger for relearning. The paper analyzes the convergence–diligence trade-off induced by two parameters (T, γ), presents simulations comparing LoSST to batch PC under stationary and nonstationary scenarios, and gives a short case study on U.S. price index volatility.

Relation to prior work
The paper clearly situates LoSST relative to batch structure learning (score- and constraint-based), online change-point detection (Bayesian and frequentist), and time-varying graph estimation that requires batch data (e.g., Talih & Hengartner; Siracusa & Fisher). The key differentiator is real-time, single-pass tracking of structure without storing large data windows. The focus on linear SEMs is sensible for tractability, and the authors note extensions to undirected models.

Strengths
- Problem importance: Time-varying structure is common in real data streams; the paper targets a recognized gap between batch structure learning and online CPD.
- Practicality and efficiency: The sufficient-statistic updates are incremental and memory-light; LoSST can operate in a single pass.
- Novelty: The combination of weighted sufficient-statistic tracking, pooled p-values, and probabilistic relearning is an inventive, pragmatic approach.
- Empirics: Simulations examine both structure and parameter changes, with multiple graph sizes/degrees, and a real-data case study shows plausible detections aligned with known macroeconomic regimes.
- Clarity of positioning: Related work is reviewed thoughtfully, and the convergence vs. diligence discussion is useful.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Statistical validity of the surprise metric: The p-values for Mahalanobis distances are computed using Hotelling’s T^2 with “effective” sample size Sr based on adaptive reweighting. This deviates from the standard assumptions (i.i.d. sampling, unweighted covariance estimation), and temporal dependence among p-values further challenges Lipták’s pooling (which assumes independent tests). Please (a) state explicitly that these uses are heuristic and outside the classical guarantees, and (b) provide empirical calibration (e.g., null simulations) to assess false-alarm rates for ρ under stationarity and to quantify sensitivity/specificity at different T, γ, ν.
- Numerical stability: Inverting Cr for the Mahalanobis distance can be ill-conditioned when the effective sample size is small or dimensionality is high. Clarify whether you use shrinkage/regularization for Cr, how you handle near-singularity, and how these choices affect detection latency and false positives.
- Use of PC with weighted statistics: PC’s conditional independence tests typically depend on (unweighted) sample covariances and sample size. Specify how you adapt PC to work with weighted sufficient statistics and the effective sample size, and whether Type I error rates are preserved.
- Ablations and sensitivity: Provide a more systematic sensitivity analysis of T, γ, and ν (beyond the illustrative settings), and, if possible, ablate the pooled-p-value mechanism versus simpler alternatives (e.g., EWMA on Mahalanobis distances).

Clarity
- Presentation: Figures are too small, with sparse labels and captions. Expand fonts, add clear legends, axis labels, and units. Improve the description of experimental setups (e.g., the relearning schedule and parameter settings) so the reader can follow without cross-referencing.
- Footnotes with proofs: Move proof sketches and technical conditions to a supplement to improve flow, and keep the main text focused on intuition and key conditions.
- Explanation of parameter-change behavior: The current explanation for transiently worse edge-removal performance when only parameters change is terse. A clearer analysis of why PC temporarily adds edges (spurious dependencies) while LoSST becomes conservative (shrinking Sr) would help, ideally with a simple illustrative example.

Originality
- The approach is novel in its specific combination and online operation. It would be helpful to broaden the related work discussion to include time-varying Gaussian graphical models and dynamic Bayesian networks learned with windowing, kernel smoothing, or fused penalties, and to clarify how LoSST differs in assumptions, memory, and responsiveness.

Significance
- The simulations suggest LoSST matches batch PC under stationarity and outperforms it under local nonstationarity, which is promising. Please report runtime and memory use versus batch baselines, and include more challenging regimes (e.g., higher dimensionality, faster switching, or slowly drifting parameters) to delineate the method’s limits.
- Reproducibility: Include implementation details (initialization of µ and C, regularization, exact T, γ, ν used per experiment), and release code if possible.

Pro acceptance
- Addresses a timely, underexplored problem with a simple, general, and efficient algorithm.
- Strong empirical indications that LoSST adapts quickly after change points while remaining competitive under stationarity.
- Clear practical contribution likely to spur follow-up work on online structure learning.

Con acceptance
- Heavy reliance on heuristics without theoretical guarantees; key statistical components are used outside their standard conditions.
- Limited analysis of calibration, sensitivity, and numerical stability; figures and experimental descriptions need significant polishing.
- Limited discussion of how to decouple parameter updates from structure updates; this coupling may cause suboptimal behavior when only parameters drift.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The problem is important and the proposed solution is practical and novel, with encouraging empirical results. I would support acceptance if the authors (i) clarify the heuristic nature of the statistical tests and provide empirical calibration, (ii) improve figures and experimental detail, (iii) discuss numerical stability and PC adaptation to weighted statistics, and (iv) expand related work and sensitivity analyses.
