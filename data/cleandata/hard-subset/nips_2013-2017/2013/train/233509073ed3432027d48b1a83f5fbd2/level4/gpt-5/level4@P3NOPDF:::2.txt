Paper summary:
This paper addresses the underexplored problem of detecting changes in the dependency structure among variables in a data stream, focusing on linear SEMs. The proposed method, LoSST, is designed to be computationally and memory efficient. LoSST uses a standard batch structure-learning algorithm (PC; Spirtes et al., 2000) when a change is suspected, and in the interim tracks summary statistics to decide when to trigger relearning. Specifically, it maintains (i) deviations from weighted means of each variable, (ii) a weighted correlation matrix (Eqs. 1–2), and (iii) an effective weighted sample size. Each incoming data point is assigned a weight based on how “surprising” it is, measured via its Mahalanobis distance (MD). Per-step MD p-values are approximated using Hotelling’s T^2 distribution and combined across variables using Lipták’s weighted pooling to form a global significance statistic ρ. A noisy-OR over current and previous ρ values determines whether to initiate new structure learning. The authors compare LoSST to a baseline that repeatedly applies batch PC on synthetic data with controlled parameter and structure changes under varying dimensionality and graph degree. They also analyze a real dataset of U.S. price indices (529 monthly observations starting in 1967; six variables), finding that LoSST detects several well-known macroeconomic shifts while ignoring short-lived fluctuations.

Strengths:
- Tackles an important and relatively underexplored problem with broad applicability in streaming causal discovery and model monitoring.
- Proposes a practical, efficient algorithm that cleverly combines techniques from causal discovery, change detection, and meta-analysis.
- Experimental evaluation explores different numbers of variables and degrees, includes both parameter and structure changes, and adds a real dataset.
- Related work is well surveyed and contextualized.
- Generally clear and readable.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Lack of theoretical guarantees. The paper explicitly notes the tension between reactivity and stability, but provides no formal guarantees (e.g., false alarm rates, detection delays, or consistency under simplifying assumptions). Even partial results—such as bounds under stationarity within epochs, or guarantees for parameter-only changes—would strengthen the contribution. At minimum, clearly delineating theoretical open problems and plausible assumptions for future analysis would be valuable.
- Heavy reliance on heuristics whose statistical validity is unclear in this context:
  - The MD p-values are approximated using Hotelling’s T^2 with the sample size set to the effective sample size S^r. Hotelling’s T^2 is derived for i.i.d. samples from a fixed distribution, not for reweighted or partially effective counts from a nonstationary process. It is unclear whether the resulting p-values are calibrated under the weighting scheme.
  - Lipták’s weighted pooling of p-values is typically used for independent tests with differing sample sizes. Here, variables may be correlated, weights are data-dependent, and the per-variable tests may not be independent even conditionally. This likely violates assumptions behind the combined p-values.
  - The noisy-OR rule to accumulate evidence over time is intuitive but ad hoc; its operating characteristics (e.g., effect on false alarms and detection delays) are not analyzed.
  These choices are sensible for a first practical attempt, but the paper should clearly state where assumptions are being stretched and discuss potential implications for calibration and error control.
- Experimental clarity and presentation:
  - Several figures are too small, have excessive white space, and lack complete labels, legends, or adequately descriptive captions. This makes it hard to interpret results.
  - The description of the experimental setup (e.g., p. 7, top paragraph) is insufficiently clear about how and when changes are introduced, how hyperparameters are set, and how performance metrics are computed.
- Interpretation issues:
  - The explanation for LoSST’s weaker performance on edge addition when only parameters change is not convincing. If the structure is fixed, triggering structural relearning should be rare; if it occurs, why does it harm performance? A deeper analysis or ablation (e.g., disabling structure updates while allowing parameter adaptation) would help.
  - More generally, it seems desirable to decouple parameter updates from structure updates, with separate triggers or thresholds. This could reduce unnecessary structure relearning and improve robustness to parameter-only drift.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Theory and diagnostics:
  - Provide at least partial guarantees under simplifying assumptions (e.g., Gaussian linear SEM with bounded-degree graph and stationary intervals) or characterize limits of detectability for parameter vs. structure changes.
  - Calibrate or empirically validate the p-values and the combined statistic ρ (e.g., via simulation under the null to assess calibration and false alarm rates).
  - Analyze the effect of the noisy-OR accumulation on detection delay and false positive rate; consider alternatives (e.g., CUSUM-like accumulation) as an ablation.
- Methodological clarity:
  - Clearly document all heuristic choices: weighting scheme, definition and use of S^r, thresholds for ρ, and the exact relearning policy. State explicitly where classical assumptions are violated and that guarantees do not directly transfer.
  - Consider decoupling parameter adaptation from structure relearning and report results for each trigger independently.
- Experiments and presentation:
  - Improve figure readability: larger plots, complete axis labels and units, consistent legends, and self-contained captions.
  - Clarify the synthetic data protocol (timing and magnitude of changes, number of runs, random seeds, metrics with confidence intervals).
  - Add ablations on key design choices (weighting scheme, forgetting factor/effective sample size, pooling method, and noisy-OR vs. alternatives).
  - If feasible, compare against standard change-point detection baselines applied to summary statistics (e.g., covariance changes) to contextualize LoSST’s benefits.
- Writing and organization:
  - Move lengthy footnote “proofs” and technical digressions to a supplementary section; keep the main text focused.
  - Briefly restate the PC assumptions and discuss how violations due to nonstationarity might affect conditional independence tests during relearning.

Overall assessment:
This is a timely and practically useful contribution that takes an important first step toward online detection of structural changes in SEMs. The algorithm is thoughtful and efficient, and the empirical results are promising. However, the paper leans heavily on heuristics whose statistical properties are unclear in this setting, and the presentation (figures, experimental details) needs strengthening. Clarifying assumptions, acknowledging where guarantees do not carry over, and adding calibration/ablation studies would substantially improve the paper. Despite these concerns, I find the direction compelling and the approach a valuable starting point for further theoretical and methodological development.
