1. Paper addresses streaming changes in dependency structure in SEMs and proposes a novel algorithm, LoSST.  
2. LoSST builds on the PC algorithm and tracks weighted mean deviations, the correlation matrix, and an effective sample size to decide when to relearn structure.  
3. Points are weighted by “surprise” and Mahalanobis distances; p-values are combined via Liptak pooling into a global statistic ρ that, with a noisy-OR prior, triggers relearning.  
4. Experiments compare LoSST to batch PC on synthetic controlled-change datasets and include a real-world US price-index case study.  
5. Problem is well-motivated and underexplored.  
6. LoSST is a novel, practical approach that creatively leverages diverse techniques for efficiency.  
7. Experiments are adequate, systematically probing parameters and offering a compelling real-world validation.  
8. Literature review is thorough and well-contextualized.  
9. Paper is generally well written.  
10. Major gap: no theoretical guarantees or convergence results under any assumptions; should be acknowledged and targeted as future work.  
11. Heavy reliance on heuristics (reweighting, effective sample size, relearning triggers) without clear justification or transparency.  
12. Statistical misuse: Hotelling’s T² p-values are applied where weighted/effective sample sizes invalidate the distributional assumptions.  
13. Methodological mismatch: Liptak’s pooling is designed for independent meta-analysis, not sequential streaming tests, and this misuse isn’t explicitly flagged.  
14. Move nonstandard/creative footnote proofs to supplementary material for clarity.  
15. Figures are too small, poorly labeled, and have unclear captions; experimental descriptions need clearer presentation.  
16. Explanation for poorer performance on edge additions (when only parameters change) is unsatisfactory; recommend decoupling parameter updates from structure updates.  
17. Overall: promising, practical first step for streaming structure learning, but weakened by lack of theoretical grounding and insufficient contextualization of methodological choices.
