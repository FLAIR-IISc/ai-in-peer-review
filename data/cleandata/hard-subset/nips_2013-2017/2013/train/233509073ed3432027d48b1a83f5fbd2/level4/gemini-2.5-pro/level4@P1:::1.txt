This paper presents LoSST, a heuristic, hypothesis-testing-based algorithm for learning graphical model structure from streaming data. The method is designed for non-stationary environments where the underlying data-generating process may change over time. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics online, dynamically re-weighting them based on how well new data points fit the current model. A poor fit, measured by Mahalanobis distance and aggregated using a pooled p-value, increases the weight of recent data, allowing the algorithm to adapt to structural changes. The authors demonstrate the algorithm's performance on both simulated and real-world economic data, showing that it outperforms a standard batch-mode learner when the underlying structure is not static.

The primary strength of this paper is that it addresses an important and practical problem. Standard structure learning algorithms that assume i.i.d. data are not applicable in many real-world scenarios where system dynamics can change. The proposed online approach is a sensible direction for tackling this challenge. However, the paper has several weaknesses, primarily related to the clarity of the problem formulation and the heuristic nature of the proposed solution.

### Quality and Originality

The technical results of the simulations appear to be correct. However, the framing of the work's contribution and its relationship to prior art could be improved.

The claim that this is the first method to address learning in non-stationary environments seems overstated. For instance, the work of Siracusa and Fisher (AISTATS 2009) also handles time-varying structures, albeit within a more restricted model space. While the proposed LoSST algorithm may be more flexible, the discussion of related work should be more precise about its specific novelties compared to existing approaches.

Furthermore, the characterization of standard learning algorithms in Section 2 is somewhat misleading. The paper states that score-based algorithms assume a single generating model. It would be more accurate to say that these algorithms typically assume the scoring function factorizes (a property of the algorithm), while the i.i.d. assumption pertains to the model being scored. This is an important distinction that should be clarified.

While the specific combination of techniques in LoSST is novel, the work seems to lack a core, innovative theoretical idea. The main contribution appears to be the specific heuristic for weighting data points based on pooled p-values.

### Clarity

While the paper is mostly readable, its clarity could be significantly improved in several key areas. A major weakness is the lack of a clear, formal problem definition. It is difficult to ascertain the precise learning objective: is it to detect changepoints, to infer the graphical structure at the most recent time step, or something else? This ambiguity makes it hard to evaluate the work's contributions rigorously. It is also unclear whether the primary contribution is a new probabilistic model for time-varying graphs or a heuristic algorithm for a previously defined problem.

Several specific parts of the paper are difficult to follow:
*   The paragraph at the end of page 3 describing the pooled p-value (ρ_r+1) is confusing. It is not clear if the equation provided is its definition or a result derived from a previous one.
*   The argument in Section 6 that improved tracking could compensate for additional computational cost is underdeveloped. This trade-off seems highly domain-specific, and the assertion requires more justification.
*   Minor presentation issues detract from readability. Sentences ending in displayed equations should be properly punctuated with periods. The font size in Figures 1-4 is too small. Figures 1 and 2 are missing legends to identify the different lines by color.

### Significance

The paper addresses a significant problem, but the proposed solution is a complex heuristic applied to an unclearly defined learning task. In its current form, the work feels more like an engineering solution than a fundamental research contribution. The main impact of this work might be in highlighting this important problem setting, which could stimulate further research into more principled methods, perhaps based on Bayesian frameworks. It is surprising that a more formal approach using existing machine learning tools was not pursued.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for:**
*   Addresses the important and practical problem of learning graphical models in non-stationary environments.
*   The proposed online algorithm is intuitive and appears to perform well in the empirical evaluations, outperforming a batch-mode baseline in dynamic settings.

**Arguments against:**
*   The learning problem itself is not clearly or formally defined.
*   The proposed method is a complex heuristic, lacking a strong theoretical or principled foundation.
*   Claims of originality are somewhat overstated, and the discussion of related work could be more accurate and nuanced.
*   The paper's clarity is hampered by an ambiguous problem statement and several presentation issues.
