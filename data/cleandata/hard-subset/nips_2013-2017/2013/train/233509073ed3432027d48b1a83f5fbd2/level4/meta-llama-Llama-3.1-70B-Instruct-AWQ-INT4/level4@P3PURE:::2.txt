Paper Summary:

The paper addresses an interesting and under-explored problem of tracking changes in the underlying dependency structure between variables in a stream of data points, such as time-based data. The authors focus on developing an efficient solution, both computationally and memory-wise, for simpler Structural Equation Models (SEMs). They propose a new algorithm, LoSST, which utilizes a standard batch learning algorithm for underlying structure learning (PC by Spirtes et al., 2000) and tracks several statistics from the data stream to determine whether to initiate new structure learning. The tracked statistics include the distance from the weighted mean of each variable, the correlation matrix (Equations 1 and 2), and the weighted sample size. A weighting scheme is used for each new point based on how "surprising" the new data point is (Equations 3 and 4). Each point's Mahalanobis distance (MD) is computed, and the estimated p-values for the MDs are combined using Liptak's weighted pooling of p-values to compute an overall "significance" statistic ρ. This statistic is then used in a noisy OR configuration with the previous step to decide whether to start a new structure learning process. The authors compare LoSST to vanilla PC batch learning on synthetic data sets, where either a change in parameters or a change of structure is introduced after a fixed set of data points, testing for different numbers of variables and maximum degree configurations. Finally, they perform a short analysis of real-life data from the US price index with 529 data points (every month, starting at 1967) and six variables, demonstrating that the algorithm detects some known changes/trends in the US economy while ignoring short-lived changes.

Pros:

1. The paper tackles an interesting and under-explored problem, as noted by the authors, leaving room for additional development of practical solutions for these and other models, as well as matching theory and theoretical guarantees.
2. The algorithm is novel and practical/efficient, making creative use of various methods for this task.
3. The experiments seem adequate, attempting to track the effect of various parameters and including an interesting real-life data set.
4. The authors provide a good review of previous work on this problem.
5. The paper is overall well-written.

Cons:

1. The biggest limitation of the paper is the lack of theory or guarantees under some assumptions. Convergence and diligence are, as the authors point out (page 4), opposing forces, but it seems plausible that some form of guarantees under simplifying conditions could be developed. At least, this could be mentioned as a direction for future work.
2. The solution proposed by the authors, LoSST, relies heavily on heuristics. While this enables the authors to develop a practical solution, the decisions involved should be more clearly noted. Since tracking the mean and correlation matrix is straightforward, these decisions primarily involve reweighting, effective sample size, and when/how to relearn. The authors make creative use of some methods under conditions that likely void their guarantees. For example, they estimate the MD p-value using Hotelling's T2 distribution, setting the sample size N to be the effective sample size S^r. However, this distribution was developed for complete counts from a given distribution, not for partial/reweighted counts. If the authors actually sampled from these nets/structures with these partial weights, would the distribution follow this form? Similarly, Liptak's weighted p-values were developed for meta-analysis using independent tests with different sample sizes. At least, if the methods are used outside their guarantees/context, the authors should clearly state this, so readers do not get the wrong impression/understanding.

Other comments:

- The authors make creative use of footnotes to outline proofs, which is probably beyond the intended use by Leslie Lamport or the conference organizers. Consider using supplementary materials for this and other missing details.
- Figures are too small, with unnecessary space between them, missing labels, missing details in the captions, legends, etc. The description of the experiments is not clear enough (e.g., the top paragraph on page 7 is cryptic).
- The explanation of why LoSST performs worse on edge addition when only the parameters are changed is not satisfactory.
- It seems desirable to decouple sparking parameter updates and structure updates.
This is a really interesting direction for future research, with a practical, though heuristic, implementation as a first attempt.
