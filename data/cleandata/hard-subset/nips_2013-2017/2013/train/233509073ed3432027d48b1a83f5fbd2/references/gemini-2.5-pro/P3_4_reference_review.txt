### Summary

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from data streams where the underlying generative model may change over time. The core idea is to maintain and update weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an "effective" sample size) in an online fashion. The weight assigned to new data points is dynamically adjusted based on how well they fit the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. A poor fit, indicated by a sequence of low p-values, increases the weight of new data, effectively "forgetting" older data and allowing the algorithm to adapt to a new structure. The paper situates this work relative to batch structure learning, standard online learning (e.g., TD-learning), and changepoint detection. It provides a theoretical analysis of the algorithm's behavior in terms of a trade-off between "convergence" (stability in a stationary environment) and "diligence" (responsiveness to change), which is controlled by key hyperparameters. The method's efficacy is demonstrated through simulations on synthetic data with abrupt changes and an application to real-world economic data.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Problem Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and challenging problem. Standard structure learning algorithms' i.i.d. assumption is frequently violated in real-world streaming data settings. LoSST provides a complete, end-to-end solution that is novel in its specific combination of online sufficient statistic tracking, a principled (if heuristic) change detection mechanism, and a probabilistic trigger for computationally expensive relearning. It successfully bridges the gap between static structure learning and simpler online tracking methods.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The core approach is intuitive and technically sound. Using the Mahalanobis distance as a measure of "surprise" is a standard and appropriate choice for multivariate Gaussian data. The use of Liptak's method to pool p-values is a clever way to avoid overreacting to single outliers while remaining sensitive to persistent, subtle shifts. The online update equations for the weighted sufficient statistics are correctly derived.

3.  **Theoretical Grounding:** A major strength is the discussion of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off (Section 3.1). This elevates the work from a purely heuristic method to one whose behavior is better understood. By showing how different parameter settings (`γ`, `T`) lead to provably convergent or diligent regimes, the authors provide a valuable conceptual framework for understanding and tuning the algorithm. This is a sophisticated and important contribution.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The simulations are well-designed. They convincingly demonstrate that LoSST performs comparably to a batch learner (PC algorithm) in a stable environment, thus "incurring no additional costs in 'normal' situations." Crucially, they also show its superior ability to adapt after a changepoint. The distinction between structural+parameter changes and parameter-only changes is insightful and highlights a nuanced aspect of the algorithm's performance. The final simulation with a probabilistic relearning schedule effectively demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed triggering mechanism. The real-world application, while not a ground-truth evaluation, provides a plausible and illustrative use case.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Scope and Related Work:** The paper focuses exclusively on recursive Structural Equation Models (SEMs) with linear-Gaussian assumptions. This simplifies the problem to tracking the covariance matrix. While the authors mention extending to other models, the current reliance on the Mahalanobis distance and covariance is a significant limitation. There is a vast literature on online learning of Gaussian Graphical Models (i.e., estimating a sparse precision matrix), often using L1-regularization (graphical lasso). Methods involving forgetting factors or time-varying graphical lasso are highly relevant and should have been discussed and potentially compared against, as they represent a major alternative approach to the same problem.

2.  **Heuristic Nature and Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm relies on several key heuristics and hyperparameters. The specific functional form for updating the weights (`ar+1` in Eq. 4) and the noisy-OR gate for triggering relearning are chosen without strong theoretical justification. The paper honestly states that `T=0.05` and `γ=3` were chosen via "unsystematic investigation." While the diligence/convergence analysis provides a framework, it doesn't offer a practical guide for setting these parameters. The performance of the algorithm seems highly dependent on these choices, and a sensitivity analysis would have significantly strengthened the paper's claims.

3.  **Clarity on Proofs:** The proofs for convergence and diligence are presented as "sketches." While space is limited in a conference paper, these are central claims about the algorithm's properties. The sketches are quite high-level, and their full justification is not immediately obvious. Providing the full proofs in an appendix would be essential for ensuring the technical correctness and reproducibility of these theoretical claims.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant, practical, and under-explored problem in the NIPS community.
*   The proposed LoSST algorithm is a novel and complete system that effectively integrates several well-motivated ideas.
*   The theoretical analysis of the convergence/diligence trade-off is a strong, non-trivial contribution that adds depth and understanding.
*   The empirical results are comprehensive, well-analyzed, and strongly support the paper's claims.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The work is limited to the linear-Gaussian case and fails to engage with the significant related literature on online precision matrix estimation (e.g., time-varying graphical lasso).
*   The core mechanism for updating weights and triggering relearning is heuristic and its performance is sensitive to hyperparameters that lack a clear selection methodology.
*   Key theoretical claims are backed only by "proof sketches," which limits the verifiability of the results.

### Final Recommendation

This is a strong paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and thoroughly evaluated solution to an important problem. The strengths, particularly the originality of the complete system and the insightful theoretical framing of the convergence/diligence trade-off, are significant. The weaknesses, while notable, are largely matters of scope and could be addressed in future work. The omission of related work on online graphical lasso is the most serious concern, but it doesn't invalidate the contribution of the proposed approach. Overall, the paper presents a clear advance in the state of the art for online graphical model learning. I recommend acceptance.
