Summary
The paper proposes LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a heuristic online algorithm for learning time-varying graphical structure in linear Gaussian SEMs. The core idea is to assume local i.i.d. segments and maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) that adapt online to changes in the data-generating process. Adaptation is driven by a pooled p-value ρ computed from the sequence of Mahalanobis distances, using Liptak’s method. When recent data fit poorly (low ρ), new observations are upweighted via ar+1, shrinking the effective sample and emphasizing recent structure. Structure learning is performed by the PC algorithm on the current sufficient statistics, with a probabilistic relearning schedule that increases the chance to relearn as ρ decreases. The paper discusses a convergence–diligence trade-off controlled by parameters T and γ, provides proof sketches for limiting behaviors, and demonstrates performance on synthetic data with changepoints (structure and/or parameter changes) and a small real data case study (U.S. price indices), where LoSST appears to detect known macroeconomic volatility shifts.

Relation to prior work
The work sits between online change detection (e.g., Bayesian and frequentist CPD) and structure learning in graphical models (score- and constraint-based methods). It differs from offline time-varying graphical model methods (e.g., Talih & Hengartner for undirected graphs; Siracusa & Fisher for Bayesian time-varying edges) by being online and frequentist, avoiding maintenance of posteriors over superexponential model spaces. It also differs from standard online TD-style tracking by explicitly targeting structure, not just parameters. However, relevant lines of work in time-varying Gaussian graphical models and dynamic Bayesian/dynamic network structure learning (e.g., sliding windows/EWMA, fused lasso penalties, or BOCPD + retraining) deserve more discussion as baselines and context.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Online tracking of changing causal/graphical structure is important in many domains.
- Simple, modular design: Weighted sufficient statistics + off-the-shelf PC; can extend to other learners.
- Single-pass memory footprint for stats; intuitive relearning schedule tied to lack-of-fit.
- Clear exposition of the convergence–diligence trade-off and how parameters mediate it.
- Synthetic experiments show LoSST matches batch PC pre-change and substantially outperforms it post-change when data are mixtures; the probabilistic relearning study shows minimal loss vs continual relearning.
- Real data case study suggests ability to detect macro volatility regime shifts.

Weaknesses
- Statistical validity of the change signal: The use of Hotelling’s T^2 p-values with an adaptively and non-uniformly weighted covariance and mean is only heuristic. Distributional assumptions (and the mapping from “effective” sample size Sr to T^2 parameters) are not justified; successive Mahalanobis p-values are dependent, yet pooled as if combining independent tests.
- Effective sample size update Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1)Sr + 1 is ad hoc; no proof it yields unbiased or well-calibrated tests or CIs for partial correlations used by PC.
- Baselines: The main comparison is against batch PC on the full data, which is a strawman under drift. Missing comparisons to sliding-window PC, EWMA covariance + PC, BOCPD + retraining PC, or time-varying GGM/DBN methods with similar capacity.
- Theory: Convergence/diligence results are high level and proof sketches are informal; no bounds on false alarm rates, detection delays, or structural recovery after change. No sample complexity characterization in terms of dimension, sparsity, and change magnitude.
- Practical details under-specified: How are partial correlations and CI tests computed from weighted stats? What α-levels/max conditioning set are used in PC? How is covariance inversion stabilized online (regularization)? Computational complexity and scalability are not analyzed; maintaining inverse each step can be O(p^3).
- Hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) are set by “unsystematic investigation”; no sensitivity study or guidance.
- Real-data validation is anecdotal with no structural ground truth or predictive evaluation.

Quality
The algorithm is sound as a heuristic and implemented reasonably, but some probabilistic steps are not theoretically justified (T^2 calibration with weighted stats; Liptak pooling under dependence). Experimental evidence is positive but limited by weak baselines and missing ablations/sensitivity. Proof sketches help intuition but do not replace guarantees on detection or recovery.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized, with useful intuition. Some typos/formatting issues (e.g., p = N vs p = V for T^2; lingering footnote markers; “graph2”/“inverse3”). Important implementation details for PC testing from weighted stats should be added for reproducibility.

Originality
Moderately novel combination: online adaptive weighting of sufficient statistics tied to a pooled lack-of-fit test, used to trigger constraint-based structure relearning. While elements (EWMA, Mahalanobis, CPD, PC) are known, their integration for online structure tracking and the convergence–diligence framing are interesting.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners needing simple online structure tracking without full Bayesian posteriors. Impact would be stronger with competitive baselines, guarantees, and scalability analysis.

Questions and suggestions
- Justify or empirically validate the calibration of Mahalanobis/T^2 p-values with weighted stats and dependent sequences; consider permutation or block-bootstrap calibration, or explicit EWMA/GLR change scores.
- Compare to sliding-window PC and EWMA+PC with tuned window/decay; and to BOCPD + periodic retraining of PC; include time-varying GGM (e.g., fused lasso) on the undirected skeleton.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν; show detection delay and false alarm trade-offs.
- Detail the CI test used in PC (Fisher z? α? max conditioning size) and how weighted partial correlations and degrees of freedom are computed from Sr. Include regularization for covariance inversion.
- Analyze runtime and memory vs p and graph density; discuss numerical stability.
- For the real data, add predictive checks (rolling forecast likelihood) and/or stability of learned edges across relearnings.

Pros for acceptance
- Tackles an important, underexplored problem with a simple, practical approach.
- Clear intuition and modular design; can be a baseline for online structure tracking.
- Positive synthetic results and an illustrative real case.

Cons for acceptance
- Heuristic statistical foundations without calibration; missing strong baselines.
- Limited theory beyond sketches; no detection/accuracy guarantees.
- Under-specified implementation and reproducibility details.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and the method is practical, but the empirical evaluation needs stronger baselines and ablations, and the statistical underpinnings of the change signal should be better justified or calibrated. With these improvements, the contribution could be significant. Confidence: medium.
