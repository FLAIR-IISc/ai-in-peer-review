Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic algorithm for online learning of time-varying graphical model structure, focused on linear-Gaussian recursive SEMs (DAGs). The key idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariance, and an “effective” sample size) that adapt to changes in the data-generating process. Adaptation is driven by a pooled p-value (via Liptak’s method) of sequential Mahalanobis distances between incoming data and the current Gaussian estimate; poor fit increases the weight of recent data (ar), shrinking the effective sample size Sr and prompting more frequent relearning of structure with the PC algorithm. The paper provides update equations for the sufficient statistics, a strategy for converting pooled p-values into adaptive weights and relearning probabilities, and a discussion of “convergence vs diligence” regimes determined by parameters γ and T, with proof sketches. Simulations on synthetic SEMs suggest LoSST matches batch PC in stationary periods and outperforms it after changes; a real-data case study on CPI categories qualitatively detects known macroeconomic regime shifts.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself between online changepoint detection (which often buffers data and assumes abrupt changes) and batch time-varying graphical structure learning (e.g., Talih & Hengartner, Siracusa & Fisher). The approach differs from Bayesian dynamic models by using frequentist sufficient-statistics tracking and from sliding-window approaches by learning adaptive data weights through pooled p-values. Related literatures that seem pertinent but are not discussed include: exponentially weighted covariance tracking and sequential change-detection (CUSUM/EWMA/Page-Hinkley) that require bounded memory; dynamic/ time-varying undirected graphs (e.g., time-varying GGMs); and windowed/forgetting-factor variants of PC as baselines. Clarifying how LoSST compares to these would strengthen positioning.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and underexplored problem: online learning of time-varying DAG structure with minimal buffering.
- Elegant sufficient-statistics updates for means and covariances; single-pass memory footprint is attractive.
- Intuitive use of pooled p-values to modulate forgetting and relearning frequency; practical knobs to trade off stability vs reactivity.
- Simulation results show compelling gains over naive batch PC after changepoints, and parity before changes; probabilistic relearning reduces compute without much loss.
- Honest discussion of convergence vs diligence trade-offs and explicit parameter regimes; useful conceptual guidance.

Weaknesses
- Statistical validity of several components is unclear: (i) Hotelling’s T2 p-values are used with weighted, adaptively estimated covariance and an ad hoc effective sample size Sr; (ii) Liptak pooling presumes independence (or at least weak dependence) of p-values, which is unlikely with overlapping estimates; (iii) feedback between weights and test statistics complicates calibration. These issues could yield miscalibrated ρ and unreliable change detection.
- The “effective sample size” update (Sr+1 = ar/ar+1 Sr + 1) is heuristic and departs from standard definitions (e.g., Kish’s neff = (sum w)^2 / sum w^2). This choice should be justified analytically or empirically.
- Theoretical claims are proof sketches and rely on strong simplifications; no guarantees are given for consistency of structure learning under weighting or for error control in relearning triggers.
- Baselines are weak: comparing only to batch PC on the entire dataset unfairly disfavors PC; sliding-window PC, exponentially weighted PC, BOCPD+windowed PC, or sequential detectors coupled with PC would be more informative.
- Practical details are missing: numerical stability of covariance/inverse updates under rapidly changing weights; regularization when Sr is small; computational complexity and runtime vs baselines; sensitivity to T, γ, ν; handling of multiple testing in PC under weighted stats.
- Real-data evaluation is qualitative and small-scale (6 variables), with no external validation.

Quality
Technically, the covariance and mean updates are sound; the ar schedule and relearning trigger are practical and easy to implement. However, the use of Hotelling’s T2 with adaptive, weighted estimates and the pooled p-value calibration are not theoretically justified. The convergence/diligence section clarifies qualitative regimes but stops short of guarantees relevant to structure recovery. Experiments are suggestive but limited in baselines, ablations, and diagnostics.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a helpful algorithmic narrative. Some notational and typographical issues: p should denote dimensionality V in the T2 p-value, but the text says “p = N”; “graph2” likely a typesetting artifact; subscript usage (Xi vs Vi) is occasionally inconsistent. Details that would aid reproducibility: how inverse covariance is maintained; regularization; CI test details for PC with weighted stats; parameter selection strategy.

Originality
The combination of online sufficient-statistics tracking with adaptive forgetting based on pooled Mahalanobis p-values, tied to probabilistic graph relearning, is novel for DAG structure learning. The idea of trading convergence against diligence and exposing parameter regimes is conceptually useful. That said, elements are related to known EWMA/TD-learning and sequential testing ideas.

Significance
If made statistically sound and evaluated against stronger baselines, LoSST could be a useful tool for practitioners dealing with drifting causal/associational structure. At present, the heuristic nature and limited validation reduce confidence in general applicability.

Pros for acceptance
- Timely problem and a simple, implementable approach.
- Promising empirical behavior on synthetic data with multiple change regimes.
- Clear exposition and useful conceptual framing.

Cons for acceptance
- Questionable statistical calibration of change detection; ad hoc effective sample size.
- Missing comparisons to natural baselines (sliding windows, EWMA/CUSUM, BOCPD+PC).
- Limited theory beyond proof sketches; no guarantees for structure recovery with weighted stats.
- Sparse practical details and sensitivity analyses; qualitative real-data demo.

Suggestions for improvement
- Justify or revise Sr using standard weighted-sample theory; analyze the distribution of Mahalanobis distances under weighted, adaptively estimated covariance; consider self-normalized statistics or bootstrap calibration.
- Replace/compare pooled p-values with standard sequential detectors (CUSUM/EWMA/GLR) that have known false-alarm properties; report detection delay/false alarm trade-offs.
- Add baselines: sliding-window PC; EWMA covariance + PC; BOCPD triggering PC; time-varying GGM methods (for undirected comparisons).
- Provide runtime and memory benchmarks; ablate ν, T, γ; test robustness to gradual vs abrupt drift and to outliers.
- Discuss and implement numerical stabilization (shrinkage covariance, forgetting-factor bounds).
- Fix notational errors; clarify PC testing with weighted stats and multiple testing control.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive is performance to T, γ, ν across graphs and SNRs? Any principled tuning?
- How do you ensure invertibility and stability of Cr with small Sr? Any shrinkage?
- Do you assume independence of p-values in Liptak pooling? If not, what is the impact?
- Could you compare Sr to Kish’s neff on your streams and assess which better predicts error?

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem and approach are interesting and potentially impactful, but the current submission relies on heuristics with unclear statistical validity and lacks strong baselines and sensitivity analyses. Strengthening calibration, theory, and evaluation would make this a solid contribution. Confidence: medium.
