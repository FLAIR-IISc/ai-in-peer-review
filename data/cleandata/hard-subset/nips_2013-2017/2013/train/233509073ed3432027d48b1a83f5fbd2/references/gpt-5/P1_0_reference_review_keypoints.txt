- Summary: Proposes LoSST, an online algorithm for time-varying DAG structure using weighted sufficient statistics and pooled Mahalanobis p-values to adapt forgetting and probabilistically relearn structure with PC.

- Relation to prior work: Positioned between changepoint detection and batch time-varying structure learning but omits comparison to EWMA/CUSUM/Page-Hinkley, time-varying GGMs, and windowed/forgetting-factor PC variants.

- Strength: Tackles an important online time-varying DAG problem with a single-pass, low-memory sufficient-statistics update.

- Strength: Elegant mean/covariance update equations and compact memory footprint.

- Strength: Intuitive pooled p-value mechanism to trade stability vs reactivity and control relearning frequency.

- Strength: Simulations show LoSST matches batch PC in stationary periods and outperforms it after changepoints; probabilistic relearning lowers compute cost.

- Strength: Clear discussion of convergence vs diligence regimes and helpful conceptual parameter guidance.

- Weakness: Statistical validity concerns: Hotelling’s T2 used with weighted/adaptive covariance and heuristic Sr, risking miscalibrated p-values and unreliable change detection.

- Weakness: Liptak pooling assumes (weak) independence of p-values, which is unlikely due to overlapping estimates and feedback between weights and tests.

- Weakness: The effective sample size Sr update is heuristic and departs from standard definitions (e.g., Kish’s neff) without justification.

- Weakness: Theoretical section contains only proof sketches under strong simplifications and gives no guarantees for structure recovery or error control.

- Weakness: Weak baselines—comparison only to batch PC; missing sliding-window, EWMA-PC, BOCPD+PC, and sequential-detector baselines.

- Weakness: Missing practical details on numerical stability, regularization when Sr is small, computational complexity, and multiple-testing in weighted PC.

- Weakness: Real-data evaluation is qualitative, small-scale (6 vars), and lacks external validation.

- Quality: Covariance/mean updates are sound and practical, but pooled p-value calibration and T2 usage lack theoretical justification; experiments are limited.

- Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized but has notational/typographical issues and missing reproducibility details (inverse updates, regularization, CI test specifics).

- Originality: Novel combination of online sufficient-statistics tracking with adaptive forgetting via pooled Mahalanobis p-values and probabilistic relearning for DAGs, though related to EWMA/TD and sequential testing ideas.

- Significance: Potentially useful if statistical soundness and stronger evaluation are provided; current heuristics and limited validation reduce confidence.

- Pros for acceptance: Timely problem, implementable approach, promising synthetic results, and clear exposition.

- Cons for acceptance: Questionable calibration of change detection, ad hoc Sr, weak baselines, limited theory, and sparse practical/sensitivity analyses.

- Suggested improvement: Justify or replace Sr with standard weighted-sample theory; analyze Mahalanobis distribution under adaptive weighting or use self-normalized/bootstrap calibration.

- Suggested improvement: Compare/replace pooled p-values with sequential detectors (CUSUM/EWMA/GLR) with known false-alarm properties and report delay/false-alarm trade-offs.

- Suggested improvement: Add baselines (sliding-window PC, EWMA covariance+PC, BOCPD+PC, time-varying GGM methods) and include runtime, memory, and sensitivity ablations.

- Suggested improvement: Implement numerical stabilization (shrinkage covariance, bounds on forgetting), clarify PC testing with weighted stats, and fix notation/typos.

- Questions for authors: Sensitivity of performance to T, γ, ν and principled tuning strategy?

- Questions for authors: How is invertibility/stability of Cr ensured for small Sr and is shrinkage used?

- Questions for authors: Do you assume independence of p-values in Liptak pooling and what's the impact if violated?

- Questions for authors: How does Sr compare empirically to Kish’s neff in predicting error?

- Recommendation: Borderline reject — interesting and promising but currently heuristic, under-validated, and lacking strong baselines and theory.
