Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), an online algorithm to learn time-varying graphical model structure for linear Gaussian SEMs/DAGs. The key idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariance, and an “effective” sample size) that adapt to potential changes in the data-generating process. Weights ar are updated based on a pooled anomaly score: the Mahalanobis distance of each incoming datapoint, converted to a p-value under a Hotelling T^2 approximation, and combined over time via Liptak’s method to produce a pooled p-value ρr. When ρr falls below a threshold, ar increases (up to a factor γ), shrinking the effective sample size so recent data dominate. Structure is relearned intermittently via the PC algorithm, with a probabilistic trigger that increases relearning frequency when ρr is small. The paper analyzes a trade-off between “convergence” and “diligence” controlled by T and γ, provides proof sketches, and presents synthetic experiments with abrupt changes in SEM graphs/parameters and a real-data case study on US price index volatility.

The paper contrasts LoSST with batch structure learning (PC), temporal-difference style tracking, and changepoint detection (CPD), and distinguishes from prior “batch” time-indexed graph estimation (Talih & Hengartner, 2005; Siracusa & Fisher, 2009). Related areas not discussed in detail but relevant include time-varying graphical models (e.g., dynamic/locally stationary GGMs, sliding-window or penalized TV methods), dynamic Bayesian networks, and adaptive forgetting/recursive least squares.

Strengths
- Problem setting: Addresses an important and underexplored setting—online structure learning under changing dependence structure—where batch methods can mislead.
- Conceptual simplicity: A modular pipeline—adaptive weighting of sufficient statistics + occasional relearning—likely extensible to other graph learners and distributions.
- Practical heuristics: Pooled anomaly scoring smooths sensitivity to single outliers; probabilistic relearning trades accuracy vs. compute.
- Empirical evidence: On synthetic data with abrupt changes, LoSST significantly outperforms a batch PC baseline after a changepoint while matching PC before the change. The long sequence experiment shows that probabilistic relearning can maintain accuracy with far fewer relearns.
- Discussion of convergence/diligence: Clear articulation of the inherent trade-off and parameter regimes, with proof sketches.

Weaknesses
- Statistical validity of anomaly scoring: The Hotelling T^2 approximation for Mahalanobis distances assumes unweighted, i.i.d. Gaussian samples; the paper uses weighted covariances and an “effective” sample size Sr. The null distribution (and thus p-values) for this weighted estimator is not established, and the sequential dependence among p-values violates the independence assumption behind Liptak pooling. This affects the interpretability/calibration of ρr.
- Effective sample size: The update Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1)Sr + 1 is introduced heuristically to satisfy constraints, not derived from variance properties of the weighted estimators. This impacts hypothesis testing (PC’s CI tests) and anomaly scoring.
- Baselines: Comparisons are limited to batch PC. Missing baselines include (i) sliding-window PC, (ii) exponential forgetting PC with fixed α, (iii) CPD + localized relearning, (iv) dynamic/time-varying graphical models (e.g., time-varying GGM or DBN), and (v) adaptive RLS/Kalman-style tracking for parameters with structure learning. Without these, the performance gains are difficult to attribute to the novel components vs. generic forgetting.
- CI testing under weighting: PC relies on tests of conditional independence using partial correlations whose sampling distributions depend on sample size. The paper substitutes Sr, but the validity of those tests under weighting and time-varying covariance is not established.
- Theory: The convergence/diligence statements are proof sketches and hinge on ar dynamics and thresholds, but do not address Type I/II error rates, detection delay, or false alarm control. No guarantees are provided for structure recovery under local stationarity (e.g., identifiability/faithfulness conditions with drifting parameters).
- Clarity and completeness: Some derivations (e.g., covariance update, Hotelling T^2 use with weighted stats) need more detail. Several references lack years and full citations. Figures are described but not visible; no error bars or statistical significance are reported. Parameter choices (T=0.05, γ=3, ν=0.005) are selected “unsystematically.”
- Real-data case study: The CPI example is suggestive but lacks external validation, alternative explanations, or robustness checks; conclusions about the Great Moderation are qualitative.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The core tracking updates are reasonable; the covariance update appears consistent with weighted incremental formulas. However, the statistical testing components (T^2 p-values with weighted stats, pooled p-values over dependent observations, use of Sr in PC tests) are heuristic and not justified theoretically, which may affect correctness of learned structures.
- Empirical support: Synthetic experiments demonstrate the intended behavior in abrupt-change settings but omit stronger baselines and uncertainty quantification.
- Completeness: Proof sketches are insufficient to establish guarantees relevant to structure learning accuracy or change detection performance.

Clarity
- Generally clear narrative and algorithm description. However, several key statistical choices and distributions should be derived or referenced precisely; notations around T^2 and effective sample size need tightening. Reference list formatting is incomplete.

Originality
- The combination of adaptive, pooled anomaly weighting of sufficient statistics with intermittent structure relearning appears novel for DAG/SEM structure learning. The method sits between CPD and time-varying graphical models, offering an online, single-pass alternative.

Significance
- If validated against stronger baselines and with more rigorous statistical treatment, LoSST could be impactful for streaming causal/graphical structure discovery. As presented, the significance is promising but not fully established.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical results on the distribution of Mahalanobis distances using weighted covariances (or adopt a robust, distribution-free anomaly score) and justify the effective sample size used in CI tests.
- Evaluate against sliding-window PC, fixed forgetting-factor baselines, CPD+relearn, and time-varying GGM/DBN methods. Include sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν and report variability (error bars).
- Calibrate and report detection metrics (false alarms, detection delay) and structure recovery metrics (precision/recall, SHD) with confidence intervals.
- Clarify computational complexity per timestep and per relearn; report runtime vs. accuracy trade-offs.
- Expand related work to include dynamic graphical models, adaptive filtering/forgetting, and time-varying networks.
- Improve citation completeness and derivational details, especially for Eqns. (2)–(4) and the testing distributions under weighting.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, practical problem with a simple, modular algorithm that performs well in illustrative simulations.
- Potentially broadly useful as a framework adaptable to other learners and models.

Arguments against acceptance
- Key statistical components are heuristic without theoretical calibration, raising concerns about correctness of tests and pooled anomaly scores.
- Missing strong baselines and quantitative uncertainty analyses limit the strength of empirical claims.
- Proofs and clarity are insufficient in parts; references are incomplete.

Overall, the work is promising but would benefit from stronger statistical justification and more comprehensive empirical validation.
