

# Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

This paper proposes the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm for online learning of time-varying graphical models. The authors address a gap in the literature where existing methods either assume static structures (batch-mode learning) or focus on tracking specific environmental features rather than full graphical model structure. The core idea is to track locally stationary sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) in an online fashion, using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values to detect structure changes and adjust weights accordingly. The authors claim LoSST performs as well as batch-mode methods in stable environments and can track changes in structure.

## Strengths

The paper identifies a genuine problem in the literature: the lack of online algorithms for learning time-varying graphical model structures. The idea of tracking sufficient statistics while dynamically adjusting weights based on model fit is conceptually sound. The simulation results show promising performance compared to batch-mode methods when structure changes occur, and the real-world application to US price index volatility demonstrates practical utility.

## Weaknesses

The paper suffers from numerous technical, methodological, and presentation flaws that significantly undermine its credibility and scientific rigor.

**Technical errors and inconsistencies:**
- Equation (4) contains a critical mathematical error: "ar+1 = ar ·max { 1, γT − γρr+1 + ρr+1 T }" should be "ar+1 = ar ·max { 1, γT / (T - γρr+1) }" or similar, but the current form is mathematically nonsensical.
- The definition of "effective sample size" (equation 3) is incorrect: "Sr+1 = ar ar+1 Sr + 1" should be "Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1) * Sr + 1" to maintain the correct relationship.
- The claim about Mahalanobis distance distribution is incorrect: "Hotelling's T^2 with parameters p = V and m = N − 1" should specify degrees of freedom as p and N-p.
- The paper incorrectly states "if ar+1 ≥ ar for all r, then Sr+1 ≤ Sr + 1," which is not true (the inequality direction is wrong).
- The reference to "Figures 1(a-c)" and "Figures 1(d-f)" appears throughout the text, but no figures are included in the submission, making the results impossible to evaluate.

**Methodological shortcomings:**
- The paper claims "LoSST is capable of exhibiting different long-run properties" but provides no rigorous theoretical justification for the convergence/diligence claims, particularly the assertion that "if γ > 1 and 0 < T < 1, then LoSST is neither convergent nor diligent."
- The use of a "noisy-OR gate" for graph relearning is not properly defined or justified, and the mathematical formulation is unclear.
- The paper states "the algorithm's performance is indistinguishable from a standard batch-mode structure learning algorithm" in stable environments but provides no statistical tests to support this claim.
- The threshold T is arbitrarily set to 0.05 without justification or exploration of sensitivity to this parameter.
- The claim that "LoSST typically stabilized on a new model by roughly 250 datapoints after the changepoint" lacks statistical significance or error bars.

**Formatting and presentation issues:**
- The reference list contains incomplete citations (e.g., "Bayesian online changepoint detection, R.P. Adams, D.J.C. MacKay, Technical report, , , , " has missing information).
- The paper inconsistently uses "SEM" vs. "recursive Structural Equation Models (SEMs)."
- Mathematical notation is inconsistent throughout (e.g., "Dr+1" vs. "D_{r+1}", "pr+1" vs. "p_{r+1}").
- The paper cites reference [5] ("Model change and methodological virtues in scientific inference") but this reference is missing from the bibliography.
- The paper claims "the Mahalanobis distance Dr+1 = (Xr+1 − µr)(Cr)−1(Xr+1 − µr)T" but the matrix multiplication is incorrectly specified (should be (Xr+1 − µr)^T(Cr)−1(Xr+1 − µr)).

**Theoretical and empirical weaknesses:**
- The paper states "if γ = 1, then LoSST is a convergent algorithm, since it follows that ar+1 = ar for all r (which is a sufficient condition for convergence)" - this is incorrect; the condition ar+1 = ar for all r is not a sufficient condition for convergence.
- The paper claims "the PC algorithm [12] as our baseline batch-mode learning algorithm" but provides no justification for why PC is the appropriate baseline rather than other algorithms.
- The paper doesn't provide any evidence that the Mahalanobis distance approach is superior to simpler methods for change detection.
- The real-world application to US price index volatility lacks statistical validation of the detected changes.

## Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Identifies a genuine problem in the literature (online learning of time-varying graphical models)
- Conceptually interesting approach to tracking sufficient statistics
- Demonstrates practical utility in a real-world application
- Simulation results show promising performance in change detection scenarios

**Weaknesses:**
- Numerous technical errors in mathematical formulations
- Inconsistent and incorrect mathematical notation throughout
- Missing figures referenced in the text
- Inadequate statistical validation of claims
- Unjustified parameter choices
- Theoretical claims lack proper justification
- Incomplete references

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an interesting problem and presents a potentially useful algorithm, the numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, and methodological shortcomings significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The mathematical formulations contain critical errors that would require substantial revision to fix, and the lack of proper statistical validation for key claims makes it difficult to assess the actual performance of the proposed method. The paper would benefit from significant reworking to correct these issues before it could be considered for publication.

The paper's approach to online graphical model structure learning is promising, but the current submission fails to provide sufficient technical rigor or validation to support its claims. The errors and omissions are too numerous and fundamental to be considered minor, and they prevent me from having confidence in the paper's results.
