- Tackles online learning of graphical model structure in non-stationary environments via a new algorithm, LoSST.  
- LoSST tracks sufficient statistics online and relearns structure when changes are detected using Mahalanobis distance and a pooled p-value.  
- Aims to perform well during both stable and changing periods while avoiding costly batch relearning when unnecessary.  
- Empirical results show LoSST can track changes in graph structure and parameters in simulation.  
- Real-world demonstration applied to US price index data.  
- Positions work relative to Bayesian/score-based and constraint-based structure learning and prior time-indexed graph methods.  
- Clearly distinguishes LoSST’s online, frequentist approach from prior offline or parametric methods.  
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem in graphical model learning for dynamic systems.  
- Presents a practical, implementable algorithm using sufficient-statistics tracking and dynamic data weighting.  
- Discusses theoretical trade-offs (convergence vs. diligence) and establishes provable diligence under certain parameters.  
- Simulation comparisons to batch PC algorithm demonstrate advantages of online learning in dynamic scenarios.  
- Writing is clear and the paper is well organized.  
- Algorithm relies on heuristic choices (e.g., thresholds, ν, weighting) with limited sensitivity analysis.  
- Parameter settings appear arbitrary and lack a principled tuning methodology.  
- Computational cost of repeated relearning (PC calls) is noted but not thoroughly analyzed for scalability.  
- Simulations are somewhat limited—need more diverse graphs, gradual changes, and higher-dimensional tests.  
- Real-world application is suggestive but lacks rigorous comparison to alternative time-series methods.  
- Overall assessment: solid and valuable contribution deserving acceptance, with recommended follow-up on sensitivity and computational analysis.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses open problem, proposes novel practical algorithm, strong empirical evidence, theoretical insight, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: heavy reliance on heuristics, limited parameter exploration, unclear computational scaling, and insufficiently diverse simulations.  
- Reviewer rating: Strong Accept (7/10).
