- Proposes LoSST: an online, single-pass method to track time-varying DAGs using adaptive exponential weighting, Mahalanobis distances → Hotelling T^2 p-values, pooled by Liptak, and periodic PC relearning.  
- Positions LoSST as complementary to batch DAG learning, CPD, and prior time-varying graph methods, but underdeveloped links to DBNs, EWMA, SPC (CUSUM/Page-Hinkley), and fused/locally-smooth GGMs.  
- Update rules for means/covariances and effective sample size are plausible but ad hoc; key statistical concern is validity of Hotelling T^2 with adaptive weights.  
- Pooling p-values over time is questionable because individual p-values may be dependent/invalid under adaptive estimation; ρr is treated as calibrated evidence without sufficient validation.  
- Theoretical convergence–diligence results are interesting but mostly sketches; need formal definitions (e.g., diligence) and explicit sample-complexity/constants.  
- Synthetic experiments are thoughtful but miss important baselines (sliding-window PC, fixed-EWMA PC, CPD+reset, time-varying GGMs) and lack sensitivity analyses, error bars, and compute/memory measurements.  
- Real-data case study is illustrative (detects “Great Moderation”) but lacks ground truth and alternative-explanation analysis (e.g., macro covariate shifts).  
- Practical implementation details are sparse: no covariance regularization for ill-conditioned/high-d settings, unclear CI significance thresholding/corrections for PC, and heuristic relearning trigger with unclear tuning.  
- Paper is generally clear and readable, but some derivations and assumptions (T^2 approximation, p-value pooling) are only sketched; needs pseudocode, parameter guidelines, and numerical-stability tips.  
- Novelty lies in integrating adaptive pooled p-value forgetting with online DAG learning and framing a convergence–diligence trade-off, though components themselves are known.  
- Significance: promising for streaming causal/associational discovery with low memory, but methodological heuristics and limited comparisons weaken impact.  
- Pros: tackles an important problem, is memory-efficient and intuitive, offers a useful conceptual trade-off, shows strong synthetic gains after changes, and has a plausible real-data signal.  
- Cons: relies on heuristic/statistically questionable approximations, omits strong baselines, lacks sensitivity/robustness analysis, omits regularization discussion, and presents sketchy theory.  
- Key suggestions: calibrate p-value machinery under stationarity and drift, compare to specific baselines (sliding-window PC, fixed-EWMA, Bayesian CPD resets, TV-glasso), run sensitivity studies for T/γ/ν, add covariance regularization and robust tests, report runtime/scaling, and expand related-work coverage.  
- Overall recommendation: promising but currently heuristic and undercompared; borderline/weak reject pending stronger validation and baselines.
