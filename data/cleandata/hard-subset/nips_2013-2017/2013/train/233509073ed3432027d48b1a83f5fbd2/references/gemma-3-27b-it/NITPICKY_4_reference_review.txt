This paper attempts to address the challenging problem of online learning of graphical model structure in non-stationary environments. While the motivation is sound – real-world systems often exhibit changing relationships – the execution suffers from a plethora of minor, yet cumulatively substantial, flaws. The core idea of tracking sufficient statistics and dynamically relearning the graph is reasonable, but the numerous implementation details, coupled with a lack of rigorous justification for design choices, raise serious concerns.

The paper begins with a reasonable introduction, adequately outlining the limitations of existing methods. However, the related work section feels somewhat selective. While it mentions relevant work, it downplays the applicability of methods designed for change *detection* coupled with periodic structure learning. A system that frequently re-runs a batch learning algorithm after a changepoint is detected could be a competitive baseline.

The LoSST algorithm itself is presented in a dense and often unclear manner. The update equations (1), (2), and (3) are presented without sufficient explanation of their derivation or connection to standard statistical estimators. While the authors state the equations reduce to batch-mode estimates under constant weights, this isn’t immediately obvious and requires careful inspection. The introduction of the ‘ar’ weights and their relationship to the Mahalanobis distance feels ad-hoc. The choice of Liptak’s method for pooling p-values, while cited, lacks justification for why it’s superior to other pooling methods. The transformation of the pooled p-value into the weight ‘ar’ via equation (4) utilizes parameters γ and T with limited exploration of their impact. The discussion of convergence vs. diligence (Section 3.1) is interesting but feels disconnected from the practical implementation and parameter tuning. The claim of diligence under certain conditions (γ > 1 and 0 < T < 1) feels insufficiently substantiated.

The simulation results (Section 4) are presented with limited detail. The choice of parameters for the simulations (e.g., variable number, max degree) feels arbitrary and lacks a clear rationale. The presentation of results in Figures 1 and 2 is cluttered and difficult to interpret. The comparison to the PC algorithm is valuable, but the fixed relearning schedule imposed on LoSST appears to unfairly favor it. The discussion of the parameter change simulation (Figure 2) reveals a nuance – LoSST performs *worse* on edge removal errors – that should have been more thoroughly investigated. The probabilistic relearning simulation (Figure 3) is better, but still lacks a comprehensive analysis of the chosen ν value. The real-world application (Section 5) feels superficial. Applying the algorithm to a limited dataset and observing changes in effective sample size doesn't constitute a rigorous validation. The interpretation of the observed shifts in terms of the "Great Moderation" and a subsequent recession feels speculative.

Numerous minor issues further detract from the paper's quality: inconsistent notation (e.g., using both ‘Gr’ and ‘Mr’ to denote graphical models), grammatical errors, and a lack of polish in the writing. The extensive use of equations without accompanying textual explanations hinders readability. References are sometimes cited without context.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem.
*   Presents a novel approach to online graphical model learning.
*   Demonstrates potential for detecting structural changes in synthetic data.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The algorithm is overly complex and lacks clear justification for many design choices.
*   The presentation is dense, unclear, and riddled with minor errors.
*   The simulation results are limited in scope and lack a thorough analysis.
*   The real-world application is superficial and speculative.
*   The cumulative effect of numerous minor flaws significantly weakens the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the approach, the paper falls short of NIPS standards. The lack of rigor, clarity, and thorough evaluation outweigh the potential significance of the work. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should significantly revise the paper, focusing on simplifying the algorithm, providing more detailed explanations and justifications, conducting more comprehensive experiments, and addressing the numerous minor flaws. A more rigorous mathematical analysis and a clearer connection to existing statistical literature would also be beneficial.
