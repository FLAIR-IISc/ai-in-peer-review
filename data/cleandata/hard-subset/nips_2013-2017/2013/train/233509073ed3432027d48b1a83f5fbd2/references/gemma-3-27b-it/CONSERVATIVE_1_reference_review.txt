This paper addresses the important problem of learning graphical model structure in non-stationary environments, a clear limitation of standard algorithms. The core idea – tracking sufficient statistics online and dynamically relearning structure – is intuitively sound and addresses a gap in the literature. However, despite the potential significance, the paper suffers from a lack of rigorous validation and relies heavily on simulation results with limited scope. 

The paper adequately surveys related work, correctly identifying the limitations of existing approaches. The proposed LoSST algorithm is reasonably well-described, though the numerous update equations (1-4) and associated parameters (γ, T, ν) introduce considerable complexity. The motivation for specific parameter choices (T=0.05, γ=3) is stated as “unsystematic investigation,” which is a significant weakness. The attempt to connect the algorithm’s parameters to theoretical properties like convergence vs. diligence is a commendable effort, but the conditions are complex and their practical implications are not thoroughly explored. 

The simulation results provide initial evidence that LoSST can track changing structure, but the experimental setup feels somewhat limited. While the comparison to a static PC algorithm is appropriate, the specific scenarios (changing graph *and* parameters, changing parameters only) seem narrowly focused. The results show LoSST outperforms PC after a changepoint, as expected, but the magnitude of the improvement is not always substantial, and the edge removal error observation warrants further investigation. The simulation with probabilistic relearning is a positive step, but again, lacks extensive parameter exploration. The application to US price index data is intriguing, but the interpretation of the detected “changepoints” (e.g., relating them to the Great Moderation) feels speculative without further economic validation. 

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem.
*   Algorithm is intuitively sound and builds on established graphical model structure learning techniques.
*   Attempts to provide theoretical justification for algorithm parameters.
*   Includes a real-world application, though limited.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited and narrowly focused simulation studies.
*   Lack of rigorous comparison to alternative online learning algorithms (beyond PC).
*   “Unsystematic” parameter tuning and insufficient exploration of parameter space.
*   Complex algorithm with numerous parameters potentially requiring significant tuning.
*   Real-world application lacks strong validation beyond anecdotal observation.
*   The paper relies heavily on heuristic arguments, especially concerning convergence and diligence.
*   The discussion of convergence/diligence trade-offs is complex and difficult to follow without deeper mathematical analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant gap in the literature.
*   The core idea has merit and potential for impact.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the algorithm.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper lacks rigorous validation and relies too heavily on simulations.
*   The algorithm’s complexity and sensitivity to parameter settings raise concerns about practical usability.
*   The theoretical analysis is incomplete and the connection to practical performance is not fully established.
*   The real-world application is not compelling enough to justify acceptance on its own.

**Overall Assessment:**

I am skeptical of this paper in its current form. While the problem it addresses is important, the lack of thorough validation and the reliance on heuristic arguments weaken the overall contribution. The algorithm is presented as a practical solution, but its complexity and sensitivity to parameter tuning are concerning. The simulations, while demonstrating some positive results, are not sufficiently comprehensive to build strong confidence in the algorithm’s performance. The application to real-world data feels more like an illustration than a validation. 

I recommend **rejection**. The authors should significantly expand the simulation studies, compare their algorithm to a wider range of online learning methods, and provide a more rigorous justification for their parameter choices. A more in-depth theoretical analysis would also strengthen the paper. I would be open to reconsidering a revised version that addresses these concerns.
