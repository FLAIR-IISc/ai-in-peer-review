Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, an online heuristic for learning time-varying graphical structure (linear-Gaussian SEMs/DAGs). It maintains exponentially reweightable sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) via single-pass updates. A pooled p-value of recent Mahalanobis distances (via Lipták’s method) drives adaptive weights ar and a probabilistic schedule for when to relearn the structure using the PC algorithm. The authors discuss parameter regimes purported to yield convergence vs diligence and present simulations with synthetic changepoints (graph and parameter shifts) and a brief case study on U.S. price index volatility.

Strengths
- Tackles an important, underexplored problem: online structure learning under nonstationarity.
- Efficient sufficient-statistics tracking is practical; single-pass memory footprint is attractive.
- The idea to modulate forgetting/weighting via a pooled goodness-of-fit signal is intuitive.
- Empirical results suggest good recovery after abrupt changes without harming performance in stable regimes.
- Discussion of convergence vs diligence is thoughtful and frames a real trade-off in online learning.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns

Quality/technical correctness
1) Distributional validity of Mahalanobis-based p-values:
- The p-value computation uses Hotelling’s T^2 with parameters p=V, m=N−1, but the text later writes pr+1 = T^2(x>Dr+1|p=N, m=Sr−1). Using p=N is wrong; p should be dimensionality V. More importantly, the T^2 reference distribution assumes i.i.d. Gaussian data and a sample covariance computed from i.i.d. samples. Here, the covariance is adaptively reweighted with changing ar and estimated online; the data are non-i.i.d. This invalidates the T^2 calibration. Using “effective sample size” Sr in place of N does not restore the T^2 distribution. No justification or calibration study is provided.

2) Lipták pooling assumptions:
- Lipták’s method assumes independence of the constituent tests. The pr are strongly dependent because they reuse the evolving μr, Cr (and the data stream). No attempt is made to account for dependence, so ρr cannot be interpreted as a valid p-value. This undermines all subsequent thresholding logic.

3) Weight update mapping (Eq. 4) appears incorrect/inconsistent:
- The text claims “increase linearly and inversely to ρ up to a maximum of γ ar at ρ=0.” The given formula ar+1 = ar·max{1, γT − γρ + ρ/T} yields at ρ=0 a factor γT, not γ. Monotonicity in ρ is also not guaranteed because derivative is −γ + 1/T, which can be positive if T < 1/γ. The stated behavior (upper bound γ, inverse in ρ for ρ<T) does not match the formula.

4) Threshold T outside [0,1]:
- Section 3.1 argues convergence for T<0 and diligence for T>1, but ρ∈(0,1), so T should be in [0,1]. Using T<0 or T>1 is conceptually and operationally meaningless for a p-value threshold. Theorems relying on such regimes are not applicable.

5) Convergence/diligence claims lack rigor:
- Proof sketches rely on problematic premises (invalid p-values, T outside [0,1]) and omit necessary conditions (e.g., how adaptive weights interact with CI tests in PC). The “provably diligent” claim leverages the incorrect Eq. 4 and T>1.

6) Use of PC with weighted covariances:
- PC’s CI tests for linear-Gaussian data rely on partial correlations and known asymptotics under i.i.d. sampling with fixed N. The paper does not specify how tests are performed with weighted covariances and an adaptive “effective” sample size. The Fisher z-transform and p-values are not calibrated under the proposed weighting. No validation (type I error control, power) is shown.

7) Complexity and numerical issues:
- Mahalanobis distance requires Cr^{-1} at each step. The paper does not describe stable/efficient rank-one updates, regularization, or conditioning safeguards. In realistic online settings, O(V^3) inversions per datastream step are prohibitive.

8) Covariance update correctness:
- Eq. (2) is stated “after some algebra.” Given the nonstandard weighting, a derivation or reference is needed. The chosen normalization (1/br) corresponds to a weighted MLE, not unbiased covariance; implications for downstream tests are not discussed.

Experimental evaluation
9) Baselines: The key baselines are missing. A sliding window PC, exponentially weighted covariance with fixed α, CUSUM/EWMA-T^2 from statistical process control, or existing online CPD + relearn would be natural comparators. Comparing only to batch PC on mixed distributions is weak.

10) Details and reproducibility: The simulation setup lacks essential details (SEM parameter ranges/noise variances, generation of DAGs with degree constraints, CI test α, how orientation errors are computed). No confidence intervals or statistical tests are provided. Hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) are chosen by “unsystematic investigation.”

11) Real-data case study: No ground truth, no alternative explanations, no normality checks for differenced series, and no baseline comparisons. The Great Moderation claim is anecdotal.

Clarity and presentation
12) Numerous formatting/notation issues:
- Footnote markers appear in-body (e.g., graph2, inverse3).
- Reference list is incomplete (missing years/venues; inconsistent formatting).
- Notation inconsistencies: p=V vs p=N in T^2; occasional missing subscripts/superscripts; “V -dimensional” spacing; typos (“occcurs”).
- The threshold T domain confusion is central and must be corrected.

Originality
- The combination of adaptive exponential weighting of sufficient statistics with periodic relearning is reasonable, but related literatures (multivariate EWMA/CUSUM, adaptive forgetting in state estimation) are not properly connected. The pooled p-value idea is novel here but invalid under dependence.

Significance
- The problem is important. However, due to the validity issues above and limited evaluation, it is premature to conclude impact.

Pros
- Addresses an important online, nonstationary structure learning problem.
- Simple, implementable heuristic with single-pass statistics.
- Empirical indications that it can quickly adapt after changepoints.

Cons
- Key statistical components are invalid or inconsistently specified (Hotelling T^2 under adaptive weights, Lipták pooling under dependence, Eq. 4).
- Convergence/diligence claims rely on impossible thresholds and incorrect derivations.
- Missing strong baselines and ablations; limited experimental rigor.
- Use of PC under non-i.i.d. weighting not justified; test calibration unclear.
- Numerous clarity/formatting/reference issues.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper tackles a valuable problem with promising ideas, but the statistical foundations (p-value computation/pooling, threshold regimes), theoretical claims, and experimental evaluation require substantial revision. I encourage the authors to (i) correct Eq. 4 and constrain T∈[0,1], (ii) replace or calibrate the p-value machinery for dependence and adaptive weights, (iii) justify CI testing under weighting or switch to robust alternatives, (iv) add strong baselines (sliding window, EWMA/CUSUM, online CPD+relearn), and (v) improve clarity and references. I would be enthusiastic to re-evaluate a revised, statistically sound version. Confidence: high.
