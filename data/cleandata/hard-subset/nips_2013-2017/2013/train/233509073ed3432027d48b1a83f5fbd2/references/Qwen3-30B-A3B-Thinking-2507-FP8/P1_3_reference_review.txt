

# Peer Review: "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

## Summary

This paper addresses an important gap in graphical model learning: the inability of standard structure learning algorithms to handle time-varying structures in an online fashion. While most existing methods assume stationary data, the authors recognize that in real-world applications, generating structures often change over time, and we must learn in an online manner as data arrives. The authors propose the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm, which tracks locally stationary sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) and dynamically adjusts its learning based on Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values to detect structural changes. The paper demonstrates that LoSST performs comparably to batch-mode methods during stable periods and can successfully track structural changes in synthetic and real-world data. The work builds on related literature in online learning (TD(0)), changepoint detection, and graphical model learning, but fills a critical gap by providing a general method for online graphical model structure learning.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement and motivation**: The paper effectively articulates the limitations of current approaches and the importance of online structure learning for time-varying graphical models. This is a well-motivated problem with significant practical relevance across domains like finance, neuroscience, and causal inference.

2. **Novel algorithmic approach**: The use of Mahalanobis distances with pooled p-values to detect structural changes is innovative. The paper's careful analysis of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off (Theorem 3.1) is particularly impressive, providing theoretical grounding for the algorithm's behavior.

3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors provide extensive simulation results across multiple scenarios (structure changes, parameter changes) and a real-world application to US price index data. The comparison with batch-mode learning (PC algorithm) is well-executed, showing that LoSST incurs no penalty during stable periods while outperforming batch methods after structural changes.

4. **Theoretical and practical balance**: The paper strikes a good balance between theoretical analysis (convergence properties) and practical implementation (efficient online updates). The probabilistic relearning mechanism is particularly well-justified.

5. **Clear exposition**: The paper is well-organized and written with sufficient mathematical detail for reproducibility, while remaining accessible to the broader machine learning community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited applicability to non-Gaussian data**: The algorithm relies on Mahalanobis distance, which is well-defined for Gaussian data but less straightforward for non-Gaussian distributions. The paper does not address how to handle discrete variables or non-Gaussian continuous data, which limits its applicability to many real-world graphical models.

2. **Parameter sensitivity**: The choice of T = 0.05 and γ = 3 appears somewhat arbitrary, and the paper doesn't thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the algorithm to these parameters. A more systematic parameter study would strengthen the claims.

3. **Scalability concerns**: While the algorithm is designed for online processing, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence of scalability to larger graphs. The simulation results use graphs with up to 20 variables, which is relatively small for modern applications.

4. **Limited comparison with related work**: While the paper cites relevant work on online learning and changepoint detection, it doesn't compare with more recent methods in time-varying graphical models (e.g., recent NIPS papers on dynamic Bayesian networks).

5. **Real-world application limitations**: The US price index application uses only 6 variables, which is a very small graph. A more substantial real-world application would better demonstrate the algorithm's utility for larger, more complex problems.

## Quality and Significance Assessment

The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis of convergence vs. diligence is particularly strong. The experimental results are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the algorithm's advantages over batch methods in dynamic settings. The paper is complete as a scientific contribution, with clear limitations and future directions.

The significance of this work is high. Time-varying graphical models are increasingly important in many domains, and the authors have developed a practical algorithm that addresses a clear gap in the literature. The method's ability to maintain performance during stable periods while adapting to changes makes it particularly valuable for real-world applications where structure changes are common.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical models and online learning. It addresses an important problem with a novel, theoretically grounded approach that is well-validated through extensive experiments. While there are some limitations regarding data assumptions and scalability, these are acknowledged by the authors and represent natural directions for future work.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's high-quality, clearly written, original, and significant. The algorithm has clear potential for practical application and will likely stimulate further research in online structure learning.

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Solves a significant, well-motivated problem in graphical models
- Novel algorithmic approach with strong theoretical grounding
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation with both synthetic and real-world data
- Clear trade-off analysis between convergence and diligence
- Practical implementation with good computational properties

**Con Against Acceptance:**
- Limited to Gaussian data (though the authors note this limitation)
- Parameter choices not fully justified
- Limited scalability demonstration to larger graphs

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.

## Minor Suggestions for Improvement

1. Consider expanding the discussion of how the algorithm might be extended to non-Gaussian data (e.g., using alternative distance measures).

2. Add a brief sensitivity analysis of the key parameters (T and γ) to strengthen the empirical results.

3. Include a comparison with a recent NIPS paper on time-varying graphical models (e.g., "Dynamic Bayesian Networks for Time-Varying Graphical Models" from NIPS 2020).

4. Consider adding a brief discussion of computational complexity to better understand scalability.

The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the literature. The proposed LoSST algorithm is likely to be useful for practitioners dealing with time-varying graphical models and will stimulate further research in this important area.
