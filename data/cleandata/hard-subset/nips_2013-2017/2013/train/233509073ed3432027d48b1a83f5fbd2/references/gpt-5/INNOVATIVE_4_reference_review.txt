Summary
This paper tackles online structure learning of graphical models under nonstationarity. The authors propose LoSST, a heuristic algorithm for tracking time-varying DAG/SEM structure by maintaining exponentially reweightable sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) and triggering structure relearning as the data’s fit to the current model deteriorates. Novel elements include: (i) online updates for weighted means/covariances with a dynamically adapted weight ar; (ii) a pooled-p-value mechanism (Liptak aggregation over Mahalanobis distance tests) to detect sustained misfit and control the forgetting rate; (iii) a probabilistic schedule for invoking a constraint-based structure learner (PC) when change is suspected; and (iv) a discussion of convergence vs. diligence trade-offs with conditions under which LoSST exhibits each behavior. Experiments on synthetic data with both structural and parametric changes and a CPI volatility case study illustrate that LoSST can match batch performance in stationary periods and adapt quickly after changes, often outperforming a naïve batch PC baseline on post-change segments.

Strengths
- Novel problem framing: general online structure learning for DAGs under local stationarity, filling a gap between changepoint detection and batch causal discovery.
- Clever and lightweight mechanism to control forgetting via pooled p-values and dynamic weights, leading to single-pass computation with modest state (μ, C, S, plus a few scalars).
- Clear articulation of the convergence–diligence tension and parameter regimes; includes proof sketches and useful intuitions.
- Practical: decouples tracking from structure learning, enabling plug-in of alternative learners; probabilistic relearning avoids constant re-optimization.
- Synthetic experiments are aligned with the goal (abrupt structural and parametric changes) and show that LoSST adapts while batch PC degrades on mixture data.
- Real-data case study links to macroeconomic phenomena (Great Moderation), lending plausibility to practical utility.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Heuristic statistical foundations: pooled p-values assume independence and correct calibration of individual p-values. Here, Mahalanobis distances are computed from estimated, adaptively weighted covariances, and tests across time are dependent. Thus ρ can be miscalibrated. Empirical calibration (e.g., permutation/online bootstrap) or control via EWMAs could strengthen the approach.
- Use of Hotelling’s T2 under weighted effective sample size S is questionable; the theoretical distribution under adaptive reweighting is not established. This affects both ar updates and CI testing (PC relies on valid p-values or z-transform thresholds).
- Baselines are too weak. Comparing LoSST only to full-batch PC on pooled data creates a strawman after a change. More appropriate baselines include:
  - Sliding-window PC (various window sizes).
  - Exponentially weighted covariance + PC with fixed forgetting factor.
  - Online CPD plus reinitialization of PC (Bayesian and frequentist CPD).
  - For undirected settings, time-varying GGMs with forgetting or fused penalties.
  - Dynamic Bayesian networks (even if not identical, they are a natural comparator).
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses. The choice of T=0.05, γ=3, and ν=.005 is “unsystematic”; the method’s performance likely depends on these. No exploration of robustness to parameter mis-specification, noise, or outliers.
- Real-data study is suggestive but lacks external validation (e.g., forecast performance, intervention checks, or comparison to known regime-shift dates). Plots of learned graphs over time would be informative; it is unclear whether the structural changes identified are stable or interpretable.
- Some theoretical claims are only sketched. Conditions for diligence/convergence are interesting but would benefit from formal proofs and clearer assumptions (e.g., distributional conditions, bounded degree).
- Clarity and polish: several footnote artifacts and typos; references are incomplete; notation around T2 parameters (p vs. N) is a bit confusing.

Quality
Technically sound at the algorithmic/mechanical level, but the statistical testing layer is heuristic and likely miscalibrated. Experiments are encouraging but incomplete given the missing baselines and sensitivity analyses. The authors are transparent about heuristic choices and limitations.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. The online update equations are clear and useful. The paper would benefit from tightening the statistical testing discussion, cleaning up footnotes/typos, and expanding experimental detail (metrics, CIs, runtime).

Originality
High. To my knowledge, there is no prior general-purpose, single-pass online DAG structure learner that adaptively controls forgetting based on data-model misfit and couples it to probabilistic relearning. The convergence–diligence framing is also insightful.

Significance
Potentially significant. Many applications require online adaptation to structural drift. Even as a heuristic, LoSST could spur follow-up work in online causal discovery and time-varying structure learning, including principled tests and better integration with score-based search.

Suggestions for the authors
- Add strong baselines: sliding window PC; fixed forgetting factor; CPD+relearn; dynamic BN where applicable.
- Calibrate or validate ρ: show empirical null distributions via online resampling; report false alarm/miss rates on stationary segments.
- Sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν; include ablations (e.g., raw Mahalanobis vs. pooled p-values).
- Clarify how PC’s CI tests use S under weighting; discuss potential type-I error inflation and any corrections.
- Provide learned graph snapshots in the CPI study and, if possible, a predictive evaluation.
- Move proof sketches to an appendix with full statements.

Pros for acceptance
- Bold, novel direction addressing an important gap (online causal/structure learning).
- Lightweight, implementable method with promising empirical behavior.
- Conceptual contribution (convergence vs. diligence) likely to influence future work.

Cons against acceptance
- Heuristic statistical layer with unclear calibration.
- Missing key baselines and sensitivity analyses limit the strength of the empirical case.
- Partial theoretical development.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite methodological imperfections and limited comparisons, the paper introduces a compelling idea and a practical algorithm for an underexplored but important problem. With stronger baselines and calibration, this could be impactful.
