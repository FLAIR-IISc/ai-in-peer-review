## Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the important and challenging problem of learning graphical model structure in non-stationary environments, where the underlying relationships between variables change over time. The authors recognize the limitations of existing structure learning algorithms, which primarily assume static generating processes, and propose a novel heuristic algorithm, LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker). LoSST operates online, tracking sufficient statistics and dynamically relearning the graphical model as changes are detected via a Mahalanobis distance-based change point detection mechanism. The key innovation lies in adapting Temporal Difference Learning (TDL) concepts for covariance estimation and using a weighted sample size to account for differing data point reliability. The authors provide simulation results demonstrating LoSST's ability to track changing structures and maintain performance comparable to batch methods in stable periods, and a compelling application to US price index volatility.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors accurately position their work within the landscape of graphical model learning and online change detection. They effectively highlight the shortcomings of both traditional batch-mode algorithms (PC, constraint-based, score-based) and existing online approaches (TDL, changepoint detection).  The discussion of Talih & Hengartner (2005) and Siracusa & Fisher III (2009) correctly identifies their limitations – batch processing and Bayesian approaches respectively – justifying the need for a frequentist, online algorithm like LoSST. The connection to TDL is particularly insightful, drawing parallels and explaining why its direct application is insufficient for tracking complex structural changes.  The referencing is generally thorough, though a mention of work on *dynamic* Bayesian networks (DBNs) might strengthen the positioning, as DBNs offer a different way of modeling time-varying relationships, even if they don't directly address online structure learning.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of online sufficient statistic tracking with a change detection mechanism integrated into a structure learning algorithm is a significant contribution. Adapting TDL concepts for covariance updates is clever and offers an efficient way to incorporate past data without requiring full re-computation.
* **Practicality:** The online nature of LoSST is highly valuable for real-world applications where data streams are continuous and immediate responses to changes are required. The authors correctly emphasize the advantage of being able to process very large datasets in a single pass.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The discussion of convergence vs. diligence is a strong point. Explicitly recognizing and analyzing this trade-off demonstrates a deep understanding of the algorithm’s behavior and limitations. The conditions for convergence and diligence are clearly articulated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulations are well-designed and demonstrate LoSST’s ability to adapt to both structural and parameter changes.  The application to US price index volatility provides a compelling real-world case study and validates the algorithm’s potential.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The algorithm is explained in sufficient detail to allow for implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Heuristic Nature:** LoSST relies heavily on heuristics, particularly in the choice of parameters (γ, T, ν) and the Mahalanobis distance-based change detection. While the authors explore parameter settings in simulations, a more systematic sensitivity analysis is needed. The justification for the chosen parameter values remains somewhat ad-hoc.
* **Computational Cost of Relearning:** The paper acknowledges the computational cost of structure learning, but doesn’t provide detailed analysis or comparisons of the runtime performance of LoSST with and without the probabilistic relearning schedule.  The choice of the PC algorithm as the structure learning method is reasonable but potentially a bottleneck, and exploring faster alternatives might be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Change Detection:**  The Mahalanobis distance is sensitive to outliers.  The authors acknowledge this, but the pooling mechanism might not be robust enough to differentiate between true structural changes and spurious signals from outliers, particularly in high-dimensional settings.  Alternative change detection methods should be considered.
* **Scalability Concerns:**  While the algorithm is designed for online learning, the computational complexity of the PC algorithm (used for structure learning) still scales poorly with the number of variables.  The paper doesn't adequately address how LoSST would perform with very large numbers of variables.
* **Pooling Method:** The Liptak method for pooling p-values isn't explained clearly and its advantages over simpler pooling methods aren't well-justified.  A more thorough discussion of this choice is needed.



**Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a significant and practically relevant problem.
* Presents a novel and well-motivated algorithm.
* Demonstrates strong empirical performance in simulations and a real-world application.
* Provides a theoretical analysis of the algorithm’s convergence and diligence properties.
* Is clearly written and well-organized.

**Con:**

* Relies heavily on heuristics with limited formal justification.
* Computational cost of structure learning could be a bottleneck.
* Change detection mechanism may be sensitive to outliers.
* Scalability to high-dimensional settings is unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical model learning. While the algorithm relies on heuristics and the scalability remains a concern, the novelty, practical relevance, and empirical validation are compelling. The theoretical analysis of convergence vs. diligence is particularly noteworthy.  I believe the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and I **recommend acceptance** with minor revisions. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Sensitivity Analysis:** Perform a more systematic sensitivity analysis of the key parameters (γ, T, ν) and provide guidelines for their selection based on data characteristics.
* **Computational Analysis:**  Include a more detailed analysis of the runtime performance of LoSST, including comparisons with and without probabilistic relearning.
* **Outlier Robustness:**  Investigate the robustness of the change detection mechanism to outliers and consider alternative methods.
* **Scalability Discussion:**  Discuss the scalability of LoSST to high-dimensional settings and potential strategies for addressing this challenge.
* **Pooling Justification:** Provide a more thorough justification for the choice of the Liptak method for pooling p-values.
