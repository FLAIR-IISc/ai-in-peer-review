- Tackles online learning of graphical model structure in non-stationary environments where the generating process changes over time.  
- Proposes LoSST, a heuristic online algorithm that tracks sufficient statistics and relearns structure when change is detected.  
- Uses a Mahalanobis distance–based change detection mechanism and dynamically weights data for relearning.  
- Empirical results (simulations + US price index) show LoSST matches batch methods during stability and outperforms them after changes.  
- Situates work within graphical-model and online-learning literature and differentiates from prior time-varying graph work by being frequentist and online.  
- Addresses a significant and timely problem with practical relevance.  
- Provides a concrete, implementable algorithm with clear update equations.  
- Strong empirical validation demonstrating tracking of parameter and structural changes.  
- Clear discussion of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off and tunable parameters.  
- Well-written and well-organized presentation.  
- LoSST is heuristic and lacks rigorous theoretical guarantees (convergence/error bounds).  
- Performance appears sensitive to parameters (T and γ) without systematic tuning analysis.  
- Reliance on Mahalanobis distance for change detection may be vulnerable to outliers/noise.  
- Computationally expensive relearning step (PC algorithm) may bottleneck high-dimensional or frequently changing settings.  
- Evaluation scenarios are limited; more complex patterns (gradual or multiple simultaneous changes) are not explored.  
- For acceptance: problem importance, novel/practical algorithm, compelling empirical results, and clear presentation.  
- Against acceptance: heuristic nature, parameter sensitivity and computational concerns, limited evaluation scope.  
- Overall assessment: solid contribution deserving accept with minor revisions.  
- Recommended revisions: add theoretical analysis (even partial), systematic parameter tuning, explore robust/alternative change detection, discuss computational complexity and optimizations, and expand evaluation to more complex change patterns.
