Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical structure for linear-Gaussian SEMs. The method maintains exponentially weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) and assesses model fit via per-sample Mahalanobis distances. It pools recent p-values using Liptak’s method to obtain a global statistic ρ; low ρ increases the weights ar and raises the probability of triggering a structure re-learning step (via the PC algorithm). The authors discuss a trade-off between “convergence” and “diligence” controlled by parameters (γ, T), provide proof sketches for extreme regimes, and present simulations (synthetic SEMs with graph and/or parameter changes) and a qualitative case study on U.S. price index volatility.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and under-explored problem: online learning of evolving graphical structures.
- Fully single-pass, low-memory sufficient statistic updates; conceptually simple and potentially practical.
- The “locally stationary” framing is natural; use of pooled evidence (rather than reacting to single outliers) is a reasonable idea for change detection.
- Empirical results suggest LoSST behaves similarly to batch PC under stationarity and adapts faster after changes than batch PC applied to all data.
- The paper is clearly written overall, with sensible discussion of limitations and future work.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological soundness
- Calibration of p-values and pooling: The Mahalanobis distance distribution used to obtain pr+1 is derived under classical assumptions (Hotelling’s T2 with known or independently estimated covariance and a well-defined sample size). Here, (i) Cr is adaptively, recursively estimated using weighted data that include past samples; (ii) “effective sample size” Sr is ad hoc; and (iii) pr’s are pooled while being strongly dependent over time and on Cr. No derivation is provided that pr is calibrated under these dependencies, nor that Liptak pooling remains valid under dependence. This undermines the statistical validity of the change signal driving ar and relearning.
- Double use of data: The same data stream defines Cr and is then “tested” against it, violating standard independence assumptions for T2-based inference. Even asymptotically, adaptive weighting further complicates the limit distribution.
- Choice of T > 1 for diligence: Since ρ ∈ (0,1], setting T > 1 makes the threshold unreachable and forces weight inflation always. While the authors use this to show diligence, it is arguably a degenerate, impractical parameter regime and casts doubt on the relevance of the proof sketch.
- Lack of theoretical analysis of false alarm/detection delay: There is no analysis of the operating characteristics (false positive rate, detection delay, stability under no-change) induced by the weighting and relearning rules. The “convergence vs diligence” discussion is high-level; results depend crucially on the empirical behavior of ρ under dependence and adaptive Cr.
- Use of PC under weighted, non-i.i.d. data: The constraint tests in PC assume i.i.d. samples; here tests are fed weighted sufficient statistics. The paper assumes this is valid but provides no justification or correction for effective degrees of freedom in partial correlation tests, and no regularization when Cr is ill-conditioned (which is likely after abrupt downweighting).
- Computational/numerical issues: Online inversion of Cr for Mahalanobis distances and partial correlations may be unstable when Sr is small; no shrinkage or incremental inverse updates are discussed. Complexity and scalability beyond p≈20 are not analyzed.

Experimental evaluation
- Baselines: The most natural baselines are missing. At minimum: (i) sliding window PC; (ii) exponential forgetting (fixed α) with PC; (iii) online change detection (e.g., CUSUM/GLR/BOCPD) to trigger restarts of a standard learner; (iv) WLS-based SEM tracking. The paper primarily compares to batch PC over all data, which predictably fails after changes.
- Metrics and rigor: The synthetic results show error curves but lack standardized metrics (e.g., SHD over time, detection delay, area under time-to-recovery) and statistical tests. Sensitivity to γ, T, ν is not systematically studied; parameter choices are “unsystematic.”
- Real data: The CPI case study is anecdotal without ground truth or baselines, and claims like detecting the Great Moderation are not substantiated by controlled analyses.
- Ablations: No ablation to isolate the contribution of pooled p-values vs simple EWMA of distances, or the choice of relearning schedule.

Clarity
- The derivation of the effective sample size update (Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1)Sr + 1) is heuristic; a principled justification is needed.
- Assumptions required for Hotelling’s T2 with Sr and Cr should be stated and justified, or the authors should abandon p-values and adopt a more robust change statistic with known behavior under dependence.
- Details necessary for reproduction are insufficient: data generation specifics, PC hyperparameters, how partial correlation tests use weights/effective N, numerical stabilization.

Originality
- The combination of online sufficient statistics, pooled p-values, and probabilistic relearning for structure learning is novel to my knowledge. However, many components (EWMA/TD style tracking, Mahalanobis distances, change detection, periodic relearning) are well-known; the contribution is mostly in the particular assembly for SEM/PC.

Significance
- If the method were better validated and statistically grounded, it could be useful for practitioners needing online structure tracking. In its current form, the lack of calibration, missing baselines, and limited theory reduce confidence and impact.

Questions and requests for the authors
- Provide a derivation (or simulation-based calibration) showing that pr based on Dr and Sr is approximately uniform under stationarity with adaptive Cr. Quantify deviations due to dependence.
- Compare against sliding window PC and fixed-α EWMA as strong baselines, and an online CPD-triggered restart method. Include detection delay and false alarm analyses.
- Add shrinkage (e.g., Ledoit–Wolf) and/or incremental inverse updates; report numerical stability and runtime scaling.
- Clarify how conditional independence tests in PC are adapted to weighted data and effective N.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for γ, T, ν and guidance for tuning.
- Justify or remove the T > 1 regime; it seems impractical.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem; simple, single-pass updates.
- Empirically promising on synthetic shifts; clear writing; acknowledges limitations.

Cons
- Core statistical mechanism (p-value computation and pooling) appears miscalibrated under adaptive dependence.
- Missing strong baselines; limited experimental rigor.
- Proofs are sketchy and hinge on impractical parameter regimes.
- Numerical/computational considerations and scalability not addressed.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the current methodological grounding and evaluation are not sufficient for NeurIPS. Strengthening the statistical foundations, adding proper baselines and analyses, and addressing numerical issues would substantially improve the work.
