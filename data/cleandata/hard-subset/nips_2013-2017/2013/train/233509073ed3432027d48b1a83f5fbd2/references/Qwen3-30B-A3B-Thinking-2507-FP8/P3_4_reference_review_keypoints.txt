- Addresses online graphical model structure learning for time-varying systems and introduces LoSST to fill this gap.  
- LoSST tracks weighted sufficient statistics and uses Mahalanobis distances plus pooled p-values to detect changes and probabilistically relearn structure.  
- Experiments show LoSST matches batch learning during stability and outperforms it after structural shifts; applied to US price index data.  

Strengths:
- Clearly states the problem and motivates need for online methods over batch approaches.  
- Solid theoretical framing for weighted sufficient-statistics updates and the convergence vs. diligence trade-off.  
- Practical, single-pass algorithm suitable for real-time and large-scale use.  
- Comprehensive synthetic experiments and a real-world demonstration.

Weaknesses:
- Claims about convergence (Section 3.1) lack proof or sufficient references.  
- Insufficient engagement and differentiation from related work (e.g., Talih & Hengartner; Siracusa & Fisher III).  
- Probabilistic relearning parameter ν is chosen heuristically without theoretical justification.  
- Method is limited to SEMs (linear models) and not shown for broader graphical model classes.  
- Real-world validation (US price indices) is superficial and doesn’t clearly show unique actionable value.

Overall assessment:
- Valuable and well-motivated contribution that advances online time-varying structure learning, but theoretical and comparative rigor need strengthening.  
- Practical utility is clear, especially the convergence–diligence trade-off, but more proof and broader validation required.

Recommendation:
- Accept with minor revisions.

Required revisions highlighted by reviewer:
- Provide rigorous justification or proof for convergence claims in Section 3.1.  
- Better differentiate LoSST from prior time-varying graphical-model approaches, especially Siracusa & Fisher III.  
- Theoretically justify or systematically study the probabilistic relearning parameter ν.  
- Strengthen real-world validation to demonstrate clear added value over simpler alternatives.

Final thought:
- The paper bridges batch and online structure learning effectively; with stronger theory and comparisons it would be a solid NeurIPS contribution.
