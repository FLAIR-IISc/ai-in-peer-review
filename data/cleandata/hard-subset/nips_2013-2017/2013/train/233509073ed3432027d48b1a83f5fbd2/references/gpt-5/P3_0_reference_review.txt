Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, an online algorithm to track time-varying graphical structure for linear Gaussian DAGs/SEMs under a locally stationary assumption. The method maintains exponentially weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, “effective” sample size) with adaptive weights ar. Weights are adapted based on the discrepancy between current data and the estimated covariance via Hotelling’s T^2/Mahalanobis distance, aggregated over time using a weighted Stouffer/Lipták pooled p-value ρr. Graph relearning is triggered probabilistically with probability increasing as ρr decreases. Structure learning is performed by rerunning a standard constraint-based learner (PC) on the current weighted sufficient statistics. The authors discuss a trade-off between convergence and “diligence” controlled by parameters γ and T, provide proof sketches, and evaluate on synthetic data with abrupt changes in parameters and/or structure, plus a brief case study on CPI component indices.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and under-explored problem: online tracking of changing graphical structures without storing the full history.
- Simple, modular design: decouples change detection/weighting from structure learning; can wrap around different learners.
- Single-pass updates with O(p^2) memory; appealing for streaming and large datasets.
- Sensible empirical desiderata: matches batch PC under stationarity; adapts quickly after changes.
- Discussion of convergence vs. diligence provides intuition for parameter effects; some provable regimes.
- The CPI example illustrates that pooled evidence over multiple points mitigates reacting to single outliers.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
1) Statistical validity of the change signal:
- The p-values used for Mahalanobis distances assume Hotelling’s T^2 under i.i.d. sampling with an unweighted sample covariance. Here, the covariance is a weighted/EWMA estimate with a time-varying “effective” sample size Sr and changing ar. The null distribution of Dr is not Hotelling’s T^2 in this setting; thus pr and ρr are not calibrated. This undermines guarantees on false alarm rates and the meaning of thresholds T.
- Lipták/Stouffer pooling assumes independent (or at least well-calibrated) p-values. Consecutive Dr are serially dependent and share parameters; weighted pooling without accounting for autocorrelation can distort ρr.
- Using Sr in place of N in the T^2 parameters is ad hoc and not justified; effective sample size for EWMA statistics is not generally Sr as defined in Eq. 3.

2) Effective sample size and testing:
- Eq. 3 is constructed to satisfy two heuristics, not derived from a likelihood or asymptotics. Yet Sr drives both the T^2 reference distribution and (presumably) CI tests within PC. There is no analysis that Fisher z or partial correlation tests with Sr behave correctly under weighting. This creates a mismatch between the data-generating process and the tests used for edge decisions.

3) Baselines and experimental design:
- Comparing LoSST to batch PC run on the full mixed dataset is a strawman after change points. Natural baselines include PC on a sliding window, PC with fixed exponential forgetting, CPD+relearn strategies (e.g., Adams & MacKay plus PC on the post-change segment), and time-varying graphical model methods (e.g., Kolar & Xing 2009/2011 kernel-smoothed TV-GGM; Zhou, Lafferty, Wasserman 2010 fused-lasso TV-GGM; joint/fused graphical lasso). For directed models, dynamic Bayesian network approaches (e.g., Xuan & Murphy 2007) are also relevant. Without these baselines, the performance gains are hard to interpret.
- No ablation on the key parameters (T, γ, ν) or sensitivity to dimensionality/sparsity beyond a few sizes. No error bars/variances are reported.

4) Numerical stability and complexity:
- Inverting Cr online with small Sr can be ill-conditioned. No shrinkage or regularization (e.g., Ledoit–Wolf, ridge) is used, and no strategy for maintaining a stable inverse (e.g., Sherman–Morrison updates) is discussed. Positive-definiteness of Cr is not guaranteed by Eq. 2 with changing weights.
- Computational costs and wall-clock/runtime vs. relearning frequency are not reported. Given PC’s cost, showing that the probabilistic schedule achieves a favorable accuracy–cost trade-off would strengthen the case.

5) Theoretical guarantees:
- Proofs are sketch-level and hinge on the specific ar dynamics rather than a statistical model. The diligence result relies on T > 1 (ρ ∈ (0,1]), which implies ar increases regardless of fit; this seems undesirable in practice and undermines the notion of a “threshold.” There are no guarantees on false alarm rates, detection delay, or consistency of the learned structure under local stationarity. No identification result that PC on weighted stats recovers the current DAG under faithfulness is provided.

6) Real data case study:
- The CPI example lacks ground truth and does not address temporal autocorrelation or seasonality beyond differencing. Since LoSST’s trigger is essentially volatility-sensitive, it may conflate heteroskedasticity with structural change. The interpretation around the Great Moderation is anecdotal.

7) Related work coverage:
- The survey omits substantial literature on time-varying graphical models and change detection for covariance structures (e.g., Kolar & Xing, 2009–2011; Zhou et al., 2010; Danaher et al., 2014; dynamic Bayesian networks with change points). These should be cited and compared conceptually and empirically.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized; the high-level idea is easy to follow. Some notation issues and small typos (e.g., “occcurs”). Eq. (4) could be rewritten for interpretability; the role and feasible range of T should be clarified. The mapping from Sr to test thresholds in PC needs explicit justification.

Originality
The combination of adaptive EWMA-style sufficient statistics with pooled discrepancy measures to trigger online DAG relearning is interesting. However, adaptive forgetting and online change detection are well studied, and substantial related work on time-varying graphs is not engaged. Novelty is moderate.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners needing single-pass online structure tracking. However, the lack of calibrated testing, missing baselines, and limited guarantees reduce immediate impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Calibrate the change statistic using prequential/predictive likelihood under the current model, GLR/CUSUM-style tests, or via online bootstrap/permutation to obtain valid thresholds and control false alarms.
- Provide regularized covariance updates and stabilized inverse maintenance.
- Justify or replace Sr with a principled effective sample size; analyze the validity of CI tests under weighting or switch to score-based learners amenable to weighted data.
- Add strong baselines (sliding window PC, exponential forgetting PC, CPD+relearn, TV-GGM) and runtimes. Include sensitivity analyses over T, γ, ν.
- Expand related work and discuss when LoSST is preferable to fused/joint methods.
- If possible, provide finite-sample guarantees on detection delay/false alarm and conditions for correct structure recovery under local stationarity.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The problem is important and the method is appealingly simple, but the statistical calibration, baselines, and theoretical support need strengthening to meet the bar.

Reviewer confidence: High.
