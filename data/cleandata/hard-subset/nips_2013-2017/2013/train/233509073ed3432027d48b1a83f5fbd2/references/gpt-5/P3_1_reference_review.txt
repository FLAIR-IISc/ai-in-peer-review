Summary
The paper addresses online learning of time-varying graphical structure for linear-Gaussian DAG models (SEMs). The proposed Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) maintains exponentially weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective sample size”) with adaptive weights that increase when recent data are unlikely under the current model. Unlikeliness is measured via the Mahalanobis distance and a pooled p-value (Lipták’s method) over recent p-values. The graph is relearned (via the PC algorithm) on a probabilistic schedule that increases with evidence for change. The authors analyze qualitative regimes (convergence vs diligence), present simulations with abrupt changes in structure or parameters, and a case study on US CPI components.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself against standard batch score- or constraint-based structure learning, online changepoint detection (BOCPD and frequentist CPD), and time-indexed graph estimation methods (Talih & Hengartner, Siracusa & Fisher). There is additional relevant work that should be discussed, especially on time-varying graphical models and online/streaming structural learning, e.g., dynamic Bayesian networks; time-varying Gaussian graphical models via kernel/locally weighted neighborhood selection and fused/graphical lasso; and changepoint detection for dependency structure (e.g., Xuan & Murphy, Kolar/Song/Xing, Zhou et al.), as well as sliding-window CI testing. These provide natural baselines and clarify the novelty.

Strengths
- Important problem: tracking structure under nonstationarity with single-pass processing is highly relevant.
- Simple, modular design: decouples tracking sufficient statistics from triggering structure relearning; can slot in alternative CI-based or score-based learners.
- Online updates are memory-light beyond O(p^2) covariance storage; no replay buffer.
- Insightful perspective on convergence vs diligence in nonstationary settings and parameter regimes that interpolate between them.
- Synthetic experiments convincingly show advantages over batch PC after abrupt changes; online probabilistic relearning reduces computation without large accuracy loss.
- Real-world example (CPI) is plausible and illustrates detection of sustained volatility shifts.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Statistical validity of the detection signal:
  - The p-values entering the Lipták pool are neither independent nor identically distributed; the covariance and mean used to compute each Mahalanobis distance are functions of all previous data and weights. The Hotelling T^2 reference distribution with “effective sample size” Sr is not justified for weighted estimators under nonstationarity. This undermines the interpretability of ρr as a p-value and the thresholding logic in Eq. (4).
  - No analysis of false-alarm rate or detection delay; no calibration showing that pooled ρr correlates with actual change events under model misspecification.
- “Effective sample size” update Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1) Sr + 1 is ad hoc. It enforces desirable qualitative behavior (recent data dominate after change) but lacks derivation from an estimator variance analysis. For weighted covariances, standard ESS is (∑w)^2 / ∑w^2, which does not match Eq. (3). This affects both CI tests and the T^2 degrees of freedom.
- CI testing under small Sr: PC relies on partial correlations and asymptotic tests; with rapidly decreasing Sr, tests are unstable. No regularization/shrinkage is used to ensure PD covariance or stable partial correlations; no discussion of test thresholds under changing Sr.
- Baselines: Comparisons are only to batch PC. Natural baselines include:
  - Sliding-window PC with window size tuned (or EWMA weighting with fixed forgetting factor).
  - CPD + reset PC (detect with BOCPD/EWMA/CUSUM, then relearn).
  - Time-varying graphical models for undirected graphs (kernel/TV-ℓ1) adapted to CI structure or moralized graphs.
  Without these, the empirical advantage of LoSST over straightforward alternatives is unclear.
- Sensitivity to T, γ, ν: Theoretical sketches give qualitative regimes, but experiments choose T=0.05, γ=3, ν=0.005 without systematic tuning or robustness analysis. Practical guidance is missing.
- Numerical and computational aspects: No runtime or scalability measurements; PC can be expensive for high degree. Inverting Cr online without regularization can be unstable. No discussion of handling p ≥ Sr or near-singular covariance.
- Real-world study: Only qualitative interpretation. No external validation (e.g., consistency of learned edges across stable periods), no predictive evaluation, and no domain-expert sanity checks. It remains a case illustration.
- Related work coverage: The manuscript under-references work on time-varying precision/graph estimation and dynamic/dependence change modeling. This weakens the positioning.

Quality
The method is coherent and implementable, but relies on heuristic statistical constructs (pooled p-values and ESS) without calibration or guarantees. Theory on convergence/diligence is high-level and depends on assumptions (e.g., PC convergence) that may not hold under adaptive weighting. Experiments are suggestive but lack strong baselines and sensitivity/risk analyses.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. Some derivations (Eq. 2 and 3) would benefit from more detail or an appendix. The definitions around T^2 and Sr need more care. Minor typos and footnote formatting issues.

Originality
The specific combination—adaptive weighted sufficient statistics gated by pooled MD p-values, with probabilistic relearning of DAG structure—is novel as a practical heuristic. However, the problem space is well-studied, and comparison to closely related time-varying graph literature is limited.

Significance
Potentially useful in practice due to simplicity and single-pass nature, but current empirical evidence and theoretical grounding are insufficient for strong claims. With stronger baselines, calibration, and numerical stability measures, impact could be meaningful.

Recommendations for improvement
- Provide a principled treatment (or at least calibration) of the change score: consider EWMA/CUSUM of MD, or approximate BOCPD on Gaussian residuals; evaluate false-alarm/delay on synthetic data with and without model violations.
- Justify or replace the ESS formula; report how CI tests are adapted to weighted data and small Sr; add shrinkage/regularization for covariance and partial correlations.
- Add baselines: sliding-window PC, CPD+reset, and a time-varying GLasso variant; compare accuracy and number of relearns as a function of compute.
- Include sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν and ablations (no pooling vs pooling; different pooling rules).
- Report runtime and scalability; discuss maintaining PD covariance and numerical stability.
- Extend simulations to gradual drift and mixtures; study robustness to non-Gaussian noise and outliers.
- Strengthen the real-data evaluation with predictive checks, stability of edges, and domain validation.

Pros
- Addresses an important, underexplored online setting
- Simple, modular algorithm; single-pass and memory-efficient
- Useful qualitative theory; clean synthetic demonstrations

Cons
- Heuristic statistical foundations without calibration
- Missing strong baselines and sensitivity analyses
- Potential numerical instability; no runtime/scalability results
- Limited related work coverage and real-world validation

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising, but the current version lacks statistical justification, comparative evaluation, and robustness analyses needed for NeurIPS. I encourage revision along the lines above.
