

# Review: Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm for online learning of graphical model structure when the underlying structure may change over time. The authors identify a significant gap in the literature: while standard graphical model structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data from a single static structure, real-world applications often require tracking changing structures in an online fashion. The paper systematically reviews related work, including Bayesian/score-based structure learning, constraint-based methods, online learning algorithms like TD(0), and changepoint detection methods, demonstrating their insufficiency for this specific problem. LoSST addresses this gap by tracking sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) with adaptive weights based on Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values, then probabilistically relearning the graphical structure.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Problem Addressed:** The paper successfully identifies and addresses an important gap in graphical model learning - online structure learning when structures change. This is a significant contribution to the field, with clear practical relevance to domains like finance and social networks.

2. **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the algorithm, including a clear analysis of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off (a novel contribution to the literature), and mathematical proofs for specific parameter regimes.

3. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The simulation results are thorough, comparing LoSST to batch-mode learning (PC algorithm) across multiple scenarios (structure change, parameter change). The real-world application to US price index volatility demonstrates practical utility and shows the algorithm can detect meaningful economic shifts.

4. **Clear Differentiation from Related Work:** The paper carefully distinguishes LoSST from previous approaches, addressing limitations of existing methods with specific examples.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Guarantees:** While the paper provides a nice analysis of the convergence/diligence trade-off, it lacks comprehensive theoretical guarantees about the algorithm's performance in general settings. For instance, how does the algorithm behave in high-dimensional settings, or when the structure changes rapidly?

2. **Ad-hoc Probabilistic Relearning Mechanism:** The probabilistic relearning schedule (using ν as a parameter) feels somewhat ad-hoc. The paper could benefit from a more principled approach to determining relearning frequency, perhaps connecting it to the Mahalanobis distance or pooled p-values.

3. **Limited Real-World Scale:** The US price index application uses only 6 variables, which is a small-scale example. A more convincing demonstration would involve larger, more complex real-world datasets with many variables where online structure learning would provide clear advantages.

4. **Comparison to Alternative Online Methods:** The paper doesn't compare LoSST to other online structure learning approaches that might be applicable, such as those using online learning for specific aspects of the structure (e.g., edge additions/deletions).

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Addresses a significant gap in graphical model learning with clear practical relevance
- Well-motivated, theoretically grounded approach with novel theoretical insights
- Comprehensive simulation results demonstrating effectiveness in both synthetic and real-world settings
- Clear differentiation from related work with thorough literature review

*Con:*
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond the convergence/diligence trade-off
- Ad-hoc probabilistic relearning mechanism without principled justification
- Real-world application too limited in scale (6 variables)
- No comparison to alternative online structure learning methods

**Recommendation and Suggestions for Improvement:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in graphical model learning. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, and the algorithm has clear potential for real-world application.

To strengthen the paper, I suggest:

1. Adding a more comprehensive theoretical analysis of performance guarantees, particularly for high-dimensional settings where graphical models are most challenging.

2. Providing a more principled justification for the probabilistic relearning mechanism, perhaps connecting it directly to the Mahalanobis distance or pooled p-value.

3. Expanding the real-world application to a larger dataset with more variables (e.g., financial market data with 20+ variables) to demonstrate scalability.

4. Adding a brief comparison to alternative online structure learning methods, even if only to explain why they're not suitable for this specific problem.

5. Clarifying the relationship between the effective sample size and the Mahalanobis distance in the algorithm description.

The paper's focus on the convergence/diligence trade-off is particularly noteworthy and represents a significant theoretical contribution. With the suggested revisions to strengthen the theoretical foundation and expand the empirical validation, this would be an excellent contribution to NeurIPS.
