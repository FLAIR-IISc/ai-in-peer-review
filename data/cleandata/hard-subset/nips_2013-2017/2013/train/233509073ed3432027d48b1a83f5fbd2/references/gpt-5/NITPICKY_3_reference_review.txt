Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical structure in linear-Gaussian SEMs. It maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariance, “effective” sample size) with adaptive weights based on the pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances (via Lipták’s method). When pooled evidence suggests change, LoSST increases weights on recent data (reducing effective sample size) and triggers probabilistic “relearning” of structure using PC. The authors discuss a convergence–diligence trade-off controlled by parameters (T, γ), present simulations comparing LoSST against batch PC in scenarios with structural or parametric changes, demonstrate a probabilistic relearning schedule, and show a small case study on US CPI series.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: online structure learning with changing generating processes.
- Conceptually simple, modular design (stat tracking + PC), potentially adaptable to other learners.
- Simulation evidence suggests LoSST can recover from changepoints faster than batch PC, with little penalty under stationarity.
- The convergence–diligence discussion is interesting and highlights a genuine tension in online learning.

Key weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and statistical calibration:
- Pooled p-values via Lipták: the paper defines ρr+1 = Φ(ηr+1, √τr+1) though Φ is defined as a Gaussian CDF with variance argument; variance should be τr+1, not √τr+1. This likely miscalibrates ρ.
- Mahalanobis/Hotelling’s T2: with estimated covariance from weighted samples, the distribution of (plug-in) Mahalanobis distances is not Hotelling’s T2 with m = Sr − 1. The effective degrees of freedom under weighting are nonstandard; using Hotelling’s T2 with “effective” N is not justified. This undermines the p-values and downstream weighting.
- Effective sample size update (Eq. 3) Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1) Sr + 1 is ad hoc and not connected to standard effective-N definitions (e.g., Kish Neff = (Σw)^2/Σw^2). No derivation is provided that this Sr yields valid test calibration for CI tests in PC or for T2.
- Weight transform (Eq. 4) appears mis-typed. As written, ar+1 = ar·max{1, γT − γρ + ρ/T} reaches arγT at ρ=0, contradicting the text (“maximum of γar”). I suspect the intended factor is (γT − γρ + ρ)/T.
- The claim “If ar = α br, then learning is the same as TD(0)” is unclear because ar depends on br recursively; likely ar = α br−1 is intended.

Use of PC with weighted statistics:
- PC’s CI tests rely on asymptotic distributions (e.g., Fisher’s Z on partial correlations) parameterized by sample size. Using Sr as “effective N” for weighted, non-i.i.d. data is not justified; the nominal p-values may be badly miscalibrated. No correction, bootstrap, or simulation-based calibration is provided.

Theory:
- Convergence/diligence claims are presented with sketches and undefined conditions (e.g., formal definition of diligence, assumptions on noise/outliers). Proof sketches depend on properties of Sr and ρ that, given the above calibration issues, are fragile.
- The condition “T > 1 & γ > 1 ⇒ diligence” is insufficiently formal; the argument conflates a bounded Sr with guaranteed change detection within a fixed window.

Comparisons and baselines:
- Comparisons are only to batch PC on the full dataset, which is not a fair baseline under nonstationarity. Missing baselines include: sliding-window PC, exponential forgetting PC, online CPD + batch PC on post-change windows, dynamic graphical models (e.g., time-varying graphical lasso: Zhou, Lafferty, Wasserman 2010; Kolar, Song, Xing 2010; Hallac et al. 2017), dynamic Bayesian network learners, and online CD methods.
- The probabilistic relearning experiment uses a single dataset repeated 500 times; broader randomization across datasets and sensitivity to ν is needed.

Reproducibility and experimental detail:
- Critical details are missing: DAG generation procedure, coefficient/noise distributions, CI test type and α, implementation details for weighted CI tests, covariance regularization, handling of singularity at small Sr, choice of T, γ, ν beyond “unsystematic investigation.”
- No complexity analysis per time step; computing Dr requires matrix inversion. Without Sherman–Morrison–Woodbury updates or regularization, O(p^3) per step may be prohibitive.

Real-data case study:
- The CPI application infers “Great Moderation” qualitatively from ρ and D, but lacks baselines, changepoint annotations, or robustness checks. No learned graphs are shown or discussed, making it hard to assess structural insights. Claims about volatility should be supported by explicit variance modeling.

Clarity and presentation:
- Numerous typos and formatting errors impede readability: footnotes appear inline (e.g., “graph2”), inconsistent variable names (Xi/Xri, Vi/Vj), misspellings (“TDL,” “occcurs,” “intially”), missing parentheses in equations, inconsistent parameterization of T2 (p=V vs p=N).
- References are incomplete (missing years, venues) and several relevant works are omitted (time-varying graphical models: Kolar & Xing; Zhou et al.; Xuan & Murphy 2007; Hallac et al. TVGL; dynamic BN literature).

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The idea is promising, but key statistical pieces (p-value pooling, effective sample size, CI calibration) are not technically sound as written; theory is informal; baselines are weak.
- Clarity: Generally understandable at a high level but marred by notation/formatting errors and missing details; insufficient for reproduction.
- Originality: Combining pooled p-values to modulate forgetting for online structure learning is interesting, though related notions (EWMA/forgetting + change detection) exist; dynamic graph literature is not adequately positioned against.
- Significance: Potentially useful if technically solid and empirically validated against strong baselines; in current form, impact is limited.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem.
- Simple, modular algorithm; empirical signs of utility.
- Thoughtful discussion of convergence vs diligence.

Cons
- Statistical miscalibration (T2, Lipták variance, Sr).
- Weak theoretical guarantees and proofs.
- Missing/weak baselines; limited experiments.
- Many presentation and reproducibility issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the Lipták pooling and clearly derive the correct normalization; validate ρ calibration via simulation.
- Replace ad hoc Sr with a principled effective sample size (e.g., Kish Neff) and recalibrate CI tests (bootstrap, permutation, or analytic derivations for weighted partial correlations).
- Justify use of Hotelling’s T2 (or use an alternative anomaly score with known properties under weighting); consider robust metrics and regularization.
- Provide per-step complexity, numerical stability strategies (e.g., rank-one updates for Σ−1, shrinkage covariance).
- Compare against sliding-window/forgetting PC, CPD+PC, and time-varying graphical model methods (Zhou et al. 2010; Kolar et al. 2010; Hallac et al. 2017; Xuan & Murphy 2007).
- Expand experiments with multiple random seeds/datasets; report sensitivity to T, γ, ν; specify all simulation and test parameters.
- Clean up notation, equations, typos, and complete references.

Recommendation: Reject (in current form). The idea is promising, but technical/statistical issues, missing baselines, and clarity problems are substantial and would overshadow the contributions at NeurIPS. I would be enthusiastic about a revised, carefully calibrated version with stronger theory and empirical validation.
