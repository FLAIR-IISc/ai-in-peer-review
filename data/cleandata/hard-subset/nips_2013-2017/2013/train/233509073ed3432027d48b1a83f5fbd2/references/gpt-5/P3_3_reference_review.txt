Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical structure in linear Gaussian SEMs (DAGs). It maintains weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, “effective” sample size) via single-pass updates, where the per-point weight ar adapts to evidence of change measured by a pooled p-value over Mahalanobis-distance p-values (Liptak’s method). Structure is relearned intermittently (PC algorithm) with probability increasing when recent data poorly fit the current covariance. The authors discuss trade-offs between convergence and “diligence” (rapid response to change), provide proof sketches for limiting behavior under parameter regimes, demonstrate synthetic experiments with graph and parameter changes, and present a qualitative macroeconomic case study on US price indices.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and underexplored problem: online structure learning for changing DAGs under local stationarity, with an approach that is conceptually simple and computationally lightweight per update.
- The adaptive weighting via pooled p-values is intuitive and potentially unifies sensitivity to both abrupt and gradual shifts.
- The design isolates relearning triggers from sufficient-statistics tracking, offering modularity (other learners than PC could be substituted).
- Experiments demonstrate that, in stable regimes, LoSST behaves comparably to batch PC, and after change points, it adapts faster than a naïve batch learner trained on all data.
- The discussion of convergence vs diligence is thoughtful and situates design choices within a broader theoretical lens.

Major concerns
1) Statistical validity of change evidence and tests:
- The use of Hotelling’s T^2 distribution for Mahalanobis distances assumes a covariance estimated from i.i.d. samples; here the covariance is a weighted estimator with time-varying weights. The calibration of p-values pr (and hence pooled ρ) is at best approximate. Dependencies arise because each pr uses statistics shared across time. Liptak pooling assumes independence (or at least known dependence structure), which is violated here. Without calibration or bounds, false alarm and delay properties are unclear.
- The “effective sample size” Sr is used to parameterize T^2 and PC’s conditional independence tests, but Sr is defined heuristically (Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1)Sr + 1) and can be fractional. No derivation connects Sr to the sampling distribution of (partial) correlations under the weighted scheme; thus Type I/II error control in PC is uncertain.

2) Baselines and empirical evaluation:
- The main baseline is batch PC on the full data, which is a strawman after a regime change. More appropriate baselines include sliding-window PC, exponentially weighted PC with fixed forgetting, CPD+reset (detect change, then restart PC), or score-based learners on windows. An oracle baseline (PC on post-change data) would contextualize adaptation speed. Without these, it is hard to assess net benefit over simpler, established alternatives.
- Hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) are selected “unsystematically”; no sensitivity analysis is provided. Given the centrality of these parameters for both detection and relearning frequency, robustness needs to be established.
- Scalability is not addressed. Computing Dr requires inverting Cr per step (O(p^3)), and PC is super-polynomial in worst-case graph density. Results are up to p=20; practical performance for p≥100 (common in GGMs) is unknown. Numerical stability and the need for regularization (e.g., shrinkage covariance, Woodbury updates) are not discussed.

3) Theoretical claims:
- The convergence/diligence statements rely on proof sketches with unverified assumptions (e.g., PC consistency under the weighted, time-varying sampling scheme; using Sr as if it were an i.i.d. sample size; sequences Tr=f(Sr) ensuring “infinite unbiased data”). The claim that T>1 and γ>1 imply diligence uses inequalities over ar but ignores stochastic variability in ρ and its distributional miscalibration. Formal theorems with conditions would strengthen the work.
- Orientation accuracy in PC depends on correctly calibrated CI tests; under weighted and time-varying statistics, standard Fisher z or T^2 thresholds are not guaranteed to hold.

4) Relation to prior work:
- The paper reviews CPD, TD learning, and two time-varying structure papers, but omits several relevant literatures: adaptive forgetting in RLS and covariance tracking; time-varying graphical models for GGMs (e.g., fused penalties, localized likelihood); sliding-window or adaptive BN/DBN learners; and change detection for multivariate Gaussians beyond T^2 with EWMA control charts. Positioning LoSST relative to adaptive forgetting/sliding windows would clarify its novelty.

5) Real-data case study:
- The CPI application primarily shows an effective sample size dip around known macro events. No quantitative evaluation of structure accuracy, stability, or predictive performance is provided, and learned graphs are not analyzed. As such, it serves as a qualitative illustration rather than validation.

Minor comments
- Clarify and justify the Sr recurrence; derive it from a variance or information perspective for weighted estimators, or cite appropriate results.
- Discuss regularization for Cr inversion (e.g., shrinkage, diagonal loading) and numerical safeguards early in the stream.
- State explicitly which CI tests are used in PC with weighted statistics and how degrees of freedom are set.
- Provide complexity analysis per time step and per relearning event; discuss possible inverse updates (Sherman–Morrison–Woodbury).
- Fix typos (e.g., “occcurs”) and footnote formatting; ensure all references are complete and current.
- Consider reporting predictive log-likelihood on hold-out rolling windows as a task-agnostic metric.

Pros for acceptance
- Timely problem; simple, implementable idea with potential practical impact.
- Single-pass maintenance of sufficient statistics; modular with existing structure learners.
- Appealing unification of change detection and adaptive forgetting.
- Clear qualitative behavior and synthetic evidence that adaptation is feasible.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient statistical grounding for p-value calibration and CI testing under time-varying weighted estimates.
- Missing strong baselines; limited empirical evidence and scale.
- Theoretical guarantees are informal; key claims hinge on unproven assumptions.
- Real-data evaluation is qualitative.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a promising and practical heuristic for online structure tracking, but it lacks rigorous statistical calibration, stronger baselines, sensitivity analyses, and scalability discussion. Strengthening these aspects would make it a compelling contribution.
