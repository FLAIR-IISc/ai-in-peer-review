Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical structure for linear-Gaussian DAG models (recursive SEMs). The key idea is to maintain “locally stationary” sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an effective sample size) via weighted online updates, where per-sample weights are adapted based on how well incoming data fit the current estimated covariance. Fit is assessed using Mahalanobis distances whose p-values are pooled over time using Lipták’s method; lower pooled p-values increase the weight of recent data and raise the probability of triggering a structure relearning step. Structure is relearned using a constraint-based method (PC). The paper analyzes the algorithm’s long-run behaviors, showing parameter regimes that yield convergence, diligence (rapid response to changes), or intermediate trade-offs, and presents simulations and a real-data case study (US price indices).

Related work is positioned along several axes: batch structure learning (score-based and constraint-based), online prediction and change detection (TD methods; Bayesian and frequentist CPD), and prior work on time-varying graphs (Talih & Hengartner for undirected graphs; Siracusa & Fisher for Bayesian time-indexed edges). The paper argues that LoSST uniquely provides single-pass, online structure tracking for DAGs without maintaining a posterior over models.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation: tracking time-varying DAG structure in an online, single-pass setting with locally i.i.d. data segments.
- Simple, implementable mechanism: weighted updates for means/covariances and an interpretable effective sample size.
- Principled triggers: change sensitivity via pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances, with a probabilistic relearning schedule that increases near changepoints.
- Analytical insight: explicit discussion of convergence vs. diligence, including sufficient conditions for each regime and an intermediate trade-off region.
- Empirical evidence: synthetic experiments show LoSST matches batch PC pre-change and outperforms it post-change; a real data case study aligns with known macroeconomic events.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation
Quality (technical soundness)
- Distributional assumptions: The method relies on p-values from Hotelling’s T^2 for Mahalanobis distances using an “effective” sample size derived from adaptive, normalized weights. Under adaptive weighting and re-estimation of mean/covariance, the null distribution of these distances is not exactly T^2; independence and uniformity assumptions required by Lipták pooling are also questionable. The paper acknowledges heuristics but does not quantify the effect of these deviations (e.g., calibration of pooled p-values under the null or with dependence).
- Covariance estimation details: The weighted covariance uses normalized weights (a_k/b_r), which corresponds to a biased moment estimator rather than an unbiased sample covariance. The consequences for test calibration and for PC’s conditional independence tests are not analyzed.
- Computational aspects: Mahalanobis distances require inversion (or solves) of the evolving covariance; complexity and numerical stability under frequent reweighting are not discussed. Maintaining an O(p^2) covariance and periodic PC runs can be costly; concrete time/memory complexity and scalability limits are not provided.
- Baselines: Post-change comparisons are primarily against batch PC on the entire dataset, which is expected to underperform due to distribution mixing. More informative baselines (e.g., sliding-window PC, windowed or exponentially weighted estimators, CPD with refitting, dynamic Bayesian networks, or time-varying graphical lasso for undirected analogs) are not evaluated.
- Hyperparameters: Key choices (T, γ, ν) are selected by “unsystematic investigation”; no sensitivity analysis, calibration strategy, or guidance is provided.

Clarity
- Overall exposition is readable and well-organized; the algorithmic components and their interactions are explained clearly.
- Some derivations are asserted “after some algebra” without proof or reference; including an appendix with derivations (e.g., covariance update, effective sample size recursion) would improve reproducibility.
- The reliance on T^2 and Lipták pooling under adaptive weighting should more explicitly state assumptions and limitations.
- Figures (not included here) are referenced with relevant descriptions. The simulation setup is generally clear, though more details on PC settings and conditional independence tests would help.

Originality
- The combination of online weighted sufficient-statistic tracking, pooled p-value change detection, and periodic constraint-based relearning for DAGs appears novel. Prior time-varying structure approaches have largely been offline (Talih & Hengartner; Siracusa & Fisher) or for other graph families.
- Related literatures on adaptive forgetting/discounting, EWMA/CUSUM, dynamic Bayesian networks, and time-varying sparse GGM are under-cited and not used as baselines. Nonetheless, the specific formulation for linear-Gaussian DAGs with single-pass updates is a distinctive contribution.

Significance
- Addressing online structure learning for nonstationary environments is important. The proposed approach is lightweight in memory and could be practical for streaming settings where storing data is infeasible.
- Empirical gains over a naïve batch baseline in change scenarios are substantial; however, the absence of stronger baselines tempers claims about state-of-the-art performance.
- Applicability is currently limited to linear-Gaussian SEMs and to constraint-based relearning; extending to discrete BNs or robust score-based updates would expand impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide calibration studies: assess empirical distribution of pooled p-values under stationarity with adaptive weighting; quantify false alarm/true detection trade-offs.
- Add stronger baselines: sliding-window PC, exponentially weighted covariance plus PC, CPD followed by refitting, and for undirected graphs, time-varying graphical lasso.
- Analyze computational complexity and stability: per-step cost for updating covariance and computing Mahalanobis distance (with or without Sherman–Morrison updates), and scaling trends with p and relearning frequency.
- Include sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν; propose heuristics or data-driven tuning (e.g., target false alarm rate).
- Clarify assumptions for T^2 and Lipták pooling under dependent, adaptively weighted statistics; consider alternative change statistics (e.g., EWMA of squared Mahalanobis distances) less dependent on exact null calibration.
- Improve references and complete bibliographic details.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clearly formulated and practically motivated problem; simple, memory-efficient algorithm.
- Novel coupling of online sufficient-statistic tracking with change-aware relearning for DAG structure.
- Insightful analysis of convergence vs. diligence trade-offs.
- Consistent synthetic results and a real-world case study indicative of practical utility.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on heuristic calibration of p-values and pooling under violated assumptions without empirical calibration or theory.
- Missing comparisons to stronger and more relevant baselines.
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and stability.
- Hyperparameter tuning lacks guidance; robustness is not demonstrated.

Overall, the paper offers a useful and reasonably novel approach to online structure tracking with promising empirical results, but would benefit from stronger empirical validation, calibration, and baseline comparisons to support its claims.
