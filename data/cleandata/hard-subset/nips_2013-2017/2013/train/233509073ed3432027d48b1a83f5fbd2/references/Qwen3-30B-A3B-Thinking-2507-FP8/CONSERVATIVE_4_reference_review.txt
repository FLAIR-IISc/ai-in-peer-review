

# Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

This paper proposes the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm for online learning of graphical models when the underlying structure may change over time. The authors correctly identify a gap in the literature: most structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data, while real-world applications often involve dynamically changing structures. The paper positions LoSST as an online alternative to batch methods that can track these changes in real-time without requiring retrospective analysis.

## Strengths
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a strong technical presentation of the algorithm. The authors provide a thoughtful discussion of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off, which is a valuable theoretical contribution. The simulation results demonstrate reasonable performance on synthetic data, particularly in detecting structure changes where batch methods fail. The real-world application to US price index volatility, while limited to six variables, provides some evidence of practical utility.

## Weaknesses

**Lack of rigorous theoretical foundation:** The paper presents LoSST as a heuristic algorithm with limited theoretical grounding. The key parameter choices (T=0.05, γ=3) appear arbitrary, with no justification for why these specific values work best. The theoretical analysis is limited to showing that certain parameter settings yield convergence or diligence, but it doesn't establish why the proposed parameter values are optimal or how they should be selected for different applications. This is particularly concerning given the authors' claim that "using this algorithm incurs no additional costs in 'normal' structure learning situations" without rigorous proof.

**Insufficient comparison with existing methods:** The paper cites several related works but fails to properly benchmark against existing online structure learning approaches. The authors claim "none are capable of general, graphical model structure learning" in online settings, yet they don't compare against the most relevant alternatives. For instance, they mention "Bayesian online changepoint detection" but don't compare LoSST's performance against these methods. The comparison with batch-mode PC algorithm is only meaningful in the context of structure change, not in the "normal" stable settings where the authors claim LoSST incurs no additional cost.

**Overstated claims:** The paper makes several claims that aren't sufficiently supported by evidence. The statement that "when there is a stable generating structure, the algorithm's performance is indistinguishable from a standard batch-mode structure learning algorithm" is only shown for the first 1000 datapoints in the simulations, but the authors don't establish how long this "indistinguishability" holds or how it scales with different parameters. The paper also overstates the novelty by presenting LoSST as a fundamentally new approach when it primarily combines existing techniques (online sufficient statistics tracking, Mahalanobis distance, and probabilistic relearning).

**Limited empirical validation:** The simulations use synthetic data with specific configurations (only 8 combinations of variable counts and degrees), and the real-world application is limited to a single dataset with only six variables. The paper doesn't explore how LoSST performs on larger graphs or with more complex structure changes. The comparison with batch methods is only meaningful when a structure change occurs, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how LoSST performs in scenarios where the structure changes slowly or continuously, which the authors acknowledge as a limitation.

**Theoretical gaps:** The paper claims that "the algorithm's performance is indistinguishable from a standard batch-mode structure learning algorithm" during stable periods, but this isn't rigorously established. The authors don't prove that the weighted statistics tracking converges to the batch estimates under the same conditions. The claim that "LoSST can also function as a single-pass, graphical model structure learner for very large datasets" is unsupported by empirical evidence or theoretical analysis of computational complexity.

## Recommendation

While the problem addressed is important and the paper demonstrates some promising results on synthetic data, the paper falls short of the high standards expected at NIPS. The method is primarily heuristic with insufficient theoretical grounding, and the empirical validation is limited. The authors fail to properly benchmark against relevant existing methods, making it difficult to assess whether LoSST provides a meaningful improvement over the state of the art.

The paper would benefit significantly from:
1) A more thorough comparison with existing online structure learning methods
2) Rigorous theoretical analysis of the parameter selection and convergence properties
3) More comprehensive empirical validation across a wider range of graph sizes and change patterns
4) Clearer articulation of the trade-offs between the algorithm's performance and computational cost

Given the paper's current state, I recommend rejection. The contributions are incremental rather than transformative, and the lack of rigorous validation against established baselines undermines the authors' claims about the algorithm's superiority. The paper would be more appropriate for a venue that prioritizes application-oriented work over methodological innovation with strong theoretical foundations.
