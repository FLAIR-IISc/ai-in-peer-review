- Paper proposes LoSST: online tracking of time-varying linear-Gaussian SEMs via weighted sufficient statistics and pooled p-value (Liptak) on Mahalanobis distances to adaptively reweight observations and trigger probabilistic PC relearning.
- Positions work relative to batch structure learning, TD learning, CPD, and prior time-varying graph papers; claims online advantage over batch methods.

Strengths
- Addresses an important real-world problem: online tracking of changing causal/graphical structure.
- Algorithm is simple, single-pass, O(V^2) memory, and integrates with standard structure learners.
- Pooled p-value smoothing and decoupling detection from relearning via probabilistic schedule are sensible design choices.
- Provides intuition and sketch theoretical conditions for a convergence vs. diligence trade-off.
- Empirical results show LoSST matches batch PC in static regimes and adapts faster after changes; probabilistic relearning reduces compute with limited accuracy loss.

Quality / Methodological concerns
- Use of Hotelling’s T^2 for Mahalanobis distances derived from adaptively weighted covariance is statistically questionable and uncalibrated.
- Liptak pooling assumes independence violated by using same data for multiple distances; false positive/negative behavior is not quantified.
- Effective sample size Sr update is ad hoc, not derived or analyzed for bias/variance or relation to Fisher information.
- Paper does not specify how PC’s conditional independence tests are calibrated under weighting, leaving Type I/II error control unclear.
- Theoretical claims are mostly sketches relying on unnatural regimes and lack guarantees (e.g., detection delay, false alarm bounds, structural consistency under local stationarity).
- Empirical baselines are weak: main comparator is batch PC on mixed data rather than sliding-window, EWMA, CPD+reset, time-varying GGMs, or other appropriate baselines.
- Sensitivity analyses for hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) are minimal and unsystematic.
- Real-data case study is qualitative with no annotated change points, stability analysis, or quantitative validation.

Clarity and reproducibility
- Paper is generally clear but contains notational slips (e.g., p = N typo, inconsistent covariance notation).
- Lacks specification of the CI testing procedure (test type, alpha, how Sr enters) and pseudocode for update-relearn loop and state variables.
- Missing discussion of numerical stability (ensuring PD covariance when Sr is small, use of shrinkage).

Originality
- Combination of pooled-p-value-driven reweighting and probabilistic DAG relearning is novel for online SEM tracking, but components are familiar and related literature (time-varying GGMs, dynamic BNs, online BN learning) is under-cited.

Significance
- Potentially impactful if made robust, but current heuristic detection, weak baselines, and limited theoretical guarantees limit NeurIPS-level significance.

Pros (concise)
- Tackles underserved online time-varying DAG problem; simple, efficient, and integrable; clear intuitions; promising synthetic gains.

Cons (concise)
- Heuristic statistical machinery, missing strong baselines, limited formal guarantees, and qualitative real-data validation only.

Key suggestions for authors
- Provide systematic sensitivity sweeps for T, γ, ν across graph sizes/densities and practical guidance for tuning.
- Compare to sliding-window PC, EWMA covariance+PC, CPD+reset PC, time-varying GGM methods, and simple EWMA detectors.
- Clarify and justify CI test calibration under weighting, including degrees of freedom and multiple-testing control as relearning frequency varies.
- Add numerical stabilization (shrinkage) and address PD guarantees when Sr is small.
- Consider more principled change statistics (e.g., sequential GLR or score-based tests) and quantify false alarm/delay tradeoffs.
- Expand related work to include time-varying GGMs, dynamic Bayesian networks, and online BN structure learning.

Recommendation
- Weak reject: promising and relevant but relies on uncalibrated heuristics, lacks strong baselines and theoretical/empirical validation needed for acceptance.
