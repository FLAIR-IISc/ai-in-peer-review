**Summary**

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a heuristic online algorithm for learning the structure of directed graphical models (specifically, recursive Structural Equation Models) from streaming data where the underlying generative structure may change over time. The core idea is to maintain and update sufficient statistics (mean, covariance) using a dynamic weighting scheme. New data points that fit the current model poorly, as measured by Mahalanobis distance, are given higher weights, effectively discounting older data. The algorithm uses a pooled p-value approach to avoid overreacting to single outliers. A probabilistic trigger, also based on model fit, determines when to re-run a standard batch structure learning algorithm (here, the PC algorithm) on the current weighted sufficient statistics. The authors analyze the algorithm's theoretical properties in terms of a trade-off between convergence to a stable structure and diligence in detecting change. Experimental results on synthetic data show that LoSST can successfully track structural and parametric changes, outperforming a batch learner applied to the full dataset, while performing comparably in stable periods. An application to US price index data is also presented.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The problem of online learning of graphical model structure in non-stationary environments is both challenging and highly relevant. Standard batch learning methods are fundamentally unsuited for this task, and the paper correctly identifies an important gap in the literature.
2.  **Novel Heuristic Approach:** The proposed LoSST algorithm combines several ideas (online updates of sufficient statistics, Mahalanobis distance for change detection, pooled p-values for robustness, probabilistic relearning) into a coherent, albeit heuristic, framework for tackling this problem.
3.  **Theoretical Framing:** The discussion of the "convergence vs. diligence" trade-off (Sec 3.1) is insightful. Framing the algorithm's behavior in terms of this known theoretical impossibility and showing how parameters can tune this trade-off adds conceptual depth to the work.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising direction, the paper suffers from a significant number of flaws in its technical presentation, methodological justification, and experimental design.

**1. Lack of Rigor and Clarity:**

*   **Heuristic Justifications:** Several core components of the algorithm are justified with informal or purely heuristic arguments. The update equation for the "effective sample size" (Eq. 3) is motivated by two "constraints" without a principled derivation. The validity of using this non-integer `Sr` as the sample size parameter for the Hotelling's T^2 distribution is not justified, which is a critical omission that undermines the statistical basis of the change detection mechanism.
*   **Insufficient Proofs:** The claims of being "provably convergent" or "provably diligent" are supported only by brief "proof sketches" in footnotes (Footnotes 4 and 5). For a top-tier publication, these claims require formal proofs, at least in an appendix. Phrases like "after some algebra" are insufficient substitutes for derivations.
*   **Parameter Selection:** Key parameters (`T`, `γ`, `ν`) are selected based on "unsystematic investigation." This is not a scientifically sound methodology. The paper lacks any sensitivity analysis, leaving the reader to wonder how brittle the algorithm is to these choices. This is a major weakness for a heuristic-based method.

**2. Presentation and Formatting Issues:**

*   **Reference Formatting:** The bibliography is poorly formatted and incomplete. Multiple entries ([1], [6], [7], [10], [12]) are missing essential information like publisher, year, or venue. The citation order also appears inconsistent. This level of carelessness is concerning.
*   **Footnote Numbering:** The footnote numbering is incorrect (jumps from 3 to 5). This is a trivial but distracting error that suggests a lack of careful proofreading.
*   **Sectioning:** The paper includes a Section 3.1 but no subsequent subsection (e.g., 3.2), which is improper formatting.
*   **Figure Quality:** The figure captions are minimal. The description of Figure 3 as containing "smooth green lines" and "blocky blue lines" is overly colloquial.

**3. Experimental Evaluation Concerns:**

*   **Baseline Choice:** The primary baseline is the PC algorithm run in batch mode on the *entire* dataset, which includes data from both before and after the changepoint. This is a weak baseline, as mixing data from two different distributions is known to create spurious correlations that would fool any batch learner. A more appropriate and challenging baseline would be a sliding-window version of the PC algorithm.
*   **Artificial Experimental Setup:** In the first two simulations, a fixed, artificial "graph relearning schedule" is imposed on LoSST for comparison purposes. This defeats the purpose of evaluating the algorithm's own adaptive relearning trigger, which is a key contribution. While the third simulation uses the probabilistic trigger, its separation from the main comparison weakens the overall evaluation.
*   **Inconsistent Results:** The discussion of Figure 2 notes that LoSST performs *worse* than the batch PC algorithm on edge removal errors for a period after a parameter-only change. While an explanation is offered, this is a significant counter-intuitive result that is not sufficiently explored. It raises questions about the algorithm's reliability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and open problem.
*   The core idea of dynamically weighting data based on model fit to track structural changes is novel and intuitive.
*   The initial results demonstrate the potential of the approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Numerous methodological components are based on ad-hoc heuristics with insufficient theoretical justification (e.g., effective sample size update and its use in the T^2 test).
*   The experimental evaluation relies on a weak baseline and an artificial setup for its main comparative results.
*   Key parameters are chosen unsystematically, with no sensitivity analysis provided.
*   The paper is marred by a lack of rigor in its theoretical claims and a surprising number of formatting and presentational errors.

**Recommendation:** Reject

While the paper addresses an important problem with a novel idea, the execution falls well short of the standards expected for NIPS. The combination of questionable methodological choices, insufficient theoretical justification, a flawed experimental comparison, and poor presentation makes the work feel premature. The sheer number of minor and major issues suggests a lack of the meticulous care required for a sound scientific contribution. The work has potential, but it needs a substantial revision to address the fundamental concerns about rigor and validation before it is ready for publication.
