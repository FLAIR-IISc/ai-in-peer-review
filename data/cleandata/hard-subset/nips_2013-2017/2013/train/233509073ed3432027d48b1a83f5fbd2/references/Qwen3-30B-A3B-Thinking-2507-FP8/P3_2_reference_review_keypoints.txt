- Proposes LoSST, an online algorithm that tracks weighted sufficient statistics to learn time-varying graphical structures and detects changes via Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values.  
- Addresses a genuine gap: existing methods assume i.i.d. batch data or detect changes without structure learning.  
- Algorithm is clearly described with precise update equations and step-by-step presentation.  
- Theoretical discussion on the convergence vs. diligence trade-off is insightful but underdeveloped.  
- Simulations are thorough (multiple scenarios, 500 runs per condition) and show LoSST matches batch methods in stable periods and outperforms them under change.  
- Real-world application to US price index volatility demonstrates practical utility but is limited in scope.  
- Theoretical claims of being “provably convergent/diligent” lack rigorous proofs and rely on practically unverifiable conditions.  
- Assumes multivariate Gaussian data (Mahalanobis distance), with no evaluation or guidance for non-Gaussian or discrete data.  
- The probabilistic relearning mechanism (parameter ν, noisy-OR) is ad-hoc, chosen unsystematically without sensitivity analysis or theoretical justification.  
- Comparison with related work is insufficient; existing methods (e.g., Siracusa & Fisher III, Talih & Hengartner) are not fairly adapted or evaluated in online settings.  
- Use of PC algorithm for structure learning is standard and not novel; performance with other structure learners is not explored.  
- The pooled p-value concept (Liptak’s method) is introduced without adequate motivation or comparison to alternatives.  
- Distinction between effective sample size and actual sample size needs clearer explanation regarding change detection power.  
- Claims that no prior methods handle general graphical model structure learning online are overstated.  
- Significance is constrained by the “slowly changing” structure assumption and lack of guidance for rapid changes or high-dimensional scaling.  
- Computational scalability concerns (PC algorithm cost, thousands of variables) are not addressed.  
- Real-world analysis (6 variables) lacks economic interpretation of detected changes beyond superficial labels.  
- Recommendation: Major revision — promising contribution but requires stronger theory, better comparisons, sensitivity analysis, non-Gaussian discussion, and deeper application analysis.
