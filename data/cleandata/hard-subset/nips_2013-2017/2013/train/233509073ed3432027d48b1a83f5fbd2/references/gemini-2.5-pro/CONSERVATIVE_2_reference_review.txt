### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a heuristic online algorithm for learning the structure of directed graphical models (specifically, recursive SEMs) from data streams where the underlying generative structure may change over time. The core idea is to maintain a weighted estimate of the sufficient statistics (mean and covariance matrix) and an "effective sample size". The weight of each new data point is determined by how well it fits the current estimated model, as measured by its Mahalanobis distance. To be robust to single outliers, the algorithm pools p-values from a sequence of recent data points using Liptak's method. When the pooled p-value indicates a poor fit, the algorithm increases the learning rate (i.e., up-weights new data) and increases the probability of re-running a standard batch structure learning algorithm (the PC algorithm) on the current sufficient statistics. The paper frames the behavior of the algorithm in terms of a trade-off between "convergence" (stabilizing on a fixed structure) and "diligence" (quickly detecting change), and provides sketches of proofs for conditions under which LoSST exhibits these properties. The method is evaluated on synthetic data and demonstrated on a real-world economic time series dataset.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The paper is technically plausible, but its methodological soundness is a significant concern. The proposed LoSST algorithm is a complex assembly of heuristic choices.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    1.  **Heuristic Design:** Key components of the algorithm lack strong theoretical justification. The update rule for the effective sample size (Eq. 3), the specific functional form for updating data point weights (Eq. 4), and the probabilistic trigger for relearning are all ad-hoc. Why is this specific transformation of the pooled p-value into a weight appropriate? The paper offers no principled reason, and the choice of crucial hyperparameters (`T=0.05`, `γ=3`) is based on "unsystematic investigation," which is not a rigorous basis for evaluation.
    2.  **Insufficient Baselines:** The experimental evaluation is critically flawed due to the choice of baseline. The paper compares LoSST to a standard batch PC algorithm applied to the entire dataset (including data from before and after the changepoint). It is a well-known fact that applying a batch algorithm designed for i.i.d. data to a mixed dataset from different distributions will produce poor results. This comparison is a "strawman" and does not provide meaningful insight into LoSST's performance relative to other viable approaches. A far more appropriate and established baseline would be a simple sliding window approach, where the PC algorithm is applied to the N most recent data points. Another reasonable baseline would be to couple a standard online changepoint detection (CPD) algorithm with a structure learner that is reset upon change detection. Without these comparisons, the claims of superior performance are unsubstantiated.
    3.  **Lack of Sensitivity Analysis:** The algorithm's behavior is highly dependent on its hyperparameters (`T`, `γ`, `ν`). A thorough evaluation would require a sensitivity analysis to understand how robust the algorithm is to the choice of these parameters. This is absent from the paper.

*   **Strengths:**
    1.  The paper addresses a well-motivated and challenging problem. Learning graphical structures in non-stationary environments is of significant practical importance.
    2.  The discussion of the trade-off between "convergence" and "diligence" is insightful and provides a useful conceptual framework for analyzing such online learning algorithms.

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow. The algorithm is described in sufficient detail, and the motivation for each component is explained. The organization is logical.

**Originality:**

The problem itself is not new, and some prior work has addressed time-varying graphs. The main novelty lies in the specific combination of existing techniques (online updates of sufficient statistics, Mahalanobis distance, p-value pooling) into a single, heuristic framework for online DAG structure learning. The use of Liptak's method for pooling p-values in this context is unconventional. While the combination is novel, the work does not introduce a fundamentally new technique or theoretical principle.

**Significance:**

The paper addresses a problem of high potential significance. However, the contribution's impact is limited by the methodological weaknesses. As presented, the algorithm is a complex heuristic that has not been rigorously validated against standard, simpler alternatives. It is unclear whether the added complexity of LoSST provides a tangible benefit over a well-tuned sliding window approach. Researchers are unlikely to adopt this method without more compelling evidence of its superiority and a better understanding of its parameterization. The work is more of a preliminary exploration of an idea than a complete, validated contribution ready for adoption.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important, practical problem in online learning and graphical models.
*   The paper is clearly written and presents an intuitive, albeit heuristic, approach.
*   The "diligence vs. convergence" framing is a valuable conceptual contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The proposed method is a collection of ad-hoc heuristics without strong theoretical grounding.
*   The experimental evaluation is critically weak, relying on a strawman baseline and failing to compare against obvious and established methods like a sliding window learner.
*   The work lacks a sensitivity analysis for its numerous and critical hyperparameters, whose settings are justified anecdotally.
*   The real-world application is purely illustrative and provides no quantitative validation.

### Conclusion

While the problem this paper tackles is important and the high-level idea is appealing, the execution and evaluation fall short of the standards required for publication at NeurIPS. The proposed LoSST algorithm is a complex system of heuristics with critical parameters that are not systematically tuned or analyzed. Most importantly, the experimental evaluation fails to compare the method against simple, established baselines for handling non-stationary data, such as a sliding window learner. Without this crucial comparison, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence that its proposed complexity yields any real benefit. The work feels preliminary and requires substantial further development and more rigorous validation before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. I recommend rejection.
