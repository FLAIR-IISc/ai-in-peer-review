Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, an online heuristic algorithm to track time-varying graphical structure for linear-Gaussian SEMs. It maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) with adaptive weights ar that increase when recent data have poor fit to the current model. Fit is assessed via the Mahalanobis distance and a pooled p-value ρ computed by Lipták’s method over past per-point p-values. When ρ is low, ar rises (to emphasize recent data), and the algorithm probabilistically triggers structure relearning using a standard constraint-based learner (PC). The authors discuss parameter regimes intended to trade off convergence and “diligence” (rapid response to changes), present simulations comparing to batch PC, and show a small case study on U.S. CPI component series.

Strengths
- Clear and timely problem: online structure learning for nonstationary processes is important.
- Simple, modular idea: adaptive forgetting via p-value pooling atop standard structure learning is practical and easy to implement.
- If weights are constant, the sufficient-statistic updates reduce to batch estimates (desirable no-regret behavior in stationary settings).
- Simulations suggest LoSST outperforms batch PC after changes; the probabilistic relearning schedule appears to cut computation without hurting accuracy.

Major weaknesses (quality, clarity, originality, significance)
1) Statistical validity of the fit test and p-value pooling is questionable.
- The Mahalanobis distance Dr+1 is computed using parameters estimated from adaptively weighted data; the distribution is not Hotelling’s T2. Using Sr − 1 as “df” is ad hoc. For weighted, non-i.i.d. samples, neither Wishart nor standard T2/F approximations hold; the ESS should be (∑w)^2/∑w^2, not the recursive formula in Eq. (3). The paper needs either a derivation under explicit assumptions or an empirically calibrated alternative (e.g., parametric bootstrap, robust distance with shrinkage).
- The definition pr+1 = T2(x > Dr+1 | p = N, m = Sr − 1) appears to use p = N (sample size) instead of p = V (dimension). This looks like a bug. Moreover, the mapping to an F or T2 cdf needs to be spelled out precisely.

2) Effective sample size update (Eq. 3) is unjustified and conflicts with standard ESS.
- Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1) Sr + 1 does not equal the standard ESS for weighted estimators and lacks a principled link to information content. Because Sr enters hypothesis tests and ρ, this is central. Please justify or replace with a principled ESS and adjust tests accordingly.

3) Weight mapping formula (Eq. 4) is inconsistent with the stated behavior.
- The text says ar+1 increases linearly in 1 − ρ up to γ ar at ρ = 0. But Eq. (4) yields ar+1 = ar · max{1, γT − γρ + ρ/T}, which equals γT ar at ρ = 0 (not γ ar unless T = 1). Either the formula or the description is wrong. This also undermines the later convergence/diligence claims that depend on the scaling.

4) Convergence/diligence claims are insufficiently rigorous and depend on fragile assumptions.
- “Provably convergent/diligent” statements rely on sketchy arguments, undefined regularity conditions, and properties of PC in the i.i.d. limit. With adaptive weights, nonstationarity, hypothesis testing errors, and CI tests based on estimated partial correlations, these claims need careful definitions (e.g., faithfulness, bounded drift, type-I/II control under weighting) and real proofs or to be presented as conjectures with empirical support.

5) Use of PC with weighted statistics is under-specified.
- PC’s CI tests (e.g., Fisher’s z on partial correlations) require a sample size. How exactly are partial correlations and their standard errors computed from the weighted covariance? Is Sr used in the z-transform? Are adjustments made for small/variable Sr or regularization applied for near-singular covariances? Without these details, results are not reproducible and the statistical validity is unclear.

6) Baselines are not representative; ablations missing.
- Comparing LoSST only against batch PC on the full mixed dataset is weak. Stronger baselines include sliding-window PC, exponential forgetting with fixed α, CPD segment-and-learn, online/streaming structure learning variants, and time-varying GGM/BN methods (e.g., Kolar & Xing (2011), Zhou et al. (2010), Hallac et al. (2017), Monti et al. (2014)). Ablate key components: fixed vs adaptive α; pooled p-values vs raw distances; relearning schedules; sensitivity to T, γ, ν.

7) Numerical stability and complexity are not addressed.
- Inverting Cr at each step is O(p^3) and unstable when Sr is small; no shrinkage (e.g., Ledoit-Wolf) or rank-one Sherman-Morrison updates to the precision are discussed. Complexity of probabilistic relearning is not quantified; no bounds.

8) Simulation details are insufficient for reproducibility.
- Missing: generation of SEMs (parameter distributions, noise variances, acyclicity/faithfulness checks), PC settings (alpha, max conditioning set size), orientation error definition for CPDAGs, handling of Markov equivalence, number of trials per condition in Fig. 3, randomness control. Claims such as “stabilized by 250 points” lack confidence intervals.

9) Real data case study is purely anecdotal.
- No ground truth, no competing methods, and no robustness checks (e.g., different α/T/γ, subsampling, pre/post windows, alternative distance metrics). Interpretation of the “Great Moderation” detection is speculative.

Clarity and presentation issues
- Many typos/notation inconsistencies: “TDL” vs TD(0); p = N vs p = V in Hotelling’s T2; “graph2 relearning”; footnotes 1/3/4/5 are interleaved with text; equations lack derivations/references; sub/superscript formatting inconsistent. References are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages), and several key related works on time-varying graphs are absent.

Originality and significance
- The main novelty is using pooled p-values to adapt a forgetting factor for online structure learning. While practical, the idea is close to existing EWMA/forgetting-factor approaches, and the paper lacks rigorous theory and comprehensive empirical comparisons to establish clear state-of-the-art advances.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem; simple, implementable method.
- Sensible intuition: adapt weighting based on fit; probabilistic relearning to manage cost.
- Promising preliminary synthetic results.

Cons
- Core statistical components (ESS, p-values, Eq. 4) are inconsistent or unjustified.
- Theoretical claims are not rigorously supported.
- Baselines and ablations are inadequate; reproducibility and numerical issues not addressed.
- Clarity/formatting and bibliography issues are numerous.

Questions for authors
- Provide a principled derivation for the distribution of Dr+1 under adaptive weighting, or propose a robust alternative test. How do you calibrate pr+1?
- Precisely define how PC’s CI tests use weighted statistics and Sr; include hyperparameters.
- Clarify Eq. (4) vs the intended behavior; fix the inconsistency and re-evaluate convergence/diligence claims.
- Compare against sliding-window PC, fixed forgetting factor, and modern time-varying GGM/BN methods.
- Add regularization/shrinkage and precision updates; analyze complexity.

Recommendation: Reject
The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the paper has multiple foundational statistical issues, insufficient theoretical support, weak baselines, and significant clarity/formatting problems. Addressing these would substantially strengthen the contribution.
