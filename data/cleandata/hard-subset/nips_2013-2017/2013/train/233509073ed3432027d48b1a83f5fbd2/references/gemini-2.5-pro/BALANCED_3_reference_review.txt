### **Summary**

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel online algorithm for learning graphical model structure from non-stationary data streams. The core problem is that standard structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data, which fails when the underlying data-generating process changes over time. LoSST addresses this by processing data sequentially and maintaining weighted estimates of the sufficient statistics (mean and covariance) and an "effective sample size". The key innovation is an adaptive mechanism for setting the data point weights: the algorithm computes the Mahalanobis distance of each new data point from the current distribution and uses Liptak's method to pool the resulting p-values over time. A low pooled p-value indicates a likely structural change, causing the algorithm to increase the weight of new data points, effectively down-weighting the influence of past data. A standard constraint-based structure learning algorithm (the PC algorithm) is then run probabilistically, with the probability of relearning increasing after a suspected change. The paper also provides a valuable theoretical discussion on the trade-off between "convergence" (stabilizing on a fixed structure) and "diligence" (reacting quickly to change), showing how LoSST's parameters can be tuned to favor one over the other. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on synthetic data with known changepoints and on a real-world dataset of US price indices.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a critical and highly practical problem. The assumption of stationarity is a major limitation of most structure learning algorithms, and developing methods that can adapt to changing environments is an important research direction. The LoSST algorithm is a novel and well-motivated contribution. The proposed mechanism—combining online updates of sufficient statistics with a principled statistical method (pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances) to detect change and modulate learning—is both elegant and effective.

2.  **Technical Quality and Conceptual Contribution:** The algorithm is technically sound, with clear derivations for the online updates. A major strength of the paper is the formalization of the "convergence vs. diligence" trade-off. This provides an insightful conceptual framework for analyzing online learning algorithms in non-stationary settings. The authors go beyond merely describing the trade-off by providing proof sketches that connect specific parameter settings of LoSST (`γ` and `T`) to these distinct behaviors, which is a significant contribution.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for each component of the algorithm is clearly articulated, from the need for weighted statistics to the use of pooled p-values to avoid overreacting to single outliers. The structure of the paper logically builds the case for the proposed method.

4.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are convincing. On synthetic data, LoSST clearly outperforms a standard batch algorithm after a changepoint, while incurring no performance cost during stationary periods. The experiments thoughtfully consider changes in both structure and parameters. The application to US economic data is a compelling demonstration of the algorithm's potential utility, showing its ability to detect known macroeconomic shifts like the "Great Moderation."

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

Despite the paper's strengths, there are several areas where it could be improved.

1.  **Choice of Experimental Baseline:** The primary weakness lies in the experimental comparison. The baseline, a batch-mode PC algorithm run on the entire dataset (including data from before and after the changepoint), is a strawman. It is designed to fail on non-stationary data. A much more compelling and informative evaluation would compare LoSST to more robust baselines commonly used for time-series data.
    *   **Suggestion:** I strongly recommend the authors include a comparison against a **sliding-window baseline**, where the PC algorithm is applied to only the last `W` data points. This is a simple but common heuristic for handling non-stationarity and would serve as a much stronger benchmark. Another valuable comparison would be against a two-stage approach using an explicit changepoint detection (CPD) algorithm (like those cited in the paper) to segment the data, followed by running PC on the data since the last detected changepoint.

2.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm's behavior is governed by several key hyperparameters, including `γ` and `T` (controlling the convergence/diligence trade-off) and `ν` (controlling relearning frequency). The paper mentions that these were set via "unsystematic investigation." This makes the results difficult to reproduce and provides little guidance for practitioners wishing to apply LoSST to their own problems.
    *   **Suggestion:** A sensitivity analysis for these parameters would significantly strengthen the paper. For example, showing how performance metrics change as `γ` and `T` are varied would provide valuable insight into the algorithm's robustness and help users understand how to tune it. Furthermore, it would be powerful to empirically demonstrate the diligence-convergence trade-off by running experiments with parameter settings from both the "diligent" and "convergent" regimes discussed in Section 3.1.

3.  **Scope and Generalizability:** The method is presented in the context of linear SEMs, where the covariance matrix contains the necessary sufficient statistics for the PC algorithm. The authors mention that the framework could be extended to other models (e.g., discrete Bayesian networks), but this is a non-trivial extension, as the sufficient statistics (e.g., contingency tables) are fundamentally different and more complex to track in a weighted, online fashion.
    *   **Suggestion:** While exploring these extensions is rightly left for future work, the authors could be more explicit about the current limitations. A brief discussion of the challenges involved in extending the method (e.g., the high dimensionality of sufficient statistics for discrete models) would help to appropriately frame the scope of the current contribution.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   Addresses a significant and practical problem in machine learning.
    *   Proposes a novel, elegant, and well-motivated algorithm.
    *   Provides a strong conceptual contribution with the formalization of the diligence vs. convergence trade-off.
    *   The paper is clear, well-written, and the empirical results, while based on a weak baseline, are compelling and demonstrate the method's promise.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The experimental evaluation lacks a strong, competitive baseline, making it difficult to fully assess the performance gains of the proposed method.
    *   The lack of hyperparameter analysis limits the practical applicability and reproducibility of the work.

### **Decision**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the literature on learning from non-stationary data. The proposed LoSST algorithm is novel and the theoretical framing of the diligence-convergence trade-off is insightful. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened with more competitive baselines and a hyperparameter analysis, these weaknesses do not undermine the core contribution of the work. The paper is well-executed and presents ideas that are likely to be influential. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
