Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic online procedure for learning time-varying DAG/SEM structures under a “locally stationary” assumption. It incrementally tracks weighted means/covariances and an “effective” sample size. Weights adapt via a pooled p-value (Liptak’s method) of per-point Mahalanobis-distance p-values (Hotelling’s T^2) computed against the current weighted covariance. When pooled evidence suggests a change, LoSST increases the forgetting factor and triggers more frequent relearning of the graph with PC. Theoretical discussion contrasts “convergence” vs “diligence” regimes as a function of hyperparameters T and γ. Simulations with synthetic SEMs show LoSST matches batch PC under stationarity and outperforms PC after abrupt changes; a small CPI case study suggests detection of macroeconomic regime shifts.

Relation to prior work
The paper sits between online change-point detection (e.g., BOCPD, CUSUM/Page-Hinkley, kernel CD), adaptive forgetting/EWMA/RLS literature, and time-varying graphical structure estimation (dynamic Bayesian networks; time-varying GGMs/graphical lasso; network segmentation approaches). The paper correctly notes that most prior structure-learning assumes stationarity and that some time-varying approaches are batch. However, related work is incomplete (e.g., sliding-window CI testing; adaptive forgetting factor RLS; dynamic BN structure learning; time-varying GGM with fused penalties), and there is limited benchmarking against these alternatives.

Strengths
- Important problem: online tracking of changing causal/graphical structure is relevant and understudied in DAGs.
- Simple, single-pass sufficient-statistics updates; easy to implement for linear Gaussian SEMs.
- Empirical evidence that under a single change LoSST adapts while batch PC fails on mixed distributions.
- Practical intuition about pooled evidence vs single outliers; discussion of computational trade-offs via probabilistic relearning.
- Attempts to articulate regimes (convergence vs diligence) and parameter effects.

Weaknesses and concerns
1) Statistical validity of the change metric and tests:
- The use of Hotelling’s T^2 assumes i.i.d. Gaussian data and fixed covariance; here both the covariance and weights are adaptively updated using past data that include earlier points. The resulting p-values (and their dependence across time) are not calibrated, and Liptak’s pooling presumes independence (or at least known dependence) that is violated. No analysis of false-alarm rate, detection delay, or robustness is provided.
- The “effective sample size” Sr update (Eq. 3) is ad hoc. For weighted samples, a standard choice is Kish’s effective sample size N_eff = (Σw)^2 / Σw^2. The proposed Sr affects distributional assumptions for T^2 and the CI tests in PC; miscalibration here undermines both change detection and structure tests.

2) CI testing and PC with weighted statistics:
- The paper does not specify how conditional independence tests are performed from the weighted covariance, including how degrees of freedom or p-values are adjusted using Sr. Without a principled derivation, Type I/II error control is unclear.
- Orientation in linear-Gaussian DAGs is generally only identifiable up to a CPDAG. The evaluation metric for “orientation errors” is not precisely defined (CPDAG vs DAG), risking unfair scoring.

3) Heuristic choices without ablation or theory:
- The weight update (Eq. 4) and the noisy-OR relearning trigger are heuristic with hand-picked T, γ, ν. The theoretical claims are only proof sketches for extreme regimes and do not provide guarantees on error rates, regret, or detection delay under realistic conditions. No sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter tuning protocol is provided.

4) Limited baselines and evaluation design:
- Main comparisons are only to batch PC, which is expected to fail on mixed distributions. Missing strong baselines: sliding-window PC with tuned window sizes; EWMA/forgetting-factor estimators feeding PC; CPD (frequentist or BOCPD) followed by restart; kernel CD (e.g., Desobry et al.) plus restart; dynamic BN structure learning; time-varying GGM/graphical lasso as an undirected benchmark.
- The second simulation fixes relearning times exogenously, partially sidestepping the online trigger. The third uses one synthetic dataset repeated 500 times—this does not probe variability across problem instances.
- No runtime/complexity analysis. Online inversion of covariances (for Mahalanobis distance) can be O(p^3) unless Sherman–Morrison or similar updates are used; numerical stability/regularization is not discussed.
- Real-data example is qualitative only, with no baseline comparison and no quantitative validation.

5) Scope and assumptions:
- Restricted to linear-Gaussian SEMs; no discussion of how to handle non-Gaussianity or discrete variables beyond brief remarks. The method may be brittle in the presence of outliers, heavy tails, or misspecification.

Questions for the authors
- How do you calibrate CI tests in PC from weighted covariances? What df and null distributions are used?
- Why not use Kish’s N_eff for Sr? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- What is the empirical false alarm rate of your ρ-based detector under stationarity? Provide calibration plots.
- Computational details: do you update (C_r)^{-1} via rank-one updates? Any shrinkage to ensure SPD?
- How did you choose T, γ, ν? Show sensitivity and a tuning protocol not using ground truth.

Suggestions to improve
- Replace/justify Sr with a standard effective sample size; derive valid CI tests for weighted stats or use permutation/bootstrap tests compatible with weights.
- Provide detection-theoretic evaluation (ROC/PR for change detection), and quantify detection delay vs false alarm across hyperparameters.
- Add baselines: sliding windows; EWMA with fixed/adaptive forgetting; CPD+restart; kernel CD; dynamic BN; time-varying GGM. Include ablations on T, γ, ν and effect of relearning schedule.
- Clarify evaluation: measure SHD between CPDAGs; report statistical significance and runtime.
- Expand related work to include adaptive filtering (VFF-RLS), CUSUM/Page-Hinkley, dynamic network inference, and time-varying graphical lasso, and compare.
- Discuss robustness to non-Gaussianity and propose regularization.

Pros
- Addresses an important, practical problem.
- Simple and potentially useful incremental machinery.
- Promising empirical behavior after change points.
- Transparent discussion of limitations and parameter regimes.

Cons
- Heuristic core without statistical calibration or guarantees.
- Ad hoc effective sample size; unclear validity of tests.
- Weak baselines and limited evaluation design.
- Missing algorithmic/complexity details and sensitivity analysis.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The problem is important, but the current version lacks methodological rigor, calibration, and strong baselines to support its claims. With the above additions and clearer statistical underpinnings, this could become a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
