Summary
This paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic online algorithm for tracking time-varying DAG structure under a locally stationary assumption. For linear-Gaussian SEMs, the method maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size), where the per-sample weight a_r adapts to detect change via a pooled p-value ρ_r constructed from per-sample Mahalanobis distance p-values using Liptak’s method. Graph relearning is triggered probabilistically based on 1−ρ_r. The authors discuss a convergence–diligence trade-off and provide simulation studies (abrupt structural and parameter changes) plus a small real-data case (US price index differences).

Strengths
- Important and timely problem: online structure learning under nonstationarity is meaningful for many applications.
- Lightweight state: tracking O(V^2) statistics is appealing for streaming.
- Clear high-level idea: adaptively reweight data based on recent misfit and periodically relearn with a standard constraint-based learner (PC).
- Simulation evidence suggests the approach can recover changes faster than naive batch PC on mixed data; probabilistic relearning shows limited accuracy loss relative to relearning every step.
- Honest discussion that convergence and diligence are incompatible; attempts to map parameter regimes to behaviors.

Weaknesses and methodological concerns
1) Statistical validity of the change signal:
- The per-sample p-values are derived from Hotelling’s T^2 using (X−μ_r)^T C_r^{-1} (X−μ_r), with degrees of freedom set via the “effective sample size” S_r. However, C_r is itself an adaptively weighted estimator with weights that depend on prior data via ρ, making the null distribution of D_r neither standard T^2 nor independent across r. The assumed T^2 p-values and the independence required by Liptak pooling are unjustified. This undermines both the interpretability and calibration of ρ_r and thus of a_r and relearning probabilities.
- S_r update (Eq. 3) is hand-crafted (“adjust the natural sample size update equation to satisfy constraints”) rather than derived from a principled effective sample size for weighted estimators. No justification that this S_r makes the T^2 approximation reasonable.

2) Weight update formula inconsistency:
- The text states: if ρ_r+1 > T, keep a_r+1 = a_r; if ρ_r+1 < T, increase a_r+1 up to γ a_r at ρ=0. The provided formula (Eq. 4) a_{r+1} = a_r max{1, γT − γρ + ρ/T} does not satisfy these properties (e.g., at ρ=0 it yields γT, not γ; for ρ>T the factor can exceed 1, contrary to “keep constant”). This appears to be a mathematical error that invalidates the convergence/diligence claims that rely on Eq. (4).

3) Use of PC with weighted stats:
- The paper states they use PC, but gives no detail on how conditional independence tests are computed under weighted covariance estimates and adaptive S_r (e.g., Fisher’s z for partial correlations: which df? which α? how to calibrate under non-i.i.d., adaptively weighted updates?). Without this, reproducibility and validity of CI testing are unclear.

4) Theory is incomplete and contains issues:
- Convergence/diligence “proof sketches” depend on the flawed weight mapping; probability-1 convergence is asserted via a threshold schedule argument but without precise assumptions on the PC procedure under adaptive weighting.
- Claims about score-based methods “not decomposing in a manner suitable for online learning” are inaccurate for standard decomposable scores (e.g., BIC/BGe/BDeu) under stationary settings; the real obstacle is the nonstationarity and adaptivity, which should be stated clearly.

5) Empirical evaluation gaps:
- Baseline comparisons are weak: comparing LoSST to batch PC on the full mixed dataset is expected to favor LoSST. More appropriate baselines include sliding-window PC, exponential forgetting in covariance + PC, change-point segmentation + per-segment PC, and time-varying GGM methods (e.g., fused/segmented Graphical Lasso; kernel-smoothed precision estimates).
- Simulation details are insufficient for replication (SEM generation, noise levels, faithfulness violations, CI test settings, α level, handling of CPDAG vs DAG edge orientation scoring). How are “orientation errors” defined relative to equivalence classes?
- Hyperparameter selection is ad hoc (“unsystematic investigation” for T=0.05, γ=3). No sensitivity analysis or calibration of false alarm vs. detection delay.
- No runtime or memory analysis versus baselines; no ablations (e.g., effect of probabilistic relearning ν).
- Real data case is anecdotal: no ground truth; no statistical validation of detected shifts; potential confounds (seasonality, heteroskedasticity, non-Gaussianity). The claim that pooled p-values prevent reacting to a single outlier is plausible but unverified.

6) Related work is incomplete/mismatched:
- Missing a substantial literature on time-varying graphical models and dynamic structure learning (e.g., Kolar et al., Zhou et al., Hallac et al. (TVGL), dynamic Bayesian networks, online precision matrix tracking). Citations [6,9] are misaligned with claims (e.g., [9] is MEMMs, not undirected GMs).
- Online change detection baselines beyond Adams & MacKay (e.g., kernel/CD, ADWIN) could be discussed.

7) Presentation and formatting issues:
- Numerous typos and inconsistencies: “TDL” vs “TD,” “graph2,” stray footnote markers (1,3,4,5) embedded in text, “occcurs,” variable notation fluctuates (C_r^{X_i,X_j} vs C_r^{V_i,V_j}), parameters in T^2 given as p=N (should be dimensionality V), and incomplete references (missing years/venues).
- Figures are essential to claims but not described in sufficient detail; axes, metrics, and CI are unspecified.

Questions for the authors
- Provide a correct, principled derivation (or bootstrap calibration) of per-sample misfit p-values under adaptive weighting, or explicitly abandon p-values and use a calibrated score (e.g., EWMA of log-likelihood under a fixed window, or self-normalized statistics).
- Correct Eq. (4) (and any proofs depending on it), and provide a clear piecewise definition guaranteeing a_r+1=a_r when ρ>T and a_r+1≤γ a_r otherwise.
- Specify the exact CI tests used in PC under weighted stats, including degrees-of-freedom, α, and handling of CPDAG equivalence.
- Add comparisons to sliding-window PC and time-varying GGM/DBN baselines, with detection delay/false alarm tradeoffs, and runtime.
- Expand related work to include time-varying graphical models and dynamic structure learning.

Assessment against criteria
- Quality: Currently weak due to invalid statistical assumptions for p-values, a flawed weight update, incomplete CI testing details, and limited baselines.
- Clarity: The high-level idea is clear, but many technical details are missing/incorrect; references and formatting need significant cleanup.
- Originality: The combination (adaptive weighting via pooled misfit + online sufficient stats + periodic relearning) is interesting, but related work gaps make novelty hard to judge definitively.
- Significance: Potentially useful if made statistically sound and benchmarked properly, but current evidence is insufficient.

Pros
- Addresses an important streaming problem.
- Simple, potentially scalable mechanism.
- Initial simulations are promising.

Cons
- Key mathematical pieces are incorrect or unjustified.
- Empirical evaluation lacks appropriate baselines and rigor.
- Clarity and references need substantial work.

Recommendation
Reject in its current form. The idea is promising, but the methodological flaws (especially the misuse of T^2 p-values under adaptive weighting and the erroneous weight update), missing details for CI testing, and weak evaluation must be addressed before this can be a reliable contribution.
