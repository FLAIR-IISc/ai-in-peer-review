This paper tackles a genuinely important and largely unexplored problem: learning graphical model structure in a non-stationary world, and doing so *online*. The motivation is clearly stated – real-world systems change, and batch learning is often impractical – and the proposed LoSST algorithm is a compelling heuristic approach. The core idea of weighting data points based on their “fit” to the current model, using a Mahalanobis distance and a pooled p-value, is clever and seems well-motivated. The algorithm's ability to revert to standard batch learning performance when the underlying structure is stable is a particularly attractive feature. The simulation results are encouraging, demonstrating the algorithm’s ability to track structural changes and outperform a standard batch method in dynamic scenarios. The application to US price index volatility, while brief, provides a tantalizing glimpse into real-world applicability.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** This is the paper’s strongest asset. Online learning of graphical model *structure* is a significant departure from the standard i.i.d. assumption, and the proposed approach is a substantial step toward addressing this challenge.
*   **Practicality:** The algorithm is designed for online processing, making it suitable for real-time applications.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a reasonable rationale for the key components of the algorithm (weighting scheme, sample size update). The discussion of convergence vs. diligence is insightful.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The simulations demonstrate the algorithm’s ability to track changes in both structure and parameters.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Heuristic Nature:** The algorithm relies on several heuristics (e.g., the choice of parameters γ and T, the update rule for *a<sub>r+1</sub>*). While justified, these choices lack a strong theoretical foundation and may be sensitive to the specific application.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of LoSST likely depends heavily on the correct setting of several parameters (ν, T, γ). The paper does not provide sufficient guidance on how to choose these parameters in practice.
*   **Limited Real-World Evaluation:** The application to US price index volatility is a good start, but more extensive real-world evaluations are needed to assess the algorithm’s robustness and generalizability.
*   **Computational Cost:** While online, the repeated structure learning (even with probabilistic triggering) could be computationally demanding for high-dimensional graphical models. This point is not sufficiently addressed.
*   **PC Algorithm Reliance:** The reliance on the PC algorithm means the algorithm's performance is capped by the PC algorithm's limitations. Exploring alternatives or modifications to the structure learning step might yield improvements.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper introduces a genuinely novel and important idea.
*   The proposed algorithm is a plausible approach to a challenging problem.
*   The simulations provide encouraging evidence of the algorithm’s effectiveness.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.
*   The potential impact of this work is significant, opening up new avenues for research in dynamic graphical modeling.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The algorithm is largely heuristic and lacks a strong theoretical foundation.
*   The parameter sensitivity could limit its practical applicability.
*   The real-world evaluation is limited.
*   The computational cost of repeated structure learning could be a bottleneck.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly lean towards accepting this paper. While the algorithm is heuristic and has some limitations, the novelty and significance of the problem addressed are substantial. The authors have presented a compelling approach that shows promise in simulation and a preliminary real-world application. The paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and opens up exciting new research directions. I believe the potential benefits of this work outweigh its current shortcomings. Minor revisions addressing the parameter sensitivity issue and computational cost could further strengthen the paper. Specifically, a discussion of techniques to reduce the frequency of full structure learning, or adaptive parameter tuning strategies, would be beneficial.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
