

# Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

This paper proposes the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm for online graphical model structure learning when the underlying structure may change over time. The authors address an important gap in the literature, as most graphical model structure learning algorithms assume stationary data, while real-world applications often involve time-varying structures. LoSST tracks sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) online, using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values to detect structural changes and adaptively relearn the graphical model.

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear contribution to the problem of time-varying graphical model structure learning, which is an important and relevant problem in many real-world applications. The algorithm is well-motivated, and the experimental results demonstrate that LoSST performs comparably to batch methods when the structure is stable and significantly better when the structure changes. The application to US price index volatility provides a compelling real-world example of the algorithm's utility.

## Weaknesses

### Technical Errors
1. **Incorrect statistical distribution**: The paper states "The Mahalanobis distance of a V-dimensional datapoint from a covariance matrix estimated from a sample of size N is distributed as Hotelling's T^2 with parameters p = V and m = N − 1." This is incorrect. Hotelling's T^2 is distributed as F(p, N-p) where N is the sample size, not m = N-1. This is a fundamental statistical error that undermines the methodological foundation of the paper.

2. **Equation formatting**: Equation (1) is written as "µr+1i = br br+1 µri + ar+1 br+1 Xr+1i" without proper parentheses. It should be "(br/br+1)µri + (ar+1/br+1)Xr+1i" to be mathematically correct.

3. **Notational inconsistency**: The paper uses "r" both as a time index and as a variable in the Mahalanobis distance calculation (e.g., "Dr+1"), which creates ambiguity.

### Minor but Significant Issues
1. **Formatting errors**: "Gr = Gs for r 6= s" should be "Gr = Gs for r ≠ s" (the "6" should be "≠"). Such small errors suggest carelessness in the final preparation of the paper.

2. **Reference formatting**: Reference [1] ("Bayesian online changepoint detection") is incomplete: "R.P. Adams, D.J.C. MacKay, Technical report,, , ," lacks proper publication details.

3. **Ambiguous claims**: The paper claims LoSST's performance is "indistinguishable from a standard batch-mode structure learning algorithm" during stable periods, but doesn't specify what metrics or statistical tests were used to establish this claim.

4. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper states "LoSST is neither convergent nor diligent" when γ > 1 and 0 < T < 1, but then explains it "strikes a balance between the desiderata." This is contradictory and confusing.

5. **Real-world application**: The Great Moderation is generally considered to have begun around 1982, not 1980. This slight mischaracterization could undermine the credibility of the real-world application.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a potentially useful algorithm, the technical errors and formatting inconsistencies are significant enough to raise concerns about the paper's quality. The incorrect statistical distribution statement is particularly problematic as it undermines the theoretical foundation of the method. The paper also lacks sufficient detail to reproduce the key algorithmic steps, which is a critical requirement for a NIPS submission.

The paper's significance is somewhat limited by the fact that it's a heuristic approach without strong theoretical guarantees, and the authors acknowledge that it may perform poorly for rapidly changing structures. While the simulations provide some evidence of the algorithm's utility, the technical errors and ambiguities in the presentation weaken the paper's contribution.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. The paper presents an interesting idea for a time-varying graphical model structure learning algorithm, but the technical errors and lack of precision in the presentation are too significant to overlook. The statistical error regarding Hotelling's T^2 is particularly concerning and suggests that the authors may have overlooked fundamental aspects of their method's theoretical foundation. The minor formatting issues, while seemingly small, further indicate a lack of attention to detail that is expected in a top-tier conference submission.

The paper could be significantly improved with careful revision to correct the statistical errors, improve notation consistency, and provide more precise claims about the algorithm's performance. However, as currently written, it does not meet the standards expected for NeurIPS.
