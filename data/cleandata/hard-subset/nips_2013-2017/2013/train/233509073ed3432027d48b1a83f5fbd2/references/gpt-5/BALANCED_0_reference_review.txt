Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses online structure learning for graphical models when the underlying generative process may change over time. Focusing on linear-Gaussian recursive SEMs (DAGs), the proposed LoSST algorithm maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) in a single pass, with adaptive weights ar intended to emphasize recent data after change. The weights are driven by a pooled p-value ρr that aggregates per-sample Mahalanobis distance p-values via Liptak’s method. Structure is relearned with a probability that increases as ρr decreases. The paper provides conditions under which LoSST is convergent (when weights stabilize) or “diligent” (rapid change detection with small effective sample sizes), and positions these properties as fundamentally in tension. Simulations show LoSST matches batch PC in stable segments, outperforms after structural changes, and remains competitive when only parameters change. A real-data case on CPI differences suggests LoSST detects volatility regime shifts (e.g., Great Moderation).

The work is related to: standard DAG structure learning (score-based and constraint-based; e.g., Chickering; Spirtes et al.), online change-point detection (Adams & MacKay; Desobry et al.), and time-varying structure learning (Talih & Hengartner for undirected; Siracusa & Fisher for Bayesian time-indexed edges). Unlike prior time-varying approaches, LoSST aims to operate online and in real time without maintaining a posterior over a super-exponential model space.

Strengths
- Problem importance: Online, nonstationary structure learning is timely and relevant to many NeurIPS areas (graphical models, streaming, change detection).
- Elegant core mechanism: Deriving online updates for weighted means/covariances and coupling them to adaptive weights is simple and memory-efficient. The probabilistic relearning policy is also intuitive.
- Empirical evidence: On synthetic data with known changes, LoSST avoids spurious associations that batch PC suffers under data mixtures, and adapts within a few hundred samples.
- Discussion of convergence vs diligence: Framing the trade-off and giving parameter regimes with proof sketches is conceptually useful.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Statistical validity of the p-value machinery is unclear. The per-step Mahalanobis distance is treated as Hotelling’s T^2 with m = Sr − 1, where Sr is an “effective” sample size derived from weights. For weighted estimators, the null distribution is not generally T^2 with m set to an effective N; additional corrections are needed and dependence on the evolving covariance estimate complicates calibration. This calls into question the interpretability of pr and thus the pooled ρr used to set ar and relearning probabilities.
- Dependence among p-values: Liptak’s combination assumes independence or requires careful dependence modeling. Here, p-values are strongly dependent (shared evolving mean/covariance and overlapping data). No correction or empirical calibration is provided.
- Heuristic choices with limited theory: The mapping from ρr to ar (Eq. 4) and the noisy-OR relearning policy are ad hoc, and parameter selection (T, γ, ν) is informal. The convergence/diligence arguments are proof sketches that rely on properties (e.g., “probability 1 convergence” of the downstream structure learner) without finite-sample guarantees.
- Baselines: Comparisons are largely to batch PC on the full mixture, which is expected to underperform after changes. Missing stronger baselines include: (a) sliding-window PC or exponentially weighted PC with fixed forgetting; (b) change-point detection (e.g., BOCPD) triggering resets of PC; (c) dynamic Bayesian networks; (d) time-varying GGM methods (e.g., fused/TV graphical lasso) as an undirected baseline; (e) kernel change detection methods paired with relearning. This limits claims of superiority.
- Computational analysis: No complexity/runtimes are reported. Inverting Cr for Mahalanobis distance can be O(d^3) per step without rank-one updates; repeated PC runs can be costly. Practical scalability is unclear.
- Real-data validation is qualitative: The CPI case shows statistics and a narrative about volatility regimes, but does not present inferred graphs over time, nor comparisons to standard econometric regime-switching or CPD methods, nor robustness checks.

Clarity
- Generally well written and structured, but there are typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., “graph2,” “schedule6,” in-text footnote markers; minor indexing issues in covariance updates; unclear phrase “Xr+1 is weighted ar+1/ar more than Xr”). References are incomplete in places.
- Important assumptions are not fully explicit: e.g., Gaussianity for Hotelling’s T^2, positive definiteness maintenance and numerical stability, and how inverses are computed/regularized online.

Originality
- The particular combination—online weighted sufficient statistics with adaptive weighting driven by pooled p-values, coupled to periodic constraint-based relearning—is novel to my knowledge. The convergence/diligence framing is a nice conceptual contribution. However, related lines (DBNs; sliding-window/forgetting factor structure learning; sequential change detection with resets; time-varying GGMs) should be more thoroughly discussed and compared.

Significance
- Potentially useful in settings with streaming, slowly varying linear-Gaussian structures. If statistically better grounded and empirically validated against stronger baselines, the approach could be impactful. As it stands, the reliance on heuristic calibration and the lack of robust baselines and runtime analysis limit confidence in broad applicability.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem: online DAG structure learning under nonstationarity.
- Simple, memory-efficient algorithm likely easy to implement and extend.
- Clear empirical advantage over naive batch learning under structural change.
- Insightful discussion of convergence vs diligence trade-offs.

Cons for acceptance
- Key statistical components (p-value calibration under weighting; dependence in p-value pooling) are not justified; risk of miscalibration.
- Baseline comparisons are insufficient; missing widely used alternatives.
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond sketches; parameter selection is ad hoc.
- Real-data study is qualitative; no quantitative validation or ablations.
- Missing runtime/scalability analysis; some clarity/typo issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace or calibrate the change metric: consider GLR/CUSUM/Page-Hinkley or martingale tests; if retaining Mahalanobis, empirically estimate the null distribution under the current weighting via online bootstrap or prequential calibration; or use dependence-robust combination tests (e.g., Cauchy combination, harmonic mean p-value).
- Compare against stronger baselines: sliding-window PC/EW forgetting, BOCPD+reset, DBNs, TV-GLasso (as undirected reference), and kernel CPD triggering PC. Include ablations (simple EWMA of Dr vs pooled p-values).
- Provide runtime and scalability results (per-step cost, number of relearns) and numerical stability details (e.g., rank-one updates/Sherman–Morrison for inverses, regularization).
- Offer guidance for T, γ, ν: relate to desired false alarm rate or detection delay; possibly adapt T over time with error-rate control.
- Strengthen theory: formalize conditions under which weighted Mahalanobis-based detectors are valid; clarify assumptions for convergence/diligence results.
- Expand the real-data study: show learned graphs at key times; compare to standard CPD/volatility regime methods; perform sensitivity analyses.
- Fix typos and complete references; clarify notation and assumptions.

Recommendation
Reject (weak). The paper tackles an important problem with a promising, simple approach and encouraging preliminary results. However, the statistical underpinnings of the change detector are insufficiently justified, the empirical evaluation lacks strong baselines and scalability analysis, and the real-data validation is qualitative. Addressing the above points would substantially strengthen the contribution.
