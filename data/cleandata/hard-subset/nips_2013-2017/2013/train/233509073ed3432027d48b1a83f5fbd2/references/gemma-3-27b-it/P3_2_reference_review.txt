## Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning graphical model structure in a non-stationary environment, specifically when data arrives sequentially (online learning). Unlike existing structure learning algorithms that assume a fixed underlying graphical model, the authors propose a heuristic algorithm, LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), which dynamically adapts to changes in the graphical structure while maintaining performance comparable to batch methods during stable periods. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics online with dynamically weighted data points, where weights are adjusted based on the “fit” of the current model to incoming data, effectively increasing the influence of recent data after a structural change. The algorithm utilizes the PC algorithm for structure learning and explores the trade-off between convergence and diligence. The authors provide simulation results demonstrating the efficacy of LoSST in tracking both changes in structure and parameters, along with a real-world application to US price index volatility.

**Related Work:**

The paper provides a solid overview of related work, correctly positioning LoSST within the landscape of graphical model structure learning and online learning.  The authors appropriately differentiate their work from Bayesian and constraint-based structure learning methods (which require i.i.d. data), changepoint detection algorithms (which store substantial data), and previous attempts at time-varying graphical models (which operate in batch mode). The discussion of Temporal Difference Learning (TDL) is insightful, highlighting its limitations in handling both abrupt and slow changes.  The citations are comprehensive and relevant.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:**  The problem of online structure learning in graphical models is relatively unexplored. LoSST addresses a genuine gap in the literature, particularly the ability to function in a truly online fashion without relying on batch processing or extensive data storage.
* **Practicality:** The algorithm is designed with practicality in mind. It leverages well-established techniques (PC algorithm, weighted sufficient statistics) and provides a clear mechanism for balancing the trade-off between responsiveness to change and stability.
* **Theoretical Considerations:** The discussion of convergence versus diligence is a strong point. Explicitly acknowledging this inherent trade-off and demonstrating how LoSST’s parameters influence it is a valuable contribution.  The provided proofs sketches regarding these properties are appreciated, though more rigorous proofs would strengthen the work.
* **Empirical Validation:** The simulation results are convincing, demonstrating that LoSST can effectively track changes in both graph structure and parameters, and performs competitively with batch methods during stable periods.  The application to US price index data provides a compelling real-world example.
* **Clear Writing:**  The paper is well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details. The algorithm is presented in a clear and concise manner.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Heuristic Nature:** The algorithm relies on several heuristic choices (e.g., the Mahalanobis distance as a change indicator, the specific weighting scheme for data points, the noisy-OR gate for relearning). While these choices seem reasonable, a more thorough justification or exploration of alternative heuristics would be beneficial.  The dependence on parameters like *T*, *γ*, and *ν* requires careful tuning and the sensitivity analysis could be expanded.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors state that the algorithm can function on large datasets, the computational complexity of the PC algorithm (especially for high-dimensional data) remains a concern. The computational cost of repeated structure learning is not fully addressed. The paper mentions balancing accuracy and computational cost but lacks a detailed analysis of this trade-off.
* **Limited Scope of Simulation:** The simulations are well-designed, but they primarily focus on relatively simple graph structures and parameter changes.  Expanding the simulations to include more complex scenarios (e.g., graphs with higher degree, more subtle parameter shifts, more frequent changes) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The real-world application reveals a shift potentially linked to the 2000 recession. However, the role of parameter tuning (*T*, *γ*, *ν*) in detecting this shift isn’t discussed. It's unclear if the detection is a genuine result of the algorithm or a consequence of specific parameter choices.
* **Alternative Structure Learning Algorithms:** The paper utilizes the PC algorithm for structure learning. While this is a reasonable choice, a brief discussion of the potential impact of using other algorithms (e.g., those better suited for high-dimensional data or specific graph types) would be helpful.




**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a significant and under-explored problem in graphical modeling.
* Provides a practical and reasonably well-motivated algorithm.
* Presents convincing simulation results and a promising real-world application.
* Demonstrates a thoughtful consideration of the trade-offs involved in online structure learning.
* Clear and well-written.

**Con:**

* The algorithm relies on several heuristics that could be further justified.
* The computational cost of repeated structure learning remains a concern.
* The simulations, while good, could be more comprehensive.
* The sensitivity of the algorithm to parameter tuning is not fully explored.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of graphical modeling. While it has some weaknesses, particularly regarding the heuristic nature of the algorithm and computational cost, the novelty of the problem addressed, the practicality of the proposed solution, and the convincing empirical results outweigh these concerns. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it accessible to a broad audience. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more thorough justification for the heuristic choices made in the algorithm.
2.  Discuss the computational complexity of LoSST in more detail, and explore potential strategies for reducing it.
3.  Expand the simulation results to include more complex scenarios.
4.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the parameter tuning process and its impact on the results.
5.  Consider discussing the potential of other structure learning algorithms within the LoSST framework.
