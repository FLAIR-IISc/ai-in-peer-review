

# Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

This paper proposes the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm for online learning of graphical model structure when the underlying structure may change over time. The authors identify a gap in the literature: existing structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data from a single generating structure, while real-world applications often require tracking changing structures in an online fashion. LoSST addresses this by tracking sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) online and using Mahalanobis distances with pooled p-values to detect structural changes, triggering relearning of the graphical model when needed.

The paper's strengths include a clear identification of the problem, good organization, and thorough simulation experiments comparing LoSST to batch-mode PC algorithm. The real-world application to US price index volatility demonstrates potential utility. The theoretical discussion of convergence vs. diligence trade-offs is interesting and well-motivated.

However, I have significant concerns about the paper's methodological soundness and evidence for its claims, which are critical for a NIPS submission. The core issue is that LoSST is presented as a heuristic algorithm without rigorous theoretical grounding or comprehensive validation against established baselines.

First, the paper's claims about the algorithm's performance lack sufficient evidence. The simulations (Section 4) only test on synthetic data with specific parameter settings (2000 datapoints, 2-20 variables, max degree 3-12), which may not reflect real-world complexity. The paper does not compare LoSST to other online methods for graphical structure learning (beyond the PC algorithm), nor does it evaluate performance under challenging conditions like rapid structural changes or noisy data. The claim that "LoSST is capable of exhibiting different long-run properties" is not substantiated with empirical evidence for the "neither convergent nor diligent" regime that the authors favor.

Second, the theoretical foundation is weak. The paper claims LoSST is "provably convergent" or "provably diligent" under certain parameter settings, but the proofs are sketchy and rely on mathematical conditions that are not practically verifiable. The convergence proof (Section 3.1) depends on the condition that "∑∞ i=1 (1− f(i)) converges," but the paper doesn't explain how to choose f(i) in practice. The "diligence" claim (T > 1 and γ > 1) is similarly unsupported by empirical evidence.

Third, the algorithm's key innovation—using pooled p-values to detect structural changes—is not convincingly justified. The paper states that "the likelihood of the (weighted) sequence of Dr's is analytically intractable," but doesn't provide evidence that alternative approaches (e.g., Bayesian online changepoint detection) would be less suitable. The choice of Mahalanobis distance and Liptak's method for pooling p-values appears arbitrary without comparison to other statistical methods.

Fourth, the paper fails to address important limitations. Section 6 briefly mentions that LoSST "will perform poorly if the generating structure changes very rapidly," but doesn't quantify this or provide guidance on when the algorithm is applicable. The "probabilistic relearning" mechanism (Section 3) is particularly underdeveloped, with the probability of relearning being determined by a "noisy-OR gate" with parameter ν that is "unsystematically investigated."

The real-world application to US price index data (Section 5) is promising but superficial. The authors observe that LoSST "detects" the Great Moderation, but provide no statistical validation of this claim or comparison to alternative methods. The paper doesn't address whether the observed changes are truly structural or simply due to the volatility of the price indices.

In terms of originality, the paper identifies a genuine gap in the literature, but the proposed solution is largely a combination of existing techniques (Mahalanobis distance, online sufficient statistics tracking, PC algorithm) without sufficient novelty. The paper acknowledges related work well, but doesn't clearly differentiate its approach from previous attempts to learn time-varying structures (e.g., Talih and Hengartner 2005, Siracusa and Fisher III 2009).

The paper's most significant weakness is its lack of rigorous validation. While the simulations show that LoSST outperforms batch learning after structural changes, they don't establish whether LoSST is better than other online methods. The paper claims LoSST "incurs no additional costs in 'normal' structure learning situations," but the evidence is limited to the first 1000 datapoints in the first simulation.

To strengthen this paper, the authors should:
1. Conduct more comprehensive experiments comparing LoSST to other online methods for graphical model structure learning
2. Provide a more thorough analysis of the algorithm's limitations and failure modes
3. Justify the choice of Mahalanobis distance and pooled p-values with statistical arguments
4. Include more detailed real-world validation with statistical significance tests

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper's heuristic nature and lack of rigorous validation concerning. While the problem is important and the proposed algorithm has potential, the evidence presented does not meet NIPS standards for methodological rigor. The paper would benefit from substantially more thorough empirical validation and a stronger theoretical foundation before being considered for publication.

I recommend rejection. The paper presents an interesting idea but fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed method is both novel and effective. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their empirical validation and theoretical justification to be competitive at NIPS.
