This paper attempts to address the challenging problem of online learning of graphical model structure in non-stationary environments. The core idea, LoSST, dynamically adjusts data weighting based on Mahalanobis distance and a pooled p-value to detect structural changes, triggering graph relearning. While the problem statement is well-motivated and relevant to many real-world applications, I find the paper’s presentation and validation insufficient to warrant acceptance to NIPS.

The primary weakness lies in the heuristic nature of the algorithm and the limited empirical validation. The algorithm introduces several parameters (γ, T, ν) with little justification for their specific values, relying on “unsystematic investigation” for their selection. While the authors acknowledge the trade-off between convergence and diligence, the exploration of this trade-off feels superficial. The simulation setup, while comprehensive in varying graph size and degree, lacks comparison to more sophisticated change point detection or adaptive filtering methods beyond the basic TD(0) mentioned in the related work. The claim that LoSST performs “indistinguishable” from batch-mode learning when the structure is stable feels overstated given the overhead of the online statistics tracking and the dynamic weighting scheme. A more thorough comparison to established batch methods (with equivalent data size) would be critical.

The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in several areas. The derivation of the update equations for the weighted covariance and sample size, while presented, feels rushed and lacks sufficient explanation. The connection between the pooled p-value and the weight update rule (Eq. 4) is not fully convincing. The explanation for why edge *removal* errors are higher than addition errors when only parameters change is also insufficient. The discussion of convergence and diligence, while theoretically interesting, is difficult to follow without a more intuitive explanation of the underlying mechanisms.

The originality of the work is moderate. The combination of change detection based on Mahalanobis distance with dynamic data weighting and online structure learning is not entirely novel. Existing work on online changepoint detection and adaptive filtering offers related concepts, though not specifically applied to graphical model structure learning. The paper’s contribution lies in the specific implementation and integration of these ideas within the context of recursive SEMs, but the step-change in innovation feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.

Regarding significance, the potential impact of successfully learning time-varying graphical models is undoubtedly high. However, the current presentation doesn't demonstrate that LoSST represents a substantial advancement over existing methods. The application to US price index volatility is a positive step towards demonstrating real-world relevance, but the analysis is descriptive and doesn't provide rigorous validation of the algorithm’s performance in a practical setting. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem.
*   Presents a potentially useful heuristic for online structure learning.
*   Includes simulation results demonstrating some performance benefits over a static batch method.
*   Provides a real-world case study.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Heuristic nature of the algorithm with poorly justified parameter choices.
*   Limited empirical validation and lack of comparison to strong baselines.
*   Insufficient clarity in the presentation of the algorithm and its theoretical justification.
*   Incremental originality.
*   Overstated claims about performance.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject.

The paper presents an interesting idea but falls short of NIPS standards due to its heuristic nature, limited validation, and lack of clarity. The authors need to provide a more thorough theoretical analysis, a more rigorous empirical evaluation with comparisons to stronger baselines, and a clearer presentation of the algorithm and its underlying principles. A more convincing demonstration of the algorithm’s practical benefits is also required. The current work feels like a promising starting point but requires substantial further development before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field.
