Summary
This paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic online method for tracking time-varying graphical structures (DAG-based SEMs) under a “locally stationary” assumption. The approach maintains exponentially-weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, “effective” sample size) updated per datapoint. It monitors goodness-of-fit via Mahalanobis distances converted to p-values and pooled over time using Liptak’s method, and adapts per-datapoint weights ar using a thresholded linear rule. Structure is relearned intermittently (via PC) with a probability that increases when pooled p-values are small, aiming to react near changepoints while avoiding excessive computation. The paper sketches conditions under which LoSST is convergent (stable regime) or “diligent” (rapid changepoint response), and empirically evaluates LoSST on synthetic data with graph and parameter changes and on a CPI volatility dataset.

Relation to prior work
Compared to batch structure learners (score-based and constraint-based) that assume a fixed generating model, LoSST targets online tracking. It is related to online change-point detection (Bayesian and frequentist) and TD-like exponential averaging, but aims to both detect change and relearn arbitrary DAG structure. Prior work on time-indexed structures includes Talih & Hengartner (undirected graphs with single-edge edits) and Siracusa & Fisher (Bayesian edges over time). Several relevant lines of work are under-cited or absent, including sliding-window/exp-decay CI testing, dynamic Bayesian networks, and time-varying graphical models with regularization (e.g., Kolar & Xing, fused-lasso/TVGL-type methods), as well as BOCPD combined with per-segment structure learning.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and practically relevant problem: online structure learning when the generating process changes.
- Simple, memory-efficient sufficient statistic tracking; single-pass capability is attractive for streams/large data.
- Intuitive mechanism to modulate forgetting via pooled evidence of misfit; the probabilistic relearning scheme is pragmatic and computationally mindful.
- Clear distinction and discussion of convergence vs. diligence trade-offs, with parameter regimes aligned to desired behavior.
- Simulation results show that in stationary periods LoSST matches batch PC, and after changepoints it adapts faster than batch PC on mixed distributions; the probabilistic relearning experiment suggests fewer relearns need not hurt accuracy substantially.
- Real-world case study is thoughtful and shows how pooled p-values avoid overreacting to single outliers.

Weaknesses
- Statistical validity of the detection mechanism is unclear. Hotelling’s T2 distributional claim assumes unweighted sample covariance; with time-varying weights and effective sample size Sr, the null distribution of Mahalanobis distances is not T2 in general. This affects p-values and therefore the pooled statistic ρ and ar.
- Liptak pooling presumes independence (or known dependence structure). Here p-values are serially dependent and adaptively weighted; no correction is provided.
- The update for effective sample size Sr is heuristic. No derivation of its connection to the variance of the weighted estimators is provided, yet Sr is reused in CI tests.
- The interface with PC is under-specified. PC requires conditional independence tests (e.g., partial correlations with Fisher’s Z) and an appropriate sample size. How are weighted partial correlations, their sampling distributions, and degrees of freedom handled? Are regularization/shrinkage used when Sr is small to ensure invertibility?
- Baselines are weak. Comparing to batch PC on the full mixture is unfavorable to the baseline. Missing comparisons: (a) sliding window PC; (b) PC on exponentially decayed sufficient statistics with fixed forgetting; (c) BOCPD (or other CPD) plus per-segment PC; (d) time-varying graphical lasso/undirected methods adapted to SEM skeletons; (e) dynamic BN baselines.
- Numerical stability and complexity are not analyzed. Inverting covariances online with small Sr can be ill-conditioned; computational cost of repeated PC runs as a function of ν, V, and max-degree is not reported.
- Theory is limited to proof sketches, with unclear assumptions (e.g., data distributions, bounded moments). The “no algorithm can be both diligent and convergent” claim cites a technical report; more context and precise definitions would help.
- Hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) are tuned informally; no sensitivity analysis or principled selection guidance is given.
- Real-world evaluation is purely descriptive, lacks validation or counterfactual checks; the CPI example has no ground truth for structural changes.

Quality
Technically sound in its engineering spirit but not yet statistically rigorous. Claims about p-values, effective N, and CI testing need justification. Experiments are suggestive but not comprehensive, and baselines are insufficient to establish state of the art.

Clarity
Generally well-written and easy to follow at a high level. Some notational quirks/typos remain (e.g., “occcurs”, “graph2”, “inverse3”). The details of conditional independence testing with weights should be clarified.

Originality
Moderate. The combination of pooled misfit-driven adaptive forgetting with online sufficient statistics and intermittent structure relearning is novel in this explicit form, but components (EWMA, CPD, PC) are known.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners needing lightweight online structure updates. However, the lack of rigorous statistical grounding and strong baselines limits impact at this stage.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a statistical analysis of the weighted Mahalanobis distance under the online estimator: null distribution, calibration of p-values, and implications for ρ; consider permutation or bootstrap calibration online, or robust distances and shrinkage covariance.
- Justify or revise the effective sample size update and its use in CI tests. Derive weighted partial correlation tests (or use Fisher’s Z with an effective df) and discuss validity.
- Add strong baselines: sliding window PC; fixed EWMA forgetting; BOCPD+PC; time-varying GGM (with conversion to DAG skeleton/orientation if feasible). Report detection delay, FPR/TPR, and error bars.
- Provide hyperparameter sensitivity and guidance. Analyze trade-offs of T, γ, ν on detection delay, stability, and computation.
- Discuss and implement numerical safeguards (e.g., ridge/shrinkage, robust covariance, Woodbury updates).
- Evaluate on slowly drifting regimes and rapid switching; test robustness to outliers and non-Gaussianity.
- Expand related work to dynamic networks, time-varying graph estimation, and online CI testing; correct and complete references.
- Clarify the PC interface and independence tests used with weighted stats; release code.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a timely online setting with a simple, implementable method and promising empirical behavior.
- Clear articulation of convergence vs. diligence with parameterized control.
- Memory- and compute-aware design; single-pass sufficient statistics.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of statistical rigor in key components (p-value calibration, effective N, CI testing).
- Missing strong baselines and comprehensive evaluation.
- Limited theoretical guarantees; proof sketches only.
- Real-world evaluation is qualitative and limited.

Recommendation
Reject for now. The idea is promising and could be impactful with a stronger statistical foundation, clearer testing methodology, and more thorough empirical comparisons.
