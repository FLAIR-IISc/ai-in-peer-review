This paper attempts to tackle the challenging problem of tracking time-varying graphical model structures in an online fashion. The core idea – to adapt sufficient statistics tracking from time series analysis (specifically, TDL) and combine it with a dynamic graph learning algorithm (PC) – is intuitively reasonable. However, despite the identified motivation and clear presentation, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing due to a lack of compelling evidence and an overreliance on simulation results that, while positive, do not sufficiently address the inherent difficulties of the proposed approach.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the LoSST algorithm. The connection to related work is also reasonably thorough, acknowledging limitations of existing online learning and changepoint detection methods. The mathematical formulation is sound, and the explanations of the update equations for means, covariances, and sample size are clear. However, the justification for many design choices—particularly the weighting scheme based on Mahalanobis distance and p-values—feels somewhat heuristic. While the authors acknowledge this (“heuristic algorithm”), the lack of a more rigorous theoretical underpinning weakens the contribution.

My primary concern lies with the originality and significance of the work. The paper essentially adapts existing techniques to a new problem, and the adaptation, while nontrivial, doesn’t present a fundamentally novel algorithmic approach. The core innovation – weighting data points based on model fit – is a common strategy in online learning, and the specific implementation here doesn’t appear to offer substantial advantages over established methods. Furthermore, the claim that LoSST incurs “no additional costs” in stable structure learning situations is misleading; the overhead of maintaining and updating the weights, Mahalanobis distances, and p-values will inevitably introduce some computational cost, even if it’s small.

The simulation results are encouraging, demonstrating that LoSST can indeed track changes in both graph structure and parameters. However, the simulations are limited in scope and don’t adequately address the challenges of real-world data. Specifically, the controlled settings with clearly defined changepoints and relatively simple graph structures don’t fully capture the complexity of scenarios where changes are gradual, noisy, or involve multiple simultaneous shifts. The comparison to the batch-mode PC algorithm is also somewhat unfair, as the batch algorithm is penalized by being applied to mixed data distributions post-changepoint, a situation it’s not designed for. The performance gain demonstrated is significant, but the conditions under which it occurs are rather specific. The probabilistic relearning schedule, while interesting, adds another layer of heuristic tuning without clear justification. 

The application to US price index volatility is a welcome addition, but it’s largely descriptive and doesn’t provide strong evidence for the practical utility of LoSST. The detected shifts correspond to known macroeconomic events, but it’s unclear whether LoSST provides any new insights beyond what could be obtained with simpler time series analysis techniques.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Clear presentation of a well-defined problem and a reasonable algorithmic approach.
*   Positive simulation results demonstrating the ability to track changing graphical structures.
*   Potential applicability to real-world scenarios where online structure learning is required.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited originality – the approach relies heavily on adapting existing techniques.
*   Lack of rigorous theoretical justification for key design choices.
*   Simulation results are limited in scope and may not generalize to real-world data.
*   The significance of the results is questionable – it’s unclear whether LoSST offers substantial advantages over existing methods.
*   The real-world application is largely descriptive and doesn’t provide strong evidence of practical utility.



Overall, while the paper presents a potentially useful algorithm, it falls short of the high standards expected at NIPS. The lack of theoretical depth, the limited scope of the simulations, and the questionable significance of the results make it difficult to justify acceptance. I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to further develop their ideas with a stronger theoretical foundation and more extensive validation on real-world datasets. A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and scalability of the algorithm would also be beneficial.
