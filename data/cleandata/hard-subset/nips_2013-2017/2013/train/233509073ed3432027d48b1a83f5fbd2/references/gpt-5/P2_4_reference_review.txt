Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses online structure learning for time-varying graphical models. It proposes LoSST, which maintains weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, “effective” sample size) for linear-Gaussian SEMs and adaptively reweights new observations based on a pooled p-value of Mahalanobis distances (via Liptak’s method). When the pooled p-value indicates mismatch with the current model, weights increase (effectively forgetting older data), and the PC algorithm is invoked with some probability to relearn the DAG. The paper sketches conditions under which LoSST is convergent vs. “diligent,” and presents synthetic experiments and a qualitative macroeconomic case study.

The work is positioned relative to batch structure learning (score-based and constraint-based), temporal-difference learning, online changepoint detection (Bayesian/frequentist), and prior time-varying graph work (Talih & Hengartner, Siracusa & Fisher). Compared to CPD methods, LoSST aims to handle both abrupt and slow change without storing large windows. Compared to prior time-varying graph papers, LoSST runs online rather than batch.

Strengths
- Problem importance: Online tracking of changing causal/graphical structure is highly relevant in many domains (finance, sensors, neuroscience).
- Simple and practical: The sufficient-statistic updates are single-pass, O(V^2) memory, and can plug into standard structure learners. The weighting mechanism is intuitive and easy to implement.
- Thoughtful design: The pooled p-value smooths over noisy Mahalanobis distances; decoupling change detection from graph relearning via a noisy-OR schedule is sensible.
- Theoretical insight: The convergence vs. diligence trade-off is well-motivated, with clear intuitions and some formal conditions (even if sketched).
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic results show LoSST matches batch PC in static regimes and adapts faster after true changes; the probabilistic relearning experiment suggests reduced compute without major accuracy loss.

Weaknesses and detailed assessment per review criteria

Quality
- Statistical foundations of change signal: Using Hotelling’s T^2 distribution for Mahalanobis distances computed from a weighted, adaptively updated covariance that also used the same data (and with an ad hoc effective sample size Sr) is questionable. The null distribution is not exactly T^2 under these dependencies/weighting, and independence assumptions underlying Liptak pooling are violated. The method is explicitly heuristic, but the resulting false positive/negative behavior is not quantified.
- Effective sample size update Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1) Sr + 1 is chosen to satisfy two constraints rather than derived from a principled estimator. Its properties (bias, variance, calibration relative to the true Fisher information in the weighted estimates) are not analyzed.
- PC with weighted statistics: The paper does not specify how conditional independence tests are calibrated under weighting (e.g., Fisher z using partial correlations estimated from C_r with Sr degrees of freedom?). Without this, Type I/II error control of PC under LoSST’s weighting is unclear.
- Theory: Convergence/diligence claims are mostly proof sketches and rely on somewhat unnatural regimes (e.g., T > 1 guaranteeing continual weight growth). There is no guarantee of structural consistency under local stationarity (e.g., bounds on detection delay vs. false alarms, or on asymptotic recovery when change ceases).
- Empirical baselines: The main comparison is to batch PC on the full mixed dataset, which is a weak baseline for a nonstationary setting. More appropriate baselines (and ablations) are missing: sliding-window PC of comparable effective size, exponentially weighted covariance + PC with fixed decay, CPD + reset PC, undirected time-varying graphical models (e.g., Talih & Hengartner; time-varying graphical lasso/Kalman-smoothing variants), and simple EWMA-based detectors. Sensitivity to hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) is minimal and unsystematic.
- Real-data analysis is qualitative; no external change-point annotations, no stability analysis of learned structures, and no quantitative validation.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The derivations of the weighted mean/covariance updates are standard and readable. However:
  - Notational slips: p = N in the T^2 cdf appears to be a typo (should be V). Covariance symbols alternate between C^r_{Vi,Vj} and C^r_{Xi,Xj}.
  - The exact conditional independence testing procedure within PC (test, alpha level, how Sr enters) should be specified for reproducibility.
  - Pseudocode would help clarify the update-relearn loop and state variables (μ, C, b_r, S_r, η, τ, ρ, a_r, P(relearn)).
  - Discussion of numerical stability (e.g., ensuring C_r is PD when Sr is small; use of shrinkage) is missing.

Originality
- The combination of (i) adaptive, pooled-p-value-driven reweighting of sufficient statistics and (ii) probabilistic relearning of DAG structure is, to my knowledge, novel for online DAG/SEM tracking. However, the individual components (EWMA-like weighting, change detection via distances, online CI tests) are familiar, and related time-varying graphical model literature (especially for GGMs) is broader than cited. Dynamic Bayesian networks and online BN structure learning are not discussed. Overall, moderate originality.

Significance
- If robust, an online, model-agnostic framework for tracking changing graph structure would be impactful. As presented, the significance is limited by the heuristic nature of the detection statistic, lack of stronger baselines, and limited theoretical guarantees. The promising empirical trends suggest potential, but stronger validation is needed for NeurIPS-level impact.

Pros
- Addresses an important and underserved problem (online, time-varying DAG structure).
- Simple, efficient algorithm; easy to integrate with existing learners.
- Clear motivation and useful intuitions (convergence vs diligence).
- Synthetic experiments show plausible and sometimes substantial gains over naive batch PC in nonstationary settings.

Cons
- Heuristic statistical machinery (T^2, Liptak pooling, Sr) without calibration or error analysis.
- Missing strong baselines (sliding-window/EWMA PC; CPD+reset; time-varying GGMs).
- Limited theory for correctness/consistency; proof sketches rely on nonstandard thresholds.
- Real-data validation is qualitative.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to T, γ, ν across graph sizes/densities? Provide a systematic sweep and guidance for setting them.
- Can you compare against sliding-window PC (with window sizes matched to observed effective S_r) and a fixed-decay EWMA covariance + PC?
- Clarify CI testing under weighting (test choice, degrees of freedom, alpha). Consider multiple-testing control as relearning frequency changes.
- Provide numerical stabilization (shrinkage covariance) and assess PD guarantees when Sr drops.
- Consider a more principled change statistic (e.g., generalized likelihood ratio using weighted estimates, or score-based sequential tests) and quantify false alarm/delay tradeoffs.
- Expand related work to include time-varying GGMs (e.g., Kolar/Xing, Zhou et al.), dynamic BNs, and online BN structure learning.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The problem and overall approach are compelling and the preliminary results are promising, but the paper relies on uncalibrated heuristics and lacks strong baselines and theoretical or empirical validation needed for acceptance. Strengthening the statistical underpinnings, adding appropriate baselines and sensitivity analyses, and clarifying the CI testing protocol would likely elevate this to a solid contribution.
