Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying DAG/SEM structure under a “locally stationary” assumption: at each time r, data are i.i.d. from an SEM Gr, but Gr may change in structure and parameters over time. LoSST maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) updated per-datum. Weights ar adapt based on a pooled p-value ρr of recent Mahalanobis distance p-values (Hotelling’s T^2), using Liptak’s method, to up-weight recent data when the current model’s fit is poor. Graph structure is relearned intermittently using PC, with the relearning probability increased when ρr is small. The authors sketch conditions under which LoSST is convergent (stable structure) or “diligent” (rapid change detection), arguing these goals are incompatible. Simulations show LoSST matches batch PC before change points and outperforms it after changes, and a small real-data application on CPI differences suggests LoSST detects known macroeconomic shifts. This work relates to batch structure learning (score- and constraint-based; Pearl, Spirtes et al.; Chickering), change point detection (Adams & MacKay; Desobry et al.), and time-varying graph estimation (Talih & Hengartner; Siracusa & Fisher). The key novelty is an online, general-purpose structure tracker combining adaptive weighting and probabilistic relearning.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: structure often changes and batch methods are ill-suited for online operation.
- Practical algorithmic framework: single-pass, low-memory updates of sufficient statistics; decouples tracking from structure relearning, enabling reuse of standard learners (PC).
- Thoughtful design choices: pooled p-values rather than per-point outlier detection to reduce reactivity to isolated spikes; probabilistic relearning to trade accuracy vs. compute.
- Simulation evidence that when the structure is stable LoSST behaves like batch PC, while after changes it adapts and improves over naive batch use of the entire history.
- Useful discussion of convergence vs diligence trade-offs and parameters (γ, T, ν).

Weaknesses and evaluation by criteria

Quality
- Statistical validity of the “fit” signal is unclear. The Hotelling T^2 p-values used for Mahalanobis distances assume i.i.d. Gaussian samples with an unweighted covariance estimator and a fixed sample size. Here, the covariance is adaptively weighted and the data are nonstationary; using Sr as “degrees of freedom” to plug into T^2 is not justified. Similarly, Liptak pooling typically assumes independence or known dependence among p-values; temporal dependence and shared estimated covariance violate that. This weakens the interpretation of ρr and the guarantees around false alarms/misses.
- Effective sample size update (Eq. 3) is ad hoc and not derived from a principled variance estimator under weighting; its suitability for calibrating CI tests in PC (e.g., Fisher z tests for partial correlations) is not demonstrated. It is also unclear how conditioning set sizes are handled when mapping Sr to test statistics.
- Theoretical results are largely proof sketches and rely on strong assumptions (e.g., asymptotic consistency of PC under faithfulness, causal sufficiency) and on properties of threshold sequences. There is no finite-sample analysis of detection delay, false positive rates, or stability under slow drift.
- Baselines are weak. Comparing to batch PC on the entire history is expected to be poor after changes. More appropriate baselines include sliding-window PC, exponentially weighted covariance with fixed forgetting factor, BOCPD combined with reinitializing PC, and time-varying Gaussian graphical model methods (e.g., fused/temporal graphical lasso, kernel/locally weighted GGMs).

Clarity
- The exposition of mean/covariance updates is clear. However, derivations/assumptions behind Eq. 3 (effective sample size) and the reuse of Sr in CI testing for PC need clarification.
- The description of how partial correlations and p-values are computed from weighted covariances (and corrected for conditioning set sizes) is missing; reproducibility would benefit from this detail.
- The probabilistic relearning schedule is intuitive but under-specified (how is ν chosen; interaction with T, γ).

Originality
- The combination of adaptive pooled-p-value-based forgetting with online sufficient statistics and probabilistic relearning for general graphical structure is novel. Prior time-varying graph work is mostly offline or model-specific. However, related online/dynamic network estimation literature is broader than cited; positioning relative to sliding-window/forgetting approaches and time-varying GGMs should be strengthened.

Significance
- Problem is important; an effective, general online structure learner would have high impact. Current evidence is promising but limited: linear-Gaussian SEMs with small graphs, synthetic changes with known timescales, and a single small real-world dataset without competing baselines. The heuristic/statistical concerns limit immediate applicability and adoption.

Questions for the authors
- How robust are results to deviations from Gaussianity and to measurement noise? Any experiments with non-Gaussian SEMs?
- How exactly are CI tests in PC calibrated with weighted covariances and Sr (especially accounting for conditioning sets)?
- How sensitive is performance to (T, γ, ν)? Can you report ablations and detection-delay/false-alarm trade-offs?
- How is the covariance inverse regularized when Sr is small or p is large (e.g., shrinkage, ridge)? Any numerical stability safeguards?

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace or justify the T^2-based p-values under weighting/nonstationarity. Consider:
  - Estimating the empirical distribution of Mahalanobis distances via rolling-window permutation/bootstrap;
  - Using robust distances or control-chart methods with proven properties under EWMA/forgetting;
  - Modeling ρr with a state-space change process and deriving a likelihood ratio test.
- Provide principled derivation of an effective sample size for weighted estimators, and adapt CI tests accordingly (e.g., n_eff for partial correlations: n_eff − |S| − 3).
- Add strong baselines: sliding-window PC (various window sizes), fixed forgetting-factor versions, BOCPD-triggered relearning, and time-varying GGM methods. Report SHD/F1 over time, detection delay, and computational cost.
- Include sensitivity analyses over T, γ, ν; ablate Liptak pooling vs simple EWMA of distances/p-values; analyze the trade-off curve between convergence and diligence empirically.
- Discuss scalability and provide results beyond p ∈ {10,20}; incorporate covariance shrinkage and report timings.
- Release code and details of the PC implementation and significance levels.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap: online, general-purpose structure learning under change.
- Simple, modular algorithm with practical appeal and low memory footprint.
- Interesting conceptual framing (convergence vs diligence) and intuitive use of pooled evidence.

Cons for acceptance
- Statistical underpinnings of the change signal and effective sample size are weak/heuristic.
- Theoretical guarantees are limited; no finite-sample or error-rate analysis.
- Empirical evaluation lacks strong baselines and sensitivity analyses; limited scale.
- Real-data case study is anecdotal without validation.

Recommendation
Reject (encourage resubmission). The idea is promising and practically motivated, but the current version lacks statistical rigor in its core detection mechanism, theoretical depth, and comparative evaluation needed for NeurIPS. Strengthening these aspects as suggested could make this a compelling contribution.
