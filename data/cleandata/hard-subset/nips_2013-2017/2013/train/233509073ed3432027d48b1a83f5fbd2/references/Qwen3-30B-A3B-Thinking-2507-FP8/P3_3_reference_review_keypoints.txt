- Proposes LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical model structure by updating sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) with adaptive weights driven by Mahalanobis-distance-based pooled p-values.

- Strength: Clearly identifies the gap between static structure learning and real-world dynamic needs, addressing an important problem.

- Strength: Methodology is well-motivated and technically sound, with weighted online updates and connections to TD(0) learning.

- Strength: Theoretical analysis of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off gives useful guidance for parameter selection.

- Strength: Comprehensive empirical evaluation shows LoSST matches batch methods when stable and adapts to structural changes; includes a real-world US price-index volatility example.

- Weakness: Lacks theoretical guarantees and discussion for high-dimensional settings where variable count is large relative to sample size.

- Weakness: Probabilistic relearning mechanism (noisy-OR with parameter ν) and pooled p-value threshold T are ad hoc and lack principled selection guidance.

- Weakness: Related work comparison is incomplete; paper does not clearly explain why recent dynamic graphical-model approaches cannot be adapted for online learning.

- Weakness: Real-world validation is limited (only 6 variables), insufficient to demonstrate practical utility at scale.

- Weakness: Assumes recursive linear SEMs and does not discuss generalization to other graphical model classes (e.g., discrete Bayesian networks).

- Overall: Technically solid and valuable contribution, but needs revisions to address scalability, principled design choices, related-work clarity, and stronger real-world validation.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.

- Revision suggestions: add discussion/solutions for high-dimensional settings; provide principled selection or empirical study for ν; expand real-world experiments to larger datasets (20+ variables); clarify why prior dynamic-graph methods aren't suitable for online adaptation; discuss extensions to other graphical-model types.
