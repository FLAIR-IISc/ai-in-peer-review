- Proposes LoSST: an online algorithm that tracks time-varying linear-Gaussian DAG/SEM structure using exponentially weighted sufficient statistics and adaptive weights to trigger probabilistic relearning with PC on weighted stats.  
- Strength: tackles online structure tracking without storing history, with modular design and O(p^2) memory suitable for streaming.  
- Strength: single-pass updates and sensible empirical goals (match batch under stationarity, adapt after changes).  
- Strength: discusses convergence vs. diligence trade-off and shows pooled evidence reduces sensitivity to single outliers.  
- Key concern: p-values for Mahalanobis distances assume i.i.d. Hotelling’s T^2, but weighted/EWMA covariances and time-varying weights invalidate that null distribution, so pr and ρr are uncalibrated.  
- Key concern: Lipták/Stouffer pooling assumes independent/well-calibrated p-values, but Dr are serially dependent and share parameters, so pooled ρr may be distorted.  
- Key concern: Using Sr as “effective” sample size in the T^2 reference and PC tests is ad hoc and unjustified; EWMA effective sample size is not shown to equal Sr.  
- Key concern: No analysis that Fisher z or partial correlation tests behave correctly under weighted data, creating a mismatch between tests and data-generating process.  
- Baselines missing: comparisons exclude sliding-window PC, fixed-forgetting PC, CPD+relearn strategies, time-varying graphical models (TV-GGM, fused/joint graphical lasso), and dynamic Bayesian networks.  
- Experimental issues: no ablation/sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν, dimensionality, sparsity, and no error bars or variance estimates reported.  
- Numerical stability concern: Cr inversion with small Sr can be ill-conditioned; no shrinkage/regularization, Sherman–Morrison, or PD guarantees discussed.  
- Computational concern: runtime, wall-clock costs, and accuracy–cost trade-offs with relearning frequency not reported.  
- Theoretical weaknesses: proofs are sketchy, rely on ar dynamics not statistical models, and lack guarantees on false alarms, detection delay, or structure recovery consistency.  
- Diligence result issue: reliance on T>1 causing ar to increase irrespective of fit is undesirable and undermines threshold interpretation.  
- CPI case study limitation: lacks ground truth, doesn’t adequately handle autocorrelation/seasonality, and may conflate heteroskedasticity with structural change.  
- Related work gap: omits substantial literature on time-varying graphs and covariance-change detection that should be cited and compared.  
- Clarity: generally well written and organized, but some notation issues, typos, and unclear interpretation/range of T and Sr-to-test mapping.  
- Originality: combination of adaptive EWMA stats with pooled discrepancy for triggering relearning is interesting but novelty is moderate given related adaptive-forgetting literature.  
- Significance: potentially useful for practitioners, but lack of calibration, baselines, and theoretical support limit immediate impact.  
- Suggested fixes: calibrate change statistics (prequential/GLR/CUSUM or bootstrap/permutation), or use predictive likelihoods for valid thresholds.  
- Suggested fixes: add regularized covariance updates and stable inverse maintenance (shrinkage, ridge, Sherman–Morrison).  
- Suggested fixes: justify or replace Sr with principled effective sample size, analyze CI tests under weighting, or use score-based learners suited to weighted data.  
- Suggested fixes: add strong baselines (sliding-window, exponential forgetting, CPD+relearn, TV-GGM), report runtimes, and include sensitivity analyses.  
- Suggested fixes: expand related work discussion and provide finite-sample guarantees on detection delay/false alarm and conditions for correct recovery.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject — interesting and simple approach but needs better statistical calibration, baselines, and theoretical support.
