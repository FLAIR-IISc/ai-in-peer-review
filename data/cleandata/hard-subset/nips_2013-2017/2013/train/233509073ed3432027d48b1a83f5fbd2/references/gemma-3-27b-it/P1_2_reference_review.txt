## Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the important problem of learning graphical model structure in non-stationary environments, a significant limitation of traditional graphical model learning algorithms which assume i.i.d. data. The authors propose LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), an online heuristic algorithm that dynamically adjusts to changes in the underlying graphical structure by tracking sufficient statistics and adaptively weighting data points based on their “fit” to the current model. The algorithm uses the PC algorithm for structure learning and demonstrates, through simulation, its ability to track changing structures and maintain performance comparable to batch methods when the structure is stable. The paper also includes a compelling application to US price index volatility data.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper effectively positions itself within the existing literature. It correctly identifies the limitations of both Bayesian and constraint-based structure learning algorithms in non-stationary settings. The authors also acknowledge and differentiate their work from existing online changepoint detection (CPD) methods and previous attempts to learn time-indexed graph structures, highlighting LoSST’s ability to operate in true online fashion and its focus on general graphical model structures (rather than specific types of changes or undirected graphs). The discussion of Temporal Difference Learning (TDL) and its limitations is also relevant.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** The problem of learning in dynamic environments is crucial for many real-world applications.
*   **Novel Approach:** LoSST presents a practical and well-motivated heuristic for online structure learning. The adaptive weighting scheme, based on Mahalanobis distance and pooled p-values, is a key contribution.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a clear, although somewhat terse, description of the theoretical foundations of the sufficient statistic updates.  The discussion of convergence vs. diligence is insightful and valuable.
*   **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The simulations demonstrate LoSST's ability to track both structural and parameter changes, and to perform competitively with batch methods during stable periods. The application to US price index data provides a convincing demonstration of real-world applicability.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible.
* **Practicality:** The algorithm can operate on a single pass and doesn't require storing large amounts of data like some CPD methods.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Heuristic Nature:** The algorithm is presented as a heuristic, and while the simulations provide strong evidence of its effectiveness, a more rigorous theoretical analysis of its convergence properties and performance bounds would be valuable.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The performance of LoSST likely depends on the choice of several parameters (γ, T, ν). While the paper provides some justification for the chosen values in the simulations, a more systematic sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:** The algorithm's computational cost is not explicitly analyzed. Relearning the graph using the PC algorithm can be expensive, and the paper only briefly mentions balancing accuracy and computational cost. A more detailed discussion of scalability would be useful.
*   **Limited Scope of Structure Learning:** The reliance on the PC algorithm limits the types of graphical models that can be learned. Exploring other structure learning algorithms (e.g., score-based methods with approximations) could expand the algorithm’s applicability.
* **Mahalanobis Distance Reliance:** While the Mahalanobis distance is a reasonable choice, its sensitivity to outliers could be problematic.  Exploring alternative distance metrics or outlier detection mechanisms might improve robustness.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by simulations. The description of the algorithm is detailed enough for reproduction.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, but some sections (e.g., the detailed derivation of the covariance update equation) could benefit from further clarification.

**Originality:** The combination of adaptive weighting with an online structure learning algorithm is novel. The specific approach of using Mahalanobis distance and pooled p-values, coupled with the diligence/convergence trade-off, represents a unique contribution.

**Significance:** The work has the potential to be significant, as it addresses a key limitation of existing graphical model learning techniques. The demonstrated ability to track time-varying structures has broad applicability across various domains.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem in graphical model learning.
*   Presents a novel and practical algorithm (LoSST) with promising empirical results.
*   Includes a compelling real-world application demonstrating the algorithm’s effectiveness.
*   Clearly articulates the trade-off between convergence and diligence.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The heuristic nature of the algorithm and the lack of rigorous theoretical analysis.
*   Potential parameter sensitivity and scalability concerns.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field.  While the heuristic nature of LoSST and the lack of a detailed computational analysis are weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, the strong empirical results, and the practical applicability outweigh these concerns.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Expand the discussion of parameter sensitivity and provide guidelines for choosing appropriate parameter values.
2.  Provide a more detailed analysis of the algorithm’s computational cost and scalability.
3.  Acknowledge the limitations of using the PC algorithm and briefly discuss potential alternatives.
