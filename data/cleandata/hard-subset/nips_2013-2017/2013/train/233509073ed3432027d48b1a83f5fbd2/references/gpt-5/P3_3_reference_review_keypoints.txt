- Summary: Proposes LoSST, an online algorithm that maintains weighted sufficient statistics and intermittently relearns DAG structure using pooled p-value change detection (Liptak) and PC algorithm triggers.

- Strength: Tackles online structure learning for time-varying DAGs with a simple, lightweight per-update procedure.

- Strength: Adaptive weighting via pooled p-values is intuitive and potentially sensitive to both abrupt and gradual changes.

- Strength: Modular design separates sufficient-statistics tracking from relearning, allowing alternative learners in place of PC.

- Strength: Experiments show LoSST matches batch PC in stable regimes and adapts faster than a naïve full-data batch learner after change points.

- Strength: Thoughtful discussion of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off situates design choices theoretically.

- Major concern (statistical validity): Mahalanobis T^2 p-values assume i.i.d. covariance estimates, but LoSST uses time-weighted covariances, making per-point p-values and pooled statistics potentially miscalibrated.

- Major concern (dependence in pooling): Liptak pooling assumes independence (or known dependence) across p-values, which is violated because statistics are shared across time, leaving false-alarm/delay properties unclear.

- Major concern (effective sample size Sr): Sr is defined heuristically and fractional, with no derivation linking it to sampling distributions of (partial) correlations under weighting, so Type I/II control in PC is uncertain.

- Major concern (baselines): Baselines are weak (batch PC on full data); missing stronger comparisons like sliding-window PC, fixed-forgetting EW PC, CPD+reset, score-based learners, and an oracle post-change PC.

- Major concern (hyperparameter tuning): Hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) are chosen unsystematically with no sensitivity/robustness analysis despite their central role in detection and relearning.

- Major concern (scalability & numerical stability): Per-step covariance inversion is O(p^3), PC is worst-case super-polynomial, experiments only up to p=20, and no discussion of regularization or efficient inverse updates.

- Major concern (theory): Convergence/diligence claims rest on proof sketches with unverified assumptions (PC consistency under weighted sampling, treating Sr as i.i.d. sample size), lacking formal theorems and distributional calibration.

- Major concern (orientation accuracy): PC orientation relies on calibrated CI tests, which are not guaranteed under the weighted, time-varying statistics used.

- Major concern (related work): Omitted relevant literatures such as RLS/adaptive forgetting, time-varying GGMs (fused penalties/localized likelihood), sliding-window DBN learners, and multivariate change-detection methods beyond T^2/EWMA.

- Major concern (real-data case study): CPI application is qualitative—only shows effective sample size dips around events with no quantitative evaluation of structure accuracy or predictive performance.

- Minor: Clarify and justify Sr recurrence (derive from variance/information viewpoint or cite results).

- Minor: Discuss regularization and numerical safeguards for Cr inversion (shrinkage, diagonal loading, Woodbury updates).

- Minor: State which CI tests are used in PC with weighted statistics and how degrees of freedom are set.

- Minor: Provide per-step and per-relearning complexity analysis and possible inverse-update strategies.

- Minor: Fix typos/formatting (e.g., “occcurs”), complete references, and consider reporting predictive log-likelihood on rolling hold-outs.

- Pros for acceptance: Timely problem, simple implementable idea, single-pass sufficient-statistics maintenance, and an appealing unification of change detection with adaptive forgetting.

- Cons for acceptance: Insufficient statistical grounding for p-value/CI calibration, weak baselines and limited scale, informal theoretical guarantees, and a qualitative real-data evaluation.

- Recommendation: Weak reject — promising heuristic but needs rigorous calibration, stronger baselines, sensitivity analyses, and scalability/theoretical strengthening.
