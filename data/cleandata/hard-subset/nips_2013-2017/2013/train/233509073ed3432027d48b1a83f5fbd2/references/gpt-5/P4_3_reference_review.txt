Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying directed graphical structure (linear-Gaussian SEMs). The key idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective sample size”) using adaptive per-datapoint weights ar. The weights increase when recent data are unlikely under the current covariance, measured via Mahalanobis distances whose p-values are pooled over time with Liptak’s method; larger weights emphasize recent data after distributional shifts. Structure is periodically relearned using a standard constraint-based algorithm (PC). The paper discusses a trade-off between “convergence” (stability in stationary regimes) and “diligence” (rapid response to changes), with heuristic/proof-sketch conditions driven by the threshold T and scaling γ that control ar dynamics. Simulations on synthetic data with both structure and parameter changes suggest that LoSST matches batch PC pre-change and adapts post-change, whereas batch PC on the full (mixed) dataset suffers spurious associations. A case study on U.S. price index differences indicates detected changes aligned with macroeconomic regime shifts.

The work is related to: (i) batch structure learning for DAGs/SEMs (PC and score-based methods), (ii) online change-point detection (Bayesian and frequentist), and (iii) time-varying graphical models (e.g., Talih & Hengartner for undirected graphs, Siracusa & Fisher for Bayesian posterior over time-indexed structures). The contribution is an online, single-pass frequentist approach for DAG structure tracking that avoids storing long histories and does not assume purely abrupt changes.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The weighted updates for means and covariances are standard; the covariance update is consistent with exponentially weighted estimators when ar = αbr. The “effective sample size” update (Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1)Sr + 1) is heuristic and not derived from first principles; statistical validity of using Sr in Hotelling’s T^2 is unclear, since the sampling distribution under adaptive weights need not be T^2. The p-value pooling via Liptak assumes (approximate) independence or at least a known dependence structure; here the test statistics are strongly dependent because they reuse the same evolving estimates. This affects calibration of ρr and the thresholding that drives ar. The mapping from pooled p-values to weights (Eq. 4) is heuristic, and conditions for positive-definite covariance and numerical stability (matrix inversions at each step) are not discussed.
- Guarantees: The convergence/diligence discussion is insightful but largely proof-sketch level. The condition for diligence (T > 1, γ > 1) derives from monotone growth of ar and bounded effective sample size; the convergence condition references sequences of thresholds but lacks complete proofs or finite-sample guarantees. No statistical guarantees are provided for change detection power, false alarm rates, or structural consistency under local stationarity.
- Empirical evaluation: Synthetic experiments show that LoSST recovers post-change structures faster than batch PC on the full mixture. However, baselines are limited. More appropriate comparators include: PC on a sliding window; exponentially discounted covariance PC; BOCPD followed by PC per segment; and undirected time-varying graphical model trackers. Hyperparameter selection (T, γ, ν) is unsystematic and sensitivity analyses are minimal. Reproducibility details (e.g., PC α level, maximum conditioning set size, weighted partial correlation tests, regularization) are not fully specified. The real data case is illustrative but lacks an objective evaluation (e.g., predictive likelihood, stability diagnostics, or external events alignment quantified).
- Computational aspects: Memory is O(d^2) for covariance; PC calls are the dominant cost. The probabilistic relearning schedule is reasonable, but complexity and practical runtime scaling with d and graph sparsity are not quantified.

Clarity
The high-level presentation is clear and well-motivated. Update equations are stated and the role of pooled p-values is explained. However:
- Some derivations are omitted (effective sample size), and distributional assumptions for T^2 under adaptive weighting are not justified.
- Implementation details for integrating weighted statistics into PC (test choices, α, finite-sample corrections, ensuring positive definiteness, conditioning set limits) are missing.
- References are incomplete; figures are referenced but exact settings and error bars are not described; pseudo-code would aid reproducibility.

Originality
The combination of online, adaptive-weighted sufficient statistics driven by pooled anomaly scores, together with periodic constraint-based structure relearning for DAGs/SEMs, appears novel. Prior work on time-varying graphs either assumes batch processing, undirected models, or Bayesian posteriors over structures; CPD methods generally require storing histories or assume abrupt shifts. The convergence–diligence framing for structure learning is a useful conceptual addition, though preliminary.

Significance
Tracking time-varying structure online is important for many applications. If validated, LoSST could be a useful tool with modest memory and single-pass operation. However, the current empirical comparisons understate competitive baselines, and theoretical/statistical calibration of the method is incomplete. The scope is limited to linear-Gaussian SEMs; extension to discrete or mixed data is nontrivial. Significance is promising but not yet demonstrated to NeurIPS standards.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and practically important problem (online structure learning under nonstationarity).
- Simple, memory-efficient updates; compatible with existing structure learners.
- Clear empirical benefit over naive batch PC on mixed data; negligible overhead in stationary settings.
- Conceptual contribution on convergence vs diligence trade-offs.

Cons for acceptance
- Heuristic methodology with weak statistical foundations: use of Hotelling T^2 and pooled p-values under adaptive, dependent sampling; ad hoc effective sample size.
- Limited theory (proof sketches, no error/control guarantees).
- Incomplete evaluation: lacks strong baselines (sliding windows, discounted PC, CPD+PC), ablations, sensitivity, and quantitative real-world validation.
- Missing implementation details hinder reproducibility; references incomplete.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a principled derivation (or empirical calibration) of test statistics under adaptive weighting; consider Stouffer/Fisher combinations with dependence-robust calibrations or use EWMA control charts with known properties.
- Justify or estimate effective sample size under weighting; alternatively, use resampling or online variance correction.
- Compare against sliding-window PC, exponentially discounted PC, BOCPD+PC segmentation, and time-varying GGM trackers adapted to DAGs.
- Add ablations isolating the impact of p-value pooling vs single-step anomalies and of the relearning schedule.
- Detail PC integration (tests, α, conditioning limits), numerical safeguards (shrinkage for Σ−1), complexity, and parameter tuning strategies.
- For real data, include predictive performance and robustness analyses; present learned graphs over time.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper targets an important problem with an interesting and potentially useful approach, but the statistical underpinnings are heuristic, theoretical results are preliminary, and the empirical evaluation lacks strong baselines and reproducibility details. Strengthening these aspects could make it suitable for NeurIPS.
