### Summary

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models in an online fashion from data streams where the underlying generating structure may change over time. The core idea is to maintain a set of weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an "effective" sample size) where the weights are dynamically updated based on the fit of new data points to the current model. Change is detected by monitoring the Mahalanobis distance of incoming data, which is then used to compute a pooled p-value that modulates the data weights. When a change is inferred (i.e., weights increase), the algorithm is more likely to trigger a computationally expensive relearning of the graphical model structure using a standard batch algorithm (here, the PC algorithm). The authors provide an analysis of the algorithm's long-run properties (convergence vs. diligence) based on parameter settings and present simulation results suggesting LoSST outperforms a batch-mode PC algorithm when structure changes, while performing comparably in stable periods. A brief application to U.S. price index data is also presented.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The paper addresses an important and practical problem: learning graphical models from evolving data streams. The high-level approach is intuitive and the distinction drawn between convergent and diligent learning algorithms is a valuable conceptual point that frames the trade-offs in this domain well. The empirical results on synthetic data, particularly in the first simulation, demonstrate the potential of this approach to successfully track changes where a naive batch-mode learner fails.

However, the paper is undermined by a significant number of flaws ranging from minor presentational issues to more substantial methodological and evaluative weaknesses. The sheer volume of these issues makes it difficult to confidently assess the technical soundness and contribution of the work.

**Quality & Clarity:**

The paper's quality is severely hampered by a lack of rigor and numerous clarity issues.

1.  **Methodological Gaps:** Key components of the algorithm are presented without sufficient justification. The update equation for the effective sample size (Eq. 3) is a core contribution, yet its derivation is omitted and its justification is purely qualitative. The update rule for the weights (Eq. 4) and the probabilistic relearning mechanism are presented as ad-hoc heuristics, with parameters (`T`, `γ`, `ν`) chosen via "unsystematic investigation."
2.  **Theoretical Claims:** The authors introduce the interesting concepts of convergence and diligence but support their claims with "proof sketches" in footnotes (4 and 5) that are far too brief and lack the rigor expected at NIPS. Furthermore, the parameter regime used for the simulations (`T=0.05, γ=3`) is stated to be "neither convergent nor diligent," a property that is left as an "open question for future research." It is problematic to base the entire empirical evaluation on a poorly understood parameter regime.
3.  **Technical Errors/Typos:** The paper contains careless errors that impede understanding. In the description of the Mahalanobis distance p-value, the distribution is given as Hotelling’s T^2 with parameter `p=N`, but `N` is never defined in this context; it should almost certainly be `p=V`, the number of variables. In footnote 1, the update equations for `η` and `τ` use `ar` and `pr`, suggesting that the weight `ar+1` is based on data fit up to time `r`, a one-step lag that is not explicitly discussed or justified.
4.  **Presentation:** The presentation is substandard. The figures are referenced without any captions, forcing the reader to extract their meaning entirely from the main text. The reference list is exceptionally sloppy, with numerous entries missing critical information like year, publisher, or conference/journal volume (e.g., [1], [3], [6], [7], [10], [12]). This level of inattention to detail is unacceptable.

**Originality:**

The problem of learning dynamic graph structures is not new (as acknowledged by the authors' citation of [14, 11]). The main novelty lies in the specific online, frequentist heuristic proposed. The combination of weighted sufficient statistics, Mahalanobis distance for change detection, and Liptak's test is a plausible, if somewhat piecemeal, approach. However, the connection to prior work on online learning could be stronger. The critique of Bayesian CPD methods for storing substantial data, for instance, ignores more modern, memory-efficient streaming Bayesian algorithms.

**Significance:**

The potential significance is high, as a robust online structure learning algorithm would have wide applicability. Unfortunately, the work's technical and evaluative weaknesses prevent it from being a solid foundation for future research in its current state.

1.  **Evaluation Flaws:** The experimental evaluation is not convincing. The third simulation, which is arguably the most important as it tests the proposed probabilistic relearning mechanism, is conducted on a *single*, randomly generated 10,000-point dataset. Running the algorithm 500 times on this one dataset only characterizes the algorithm's stochasticity on that specific data path, not its general performance. A proper evaluation would require averaging results over hundreds of different generated datasets.
2.  **Arbitrary Choices:** The fixed relearning schedule used for the direct comparison with PC (footnote 6) seems arbitrary and is suspiciously dense immediately following the changepoint, which could artificially inflate LoSST’s comparative performance.
3.  **Reproducibility:** The combination of heuristic components, underspecified derivations, and vaguely justified parameter choices makes the work difficult to reproduce, analyze, or build upon with confidence.

### Conclusion

While the paper tackles a valuable problem with an interesting high-level idea, the execution is critically flawed. The manuscript is replete with methodological gaps, insufficient theoretical justification, careless technical errors, and a weak experimental design. The poor presentation, particularly the incomplete references and missing figure captions, further detracts from the work. The accumulation of these issues, both major and minor, overshadows the potential contributions and prevents a positive recommendation. The paper requires a substantial revision to address the lack of rigor in its methodology, theory, and evaluation before it can be considered a sound contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:** Reject.
