## Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning graphical model structure in non-stationary environments, where the underlying data generating process changes over time. The authors identify a gap in existing literature – the lack of online algorithms capable of tracking these changes, particularly when data is limited. They propose LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a heuristic algorithm that dynamically updates sufficient statistics and relearns the graphical model structure as needed. LoSST aims to achieve performance comparable to batch methods during stable periods, while effectively adapting to structural shifts.  The paper includes simulation results demonstrating LoSST's ability to track changes in both graph structure and parameters, and a preliminary application to US price index data.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important and under-explored problem:** Learning in non-stationary environments is crucial for many real-world applications, and this paper directly addresses this need within the context of graphical models.
* **Practical Algorithm:** LoSST appears to be a reasonable and potentially effective heuristic. The focus on tracking sufficient statistics online is a sensible approach for handling data streams.
* **Theoretical Discussion of Trade-offs:** The analysis of convergence vs. diligence is a valuable addition, highlighting the inherent challenges in designing algorithms for dynamic environments. The discussion of parameter tuning (γ and T) and their impact on behavior is insightful.
* **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The simulation results are well-designed and demonstrate that LoSST performs comparably to batch methods during stable periods and outperforms them after structure changes. The comparison with the PC algorithm is helpful.
* **Real-World Application:** The application to US price index data provides a preliminary demonstration of the algorithm's potential in a real-world setting.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and the algorithm is presented in a clear and understandable manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Heuristic Nature:** The algorithm is explicitly described as a heuristic, and lacks strong theoretical guarantees beyond the convergence/diligence discussion. This is acknowledged by the authors, but it limits the paper’s impact.  A more rigorous analysis of the algorithm’s properties (even under simplifying assumptions) would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of LoSST likely depends heavily on the choice of parameters (ν, γ, T, and the Mahalanobis distance threshold). While the authors discuss these parameters, a more systematic sensitivity analysis would strengthen the results.
* **Limited Scope of Simulations:** While the simulations are well-executed, they are somewhat limited in their scope.  Exploring a wider range of graph structures, change frequencies, and noise levels would be valuable.  Specifically, the simulations focus on relatively simple changes – a more complex scenario with multiple simultaneous changes could be explored.
* **Real-World Application is Exploratory:** The application to the US price index data is presented as exploratory, and the interpretations are tentative. More in-depth analysis and comparison with alternative methods would be needed to establish the practical benefits of LoSST in this domain.
* **Mahalanobis Distance:** The use of Mahalanobis distance as a change detection mechanism implicitly assumes multivariate Gaussian data. The paper acknowledges this, but the impact of deviations from this assumption is not fully addressed.

**Quality:** 7/10. The paper is technically sound and presents a well-motivated and reasonably implemented algorithm. However, the heuristic nature and lack of strong theoretical guarantees detract from its overall quality.

**Clarity:** 8/10. The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The algorithm is clearly explained, and the figures are helpful.

**Originality:** 7/10.  The combination of online sufficient statistic tracking with dynamic relearning of graphical model structure is novel. However, the individual components (online learning, graphical model structure learning) are well-established.

**Significance:** 7/10. The problem addressed is significant, and the proposed algorithm has the potential to be useful in a variety of applications. However, the practical impact will depend on the algorithm’s performance in more complex real-world scenarios.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important open problem in graphical model learning.
* Proposes a practical and intuitive algorithm (LoSST).
* Presents solid simulation results demonstrating the algorithm's effectiveness.
* Offers a thoughtful discussion of the trade-offs between convergence and diligence.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The algorithm is a heuristic and lacks strong theoretical guarantees.
* The parameter sensitivity and limited scope of simulations raise concerns about generalizability.
* The real-world application is preliminary and exploratory.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of graphical model learning, particularly for applications where the underlying data generating process is non-stationary. The authors have identified an important problem, proposed a reasonable solution, and provided compelling empirical evidence to support their claims.  However, the paper would be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I recommend:

*  A more thorough sensitivity analysis of the algorithm's parameters.
*  Expanding the simulation experiments to include more complex scenarios and a wider range of graph structures.
*  Adding a discussion of the limitations of the Mahalanobis distance assumption and potential alternatives.
*  Exploring the possibility of incorporating prior knowledge or constraints into the learning process.
