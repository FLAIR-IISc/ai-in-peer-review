### Summary

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models in an online, single-pass fashion from data streams where the underlying generating process may change over time. The algorithm assumes the data generating process is "locally stationary" and is specifically implemented for recursive Structural Equation Models (SEMs) over continuous variables. The core of LoSST is a mechanism for tracking weighted sufficient statistics—specifically the mean, covariance matrix, and an "effective" sample size. At each timestep, a new data point is weighted based on its fit to the current estimated model, as measured by its Mahalanobis distance. A sequence of poor fits, aggregated using a weighted pooled p-value (Liptak's method), signals a potential change in the underlying structure. This triggers an increase in the weights of recent data points, effectively "forgetting" older, potentially irrelevant data. The updated sufficient statistics are then used to learn a new graphical model structure, with the relearning process itself triggered probabilistically based on the same change-detection signal.

The work is situated in the context of graphical model learning, correctly noting that standard batch-mode algorithms (both score-based and constraint-based) assume i.i.d. data and are thus unsuited for time-varying structures. It also distinguishes itself from online parameter estimation methods (like TD-learning) that do not learn structure, and from changepoint detection (CPD) algorithms that typically require storing significant data history and assume abrupt changes. The approach is presented as a frequentist alternative to prior Bayesian batch-mode methods for time-indexed graphs, such as Siracusa & Fisher (2009).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation of the online update rules for the weighted mean and covariance (Equations 1 and 2) is sound. The use of Mahalanobis distance as a measure of fit for multivariate Gaussian data is standard and appropriate. The core idea of using a pooled p-value to distinguish persistent change from isolated outliers is a principled and intelligent heuristic. The authors provide a clear theoretical analysis of the algorithm's long-run properties, formally connecting hyperparameter choices (γ, T) to the trade-off between *convergence* (stability in a stationary environment) and *diligence* (rapid detection of change). The inclusion of proof sketches for these properties adds significant technical depth.

The experimental evaluation on synthetic data is thorough. By testing scenarios with both structure+parameter changes and parameter-only changes, the authors provide a nuanced view of the algorithm's performance. The results convincingly demonstrate that LoSST successfully adapts to changepoints while performing comparably to a standard batch-mode algorithm (the PC algorithm) in stable periods, incurring little to no cost for its online capability. The application to US price index data provides a plausible real-world demonstration, correctly identifying a known period of macroeconomic change (the "Great Moderation").

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is established effectively in the introduction, and the related work section precisely positions the contribution. The LoSST algorithm is described step-by-step with all necessary equations. The simulation setup and results are presented with clear figures and explanations. The paper is written at a level that should make it accessible and reproducible for an expert in the field.

**Originality:** The paper's contribution is highly original. While its components are based on existing statistical tools (Mahalanobis distance, Liptak's method, PC algorithm), their synthesis into a single-pass, online algorithm for *structure* learning in non-stationary environments is novel. To my knowledge, this is one of the first works to present a complete, functional algorithm for this specific and important problem. It effectively bridges the gap between static structure learning and online time-series analysis. The formalization of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off in this context is also a novel contribution.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is of high significance. The i.i.d. assumption underlying most machine learning is a major limitation, and methods that can handle non-stationary environments are crucial for real-world applications in fields like finance, econometrics, climate science, and neuroscience. LoSST provides a practical and effective tool for these domains. Its ability to operate in a single pass on streaming data makes it suitable for applications where batch processing is infeasible. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by other researchers working on learning from dynamic systems.

---

Despite these strengths, the paper has a few weaknesses that could be addressed:

1.  **Heuristic Components and Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Several key design choices are heuristic. The specific functional form for updating the weight `a_r+1` (Equation 4) and the probabilistic relearning schedule are well-motivated but ultimately ad-hoc. The paper would be stronger with a sensitivity analysis of the key hyperparameters (γ, T, ν), as the performance seems to depend on "unsystematic investigation" to find good values.

2.  **Baselines in Experiments:** The primary baseline is the batch-mode PC algorithm applied to the entire dataset. While this effectively demonstrates the failure of i.i.d. assumptions, it is a somewhat weak baseline. A more competitive and common heuristic for non-stationary data is a *sliding window* approach (e.g., running PC on the last *N* data points). A comparison against such a baseline would provide a more compelling case for LoSST's superiority.

3.  **Evaluation on Gradual Change:** The simulations focus exclusively on abrupt changepoints. A key motivation for the proposed method over standard CPD is its potential to handle slow, continuous drift. However, this capability is not empirically demonstrated. Adding a simulation with a gradually evolving structure would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

4.  **Computational Complexity:** The algorithm requires computing the inverse of the covariance matrix at each time step to find the Mahalanobis distance. This is an O(V³) operation, where V is the number of variables. This cost is not discussed but could render the algorithm impractical for high-dimensional problems (V > 100), limiting its applicability. A discussion of this limitation and potential approximations would be welcome.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Novelty and Significance:** Addresses the important and challenging problem of online graphical model structure learning in non-stationary environments with a novel and practical algorithm.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The algorithm is built on solid statistical principles, and the paper includes a valuable theoretical analysis of its asymptotic properties (convergence vs. diligence).
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The synthetic experiments are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate the algorithm's ability to track changes while matching batch performance in stable periods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Limited Baselines:** The experimental comparison is made against a naive batch-mode baseline, omitting more relevant baselines like a sliding-window approach.
*   **Heuristic Nature:** Some core components of the algorithm are heuristic, and the paper lacks a systematic analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   **Computational Scalability:** The computational cost per time step is not addressed and appears to scale cubically with the number of variables, which could be a major practical limitation.

In conclusion, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty and the importance of the problem it addresses, strongly outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a complete and well-evaluated contribution that is likely to have a significant impact on the field.
