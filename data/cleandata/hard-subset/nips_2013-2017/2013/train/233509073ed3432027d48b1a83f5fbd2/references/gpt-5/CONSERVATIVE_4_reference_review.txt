Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic online method for tracking time-varying graphical structure in linear-Gaussian SEMs (DAGs). The key idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) with adaptive weights ar that increase when recent data look unlikely under the current model. Unlikeliness is judged via Mahalanobis distances combined over time using Liptak’s pooled p-value. The resulting pooled p-value also modulates a probabilistic trigger to relearn the graph structure using the PC algorithm. The authors discuss parameter regimes trading off “convergence” versus “diligence” (fast change detection) and present simulations comparing LoSST to batch PC, and a small real-data case (US CPI components).

Relation to prior work: The paper contrasts LoSST with batch structure learning, TD learning, online changepoint detection (BOCPD and frequentist CPD), and two batch methods for time-varying graphs. It positions LoSST as an online, general DAG structure learner that relies only on local stationarity and sufficient-statistics updates.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and challenging problem (online learning of changing dependency structure) that standard batch learners handle poorly.
- Simple, computationally light tracking of first- and second-order moments; single-pass memory footprint for sufficient statistics is attractive.
- The convergence/diligence lens is a useful way to conceptualize tradeoffs in online learning with structural change.
- Synthetic experiments convincingly show that batch PC can fail post-change, while a forgetting/weighting mechanism can adapt.
- The real-data case illustrates that the pooled p-value scheme is less sensitive to isolated outliers than raw Mahalanobis distances.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Validity of p-values and pooling: Successive Mahalanobis distances (and their p-values) are highly dependent because they share estimated moments; Liptak’s method assumes independence or at least weak dependence. No adjustment or justification is provided. This undermines the interpretability of ρr as a well-calibrated signal.
- Hotelling’s T2 under weighted samples: The mapping from Mahalanobis distances to T2 with df depending on an “effective” sample size Sr seems ad hoc. For adaptively weighted, non-i.i.d. samples, the T2 reference distribution and degrees of freedom are not standard. Using Sr as N is not theoretically justified.
- Use of PC under non-i.i.d. weighted data: Conditional independence tests (e.g., Fisher’s z) assume i.i.d. sampling and known sample size. How are these tests adapted to weighted samples and to Sr? What are the Type I/II error implications?
- Theoretical claims are mostly informal. Proof sketches contain unclear or likely incorrect algebra (e.g., the inequality in the diligence claim for T>1; notation like γ − γ−1 T). The sufficient conditions for convergence/diligence rely on the behavior of ρr but do not account for the invalid calibration issues above.
- Change detection literature: The dismissal that BOCPD “stores substantial portions of the input” is overstated; BOCPD maintains sufficient statistics for conjugate-exponential models. There are also well-studied adaptive forgetting/GLR/CUSUM/EWMA methods with theoretical guarantees that are close in spirit to LoSST’s goals.
- Numerical issues: Maintaining Cr and its inverse online with adaptive weights can yield ill-conditioned or non-PD matrices when Sr is small; no regularization or stabilization is discussed.

Experimental evaluation
- Baselines are insufficient. The only baseline is batch PC on the full mixture—an obviously disadvantaged setting post-change. Missing comparisons include: (i) sliding window PC; (ii) exponential forgetting with a fixed α; (iii) CPD + restart PC; (iv) online sparse precision estimation with time-varying regularization; (v) dynamic/temporal graphical models (e.g., time-varying graphical lasso, dynamic Bayesian networks).
- Hyperparameter sensitivity is largely unexplored. T, γ, and ν are chosen “unsystematically,” and there is no robustness analysis. The convergence/diligence discussion suggests behavior is highly sensitive to these choices.
- The probabilistic relearning experiment uses a single dataset with repeated runs, limiting generality. There is no cost–accuracy analysis; PC is expensive and frequent relearning could be prohibitive.
- Real-data case is anecdotal; no ground truth and no comparison to established volatility/change detectors. The interpretation (Great Moderation) is plausible but unvalidated.

Clarity
- Generally readable, but some derivations and definitions are terse or ambiguous (e.g., exact CI tests used within PC under weighting; effective sample size update rationale; proofs and inequalities).
- Important implementation details (maintaining Cr inverse, regularization, CI-test calibration with weights) are missing. Reproducibility would be challenging without code.

Originality
- The combination of adaptive weighting (based on pooled improbability) with online sufficient-statistics tracking and occasional relearning is a reasonable heuristic. However, the components (EWMA/forgetting, change detection, periodic relearning) are well known; the novelty lies in a particular pooling/weighting scheme. The paper does not engage with a broader body of time-varying graphical modeling that targets similar goals.

Significance
- The problem is significant, but the heuristic nature, shaky statistical calibration, and limited evaluation reduce confidence that this will be a reliable tool. Without stronger theory or comprehensive baselines, impact is uncertain.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide a principled treatment (or at least empirical calibration) of the distribution of Dr and ρr under adaptive weighting; quantify false alarm and detection delay tradeoffs.
- Explain how PC’s CI tests are modified for weighted samples and small effective sample sizes; report empirical Type I/II error under weighting.
- Add baselines: sliding window PC, fixed forgetting factor, CPD+restart, and time-varying graphical models (e.g., Kolar/Xing, Zhou/Lafferty/Wasserman). Include wall-clock cost vs. accuracy.
- Study sensitivity to T, γ, ν and offer guidance for setting them.
- Clarify and correct the convergence/diligence results; provide formal statements with correct conditions and proofs.
- Discuss numerical stabilization (e.g., shrinkage of Cr) and complexity of maintaining inverses.

Pros
- Addresses an important problem; simple online sufficient-statistics updates.
- Intuitive mechanism to modulate forgetting and relearning frequency.
- Synthetic results show clear failure modes of batch PC post-change.

Cons
- Heuristic core with questionable statistical calibration.
- Limited theory and ambiguities in proofs/assumptions.
- Weak baselines and limited empirical scope; sensitivity unexplored.
- Practical implementation details and reproducibility gaps.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The problem is important and the approach is interesting, but the paper relies on unvalidated statistical assumptions and provides insufficient comparisons and theoretical support. With stronger calibration/theory, rigorous baselines, and clearer methodology, this could become a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
