### Paper Summary
This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from streaming data where the underlying data-generating process may change over time. The core idea is to move away from the standard i.i.d. assumption and instead assume local stationarity. LoSST works by maintaining and updating weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, effective sample size) in an online fashion. To detect changes, it uses the Mahalanobis distance of incoming data points from the current estimated model. Rather than reacting to single outliers, it uses a pooled p-value over a sequence of these distances to identify sustained shifts. A poor fit (low pooled p-value) increases the weight of recent data, effectively allowing the algorithm to "forget" the past and adapt to the new structure. The full graphical model is then relearned probabilistically, with relearning becoming more frequent following a detected change. The authors also provide an insightful theoretical framing, discussing the tradeoff between "convergence" (stabilizing on a fixed structure) and "diligence" (quickly detecting change) and show how LoSST can be parameterized for either property. The algorithm's effectiveness is demonstrated through simulations comparing it to a batch-mode PC algorithm and on a real-world dataset of U.S. price indices.

### Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)
1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a problem of fundamental importance that is often overlooked in standard structure learning literature: how to learn graphical structures when they are not static. The proposed approach is genuinely novel. While components like online parameter updating or changepoint detection exist, their synthesis into a coherent framework for *online structure learning* is a significant and bold contribution. This work moves beyond incremental improvements to existing batch algorithms and proposes a new way of thinking about the problem, which could open up a new research direction.

2.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Heuristics:** The core mechanism of the LoSST algorithm is clever and intuitively appealing. Using the Mahalanobis distance to measure "surprise" is a standard and sound choice for multivariate Gaussian data. The key innovation—using a *pooled* p-value (Liptak's method) to distinguish sustained change from isolated outliers—is an elegant solution to a critical problem in online learning. This prevents the algorithm from being overly sensitive while still allowing it to react to genuine shifts, as demonstrated in the real-world price index example.

3.  **Sophisticated Theoretical Grounding:** The discussion of the provably incompatible properties of convergence and diligence is a major strength. It demonstrates a deep understanding of the fundamental challenges of learning in non-stationary environments. The fact that the authors can show that LoSST can be parameterized to be provably one or the other (or a blend of the two) elevates this work from a simple collection of heuristics to a more principled framework. This is a valuable conceptual contribution in its own right.

4.  **Strong and Clear Empirical Validation:** The simulation results are compelling. The experiments are well-designed to show that LoSST incurs no performance penalty in stable environments (where it performs identically to the batch PC algorithm) but offers a dramatic improvement after a changepoint. Separating the cases of structure change vs. parameter change was a good choice, as it highlights different performance characteristics. The application to real-world economic data, where LoSST identifies the well-known "Great Moderation," provides a convincing demonstration of its potential utility.

### Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance)
1.  **Ad-hoc Parameterization:** Several key components of the algorithm rely on heuristic choices and manually set parameters. For example, the weight update function in Equation 4, which links the pooled p-value `ρ` to the new weight `a`, has a specific functional form that is not deeply justified beyond being intuitively plausible. Similarly, the probabilistic relearning schedule depends on a parameter `ν`. The paper provides little guidance on how to set these parameters (`T`, `γ`, `ν`) in a principled way for a new problem, and their sensitivity is not thoroughly explored. While the core idea is strong, its practical implementation feels somewhat like an art.

2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The experiments are convincing for the scenarios tested (abrupt, single changepoints), but the paper itself notes that LoSST would perform poorly with very rapid or continuous, slow-drifting changes. An exploration of these failure modes, even a preliminary one, would have strengthened the paper by better characterizing the algorithm's operational boundaries.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The per-datapoint update requires calculating the Mahalanobis distance, which involves inverting the current covariance matrix (`Cr`). For a model with `V` variables, this is an O(V³) operation at each time step. While perhaps feasible for the small number of variables tested (up to 20), this could become a significant bottleneck in high-dimensional settings, which are common target domains for graphical models. A discussion of this computational limitation and potential approximations would be beneficial.

### Recommendation
**Accept.**

This paper presents a bold and highly novel approach to a difficult and important problem. The core idea of an online structure learner that dynamically weights data based on a principled measure of model fit is a significant conceptual advance. While some of the implementation details are heuristic and the validation could be more extensive, these are minor flaws in the context of the new direction this paper charts. I value the novelty of the core idea and the clarity of the problem formulation far more than these methodological imperfections. The theoretical discussion around convergence versus diligence is particularly insightful and adds significant depth. This work is likely to inspire follow-up research and provides a strong foundation for future developments in learning from non-stationary data streams. It is an excellent fit for NIPS.
