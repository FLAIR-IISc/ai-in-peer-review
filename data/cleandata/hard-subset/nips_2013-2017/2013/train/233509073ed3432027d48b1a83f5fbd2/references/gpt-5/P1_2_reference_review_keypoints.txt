- Summary: Proposes LoSST, an online method that maintains adaptively weighted sufficient statistics and uses pooled Mahalanobis-distance p-values (Lipták) to trigger intermittent PC-based structure relearning for time-varying linear-Gaussian DAGs/SEMs.  
- Summary (cont.): Analyzes trade-offs between “convergence” and “diligence” via T and γ, provides proof sketches for some regimes, and evaluates on synthetic changes and a CPI volatility case study.  
- Relation to prior work: Positions versus batch structure learning and online CPD but omits close connections to adaptive forgetting/EWMA/CUSUM, recursive least squares, dynamic BNs, and time-varying graphical models.  
- Relation to prior work (shortfall): Lacks explicit comparisons to sliding-window or exponentially-weighted baselines and recent time-varying graph methods.  
- Strength: Addresses an important, timely problem of online causal/graphical structure tracking.  
- Strength: Memory-efficient single-pass sufficient-statistic tracking and probabilistic relearning to control compute.  
- Strength: Conceptually simple and implementable—adaptive weighting + off-the-shelf PC is appealing.  
- Strength: Synthetic experiments show faster recovery after changes than batch PC; CPI case study is an interesting demonstration.  
- Strength: Clear discussion of the convergence vs. diligence tension and how parameters influence behavior.  
- Weakness (statistical): Mahalanobis p-values and Lipták pooling are likely miscalibrated under adaptive weighting and dependence, risking false alarms or missed detections.  
- Weakness (numerical): Inverting Cr online is unstable when effective sample size Sr is small; regularization and inverse-update strategies are not discussed.  
- Weakness (interface to PC): Using weighted covariances and an “effective” Sr within PC is heuristic and lacks justification for CI test error rates.  
- Weakness (theory): Proof sketches lack formal guarantees on structure recovery, detection delay, or false alarm control; T>1 lacks p-value interpretation.  
- Weakness (experiments): Missing fair baselines (sliding-window, EWMA, CPD+PC, time-varying GGMs), limited sensitivity analyses, and parameters selected unsystematically.  
- Weakness (real data): CPI analysis is anecdotal without external validation or before/after graph comparisons.  
- Clarity issues: Need pseudocode, explicit parameter definitions, clearer Sr update intuition, cleaned references/notation, and per-step/run-time complexity reporting.  
- Originality: The specific combination appears novel, but the manuscript should better situate itself within adaptive-forgetting and time-varying graph literatures.  
- Significance: Potentially useful engineering solution, but impact is limited by uncalibrated detection, heuristic PC coupling, and weak baselines/theory.  
- Pros for acceptance: Important problem, practical and memory-light method, simple implementation, promising synthetic and real-data demonstrations.  
- Cons for acceptance: Uncalibrated detection/pooling, heuristic CI testing, missing strong baselines, limited theoretical guarantees, and unaddressed numerical issues.  
- Key suggestions: Compare to sliding-window/EWMA/BOCPD+PC/time-varying GGM baselines; calibrate detection via EWMA/CUSUM/bootstrap; run ablations and sensitivity studies for T, γ, ν.  
- Key suggestions (implementation): Add regularization and Sherman-Morrison-style inverse updates, report runtimes, and validate CI tests under weighting or adopt tests that accept weighted covariances/df.  
- Overall recommendation: Borderline reject; encourage resubmission after addressing calibration, baselines, theoretical guarantees, numerical stability, and clarity.
