- Addresses online structure learning for time-varying graphical models and proposes LoSST to track locally stationary sufficient statistics.  
- LoSST detects structural changes using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values and adapts learning online.  

Strengths:
- Clearly motivated problem with real-world relevance (finance, neuroscience, causal inference).  
- Novel use of Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values with theoretical analysis of convergence vs. diligence (Theorem 3.1).  
- Comprehensive evaluation: synthetic scenarios and a real-world US price index application, comparing to batch PC algorithm.  
- Good balance of theory and practical implementation, including probabilistic relearning mechanism.  
- Clear, well-organized exposition enabling reproducibility.  

Weaknesses:
- Relies on Mahalanobis distance and assumes Gaussianity; not addressed for discrete or non-Gaussian data.  
- Key parameters (T = 0.05, γ = 3) appear arbitrary without systematic sensitivity analysis.  
- Scalability not convincingly demonstrated; experiments only up to 20 variables.  
- Limited comparison to recent time-varying graphical model methods (e.g., recent NIPS work).  
- Real-world demonstration is small-scale (6 variables), limiting evidence of practical utility on larger problems.  

Quality and significance:
- Technically sound with strong theoretical and empirical support; fills an important gap in online structure learning.  
- High significance: maintains performance during stable periods and adapts after structural changes, offering practical value.  

Recommendation:
- Strong accept: novel, well-grounded algorithm with thorough evaluation and clear contribution to the field.  
- Concerns against acceptance: Gaussian assumption, parameter justification, and limited scalability evidence.  

Minor suggestions:
- Discuss extensions to non-Gaussian data (alternative distance measures).  
- Add sensitivity analysis for key parameters T and γ.  
- Compare with recent NIPS papers on time-varying graphical models.  
- Include discussion of computational complexity to clarify scalability.
