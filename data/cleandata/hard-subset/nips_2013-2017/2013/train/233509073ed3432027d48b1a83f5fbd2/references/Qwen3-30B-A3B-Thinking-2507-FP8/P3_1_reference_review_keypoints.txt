- Addresses the missing capability of online learning for time-varying graphical model structure.  
- Proposes LoSST, an online algorithm that tracks sufficient statistics and dynamically weights data using Mahalanobis distances.  
- Introduces pooled p-values and probabilistic relearning to balance convergence (stability) and diligence (responsiveness).  
- Situates work relative to Bayesian/score-based, constraint-based, online learning, change-point detection, and prior batch time-varying methods.  
- Theoretical analysis provides proofs of convergence and diligence trade-offs under specific parameter settings.  
- Innovative algorithm design, notably the use of Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values for dynamic weighting.  
- Comprehensive simulation experiments demonstrate effectiveness in both stable and changing regimes.  
- Real-world application to US price index volatility shows practical utility.  
- Scope limited to SEMs with continuous variables, excluding discrete graphical models.  
- No theoretical guarantees on convergence rate after changes or probability of correctly identifying change points.  
- Parameter sensitivity analysis is superficial and not systematically explored for T, γ, ν.  
- Computational complexity analysis is limited; full algorithm cost not detailed despite graph learning being expensive.  
- Comparisons limited primarily to the PC algorithm, lacking benchmarks against recent online/time-varying methods.  
- Some clarity issues: pooled p-value approach could be more intuitive for unfamiliar readers.  
- Probabilistic relearning is mathematically described but lacks intuitive justification for the chosen formulation.  
- Relationship among Mahalanobis distance, pooled p-value, and weight adjustment could be more explicitly explained.  
- Novel contribution: combination of weighted sufficient statistics, Mahalanobis distances, pooled p-values, and probabilistic relearning is original.  
- High significance due to addressing a fundamental limitation with broad practical applications (finance, social networks, biology).  
- Demonstrates no performance penalty in stable regimes while improving adaptability when structures change.  
- Overall assessment: strong, well-designed, theoretically sound, and empirically validated paper recommended for acceptance.  
- Suggestions: provide detailed parameter sensitivity analysis, discuss extensions to other graphical model types, compare to more recent methods, and give more intuitive explanations of pooled p-values and relearning.
