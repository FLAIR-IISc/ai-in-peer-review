Summary
The paper addresses online learning of time-varying graphical structure for linear-Gaussian SEMs. The proposed Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, “effective” sample size) using per-datapoint weights that adapt to model misfit. Misfit is quantified via Mahalanobis distances converted to p-values (Hotelling’s T^2), which are pooled across time using a weighted Lipták method; low pooled p-values increase the forgetting factor ar and raise the probability of triggering structure relearning with the PC algorithm. The authors discuss a trade-off between convergence and “diligence” (fast reaction to changes) via parameters T and γ, and provide simulation studies with abrupt changes in SEM structure/parameters and a brief CPI case study.

Strengths
- Tackles an important and underexplored problem: online tracking of changing causal/graphical structure with single-pass memory.
- Attractive engineering: simple, low-memory sufficient-statistics updates; can interface with different structure learners.
- Empirical results show plausible behavior: indistinguishable from batch PC in stationary periods and faster adaptation than batch PC after abrupt changes.
- The “effective sample size” interpretation and separation of change-detection vs. relearning are intuitively appealing.

Weaknesses
- Statistical validity of the core detection/weighting pipeline is not established. Specifically:
  - The use of Hotelling’s T^2 for Mahalanobis distances based on a weighted covariance estimator with an “effective” sample size Sr is not justified; the reference distribution and degrees of freedom under weighting/forgetting are nonstandard.
  - The pooled p-values via Lipták assume independence (or require dependence-robust calibration), which is violated here (overlapping data, time-varying parameters).
  - Mapping pooled p-values to weights (Eq. 4) and to relearning probabilities is ad hoc, with no control of false-alarm rates or detection delay.
- Theoretical claims are weak. Convergence/diligence statements are informal, with proof sketches relying on threshold sequences and properties of pooled p-values; there are no consistency or error-rate guarantees for structure recovery under local stationarity.
- Experimental validation is limited and does not use appropriate baselines:
  - Only vanilla batch PC is compared. Missing comparisons to more realistic online/streaming baselines: sliding-window PC, exponentially-weighted covariance PC, BOCPD or GLR-based changepoint detection coupled with restart, dynamic Bayesian networks, or time-varying Gaussian graphical model methods (e.g., time-varying graphical lasso).
  - No ablations on T, γ, ν or on pooling methods; sensitivity and robustness (e.g., heavy tails/outliers) are not studied.
  - No runtime/memory profiling or scalability experiments (n, degree, conditioning set sizes).
- Methodological details are under-specified:
  - How are CI tests in PC calibrated with weighted data? Replacing N by Sr in Fisher Z tests is not justified.
  - The distributional assumptions used to compute p-values change over time; how is Type I error controlled?
- Real-data case study is anecdotal. There is no ground truth, no comparison to standard changepoint detectors on macro series, and no assessment of predictive performance or stability of learned structure.

Quality
The paper is technically ambitious but presently heuristic. The most central statistical pieces (detection statistic distribution under weighting, pooled p-value validity, CI testing with Sr) are not established, making the resulting procedure difficult to trust. Experimental evidence is suggestive but not sufficient given the scope of claims, and the absence of competitive baselines is a serious deficiency.

Clarity
Generally readable and well-organized. However, key choices are insufficiently justified. Provide precise pseudocode; detail how weighted statistics interface with PC’s tests; clarify which data are used in Dr+1 (pre- or post-update). Figures are referred to but not shown here; ensure they report variance/error bars and not only means.

Originality
The combination—adaptive forgetting driven by pooled misfit statistics plus periodic constraint-based relearning—is a novel synthesis. However, prior related literatures (dynamic BNs, time-varying GGMs/precision tracking, sliding-window CI testing, BOCPD+restart) are not adequately engaged and compared against.

Significance
The problem is significant, and a robust solution would be impactful. As it stands, the lack of rigorous foundations and limited empirical validation against established streaming baselines make it premature for publication.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a principled detection/forgetting mechanism with statistical guarantees. Options: GLR/CUSUM/EWMA detectors with known false-alarm control; or BOCPD-style run-length posteriors with tractable approximations. Derive how forgetting affects the null distribution of the misfit statistic.
- Justify CI testing with weighted data: derive or approximate the sampling distribution for partial correlations under exponential weighting; otherwise use resampling/online calibration to control Type I error.
- Substantially expand experiments:
  - Baselines: sliding-window PC and GES, exponentially-weighted versions, BOCPD+restart PC, dynamic BN structure learners, time-varying graphical lasso (for undirected comparisons).
  - Ablations over T, γ, ν and pooling alternatives; robustness to outliers/heavy tails; gradual vs abrupt drift; intermittent regimes; latent confounding.
  - Scalability and wall-clock profiling.
- Provide stronger theory: conditions under which LoSST recovers the correct CPDAG in locally stationary segments; bounds on detection delay vs. false-alarm rate; impact of relearning frequency on error.
- Strengthen the real-data study with quantitative evaluation: alignment with known macro regime changes using standard CPD benchmarks, predictive performance, and stability analyses.

Pros
- Important problem; online, memory-light approach.
- Simple and potentially practical update rules.
- Promising initial empirical behavior.

Cons
- Core statistical assumptions not justified; heuristic mapping to weights/relearning.
- Weak theory; no finite-sample guarantees.
- Missing strong baselines and ablations; limited experiments.
- Unclear calibration of CI tests with weighted data.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper tackles a valuable problem with an appealing high-level idea, but the current version lacks the methodological rigor and comparative evidence necessary to support its claims. With a principled detection/calibration framework and comprehensive comparisons, it could become a strong contribution. Confidence: high.
