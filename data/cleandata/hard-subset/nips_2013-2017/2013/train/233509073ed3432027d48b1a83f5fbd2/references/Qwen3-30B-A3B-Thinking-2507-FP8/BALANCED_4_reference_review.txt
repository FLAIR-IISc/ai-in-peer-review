

# Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

## Summary
This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm for online learning of graphical model structure in settings where the underlying structure may change over time. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: current graphical model structure learning algorithms assume stationary data (i.i.d.), while real-world applications often involve dynamically changing structures. LoSST tracks weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values to detect structure changes, then dynamically relearns the graphical model. The paper demonstrates that LoSST performs comparably to batch-mode structure learning when structure is stable, but significantly outperforms batch methods when structure changes, as shown in synthetic experiments and a real-world application to US price index volatility.

## Strengths
1. **Problem Significance**: The paper identifies a genuinely important problem in graphical models that has received insufficient attention in the literature. Real-world applications (e.g., financial markets, biological systems) often involve time-varying structures, making this work highly relevant.

2. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical basis for the algorithm, including proofs of convergence and diligence properties under specific parameter settings. The conceptual distinction between convergence and diligence is particularly insightful.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments with synthetic data across multiple parameter settings, showing clear advantages of LoSST over batch methods after structure changes. The real-world application to US price index volatility is well-motivated and demonstrates practical utility.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written, logically organized, and provides sufficient mathematical detail for reproducibility. The distinction between the algorithm's theoretical properties and practical implementation is particularly clear.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Theoretical Guarantees**: While the paper provides proofs for specific parameter regimes, it lacks general theoretical guarantees about the algorithm's performance. The reliance on Gaussian assumptions for the Mahalanobis distance limits applicability to non-Gaussian data, which is a significant constraint given the paper's claim of "general" graphical model structure learning.

2. **Ad Hoc Parameter Selection**: The choice of parameters T=0.05 and γ=3 appears arbitrary, with limited justification. The paper doesn't provide guidance on selecting these parameters for different datasets or problem domains, which would be crucial for practical adoption.

3. **Comparison with Alternatives**: The paper doesn't sufficiently compare LoSST with alternative online structure learning approaches. For instance, it doesn't evaluate against incremental learning methods that maintain a single graphical model and update it incrementally as new data arrives.

4. **Scalability Concerns**: The real-world application uses only 6 variables, which doesn't demonstrate the algorithm's scalability to larger problems. The paper mentions computational cost of structure learning but doesn't address how LoSST's performance scales with the number of variables.

5. **Lack of Discussion on Limitations**: The paper acknowledges limitations (rapidly changing structures, random-order mixtures), but doesn't quantify these limitations or provide guidance on when LoSST would fail.

## Recommendations for Improvement
1. **Expand Theoretical Analysis**: Provide more general guarantees about the algorithm's performance under different data distributions, not just Gaussian. Consider analyzing the impact of different Mahalanobis distance thresholds on performance.

2. **Parameter Sensitivity Analysis**: Include a thorough analysis of how parameter choices (T and γ) affect performance across different problem settings. Consider developing data-driven methods for selecting these parameters.

3. **Additional Experiments**: Add experiments on larger synthetic datasets to demonstrate scalability. Compare LoSST against incremental learning methods that might be more directly comparable.

4. **Non-Gaussian Extensions**: Discuss potential extensions to non-Gaussian data, perhaps by using alternative distance metrics or transformations.

5. **Clarify Scope**: Be more precise about the types of graphical models the algorithm can handle (e.g., specify which types of SEMs are supported beyond linear models with Gaussian noise).

## Final Assessment
This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in graphical models with a novel and well-justified approach. The theoretical foundation is solid, the empirical evaluation is comprehensive, and the practical application is compelling. The main weaknesses are the limited theoretical guarantees for non-Gaussian data and the ad hoc parameter selection. With some additional analysis and experiments addressing these limitations, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

**Quality**: 7/10 (Good technical foundation but limited theoretical guarantees for non-Gaussian data)
**Clarity**: 9/10 (Well-written and well-organized)
**Originality**: 8/10 (Novel algorithm with a useful conceptual distinction between convergence and diligence)
**Significance**: 8/10 (Addresses an important problem with clear practical relevance)

Overall, this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of graphical models and online learning, though it would benefit from addressing the limitations noted above. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
