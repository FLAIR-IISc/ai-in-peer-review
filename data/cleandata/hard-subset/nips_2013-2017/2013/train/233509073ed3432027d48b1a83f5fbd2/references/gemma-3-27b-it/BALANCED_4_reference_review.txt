This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), an online algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models when the underlying generative process changes over time. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics in an online fashion and dynamically relearn the graphical model structure as needed. The algorithm is designed to perform competitively with batch-mode learning when the structure is stable, and to adapt quickly to changes when they occur. The authors provide simulation results demonstrating the algorithm’s effectiveness and a case study applying it to US price index volatility.

The paper addresses a significant and practically relevant problem. Most existing graphical model structure learning algorithms assume a stationary underlying structure, which is often unrealistic in real-world applications. The ability to learn structure online and adapt to changes is a valuable contribution. The theoretical discussion connecting convergence, diligence, and the algorithm’s parameters (γ and T) is interesting and provides insights into the algorithm’s behavior. The authors thoughtfully discuss the trade-offs involved. The simulation results are convincing, demonstrating the algorithm's performance in various scenarios, including changes in both structure and parameters. The application to price index volatility is a good example of a real-world use case, and the observed results are plausible and well-explained.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The algorithm, as presented, relies on several parameters (α, γ, T, ν) that require tuning. While the authors provide some guidance on choosing these parameters, a more systematic exploration of their sensitivity and impact on performance would be beneficial. The computational complexity of the algorithm is not thoroughly discussed. Relearning the graph structure using the PC algorithm can be expensive, especially for high-dimensional data. A more detailed analysis of the algorithm's computational cost and scalability is needed. The description of the Mahalanobis distance update is somewhat terse. More details on the computational aspects of updating the covariance matrix would be helpful.  The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive. While the authors mention several relevant papers, there is limited discussion of more recent work on online structure learning or changepoint detection in graphical models.  For example, work on dynamic Bayesian networks or switching state-space models could be relevant. The connection between the proposed approach and existing methods for adaptive filtering or recursive estimation (beyond TD(0)) is not fully explored.  While the authors position their work as filling a gap, it would be strengthened by more explicitly drawing parallels or contrasts with these broader fields.  Finally, the notation could be more consistent. For instance, the subscripting of variables and time steps is sometimes ambiguous. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and under-explored problem in graphical model learning.
*   Presents a novel algorithm that combines online learning with structure learning.
*   Provides theoretical insights into the trade-offs between convergence and diligence.
*   Demonstrates promising empirical results through simulations and a real-world case study.
*   The algorithm is conceptually clear and reasonably well-explained.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The algorithm relies on several parameters that require tuning, and the sensitivity analysis is limited.
*   The computational complexity of the algorithm is not thoroughly discussed.
*   The related work section could be more comprehensive.
*   The connection to broader fields like adaptive filtering is not fully explored.
*   The notation could be more consistent.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a solid contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The LoSST algorithm is a promising approach for online structure learning in non-stationary environments. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I believe that with some revisions addressing the points mentioned above, this paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS. 

**Recommendation:**

Reject with request for revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more systematic sensitivity analysis of the algorithm's parameters.
2.  Discuss the computational complexity of the algorithm in more detail, and explore potential optimizations.
3.  Expand the related work section to include more recent and relevant publications.
4.  Explicitly connect the proposed approach to existing methods in adaptive filtering and recursive estimation.
5.  Improve the clarity and consistency of the notation.
