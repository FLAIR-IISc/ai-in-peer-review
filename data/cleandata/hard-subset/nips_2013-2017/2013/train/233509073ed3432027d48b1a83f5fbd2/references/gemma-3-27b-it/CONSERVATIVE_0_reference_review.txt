This paper proposes a heuristic algorithm, LoSST, for tracking time-varying graphical model structure in online settings. The core idea is to adapt sufficient statistics tracking techniques (mean, covariance, sample size) alongside a constraint-based structure learning algorithm (PC) to react to changes in the underlying generating process. The authors acknowledge the heuristic nature of their approach and present simulation results suggesting it performs favorably against a batch-mode baseline after a structural change, and indistinguishably from it during stable periods.

The paper clearly identifies an important and challenging problem: learning graphical model structure in non-stationary environments with limited data. The motivation is well-stated, and the connection to real-world applications is plausible. However, my overall assessment is skeptical. While the idea is conceptually reasonable, the presented work lacks the rigor and extensive validation expected for a NIPS publication, particularly given the heuristic nature of the proposed approach.

The primary weakness lies in the limited scope of the experimental validation. The simulations, while demonstrating *some* benefit after a structural change, are relatively simple and focus heavily on scenarios where the change is abrupt and complete. The paper acknowledges limitations regarding rapid changes or mixture models, but doesn't adequately address how the algorithm would perform in more realistic scenarios with gradual structural drift or partial changes. Furthermore, the choice of parameters (T, γ, ν) seems somewhat arbitrary, justified by “unsystematic investigation”. A more thorough parameter sensitivity analysis, and ideally a principled method for setting these parameters, is crucial. The comparison to the batch-mode PC algorithm is important, but the experimental setup—relearning the graph at fixed intervals—feels contrived and doesn’t fully capture the potential benefits or drawbacks of the online approach. 

The theoretical discussion around convergence vs. diligence is interesting, but ultimately feels disconnected from the practical implementation. The conditions for achieving each property seem quite specific and may not reflect the actual behavior of the algorithm in complex real-world datasets. The discussion of the trade-offs is appreciated, but further investigation into how these trade-offs manifest in practice would be beneficial.

The application to US price index volatility is suggestive, but hardly conclusive. It’s unclear whether the detected “changepoints” genuinely represent meaningful shifts in the underlying relationships between the price indices, or merely statistical fluctuations. A more rigorous evaluation, perhaps involving out-of-sample prediction or comparison with established economic models, would be necessary to validate the findings.

The paper’s strengths include a clear presentation of the algorithm and its underlying principles. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of relevant literature in graphical model learning and online changepoint detection. The adaptation of sufficient statistics tracking for online structure learning is a reasonable approach, and the consideration of both convergence and diligence is commendable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem.
*   Presents a conceptually reasonable approach that combines established techniques.
*   Demonstrates preliminary evidence of effectiveness in simulation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental validation, focusing on simplified scenarios.
*   Parameter settings appear arbitrary and lack principled justification.
*   Theoretical discussion feels disconnected from practical implementation.
*   Real-world application lacks rigorous validation.
*   Heuristic nature of the algorithm demands more extensive testing and analysis.

**Overall:**

I believe this paper is currently not suitable for NIPS. It presents a potentially interesting idea, but the lack of rigorous validation and the heuristic nature of the approach raise significant concerns. The authors need to substantially strengthen the experimental evaluation, provide a more principled justification for the algorithm’s parameters, and demonstrate the practical benefits of LoSST in more realistic scenarios. I would encourage the authors to consider submitting a revised version to a more specialized workshop or journal where a less stringent level of novelty may be expected. The work is a good starting point, but falls short of the standards expected at NIPS.
