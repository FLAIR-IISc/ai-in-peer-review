Summary
The paper addresses online structure learning for directed graphical models when the data-generating process can change over time. The proposed Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) with adaptive weights ar. Weights are increased when incoming data are unlikely under the current Gaussian model, assessed via Mahalanobis distances combined into a pooled p-value using Liptak’s method. Structure is relearned intermittently (using PC) with probability increasing as the pooled p-value drops. The paper discusses a convergence–diligence tradeoff, provides synthetic experiments with changepoints in parameters and structure, and a brief application to US CPI components.

Strengths
- Problem motivation is timely and important: online tracking of changing causal/graphical structure is under-explored in DAGs compared to undirected time-varying GGMs.
- The sufficient-statistic updates are memory- and pass-efficient (O(p^2) state, single pass), attractive for streams.
- The convergence vs. diligence framing is insightful, with some crisp parameter regimes yielding different qualitative behaviors.
- Synthetic experiments are reasonably thorough over multiple graph sizes/degrees and cover both structure and parameter changes; the probabilistic relearning schedule is a pragmatic idea.
- The method appears to reduce spurious edges after a structural change relative to a naive batch PC baseline.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Statistical calibration of the change signal is questionable. The paper assumes the Mahalanobis distance with covariance estimated from weighted, adaptively updated data follows Hotelling’s T^2 with degrees based on the “effective” sample size Sr. This is not justified. With adaptive (data-dependent) weights and non-i.i.d. history, the null distribution is not T^2, and even in the i.i.d. unweighted case, using S from prior data rather than a fixed covariance complicates calibration. The pooled p-values via Liptak also assume independent tests; here the sequence of distances is strongly dependent. The authors acknowledge intractability but still use p-values operationally; at minimum, empirical calibration (e.g., online permutation/bootstrap or simulation under stationarity) should be provided to show ρr’s behavior under the null.
- Effective sample size update Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1)Sr + 1 is ad hoc. For general weights, the standard “effective sample size” is (∑w)^2/∑w^2. The proposed recursion doesn’t correspond to that and lacks derivation or validation. This choice impacts CI test thresholds within PC and the T^2 degrees of freedom.
- Use of PC with weighted covariances: The CI tests in PC (partial correlations with Fisher’s z) assume i.i.d. Gaussian samples of size N. Feeding PC an adaptively weighted covariance and Sr as a pseudo-sample size lacks justification. What is the null distribution of partial correlations under these weights? How is type I error controlled? This is central to correctness.
- The “diligence” proof sketch relies on setting T>1, which forces ar to grow monotonically regardless of data (since ρ∈[0,1]). That implies perpetually shrinking effective sample size even during stationarity, potentially degrading accuracy. This seems a property of the schedule, not of detection power, and raises concerns about steady-state performance.
- Numerical stability and regularization are not discussed. Maintaining an invertible, positive-definite Cr with very small Sr or after abrupt weight changes is nontrivial; practical schemes (jitter, shrinkage, or Sherman–Morrison rank-1 updates to the inverse) should be specified.
- Experimental baselines are weak. Comparing LoSST to batch PC on the entire mixed dataset is unfair after a change. More appropriate baselines include: sliding-window PC; exponentially weighted PC with a fixed forgetting factor; CPD+relearn; time-varying graphical lasso and related undirected baselines (Kolar et al., Hallac et al., Zhou et al.) for Gaussian data; and change-adaptive BN/SEM learners. Sensitivity analyses of T, γ, ν are limited to a single unsystematic choice. No ablations (e.g., fixed forgetting vs. pooled p-values) are shown.
- Real-data case study is anecdotal. Claims about detecting the Great Moderation are plausible but unvalidated; no comparison to standard volatility-change tests (CUSUM/EWMA) or to domain baselines; learned graphs over time are not shown, so substantive interpretability is limited.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but there are typos and notational issues (e.g., p=N in the T^2 line; likely p=V). The paper would benefit from:
  - Clear pseudo-code for LoSST with all maintained state listed.
  - Explicit statement of how PC is modified to use weighted stats and Sr.
  - Complexity analysis: per-step O(p^2) updates, O(p^3) relearn cost; expected relearn frequency under ν, ρ dynamics.
  - Guidance on parameter selection and stability safeguards (regularization).
- The convergence/diligence section mixes proof sketches with informal claims; it would help to demarcate rigor vs. conjecture and to align definitions of “diligence” with standard change detection metrics (detection delay bounds).

Originality
- The combination of online adaptive weighting driven by pooled Mahalanobis p-values with intermittent constraint-based structure relearning is a novel synthesis for DAGs/SEMs. However, many components echo known ideas: EWMA/forgetting in SPC and RL; changepoint detection triggering relearns; time-varying graphical modeling. The paper’s novelty rests on the pooled p-value mechanism and the convergence–diligence framing.
- Related work is incomplete. Missing or underemphasized: time-varying GGMs (Kolar, Xing; Hallac et al., fused GLasso), streaming covariance monitoring, sliding-window/exp-weighted CI testing for PC, dynamic Bayesian networks, and online BN structure updates.

Significance
- If made statistically sound and benchmarked against stronger baselines, the approach could be impactful for streaming causal discovery and adaptive modeling. As is, the heuristic nature and lack of calibration/guarantees limit adoption. The real-data example does not yet demonstrate practical utility beyond detecting broad volatility shifts.

Questions for authors
1. How do you justify the T^2 and the degrees of freedom with adaptive weights? Have you empirically calibrated ρr under stationarity (QQ plots, false alarm rates)?
2. How exactly are CI tests in PC adapted to use weighted Cr and Sr? What is the effective α and its control?
3. Can you compare to sliding-window PC and to a fixed exponential forgetting baseline? Also to CPD+relearn?
4. What regularization do you use to keep Cr invertible? Any safeguards when Sr is small?
5. Can you provide sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν and ablations (e.g., pooled p-values vs. raw EWMA)?
6. In the CPI case, can you show the learned graphs across epochs and compare to econometric baselines?

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses an important, underexplored online problem.
- Simple, efficient updates; practical triggering scheme.
- Insightful qualitative discussion of convergence vs. diligence.

Cons:
- Core statistical components (p-values, effective N, CI tests) are not well-founded under adaptive weighting.
- Baselines are inadequate; limited sensitivity/ablation studies.
- Real-data validation is weak; clarity issues and missing details.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The direction is promising, but the current submission relies on heuristics without adequate calibration or comparison to strong baselines. With stronger theoretical justification (or empirical calibration), better baselines, and clearer algorithmic details, this could become a solid contribution.
