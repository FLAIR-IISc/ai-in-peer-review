### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from streaming data where the underlying data-generating process may change over time. The core assumption is that the process is "locally stationary," meaning it is i.i.d. for short periods but can change over longer timescales. The authors contrast their work with standard batch-mode structure learning algorithms (e.g., PC, score-based methods), which assume a single stationary distribution, and with other online methods that either track simple parameters or are not designed for general graphical model structure.

The LoSST algorithm works by maintaining online estimates of sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an "effective" sample size). Crucially, incoming data points are weighted based on their fit to the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. A sequence of poor-fitting points (quantified using a weighted pooling of p-values) increases the weight of new data, effectively down-weighting historical data and allowing the algorithm to adapt to a new regime. A standard structure learning algorithm (in this case, PC) is then run intermittently on these dynamically weighted statistics to produce an updated graph. The paper provides a theoretical analysis of a trade-off between "convergence" (stabilizing on a correct model in a stationary environment) and "diligence" (quickly detecting change), showing how LoSST's parameters can be set to favor one, the other, or a balance. The algorithm's performance is demonstrated on synthetic data with abrupt changes (in structure and/or parameters) and on a real-world dataset of US price indices, where it identifies known macroeconomic shifts.

### Strengths

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a critical and practical problem that is largely overlooked by mainstream graphical model learning literature: how to learn structure when the underlying system is non-stationary. The proposed approach is novel and elegant. While online updates for sufficient statistics are known, the dynamic, fit-based re-weighting scheme to handle structural change is a creative and well-motivated contribution. The formalization of the convergence-diligence trade-off is a particularly strong and original contribution, providing a principled foundation for what might otherwise be seen as a purely heuristic method.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivations for the online updates of weighted sufficient statistics are clear. The use of Mahalanobis distance to measure fit and Liptak's method to pool evidence over time is statistically well-founded and avoids overreacting to single outliers. The empirical evaluation is thorough, testing different change scenarios (structure vs. parameter change) and demonstrating a clear advantage over a naive batch-mode baseline. The authors are also commendably honest about the results, for instance, by analyzing and explaining the temporary increase in edge removal errors in the parameter-shift experiment.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is established clearly in the introduction, the related work is surveyed concisely, and the algorithm is described in sufficient detail to be understood and likely implemented. The figures effectively communicate the results of the simulation studies.

### Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions

While this is a strong piece of work, there are a few areas where it could be improved.

1.  **Baseline Comparisons:** The primary baseline is the PC algorithm run on the entire dataset up to the current time. While this effectively demonstrates the failure of standard batch algorithms in a non-stationary setting, it is not the most competitive online baseline. A stronger comparison would be against a **sliding-window approach**, where a batch learner is run on only the most recent `k` data points. This is a common and simple heuristic for handling non-stationarity. It would be valuable to understand how LoSST's adaptive weighting compares to a fixed-window approach. Another relevant baseline could be a combination of a standard changepoint detection (CPD) algorithm and a batch learner, which would represent a different philosophy for tackling the same problem.
2.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** The algorithm computes the Mahalanobis distance at every time step, which requires inverting the current covariance matrix, an O(V^3) operation for V variables. This could become a bottleneck for high-dimensional problems. While structure learning itself is computationally expensive and run less frequently, this per-instance cost is a significant practical limitation that is not discussed. A brief discussion of this complexity and potential avenues for mitigation (e.g., using approximations for the matrix inverse or diagonal approximations for the Mahalanobis distance) would strengthen the paper's practical relevance.
3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm's behavior is governed by key hyperparameters, notably the p-value threshold `T`, the weight increase factor `γ`, and the relearning rate `ν`. The paper states that `T=0.05` and `γ=3` were chosen via "unsystematic investigation." Given their central role in controlling the convergence-diligence trade-off, the work would be more complete with a more systematic sensitivity analysis. Even a small study in an appendix showing how performance varies with different settings of `T` and `γ` would give readers a better sense of the algorithm's robustness.
4.  **Generalizability to Other Model Classes:** The method is presented for linear Structural Equation Models over continuous variables. The authors correctly note that extending it to other models, such as Bayesian networks over discrete variables, is a direction for future work. However, the paper could benefit from a short discussion of the specific challenges involved. For instance, what would be an appropriate analog to the covariance matrix and Mahalanobis distance for multinomial variables? Acknowledging these challenges would provide a more complete picture of the proposed method's current scope.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** Addresses a significant and practical problem with a novel, well-motivated, and elegant solution.
*   **Pro:** The theoretical analysis of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off provides a principled understanding of the algorithm's behavior, elevating it beyond a simple heuristic.
*   **Pro:** The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate the algorithm's ability to effectively track changes in graphical structure, substantially outperforming a standard batch approach.
*   **Pro:** The paper is very clearly written, making a complex topic accessible.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   **Con:** The experimental comparison could be stronger; it lacks common online baselines like a sliding-window method.
*   **Con:** The computational complexity and its implications for scalability in high-dimensional settings are not addressed.
*   **Con:** The sensitivity to key hyperparameters is not systematically analyzed.

### Decision

The strengths of this paper substantially outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, technically sound, and empirically validated solution to an important problem. The weaknesses are primarily related to the scope of the evaluation and discussion, not fundamental flaws in the method itself. They can be addressed in future work and do not detract from the core contribution. This is a high-quality paper that would be of significant interest to the NeurIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
