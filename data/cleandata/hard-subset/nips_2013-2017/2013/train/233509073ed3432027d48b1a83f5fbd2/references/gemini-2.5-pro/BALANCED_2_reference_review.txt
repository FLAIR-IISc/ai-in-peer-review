This paper presents LoSST, a novel online algorithm for learning graphical model structure from data streams where the underlying generating process may be non-stationary. The key idea is to track sufficient statistics (mean, covariance) using a dynamic weighting scheme. The weight assigned to each new data point is adjusted based on how well it fits the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. To distinguish systematic change from random outliers, the algorithm aggregates evidence over time by pooling the p-values of the Mahalanobis distances. When a change is detected (indicated by a low pooled p-value), the algorithm increases the weights on recent data, effectively "forgetting" the past and allowing for rapid adaptation. A standard structure learning algorithm (here, PC) is then periodically run on these dynamically updated sufficient statistics. The paper relates this approach to prior work in online learning, changepoint detection, and time-varying graph estimation, clearly distinguishing itself as a fully online, single-pass method. A theoretical analysis of the algorithm's parameters reveals a trade-off between convergence to a stable structure and diligence in detecting change. The effectiveness of LoSST is demonstrated on synthetic data with abrupt changes and on a real-world macroeconomic dataset.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The problem of learning graphical structures in non-stationary environments is of high practical importance, with applications ranging from finance to neuroscience. Existing methods are often ill-suited, typically requiring batch processing or assuming stationarity. This paper presents a novel and elegant solution that combines online sufficient statistic tracking, a principled measure of model misfit (Mahalanobis distance), and a clever evidence aggregation scheme (Liptak's p-value pooling) to create a fully online, single-pass algorithm. This is a significant contribution to the field.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The online update equations for the weighted sufficient statistics are well-derived. The proposed mechanism for detecting change and adjusting data weights is well-motivated and intuitive. The theoretical analysis of the "convergence vs. diligence" trade-off is a strong point, providing formal grounding for the algorithm's behavior under different parameter settings and linking it to broader principles of statistical inference [5]. The empirical evaluation is thorough, using controlled simulations that clearly demonstrate the advantage of LoSST over a batch-mode baseline in non-stationary settings. The authors are also commendably transparent about a scenario where LoSST is briefly outperformed (edge removal errors after a parameter-only shift) and provide a plausible explanation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the algorithm is described in a step-by-step manner, and the experimental results are presented with clear figures and interpretations. The paper provides sufficient detail for the work to be understood and likely reproduced.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Dependence on Backend Learner:** The performance of LoSST is inherently tied to the chosen backend structure learner (PC algorithm). The paper does not discuss the potential interactions between LoSST's dynamic sample size and the properties of PC. For instance, after a detected changepoint, the effective sample size drops significantly. Constraint-based methods like PC can be unstable and produce dense graphs with small sample sizes due to low statistical power in independence tests. This interaction is a critical aspect of the overall system's performance and warrants more discussion.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm introduces several key hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) that control the trade-off between stability and reactivity. The authors state that the chosen values resulted from "unsystematic investigation." While the results are good, the paper would be strengthened by a more systematic sensitivity analysis to guide practitioners in setting these parameters for new problems.
*   **Evaluation on Real Data:** The application to US price index data is a compelling illustration of the algorithm's change-detection capabilities, successfully identifying a known period of macroeconomic change. However, the evaluation remains qualitative. The analysis focuses on the change-detection mechanism (e.g., drops in effective sample size) rather than the learned graph structures themselves. An analysis of whether the learned graphs before and after the changepoint are economically meaningful would provide a more powerful validation of the method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Addresses a significant and challenging problem: learning graphical models from non-stationary data streams.
2.  Proposes a novel, practical, and well-motivated online algorithm (LoSST).
3.  The technical approach is sound, combining established statistical techniques in an original way.
4.  Includes a valuable theoretical discussion on the convergence-diligence trade-off.
5.  Empirical results on synthetic data convincingly demonstrate the method's effectiveness over a standard batch baseline.
6.  The paper is very clearly written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The choice of the backend structure learning algorithm (PC) and its interaction with the dynamically changing effective sample size is not sufficiently discussed.
2.  The selection and sensitivity of key hyperparameters are not systematically explored.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend acceptance. The paper tackles an important problem with a novel, sound, and well-executed approach. The strengths, particularly the originality of the method and the quality of the experimental validation, significantly outweigh the weaknesses. This work is a valuable contribution that is likely to be built upon by other researchers.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   In the discussion, it would be beneficial to elaborate on the choice of the PC algorithm. Acknowledging the potential issues with constraint-based methods on small effective sample sizes and briefly discussing alternatives (e.g., score-based methods with appropriate modifications for online computation, or L1-regularized methods) would add valuable context.
*   If space permits (perhaps in an appendix), a small study on the sensitivity of the results to the key parameters T and γ would be very helpful for readers looking to apply this method. For example, showing how the error curves in Figure 1 change for a more "diligent" (higher γ) or more "convergent" (γ=1) setting would nicely complement the theoretical discussion in Section 3.1.
*   Regarding the real-world application, even a brief qualitative analysis of the learned graphs would be a welcome addition. For example, are there any edges that consistently appear or disappear before and after the 1980 changepoint? This would help move the application from a demonstration of change-detection to a demonstration of meaningful structure tracking.
*   A minor point on clarity: The notation surrounding the pooled p-value in Section 3 is slightly confusing. `ar+1` is based on `ρr+1`, which is calculated using data only up to time `r`. It might be clearer to denote the pooled p-value based on data up to time `r` as `ρ_r` and then state that `ar+1` is a function of `ρ_r`. This is a very minor point in an otherwise excellently written paper.
