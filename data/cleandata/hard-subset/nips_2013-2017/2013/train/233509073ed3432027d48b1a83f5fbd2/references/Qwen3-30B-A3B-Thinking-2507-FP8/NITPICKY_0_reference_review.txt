

# Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

## Summary
This paper proposes the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm for online learning of time-varying graphical model structures. The authors correctly identify a gap in current literature: most graphical model structure learning algorithms assume stationary data (i.i.d.), while real-world applications often involve changing structures that require online adaptation. LoSST tracks sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) online and uses Mahalanobis distances with pooled p-values to detect structural changes, adjusting weights to focus on recent data. The paper demonstrates through simulations that LoSST performs comparably to batch methods when the structure is stable and outperforms them when structures change.

## Strengths
The paper identifies a meaningful gap in the literature and makes a clear contribution to online graphical model learning. The problem statement is well-motivated, and the technical approach is novel within the context of online structure learning. The simulation results demonstrate reasonable performance, and the application to US price index volatility provides a compelling real-world use case. The discussion of convergence vs. diligence trade-offs is theoretically interesting and adds depth to the methodological discussion.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Technical Errors
The paper contains several formatting inconsistencies and technical errors that undermine its credibility:

1. **Mathematical Notation Errors**: 
   - "r 6= s" should be "r ≠ s" (using "≠" instead of "6=").
   - "aji's" should be "a_{ji}'s" (proper LaTeX formatting for subscripts).
   - "Dr+1 = (Xr+1 − µr)(Cr)−1(Xr+1 − µr)T" incorrectly places the transpose symbol; it should be on the vector (Xr+1 - µr)^T.
   - In equation (4), the expression "γT − γρr+1 + ρr+1 T" is ambiguous without parentheses. It should be "γ(T - ρr+1) + ρr+1" or similar.
   - "Sr+1 = ar ar+1 Sr + 1" (equation 3) is mathematically incorrect as written. The intended meaning appears to be "Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1)Sr + 1" (division), not multiplication.

2. **Incorrect Statistical Claims**:
   - "The Mahalanobis distance of a V-dimensional datapoint from a covariance matrix estimated from a sample of size N is distributed as Hotelling's T² with parameters p = V and m = N − 1" is fundamentally incorrect. Hotelling's T² statistic is distributed as F(p, m-p+1) with appropriate scaling, not as T². This error could invalidate the p-value calculation.
   - The paper claims "If ar = αbr, then the learning is the same as if one uses TD(0) learning for each covariance with a learning rate of α." TD(0) is designed for scalar random variables, while this is for covariance matrices. The relationship between these two concepts is not properly explained.

3. **Theoretical Inconsistencies**:
   - The paper states "LoSST is neither convergent nor diligent, but rather strikes a balance between the desiderata" without providing theoretical justification for this claim. The paper's own analysis suggests it can be both convergent and diligent under different parameter settings, so the "balance" claim requires stronger evidence.
   - The paper claims "If γ = 1, then LoSST is a convergent algorithm" but provides no proof or reference for this claim. The proof sketch provided in the paper is incomplete and contains logical gaps.

4. **Methodological Weaknesses**:
   - The parameter selection ("T = 0.05 and γ = 3") is described as "unsytematic investigation," which is insufficient for a methodological contribution. A more rigorous parameter selection procedure would be expected.
   - The paper states "We conjecture that any other standard graphical model structure learning algorithm would perform similarly" without evidence, which is a weak justification for using PC as the sole baseline.
   - The paper claims "LoSST can learn graphical model structure in an online fashion" but doesn't provide a clear explanation of how the PC algorithm (which is batch-based) is adapted to the online setting.

### Clarity and Presentation Issues
- The paper uses inconsistent notation for the Mahalanobis distance (sometimes "Dr+1", sometimes "Dr").
- The probabilistic relearning mechanism (section 3.1) is described in a confusing manner, with the probability formula being unnecessarily complex.
- The paper fails to address how it handles singular covariance matrices, which can occur with small effective sample sizes.
- The discussion of the Great Moderation in Figure 4 lacks statistical significance testing for the detected change.

## Recommendation
While the paper addresses an important problem and makes a novel contribution to online graphical model learning, the numerous technical errors, formatting inconsistencies, and theoretical weaknesses significantly undermine its credibility. The incorrect statistical claim about Hotelling's T² distribution is particularly concerning as it affects the core mechanism of the algorithm. The paper's claims about convergence and diligence are also not well-supported by the provided analysis.

The authors have made a meaningful contribution to the literature, but the paper requires substantial revision to address these technical issues before it can be considered for publication. Given the stringent standards of NIPS and the number of minor but critical flaws that need correction, I recommend rejection.

## Pro and Con Acceptance
**Pro**:
- Addresses a meaningful gap in online graphical model learning
- Provides a theoretically interesting approach to the convergence vs. diligence trade-off
- Demonstrates reasonable performance in simulations
- Includes a real-world application to US price index volatility

**Con**:
- Contains multiple mathematical errors and inconsistencies
- Makes incorrect statistical claims about Hotelling's T² distribution
- Lacks rigorous parameter selection and theoretical justification
- Has numerous formatting inconsistencies that undermine professionalism
- Weak justification for using PC as the sole baseline algorithm

The paper's potential contribution is overshadowed by these technical and presentation issues, making it unsuitable for publication in NIPS in its current form.
