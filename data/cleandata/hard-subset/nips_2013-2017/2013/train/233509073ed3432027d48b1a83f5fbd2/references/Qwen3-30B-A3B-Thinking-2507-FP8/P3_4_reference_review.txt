

# Peer Review: "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

## Summary
This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), an algorithm for online graphical model structure learning when the underlying structure may change over time. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: standard structure learning algorithms assume stationary data (i.i.d.), while real-world applications often require tracking time-varying structures in an online manner. LoSST tracks weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values to detect structural changes, then probabilistically relearns the graphical model structure. The paper demonstrates through simulations that LoSST performs as well as batch-mode learning during stable periods but significantly outperforms it after structural changes, and applies it to US price index data.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement**: The paper effectively identifies a genuine gap in the literature - online graphical model structure learning - and justifies why standard batch methods fail in dynamic environments.

2. **Theoretical foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical basis for the weighted sufficient statistics update rules, showing how they generalize TD(0) learning. The convergence vs. diligence trade-off is particularly insightful and well-explained.

3. **Practical implementation**: The algorithm is designed for real-time processing with single-pass data requirements, making it suitable for large-scale applications where batch processing is infeasible.

4. **Comprehensive experiments**: The synthetic data experiments cover multiple scenarios (structure change, parameter change) and demonstrate clear advantages of LoSST over batch learning after structural shifts. The real-world application to US price indices provides a meaningful demonstration of the method's utility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical gaps**: The paper makes a significant theoretical claim about convergence properties (Section 3.1) without providing a proof or sufficient reference. The statement "LoSST is provably convergent if T is time-indexed..." lacks mathematical rigor and would benefit from a proper proof sketch or reference.

2. **Limited comparison with related work**: While the paper cites Talih & Hengartner (2005) and Siracusa & Fisher III (2009), it doesn't sufficiently engage with their approaches or explain why LoSST is fundamentally different. For instance, how does LoSST's probabilistic relearning differ from the Bayesian approach in Siracusa & Fisher III?

3. **Lack of theoretical justification for key parameters**: The probabilistic relearning mechanism (with parameter ν) is introduced heuristically without theoretical justification. The paper mentions "unsystematic investigation" led to ν = 0.005, which is insufficient for a rigorous contribution.

4. **Limited scope**: The focus on SEMs (linear models) restricts applicability to more general graphical models (e.g., discrete Bayesian networks). The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't adequately address how the method could be extended.

5. **Weak real-world validation**: The US price index application is interesting but superficial. The paper doesn't clearly demonstrate how LoSST's output provides actionable insights beyond what simpler methods would yield, and the "Great Moderation" detection appears somewhat speculative.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the growing literature on time-varying graphical models. The core idea of using weighted sufficient statistics with Mahalanobis-based change detection is sound and well-motivated. The algorithm demonstrates clear practical advantages over batch learning in dynamic environments, and the theoretical exploration of the convergence-diligence trade-off is particularly strong.

However, the paper's theoretical claims require more rigorous treatment, and the connection to prior work on time-varying graphical models could be strengthened. The real-world application, while suggestive, doesn't fully demonstrate the method's unique value.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper addresses an important problem with a well-motivated solution that advances the state of the art in online graphical model learning. The main issues to address are:

1. Provide a more rigorous justification for the convergence claims in Section 3.1
2. Better differentiate LoSST from prior work on time-varying graphical models (particularly Siracusa & Fisher III)
3. Add more theoretical justification for the probabilistic relearning parameter ν
4. Strengthen the real-world validation by more clearly demonstrating the added value of LoSST over simpler alternatives

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses a significant gap in the literature. With minor revisions to address the theoretical gaps and strengthen the connection to related work, it would be a strong contribution to NeurIPS.

## Final Thoughts

This work successfully bridges a critical gap between traditional batch structure learning and the reality of dynamically changing graphical structures. While the theoretical foundation needs strengthening, the practical utility of the method is clear, and the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of graphical models. The algorithm's ability to balance convergence and diligence through parameter tuning is particularly noteworthy and could inspire further research on adaptive learning algorithms for non-stationary environments.
