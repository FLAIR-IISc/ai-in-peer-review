## Peer Review for "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning graphical model structure in a non-stationary environment, where the underlying generating process changes over time. Unlike existing methods that assume a fixed structure or require batch processing, the authors propose a novel heuristic algorithm, LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), that learns structure online with only a local i.i.d. assumption. LoSST dynamically tracks sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) and relearns the graphical model when changes are detected using a Mahalanobis distance-based change detection mechanism. The authors demonstrate through simulations and a real-world application that LoSST can effectively track changing structures, performing comparably to batch methods during stable periods and outperforming them after changes occur.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper effectively situates itself within the existing literature on graphical model structure learning and online learning. It correctly points out the limitations of traditional methods (PC algorithm, Bayesian approaches) which rely on stationarity and batch processing. The review of Temporal Difference Learning (TDL) and changepoint detection (CPD) algorithms highlights why these are insufficient for the specific task of *structural* online learning. The authors appropriately differentiate their work from the limited existing work on time-varying graph structures (Talih & Hengartner 2005, Siracusa & Fisher III 2009), emphasizing the online, frequentist nature of their approach and its applicability to directed acyclic graphs.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a critical gap:** The problem of online, non-stationary graphical model learning is highly relevant in many real-world applications, and this paper offers a valuable initial contribution to addressing it.
*   **Practical Algorithm:** LoSST provides a concrete algorithm with clear update equations for sufficient statistics, making it implementable and testable. The approach of dynamically weighting data points based on change detection is intuitive and effective.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The simulation results convincingly demonstrate LoSST's ability to track both parameter and structural changes, particularly its advantage over batch methods after a change point. The application to US price index data further supports its practical viability.
*   **Clear Discussion of Trade-offs:** The paper thoughtfully discusses the inherent trade-off between convergence and diligence, outlining how LoSST’s parameters can be tuned to favor one over the other.
*   **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The explanations are clear, and the mathematical notation is used appropriately.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Heuristic Nature:** LoSST is presented as a heuristic algorithm, and lacks a strong theoretical foundation. While the authors acknowledge this, a more rigorous analysis of its convergence properties or error bounds would strengthen the work.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The performance of LoSST appears sensitive to parameters like `T` (threshold for p-value) and `γ` (weight adjustment factor). The paper mentions that `T=0.05` and `γ=3` “seemed to appropriately trade off convergence vs. diligence,” but a more systematic parameter tuning analysis would be beneficial.  The sensitivity to these parameters isn’t fully explored.
*   **Mahalanobis Distance Dependence:** Relying solely on the Mahalanobis distance for change detection could be susceptible to outliers or noise. Investigating alternative change detection methods or incorporating robustness measures could improve performance.
*   **Computational Cost of Relearning:** While the algorithm is online, the computationally expensive graph relearning step (using the PC algorithm) could become a bottleneck for high-dimensional problems or very frequent changes. The paper doesn't delve into the computational complexity implications.
*   **Limited Scope of Evaluation:**  The simulation scenarios are somewhat limited.  Exploring more complex change patterns (e.g., gradual changes, multiple simultaneous changes) would further demonstrate the algorithm’s robustness.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is important and timely.
*   The proposed algorithm is novel and practical.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the algorithm’s effectiveness.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The algorithm is heuristic and lacks a strong theoretical guarantee.
*   Parameter sensitivity and potential computational bottlenecks could limit its practical applicability.
*   The evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of graphical model learning. While the algorithm is heuristic and has limitations, it addresses a significant and challenging problem with a practical and well-motivated approach. The empirical results are convincing, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Rating:**  **Accept (with minor revisions)**

**Recommendations for Revision:**

*   **Theoretical Analysis:**  Even a partial theoretical analysis of convergence properties or error bounds would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Parameter Tuning:**  Conduct a more systematic parameter tuning analysis to assess the sensitivity of LoSST to `T` and `γ`.
*   **Robustness Investigation:** Explore alternative or robust change detection methods to mitigate the potential impact of outliers.
*   **Computational Complexity:** Discuss the computational complexity of the relearning step and potential strategies for optimization.
*   **Expanded Evaluation:**  Consider adding simulation scenarios with more complex change patterns (e.g., gradual changes, multiple simultaneous changes).
