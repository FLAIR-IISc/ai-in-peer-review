Summary
The paper introduces LoSST, a heuristic online procedure for learning time-varying graphical structures (linear SEMs/DAGs) under a “locally stationary” assumption. The method maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) with adaptive weights ar. Weights are increased when recent data look unlikely under the current covariance via a pooled p-value of Mahalanobis distances (Liptak’s method). Structure is relearned periodically using PC; the relearning probability is a function of the pooled p-value. The authors discuss “convergence vs. diligence,” offering proof sketches for regimes of parameters, and provide simulations comparing LoSST to a batch PC baseline under piecewise changes, plus a CPI case study.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself against batch DAG learning and classical CPD. However, the literature on time-varying structure is broader than cited. For undirected models, there is substantial work on estimating time-varying Gaussian graphical models via kernel smoothing or fused/temporal penalties and on change detection (e.g., Kolar and Xing; Zhou and Lafferty; Talih and Hengartner is cited). For directed models, dynamic Bayesian networks and online/recursive BN structure learning, as well as sliding-window or forgetting-factor adaptations of constraint-based methods, are relevant baselines. The paper’s use of adaptive forgetting in sufficient statistics is close in spirit to exponentially weighted covariance estimation and adaptive filters, and change detection via generalized likelihood or EWMA statistics could be natural comparators. These connections are not fully developed, and empirical comparisons to such baselines are absent.

Strengths
- Addresses an important setting: online, nonstationary structure learning with limited memory.
- Algorithm is simple to implement and computationally frugal between relearning events (single-pass maintenance of sufficient statistics).
- Sensible design goal: match batch performance under stationarity while adapting after changes.
- Simulation results show plausible behavior and highlight failure modes for naïve batch learners on mixtures.
- The convergence vs. diligence discussion is thought-provoking and clarifies intended operating regimes.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Statistical validity of the pooled p-values is questionable. The use of Hotelling’s T^2 with parameters p = V, m = Sr−1 assumes i.i.d. Gaussian samples with a classical (or at least well-characterized) estimator of the covariance. Here, the covariance is a weighted/adaptively updated estimate with time-varying weights, so the null distribution of the Mahalanobis distance and its p-value calibration are not justified. Moreover, Liptak’s pooling assumes independence (or at least a known dependence structure) of p-values; here the p-values are serially dependent and their weights are data-dependent, undermining the interpretation of ρ.
- The “effective sample size” recursion Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1) Sr + 1 is ad hoc. No derivation is given to link Sr to the degrees of freedom of (partial) correlation tests used in PC. Consequently, type I/II error control for constraint-based tests under weighting remains unclear.
- How PC’s conditional independence tests are adapted to weighted sufficient statistics is not specified (e.g., Fisher’s z, degrees of freedom with weights, multiple testing control). Without this, guarantees of correct CPDAG recovery under (local) faithfulness do not transfer.
- The “relearning probability” update is a heuristic with no analysis of false alarm rate, expected detection delay, or computational trade-offs.
- Theoretical claims are mostly proof sketches and rest on strong assumptions or unverifiable surrogates (e.g., pooled p-values as reliable indicators). The “no algorithm can be both diligent and convergent” claim is cited to a technical report; more formal treatment is needed.

Originality
- The core ingredients—adaptive/decayed covariance tracking, anomaly scores based on Mahalanobis distance, pooled p-values, and periodic structure relearning—are individually well-known. The contribution is an engineering combination tailored to DAG structure learning. Given existing work on time-varying GGMs and DBNs, the novelty is moderate.

Significance
- Empirical validation is limited by weak baselines. Comparing LoSST to batch PC on a mixture is not compelling; trivial fixes (sliding window PC, exponentially weighted PC, BOCPD + reinitialize PC, kernel-smoothed TV-GGM for undirected case) are missing. Runtime/compute comparisons and sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν are not provided.
- The real-data example lacks ground truth and baseline comparisons; the interpretation as “Great Moderation” detection is suggestive but not a rigorous evaluation.

Clarity
- Generally readable, but key details are missing: exact CI test with weights; how orientation errors are defined for CPDAGs; parameter selection strategy; complexity and memory footprint; hyperparameter sensitivity. References are incomplete.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant problem with a simple online algorithm.
- Positive simulations illustrating adaptation after change and minimal overhead under stationarity.
- Could stimulate interest in online DAG learning under local stationarity.

Cons for acceptance
- Questionable statistical calibration of the detection mechanism and “effective sample size.”
- Lack of strong baselines and ablations; conclusions about superiority are not convincing.
- Limited theoretical guarantees; proof sketches rely on heuristics.
- Real-data demonstration is anecdotal.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide principled derivations for weighted CI tests (partial correlations) and degrees of freedom under adaptive weights, or use resampling/online bootstrap to calibrate tests and detection thresholds.
- Compare against strong baselines: sliding-window PC with tuned window sizes; exponentially weighted PC; BOCPD driving reinitialization; for undirected comparisons, TV-GLASSO/kernel-smoothed precision estimates with neighborhood selection.
- Conduct sensitivity analyses over T, γ, ν and report detection delays, false positive rates, and overall structural Hamming distance with confidence intervals.
- Clarify how orientation errors are computed for CPDAGs and ensure fair comparison.
- Report computational costs and memory relative to baselines.
- Either supply complete proofs or temper claims about convergence/diligence. If possible, give bounds on detection delay as a function of change magnitude under Gaussianity.
- Strengthen related work, especially dynamic Bayesian networks and time-varying graphical model literature.

Recommendation
Reject. While the problem is important and the heuristic is interesting, the current version lacks statistical grounding for its core tests, omits key methodological details, and does not compare against appropriate baselines. Stronger theory and more rigorous empirical evaluation are needed.

Confidence: High.
