## Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning graphical model structure in non-stationary environments, a significant limitation of standard algorithms that assume i.i.d. data. The authors present the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm, a heuristic approach that dynamically updates sufficient statistics and relearns the graphical model structure online as data streams in. LoSST aims to balance convergence (stability when the structure *is* constant) and diligence (rapid response to changes). The paper demonstrates through simulations and a real-world application to US price index data that LoSST can effectively track structural changes while performing comparably to batch methods during stable periods.

**Related Work:**

The authors provide a solid review of related work, correctly positioning their contribution. They appropriately highlight the limitations of existing structure learning algorithms (Bayesian/score-based, constraint-based) in dynamic settings. The discussion of online learning methods like TDL and changepoint detection accurately points out their shortcomings – either inability to handle structural changes directly or reliance on storing substantial historical data. The review of previous work on time-indexed graph structures [14, 11] accurately highlights their batch-mode nature and differences in approach.  

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:**  The assumption of static graphical structure is a major limitation in many real-world applications. This work directly addresses this gap.
* **Online learning capability:** The key strength is the algorithm’s ability to learn in an online fashion, processing data as it arrives, crucial for many practical scenarios.
* **Balance between convergence and diligence:** The exploration of the trade-off between these two properties, and the mechanism for controlling it via parameters γ and T, is a significant contribution. The theoretical discussion of this trade-off adds depth.
* **Strong experimental validation:** The simulation results are convincing, demonstrating LoSST’s ability to track changes in both structure and parameters, and its competitive performance during stable periods. The real-world application to price index data provides further evidence of its practical utility.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithm is described in a clear and understandable manner. The mathematical formulation is reasonably accessible.
* **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of computational complexity and the need to balance relearning frequency with accuracy are important practical considerations.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Heuristic nature:** The algorithm is described as a "heuristic," and lacks a strong theoretical foundation guaranteeing performance in all scenarios. While the authors address convergence vs. diligence, a more thorough theoretical analysis of its limitations would be beneficial.  The choice of the PC algorithm as the structure learner is a reasonable one, but the performance of LoSST is inherently tied to the performance of the underlying structure learner.
* **Parameter sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the parameters γ and T. The authors mention an unsystematic search for appropriate values in the simulations. A more robust parameter tuning strategy or sensitivity analysis would strengthen the findings.  How these parameters should be set in practice isn't fully clarified.
* **Mahalanobis distance threshold (T):** The use of a fixed threshold T for the pooled p-value feels somewhat arbitrary. Exploring adaptive thresholding methods could be valuable. The reliance on the Mahalanobis distance might be sensitive to outliers, despite the weighting scheme.
* **Limited scope of real-world application:** The application to US price index data is a good starting point, but expanding to other datasets with different characteristics (e.g., higher dimensionality, more frequent changes) would further validate the algorithm’s robustness.
* **Computational cost of PC algorithm:** While the authors acknowledge the computational expense of structure learning, the repeated application of the PC algorithm can become prohibitive for high-dimensional data, even with probabilistic relearning. Exploring more scalable structure learning alternatives would be beneficial.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamentally important problem in graphical model learning.
* The proposed LoSST algorithm provides a practical and effective solution for online learning of time-varying graphical structures.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the algorithm’s performance in both simulated and real-world settings.
* The exploration of the convergence-diligence trade-off is a valuable theoretical contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The algorithm is heuristic and lacks strong theoretical guarantees.
* Parameter tuning can be challenging, and a more robust strategy is needed.
* The dependence on the Mahalanobis distance may make it sensitive to outliers.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field and warrants acceptance to NIPS. While the heuristic nature and parameter sensitivity are weaknesses, the importance of the problem addressed, the practical utility of the proposed algorithm, and the thorough experimental evaluation outweigh these concerns. The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and offers valuable insights into the challenges of learning graphical models in dynamic environments. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Minor suggestions for improvement:**

* Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of LoSST, particularly concerning the repeated application of the PC algorithm.
* Explore adaptive thresholding methods for the pooled p-value.
* Conduct a more systematic parameter sensitivity analysis.
* Consider applying LoSST to additional real-world datasets.
