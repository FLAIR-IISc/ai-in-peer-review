This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), an online algorithm for learning graphical model structure in non-stationary environments. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics in an online fashion and dynamically relearn the graphical model when changes in the data distribution suggest a shift in the underlying structure. The authors provide a detailed algorithmic description, simulation results, and a real-world application to US price index volatility.

**Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses a critical gap in existing graphical model literature: the assumption of i.i.d. data.  Most structure learning algorithms (e.g., [9, 10, 12, 3]) are designed for static structures and struggle when the generating process evolves over time.  While online learning exists, it hasn't been adapted to the problem of tracking *structural* changes in graphical models. The authors correctly position their work in relation to time-series changepoint detection [1, 4] and previous, batch-mode attempts at time-varying graph learning [11, 14]. LoSST distinguishes itself by operating truly online, without requiring full datasets, and by employing a frequentist approach, enabling scalability compared to Bayesian methods like Siracusa and Fisher III (2009). The connection to Temporal Difference Learning (TDL) [13] is also well-made, highlighting the limitations of TDL in detecting abrupt structural changes.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Relevance:** The problem addressed is genuinely important and relatively unexplored in the NIPS community. Many real-world applications require adapting to evolving relationships between variables, making this work highly relevant.
* **Technical Soundness:** The algorithm is well-defined, and the update equations for sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) are clearly presented and mathematically justifiable. The use of Mahalanobis distance to detect changes and the weighting scheme based on pooled p-values appear reasonable.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation, including simulations with both structure and parameter changes, as well as a real-world application.  The comparisons to a standard batch-mode algorithm (PC algorithm) are insightful.
* **Discussion of Trade-offs:**  The authors thoughtfully discuss the trade-off between convergence and diligence, offering valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior under different parameter settings.  The analysis of γ and T is particularly strong.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithmic description is easy to follow, and the simulation results are presented clearly with informative figures.
* **Practical Application:** Applying LoSST to US price index data demonstrates its potential for real-world impact.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge computational cost, a more detailed analysis of the scalability of the algorithm (especially the PC algorithm relearning step) would be beneficial.  How does the runtime scale with the number of variables? Are there alternative structure learning algorithms that could be used to improve efficiency?
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm has several parameters (α, γ, T, ν) that need to be tuned. The paper provides some guidance, but a more systematic sensitivity analysis would be valuable. How robust is the performance to different parameter settings?
* **Pooling Method:** The choice of Liptak’s method for pooling p-values, while valid, might not be optimal. A brief discussion of alternative pooling methods and their potential impact on performance would be helpful.
* **Limited Real-World Validation:** The application to US price index data is a good start, but a more extensive evaluation on diverse real-world datasets would strengthen the paper.
* **Dependence on PC Algorithm:**  The performance heavily relies on the chosen structure learning algorithm (PC algorithm). The authors acknowledge this, but further exploration of alternative algorithms or hybrid approaches (e.g., combining constraint-based and score-based methods) could be considered.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant gap in the literature on graphical model learning.
* Technically sound and well-motivated algorithm.
* Thorough evaluation with both simulations and a real-world application.
* Insightful discussion of trade-offs and parameter settings.
* Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity might limit scalability to very high-dimensional datasets.
* Parameter sensitivity requires careful tuning.
* Reliance on the PC algorithm could be a bottleneck.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The LoSST algorithm is a promising approach for tracking time-varying graphical structures, and the authors have provided compelling evidence of its effectiveness. While there are some areas for improvement, particularly regarding computational complexity and parameter sensitivity, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically by:

1. Providing a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm and potential strategies for improving scalability.
2. Conducting a sensitivity analysis of the algorithm's performance to different parameter settings.
3. Briefly discussing alternative p-value pooling methods.
4. Considering exploring alternative structure learning algorithms or hybrid approaches.
