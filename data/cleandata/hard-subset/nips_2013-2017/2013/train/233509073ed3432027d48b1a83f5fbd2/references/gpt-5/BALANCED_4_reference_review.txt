Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying DAG structure in linear Gaussian SEMs. The core idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) with datapoint-specific weights ar that adapt to potential structure changes. Adaptation is driven by a pooled p-value (Lipták method) of per-step Mahalanobis distances; when the pooled p-value is small, the algorithm increases the weight on recent data and more frequently triggers re-learning of the graph via a constraint-based learner (PC). The paper analyzes parameter regimes that encourage “convergence” versus “diligence” (rapid response to change), presents simulations comparing LoSST to batch PC under both structure and parameter changes, explores a probabilistic re-learning schedule, and applies the method to U.S. price index data.

The paper is positioned against standard batch structure learning (score- and constraint-based), online changepoint detection (BOCPD, kernel CD), and a small number of prior works on time-varying structure learning (Talih & Hengartner; Siracusa & Fisher). However, the connection to the substantial literature on time-varying graphical models (especially for Gaussian models), sliding-window/forgetting-factor baselines, and dynamic Bayesian networks is limited.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important gap: online learning of changing graphical structures, with a simple, memory-efficient approach.
- Clear, modular design: online sufficient statistics + adaptive weighting + probabilistic re-learning with a standard structure learner.
- Sensible intuition: pooled p-values capture sustained deviations (not isolated outliers), and re-learning frequency adapts accordingly.
- Demonstrates that, when stable, LoSST behaves like batch PC; after change, it adapts and outperforms batch PC on synthetic data.
- Interesting conceptual framing of convergence vs. diligence and how parameters trade off responsiveness vs. stability.
- Single-pass processing and low memory for statistics are appealing for streams.

Weaknesses
- Methodological choices are largely heuristic without rigorous calibration:
  • The “effective sample size” update (Eq. 3) appears ad hoc and inconsistent with standard weighted-sample theory (where S_eff typically equals (∑w)^2/∑w^2).
  • The Mahalanobis distance distribution under dynamically reweighted, estimated covariances is not Hotelling’s T^2; the pooled p-values are likely miscalibrated and (strongly) dependent across time, violating assumptions of Lipták’s combination.
  • No analysis of false alarm or detection delay trade-offs; no guarantees for Type I error under stationarity.
- Comparisons are weak. The main baseline (batch PC over all data) is inappropriate after changes. Missing baselines include:
  • Sliding-window PC with varied windows; exponentially weighted PC with fixed forgetting.
  • Time-varying GGM methods (e.g., kernel- or fused-lasso-based estimation; Kolar, Song, Ahmed, Xing; Zhou, Lafferty, Wasserman) adapted as baselines for undirected structure.
  • CPD + per-segment structure learning; BOCPD with conjugate Gaussians and truncation/particles (which do not require storing all data).
- Theoretical results are sketchy. Convergence/diligence claims depend on thresholds with limited formal proof; no results on consistency of learned structure under local stationarity, nor on conditions ensuring positive-definite covariance updates.
- Practical concerns left unaddressed:
  • Numerical stability and regularization of online covariance/inverse; PD guarantees; high-dimensional regimes.
  • Computational cost of repeated PC invocations; no runtime analysis or caching strategy.
  • Hyperparameter sensitivity (T, γ, ν) not systematically studied; choices are heuristic.
- Real-data validation is anecdotal (one CPI dataset, no ground truth or predictive validation).

Quality
The algorithm is clearly described and easy to implement, but several components are heuristic and insufficiently justified statistically (effective sample size, p-value calibration/combination, distributional assumptions). Empirical evidence is positive but limited by weak baselines and no sensitivity/runtime analyses. Theoretical arguments are suggestive but not rigorous.

Clarity
Overall clearly written with helpful intuition. However, important details are missing: exact error metrics (e.g., CPDAG vs DAG, SHD), how partial correlations/conditioning sets are computed online, numerical stabilization, and how inverse covariances are maintained. The proofs are largely sketches.

Originality
The idea of adaptive forgetting driven by pooled “misfit” statistics and probabilistic re-learning of DAG structure online is novel and practically interesting. However, related work on time-varying graphical models (especially for Gaussians) and dynamic networks is under-cited and not empirically compared.

Significance
Potentially significant if validated: online structure tracking is valuable in many domains. As presented, the heuristic nature and evaluation gaps limit confidence and impact. Stronger theory and baselines would substantially raise significance.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem with a simple, modular approach.
- Promising empirical behavior without extra cost in stationary regimes and with gains after change.
- Memory efficiency and single-pass operation are practical.

Arguments against acceptance
- Key statistical components are ad hoc or miscalibrated; limited theory.
- Missing strong baselines (sliding window, EWMA, time-varying GGMs, CPD+PC) and ablations.
- No analysis of runtime/numerical stability; limited real-data validation.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity and trade-offs not characterized.

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace Eq. (3) with principled S_eff = b_r^2 / τ_r (you already track b_r and τ_r = ∑a_i^2), and align covariance estimation with standard weighted MLEs; ensure PD via shrinkage/regularization and consider Sherman–Morrison updates for inverses.
- Calibrate change evidence: use predictive log-likelihood ratios with conjugate updates for Gaussians, or bootstrap/permutation to estimate empirical nulls; avoid assuming T^2 distribution under adaptive weighting. Consider sequential CUSUM/GLR statistics.
- Analyze false alarm and detection delay; provide sensitivity curves over T, γ, ν; report computational cost.
- Compare against:
  • Sliding-window PC; exponentially decayed PC with fixed α.
  • Time-varying GGM baselines (kernel/fused-lasso smoothing over time).
  • BOCPD + per-segment PC (with truncation/particles to limit memory).
- Provide ablations: (i) pooled p-values vs unpooled; (ii) different pooling (Fisher, Tippett) and dependence-robust combinations; (iii) re-learning schedules.
- Expand real-data validation with additional datasets and predictive tasks (e.g., forecasting conditional dependencies or interventions), and external event alignment.
- Strengthen theory: formal conditions for PD and consistency under local stationarity; rigorous proofs for convergence/diligence regimes; bounds on error under drift.

Recommendation
Reject. The problem is important and the paper is promising, but the current version relies on heuristic statistical components, lacks rigorous validation against strong baselines, and provides only sketch-level theory. With principled calibration of statistics, stronger comparisons, and more complete analyses, this work could be a compelling contribution in a future submission.
