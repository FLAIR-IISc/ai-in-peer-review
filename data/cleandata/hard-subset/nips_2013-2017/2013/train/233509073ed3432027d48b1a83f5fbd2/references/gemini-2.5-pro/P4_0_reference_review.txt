This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models in an online fashion from streaming data. The core problem addressed is that standard structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data from a stationary distribution, an assumption often violated in real-world scenarios where the underlying data-generating process can change over time. The proposed LoSST algorithm operates in a single pass over the data, maintaining weighted estimates of the sufficient statistics (mean and covariance for Structural Equation Models). The weight assigned to each new data point is dynamically adjusted based on how well it fits the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. A sequence of poorly fitting points, identified using Liptak's method for pooling p-values, triggers an increase in the weights of recent data, effectively discounting older information and allowing the algorithm to adapt to a new structure. A probabilistic mechanism, also driven by the pooled p-value, determines when to re-run a standard batch structure learning algorithm (here, the PC algorithm) on the current weighted sufficient statistics.

This work sits at the intersection of graphical model learning, online learning, and changepoint detection. It distinguishes itself from standard batch structure learning algorithms [3, 12] by explicitly handling non-stationarity. Unlike classic online learning methods like TD(0) [13] that track parameters, LoSST is designed for structural changes. It also differs from many changepoint detection methods [1, 4] by not requiring a large data buffer and being capable of tracking both abrupt and gradual changes. Finally, in contrast to previous work on learning time-varying graphs [11, 14], this method is truly online and does not require the entire dataset to be available in advance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The update equations for the weighted sufficient statistics are well-founded. The use of Mahalanobis distance for fit-testing in a multivariate Gaussian context is appropriate. A significant strength is the formal analysis of the algorithm's long-run properties, introducing the concepts of "convergence" and "diligence." The authors provide proof sketches showing how parameter choices (specifically γ and T) allow LoSST to be configured as either convergent (guaranteed to learn a stable structure in the limit) or diligent (guaranteed to respond to changes within a fixed window), or to strike a balance. The empirical evaluation is well-designed, testing the algorithm against a batch-mode baseline (PC) in scenarios with both structural and parametric changes. The inclusion of a real-world application to US price index data, where the algorithm identifies a known macroeconomic shift, adds substantial credibility.
*   **Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first algorithm proposed for fully online, single-pass learning of graphical model structure in non-stationary environments. The synthesis of weighted sufficient statistics, a change-detection mechanism based on pooled p-values, and a probabilistic trigger for relearning is a novel and clever combination of existing techniques to solve a new and important problem. The formalization of the convergence-diligence trade-off is also a novel and insightful theoretical contribution for this problem domain.
*   **Significance:** The problem addressed is of high practical and theoretical importance. The i.i.d. assumption is a major limitation of most current structure learning methods. An effective online learner could enable applications in dynamic domains such as finance, neuroscience, and climate science. The finding that LoSST's performance is indistinguishable from a standard batch algorithm in stable periods is a critical feature, as it means there is little to no performance cost for using this more flexible approach. This significantly increases its potential for adoption. The ideas presented are likely to inspire follow-up work on online learning for other classes of graphical models.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally very well-written and clear. However, a few points could be clarified. The update equations for η and τ in Section 3 appear to be based on `pr` and `ar`, but the surrounding text and footnote 1 discuss the circularity of including `pr+1`. The timing of the indices (`r` vs. `r+1`) in the calculation of `ρr+1` and `ar+1` could be stated more explicitly to avoid confusion. Additionally, the effective sample size update (Equation 3) is justified intuitively but lacks a more formal derivation, making it seem somewhat ad-hoc.
*   **Quality:** The algorithm relies on several key hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) that govern its sensitivity and behavior. The paper states their values were chosen via "unsystematic investigation." While the results are good, the work would be strengthened by a more systematic sensitivity analysis of these parameters or a discussion of principled ways to set them. The reliance on the PC algorithm for structure learning is reasonable for a proof of concept, but the paper does not discuss potential interactions, such as how PC's performance is affected when the effective sample size `Sr` becomes very small after a detected change.
*   **Significance:** The experiments are conducted on graphs of up to 20 variables. The primary computational bottleneck is the structure learning step itself, which is NP-hard. The paper does not address the scalability of the approach to high-dimensional problems where running even a single instance of PC can be computationally prohibitive. While the probabilistic relearning helps, this remains a significant practical limitation for larger systems. The work is also limited to linear SEMs over continuous variables, and its extension to discrete or mixed data types is left as a major open question.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Addresses a novel and highly significant problem: online structure learning in non-stationary environments.
2.  The proposed algorithm is elegant, well-motivated, and demonstrates strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real-world data.
3.  Includes a valuable theoretical analysis of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off, connecting parameter choices to fundamental algorithm behavior.
4.  The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, making a complex contribution accessible.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The method's performance relies on several hyperparameters whose selection is not well-justified.
2.  The scalability of the approach to high-dimensional graphical models is not addressed and remains a significant concern.
3.  The proposed update for the "effective sample size" is heuristic and could benefit from stronger theoretical justification.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty and significance in addressing a fundamental gap in the literature, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a complete and well-executed piece of research that is likely to be impactful. I recommend acceptance.
