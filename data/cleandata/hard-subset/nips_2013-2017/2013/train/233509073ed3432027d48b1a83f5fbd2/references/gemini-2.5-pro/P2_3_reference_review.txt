### Summary

This paper presents the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel heuristic algorithm designed for the online learning of graphical model structure from data where the underlying generative process may change over time. The core idea is to maintain and update weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an "effective" sample size) for a set of continuous variables. The weight assigned to each new data point is dynamically adjusted based on how well it fits the current statistical model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. To avoid overreacting to single outliers, the algorithm pools p-values of these distances over time using Liptak's method. A significant deviation (low pooled p-value) triggers an increase in the weight of recent data, effectively "forgetting" older, potentially obsolete information. The algorithm then probabilistically triggers a relearning of the graphical model structure (using the PC algorithm in this implementation) based on these updated statistics, with relearning becoming more frequent after a detected change. The authors evaluate LoSST on synthetic data, showing it successfully tracks changes and outperforms a standard batch-mode algorithm applied to the full dataset, while performing comparably in stable periods. A real-world application to US price index data is also presented.

### Review

**Quality:** The paper is of high quality. The technical approach is sound, and the claims are well-supported by a thorough set of simulations.

*   The method for tracking sufficient statistics via weighted updates is well-established, but its application here, driven by a principled change detection mechanism, is effective. The use of Mahalanobis distance as a measure of fit is appropriate for the assumed multivariate Gaussian data, and the decision to pool p-values rather than react to individual large distances is a sensible way to distinguish structural change from isolated outliers.
*   The experimental evaluation is strong. The authors wisely compare LoSST not only in a changing environment but also in a stable one, demonstrating that their online approach incurs little to no performance cost compared to a standard batch learner (PC) when the i.i.d. assumption holds. The separation of experiments into "structure + parameter change" and "parameter-only change" is insightful, and the authors are honest about the results, noting and explaining a temporary performance dip in edge removal errors in the latter case. The third simulation, analyzing the probabilistic relearning schedule, successfully demonstrates its utility in balancing computational cost and accuracy.
*   A major strength is the formal discussion of the "convergence vs. diligence" trade-off. This provides a theoretical grounding for the algorithm's behavior, linking it directly to the hyperparameters γ and T. While the proofs are only sketched, they lend significant credibility to the design and provide users with a conceptual framework for parameter tuning.

A minor weakness is that some components are heuristic. The specific functional form for updating the weights (Equation 4) and the noisy-OR mechanism for triggering relearning are reasonable but not uniquely justified. Furthermore, the parameter tuning (`T = 0.05`, `γ = 3`) is described as the result of "unsystematic investigation." A sensitivity analysis would have strengthened the paper by showing how robust the algorithm's performance is to these choices.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, the related work is concisely summarized, and the algorithm is developed in a logical, step-by-step manner. The equations for updating the sufficient statistics are presented clearly. The distinction between convergence and diligence is explained with great clarity and is a highlight of the paper. The figures are well-designed and effectively communicate the results of the simulations. The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the method's mechanics, though reproducing the exact probabilistic relearning results would require knowing the random seed.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant original contribution. While online learning and graphical model discovery are mature fields, the problem of *online structure learning in non-stationary environments* is relatively underexplored. This work directly addresses this gap. The combination of techniques is novel: integrating weighted sufficient statistic tracking, a change-detection mechanism based on pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances, and a standard constraint-based structure learner into a single, coherent algorithm is new. The explicit framing of the algorithm's behavior in terms of the convergence-diligence trade-off is also a novel and valuable conceptual contribution to this problem space. The work is well-situated within the literature, clearly distinguishing itself from batch-mode time-varying graph methods and standard online parameter estimation techniques.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to be influential. The assumption of i.i.d. data required by most structure learning algorithms is a major limitation for their application to many real-world domains, such as economics, neuroscience, and climate science, where systems evolve over time. LoSST provides a practical, computationally plausible solution to this problem.

*   The algorithm addresses a difficult and important problem.
*   It demonstrably advances the state of the art by providing a method that can handle structural changes that would confound traditional batch algorithms.
*   The fact that it performs on par with batch methods in stable situations makes it a compelling "drop-in" alternative, removing the need for a practitioner to decide *a priori* whether the environment is stationary.
*   The ideas presented here, particularly the change-detection-driven weighting scheme and the convergence/diligence framework, are likely to be built upon by future research in this area.

---

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **Addresses a Critical Problem:** Tackles the important and practical problem of learning graphical model structure in non-stationary environments, a common scenario where standard methods fail.
2.  **Novel and Sound Algorithm:** Proposes a novel and well-motivated algorithm (LoSST) that cleverly combines existing statistical tools in a new way to track structural changes online.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The algorithm's effectiveness is demonstrated through comprehensive simulations that cover different types of change and compare against a relevant baseline. The results clearly show a significant performance gain over batch methods when the structure changes.
4.  **Theoretical Insight:** The discussion of the convergence-diligence trade-off provides valuable theoretical depth, explaining the fundamental principles governing the algorithm's behavior and offering guidance on parameterization.
5.  **High Clarity and Impact:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and the problem it solves is of broad interest. It is likely to be highly cited and to inspire follow-up work.

### Arguments for Rejection:

1.  **Heuristic Components:** Some design choices, such as the specific weight update function and the relearning trigger, are heuristic. The paper would be stronger with more formal justification or an ablation study for these choices.
2.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The parameters (`T`, `γ`, `ν`) appear crucial, yet their selection is not systematically explored. The lack of a sensitivity analysis makes it hard to gauge the algorithm's robustness.
3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The need to invert the covariance matrix at each time step to compute the Mahalanobis distance may not scale well to very high-dimensional problems, a point not addressed by the authors.

**Overall Recommendation:** The strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, elegant, and effective solution to a significant problem, backed by strong empirical evidence and valuable theoretical insights. It represents a clear contribution to the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
