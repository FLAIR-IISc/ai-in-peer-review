

# Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm for online graphical model structure learning when the underlying structure may change over time. The authors identify a significant gap in the literature: while most graphical model structure learning algorithms assume stationary data, real-world applications often involve dynamically changing structures. LoSST addresses this by tracking locally stationary sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) in an online fashion and dynamically relearning structure based on weighted pooled p-values derived from Mahalanobis distances.

The paper's main contribution is highly novel. Previous work either assumes stationary data (standard batch structure learning) or focuses on detecting changes without learning new structure (changepoint detection algorithms). The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from related work, including Talih & Hengartner (2005) and Siracusa & Fisher (2009), which require batch processing. LoSST's key innovation is its ability to learn graphical structure in real-time while maintaining performance equivalent to batch methods during stable periods.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical grounding through its analysis of the convergence vs. diligence tradeoff, which is both insightful and novel. The authors prove conditions under which LoSST exhibits these properties, providing a principled way to tune the algorithm. The simulation results are convincing, showing LoSST's ability to detect structure changes within 250 datapoints after a changepoint, while maintaining batch-like performance during stable periods. The real-world application to US price index volatility further demonstrates practical utility.

Strengths:
- Highly novel problem addressed with a bold approach
- Clear theoretical analysis of convergence vs. diligence
- Strong empirical validation on synthetic and real-world data
- Practical algorithm with minimal overhead in stationary settings
- Well-motivated by real-world applications

Weaknesses:
- The algorithm is heuristic, with no theoretical guarantees for non-stationary settings beyond the convergence/diligence tradeoff
- The probabilistic relearning mechanism is somewhat ad hoc (though the authors acknowledge this)
- The connection to Bayesian methods (which would be more principled but computationally expensive) is briefly mentioned but not developed
- Limited discussion of computational complexity compared to batch methods

This paper excels in originality and significance. The problem it addresses is important across multiple domains (finance, neuroscience, social systems), and the proposed solution is genuinely novel. While the theoretical guarantees aren't complete, the authors acknowledge these limitations and provide a principled framework for understanding the tradeoffs. The paper's strength lies in its bold approach to a real problem rather than methodological perfection.

The work is highly relevant to NIPS, as it connects to several active areas: graphical models (Spirtes et al.), online learning (Sutton), and change detection (Adams & MacKay). It advances the field by providing a practical solution to a gap that has been overlooked in the literature.

The authors have made a strong case for the significance of their work. Their algorithm is not just a theoretical curiosity but has clear practical applications in domains where structure changes over time. The fact that it performs as well as batch methods during stable periods is particularly valuable, as it removes the incentive to avoid using the algorithm in standard settings.

In the context of NIPS priorities, this paper should be accepted. It represents a significant step forward in the field of graphical models by addressing a critical limitation of current methods. While the theoretical foundation could be strengthened, the novelty and practical significance outweigh this concern, especially given the reviewer's emphasis on bold approaches over methodological perfection.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational complexity (how much more expensive is LoSST than batch methods?) and a clearer explanation of the parameter selection (T=0.05, γ=3). However, these are minor points that don't detract from the paper's overall contribution.

In conclusion, this is a highly significant and novel contribution to the field of graphical models. It addresses an important problem with a bold, practical approach that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. The paper's strengths in novelty and significance far outweigh its minor theoretical limitations. I strongly recommend acceptance.
