Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic online method for tracking time-varying graphical model structure under a local stationarity assumption. The data-generating process at time r is a recursive linear SEM on a DAG Gr. Rather than storing past data, LoSST maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) that are updated per datum. Weights ar increase when recent data fit poorly under the current covariance estimate, where “fit” is quantified via the Mahalanobis distance per timepoint and a pooled p-value across time using Liptak’s method. The update yields a forgetting mechanism that emphasizes recent data after apparent changes. Structure is relearned intermittently (probabilistically) using the PC algorithm on the tracked statistics. The paper gives conditions under which LoSST is convergent (when weights are constant or thresholds shrink appropriately) or “diligent” (fast response to changes), showing these desiderata cannot be simultaneously satisfied. Simulations show LoSST matches batch PC before a change and more rapidly recovers the new structure afterward, with an additional case study on CPI volatility suggesting LoSST detects macroeconomic regime shifts.

The paper situates LoSST relative to (i) Bayesian/score-based and constraint-based batch structure learning (e.g., Chickering; Spirtes et al.), (ii) online change-point detection (e.g., Adams & MacKay; Desobry et al.), and (iii) batch time-varying graphical models (Talih & Hengartner for undirected; Siracusa & Fisher for DAGs, Bayesian). Additional relevant prior work that could be discussed includes dynamic Bayesian networks (e.g., Murphy, 2002) and time-varying undirected models using kernel/regularized estimators (e.g., Zhou & Lafferty, 2010; Kolar & Xing, 2011), which also address evolving structure/parameters albeit in different settings.

Quality (technical soundness)
Strengths:
- The sufficient-statistics updates for weighted means/covariances are correct and efficient, enabling single-pass processing and constant memory beyond O(p^2).
- The pooled p-value heuristic is simple and operationally effective in simulations; the probabilistic relearning schedule is reasonable and reduces computation without large accuracy losses in the reported setting.
- The convergence/diligence tradeoff is clearly articulated, with plausible parameter regimes and proof sketches.

Concerns:
- Distributional calibration: The use of Hotelling’s T^2 to obtain per-datum p-values assumes i.i.d. Gaussian samples and a sample covariance with N degrees of freedom. Here, both the mean and covariance are updated with time-varying weights, and the effective sample size Sr substitutes for N. The extent to which T^2 and the resulting p-values are well-calibrated under weighted, dependent updates is not established. Similarly, Liptak’s pooling typically assumes independence (or known dependence) of p-values; temporal dependence is present here. Miscalibration could bias ar and the relearning probability.
- Conditional independence testing: PC requires partial correlations (or equivalent CI tests) with an appropriate sampling distribution. It is unclear how the CI tests are calibrated using the weighted covariance and Sr (e.g., Fisher z test with degrees of freedom adjusted for effective sample size). This should be specified and justified.
- Baselines: The main baseline is batch PC on all accumulated data, which is disfavored in nonstationary settings. More appropriate comparisons include sliding-window PC, exponentially weighted forgetting with fixed decay, or CPD combined with reinitialization. Without these, it is hard to quantify the specific value of pooled p-values and adaptive ar versus simpler forgetting strategies.
- Theory: The convergence/diligence claims rely on proof sketches. While acceptable for a heuristic paper, stronger theoretical support (e.g., consistency under piecewise-stationary regimes, bounds on detection delay/false alarm under specified assumptions) would substantiate the method’s guarantees.
- Numerical stability: Inverting a covariance that is rapidly changing or poorly conditioned is challenging, especially when Sr is small after a change. Regularization/shrinkage is not discussed.

Clarity
- Overall, the exposition is clear and well-organized, with explicit update equations and rationale. The description of the relearning policy is clear.
- Missing details: precise CI testing procedure within PC using weighted statistics; exact parameter settings (e.g., PC alpha), and any covariance regularization; computational complexity/runtimes.
- Minor issues: some typos and footnote formatting; a pseudocode summary of LoSST would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- The combination of online, adaptively weighted sufficient statistics driven by pooled p-values and integration with constraint-based DAG learning appears novel for DAG structure tracking under local stationarity. The convergence/diligence framing is also a useful conceptual contribution.
- Related work on time-varying GGMs and DBNs should be more thoroughly discussed to distinguish assumptions and capabilities.

Significance
- If validated against stronger baselines and broader settings, LoSST could be a practical tool for single-pass, resource-limited environments where structures drift. The ability to match batch performance in stationary segments while adapting after changes is valuable.
- Current evidence is promising but limited: synthetic studies favor LoSST over an arguably weak baseline; the real-data case study is illustrative but lacks ground truth.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem: online DAG structure tracking with limited storage.
- Simple, implementable algorithm with clear intuition and low memory footprint.
- Empirical results indicate effective adaptation after changes without harming stationary performance.
- Useful conceptualization of convergence vs diligence.

Cons against acceptance
- Statistical calibration of the detection mechanism under weighted, dependent data is unclear; risk of ad hoc thresholds and miscalibration.
- Missing key baselines (sliding-window/EWMA PC, CPD+relearning), ablations (e.g., pooled p-values vs simple EWMA), and sensitivity analyses (T, γ, ν).
- Incomplete description of CI testing with weighted statistics; no discussion of regularization and numerical stability.
- Theoretical results are largely sketches; limited guarantees.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Compare against sliding-window PC, fixed forgetting-factor PC, and CPD-triggered restarts; include ablations replacing pooled p-values with EWMA of Mahalanobis distances.
- Detail the CI tests used in PC with weighted covariance: how are partial correlations and their sampling distributions computed; what degrees of freedom are used?
- Analyze the calibration of T^2 and pooled p-values under weighting and temporal dependence; consider robust/regularized covariance (e.g., shrinkage) and discuss its impact.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν and guidance for selection; report runtime and scalability.
- Include gradual-drift scenarios, different change frequencies/magnitudes, and larger graphs.
- Discuss and cite work on time-varying GGMs/DBNs to better position the contribution.

Scope
The topic—online learning and tracking in graphical models—is squarely within NeurIPS scope.
