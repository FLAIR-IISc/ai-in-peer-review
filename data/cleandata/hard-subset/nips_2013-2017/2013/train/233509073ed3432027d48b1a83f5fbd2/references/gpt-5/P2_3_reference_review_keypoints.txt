- Proposes LoSST: a single-pass online algorithm for linear-Gaussian SEMs that maintains weighted sufficient statistics, pools Mahalanobis-distance p-values (Lipták), and probabilistically triggers structure relearning (PC).
- Incremental weighted mean/covariance updates are standard and memory-efficient (O(p^2)).
- The pooled p-value / learning-rate adaptation is plausible but statistically uncalibrated due to non-i.i.d. data and recursively estimated covariances.
- The independence assumption behind p-value pooling is violated because Mahalanobis distances depend on the evolving covariance estimate.
- The “effective sample size” update is nonstandard (not the usual ∑w^2 formula), risking miscalibrated downstream tests (e.g., Fisher-z in PC).
- The paper acknowledges heuristics but fails to quantify their impact on coverage, Type I/II error, or false positive rates.
- Using weighted, non-i.i.d. statistics in PC likely miscalibrates nominal p-values; the paper should state which CI tests are used and how Sr maps to degrees of freedom.
- Theoretical “convergence vs. diligence” discussion is useful but relies on extreme cases and lacks rates or finite-sample guarantees for practical regimes (0 < T < 1).
- Synthetic experiments show intended behavior (matches batch PC under stationarity, adapts after change) but compare mainly to weak baselines (batch PC on all data).
- Missing baselines: sliding-window PC, fixed-α EWMA, CPD+relearn, and time-varying graphical model methods (e.g., dynamic GLasso).
- No ablation isolating the value of pooled p-values versus simpler triggers (e.g., recent average Mahalanobis distance).
- Computational reporting is incomplete: wall-time, number of relearns, and scalability details are only partially reported.
- Real-data example (CPI/GDP) is small and lacks external validation of detected shifts beyond qualitative anecdotes.
- Paper is generally well organized and readable, with sufficient sketched derivations for experts.
- Clarity issues: missing full pseudocode of maintained state and relearning trigger; misplaced footnotes; unclear CI test mapping and Sr usage.
- Numerical stability and the online inversion/regularization of covariance are not discussed.
- Hyperparameter choices (γ, T, ν) are ad hoc; no sensitivity analysis or principled selection guidance is provided.
- Originality: novel combination of online sufficient statistics, pooled p-value-driven forgetting, and probabilistic relearning, but under-cites related areas (adaptive RLS, CPD methods, dynamic graphical models).
- Significance is promising but limited currently by heuristic calibration, weak baselines, and limited hyperparameter and compute guidance.
- Pros: tackles an important problem, simple memory-efficient updates, compatible with off-the-shelf learners, and shows promising synthetic behavior.
- Cons: heuristic statistical machinery without calibration, missing strong baselines and ablations, unclear mapping to PC testing, limited real-data validation and compute characterization.
- Key suggestions: add sliding-window and fixed-α baselines, CPD+relearn and dynamic GLasso comparisons; ablate pooled p-values; report Type I error on stationary data; consider principled ESS or bootstrap/permutation tests; report compute/time and regularization details.
- Overall assessment: promising and implementable idea but currently too heuristic and empirically under-validated; reviewer leans toward rejection pending stronger comparisons, calibration, and clarity.
