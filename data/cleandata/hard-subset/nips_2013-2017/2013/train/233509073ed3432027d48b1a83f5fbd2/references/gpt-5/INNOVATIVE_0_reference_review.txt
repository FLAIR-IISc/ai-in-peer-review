Summary
The paper proposes LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a heuristic online method to track time-varying graphical structure for linear-Gaussian DAGs/SEMs. The key idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) with adaptive weights ar chosen from a pooled sequence of Mahalanobis-distance-based p-values (Hotelling’s T^2), aggregated via Liptak’s method. Low pooled p-values increase weights (effectively “forgetting” older data), which shrinks the effective sample size and triggers more frequent structure relearning via a constraint-based learner (PC). The authors discuss a “convergence vs diligence” dichotomy: parameter settings can yield convergence in stable regimes, rapid detection (“diligence”) in nonstationary regimes, or a tunable balance. Simulations compare LoSST to batch PC in settings with a structural changepoint and with parameter-only changes, and a long run with repeated changes using probabilistic relearning. A small real-data case study on US CPI differences suggests LoSST detects the Great Moderation transition.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates LoSST relative to (i) standard score- and constraint-based structure learning (Pearl; Spirtes et al.; Chickering), which assume i.i.d. data; (ii) temporal difference learning and basic adaptive estimators, which track gradual drift; and (iii) online changepoint detection (e.g., Adams & MacKay) that assume abrupt changes and require storing segments. It also contrasts with prior batch time-varying graphical structure methods (Talih & Hengartner; Siracusa & Fisher), arguing LoSST is online, frequentist, and model-agnostic w.r.t. the structure learner. Relevant additional related work that could be acknowledged includes time-varying undirected models (e.g., Kolar & Xing’s time-varying graphical lasso), dynamic Bayesian networks, and sliding-window or exponential-forgetting baselines for CI testing—these constitute natural baselines for the proposed setting.

Strengths
- Novelty and scope: A general-purpose, online approach to DAG structure tracking with an explicit, tunable responsiveness/stability trade-off is a valuable and underexplored direction. The pooled-p-value mechanism for adaptive weighting is simple, online, and model-agnostic.
- Minimal memory footprint: The method stores only sufficient statistics and a few scalar trackers (ρ, η, τ, br), enabling true single-pass operation.
- Integration with standard learners: Plugging the weighted sufficient statistics into PC makes the approach accessible and modular; in stationary regimes, LoSST reduces to batch estimates.
- Conceptual clarity: The “convergence vs diligence” framing is insightful; showing that LoSST can realize both regimes (or trade-offs) via parameters is useful for practitioners.
- Empirical signal: Synthetic experiments show LoSST matches batch PC when stationary and clearly outperforms it after structural changes. The long-run experiment demonstrates that probabilistic relearning reduces compute without hurting accuracy. The CPI case is a plausible qualitative validation.

Weaknesses
- Statistical validity of the weighting signal: Using Hotelling’s T^2 with Sr as an “effective” sample size under non-identical weights is not justified; distributional assumptions are questionable. Successive Mahalanobis distances (and their p-values) are dependent, but Liptak’s pooling assumes independence; the paper does not address serial dependence or calibration of ρ.
- CI testing under weights: PC relies on tests on (partial) correlations. It is unclear how degrees of freedom and test statistics are adjusted for the weighted/forgetting setting, and whether type I error is controlled. This is central to structure correctness.
- Baselines: Comparing only to batch PC on a mixed dataset stacks the deck against PC. Natural online baselines include sliding windows, exponential forgetting (i.e., constant ar with α), CUSUM/GLR-based CPD + per-segment PC, and time-varying GGMs for undirected structure. Without these, it is hard to quantify the gain from the pooled-p-value design versus simpler forgetting.
- Theory: Proof sketches are informal, and key claims about convergence/diligence rely on threshold sequences without precise conditions for practical settings. No guarantees on false alarm/delay trade-offs exist beyond intuition.
- Numerical issues: No discussion of ensuring positive-definite covariance under rapidly changing weights, numerical stability, or incremental inverse updates (e.g., Sherman-Morrison), which matter in practice.
- Hyperparameters: T, γ, ν are chosen unsystematically; no sensitivity analysis or guidance is given.
- Real data: The CPI example is qualitative; no external validation, predictive evaluation, or comparison to standard volatility shift detectors is provided.

Questions for the authors
- How are CI tests in PC adapted to weighted statistics? What degrees of freedom are used for partial correlations? Do you control nominal alpha under weighting?
- How sensitive are results to T, γ, and ν? Can you provide ablations or default guidance?
- How do you maintain positive definiteness and numerical stability of Cr and its inverse as ar changes?
- Can you report runtime/compute, and compare against sliding-window baselines of matched memory?
- Have you considered dependence-robust pooling (e.g., robust combination tests) or sequential methods (e.g., CUSUM/GLR/e-values) instead of Liptak under serial dependence?

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a fresh, broadly applicable idea for online structure learning with a principled responsiveness/stability knob.
- Minimal-memory, single-pass design is attractive for streaming applications.
- Compelling synthetic evidence that the approach can handle structural changes while not harming stationary performance.
- The convergence-vs-diligence framing could seed follow-up work and theoretical development.

Cons for acceptance
- Statistical calibration of the pooled-p-value mechanism and weighted CI tests is weak/heuristic.
- Missing comparisons to strong, simple baselines (sliding window, exponential forgetting, CPD+PC) and to time-varying GGMs.
- Theoretical results are sketchy, with limited practical guidance on parameters.
- Real-data validation is anecdotal.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite methodological caveats, the paper opens an interesting, underexplored direction: online DAG structure tracking via adaptive sufficient statistics with an explicit trade-off between stability and responsiveness. The core idea is novel and potentially impactful, and the empirical results are promising. Strengthening the statistical calibration, adding competitive online baselines, and clarifying the adaptation of CI tests to weighted data would significantly improve the paper. I would be inclined to accept given NeurIPS’s interest in bold new directions, with the expectation that the authors address the most pressing concerns in the final version.

Confidence
Moderate. I am familiar with graphical model structure learning and online change detection, though not all implementation details of the authors’ system.
