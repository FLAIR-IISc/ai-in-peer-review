This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), an online algorithm for learning graphical model structure in non-stationary environments. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) incrementally and dynamically relearn the graph structure based on a change detection mechanism using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values. The algorithm is designed to perform competitively with batch methods when the underlying structure is stable, and to adapt when changes occur.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a clear gap in existing graphical model structure learning literature which largely assumes i.i.d. data. The related work section is thorough, correctly identifying the limitations of existing approaches (batch methods, changepoint detection, and limited prior work on time-varying graphs). The description of the LoSST algorithm is detailed and mathematically sound, with clear update equations for the relevant statistics. The discussion of the trade-off between convergence and diligence is particularly insightful and provides a valuable theoretical grounding for the algorithm’s behavior. The simulation results demonstrate the effectiveness of LoSST in detecting and adapting to changes in both graph structure and parameters, and the application to US price index data provides a compelling real-world example.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The algorithm addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing an online method for tracking time-varying graphical structures.  Existing methods are either batch-oriented or lack the capability to learn complex graphical dependencies.
* **Technical Soundness:** The algorithm is mathematically well-defined, and the update equations for the sufficient statistics are clearly presented and justified. The use of Mahalanobis distance and pooled p-values for change detection is reasonable and well-motivated. The theoretical discussion of convergence vs. diligence adds depth.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The algorithm is presented in a step-by-step manner, and the equations are clearly explained. The simulation setup and results are presented clearly.
* **Significance:** The ability to learn graphical models in non-stationary environments has broad applicability in many domains (e.g., financial modeling, climate science, systems biology).  The demonstrated performance and the potential for real-world impact are substantial.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm has several parameters (α, γ, T, ν) that need to be tuned. While the paper discusses the trade-offs involved, the sensitivity of the performance to these parameters is not thoroughly investigated. A more systematic analysis of parameter tuning would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the algorithm is designed for online learning, the computational cost of relearning the graph structure (using the PC algorithm in the simulations) could be substantial for high-dimensional data. The paper mentions this, but a more detailed discussion of computational complexity and potential optimization strategies would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison to Alternatives:** The primary comparison is with the PC algorithm rerun after each change. While a reasonable baseline, comparing LoSST against other online structure learning algorithms (if they exist) or adaptive versions of the PC algorithm could strengthen the evaluation.
* **Real-World Data Analysis Depth:** The real-world analysis is a good starting point, but the interpretation of the detected changes in the US price index data is somewhat limited. A more in-depth economic analysis connecting the detected structural changes to known macroeconomic events could strengthen the conclusions.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant open problem in graphical model learning.
* The proposed algorithm is technically sound and well-motivated.
* The simulation results demonstrate the effectiveness of the algorithm.
* The application to real-world data provides a compelling example.
* The theoretical discussion of convergence vs. diligence is a valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The algorithm has several parameters that require tuning, and the sensitivity to these parameters is not fully explored.
* The computational complexity of graph relearning could be a limitation.
* The comparison to alternative algorithms is limited.
* The real-world data analysis could be more in-depth.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The proposed algorithm is novel, technically sound, and well-motivated. While there are some weaknesses, they are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the parameter sensitivity and computational complexity concerns, and to provide a more thorough comparison to alternative algorithms.  Specifically, I suggest adding a section discussing the computational cost as a function of the number of variables and edges, as well as a more detailed sensitivity analysis on the key parameters.
