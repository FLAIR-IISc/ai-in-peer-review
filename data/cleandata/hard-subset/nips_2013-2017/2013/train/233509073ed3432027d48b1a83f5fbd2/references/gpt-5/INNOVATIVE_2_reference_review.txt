Summary
This paper tackles online structure learning for graphical models under nonstationarity. The proposed Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective sample size”) for linear-Gaussian SEMs and adaptively adjusts the weights via a pooled p-value computed from a sequence of Mahalanobis distances. When the pooled surprise is high (low p-value), LoSST increases the weight on new data and shrinks the effective sample size; it also triggers graph relearning events with probability tied to the pooled p-value. Structure is relearned using a constraint-based method (PC) from the current sufficient statistics. The authors analyze regimes of a couple of hyperparameters (T, γ) and give conditions under which LoSST is convergent, diligent, or trades off between the two. Simulations show LoSST matches batch PC before a change and adapts faster after change points; a small real-data study on US CPI components suggests LoSST flags known volatility shifts.

Originality
This is a bold and timely contribution: an online, single-pass approach to learning time-varying DAG structure that bridges change detection and structure learning with a lightweight sufficient-statistics tracker. The convergence–diligence framing is novel and insightful. While there is related work on time-varying undirected models and change detection, I am not aware of prior online DAG structure learners that operate via adaptive weighting and pooled surprise to drive relearning.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself relative to batch structure learning and online CPD. I recommend adding comparisons/citations to time-varying GGMs and dynamic structures, e.g., Kolar et al. (2010, 2012) on time-varying networks, Zhou et al. (2010) on tracking sparse inverse covariance, Xuan & Murphy (2007) on switching structures, sliding-window/forgetting-factor covariance estimators (EWMA), and classical sequential tests (CUSUM/Page-Hinkley). Dynamic Bayesian networks are relevant baselines for temporal structure. These will better delineate LoSST’s niche and guide baselines.

Quality
- Soundness: The sufficient-statistics updates are sensible and efficient; connecting ar = αbr to TD(0) is nice. However, several components are heuristic and would benefit from tighter justification:
  - Using Hotelling’s T^2 with a weighted covariance and an effective sample size Sr is not standard; the distribution of Mahalanobis distances under adaptively estimated, weighted covariances is unclear. Likewise, Liptak pooling assumes independence that likely does not hold for temporally correlated Dr and adaptive estimates. The authors acknowledge intractability, but some calibration (e.g., via simulation under stationarity) of ρ’s null distribution would help.
  - The use of T > 1 as a p-value threshold is conceptually odd; it works as a control parameter but breaks the “p-value” semantics. Please clarify interpretation and provide a principled mapping from desired forgetting rate to (T, γ).
  - The convergence/diligence arguments are given as proof sketches; they are intriguing but need formalization (and clear, correct statements of assumptions).
- Empirics: Synthetic studies are thorough within the tested regime and clearly show LoSST’s advantages after change points. The real-data case is illustrative but anecdotal (no ground truth or predictive validation).

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. The algorithmic description is clear and the online update equations are easy to follow. A few suggestions:
- Explicitly state computational complexity per step (O(p^2) updates; cost of occasional PC runs) and memory (O(p^2)).
- Summarize the conditional independence tests used by PC from weighted covariances (partial correlations), to reassure that raw data are not needed.
- Provide a short algorithm box for LoSST (inputs, updates, relearning decision).
- Clarify hyperparameter roles (T, γ, ν), recommended ranges, and sensitivity.

Significance
The problem is important, and a practical, single-pass approach to time-varying DAG structure is valuable. If further validated against stronger baselines, this could influence how practitioners approach streaming causal/graphical modeling. The simplicity of LoSST (O(p^2) state, occasional relearning) is a strong point.

Strengths
- Addresses a real gap: online DAG structure learning under nonstationarity.
- Lightweight, single-pass sufficient-statistics tracker; memory and computation are modest between relearnings.
- Clever use of pooled surprise to drive both forgetting and relearning frequency; unifies CPD and structure adaptation.
- Convergence–diligence lens is thought-provoking and useful for practitioners.
- Solid synthetic evidence; when stationary, LoSST matches batch; after changes, it recovers quickly.

Weaknesses
- Heuristic statistical components (T^2 with weighted covariance, Liptak pooling under dependence); lack of calibration guarantees.
- Unnatural “p-value” threshold >1 to ensure diligence.
- Limited baselines: no sliding-window/forgetting-factor PC, no CPD+reset, no dynamic BN / time-varying GGM comparisons.
- Hyperparameter tuning is ad hoc; no sensitivity analysis.
- Scalability to larger p and higher degrees is untested; PC may be a bottleneck.
- Real-data evaluation is qualitative only.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide calibration experiments showing the empirical distribution of ρ under stationarity and the operating characteristics (false alarm rate, detection delay) as functions of (T, γ, ν).
- Add baselines: (a) sliding-window PC; (b) EWMA covariance + PC; (c) BOCPD with resets + PC; (d) a time-varying GGM method (for undirected comparison).
- Include sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν and a simple rule-of-thumb for setting them.
- Evaluate on higher-dimensional graphs (e.g., p = 50–200, bounded degree) and report runtime.
- Consider alternative, computationally light detectors (CUSUM/GLR) in place of the pooled p-value; or at least an ablation: pooled p-value vs EWMA surprise.
- Clarify the T > 1 regime: present it as a separate “forgetting control” parameter, not a p-value threshold.
- Release code for reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, bold approach to an important problem with a practical, lightweight algorithmic design.
- Conceptual contribution (convergence vs diligence) with actionable parameterization.
- Clear empirical gains in nonstationary settings with no cost under stationarity.

Cons for acceptance
- Statistical underpinnings are heuristic and partially uncalibrated.
- Missing strong baselines; external validity and scalability remain to be demonstrated.
- Some theoretical claims are only sketched.

Recommendation
Weak accept. Despite heuristic elements and missing baselines, the paper introduces a fresh, pragmatic framework for online, time-varying DAG structure learning and a compelling convergence–diligence perspective. With some added comparisons and clarifications, this could stimulate useful follow-on work. My bias toward encouraging novel directions leads me to recommend acceptance. Confidence: medium.
