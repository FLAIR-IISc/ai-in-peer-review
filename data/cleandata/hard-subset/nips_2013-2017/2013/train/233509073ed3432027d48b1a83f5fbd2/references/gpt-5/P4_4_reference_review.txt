Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a heuristic online algorithm for learning and tracking time-varying graphical model structure, specifically linear-Gaussian recursive SEMs (DAGs). The core idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective sample size”) with adaptive per-sample weights ar that increase when recent data are unlikely under the current model. Unlikeliness is measured via Mahalanobis distances converted to p-values using Hotelling’s T^2 and then pooled over time using Lipták’s method; a thresholded transformation of the pooled p-value controls the forgetting factor (ar+1/ar). Structure is relearned periodically, either on a fixed schedule (for comparison to batch PC) or stochastically with a “noisy-OR” update whose probability increases when pooled p-values are small. The paper discusses a convergence–diligence trade-off parameterized by the threshold T and multiplier γ, with informal proof sketches for limiting behavior in certain regimes. Synthetic experiments compare LoSST to a batch PC algorithm under abrupt changes to both structure+parameters and parameters-only. A small real-data study on US price index volatility suggests LoSST detects macroeconomic regime shifts.

The work is related to: (i) batch score-based and constraint-based structure learning for DAGs (e.g., Pearl; Spirtes et al.; Chickering), which assume i.i.d. data; (ii) online changepoint detection methods (Bayesian and frequentist), which typically assume abrupt shifts and maintain substantial histories; (iii) time-varying graph estimation in undirected models (Talih & Hengartner) and time-indexed Bayesian edges (Siracusa & Fisher), which are batch. The method also connects to adaptive forgetting and exponentially weighted statistics (e.g., TD(0)) and to statistical process control via monitoring Mahalanobis distances.

Strengths
- Importance: Addresses a practical and underexplored problem—online tracking of changing DAG structure—where batch assumptions are violated.
- Simplicity and modularity: Maintains O(p^2) sufficient statistics with single-pass updates; can pair with different structure learners; probabilistic relearning trades accuracy vs compute.
- Empirical signal: On synthetic data with abrupt shifts, LoSST substantially outperforms batch PC after changes while matching it during stable periods. The CPI case study is plausible and interpretable.
- Conceptual contribution: Clear articulation of the convergence–diligence tension with parameterization that gives practitioners a knob to tune responsiveness.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Effective sample size calibration: The update Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1)Sr + 1 is ad hoc. Standard ESS for weighted samples is (Σw)^2/(Σw^2). Using Sr as degrees of freedom in Hotelling’s T^2 may miscalibrate p-values (affecting both false alarms and detection delay). A derivation, calibration study, or replacement with standard ESS would strengthen validity.
- Distributional assumptions and dependence: Lipták (Stouffer) pooling assumes independent, uniform p-values under the null. Here, p-values are serially dependent and computed using parameters estimated from the same data window. Consequences for Type I error and adaptivity are not analyzed. Alternatives (e.g., EWMA/CUSUM-style statistics, Brown’s method for dependent p-values, block-bootstrap calibration) or empirical calibration would help.
- Numerical stability: Frequent inversion of Cr is required for Mahalanobis distances. With small effective sample sizes or near-singularity, this can be unstable. No discussion of regularization (e.g., ridge/shrinkage to ensure SPD) or rank-deficiency handling is provided.
- Interface to PC: It is unclear how conditional independence tests are calibrated with weighted statistics. Does PC use Fisher’s z based on partial correlations with N = Sr? If so, miscalibration of Sr again matters. The paper should specify the exact tests and how weighting affects them.
- Theory: Proofs of convergence/diligence are sketch-level and rely on idealized conditions (e.g., “probability 1 convergence of the structure learner”). No finite-sample guarantees, false alarm rates, or detection delay bounds are provided.
- Baselines: Missing strong baselines that are natural for this setting, such as:
  - Sliding-window PC and exponentially weighted PC with fixed forgetting factor.
  - Online CPD (e.g., Adams & MacKay) coupled with retraining on post-change data.
  - Time-varying Gaussian graphical models (e.g., kernel-smoothed/TV-regularized GGM), dynamic/temporal GGM (Kolar & Xing; Hallac et al.), or dynamic Bayesian networks.
These are important to establish LoSST’s advantage beyond batch PC on mixtures.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: T, γ, ν are chosen heuristically (e.g., T=0.05, γ=3). A sensitivity analysis is needed to understand robustness, failure modes, and how to set parameters in practice.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but some notation and editorial issues impede reproducibility:
  - T denotes both the threshold and Hotelling’s T^2 (consider distinct symbols).
  - Indexing of pr, pr+1 and inclusion/exclusion in ρr is easy to misread; a compact algorithm box would help.
  - Typos (e.g., “occcurs,” “graph2”) and footnotes embedded in text disrupt flow.
  - Complexity per step and memory costs (and any inverse updates, e.g., Sherman–Morrison) are not detailed.

Originality
- The particular combination—adaptive forgetting based on pooled Mahalanobis p-values to drive online sufficient statistics, coupled to episodic constraint-based structure relearning—is, to my knowledge, novel for DAG SEMs. However, the idea of adaptively weighted statistics and time-varying network estimation has substantial prior art. Positioning against sliding/exponential windows and time-varying GGM/DBN literature is incomplete.

Significance
- Potentially useful for practitioners needing online tracking with small memory and simple updates. Current evidence indicates promise for abrupt-change scenarios. However, without stronger baselines, calibration, and theory, it is difficult to assess broader impact or reliability in varied regimes (gradual drift, high dimensionality, non-Gaussian noise, mixed discrete/continuous).

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Tackles a relevant problem with a simple, modular approach.
- Positive synthetic and real-data indications; matches batch performance when stationary.
- Conceptual framing of convergence vs diligence is insightful.

Cons:
- Key statistical components (ESS, p-value pooling, CI testing) are insufficiently justified and may be miscalibrated.
- Missing comparisons to natural baselines and related time-varying network methods.
- Limited theory and sensitivity analysis; some implementation details unclear.

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace or validate Sr with standard ESS and calibrate Hotelling’s T^2 accordingly; include simulations showing p-value calibration.
- Regularize covariance estimates; detail inverse update method and numerical safeguards.
- Specify the CI tests used by PC with weighted stats and validate their calibration under weighting.
- Add baselines: sliding-window/exponential-forgetting PC; CPD+retrain; time-varying GGM/DBN.
- Provide ablations on pooling method (Fisher/Stouffer/Brown), hyperparameters (T, γ, ν), and drift types (abrupt vs gradual).
- Clarify computational complexity and provide pseudocode.
- Expand related work to include time-varying network estimation literature.
- Improve notation and fix typos.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem is important and the approach is promising, but the current version lacks statistical calibration, key baselines, and theoretical/empirical rigor needed for NeurIPS. Strengthening these aspects could make it a strong submission.
