This paper presents LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a novel and heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models in an online fashion from data whose underlying generating process may change over time. The core idea is to move away from batch learning on i.i.d. data and instead track the sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an "effective" sample size) using a dynamic weighting scheme. The weights are updated based on the "surprise" of new data points, quantified by their Mahalanobis distance from the current estimated distribution. By pooling p-values of these distances, the system robustly detects changes, increases the weight of recent data, and triggers a relearning of the graphical model structure using a standard algorithm (here, the PC algorithm). The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the algorithm's behavior in terms of the "diligence vs. convergence" trade-off, and presents simulation results and a real-world application to demonstrate its efficacy.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a critical and practical limitation of most graphical model structure learning algorithms: the i.i.d. assumption. The problem of learning in non-stationary environments is highly relevant. The proposed approach is novel and elegant. Instead of developing a completely new structure learning algorithm, LoSST provides a clever "wrapper" that dynamically maintains weighted sufficient statistics, allowing existing, well-understood batch learners to be deployed in an online setting. This is a powerful and pragmatic contribution.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The core mechanics of the algorithm are well-motivated. The use of Mahalanobis distance is a standard way to measure the surprise of a multivariate data point. The decision to pool p-values over time using Liptak's method, rather than reacting to a single outlier, adds significant robustness. The online update equations for the weighted mean and covariance are correct and efficient.

3.  **Theoretical Grounding:** A major strength of the paper is the discussion in Section 3.1 regarding the trade-off between convergence and diligence. This shows a deep understanding of the fundamental challenges in online learning. Providing explicit parameter conditions (`γ`, `T`) that provably place LoSST in convergent or diligent regimes elevates the work from a pure heuristic to a more principled framework. This analysis is insightful and adds significant value.

4.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The simulations are well-designed. Comparing LoSST to a batch PC algorithm clearly demonstrates the failure of standard methods when the i.i.d. assumption is violated and highlights the benefits of LoSST. The experiments rightly distinguish between structure+parameter changes and parameter-only changes, revealing interesting nuances in performance (e.g., LoSST's initial poorer performance on edge removal in the latter case). The probabilistic relearning experiment effectively shows that the method can achieve good performance without the prohibitive cost of relearning at every step. The application to US price index data is a compelling real-world demonstration, plausibly identifying a known macroeconomic shift.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Justification of Effective Sample Size:** The update equation for the effective sample size (Eq. 3) is a central, yet heuristic, part of the model. The p-value calculation for the Mahalanobis distance relies on this effective sample size (`Sr`). This introduces a potential circularity or at least a strong approximation that is not fully analyzed. The distribution of the Mahalanobis distance assumes the covariance matrix is estimated from a fixed number of i.i.d. samples. Here, it is estimated from differentially weighted samples, and the "sample size" itself is a function of those weights. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the statistical validity of using this `Sr` in the T-squared distribution and the potential impact of this approximation.

2.  **Comparison to Baselines:** While the comparison to a batch PC algorithm run on the entire dataset is useful for illustration, it is a relatively weak baseline. A more informative comparison would include other plausible online/dynamic approaches. For instance:
    *   **A Sliding Window Approach:** Running the PC algorithm on a sliding window of the last `k` data points is a simple and common heuristic for non-stationary data. How does LoSST compare to this?
    *   **CPD + Batch Learning:** A more sophisticated baseline would be to first run an online changepoint detection (CPD) algorithm (like the cited Bayesian CPD) and then apply the batch PC learner to the data segments between detected changepoints. This would provide a stronger test of LoSST's integrated approach.

3.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The key parameters (`T`, `γ`, `ν`) appear to be set based on "unsystematic investigation." While hyperparameter tuning is always a challenge, the paper would benefit from a sensitivity analysis. How does performance degrade as these parameters move away from their chosen values? Providing practitioners with more guidance on setting these parameters (perhaps based on desired sensitivity to change or computational budget) would increase the paper's practical impact.

4.  **Clarity on Generalizability:** The paper claims the core algorithm is readily adaptable to other models (undirected graphs, discrete variables). However, the challenges are non-trivial. For discrete Bayesian networks, tracking sufficient statistics (conditional count tables) can be memory-intensive, especially with changing weights. For score-based learning, the decomposability of scores like BIC/BDeu might be lost under this differential data weighting scheme. A brief but more concrete discussion of these challenges in the "Discussion" section would make the claims of generalizability more credible.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and effective solution to the important problem of online graphical model structure learning. The core idea of tracking weighted sufficient statistics is both simple and powerful. The theoretical analysis of the diligence-convergence trade-off is a standout feature. The experiments are convincing, though the choice of baseline could be stronger. The weaknesses identified, particularly concerning the statistical justification for the effective sample size update and the lack of parameter sensitivity analysis, are areas for improvement but do not undermine the core contribution.

This work represents a significant step forward for learning in non-stationary environments and is likely to be influential. It addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and provides a pragmatic approach that others can build upon. I recommend acceptance.
