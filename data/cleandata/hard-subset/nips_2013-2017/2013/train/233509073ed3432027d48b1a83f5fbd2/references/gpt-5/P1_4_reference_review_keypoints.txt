- Proposes LoSST: a heuristic single-pass online algorithm that learns/tracks time-varying graphical model structure using weighted sufficient statistics and periodic PC relearning.
- Maintains adaptive weighted mean, covariance, and an “effective” sample size ar that increases when recent data poorly fit the current model.
- Detects poor fit via Mahalanobis distances → p-values, pools them over time with Liptak’s method to produce ρr, which drives weight updates and relearning probability.
- Provides update equations, discusses a convergence–diligence trade-off controlled by T and γ, and offers proof sketches for extreme regimes.
- Synthetic experiments: LoSST matches batch PC in stationary settings and adapts faster than pooled-batch PC after changepoints.
- Real-data case study (CPI components) qualitatively detects the Great Moderation, but evaluation is limited and qualitative.
- Positioned against batch structure learning, TD tracking, CPD, and two older offline time-varying graph methods; main novelty is the pooled-GOF-driven adaptive forgetting tied to online sufficient statistics.
- Fails to compare against more recent/time-varying Gaussian-graphical-model baselines (sliding-window, exponential weighting, CPD+window, fused penalties, kernel smoothing).
- Strength: addresses real-time structure learning with a memory-light O(p^2) footprint and practical update rules.
- Strength: conceptually appealing coupling of pooled goodness-of-fit to adaptive forgetting and relearning schedule.
- Strength: clear articulation of the convergence vs diligence trade-off and honest discussion of heuristics.
- Strength: synthetic results suggest practical benefits of adaptive weighting; real data shows plausible regime detection.
- Major concern: statistical validity of pooled p-value ρ under adaptive, dependent estimation is not justified; null calibration and false-alarm control are unclear.
- Major concern: the “effective sample size” update is ad hoc with no derivation supporting downstream distributional use.
- Major concern: theoretical guarantees rely on impractical extreme thresholds (T outside [0,1]); practically relevant regimes lack formal guarantees.
- Major concern: baselines used are weak/unfair (pooled batch PC post-change); missing fair online baselines weakens empirical claims.
- Major concern: interface to PC is under-specified—how are CI tests adapted to weighted covariances/effective n, how is regularization handled, and how are α-levels/multiple testing addressed?
- Major concern: computational cost of repeated inversions and frequent PC runs is not analyzed—no timing or complexity vs p, degree, ν.
- Major concern: real-data evaluation is qualitative—no time-varying learned graphs, predictive checks, or comparisons to alternative detectors.
- Quality assessment: moderate—sound heuristic engineering and suggestive simulations but insufficient inferential justification, theory, and baselines.
- Clarity: generally well written; needs clearer derivations for Eq.(3), explicit PC CI-test adaptation, covariance regularization discussion, and correction/clarification of threshold use.
- Originality: moderate—the synthesis of pooled-GOF adaptive weights with online sufficient statistics is novel but not well connected to related time-varying GGM literature.
- Significance: potentially useful for practitioners seeking lightweight online methods, but needs stronger theory or empirical validation to be impactful.
- Key questions for authors: can ρ be empirically calibrated under stationarity; how are ill-conditioned covariances handled; how exactly are CI tests adapted; sensitivity to T, γ, ν; comparisons to sliding-window and CPD+window baselines?
- Suggestions: add theory or empirical calibration for ρ, include runtime/scalability analysis, strengthen baselines and ablations, and show learned graphs over time with interpretability checks.
- Pros for acceptance: tackles an important online problem, simple implementable method, promising synthetic results and real-world case study.
- Cons for acceptance: unclear inferential validity of pooled p-values/effective n, theory doesn’t cover practical regime, missing strong baselines, under-specified PC integration and regularization.
- Overall recommendation: lean reject—interesting and potentially useful but needs stronger theoretical grounding or more comprehensive empirical validation.
