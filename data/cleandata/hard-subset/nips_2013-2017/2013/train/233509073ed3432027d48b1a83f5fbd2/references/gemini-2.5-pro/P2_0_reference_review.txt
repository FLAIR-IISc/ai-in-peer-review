### NIPS Review

**Summary:**
This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel online algorithm for learning directed graphical model structure from data where the underlying generating process may change over time. The core assumption is that the data generating process is "locally stationary." The algorithm works by maintaining and updating weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an "effective" sample size) for a set of continuous variables. New data points are weighted based on their fit to the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. A poor fit, indicated by a low pooled p-value over recent data, increases the weight of new data, effectively "forgetting" older, potentially irrelevant data. This allows the algorithm to adapt to changes. A standard constraint-based structure learning algorithm (PC) is then run on these dynamically updated statistics, with a probabilistic trigger to control the computational cost of frequent relearning. The authors provide simulation results showing that LoSST successfully tracks both structural and parametric changes, outperforming a batch-mode learner, while incurring no performance penalty in stable environments. They also demonstrate its application on a real-world macroeconomic dataset.

**Quality:**
The paper is of high quality and technically sound. The proposed method is well-motivated, and the mathematical derivations for the online updates of weighted mean, covariance (Eq. 1 & 2), and effective sample size (Eq. 3) are clearly presented. The use of Mahalanobis distance as a measure of "surprise" is a standard and appropriate choice for multivariate Gaussian data. The mechanism for converting this surprise into a dynamic weight via a pooled p-value is clever, correctly identifying that a single outlier should be treated differently from a persistent shift.

The experimental evaluation is well-designed. The simulations systematically test the algorithm's response to both structural and parametric shifts, which are important and distinct scenarios. The comparison against the batch-mode PC algorithm serves as a strong baseline to demonstrate the necessity of an online approach. The inclusion of a simulation with the probabilistic relearning mechanism (Fig. 3) validates this practical component of the algorithm, showing it can reduce computational load without a significant loss in accuracy.

A particularly strong aspect is the theoretical discussion of the "convergence vs. diligence" trade-off. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the fundamental challenges in online learning and provides a principled way to reason about the algorithm's behavior under different parameter settings (γ and T). The authors provide proof sketches for conditions under which LoSST is provably convergent or diligent, which adds significant rigor to the work.

**Clarity:**
The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The authors first motivate the problem, situate their work within the literature, and then build up the LoSST algorithm piece by piece: tracking sufficient statistics, updating weights, and triggering relearning. The equations are presented logically, and the text explains the intuition behind them. The experimental setup and results are easy to follow, and the figures are informative. The paper provides enough detail that an expert could likely reproduce the core algorithm.

One minor point for improvement would be a more detailed justification for the specific functional form of the weight update (Eq. 4) and the probabilistic relearning trigger. While the general principle is sound, the specific choices seem somewhat heuristic. A brief discussion of why these specific forms were chosen over alternatives would enhance clarity.

**Originality:**
The paper addresses a problem—online graphical model *structure* learning—that is both important and relatively under-explored. While online parameter learning (e.g., TDL) and changepoint detection (CPD) are established fields, their application to the super-exponential search space of graph structures is non-trivial. LoSST presents a novel and pragmatic synthesis of ideas from these different areas. It combines online sufficient statistic tracking with a principled change detection mechanism (based on pooled p-values) to dynamically control the "memory" of a standard batch structure learner. This approach of creating a dynamic, weighted "virtual" dataset for an off-the-shelf learner is an original and effective contribution.

The work is well-contextualized with respect to related literature. The authors clearly distinguish their online, real-time approach from previous batch-mode methods for time-varying graphs [14, 11] and highlight the shortcomings of standard online learning and CPD methods for this specific task.

**Significance:**
The significance of this work is high. The i.i.d. assumption underlying most structure learning algorithms is a major limitation for their application to many real-world domains, such as economics, neuroscience, and social networks, where relationships are known to evolve. This paper provides a practical, well-justified algorithm that directly tackles this limitation. The fact that LoSST's performance is indistinguishable from a batch learner in stable settings is a crucial feature, as it means practitioners can deploy it without penalty even if they are unsure whether the environment will change. This makes it a strong candidate for real-world application.

The conceptual framework of balancing convergence and diligence is also a significant contribution that could inform the design of other online learning systems. By making this trade-off explicit and controllable via parameters, the authors provide a valuable lens for reasoning about algorithmic design for non-stationary environments. Researchers working on related problems are likely to build upon these ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Addresses a critical, practical problem:** It relaxes the restrictive i.i.d. assumption of standard graphical model structure learning.
2.  **Novel and sound algorithm:** LoSST is a well-designed synthesis of existing statistical tools into a new framework for online structure learning.
3.  **Strong empirical validation:** The simulation studies are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the algorithm's ability to track changes and its advantages over a standard batch approach.
4.  **Insightful theoretical contribution:** The discussion and formalization of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off is a valuable and generalizable insight.
5.  **High potential impact:** The algorithm is directly applicable to a wide range of fields where dynamic systems are studied.

**Arguments for Rejection/Major Revision:**
1.  **Limited baseline comparison:** The primary baseline is a batch-mode algorithm, which is expected to fail on non-i.i.d. data. A stronger comparison would be against a hybrid approach, such as coupling a formal changepoint detection algorithm (e.g., [1]) with a batch learner run on data within detected segments. This would better establish the state of the art.
2.  **Parameter sensitivity:** The key hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) are chosen based on "unsystematic investigation." The paper would be significantly strengthened by a sensitivity analysis to show how performance varies with these parameters and provide guidance on setting them.
3.  **Heuristic components:** The specific functional forms for weight updates and relearning probability are heuristic. While effective, a more formal justification or exploration of alternatives would improve the work's rigor.

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and effective solution to an important problem. The strengths—particularly the clear presentation, thorough experiments, and insightful theoretical discussion—far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance. The suggested improvements, such as a stronger baseline comparison and a parameter sensitivity analysis, would further enhance the paper but are not critical for its acceptance.
