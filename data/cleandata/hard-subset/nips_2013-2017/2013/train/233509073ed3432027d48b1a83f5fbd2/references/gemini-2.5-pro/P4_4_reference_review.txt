### Summary

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a heuristic online algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from data where the underlying generating process may change over time. The core assumption is that the data-generating process is "locally stationary," meaning it is i.i.d. for short periods but can change between periods. The proposed method works by tracking weighted sufficient statistics—specifically the mean, covariance, and an "effective sample size"—for continuous variables under a Structural Equation Model (SEM) assumption.

The algorithm updates these statistics with each new data point. The weight assigned to new data is dynamically adjusted based on how well recent data points fit the current learned model. Fit is measured by the Mahalanobis distance, and a sequence of poor fits (identified by pooling p-values using Liptak's method) is interpreted as a change in the underlying structure. This triggers an increase in the weights of new data points, effectively down-weighting old data and allowing the algorithm to adapt. The actual, computationally expensive, structure learning (using the PC algorithm in this implementation) is performed probabilistically, with the probability of "relearning" increasing when a change is detected.

The paper provides a theoretical analysis of the algorithm's behavior, framing it in terms of a provably incompatible trade-off between "convergence" (stabilizing on a correct model in a stable environment) and "diligence" (rapidly detecting change). It shows how LoSST's parameters can be set to favor one property over the other, or to strike a balance. The algorithm is evaluated on synthetic data with known changepoints and on a real-world macroeconomic dataset, demonstrating its ability to track structural changes and its superior performance over a standard batch-mode learning algorithm applied to the entire non-stationary dataset.

This work relates to several areas. It extends standard batch structure learning algorithms [3, 12] to the online, non-stationary setting. Unlike simple online parameter estimation methods like TD-learning [13], it focuses on learning graph *structure*. Compared to online changepoint detection (CPD) algorithms [1, 4], it is designed for general structure learning rather than detecting shifts in specific parameters and avoids the need to store a large history of data points. Finally, unlike previous work on time-varying graphs [11, 14], LoSST is a true online, single-pass algorithm.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is sound and well-motivated. The derivations of the online update equations for the weighted sufficient statistics (Eqs. 1-3) are standard but correctly applied to this novel context. The use of Mahalanobis distance as a measure of fit and, crucially, the use of pooled p-values to distinguish between single outliers and genuine structural changes, is a principled approach.

The theoretical analysis of the convergence-diligence trade-off is a significant strength. It grounds the heuristic nature of the algorithm in a formal framework [5] and provides clear conditions on the parameters (`γ`, `T`) that determine the algorithm's long-run behavior. This adds a layer of rigor not always present in heuristic online methods.

The claims are well-supported by experimental results. The simulations are thoughtfully designed to test different scenarios (structure vs. parameter change) and provide clear quantitative comparisons against a reasonable baseline. The authors are careful in their analysis, for instance, by explaining why LoSST might temporarily underperform on edge removal errors after a parameter-only change. The application to real-world data, while not a substitute for a controlled experiment, serves as a good plausibility check, showing that the method identifies a well-known period of macroeconomic change.

### Clarity

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, and the related work section precisely situates the contribution with respect to existing literature. The algorithm's components are introduced sequentially and logically, with clear explanations for the role of each part (weighted statistics, change detection, probabilistic relearning). The mathematical notation is consistent and the derivations, while condensed, are comprehensible. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly with informative figures. The paper provides enough detail that an expert could likely reproduce the core algorithm.

### Originality

The paper's contribution is highly original. While online learning and graphical model learning are established fields, the problem of *online structure learning* in non-stationary environments has received relatively little attention. The LoSST algorithm presents a novel and complete solution to this problem. The combination of techniques is also new: using dynamically weighted sufficient statistics, where the weights are controlled by a principled statistical test (pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances), to trigger probabilistic structure relearning is a novel approach. The formalization of the problem in terms of the convergence-diligence trade-off is also an original contribution in this specific context. The work is clearly differentiated from prior art, which is either batch-based, focused only on parameters, or assumes specific types of change.

### Significance

The problem addressed by this paper is of high significance. The assumption of i.i.d. data is a major limitation of most current structure learning methods, yet many real-world domains, from finance and neuroscience to climate science, involve systems with dynamically changing structures. An effective online algorithm like LoSST could enable new applications in these areas. The fact that the algorithm performs comparably to a batch learner in stable environments means it could be deployed with little-to-no performance cost, even when changes are rare.

Researchers are likely to build on these ideas. The modular design allows for swapping in different base structure learners (beyond PC) or different statistical tests for change detection. The theoretical framework of convergence vs. diligence provides a useful lens for analyzing other online learning algorithms. The work demonstrably advances the state-of-the-art by providing the first, to my knowledge, practical and general-purpose online algorithm for graphical model structure learning.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** Addresses an important, under-explored problem with a novel and complete algorithmic solution. The potential impact is high.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The algorithm is well-designed, and its key trade-offs are analyzed with theoretical rigor.
3.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive, using multiple synthetic scenarios and a real-world dataset to demonstrate the method's effectiveness.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is restricted to linear SEMs on continuous data. The paper acknowledges that extending to discrete models is challenging, but the difficulty of this extension might limit immediate applicability.
2.  **Baseline Comparison:** The baseline (batch PC on the full dataset) is a sensible but perhaps weak choice, as it is expected to fail. A comparison against a sliding-window approach or a state-of-the-art batch CPD algorithm followed by learning could have provided a stronger benchmark.
3.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm relies on several key parameters (`γ`, `T`, `ν`), and their selection is described as "unsystematic." A more detailed sensitivity analysis would strengthen the paper's practical utility.

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, sound, and significant contribution to the field of machine learning.
