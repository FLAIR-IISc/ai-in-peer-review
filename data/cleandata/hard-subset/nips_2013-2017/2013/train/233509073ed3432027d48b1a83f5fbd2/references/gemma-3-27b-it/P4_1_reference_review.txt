The paper "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure" introduces a heuristic algorithm, LoSST, for online learning of graphical model structure in non-stationary environments. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) dynamically, weighting datapoints based on their “fit” to the current model, and then relearning the graphical model structure when a change is detected via Mahalanobis distance and a pooled p-value. The algorithm is framed as a trade-off between convergence and diligence, and simulation results demonstrate its ability to track changes in both structure and parameters, outperforming a batch-mode algorithm after a change point. A real-world application to US price index volatility is also presented.

This paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem. Existing graphical model structure learning algorithms largely assume i.i.d. data, a limitation in many real-world applications where the underlying generating process evolves. The proposed approach attempts to bridge this gap with an online learning framework, allowing for adaptation to changing structures.  Related work is well-summarized, covering Bayesian/score-based and constraint-based methods, online changepoint detection, and previous attempts at time-indexed graph structures. The authors correctly point out the limitations of these existing approaches for the specific problem of *online* structure learning.  The connection to Temporal Difference Learning (TDL) is also insightful.

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear presentation of a novel algorithm and the thorough evaluation through simulations.  The algorithm's components (sufficient statistics tracking, change detection based on Mahalanobis distance and p-values, and probabilistic relearning) are well-motivated and explained. The simulation setup is comprehensive, exploring scenarios with changes in both structure and parameters, and the results convincingly demonstrate LoSST’s superior performance compared to a batch-mode approach *after* a change point.  The real-world application provides a compelling illustration of the algorithm's potential. The discussion of the convergence-diligence trade-off is a valuable contribution, and the mathematical formulation of the algorithm’s parameters in relation to these properties is strong. 

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The heuristic nature of the algorithm is not fully acknowledged as a limitation. While the authors discuss the trade-off between convergence and diligence, the parameter tuning (T and γ) appears somewhat ad-hoc, with the chosen values (T=0.05, γ=3) based on “unsystematic investigation”. A more systematic exploration of the parameter space and its impact on performance would be beneficial. The choice of the PC algorithm for structure learning is reasonable, but the algorithm's sensitivity to parameter settings is not addressed.  Could other structure learning algorithms improve performance? The computational complexity of the algorithm, beyond the mention of "efficient computation," is not explicitly analyzed. How does the runtime scale with the number of variables and the frequency of change?  Furthermore, while the simulations demonstrate performance improvement, quantifying the statistical significance of these improvements (e.g., using confidence intervals) would strengthen the claims.  The application section presents a high-level observation rather than a rigorous evaluation.  Simply demonstrating that LoSST “detects” a change is not sufficient; a comparative analysis with other changepoint detection methods would be more convincing. The writing quality, while generally good, contains some instances of repetitive phrasing (e.g., reiterating the advantages over batch-mode learning).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a challenging and practically relevant problem in graphical model learning.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated algorithm (LoSST) for online structure learning.
*   Thorough evaluation through simulations demonstrating superior performance after a change point.
*   Clear presentation and discussion of the convergence-diligence trade-off.
*   Real-world application illustrates the algorithm’s potential.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Heuristic nature of the algorithm and ad-hoc parameter tuning.
*   Limited analysis of computational complexity.
*   Lack of statistical significance testing in simulation results.
*   Weak evaluation in the real-world application.
*   Potential for improvement by exploring alternative structure learning algorithms.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field.  The proposed algorithm addresses an important gap in the literature, and the simulations and real-world application demonstrate its potential. While there are some weaknesses, they do not fundamentally undermine the paper’s value.  I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding parameter tuning, computational complexity, and statistical significance.  Specifically, the authors should provide a more systematic analysis of parameter sensitivity and include confidence intervals in the simulation results.
