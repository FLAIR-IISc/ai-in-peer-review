

# Peer Review: "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

## Summary
This paper introduces LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), an algorithm for online learning of graphical model structures when the underlying generating structure may change over time. The authors address a critical gap in the literature: existing structure learning methods assume i.i.d. data from a stable structure, while real-world applications often require tracking dynamic structures in real-time. LoSST tracks sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) online using a weighted approach where weights increase when data fit the current model poorly (detected via Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values). The paper demonstrates that LoSST performs as well as batch methods when structure is stable, and adapts to structural changes when they occur. The work builds on online learning (TD(0)) and changepoint detection literature but makes a novel contribution to graphical model structure learning.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement and important contribution**: The paper effectively identifies the gap between standard graphical model structure learning (which assumes stable structure) and real-world applications where structure changes dynamically. This is a significant and timely problem in the field.

2. **Well-motivated methodology**: The online tracking of sufficient statistics using weighted updates is theoretically sound and well-explained. The connection to TD(0) learning and the careful derivation of update equations for means, covariances, and effective sample size demonstrate strong technical understanding.

3. **Insightful theoretical analysis**: The paper's exploration of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off (proving conditions under which LoSST is convergent or diligent) is particularly strong. This provides valuable guidance for practitioners on parameter selection.

4. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The simulation studies thoroughly test LoSST's performance across different structural change scenarios (graph changes vs. parameter changes) and provide clear comparisons to batch methods. The real-world application to US price index volatility demonstrates practical utility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited theoretical guarantees for high-dimensional settings**: While the algorithm is well-described for low-dimensional problems, the paper doesn't address how it scales to high-dimensional graphical models (where the number of variables is large relative to sample size). This is a major limitation for practical applications of graphical models, as high-dimensionality is common.

2. **Ad hoc aspects of the algorithm**: The probabilistic relearning mechanism (using a noisy-OR gate with parameter ν) lacks principled justification. The paper doesn't provide guidance on selecting ν, which significantly impacts performance. The threshold T for the pooled p-value is also chosen empirically without theoretical justification.

3. **Incomplete comparison to relevant work**: The paper claims to be the first online graphical structure learning algorithm, but the related work section doesn't fully address how LoSST differs from recent work in dynamic graphical models (e.g., [14, 11] mentioned in the paper). The authors should more clearly explain why these previous approaches can't be adapted for online learning.

4. **Limited real-world validation**: The US price index application uses only 6 variables, which is too small to demonstrate the algorithm's utility for meaningful applications. The paper would be stronger with a more substantial real-world application.

5. **Assumption limitations**: The paper assumes a specific model class (recursive SEMs with linear equations) but doesn't discuss how the algorithm would generalize to other graphical models (e.g., Bayesian networks with discrete variables), which are widely used in practice.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by addressing an important problem with a well-constructed algorithm. The core methodology is technically sound, the theoretical analysis is insightful, and the empirical evaluation is thorough for the settings tested. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the limitations noted above.

The main concerns are the lack of high-dimensional scalability analysis, the ad hoc nature of some algorithmic choices, and the limited real-world validation. These are not deal-breakers, but they should be addressed in revision.

## Recommendation

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The paper addresses an important problem with a technically sound solution that advances the state of the art in graphical model learning. The authors should address the weaknesses noted above, particularly by:
1. Providing more discussion of high-dimensional scalability
2. Justifying the probabilistic relearning mechanism more thoroughly
3. Expanding the real-world application to a more substantial dataset
4. Clarifying the relationship to related work

The paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS, particularly given the growing interest in dynamic models and online learning. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field that will likely be useful to both practitioners and researchers.

## Specific Suggestions for Revision

1. Add a section discussing high-dimensional considerations and potential modifications for high-dimensional settings.

2. Provide a more principled approach for selecting the probabilistic relearning parameter ν, perhaps with a small empirical study showing how performance varies with ν.

3. Extend the real-world application to a larger dataset (e.g., with 20+ variables) to better demonstrate the algorithm's utility.

4. Clarify in the related work section why previous approaches (Talih & Hengartner, Siracusa & Fisher) cannot be adapted for online learning, rather than simply stating they require batch processing.

5. Discuss how the algorithm would need to be modified for other graphical model types (e.g., Bayesian networks with discrete variables).
