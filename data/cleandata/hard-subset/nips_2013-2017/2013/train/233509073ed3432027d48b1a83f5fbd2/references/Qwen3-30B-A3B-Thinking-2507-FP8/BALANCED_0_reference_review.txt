

# Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

This paper proposes the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm for online learning of graphical model structure when the underlying generating structure changes over time. The authors address a significant gap in the literature, as most existing structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data from a single static structure. LoSST assumes only local stationarity (i.i.d. within short time windows) and tracks sufficient statistics online, dynamically adjusting weights based on Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values to detect structure changes. The algorithm demonstrates comparable performance to batch-mode learning in stable periods and effectively tracks structure changes in subsequent periods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles an important practical problem in graphical model learning where data streams exhibit changing underlying structures. This is a genuine gap in the literature, as the authors correctly note that existing online learning methods cannot handle general graphical model structure learning. The problem is well-motivated by real-world applications in finance, economics, and other domains where structures change over time.

2. **Quality:** The theoretical foundation is sound, with careful treatment of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off. The algorithmic design is well-justified, and the mathematical derivations are precise. The simulation experiments are thorough, with multiple scenarios testing different aspects of structure change (graph changes vs. parameter changes), and the real-world application to US price index data provides compelling evidence of the method's utility.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the algorithm and its components. The mathematical formulations are precise and well-organized, making the algorithm accessible to readers familiar with graphical models and online learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope and Generalizability:** The paper focuses exclusively on recursive SEMs with continuous variables, which is a narrow class of graphical models. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately address how the algorithm could be extended to other graphical model types (e.g., Bayesian networks with discrete variables or Markov random fields). This significantly limits the paper's applicability to broader domains.

2. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The related work section doesn't sufficiently compare with recent advances in time-varying graphical models. For instance, the paper doesn't discuss or contrast with methods like dynamic Bayesian networks, sliding window approaches, or more recent online structure learning algorithms that have emerged since the cited works. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the paper's positioning.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity and Implementation Details:** The performance of LoSST depends on several parameters (T, γ, ν), but the paper doesn't provide a systematic analysis of how sensitive the algorithm is to these choices. The authors mention "unsystematic investigation" led to T=0.05 and γ=3, which is insufficient for a rigorous evaluation. Additionally, the computational complexity and memory requirements of the algorithm aren't discussed, which are critical for real-world implementation.

4. **Experimental Validation:** While the synthetic experiments are well-designed, the real-world application to US price index data is somewhat limited. The paper doesn't compare LoSST with other time-varying graphical model methods on this dataset, making it difficult to assess the relative performance improvements. A more comprehensive evaluation on both synthetic and real-world benchmarks would strengthen the claims.

**Recommendation:**

The paper addresses an important problem with a novel solution that is theoretically sound and empirically validated. However, the limited scope and insufficient comparison with recent work on time-varying graphical models prevent it from meeting the high bar for NeurIPS acceptance. The authors should significantly strengthen the paper by:

1. Extending the algorithm to handle more general graphical models (e.g., discrete variables, Bayesian networks)
2. Providing a thorough comparison with recent methods for time-varying graphical models
3. Conducting a systematic analysis of parameter sensitivity and computational complexity
4. Expanding the experimental validation to include more comprehensive real-world benchmarks

With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the current version presents a promising approach but lacks sufficient novelty and breadth to warrant acceptance at NeurIPS. I recommend rejection, but with the understanding that with substantial revision, this work could become a strong candidate for future publication.
