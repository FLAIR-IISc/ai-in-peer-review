**Summary:**
This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel online algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from streaming data where the underlying generative process may change over time. The core problem it addresses—learning in non-stationary environments—is a critical and often overlooked limitation of standard structure learning algorithms that assume i.i.d. data. The proposed method cleverly tracks weighted sufficient statistics (mean and covariance for SEMs), where the weights are dynamically updated based on the fit of new data. A poor fit, quantified by the Mahalanobis distance and aggregated over time using a pooled p-value (Liptak's method), signals a potential change. This triggers an increase in the weights of recent data points, effectively "forgetting" the old statistics, and probabilistically initiates a relearning of the graphical structure using a standard batch algorithm (here, the PC algorithm). The paper provides a theoretical discussion of the trade-off between convergence (stability in a static environment) and diligence (rapid detection of change), showing how LoSST's parameters can navigate this trade-off. Empirical validation on synthetic data demonstrates LoSST's ability to outperform a batch learner after a structural change, while matching its performance in stable periods. A real-world application to US economic data further illustrates its potential.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a highly significant and practical problem. The i.i.d. assumption is a major barrier to applying graphical model learning in many real-world domains (e.g., finance, neuroscience, climate science). This work presents a well-motivated and principled approach to relax this assumption for online learning, a clear departure from prior batch-mode approaches to time-varying graphs.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The core mechanism of LoSST is elegant and well-reasoned. The use of Mahalanobis distance is a natural choice for detecting distributional shifts in Gaussian data. The key innovation is the use of a weighted pooled p-value to decide when to update weights, which robustly distinguishes between single outliers and persistent changes. The update equations for the weighted sufficient statistics are correctly derived and allow for efficient online processing.

3.  **Theoretical Insight:** The discussion in Section 3.1 on the "convergence vs. diligence" trade-off is a major strength. It demonstrates a deep understanding of the fundamental challenges in online learning and provides a formal characterization of the algorithm's behavior under different parameter settings. This elevates the paper from a purely heuristic contribution to one with solid theoretical grounding.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The simulations are well-designed.
    *   They correctly use a batch learner as a baseline and show that LoSST incurs no performance penalty in stable environments.
    *   The separation of experiments into "structure change" and "parameter change" is insightful, revealing interesting nuances in performance (e.g., the temporary underperformance on edge removal errors).
    *   The experiment with probabilistic relearning validates the efficiency of the proposed trigger mechanism.
    *   The application to real-world economic data, successfully identifying a known period of structural change (the Great Moderation), provides a compelling proof-of-concept.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
1.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The paper claims the algorithm is efficient, but a critical step involves calculating the Mahalanobis distance, which requires inverting the current covariance matrix `Cr`. This is an O(V^3) operation at every single timestep for a V-variable system. This cost is prohibitive for high-dimensional problems (e.g., V > 100) and should be explicitly discussed as a major limitation. Are there approximations (e.g., using a sparse or low-rank inverse) that could mitigate this without compromising the change detection capability?

2.  **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The algorithm's behavior is critically dependent on the hyperparameters `T` (p-value threshold), `γ` (maximum weight increase), and `ν` (relearning rate). The paper states that `T=0.05` and `γ=3` were chosen based on "unsystematic investigation." This is a significant weakness. The paper would be much stronger with a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with these parameters. How does one set these parameters in a principled way for a new problem domain?

3.  **Dependence on the PC Algorithm:** The choice of the PC algorithm for structure learning is a potential vulnerability. PC is a constraint-based method known to be sensitive to errors in conditional independence tests, especially with small effective sample sizes. After a change is detected, the effective sample size `Sr` drops significantly, which is precisely when the PC algorithm is least reliable. While the paper notes other algorithms could be used, the interaction between the tracker's state (especially `Sr`) and the chosen learner's properties deserves more discussion. How might a score-based method, if adapted for this context, perform differently?

4.  **Scope of the Model:** The methodology is developed for linear SEMs with Gaussian noise. While the paper acknowledges this and mentions future work on discrete variables (Bayesian networks), the difficulty of this extension should not be understated. For Bayesian networks, the sufficient statistics are conditional count tables, which are exponential in the parent set size. Tracking these online in a weighted fashion is non-trivial. The current applicability of the method is therefore limited to a specific, albeit important, class of models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and important problem in machine learning.
*   Presents a novel and elegant online algorithm with a solid methodological foundation.
*   Includes valuable theoretical analysis of the convergence/diligence trade-off.
*   Supported by a comprehensive and convincing set of experiments, including a real-world application.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Fails to address the O(V^3) per-timestep computational cost, which severely limits scalability.
*   Lacks a systematic analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, making the method difficult to apply in practice.
*   The chosen structure learning component (PC algorithm) has known weaknesses that are particularly relevant in the post-changepoint, small-sample regime.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The problem is important, the proposed LoSST algorithm is novel and well-motivated, and the evaluation is largely convincing. The theoretical discussion adds significant depth. While the weaknesses regarding computational scalability and hyperparameter tuning are notable, they represent avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws in the core contribution. The paper is clearly written and provides sufficient detail to understand the method. I recommend acceptance. The authors should, however, be strongly encouraged to address the identified weaknesses in the final version, particularly by adding a discussion of the computational complexity and providing at least a preliminary analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity.
