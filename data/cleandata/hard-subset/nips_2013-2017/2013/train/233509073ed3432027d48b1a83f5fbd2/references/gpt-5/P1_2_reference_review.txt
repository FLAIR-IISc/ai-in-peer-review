Summary
The paper proposes LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), an online algorithm to learn and track time-varying graphical model structure for linear-Gaussian DAGs/SEMs. The core idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) that adapt to nonstationarity via data-dependent weights ar. The weights are increased when recent data poorly fit the current covariance, as measured by a pooled p-value ρ of Mahalanobis-distance-based p-values using Lipták’s method. The algorithm (re)learns graph structure intermittently—more often when ρ is small—using a standard constraint-based learner (PC). The authors analyze trade-offs between “convergence” and “diligence” via parameters T and γ controlling the weighting, provide proof sketches for some regimes, and evaluate on synthetic data with both structural and parametric changes (comparing to batch PC), on a single long series with probabilistic relearning, and on a CPI volatility dataset, where LoSST detects shifts consistent with the Great Moderation.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions LoSST against batch structure learning (score-based and constraint-based), online change-point detection (Bayesian and frequentist), TD learning, and two batch time-varying graph learners. The approach is different from storing large windows or maintaining posterior over structures; instead it adaptively reweights sufficient stats and triggers relearning. Related literatures that seem closely relevant but are not discussed include adaptive forgetting factors/EWMA/CUSUM for covariance tracking, recursive least squares with adaptive forgetting, dynamic/temporal Bayesian networks, and time-varying graphical models (e.g., graphical lasso with temporal regularization). Explicit comparisons to sliding-window or exponentially weighted baselines would strengthen the positioning.

Strengths
- Problem importance: Online tracking of changing causal/graphical structure is timely, challenging, and relevant.
- Practicality: Single-pass tracking of sufficient statistics is memory efficient; probabilistic relearning controls computational cost.
- Conceptual simplicity: The combination of adaptive weighting via pooled goodness-of-fit and off-the-shelf structure learning is appealing and easy to implement.
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic experiments show behavior pre- and post-change, and that LoSST can recover the new structure faster than batch PC on mixed data. The CPI case study is an interesting demonstration.
- Discussion of convergence vs. diligence: Nice articulation of a key tension and how parameters steer behavior.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Statistical validity of p-values: Using Mahalanobis distances with a covariance estimated from adaptively weighted and overlapping data does not yield the classical Hotelling T^2 distribution; the effective sample size Sr and dependence across p-values violate the assumptions behind both the single-point p-values and Lipták pooling. This risks miscalibrated alarms. Empirical calibration, permutation-based control, or a more principled change statistic (e.g., likelihood ratio, CUSUM/EWMA with known false alarm rates) would improve rigor.
- Numerical/stability issues: Inverting Cr online at each step can be costly and unstable when Sr is small. Regularization (shrinkage, diagonal loading), and/or maintaining Cr^{-1} via Sherman-Morrison-Woodbury updates should be discussed. What happens when Cr is near-singular?
- Interface to PC: Constraint tests in PC typically assume i.i.d. Gaussian samples with known sample size. Passing a weighted covariance and an “effective” Sr is heuristic; please justify or validate empirically that type I/II error rates are acceptable under weighting.
- Theory: The convergence/diligence section provides intuitive proof sketches, but lacks guarantees about correctness of structure recovery under piecewise-stationary regimes, detection delay, or false alarm control. The case T>1 is mathematically convenient but not interpretable as a p-value threshold.
- Experimental baselines: Comparing LoSST to batch PC on the full (mixed) dataset is expected to favor LoSST. Missing are fair baselines such as sliding-window PC, exponentially weighted PC, CPD+PC (segment then learn), or recent time-varying graphical model methods. Sensitivity analyses over T, γ, ν are limited; parameters were selected unsystematically.
- Real data validation: The CPI analysis is suggestive but anecdotal; no external validation of change points, and no presentation of learned graphs before/after to assess plausibility.

Clarity
- Overall writing is understandable, but some derivations are terse. Provide pseudo-code, define all parameters clearly, and specify the conditional independence tests used within PC under weighting.
- Clarify the update for Sr and the intuition behind ar/ar+1 scaling. Explain how Sr interacts with CI tests.
- Clean up references (missing years/pages), fix typos, and standardize notation.
- Discuss computational complexity per time step and per relearn, and provide run-time measurements.

Originality
- The specific combination—adaptive reweighting via pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances, with online sufficient-statistics tracking and intermittent structure relearning—appears novel. However, the connection to extensive prior work on adaptive forgetting, online covariance/change detection, and time-varying graphical models should be strengthened.

Significance
- If further validated, LoSST could be a useful engineering solution for online structure tracking with modest memory. As presented, the lack of calibrated detection, limited baselines, and heuristic interfaces to PC reduce impact. Strengthening empirical comparisons and theoretical guarantees would raise significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and understudied online problem with a simple, implementable method.
- Single-pass, memory-light sufficient statistics; probabilistic relearning is practical.
- Clear qualitative behavior and useful discussion of convergence vs. diligence.
- Promising synthetic results and a real-data illustration.

Cons for acceptance
- Detection statistic and pooling lack statistical calibration under adaptive weighting; risk of false alarms/missed detections.
- Heuristic coupling to PC; unclear validity of CI tests with weighted stats.
- Missing strong baselines (sliding window, CPD+learning, time-varying graphical models).
- Limited theoretical guarantees beyond proof sketches; parameter sensitivity not systematically explored.
- Numerical stability, inverse updates, and regularization not addressed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Compare against: (a) sliding-window PC with tuned window sizes; (b) EWMA/forgetting-factor versions; (c) CPD (e.g., BOCPD) followed by per-segment PC; (d) time-varying GGMs/DBNs when applicable.
- Calibrate change detection: use EWMA/CUSUM-style statistics with control of average run length; or empirically calibrate thresholds via bootstrap/permutation under stationarity.
- Provide ablations: fixed vs adaptive ar; with/without pooling; different ν schedules; sensitivity plots for T, γ, ν.
- Add regularization and inverse updates; report computational costs.
- Clarify CI testing under weighting; consider tests that accept covariance and effective df explicitly.
- Expand related work to include adaptive forgetting, online covariance estimation, dynamic BN/DBN, and time-varying graphical models.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The problem is important and the approach is promising and practical, but the statistical underpinnings of the detection mechanism and the evaluation (missing baselines/calibration) need strengthening to meet NeurIPS standards. I encourage resubmission after addressing the above issues.
