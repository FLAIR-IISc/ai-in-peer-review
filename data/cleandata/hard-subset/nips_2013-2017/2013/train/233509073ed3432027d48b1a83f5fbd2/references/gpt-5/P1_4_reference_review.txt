Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic online algorithm for learning and tracking time-varying graphical model structure under a locally stationary assumption. The setting is linear-Gaussian recursive SEMs over continuous variables, but the authors argue the ideas extend more broadly. The method maintains weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an “effective” sample size) with adaptive weights ar, where ar increases when recent data fit poorly under the current covariance. Poor fit is detected via Mahalanobis distances transformed to p-values and then pooled over time using Liptak’s method to produce a pooled p-value ρr. This pooled statistic drives both the weight update (larger ar when ρr is small) and the probability of triggering a re-run of a constraint-based structure learner (PC). The paper provides update equations for the sufficient statistics, discusses a convergence–diligence trade-off controlled by parameters T and γ (with proof sketches), presents simulations with synthetic data demonstrating that LoSST matches batch PC in stationary regimes and adapts after changepoints, and applies the method to CPI component series, qualitatively detecting the Great Moderation.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself against batch structure learning (score-based and constraint-based), temporal-difference tracking, changepoint detection (Bayesian and frequentist), and two prior offline approaches for time-varying graphs (Talih & Hengartner 2005; Siracusa & Fisher 2009). The main novelty is a single-pass online scheme that ties adaptive, pooled-goodness-of-fit-driven weights to online sufficient statistics, coupled with periodic PC relearning. However, the paper omits comparisons to more recent time-varying graphical model approaches relevant to Gaussian settings (e.g., sliding windows, exponentially weighted covariances, CPD+windowed learning baselines, or time-varying GGMs based on fused penalties or kernel smoothing), which weakens the empirical positioning.

Strengths
- Addresses an important gap: real-time structure learning for changing environments, with a simple, memory-light implementation using O(p^2) sufficient statistics plus occasional structural relearning.
- Conceptually appealing coupling of goodness-of-fit pooling (Liptak) to adaptive forgetting and to a relearning schedule; elegant, practical update rules for weighted means/covariances.
- Clear articulation of the convergence vs diligence trade-off, with parameter regimes and proof sketches; honest about the limits and heuristics.
- Synthetic experiments show that in stationary regimes LoSST behaves like batch PC, and after changes it recovers structure substantially faster than batch PC on the full mixture—illustrating the potential benefit of the weighting mechanism.
- Real data case study suggests the method can detect known macroeconomic regime changes.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Statistical validity of the pooled p-value ρ under adaptive estimation is unclear. The Mahalanobis distance is computed using an estimated, adaptively updated covariance with “effective” sample size; p-values are not i.i.d. uniform under the null, and successive p-values are dependent. Using Hotelling’s T2 with effective sample size and non-i.i.d. weighting is not justified. Liptak’s method requires care under dependence; the null calibration of ρ is not established. As a result, thresholds T lack interpretable false-alarm guarantees.
- The effective sample size update Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1)Sr + 1 is ad hoc; no derivation showing that this leads to valid inference downstream. Using Sr in distributional assumptions is problematic.
- Convergence/diligence claims rely on extreme thresholds (e.g., T > 1 or T < 0), which are not meaningful for p-values in [0,1]; these regimes prove existence but are not practically relevant. The interesting regime (0 < T < 1, γ > 1) lacks formal guarantees.
- Baselines are weak. Comparing LoSST to batch PC on the entire pooled data is unfair post-change. Missing comparisons include: sliding-window PC, fixed exponential forgetting PC, CPD followed by windowed PC, and time-varying GGM/SEM estimators (e.g., kernel-smoothed or fused penalties). Without these, the empirical gains are hard to attribute.
- The interface to PC is under-specified. How are conditional independence tests adapted to weighted covariances and effective n? How is regularization handled to ensure invertibility and stable partial correlations when Sr is small or p is large? No discussion of alpha levels, multiple testing, or stability of PC under frequent relearning.
- Computational analysis is limited. Repeated matrix inversions and frequent PC runs can be costly; no timing or complexity vs p, degree, and ν is reported.
- Real data evaluation is qualitative; no learned graphs over time, no predictive or causal plausibility checks, and no comparison to alternative detectors.

Quality
Moderate. The algorithmic ideas and engineering are sound at a heuristic level, and the simulations are suggestive. However, key inferential components (distributional assumptions for p-values and pooled statistic under adaptive weighting) are not justified, the theoretical guarantees are mostly sketch-level and avoid the practically relevant regime, and empirical baselines are insufficient.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with helpful intuition. Some notation and derivations could be clearer:
- Clarify the derivation and assumptions behind Eq. (3).
- Specify precisely how PC’s CI tests use the weighted covariance and effective n.
- Discuss regularization/shrinkage for covariance inversion.
- Correct the use of thresholds T outside [0,1] as existence proofs, and explicitly state that they are not practical operating points.

Originality
Moderate. The combination of pooled goodness-of-fit-driven adaptive weights and online sufficient statistics feeding an off-the-shelf structure learner is a novel and practical synthesis. However, related literature on time-varying Gaussian graphical models and sliding-window/forgetting-factor CI testing is not adequately engaged.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners who need an online, lightweight approach and can accept heuristic calibration. To be impactful, the paper needs stronger theory or stronger empirical comparisons demonstrating superiority to standard online baselines.

Questions for the authors
- Can you empirically calibrate ρ under stationarity (e.g., via permutation or block bootstrap) to justify threshold selection and estimate false alarm rates?
- How do you handle non-invertible or ill-conditioned covariance estimates when Sr ≈ p? Do you use shrinkage?
- How exactly are CI tests in PC adapted to the weighted statistics? What alpha levels and corrections are used?
- How sensitive are results to T, γ, and ν? Provide sensitivity plots.
- Can you compare to sliding-window PC and fixed forgetting-factor baselines, and to a CPD+window scheme?

Suggestions
- Add theory or empirical calibration for ρ under adaptive weighting.
- Include runtime and scalability analysis.
- Strengthen baselines and ablations (fixed vs adaptive weights, alternative pooling methods).
- Show learned graphs over time on the CPI data and provide economic interpretability checks.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important online problem with a simple, implementable method.
- Clear, intuitive algorithm with practical update rules.
- Promising synthetic results and a real-world case study.

Cons for acceptance
- Inferential validity of the pooled p-value and effective sample size is unclear.
- Theoretical guarantees do not cover the practical operating regime.
- Missing strong baselines; empirical evaluation may overstate gains.
- Under-specified integration with PC and lack of regularization details.

Overall recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the paper needs stronger theoretical grounding or more comprehensive empirical validation against appropriate online baselines to meet NeurIPS standards. Confidence: moderate.
