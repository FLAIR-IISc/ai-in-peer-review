This paper presents the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel online algorithm for learning graphical model structure from data streams where the underlying generative model may change over time. The core idea is to move away from the standard i.i.d. assumption and instead assume local stationarity. The algorithm does not learn a new graph from each datapoint, but instead maintains a set of weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an "effective" sample size). The key contribution is the mechanism for dynamically updating the weights assigned to datapoints. This is achieved by assessing the "fit" of each new datapoint to the current estimated covariance matrix using the Mahalanobis distance. The p-values from these distances are pooled over time using Liptak's method, and a low pooled p-value (indicating a persistent deviation) causes the algorithm to up-weight new data, effectively "forgetting" the old statistics. A standard batch structure learning algorithm (here, the PC algorithm) is then periodically run on these dynamically updated statistics. The decision to relearn the graph is handled probabilistically, with the probability increasing when a change is suspected.

The work is well-positioned relative to prior art. The authors correctly identify that standard batch learners are unsuitable for this task. They also distinguish their approach from simpler online methods like TD-learning (which are not for structure learning) and formal changepoint detection (CPD) methods, which often store significant data history and assume abrupt, rather than gradual, changes. The paper also differentiates itself from previous batch-mode time-varying graph learners by being a true online, single-pass algorithm.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a highly significant and practical problem. The assumption of a static data-generating process is a major limitation of most structure learning algorithms, and this work provides a principled, heuristic approach to relax it. The core idea of tracking weighted sufficient statistics, with weights adaptively set by a pooled anomaly score, is both elegant and novel in this context.

2.  **Technical Depth:** A major strength is the theoretical discussion in Section 3.1 regarding the trade-off between "convergence" and "diligence." This demonstrates a deep understanding of the challenges in online learning. Providing proof sketches for conditions under which LoSST exhibits these different behaviors (controlled by parameters γ and T) elevates the paper from a purely heuristic method to one with a solid conceptual foundation.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The simulation study is well-designed and convincing.
    *   It demonstrates that LoSST incurs no performance cost in stationary environments, matching the batch PC algorithm's performance.
    *   It clearly shows LoSST's superior ability to adapt to both structural and parametric changes, where the batch algorithm fails due to data mixing.
    *   The authors are honest about the results, for example, by analyzing and explaining why PC might temporarily outperform LoSST on edge removal errors after a parameter-only change.
    *   The experiment on probabilistic relearning validates the heuristic trigger, showing it achieves performance comparable to constant relearning but at a much lower computational cost.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the algorithm is explained step-by-step with all necessary equations, and the results are presented logically and interpreted thoughtfully. The application to real-world US price index data serves as a compelling demonstration of the algorithm's utility.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Dependence on Heuristics and Hyperparameters:** While the overall framework is well-motivated, several key components are heuristic. The specific form of the weight update function (Eq. 4) and the probabilistic relearning mechanism are chosen for their functional properties but lack deeper theoretical justification. The performance seems sensitive to the choice of hyperparameters (T, γ, ν). While the paper provides the values used, a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with these parameters would significantly strengthen the empirical results and provide guidance for future users.

2.  **Limited Scope of Structure Learner:** The implementation relies on the PC algorithm. While the authors state that other algorithms could be used, the method of tracking the covariance matrix is specific to learners that use second-order statistics (like many constraint-based and score-based methods for Gaussian models). The paper would be stronger if it discussed the challenges of extending this framework to other model classes (e.g., discrete Bayesian networks) in more detail in the main body, rather than briefly in the future work section. The claim that score-based methods are unsuitable for online learning due to non-decomposability warrants a more detailed explanation or citation.

3.  **Computational Complexity:** The paper notes that structure learning is computationally expensive, which motivates the probabilistic relearning. However, it does not provide an analysis of the computational complexity of the LoSST algorithm itself. A brief discussion of the per-step cost of updating the statistics (including the matrix inversion for the Mahalanobis distance) versus the cost of a full relearning step would be beneficial for understanding the practical trade-offs.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical, open problem in graphical model learning with a novel and effective online algorithm.
*   The conceptual framework, particularly the convergence vs. diligence trade-off, provides a valuable new perspective for the field of online learning.
*   The experimental validation is comprehensive, robust, and includes both synthetic and real-world data.
*   The paper is extremely well-written, clear, and presents a complete piece of work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The method relies on several heuristic choices and hyperparameters whose selection is not thoroughly explored or justified.
*   The generality of the approach beyond covariance-based learners for continuous variables is asserted but not demonstrated.

**Conclusion:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The strengths—a novel solution to an important problem, deep conceptual insights, and a strong empirical evaluation—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily centered on the heuristic nature of some components. The work is likely to be influential and inspire follow-up research on learning in non-stationary environments. I strongly recommend acceptance.
