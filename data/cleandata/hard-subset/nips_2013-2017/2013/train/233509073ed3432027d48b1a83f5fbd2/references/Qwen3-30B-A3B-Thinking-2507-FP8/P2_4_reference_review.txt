

# Review: "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

## Summary
This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm for online learning of graphical model structure when the underlying structure may change over time. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: while most structure learning algorithms assume static structure (i.e., i.i.d. data), real-world applications often involve dynamically changing structures that require online detection and adaptation. LoSST tracks sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) in an online fashion using weighted updates, where weights increase when data show poor fit with the current model (measured via Mahalanobis distance and pooled p-values). It then probabilistically relearns the graphical model structure when necessary. The paper demonstrates through simulations and a real-world application to US price index volatility that LoSST performs as well as batch methods during stable periods and quickly adapts to structural changes.

## Strengths

**Originality (High):** The paper identifies a clear gap in the literature—online graphical model structure learning for time-varying structures—and proposes a novel solution. Previous work either assumes static structure (batch learning) or focuses on change detection without structure learning (changepoint detection). The authors thoroughly survey related work and clearly distinguish their approach, making the novelty evident. The use of pooled p-values to weight data and the probabilistic relearning mechanism represent significant methodological contributions.

**Significance (High):** The problem addressed is highly relevant to real-world applications where data streams evolve over time (e.g., financial markets, social networks, biological systems). The paper demonstrates practical utility through a compelling real-world application to US price index volatility, where LoSST successfully detects the "Great Moderation" period. The algorithm's ability to process data in a single pass with no additional cost during stable periods makes it particularly valuable for large-scale or streaming applications.

**Quality (Moderate-High):** The paper presents a well-motivated algorithm with detailed theoretical grounding. The simulation results are comprehensive, covering multiple graph sizes, degrees, and types of structure changes (both structural and parameter changes). The comparison to batch methods (PC algorithm) is appropriate and shows clear advantages for LoSST after structural changes. The discussion of convergence vs. diligence properties adds theoretical depth to the method.

**Clarity (High):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The algorithm is described with clear mathematical formulations, and the connection between Mahalanobis distance, pooled p-values, and weight updates is well-explained. The simulation results are presented with informative figures that clearly convey the algorithm's performance.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Rigor (Moderate):** While the paper provides a thoughtful discussion of convergence vs. diligence properties, it lacks formal proofs of key claims. For instance, the statement that "LoSST is provably convergent if T is time-indexed such that Tr = f(Sr) for some f with (0, 1] range" needs stronger justification. The paper would be significantly strengthened by formal guarantees on the algorithm's performance under different conditions.

**Parameter Justification (Moderate):** The choice of parameters (T=0.05, γ=3) is described as "unsystematic investigation," which is insufficient for a scientific contribution. A more principled approach to parameter selection (e.g., cross-validation, theoretical bounds) would strengthen the paper.

**Scope Limitation (Moderate):** The paper focuses exclusively on recursive SEMs (Gaussian DAGs), while acknowledging that extensions to other graphical models (e.g., Bayesian networks with discrete variables) would be valuable. The discussion of these extensions is brief, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how the approach would scale to more complex models.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of graphical model structure learning by addressing an important practical problem: online learning when the underlying structure may change. The LoSST algorithm is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear advantages over batch methods in dynamic settings. While the theoretical rigor could be strengthened, the paper's practical significance and thorough experimental evaluation make it a strong candidate for NIPS.

The paper meets all the key criteria for NIPS: it's technically sound (with appropriate experimental validation), clearly written, addresses an important problem with a novel solution, and has significant practical implications. The authors have made a substantial contribution to the field of online structure learning.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Pro Accept Arguments:**
- Addresses a clear, important gap in the literature
- Novel algorithm with clear theoretical motivation
- Comprehensive experimental validation
- Demonstrated practical utility in real-world application
- Well-written and clearly organized

**Con Accept Arguments:**
- Some theoretical claims lack formal justification
- Parameter choices could be better justified
- Limited discussion of extensions to other graphical models

This paper represents a significant step toward practical online structure learning in real-world applications where data streams evolve over time. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field that will likely inspire future research on online learning of graphical models.
