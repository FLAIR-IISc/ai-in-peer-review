The paper proposes a heuristic algorithm, LoSST, for online learning of graphical model structure in non-stationary environments. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) in an online fashion, weighting recent data more heavily when changes in the underlying generating structure are detected via Mahalanobis distance and a pooled p-value test. The algorithm then periodically relearns the graphical model structure using a constraint-based approach (PC algorithm). The authors provide simulation results demonstrating LoSST’s ability to track changing structures and maintain performance comparable to batch-mode learning when the structure is stable. A preliminary application to US price index volatility is also presented.

While the problem addressed – online structure learning – is undeniably important and under-explored, and the core idea of adaptively weighting data points based on their fit to the current model is reasonable, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet collectively substantial, flaws. 

First, the mathematical derivations, while present, are often terse and lack sufficient explanation. For example, the justification for equation (2) regarding covariance update isn’t fully elucidated, and the reader is left to fill in the algebraic steps. Similarly, the justification for equation (3) – the sample size update – feels ad-hoc rather than theoretically grounded. While the authors claim a connection to TD(0) learning, this connection is superficial and doesn’t offer deeper insight into the algorithm's behavior.

Second, the parameter settings, particularly γ and T in equation (4), are chosen “unsystematically” based on investigation, lacking a rigorous justification. The authors acknowledge the trade-off between convergence and diligence but provide limited guidance on how to select appropriate parameters for different scenarios. The discussion of convergence and diligence (Section 3.1) is valuable but lacks formal analysis.  The proofs sketched are hand-wavy and lack the detail needed for a convincing argument.

Third, the simulation setup is insufficiently detailed. The choice of 〈#variables, MaxDegree〉 pairs feels arbitrary, and the rationale for using the PC algorithm as a baseline is not fully explained.  The figures are also problematic; the axes labels are often too small, making them difficult to read, and the choice of line styles makes it challenging to distinguish between different curves.  The observation that LoSST performs worse on edge removal errors when only parameters change is interesting, but the explanation feels incomplete. Why does LoSST initially *add* edges in this scenario? 

Fourth, the application to US price index data is descriptive rather than analytical. There's no quantitative evaluation of LoSST’s performance; the authors simply point out shifts detected by the algorithm. The claim about detecting the “Great Moderation” feels somewhat speculative without further substantiation.

Fifth, there are numerous minor formatting and stylistic issues throughout the paper. The notation is inconsistent (e.g., pa(Vi) is sometimes used, sometimes not).  There are also instances of awkward phrasing and grammatical errors.  The repeated use of “obviously” feels dismissive and unhelpful.  Footnotes are overused and disrupt the flow of the text.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and relatively unexplored problem.
*   The core idea of adaptive weighting is intuitive and potentially effective.
*   Simulation results suggest that the algorithm can track changing structures.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Numerous mathematical and algorithmic details are insufficiently explained.
*   Parameter settings are chosen arbitrarily, lacking theoretical justification.
*   Simulation setup and results presentation are suboptimal.
*   Real-world application lacks quantitative evaluation.
*   The paper suffers from numerous minor formatting and stylistic issues.
*   The claimed convergence/diligence properties lack rigorous proof.



Overall, while the paper presents a potentially interesting approach to online structure learning, the multitude of flaws – ranging from minor stylistic issues to significant gaps in the mathematical derivations and experimental evaluation – outweigh its strengths. The work feels incomplete and lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS publication. I recommend **rejection**. A substantially revised version, addressing the points raised above, might be suitable for resubmission.
