This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of learning graphical model structure from data streams where the underlying generative process is non-stationary. While most structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data, this work develops an online algorithm, the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), designed to handle abrupt changes in the data-generating graph structure and/or its parameters. The core idea is to maintain and update weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an "effective sample size") for a set of continuous variables. The weight assigned to each new data point is dynamically adjusted based on how well it fits the current statistical model. A poor fit, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance, is taken as evidence of a change. To distinguish sustained changes from outliers, the algorithm uses a pooled p-value over a recent history of these distances. A significant drop in the pooled p-value triggers an increase in the weights of new data points, effectively "forgetting" past data and adapting the model to the new regime. A standard structure learning algorithm (here, the PC algorithm) is then run on the updated sufficient statistics to infer the new graph. This approach is novel in its synthesis of online statistical tracking, principled change detection, and graphical model structure learning, setting it apart from batch methods for time-varying graphs (e.g., Talih & Hengartner, 2005; Siracusa & Fisher III, 2009) and from general online learning methods that do not perform structure discovery.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a problem of high practical and theoretical importance. The i.i.d. assumption is a major limitation of most structure learning methods, and this work provides a well-reasoned and novel approach to relax it. The LoSST algorithm is an elegant solution that combines existing statistical tools in a new and effective way for online structure learning.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The online update equations for the weighted sufficient statistics are correctly derived. The use of Mahalanobis distance for anomaly detection and Liptak’s method for pooling p-values provides a principled way to detect structural changes while maintaining robustness to single outliers. A significant strength is the theoretical analysis of the algorithm's long-run properties, formally connecting the choice of hyperparameters (`γ`, `T`) to the trade-off between "convergence" (stability in a static environment) and "diligence" (rapid detection of change). This provides users with a clear understanding of the algorithm's behavior.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are convincing. The simulations clearly demonstrate that LoSST performs on par with a standard batch algorithm (PC) in stationary periods and significantly outperforms it after a change. The batch algorithm's failure on the mixed data highlights the necessity of an adaptive approach like LoSST. The inclusion of experiments with both structure change and parameter-only change provides a more nuanced view of performance. The application to US price index data is a compelling demonstration on a real-world problem, showing that the algorithm can identify meaningful, historically documented macroeconomic shifts.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The selection of key hyperparameters (`T = 0.05`, `γ = 3`, `ν = .005`) is based on "unsystematic investigation." The algorithm's performance likely depends significantly on these choices, which govern the trade-off between stability and plasticity. The paper would be substantially strengthened by including a sensitivity analysis that shows how performance metrics (e.g., error rates, detection delay) vary with these parameters. This would also provide valuable guidance for practitioners seeking to apply LoSST to new problems.
*   **Limited Baselines:** The primary baseline is the batch PC algorithm applied to the entire dataset. While effective at demonstrating the problem with naive batch processing, this is a weak baseline for a non-stationary task. A more informative comparison would be against a sliding window approach, where PC is run on a window of the most recent `k` data points. This is a common and simple heuristic for handling non-stationary data, and showing that LoSST outperforms it would make a stronger case for the proposed weighting and change-detection mechanism.
*   **Heuristic Components:** The probabilistic trigger for relearning the graph structure is a reasonable heuristic, but it adds another parameter (`ν`) and is not as principled as the change detection part of the algorithm. Have the authors considered alternative relearning triggers? For instance, could relearning be initiated deterministically when the pooled p-value `ρ` drops below a certain threshold, or when the change in the covariance matrix exceeds a certain magnitude? Exploring this could lead to a more robust or parameter-free relearning scheme.
*   **Clarity on Experimental Setup:** For the first two simulations, a fixed relearning schedule was imposed on LoSST for a fair comparison with batch PC. This is a reasonable choice, but the distinction between this setup and the probabilistic relearning described as part of the full LoSST algorithm could be stated more explicitly in the main text (rather than just in a footnote) to avoid potential confusion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces a novel and practical algorithm for a fundamental and largely open problem in machine learning.
2.  **Soundness:** The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and supported by both theoretical analysis and strong empirical results.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem, the proposed solution, and the results.
4.  **Impact:** The work is likely to be influential and inspire further research into online learning of dynamic causal or probabilistic structures.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Evaluation Thoroughness:** The lack of hyperparameter sensitivity analysis and comparison to stronger baselines (like a sliding window method) makes it difficult to fully gauge the algorithm's practical robustness and state-of-the-art performance.

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel, well-executed, and important contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The weaknesses identified are primarily related to the thoroughness of the evaluation rather than fundamental flaws in the proposed method. They represent excellent avenues for future work and could potentially be addressed in a camera-ready revision. The paper's strengths in originality, technical quality, and significance are compelling. I recommend acceptance.
