Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical model structure in linear-Gaussian SEMs (DAGs). The key idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) with weights adapted online based on pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances (via Lipták’s method). When the pooled p-value drops, weights increase to emphasize recent data; a probabilistic trigger then relearns structure using a standard constraint-based method (PC). The authors analyze a convergence–diligence trade-off induced by parameters T and γ, provide simulations where LoSST outperforms batch PC after distributional changes, and demonstrate on a CPI volatility dataset that LoSST detects periods consistent with the Great Moderation.

The paper is positioned against (i) batch structure learning (score- and constraint-based) that assume i.i.d. data, (ii) online changepoint detection (e.g., BOCPD) that typically buffers substantial data and assumes abrupt changes, and (iii) prior work on time-varying graphs, notably Talih & Hengartner (2005, undirected, batch) and Siracusa & Fisher (2009, Bayesian, batch). Relative to NeurIPS literature, this work is related to BOCPD (Adams & MacKay, NIPS 2007), dynamic/time-varying graphical models (e.g., Kolar et al., NIPS 2009/2010; Song & Xing; Zhou et al.), DBNs, and adaptive/forgetting-factor covariance estimation. Compared to those, LoSST aims for a simple, single-pass, model-agnostic front-end that can plug into existing structure learners.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely problem: online tracking of changing graphical structures, relevant for streaming and nonstationary environments.
- Clear algorithmic idea: weighted sufficient-statistics with adaptive weights tied to pooled goodness-of-fit; probabilistic relearning reduces computational burden.
- Derives incremental updates for means/covariances and proposes an interpretable “effective sample size” mechanism.
- Discusses convergence vs. diligence trade-offs explicitly and connects them to parameter regimes.
- Simulation suite with multiple graph sizes/degrees; shows LoSST matches batch PC pre-change and recovers faster post-change; a long-run experiment illustrates relearning dynamics.
- Real-world case study illustrates interpretability of diagnostics (pooled p-values, Mahalanobis distances).

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines: comparisons are primarily against batch PC on the full data, which is a weak post-change baseline. Absent are sliding-window PC, exponential forgetting/ EWMA baselines, BOCPD combined with recent-window PC, time-varying GGM methods, or DBN-style approaches.
- Theoretical underpinnings are partial. Key components (effective sample size update, validity of Hotelling’s T^2 with adaptively weighted and evolving covariances, Lipták pooling under strong temporal dependence) are heuristic; proofs are sketched and rely on strong assumptions. No finite-sample guarantees or detection delay bounds.
- Assumption set is narrow: linear-Gaussian SEMs, independence within local windows, and reliance on invertible covariances; numerical stability/regularization not discussed. Scalability to high-dimensional p>>n is unclear.
- Statistical testing details for PC with weighted stats are under-specified (e.g., how partial correlations and degrees of freedom are adjusted using Sr; multiple testing control; stability of CI tests under rapid weight changes).
- The real data example lacks ground truth and quantitative evaluation; conclusions are descriptive.
- Missing relevant related work and citations from NeurIPS and statistics on time-varying graphical models and adaptive covariance estimation.

Quality
The algorithm is sound as a practical heuristic and well-engineered for single-pass updates. However, several statistical justifications are tenuous: Eq. (3) for Sr is asserted, not derived; T^2 calibration with weighted/evolving covariance is questionable; Lipták pooling assumes independence/weak dependence among p-values, which is violated here. Empirical evidence is positive but would be stronger with stronger baselines and ablations (e.g., fixed forgetting factor vs. pooled p-value weighting; with/without probabilistic relearning).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Useful equations and a coherent narrative. Some notational hiccups and typos appear. Important implementation details are missing: numerical stabilization (e.g., shrinkage/diagonal loading), handling near-singular covariances, PC test thresholds and dof with Sr, complexity and memory scaling, and pseudo-code.

Originality
Combining adaptively weighted sufficient statistics driven by pooled p-values with online structure relearning is a novel assembly. However, related literature on time-varying graphs, online/forgetting-factor estimation, and BOCPD-style pipelines suggests the conceptual space is active; the paper should better delineate what is truly new vs. extensions of known ideas.

Significance
If validated with stronger baselines and theory, LoSST could be a practical tool. In its current form, significance is moderate due to heuristic components and limited comparisons. The idea of pooled p-value–driven adaptive forgetting is interesting and potentially impactful.

Questions for the authors
- Can you derive Eq. (3) rigorously and justify the T^2 calibration under time-varying, weighted covariances? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- How are partial correlations and CI tests in PC adapted to use Sr? Do you adjust degrees of freedom? Any multiple testing correction?
- What is the computational complexity per step and memory use as a function of V? Any results for p>>n with regularization?
- How robust is Lipták pooling to dependent p-values? Did you consider alternatives (e.g., moving-average of log p-values, e-Bayes factors, or CPD with hazard models)?
- Sensitivity analysis for T, γ, ν? Can these be tuned online (e.g., via cross-validation on recent windows)?
- How do results compare to: (a) sliding-window PC with window sizes chosen to match LoSST’s effective sample size; (b) exponential forgetting with fixed λ; (c) BOCPD feeding a recent segment to PC; (d) time-varying GGM methods?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong baselines as above; include ablations (pooling vs. no pooling; fixed vs. adaptive weights).
- Provide numerical stabilization and high-dimensional variants (e.g., shrinkage covariance; graphical lasso for CI tests).
- Tighten theory: conditions under which pooled p-values are valid; detection delay vs. false alarm trade-offs; bounds under piecewise-stationary models.
- Clarify how PC is modified for weighted stats and degrees of freedom; include pseudo-code and complexity analysis.
- Expand related work to include time-varying GGMs/DBNs and NeurIPS papers (Kolar, Song & Xing; Zhou et al.; Xuan & Murphy).
- Evaluate the real-world example quantitatively (e.g., ex post predictive performance, consistency with documented regime changes).

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important online structure learning problem with a simple, implementable method.
- Empirical results indicate clear advantages over batch learning after changes.
- Convergence–diligence discussion is insightful.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heuristic/statistical foundations need strengthening; key assumptions questionable.
- Missing strong baselines and ablations; limited empirical validation.
- Scope limited to linear-Gaussian SEMs with unclear scalability to higher dimensions.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is promising and practically appealing, but the paper needs stronger validation (comparisons and theory) and clearer statistical justification to meet NeurIPS standards. I encourage the authors to revise accordingly; with these improvements, the work could make a solid contribution.
