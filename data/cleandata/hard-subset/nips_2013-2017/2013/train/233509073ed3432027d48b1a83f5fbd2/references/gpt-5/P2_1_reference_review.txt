Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical structure in linear-Gaussian SEMs (DAG models). The key idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariance, “effective” sample size) via single-pass updates, where the weights adapt to evidence of non-stationarity measured by the pooled p-values of recent Mahalanobis distances (using Liptak’s method). When pooled p-values are small, older data are down-weighted and the algorithm is more likely to trigger a structure relearning step (here, PC). The authors analyze parameter regimes trading off convergence (stability under stationarity) and diligence (rapid response to change), provide simulations with abrupt changes in parameters and/or structure, and a small real-data case study on CPI component volatilities.

Relation to prior work: The paper contrasts with batch structure learning (score- and constraint-based), TDL methods (slow-change tracking), and changepoint detection (CPD) methods. It differs from prior time-varying graph work such as Talih & Hengartner’s undirected, batch setting and Siracusa & Fisher’s Bayesian posterior over edges, by offering a frequentist, single-pass, online DAG learner. However, the paper does not engage with a broader literature on time-varying graphical models and dynamic Bayesian networks (e.g., Xuan & Murphy 2007; Kolar et al. 2010/2012 for TV-GGMs; Song et al. for time-varying DBNs), nor with sliding-window/exponential-forgetting baselines, which are highly relevant comparators for the proposed online approach.

Quality
- Soundness: The online updates for weighted mean/covariance are standard and correct in spirit; the “effective sample size” update is a reasonable heuristic. The weight adaptation via pooled p-values and the relearning probability are heuristic but well-motivated. The convergence vs. diligence discussion is insightful, but proofs are sketch-level and hinge on weight behavior rather than guarantees of structural consistency under local stationarity. Using Hotelling’s T^2 p-values assumes approximate calibration with an “effective N” and independence assumptions for Liptak pooling that are unlikely to hold given adaptive re-use of the same estimated covariance; this should be discussed and empirically validated (calibration checks).
- Experiments: The simulations are sensible and match the claims: LoSST tracks changes and is comparable to batch PC under stationarity. The parameter-only change case reveals a transient weakness, which the authors acknowledge. The probabilistic relearning experiment shows computational savings with little accuracy loss. However, baselines are weak: batch PC on a mixture is a strawman for streaming. Missing are strong online baselines such as sliding-window PC, exponential forgetting in covariance and CI tests, CPD + per-segment learning, or time-varying GGM/DAG methods adapted to streaming. Sensitivity analyses to T, γ, ν and to the pooling choice are limited (“unsystematic investigation”); robustness to outliers, slow drift (rather than abrupt changes), misspecification, and high-dimensional regimes (p ≳ n_eff) is not assessed. Computational aspects (matrix inversion updates, regularization when Sr < p) are not discussed.
- Reproducibility: Core equations are provided, but many implementation details (tests used within PC with weighted stats, regularization, parameter selection, code/data availability) are missing.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and the algorithmic components are clearly described. The convergence/diligence discussion is useful. There are some notation slips (e.g., parameters of T^2; “graph2 relearning”), footnote formatting, and several incomplete citations. The choice of tests within PC using weighted covariance and how Sr is used in p-value calibration should be spelled out. A complexity discussion (per-step cost, update of inverse covariance via Sherman–Morrison, numerical stability) would improve clarity.

Originality
The combination of online tracking of sufficient statistics with pooled p-value–driven adaptive forgetting and probabilistic triggering of structure relearning is novel in the DAG setting and practical. However, the space of time-varying graphical modeling is broader than the related work reflects. The contribution is more an engineering framework than a new theoretical foundation; the convergence/diligence framing is interesting but not fully formalized for structure recovery.

Significance
Tracking time-varying causal/graphical structure online is important, and a memory-light, single-pass method is valuable. The approach could be impactful as a practical template. That said, the empirical support is limited in scope and lacks comparisons to the most pertinent baselines. Without stronger evidence and discussion of statistical calibration and computational behavior, it is hard to assess readiness for broad adoption.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses an important and under-explored online DAG structure learning problem with a practical, single-pass method.
- Clear algorithmic design; weighted stats + pooled p-values + probabilistic relearning is simple and implementable.
- Sensible simulations showing benefits in change scenarios and parity under stationarity; honest discussion of trade-offs and limitations.
- Convergence vs. diligence perspective is insightful and may stimulate further work.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Missing strong baselines (sliding windows, exponential forgetting, CPD+segment learning, time-varying GGM/DAGs), making gains hard to contextualize.
- Heuristic statistical components (pooled p-values, effective N) lack calibration analysis; independence assumptions are dubious.
- Theoretical claims are partial; no guarantees for structure recovery under local stationarity.
- Limited experimental breadth (small p, few settings), minimal sensitivity analyses, and sparse real-data validation.
- Clarity issues: incomplete references, some notation/typos; missing computational/regularization details.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines (windowed PC, exponentially weighted CI tests; BOCPD+PC) and compare across abrupt and gradual change, outliers, and varying p/n regimes.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν and ablate the pooling mechanism (e.g., simple EWMA of distances).
- Assess p-value calibration empirically and discuss dependence/optional stopping; consider alternative detectors (CUSUM, Page-Hinkley).
- Detail computational updates (rank-one inverse updates, shrinkage for Σ when Sr < p) and report per-step runtime.
- Expand related work to dynamic BNs and time-varying graphical models.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The problem and approach are promising and the paper is thoughtfully written, but stronger empirical comparisons, calibration/robustness analyses, and clearer positioning within the time-varying graphical model literature are needed to meet the bar. With these additions, this could become a valuable contribution.
