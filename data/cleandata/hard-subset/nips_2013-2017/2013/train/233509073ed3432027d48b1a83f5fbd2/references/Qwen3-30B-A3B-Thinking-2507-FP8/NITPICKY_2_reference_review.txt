

# Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

## Summary
This paper proposes the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm for online graphical model structure learning in environments where the underlying structure may change over time. The authors argue that while existing methods for graphical model structure learning assume stationary data (i.e., i.i.d. data from a single structure), real-world applications often require tracking time-varying structures. LoSST tracks sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) using weighted updates based on Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values, and dynamically relearns the graphical structure using a constraint-based method (PC algorithm). The paper demonstrates the algorithm's performance on synthetic data and a real-world application to US price index volatility.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a meaningful problem in graphical model learning that has received limited attention in the literature. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches (batch learning, online learning without structure tracking, changepoint detection) and position their work as filling a gap. The simulation results demonstrate that LoSST outperforms batch learning methods after structure changes, and the real-world application to US price index volatility provides a compelling case study. The theoretical analysis of convergence vs. diligence is a notable contribution to understanding the trade-offs in online structure learning.

## Weaknesses

1. **Technical Errors in Formulation**: The paper contains several critical mathematical errors that undermine the technical soundness of the approach. The most glaring issue is in the Mahalanobis distance formulation: "Dr+1 = (Xr+1 − µr)(Cr)−1(Xr+1 − µr)T" (equation 1) is inconsistent with standard notation. The correct form should be (Xr+1 − µr)^T (Cr)−1 (Xr+1 − µr), not the transpose as written. This is a fundamental error that would affect all subsequent calculations.

2. **Circular Definition in Weight Update**: The weight update rule (equation 4) is circular: "ar+1 = ar ·max { 1, γT − γρr+1 + ρr+1 T }" depends on ρr+1, but ρr+1 itself depends on ar+1 (as ρr+1 is defined using the pooled p-value that incorporates the weight ar+1). This creates a logical inconsistency that makes the algorithm's implementation unclear.

3. **Citation and Reference Inconsistencies**: The paper cites [13] for TD(0) but [13] is missing from the reference list. The reference for Liptak's method [7] is incomplete in the reference list. The citation [12] for the PC algorithm refers to "Causation, Prediction, and Search" (a book), but the PC algorithm was introduced in a specific paper by Spirtes et al. (not the book).

4. **Inconsistent Notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for time indices (e.g., Gr vs. G_r, Xr+1 vs. X_{r+1}) and mathematical expressions. For example, "Xr+1 is weighted ar+1ar more than Xr" should be "weighted ar+1/ar more than Xr," which is a critical typo that changes the meaning.

5. **Theoretical Claims Lack Justification**: The paper claims "If γ = 1, then LoSST is a convergent algorithm" but provides no proof or reference. The subsequent claim that "LoSST is provably convergent if T is time-indexed such that Tr = f(Sr)..." lacks a proof sketch or reference, making it difficult to evaluate the theoretical contribution.

6. **Reference List Issues**: The reference list contains incomplete entries (e.g., [1] is "Bayesian online changepoint detection, R.P. Adams, D.J.C. MacKay, Technical report,, , , " with missing publication details).

7. **Ambiguous Terminology**: The term "heuristic algorithm" is used without justification, suggesting the authors are aware of the lack of strong theoretical guarantees, which is problematic for a NIPS submission. The paper also uses "effective sample size" without a clear definition or justification of its relationship to the weighted data.

## Recommendation

This paper addresses an important problem in graphical model learning, but the numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, and theoretical claims without proper justification significantly undermine its quality. The mathematical errors in the core algorithm (particularly the Mahalanobis distance formulation and the circular weight update) are critical and would make the algorithm impossible to implement correctly. The citation errors and reference list inconsistencies are also unacceptable for a NIPS submission.

While the overall concept is interesting and the simulation results seem promising, the technical shortcomings prevent the paper from meeting NIPS standards for technical soundness. The paper would require substantial revision to correct these errors before it could be considered for publication.

I recommend rejection. The authors should address these technical issues thoroughly before resubmitting, and the paper would need to be re-evaluated with a corrected theoretical foundation.
