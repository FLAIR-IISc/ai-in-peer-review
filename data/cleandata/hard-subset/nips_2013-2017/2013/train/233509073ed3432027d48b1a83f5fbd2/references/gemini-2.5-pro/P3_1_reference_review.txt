### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel online algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from streaming data where the underlying data-generating process may change over time. The core idea is to maintain a set of weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an "effective sample size"). The weight of each new data point is determined by how well it fits the current model, as measured by its Mahalanobis distance. A poor fit, indicated by a low pooled p-value over recent data, signals a potential change in structure, causing the algorithm to increase the weights of subsequent data points and effectively "forget" older data. A standard constraint-based structure learning algorithm (the PC algorithm) is then probabilistically triggered to relearn the graph structure from the updated sufficient statistics. The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the algorithm's behavior, framing it in terms of a trade-off between "convergence" (stabilizing on a correct model in a stationary environment) and "diligence" (quickly detecting and adapting to change). The method is evaluated on synthetic data with abrupt changepoints and applied to a real-world macroeconomic time series.

### Strengths

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses a crucial and practical limitation of most graphical model structure learning algorithms: the assumption of i.i.d. data. Developing methods that can operate online and adapt to non-stationarity is of high importance for real-world applications in fields like finance, neuroscience, and sensor networks.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Approach:** The LoSST algorithm provides a coherent and well-motivated framework. The use of weighted sufficient statistics is a natural way to handle non-stationarity. The mechanism for adapting these weights based on Mahalanobis distance and pooled p-values is clever, as it allows the system to be robust to single outliers while remaining sensitive to persistent changes. The concept of an "effective sample size" is an elegant solution to a tricky problem.

3.  **Theoretical Insight:** The discussion of the diligence vs. convergence trade-off is a significant strength. It elevates the paper from a purely heuristic proposal to one with a deeper theoretical grounding. By showing that the algorithm's parameters (γ and T) control its position on this spectrum and providing proof sketches for the conditions leading to convergence or diligence, the authors demonstrate a sophisticated understanding of the problem. This analysis is valuable for both understanding LoSST and for the broader field of online learning in changing environments.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Validation (on Synthetic Data):** The simulation studies are well-designed.
    *   They correctly benchmark against a batch-mode algorithm (PC) to show that LoSST incurs no performance penalty in stationary settings.
    *   They demonstrate LoSST's clear superiority after a changepoint.
    *   They wisely test two different types of changes (structure+parameter vs. parameter-only), revealing interesting nuances in the algorithm's performance (e.g., the temporary increase in edge removal errors).
    *   The experiment with probabilistic relearning effectively demonstrates that the method can achieve good performance without the prohibitive cost of relearning at every step.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The primary baseline is a batch-mode PC algorithm. While this comparison is useful to establish a baseline and demonstrate the failure of i.i.d.-assuming methods, it's not a strong benchmark for the task of online, time-varying structure learning. The paper would be substantially stronger if compared against other relevant approaches. Examples could include:
    *   Sliding-window versions of structure learning algorithms.
    *   More sophisticated dynamic Bayesian network (DBN) structure learning methods.
    *   Other online changepoint detection methods coupled with a structure learner (e.g., [1, 4] from their references).
    While the related work section mentions some of these, an empirical comparison is necessary to properly situate LoSST's performance.

2.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The paper does not adequately address the computational cost. At each time step `r`, the algorithm must compute the Mahalanobis distance, which requires inverting the current covariance matrix `Cr`. This is an O(V³) operation, where V is the number of variables. This cost makes the method infeasible for even moderately high-dimensional problems (e.g., V > 100). Furthermore, the relearning step itself uses the PC algorithm, which is known to have worst-case complexity that is exponential in the maximum degree of the graph. These limitations should be explicitly stated and discussed. Perhaps low-rank updates or approximations to the matrix inverse could be suggested as future work for improving scalability.

3.  **Heuristic Nature of Key Components:** While the overall framework is well-motivated, some specific choices feel ad-hoc and could benefit from further justification.
    *   The weight update rule in Equation 4 is a specific linear function. Why was this form chosen over others (e.g., exponential)? A sensitivity analysis or a more principled derivation would be welcome.
    *   Similarly, the probabilistic relearning schedule uses a noisy-OR gate. The motivation for this specific choice is not entirely clear.

4.  **Analysis of the Real-World Application:** The application to US price index data is interesting but feels incomplete. It successfully demonstrates that the change-detection part of the algorithm identifies known macroeconomic periods (the "Great Moderation"). However, it stops short of analyzing the primary output of the algorithm: the learned graphical structures. A crucial part of the evaluation would be to show the learned graphs before and after the detected changepoints and provide a domain-specific interpretation of how the conditional independence relationships between economic indicators changed. Without this, the application only validates the change-point detector, not the full structure learning system.

### Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that introduces a novel, well-motivated, and generally sound algorithm for a significant and challenging problem. The theoretical discussion of the convergence/diligence trade-off is a standout feature. The empirical results on synthetic data are convincing. The main weaknesses are the lack of comparison to other relevant online/dynamic methods and an incomplete analysis of the real-world application. Despite these points, the paper presents a clear and important contribution to the field. The ideas are likely to be built upon by other researchers, and the proposed algorithm could be useful for practitioners dealing with non-stationary systems. It is a solid piece of work suitable for publication at NIPS.
