**Summary:**
This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a heuristic online algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from data where the underlying generative process may change over time. The core idea is to maintain and update weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an "effective" sample size). The weight assigned to each new data point is dynamically adjusted based on how well it fits the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. To avoid overreacting to single outliers, the algorithm uses Liptak's method to pool p-values over time. When a significant deviation is detected (indicated by a low pooled p-value), the weights on new data points increase, effectively "forgetting" older data. The graphical model structure is then re-learned from these updated sufficient statistics, using the PC algorithm as a backend. The re-learning process is triggered probabilistically, with a higher probability following detected changes. The authors present a theoretical discussion on the trade-off between "convergence" (stabilizing on a fixed structure) and "diligence" (quickly detecting change), which is controlled by the algorithm's hyperparameters. The method is evaluated on synthetic data with abrupt structural and parametric changes and is demonstrated on a real-world dataset of US price indices.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and important problem. The standard i.i.d. assumption for structure learning is often violated in real-world streaming data applications, and developing methods that can adapt to non-stationary environments is a valuable research direction.
2.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The algorithmic components—weighted sufficient statistics, change detection via Mahalanobis distance, and the probabilistic re-learning trigger—are explained in a logical and understandable manner.
3.  **Intuitive Approach:** The core concept of up-weighting surprising data points to adapt to change is intuitive. The use of a pooled p-value to distinguish sustained change from isolated outliers is a sensible heuristic.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary weaknesses of this paper lie in its methodological justification, the lack of rigorous comparison to appropriate baselines, and the limited scope of the experimental validation.

1.  **Heuristic Nature and Lack of Principled Derivation:** The proposed LoSST algorithm is a complex combination of several heuristic choices.
    *   The weight update rule in Equation (4) is ad-hoc. While the linear increase in weight as a function of the p-value is plausible, it is one of many possible functions. There is no theoretical justification for this specific form over others (e.g., exponential).
    *   Similarly, the probabilistic re-learning schedule is a heuristic. The choice of a noisy-OR gate update is not motivated from first principles.
    *   The settings for the key hyperparameters (`T`, `γ`, `ν`) are based on "unsystematic investigation." This is a significant concern. For the method to be useful, clear guidance on parameter selection or a demonstration of robustness to their settings is essential. The paper provides neither, making it difficult to assess the algorithm's general applicability.

2.  **Inadequate Baselines and Comparisons:** The experimental evaluation is a major weakness. The sole baseline is the standard batch-mode PC algorithm. This is not an appropriate comparison for an online, change-point detection setting. A batch algorithm applied to a concatenated dataset from different distributions is expected to perform poorly; demonstrating superiority over it is not a strong result. More relevant and established baselines are missing:
    *   **Sliding Window Approach:** A simple and widely used baseline for non-stationary data is to apply a standard batch learner (like PC) on a sliding window of the `k` most recent data points. This is a direct competitor, and its absence is a critical omission.
    *   **Explicit Change-Point Detection + Learning:** The authors mention online change-point detection (CPD) algorithms in the related work but do not compare against them. A natural baseline would be to couple a standard CPD method (e.g., Bayesian Online CPD) with a structure learner. When a change point is detected with high probability, the learner would be reset and trained on new data. A comparison is needed to justify the proposed integrated approach over such a modular one.

3.  **Limited and Unconvincing Experimental Results:**
    *   The simulations only test abrupt, step-wise changes. The paper motivates the problem by also mentioning slowly evolving structures ("slowly drifting"), but this scenario is never tested. It is unclear how LoSST would perform in a slowly drifting environment, where the Mahalanobis distance might not show a distinct spike.
    *   The claim that LoSST incurs "no additional costs in 'normal' structure learning situations" is not fully supported. In the described parameter setting (`γ=3`), the algorithm is non-convergent and remains sensitive to outliers. This means that even in a stable i.i.d. setting, a sequence of improbable (but possible) data points could trigger a spurious change detection, leading to a drop in effective sample size and a degradation of the learned model. This is a cost compared to a standard batch learner.
    *   The real-world application on price index data is purely illustrative. Without ground truth or a quantitative evaluation metric, it serves only as a demonstration of the algorithm's mechanics. The interpretation of the detected changes is speculative and does not constitute rigorous evidence of the method's effectiveness.

4.  **Insufficient Theoretical Analysis:** The discussion of convergence vs. diligence is interesting, but the proofs are only sketched. For a contribution intended for a top-tier venue like NeurIPS, a more formal treatment with explicit assumptions and detailed proofs is expected.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper tackles an important and challenging problem in machine learning.
*   The proposed approach is novel and combines ideas from online learning and structure learning in an interesting way.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The method is a collection of heuristics without strong theoretical grounding.
*   The experimental evaluation is critically flawed due to the lack of appropriate and competitive baselines.
*   The experiments do not cover key scenarios (like slow drift) and lack a proper sensitivity analysis of the crucial hyperparameters.
*   The claims of performance and "no cost" are not sufficiently substantiated by the evidence provided.

**Conclusion:**
While the problem is significant and the high-level idea is appealing, this paper does not meet the standards of rigor required for publication at NeurIPS. The validation of the proposed LoSST algorithm is insufficient. The method's value is difficult to assess without a comparison to standard, practical baselines like sliding-window methods or modular CPD-then-learn approaches. Furthermore, the ad-hoc nature of several core algorithmic components and the lack of guidance on hyperparameter tuning limit the work's potential impact and reproducibility. I recommend rejecting this paper but would encourage the authors to conduct a more thorough and rigorous empirical evaluation against stronger baselines to properly situate their contribution.
