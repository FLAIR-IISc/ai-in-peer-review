This paper proposes a heuristic algorithm, LoSST, for tracking time-varying graphical model structures in online settings. The core idea is to dynamically weight data points based on their “fit” to the current estimated covariance matrix, enabling rapid adaptation to structural changes. The authors position this work within the context of existing online learning and graphical model structure learning techniques, highlighting their limitations in handling non-stationary environments. The paper includes simulation results demonstrating LoSST's ability to track changes in both structure and parameters, and a preliminary application to U.S. price index data.

While the problem addressed is important—online learning of dynamic graphical models is a clear gap in the literature—and the proposed heuristic is conceptually sound, the paper suffers from a multitude of small but significant flaws that, collectively, diminish its overall impact. My assessment is largely negative, despite acknowledging the potential of the core idea.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** The problem of learning graphical models from non-i.i.d. data streams is undeniably important.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches.
*   **Conceptually Sound:** The dynamic weighting scheme based on Mahalanobis distance seems reasonable for detecting changes.
*   **Simulation Results:** The simulations demonstrate LoSST's ability to track changes, especially when compared to a static batch-mode approach.
*   **Real-World Application:** Applying the method to price index data is a positive step, although preliminary.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigor:** The algorithm relies heavily on heuristics (e.g., the choice of γ, T, ν) without a principled justification. The discussion of convergence vs. diligence, while interesting, feels somewhat tacked on and doesn't provide concrete guidance for parameter selection. The “provable” diligence claim requires significant assumptions about the data.
*   **Insufficient Detail:** Crucially, the implementation details are lacking. For instance, the choice of the PC algorithm as the structure learner is mentioned, but the specific PC settings (e.g., significance level, maximum degree) are not. This makes reproducibility challenging. The section on probabilistic relearning is also vague.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The claim that LoSST incurs “no additional costs” in stable situations is misleading. While the weights may converge to constant values, the *computational* cost of maintaining and updating the weights and p-values is still present.
*   **Statistical Issues:** The pooling of p-values using Liptak’s method seems unnecessarily complex and potentially sensitive to the choice of parameters. Why is this approach superior to simpler alternatives? The justification is weak.
*   **Limited Scope of Simulations:** The simulations are relatively limited in scope and focus primarily on directed acyclic graphs (DAGs). The authors acknowledge adaptability to other graph types, but don't demonstrate it.
*   **Presentation & Writing Quality:** The paper contains numerous minor grammatical and stylistic issues. The notation is sometimes inconsistent (e.g., using both *r* and *t* as time indices). Equation (3) is awkwardly worded and difficult to follow. There are also redundancies and unnecessary jargon. For instance, defining ‘locally stationary’ is not strictly necessary, and the repetition of the phrase is grating.
* **Missing References:** Reference [8] is cited without specific details making it hard to find and verify. 

**Specific Concerns:**

*   Equation (1): The subscript indexing is confusing. Why is *br+1* used as a denominator when it represents a future quantity?
*   The explanation of how ρr+1 is used in Equation (4) is unclear. Why is *ar+1* capped at *γar*?
*   Figure 3: The figure caption refers to "25-datapoint window," but the x-axis appears to show individual datapoints.
*   The discussion of the Great Moderation seems superficial and doesn't provide a strong justification for the choice of variables or the interpretation of the results.
*   The statement "LoSST typically stabilized on a new model by roughly 250 datapoints after the changepoint." appears without any statistical backing.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and relatively unexplored problem.
*   Presents a novel heuristic approach.
*   Demonstrates promising results in simulations.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of theoretical rigor and justification for design choices.
*   Insufficient detail and reproducibility concerns.
*   Overstated claims and potential for misleading interpretations.
*   Numerous minor flaws in writing and presentation.
*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper identifies a valuable research direction, the lack of rigor, insufficient detail, and numerous minor flaws detract from its overall quality. The paper feels more like a preliminary investigation than a complete and polished contribution to the NIPS community. The authors should significantly revise the manuscript, addressing the concerns outlined above, before resubmitting it to a more appropriate venue. A stronger theoretical foundation, more comprehensive experiments, and a clearer presentation are crucial for establishing the merit of this work. Specifically, the authors must provide sufficient detail to enable reproduction, justify the choice of parameters and heuristics, and address the statistical concerns raised in this review.
