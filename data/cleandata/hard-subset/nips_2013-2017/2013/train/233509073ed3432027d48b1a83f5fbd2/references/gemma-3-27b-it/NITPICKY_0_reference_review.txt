This paper proposes an online algorithm, LoSST, for tracking time-varying graphical model structures. The core idea is to maintain sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) in an online fashion, weighting datapoints based on their “fit” to the current model, and periodically relearning the graph structure. The algorithm is presented with a focus on recursive SEMs, but claims adaptability to other graphical model types. The paper includes a theoretical analysis of convergence vs. diligence, and simulation results demonstrating performance on both static and changing structures, as well as an application to US price index data.

While the problem addressed – online learning of graphical models – is undoubtedly important and under-explored, and the proposed algorithm represents a reasonable attempt to fill this gap, I have substantial concerns regarding the paper’s technical detail, clarity, and ultimately, its justification for acceptance at NIPS. Despite presenting a novel algorithm, the multitude of minor flaws and the lack of rigorous validation significantly undermine the paper’s contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** The problem of learning dynamic graphical models is crucial in many real-world applications.
*   **Algorithm Idea:** The core idea of online sufficient statistic tracking with dynamic weighting is conceptually sound.
*   **Convergence/Diligence Analysis:** The discussion of the trade-off between convergence and diligence is insightful.
*   **Simulation Results:** The simulation results offer initial evidence of the algorithm's functionality.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigor in Theoretical Analysis:** The convergence/diligence analysis, while conceptually interesting, lacks formal proofs beyond sketches. The conditions for convergence and diligence are presented without sufficient justification or a clear understanding of their practical implications. Specifically, the claim regarding convergence when γ = 1 feels overly simplistic and potentially incorrect without more careful consideration of potential non-stationarity in the underlying data distribution even if the structure itself remains constant.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity & Justification:** The choice of parameters (T, γ, ν) appears largely ad-hoc. The simulation results state “unsystematic investigation led to T = 0.05 and γ = 3,” which is unacceptable for a NIPS submission. A sensitivity analysis is missing, and there's no guidance on how to choose these parameters in practice. The justification for these values isn't provided.
*   **Mahalanobis Distance & Outlier Handling:** The reliance on the Mahalanobis distance for change detection is problematic. The algorithm doesn't adequately distinguish between genuine structural changes and outliers. The pooling method for p-values, while cited, is presented without sufficient detail to fully understand its properties and potential limitations. The assumption that a sequence of “slightly above average” Mahalanobis distances reliably indicates structural change is weak.
*   **Computational Complexity Concerns:** The paper briefly mentions computational expense of graph learning. However, no analysis of LoSST's computational complexity is provided, and it's unclear how well it will scale to higher-dimensional data. The impact of frequent graph relearning on computational cost is glossed over.
*   **Clarity & Organization:** The paper is often dense and difficult to follow. The notation is inconsistent in places (e.g., differing uses of subscripts and superscripts), and the explanations for some equations (particularly Eq. 2 and 3) are insufficient. Section 3.1 is particularly convoluted.
*   **Simulation Details:** The simulation setup lacks crucial details. What are the specific SEMs generated? How are the graph and parameter changes implemented? Were different types of changes investigated (e.g., addition vs. deletion of edges)? The limited exploration of simulation scenarios weakens the conclusions. The comparison to the batch PC algorithm is not entirely fair, as the LoSST algorithm is given a fixed relearning schedule. A more adaptive comparison, based on some performance criterion, would be more informative.
*   **Application Section – Weak Interpretation:** The application to US price index data is superficial. The interpretation of the detected “changepoints” feels speculative and isn’t strongly supported by the analysis. The claim that LoSST detects the “Great Moderation” is a significant assertion that requires more rigorous justification.
*   **Minor Errors/Inconsistencies:** Numerous minor typographical errors, grammatical mistakes, and inconsistencies are present throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent use of terminology, missing references).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important open problem.
*   Presents a novel algorithm that offers a potentially viable solution.
*   Initial simulation results suggest promise.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of rigorous theoretical analysis.
*   Insufficient justification for parameter choices.
*   Sensitivity to outliers and lack of robust change detection.
*   Unclear computational complexity.
*   Poor clarity and organization.
*   Limited and poorly-described simulation setup.
*   Weak application study.
*   Numerous minor errors.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially valuable contribution of addressing online graphical model learning, the numerous shortcomings detailed above lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper feels premature and requires substantial revisions to address the technical flaws, improve clarity, and provide more compelling evidence of its effectiveness. The lack of rigorous analysis and the ad-hoc nature of many decisions undermine the paper's credibility. While the idea is promising, the execution falls short of NIPS standards. I would encourage the authors to address these concerns and resubmit in the future.
