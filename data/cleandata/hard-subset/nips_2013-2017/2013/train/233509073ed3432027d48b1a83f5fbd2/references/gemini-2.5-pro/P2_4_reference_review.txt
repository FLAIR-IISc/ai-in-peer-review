This paper introduces LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models in an online fashion from data where the underlying generative process may change over time. The core problem—learning from non-stationary data streams—is a significant challenge, as most standard structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data. The proposed solution tracks weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and effective sample size) and updates the weights based on the fit of new data to the current model. A poor fit, measured by a sequence of large Mahalanobis distances (assessed via a pooled p-value), signals a potential change, causing the algorithm to up-weight recent data and effectively "forget" the past. A standard structure learning algorithm (here, the PC algorithm) is then run on these dynamic sufficient statistics, with the frequency of re-learning being probabilistically tied to the likelihood of a recent change. The paper relates this approach to existing work in change-point detection (CPD) and time-series graph models, correctly positioning LoSST as a unique, fully online, real-time method for directed graphical model structure learning.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a critical and practical problem that is largely neglected by mainstream graphical model learning literature. The assumption of i.i.d. data is fragile in many real-world applications (e.g., finance, sensor networks, social dynamics), and this work provides a well-motivated and principled approach to relax that assumption. The proposed method is novel in its combination of online sufficient statistic tracking, a robust change detection mechanism based on pooled p-values, and a probabilistic trigger for computationally expensive relearning steps.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The algorithm's design is sound and elegant. Using Mahalanobis distance to measure multivariate surprise is standard and appropriate. The key innovation of using Liptak's weighted pooling of p-values is clever, as it allows the system to be robust to single outliers while remaining sensitive to persistent, subtle changes—a crucial feature for this problem. The online update equations for the weighted mean, covariance, and effective sample size are clearly derived.

3.  **Theoretical Insight:** The discussion of the "convergence vs. diligence" trade-off is a major strength. It frames a fundamental dilemma in online learning within the context of the proposed algorithm and demonstrates how LoSST’s hyperparameters (`γ` and `T`) can be set to provably achieve one behavior or the other (or a balance). This elevates the paper from a purely heuristic contribution to one with a solid conceptual and theoretical grounding.

4.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation is strong. The simulations on synthetic data are well-designed, covering changes in both graph structure and parameters. The comparison with a batch-mode PC algorithm clearly demonstrates LoSST's superior ability to adapt after a changepoint, while importantly showing it incurs no performance penalty in stable periods. The experiment on probabilistic relearning effectively shows that computational costs can be managed without a significant drop in accuracy. Finally, the application to US price index data, while qualitative, serves as a compelling demonstration of the algorithm's potential on real-world, noisy data, plausibly identifying a known macroeconomic event.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm's performance depends on several key hyperparameters (`T`, `γ`, `ν`), which the authors state were chosen via "unsystematic investigation." While the theoretical discussion provides guidance on `T` and `γ` for achieving diligence or convergence, the practical trade-offs in the "balanced" regime are left as an open question. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with these parameters. This would provide users with better guidance for applying the algorithm in new domains.

2.  **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The only baseline used is a batch-mode PC algorithm applied to the entire dataset. While this effectively highlights the problem with ignoring non-stationarity, it is not a strong online learning baseline. A more compelling comparison would involve other plausible online strategies, such as a sliding-window approach (running PC on the last `k` data points) or a combination of an off-the-shelf CPD algorithm that triggers a relearning on a recent window of data. While the authors argue against these methods in the text, an empirical comparison would make their case more definitive.

3.  **Scalability:** The experiments are conducted on graphs with a maximum of 20 variables. The underlying structure learning engine (PC algorithm) is known to have scalability issues. It is unclear how LoSST would perform on higher-dimensional problems, both in terms of computational feasibility (especially the covariance matrix inversion) and statistical accuracy. Some discussion of the computational complexity and potential scalability challenges would be beneficial.

4.  **Clarity on Proof Sketches:** The paper provides "proof sketches" for the convergence and diligence claims. While detailed proofs are not always required for a conference paper, the sketches are very brief. For instance, the diligence proof relies on the weight `ar` strictly increasing, but this depends on the `max{1, ...}` term in Equation 4 being greater than 1, a condition that could be stated more explicitly in terms of `ρr+1`.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the field of graphical model learning. It tackles an important, practical problem with a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound algorithm. The theoretical analysis of the convergence/diligence trade-off is a standout feature, and the experimental results are comprehensive and convincing. While there are minor weaknesses regarding hyperparameter tuning and the choice of baselines, these do not detract from the core contribution. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. It is highly likely that other researchers will use and build upon these ideas. I strongly recommend acceptance.
