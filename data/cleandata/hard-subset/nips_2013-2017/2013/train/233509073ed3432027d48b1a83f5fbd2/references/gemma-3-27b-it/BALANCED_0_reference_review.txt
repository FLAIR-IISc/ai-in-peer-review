This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a heuristic algorithm for online learning of graphical model structure in non-i.i.d. data streams. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) online, weighting recent data more heavily when changes in the underlying structure are detected using a Mahalanobis distance-based change point detection scheme, and then periodically relearning the graphical model structure. The authors provide simulation results demonstrating LoSST's ability to track changing structures, and show that it performs comparably to batch-mode learning when the structure is stable. A real-world application to US price index volatility is also presented.

The paper addresses an important and challenging problem. Existing graphical model structure learning algorithms largely assume i.i.d. data, a limitation in many real-world applications where the underlying relationships evolve over time.  The online learning setting adds another layer of difficulty, as the algorithm must adapt quickly to changes without requiring large amounts of data. Several recent NIPS conferences have seen work on related topics, such as dynamic Bayesian networks and time-varying Gaussian graphical models (e.g., see work by Siracusa and Fisher III, Talih and Hengartner, and related papers citing these), but these generally require full datasets or focus on specific types of changes. This work fills a gap by providing an algorithm that operates truly online and is adaptable to arbitrary structural changes.  The connection to Temporal Difference Learning (TDL) is also well-made, clearly positioning the work within the existing literature on online adaptation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of online sufficient statistic tracking with a change detection mechanism and periodic graph relearning is a novel approach. LoSST effectively bridges the gap between static structure learning and online adaptation.
* **Practicality:**  The algorithm is designed with practicality in mind, aiming for efficiency and minimal overhead in stable environments.  The use of a readily available structure learning algorithm (PC algorithm) as a subroutine further enhances its practicality.
* **Theoretical Considerations:** The authors thoughtfully discuss the trade-off between convergence and diligence, demonstrating a nuanced understanding of the limitations of online learning algorithms. Providing a proof sketch of convergence under certain parameters is a strong addition.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The simulation results convincingly demonstrate LoSST's ability to track changing structures and maintain performance in stable environments. The real-world application provides further evidence of its potential usefulness.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its underlying principles. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Heuristic Nature:** LoSST relies on several heuristic choices, such as the Mahalanobis distance threshold (T) and the weighting parameter (γ). The sensitivity of the algorithm to these parameters is not thoroughly investigated. While the authors conducted some parameter exploration, a more systematic sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors emphasize the online nature and efficiency, the computational cost of relearning the graph structure using the PC algorithm (or any comparable algorithm) can be significant, especially for high-dimensional data. The frequency of relearning, governed by ν, could become a bottleneck. The paper doesn’t detail the computational cost scaling with the number of variables, or any consideration given to approximate structure learning.
* **Limited Real-World Evaluation:** The application to US price index data is interesting, but it’s a single case study.  More diverse and extensive real-world evaluations would strengthen the claims about the algorithm's generalizability.  The details of the data preprocessing (e.g., why month-to-month differences were used) could be more thoroughly explained.
* **Lack of Comparison to Alternatives:** While the paper compares to batch-mode learning, there’s limited comparison to other online change point detection or online learning algorithms that might be relevant (e.g., online Bayesian methods or other sequential hypothesis testing approaches).
* **Tuning of ν:** The choice of ν = 0.005 seems somewhat arbitrary. Justification, or exploration of other values, would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and under-explored problem in graphical model learning.
* The proposed algorithm is novel, practical, and theoretically grounded.
* The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the algorithm and its underlying principles.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The algorithm relies on several heuristic choices that require careful tuning.
* The computational complexity of graph relearning could be a bottleneck.
* The real-world evaluation is limited.
* The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to alternative online learning algorithms.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The LoSST algorithm is a promising approach for online structure learning in non-i.i.d. data, and the authors have provided compelling evidence of its effectiveness. While the paper has some weaknesses, they are not critical, and the strengths outweigh the drawbacks. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Conduct a more thorough sensitivity analysis of the key parameters (T, γ, ν) to understand their impact on performance.
*   Discuss the computational complexity of the algorithm in more detail, particularly the cost of graph relearning, and consider potential optimizations or approximations.
*   Expand the real-world evaluation to include more diverse datasets and applications.
*   Compare LoSST to other relevant online change point detection or online learning algorithms.
*   Provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the choice of ν = 0.005.
*   Clarify the data preprocessing steps in the real-world application.
