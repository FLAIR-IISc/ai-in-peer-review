Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical model structure in linear-Gaussian SEMs (DAGs). The core idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) and adapt the weights online via pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances to detect distributional mismatch. When the pooled p-value indicates change, the algorithm increases the weight on recent data (akin to adaptive forgetting), shrinks the effective sample size, and increases the probability of triggering structure relearning using a constraint-based learner (PC). Theoretical discussion distinguishes “convergence” vs “diligence” regimes controlled by parameters T and γ; the algorithm is shown to be convergent when weights remain constant and (under certain settings) diligent when weights grow, with proof sketches. Experiments on synthetic SEMs with abrupt changes to graph structure/parameters and a real CPI volatility dataset show that LoSST matches batch PC in stationary segments and adapts much better after changes; probabilistic relearning reduces computation without degrading error.

Relative to prior work, LoSST combines ideas from TD-style tracking, changepoint detection (e.g., BOCPD), and constraint-based causal discovery. It differs from batch time-varying structure learners and related Bayesian approaches by being single-pass and frequentist. However, the paper does not compare to common baselines such as sliding-window/exponential-forgetting PC, CPD-triggered relearning, or time-varying Gaussian graphical models (e.g., graphical lasso with temporal regularization), and it omits references to dynamic Bayesian networks and time-varying network estimation in the GGM literature.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The online updates for weighted mean/covariance are standard and appear correct; the “effective sample size” update is heuristic but reasonable. Using Hotelling’s T^2 for Mahalanobis distances assumes Gaussianity and an unweighted covariance estimate; applying it with a weighted covariance and “effective” sample size is an approximation and should be justified or calibrated. The Lipták p-value pooling and the weight mapping (Eq. 4) are heuristic choices; they are plausible but not unique.
- Theory: The convergence/diligence discussion is insightful, but only proof sketches are provided and without finite-sample guarantees or bounds on false alarms/detection delay. No guarantees are given for consistency of learned structure under slow drift or for the relearning trigger’s statistical properties.
- Experiments: Synthetic evaluations are clear and demonstrate adaptation after changes and parity with batch PC pre-change. The parameter-change-only scenario helps tease apart behavior. The probabilistic relearning experiment is useful. However, key comparisons are missing: (1) sliding-window PC with tuned window size, (2) exponential forgetting with fixed rate, (3) BOCPD (or frequentist CPD) coupled to batch PC, and (4) time-varying GGM methods for undirected structure as a sanity check on the detection mechanism. There is no ablation on T, γ, ν or on the p-value pooling choice; no robustness study to outliers vs sustained shift; and no runtime/memory profile or high-dimensional regimes. The real-data case study is illustrative but lacks ground truth and statistical evaluation.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, and the algorithmic steps are explicit. A few issues:
- The T^2 p-value expression seems to mix notation (p vs V and N vs S_r); please check consistency and clarify the appropriateness under weighted estimates.
- Numerical stability when inverting C_r with small effective sample sizes is not addressed; suggest regularization or shrinkage.
- The role and tuning of T, γ, ν are only lightly motivated; add guidance and sensitivity plots.
- The conditions for diligence/convergence would benefit from fuller proofs in the supplement and concrete parameter regions.

Originality
The combination of pooled p-values to drive adaptive forgetting for online structure learning in DAGs is novel to my knowledge, and the convergence–diligence framing is interesting. That said, the overall recipe (adaptive weighting + periodic PC relearning) is a natural combination of known components, and related time-varying network literatures (dynamic BNs, time-varying GGMs, CPD-triggered learners) are not fully engaged or compared.

Significance
The problem is important: online structure learning for nonstationary settings is relevant to causal discovery and streaming ML. If robust, the approach could be a practical baseline for streaming SEMs and potentially adaptable to other graphical families. However, the current evidence is limited by missing baselines, lack of guarantees, and modest scale; impact would be stronger with broader comparisons and analysis.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and practical, single-pass algorithm.
- Intuitive mechanism tying detection to adaptive effective sample size.
- Useful conceptualization of convergence vs diligence.
- Synthetic results convincingly show adaptation; probabilistic relearning reduces compute.

Cons
- Heuristic statistical machinery (T^2 with weighted covariance and “effective” N; pooled p-values) lacks calibration/guarantees.
- No comparisons to strong, simple baselines (sliding windows, exponential forgetting, CPD-triggered relearning) or to time-varying GGM/DBN methods.
- Limited sensitivity analysis and guidance for hyperparameters; potential numerical issues not discussed.
- Real-data evaluation is qualitative with no ground truth or alternative methods for cross-validation.
- Scope limited to linear-Gaussian SEMs; PC repeatedly re-run can be expensive; scalability not demonstrated.

Questions and suggestions
- How sensitive are results to T, γ, ν and to the choice of p-value pooling? Provide ablations.
- Can you justify the use of Hotelling’s T^2 with weighted covariance and effective S_r (e.g., via simulation calibration of p-values) or switch to a statistic with known properties under weighting?
- Compare against sliding-window PC (with window size chosen by validation or ADWIN), exponential forgetting with fixed rate, and a CPD-triggered relearning baseline.
- Add regularization for C_r inversion and report behavior in p >> n or small effective sample regimes.
- Provide runtime and memory results; how does probabilistic relearning trade accuracy vs compute?

Recommendation
Weak reject. The problem is timely and the approach is promising, but the paper falls short on empirical comparisons and statistical justification of its detection/weighting mechanism. With stronger baselines, sensitivity analyses, runtime results, and clearer theoretical guarantees or calibration, this could become a solid contribution.
