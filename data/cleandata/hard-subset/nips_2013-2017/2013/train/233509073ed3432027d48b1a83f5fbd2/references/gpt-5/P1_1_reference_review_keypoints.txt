- Proposes LoSST: an online heuristic that maintains weighted sufficient statistics and upweights recent data using a pooled Mahalanobis p-value to track time-varying linear-Gaussian SEM structure.  
- Uses Liptak pooling of successive Mahalanobis p-values to compute a pooled lack-of-fit ρ that drives adaptive effective sample size updates and probabilistic relearning.  
- Learns structure by running the PC algorithm on current weighted statistics with a relearning probability that rises as ρ decreases.  
- Frames a convergence–diligence trade-off controlled by parameters T and γ; provides informal proof sketches for limiting behaviors.  
- Evaluated on synthetic changepoint experiments (structure and/or parameter shifts) and a small U.S. price-index case study showing detection of macro volatility shifts.  
- Positioned between online change-point detection and structure learning literature; claims novelty as an online frequentist approach avoiding posterior maintenance.  
- Omits deeper comparison to relevant baselines like sliding-window PC, EWMA+PC, BOCPD+retraining, fused-lasso/time-varying GGM, and other dynamic network methods.  
- Strength: tackles an important practical problem with a simple, modular single-pass design that can use off-the-shelf learners.  
- Strength: intuitive relearning schedule tied to lack-of-fit and shown to outperform batch PC after changes in synthetic experiments.  
- Strength: clear exposition of the convergence–diligence trade-off and modularity for extension.  
- Weakness: statistical calibration of Hotelling’s T^2 p-values with adaptively weighted means/covariances is heuristic and unjustified; dependence between p-values ignored in pooling.  
- Weakness: effective sample-size update rule is ad hoc with no proof of unbiasedness or correct CI calibration for partial correlations.  
- Weakness: theoretical results are informal; lacks bounds on false alarm rates, detection delay, structural recovery, and sample complexity in p/sparsity/change magnitude.  
- Weakness: crucial implementation details missing—how partial correlations/CIs are computed from weighted stats, α-levels, max conditioning set, covariance regularization, and numerical stability.  
- Weakness: hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) tuned unsystematically; no sensitivity or ablation studies provided.  
- Weakness: computational complexity and scalability not analyzed; online matrix inversion may be O(p^3).  
- Weakness: real-data validation is anecdotal without ground-truth structure or predictive/forecasting evaluation.  
- Quality: algorithm is a reasonable heuristic with positive but limited empirical evidence; probabilistic steps lack theoretical grounding (T^2 calibration, Liptak pooling under dependence).  
- Clarity: paper is generally clear and organized but has typos and misses reproducibility-critical implementation details.  
- Originality: moderately novel integration of adaptive weighted sufficient statistics, pooled lack-of-fit, and PC-based relearning; components individually are known.  
- Significance: potentially useful as a simple online structure-tracking baseline, but impact limited by weak baselines, missing guarantees, and unclear scalability.  
- Suggestions: validate/calibrate T^2 p-values (permutation/block-bootstrap), compare to sliding-window/EWMA/BOCPD/fused-lasso baselines, perform sensitivity and detection-delay vs false-alarm studies, detail PC test computation and regularization, and analyze runtime.  
- Pros for acceptance: addresses an underexplored problem with a practical, modular approach and promising synthetic/illustrative real-data results.  
- Cons for acceptance: heuristic foundations without calibration, weak baselines, informal theory, and under-specified implementation hurt reproducibility and confidence.  
- Recommendation: borderline reject—promising idea but requires stronger empirical comparisons, calibration of change signals, ablations, and clearer theoretical/implementation details.
