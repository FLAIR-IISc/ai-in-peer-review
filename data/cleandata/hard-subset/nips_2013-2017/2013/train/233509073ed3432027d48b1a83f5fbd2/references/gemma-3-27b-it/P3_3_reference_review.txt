## Review of "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper tackles a crucial and largely unexplored problem: learning graphical model structure in non-stationary environments, particularly in an online fashion with limited data. The proposed LoSST algorithm offers a compelling heuristic approach that balances responsiveness to change with stability, demonstrating promising results in synthetic data and a real-world application. While the theoretical guarantees are somewhat limited and the methodology relies on several practical choices (e.g., PC algorithm, weight tuning), the contribution is significant and well-motivated. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses the limitations of existing graphical model structure learning algorithms, which generally assume static underlying structures. It proposes LoSST, an online algorithm that dynamically tracks sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) and re-learns the graphical model structure as needed. The core idea is to differentially weight datapoints based on their fit to the current model, increasing the weight of recent data when a change is detected. 

The authors provide a solid review of related work, accurately highlighting the shortcomings of Bayesian/score-based and constraint-based methods in dynamic settings. They correctly identify the limitations of online changepoint detection algorithms (data storage, assumptions about change type) and existing time-indexed graph learning approaches (batch mode requirement).  The positioning of LoSST as a frequentist, real-time alternative to Bayesian approaches (like Siracusa & Fisher III, 2009) is well-articulated. The connection to Temporal Difference Learning (TDL) and its limitations are also insightful.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:**  The problem of learning dynamic graphical models is highly relevant across numerous domains, where underlying relationships are not static (e.g., financial markets, climate modeling, biological systems).
* **Novelty of Approach:** LoSST presents a novel combination of online statistic tracking and dynamic graph re-learning. The weighting scheme based on Mahalanobis distance and pooled p-values is a clever heuristic for detecting structural changes.
* **Theoretical Considerations:** The authors thoughtfully address the inherent trade-off between convergence and diligence, providing a theoretical framework for understanding the algorithm’s behavior under different parameter settings.  The analysis of convergence/diligence based on the γ and T parameters is valuable.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The simulations demonstrate LoSST's ability to track both structural and parameter changes.  The comparison with the batch-mode PC algorithm clearly highlights the benefits of online learning in dynamic environments.  The real-world application to US price index data provides a compelling case study.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas. The algorithm is explained in detail, making it relatively easy to understand.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Heuristic Nature:**  LoSST relies heavily on heuristics, particularly in the choice of the PC algorithm for structure learning and the specific functions used for weighting datapoints (Eq. 4). The sensitivity of the algorithm to these choices could be a limitation. While justified, a more thorough sensitivity analysis exploring alternatives would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The performance of LoSST depends on carefully tuning several parameters (α, γ, T, ν). The paper provides some guidance, but a more systematic study of the parameter space would be valuable. The selection of T=0.05 and γ=3 feels somewhat arbitrary, and the justification ("seemed to appropriately trade off...") is weak.
* **Theoretical Gaps:** The theoretical analysis of convergence/diligence is insightful, but doesn't provide strong guarantees about the algorithm's performance in all scenarios. Further theoretical work characterizing the conditions under which LoSST converges or exhibits diligence would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost:** While the algorithm is designed for online learning, the computational cost of re-learning the graph structure using the PC algorithm can still be significant, especially for high-dimensional data.  This isn’t fully addressed.
* **Limited Real-World Evaluation:** The real-world application is a good starting point, but it is limited to a single dataset. Applying LoSST to a wider range of real-world problems would strengthen the conclusions.



**4. Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Solves a critical and under-addressed problem in graphical model learning.
* Presents a novel and promising algorithmic approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical performance in synthetic data and a real-world case study.
* Offers a thoughtful theoretical discussion of the trade-offs involved.
* Well-written and clearly explained.

**Con:**

* Relies on several heuristics that could impact performance.
* Requires careful parameter tuning.
* Lacks strong theoretical guarantees.
* Computational cost of graph re-learning could be a bottleneck.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more detailed sensitivity analysis of the key parameters (α, γ, T, ν)?  How does the algorithm’s performance vary with different parameter settings?
*  Are there alternative structure learning algorithms (beyond PC) that could be used with LoSST? What are the trade-offs involved?
*  Could you discuss the computational complexity of LoSST in more detail, particularly with respect to the graph re-learning step?
*  Do you have any plans for extending LoSST to handle discrete variables (e.g., Bayesian networks)?
*  The application to US price index data is interesting.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the insights gained from this analysis?



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Expand the sensitivity analysis of the key parameters.
2.  Provide a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity.
3. Briefly address the potential for exploring alternative structure learning algorithms.

These revisions would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the field. The work represents a significant step towards enabling the development of dynamic graphical models for real-world applications.
