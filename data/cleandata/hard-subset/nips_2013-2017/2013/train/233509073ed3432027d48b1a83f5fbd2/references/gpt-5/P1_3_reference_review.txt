Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), an online method for learning and tracking time-varying graphical structure in linear-Gaussian DAG models (recursive SEMs). The core idea is to maintain sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective sample size”) using adaptive exponential weighting. Weights ar are adjusted based on the pooled evidence that recent data are unlikely under the current estimated covariance: each incoming point yields a Mahalanobis distance, converted to a p-value via a Hotelling T^2 approximation, and pooled over time with Liptak’s method into ρr. When ρr is small, ar is increased via a piecewise-linear schedule with a maximum multiplier γ; this shrinks the effective sample size and emphasizes recent data. Structure is relearned periodically using the PC algorithm, with a probabilistic triggering rule that increases relearning probability when ρr is small. The paper provides heuristic update formulas, proofs/sketches for a convergence-versus-diligence trade-off, synthetic experiments with change points, and a small real-data case study (U.S. price index volatilities) that qualitatively detects the “Great Moderation.”

The work complements: (i) batch structure learning (score-based or constraint-based) that assumes stationarity; (ii) change point detection (Bayesian and frequentist) that focuses on detecting abrupt changes but not learning a DAG structure online; and (iii) prior time-varying graph learning that is batch and often undirected (e.g., Talih & Hengartner) or Bayesian with high computational cost (Siracusa & Fisher). The paper distinguishes LoSST by its real-time, single-pass, DAG capability with minimal memory. That said, the connection to dynamic Bayesian networks, adaptive forgetting/EWMA covariance tracking, classical statistical process control (CUSUM/Page-Hinkley), and time-varying GGMs (e.g., fused lasso/locally linear smoothness) is not fully developed.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The update rules for means/covariances are standard for exponentially weighted statistics; the effective sample size update Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1)Sr + 1 is plausible but ad hoc. A key statistical concern is the use of Hotelling T^2 with “effective” degrees of freedom derived from weighted statistics; the null distribution is not guaranteed under adaptive weighting and non-i.i.d. sequences. Similarly, pooling p-values assumes independence/validity of the individual p-values, which is doubtful when the same evolving covariance is estimated using adaptively weighted data. These choices are reasonable heuristics, but the paper sometimes treats the resulting ρr as calibrated evidence. More discussion or calibration (e.g., via simulations of false positive rates under stationarity) would strengthen the claims.
- Theory: The convergence/diligence results are interesting, but largely given as sketches and depend on thresholds and monotonicity of weights. A formal definition of diligence and a proof with explicit constants (how many samples to detect a change of given size) would add rigor.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments are thoughtful and reproduce two regimes (structure+parameter changes and parameter-only changes) and a many-change scenario with probabilistic relearning. However, important baselines are missing: sliding-window PC with tuned window size; exponentially weighted PC with fixed forgetting; CPD+reset; and time-varying graphical models (even if undirected). There are no sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν, and no error bars. Computational/memory costs are not quantified. The real-data example is illustrative but lacks ground truth and alternative explanations (e.g., macro covariate shifts).
- Practical details: In high dimension or shortly after a change, the covariance may be ill-conditioned; no regularization is described (e.g., shrinkage, ridge). PC’s CI tests depend on partial correlations; how significance thresholds are set and corrected is not specified. The relearning trigger is heuristic (“noisy-OR”); how to tune ν/T/γ in practice is unclear.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The high-level intuition is clear, and the algorithmic pieces are explicit. However, some derivations are only sketched; assumptions for the T^2 approximation and pooled p-value validity are not spelled out; and implementation/tuning details are sparse. Adding a pseudo-code summary, parameter guidelines, and numerical stability/regularization strategies would help reproducibility.

Originality
The combination of adaptive, pooled p-value-based forgetting with online maintenance of sufficient statistics for DAG structure learning appears novel, particularly for directed models. Prior work on dynamic networks has focused on undirected models or batch Bayesian approaches. The formalization of a convergence vs. diligence trade-off in this setting is interesting. Nonetheless, the components (EWMA-like updates, Mahalanobis distances, p-value pooling, periodic relearning) are individually known; the novelty is in their integration and focus on online DAG learning.

Significance
If validated, LoSST would be a useful tool for streaming causal/associational structure discovery with low memory. This is an important problem. The current evidence suggests promise, but the methodological heuristics and missing baselines limit how strongly we can conclude superiority over simpler alternatives (e.g., sliding windows). Strengthening the statistical foundations and comparisons would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Tackles an important, underexplored problem: online DAG structure tracking.
- Simple, memory-efficient algorithm with intuitive components.
- Clear articulation of a convergence–diligence trade-off; potentially useful conceptual contribution.
- Synthetic results indicate substantial gains after changes, with parity during stationarity.
- Real-data case suggests plausible macroeconomic regime detection.

Cons for acceptance
- Reliance on heuristic statistical approximations (T^2 with adaptive weighting, pooled p-values) without calibration or formal guarantees.
- Missing strong baselines (sliding-window PC, fixed forgetting, CPD+reset, time-varying GGMs/DBNs).
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to parameters and robustness to non-Gaussian noise/outliers.
- No discussion of regularization/conditioning when covariance is singular or ill-conditioned.
- Theoretical results are sketches; diligence claim would benefit from precise bounds.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide calibration of the p-value machinery under stationarity and controlled drifts (type I error, detection delay).
- Compare against: sliding-window PC (tuned by CV), fixed EWMA forgetting, Bayesian online CPD triggering resets, and time-varying graphical lasso (as an undirected benchmark).
- Add parameter sensitivity studies for T, γ, ν; offer default guidelines.
- Incorporate covariance regularization and discuss its impact on CI tests and PC.
- Consider robustness (heavy tails/outliers): robust covariance/Mahalanobis, rank-based tests.
- Clarify computational cost per step and scaling with variables; report runtime.
- Expand related work to dynamic Bayesian networks and SPC methods (CUSUM, Page-Hinkley), and recent time-varying network estimation.

Overall recommendation
Promising and relevant contribution with an elegant, lightweight approach, but currently somewhat heuristic and empirically undercompared. With stronger validation and baselines, it could be a solid addition. As is, I lean toward a borderline/weak reject, encouraging a revision addressing the above points.
