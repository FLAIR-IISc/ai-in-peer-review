### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel online algorithm for learning the structure of directed graphical models (specifically, recursive Structural Equation Models) from data where the underlying generative process may change over time. The core assumption is that the data-generating process is "locally stationary." LoSST operates by tracking sufficient statistics (weighted mean and covariance) in an online fashion. The key innovation is an adaptive weighting scheme where each new data point's contribution is determined by its fit to the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. The p-values of these distances are pooled over time using Liptak's method to provide a robust signal of model misfit, which in turn adjusts the data weights and triggers probabilistic relearning of the graph structure. The authors introduce a theoretical trade-off between "convergence" (stability in a stationary environment) and "diligence" (rapid detection of change), showing how LoSST's parameters can navigate this trade-off. Experimental results on synthetic data show that LoSST successfully tracks changes in both graph structure and parameters, significantly outperforming a standard batch learning algorithm (PC) in non-stationary settings while matching its performance in stationary ones. An application to US price index data demonstrates the algorithm's ability to detect known macroeconomic shifts.

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a critical and surprisingly under-explored problem in the graphical models literature. While online parameter learning and offline change-point detection are well-studied, online *structure* learning for graphical models in non-stationary environments is a significant open challenge. This work provides a principled and practical first-pass solution, representing a novel and important contribution to the field.

2.  **Elegant Methodology:** The proposed LoSST algorithm is technically sound and elegant. The use of Mahalanobis distance to measure a new data point's surprise is a natural choice. The crucial step of pooling the corresponding p-values (via Liptak's method) is a clever way to build a robust change detector that is not overly sensitive to individual outliers but responds to persistent model misfit. This provides a more principled mechanism than simple thresholding on the distance itself.

3.  **Theoretical Depth:** The discussion of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off is a major strength. It places the heuristic algorithm within a formal theoretical context, borrowing from work by Kelly [5]. Providing proof sketches for the conditions under which LoSST is convergent or diligent (controlled by parameters `γ` and `T`) elevates the paper beyond a purely empirical contribution and demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem's theoretical foundations.

4.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The simulations are well-designed and convincing.
    *   The comparison against a batch PC algorithm clearly demonstrates the catastrophic failure of standard methods when the i.i.d. assumption is violated and highlights the value of LoSST.
    *   The separation of experiments into (a) structure+parameter change and (b) parameter-only change provides valuable insight. The authors are commendably honest in reporting and explaining the temporary worse performance of LoSST on edge removal errors in the parameter-only case, which strengthens the credibility of the results.
    *   The experiment with probabilistic relearning effectively demonstrates that the system can achieve good performance without the prohibitive cost of relearning the graph at every time step.
    *   The application to real-world economic data, while brief, serves as a good proof of concept, showing that the detected change points align with known historical events (the "Great Moderation").

### Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection / Areas for Improvement)

1.  **Computational Cost and Scalability:** The paper overlooks a critical computational bottleneck. Calculating the Mahalanobis distance at each step `r+1` requires computing `(C_r)⁻¹`, the inverse of the current V x V covariance matrix. This operation is O(V³), which makes the per-sample update cost prohibitive for high-dimensional problems (e.g., V > 100). While the probabilistic relearning reduces the cost of the expensive *structure search* step, the O(V³) cost is incurred *for every single data point*. This is a major limitation for many potential applications and should be explicitly discussed. Are there ways to approximate this, for instance, using updates to the precision matrix?

2.  **Baseline Competitiveness:** The choice of baseline—a batch PC algorithm applied to the entire dataset—is a straw man. It is designed to fail in a non-stationary setting. A more compelling and realistic comparison would involve stronger baselines designed for non-stationary data, such as:
    *   A **sliding window approach**, where PC is run on only the last `N` data points. This is a common and simple heuristic for tracking change.
    *   A **combination of a standard changepoint detection (CPD) algorithm and a batch learner**. For example, one could use a method like [1] or [4] to segment the data and then run PC on each detected segment. Comparing LoSST to such a two-stage pipeline would provide a much stronger statement about its relative advantages.

3.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The key parameters `γ`, `T`, and `ν` appear to be critical to the algorithm's performance, governing the diligence-convergence trade-off and relearning frequency. However, they are set based on "unsystematic investigation" for the simulations. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with these parameters. Guidance on how a user might set these parameters in a principled way for a new problem is currently lacking.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:**
    *   The derivation of the weighted covariance update (Equation 2) is omitted. While this might be standard for specialists (related to weighted Welford's algorithm), including a brief derivation in an appendix or providing a specific citation would improve the paper's self-containedness and reproducibility.
    *   The real-world application is interesting but incomplete. It shows *that* LoSST detects change but not *what* change it detects. Visualizing the learned graph structures before and after the major detected change point around 1980 would provide a much more powerful and interpretable result.

### Overall Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that tackles an important and challenging problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. The strengths—particularly the originality of the problem formulation, the elegance of the method, and the insightful theoretical discussion—are significant. The empirical results are strong and honestly reported. While the weaknesses concerning computational scalability, the choice of baselines, and parameter sensitivity are notable, they represent avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws in the core contribution. The paper clearly advances the state of the art and is likely to inspire follow-up research.

**Decision: Accept.**

The authors should be strongly encouraged to address the weaknesses in their camera-ready version, especially by adding a discussion of the O(V³) update cost and its implications for scalability, and by acknowledging the potential for stronger baselines in the related work or future work sections.
