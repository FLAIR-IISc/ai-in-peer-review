This paper attempts to address the challenging problem of learning graphical model structure in a non-stationary environment, specifically in an online fashion. The core idea – the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) – is to track sufficient statistics and dynamically relearn the graphical model when evidence suggests a structural change. While the problem statement is relevant and potentially impactful, the paper suffers from a lack of rigorous evaluation and relies heavily on heuristic choices without sufficient justification.

The primary strength lies in identifying a gap in existing methods. Current structure learning algorithms are fundamentally batch-oriented, and directly applying online learning techniques like TD(0) or changepoint detection proves insufficient for the task. The authors correctly point out the limitations of these approaches. Furthermore, the attempt to balance convergence and diligence, recognizing their inherent incompatibility, is a thoughtful consideration.

However, the weaknesses are significant. The algorithm itself relies on a cascade of heuristic decisions: the Mahalanobis distance-based change detection, the pooling method for p-values (Liptak’s method), the transformation from pooled p-value to weighting factor, and the probabilistic relearning schedule. While these choices are explained, the justification for *these specific* implementations is weak. Why Liptak’s method? Why the chosen weighting function? These choices dramatically impact performance, and their selection lacks a strong theoretical or empirical basis. 

The simulation results, while demonstrating *some* improvement over a static batch-mode learner after a change, are presented in a rather hand-wavy fashion. The figures show lines representing different parameter settings, but without a systematic exploration of the parameter space. The parameter choices for the real-world application (T=0.05, γ=3) are described as "unsystematic investigation," which is unacceptable for a rigorous evaluation. The discussion of the application to US price index data is similarly superficial. Detecting shifts in volatility is interesting, but the connection to the LoSST algorithm's mechanics and the significance of those shifts remain unclear. The assertion that LoSST "detects exactly such a shift" needs more concrete backing.

The paper lacks a thorough comparison to simpler, well-established baselines. While the PC algorithm is used as a batch-mode comparison, the performance of even basic online adaptation techniques applied *to* PC (e.g., retraining PC periodically with a fixed window of recent data) isn't presented.  This makes it difficult to assess the true value added by LoSST’s complexity.  The claim of minimal overhead during stable periods is not convincingly demonstrated, as the computational cost of updating sufficient statistics and evaluating the Mahalanobis distance isn’t quantified.

The writing is generally clear, but the mathematical notation could be improved for readability. For example, the numerous subscripts and superscripts in the sufficient statistics updates become difficult to follow quickly.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a relevant and under-explored problem.
* Identifies limitations of existing methods.
* Demonstrates *potential* for improved performance in dynamic environments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Heavily relies on heuristic choices without sufficient justification.
* Evaluation is limited, lacks systematic parameter exploration, and comparison to strong baselines.
* Real-world application lacks concrete analysis and significance assessment.
*  The methodological soundness is questionable given the lack of rigorous validation. 



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the problem is interesting and the authors identify a valid gap, the paper lacks the rigor and thorough evaluation required for acceptance at NIPS. The reliance on heuristics, the superficial evaluation, and the absence of strong baselines undermine the claims of novelty and significance. The paper reads more like a proof-of-concept than a fully developed research contribution.  Significant revisions, including a more systematic evaluation, comparison to stronger baselines, and a more detailed justification for the algorithmic choices, would be necessary for reconsideration.
