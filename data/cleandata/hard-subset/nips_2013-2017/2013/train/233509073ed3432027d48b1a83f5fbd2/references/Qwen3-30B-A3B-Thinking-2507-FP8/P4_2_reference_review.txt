

The paper proposes the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm for online learning of graphical model structure when the underlying structure may change over time. The authors identify a gap in the literature: standard graphical model structure learning algorithms assume stationary structures (batch learning), while online changepoint detection methods do not learn new graphical structures. LoSST tracks sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) in an online fashion using weighted updates based on Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values. The algorithm dynamically relearns the graphical model structure when changes are detected, using a probabilistic relearning mechanism that balances convergence (stabilizing on stable structures) and diligence (responding quickly to structural changes).

The paper relates its work to key NIPS contributions on graphical model structure learning (Chickering [3], Spirtes et al. [12]), online learning (Sutton [13]), and changepoint detection (Adams and MacKay [1]). It contrasts with two relevant prior works on time-varying graph structures (Talih and Hengartner [14], Siracusa and Fisher [11]), noting those methods require batch processing and make specific assumptions about structure changes.

Strengths:
1. The problem addressed is significant and relevant to real-world applications where data streams exhibit time-varying dependencies (e.g., financial markets, biological systems).
2. The algorithm is technically sound, with detailed mathematical derivations of the sufficient statistics update rules and theoretical analysis of convergence vs. diligence properties.
3. The simulation results comprehensively demonstrate LoSST's performance: it matches batch learning during stable periods and significantly outperforms batch learning when structure changes (Figures 1-3).
4. The real-world application to US price index volatility (Figure 4) demonstrates practical relevance, showing LoSST detects meaningful economic shifts (e.g., the Great Moderation) while avoiding false positives from short-term fluctuations.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper does not thoroughly compare LoSST to alternative online learning approaches for graphical models, despite acknowledging the gap in the literature.
2. The parameter choices (T=0.05, γ=3) lack rigorous justification through ablation studies or sensitivity analysis.
3. The probabilistic relearning mechanism is described but not rigorously analyzed for its impact on performance trade-offs.
4. The focus on recursive SEMs over continuous variables limits applicability; the extension to discrete Bayesian networks is mentioned as future work but not demonstrated.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper fills a clear gap in the literature with a technically sound solution to an important problem. The empirical validation is comprehensive, showing both theoretical properties and practical utility. The significance is high, as online structure learning is critical for real-world applications with streaming data.

Arguments against acceptance: The novelty is somewhat incremental (combining existing techniques rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach), and the lack of thorough comparison with alternative methods weakens the novelty claim.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations and provide specific directions for future work. The technical content is appropriate for NIPS, with strong connections to previous work in the field.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims (Quality), clearly written with sufficient detail (Clarity), addresses a novel problem (Novelty), and demonstrates significant practical relevance (Significance). The algorithm provides a practical solution to a meaningful problem with minimal overhead in stable conditions, making it valuable for practitioners working with streaming data.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of graphical models and online learning, with clear potential for real-world application. The theoretical insights regarding convergence vs. diligence provide valuable methodological guidance for future work. The empirical results are convincing, and the real-world case study demonstrates the algorithm's practical utility.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It is a well-executed study that addresses an important gap in the literature with a technically sound and practically useful approach. The contribution is significant enough to warrant publication at NIPS.
