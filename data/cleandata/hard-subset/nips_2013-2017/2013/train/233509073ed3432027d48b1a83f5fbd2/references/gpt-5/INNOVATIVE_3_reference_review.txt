Summary
This paper tackles online learning of time-varying graphical structure for linear-Gaussian SEMs. The proposed Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) maintains exponentially weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, and an “effective” sample size) using single-pass updates. A key novelty is how the algorithm adapts weights over time: it computes Mahalanobis distances of incoming points under the current covariance, converts these to p-values (Hotelling’s T^2), then pools them over time via Lipták’s method to obtain a running pooled p-value ρ that drives (i) adaptive per-time-step weights ar and (ii) a probabilistic trigger for when to re-run a constraint-based structure learner (PC). The authors provide proof sketches showing that parameter choices trade off “convergence” vs. “diligence” (rapid response to changes), simulation studies on synthetic data with structural and parametric changes, and a real-data illustration on CPI volatility shifts.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself against: (a) batch structure learning (score-based and constraint-based), which assumes stationarity; (b) online changepoint detection (Bayesian and frequentist), which typically stores substantial data and assumes piecewise constant regimes; and (c) prior time-varying graph learning that is batch and often constrained (Talih & Hengartner on undirected graphs with single-edge changes; Siracusa & Fisher on Bayesian time-indexed edges). The proposed approach differs by (i) online, single-pass sufficient-statistics updates; (ii) a pooled-p-value mechanism for adaptively emphasizing recent data and triggering relearning; and (iii) using standard DAG structure discovery (PC) on the fly. It is also related to streaming/forgetting-factor methods and time-varying GGMs, but addresses directed structure and explicit online triggering.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A simple but clever bridge between online statistics (weighted means/covariances with adaptive weights), sequential testing (pooled p-values), and constraint-based DAG learning. The diligence vs. convergence framing is insightful and likely to stimulate follow-on work.
- Practicality: Single-pass, low memory footprint; only sufficient statistics and a small number of scalars are maintained. Clean interface to standard learners (PC).
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic studies show that LoSST matches batch PC in stable phases and substantially improves post-change performance, particularly for structural changes; probabilistic relearning achieves similar accuracy with far fewer rebuilds.
- Real-world case study: The CPI analysis is plausible and shows the method is not hypersensitive to single outliers, highlighting the utility of pooled p-values.

Weaknesses and questions
- Baselines: Comparisons are limited to batch PC. For a convincing case, please add:
  - Sliding-window PC with varying window sizes.
  - Exponential forgetting with fixed α (i.e., your method with constant ar; or a fixed EWMA baseline).
  - Online/batch time-varying graphical models (e.g., dynamic/temporal GGMs, time-varying graphical lasso, DBNs) adapted to linear-Gaussian settings.
  - CPD+relearn hybrids (e.g., BOCPD or CUSUM to trigger PC).
- Statistical validity of tests: PC’s conditional independence tests rely on asymptotic distributions that depend on unweighted i.i.d. samples. Here, both the covariance and “effective sample size” Sr are weighted and time-varying. Please justify the use of T^2 and Fisher’s z (or whichever test you used) under weighted sampling. Are Type I/II error rates controlled? A small-sample simulation to assess calibration would help.
- Assumptions behind pooled p-values: ρ relies on Hotelling’s T^2 under Gaussianity and i.i.d. samples from the current regime. With adaptive weights and drifting parameters, the null distribution will deviate. How robust is ρ to misspecification and heavy tails? Consider robust variants (e.g., shrinkage covariance, robust Mahalanobis) and report sensitivity.
- Parameter setting: The choice of T, γ, ν appears heuristic (“unsystematic investigation”). Provide guidance or a simple tuning heuristic. Ablations quantifying the convergence/diligence tradeoff would be valuable.
- Numerical stability: Maintaining an invertible Cr online may require regularization. Please specify stabilization (e.g., shrinkage, jitter) and its effect on Dr and tests.
- Scope: The method is designed for locally stationary regimes; performance when changes are faster than relearning cadence or when data are random mixtures should be quantified (stress tests with varying drift speed, mixture proportion).
- Clarity and notation: A few notational hiccups (e.g., ρr+1 defined using p-values up to r; footnotes embedded in text; typos around indices and capitalized variables) make sections 3 and 3.1 harder to parse. A concise algorithm box would help.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but some inferential details need tightening (distributional assumptions for weighted tests; calibration; regularization). The theory articulating convergence vs. diligence is compelling, though currently proof sketches. Experiments are informative but would benefit from stronger baselines and sensitivity analyses.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized; equations for online updates are clear. Improve exposition of the pooled p-value mechanism and the relearning trigger; add an algorithm summary and discuss computational complexity with typical V and degree.

Originality
High. The integration of pooled p-values to drive adaptive forgetting and structure relearning for DAGs in an online setting is, to my knowledge, novel and bold.

Significance
Potentially high. Many applications need online structure tracking; this work could catalyze further research and practical systems. Even as a heuristic, providing a viable, lightweight alternative to CPD-based pipelines is valuable.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and bold approach to an important problem.
- Simple, general mechanism with low memory and single-pass operation.
- Insightful convergence–diligence tradeoff framing.
- Promising empirical results and a real-data illustration.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing strong baselines and ablations; limited experimental scope.
- Statistical validity of independence testing under adaptive weighting is under-justified.
- Heuristic parameter choices without clear tuning guidance.
- Theory presented as sketches; some clarity issues.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper introduces a fresh, impactful idea that opens a new direction in online structure learning. Despite methodological imperfections and limited baselines, the novelty and potential are high, and the empirical evidence is encouraging. With additional baselines, calibration checks, and clearer guidance on parameters, this could be a strong contribution.

Confidence
Medium-high.
