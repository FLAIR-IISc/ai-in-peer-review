This paper tackles a genuinely important and largely unexplored problem: learning graphical model structure in a non-stationary world, and doing so online. The core idea of tracking sufficient statistics and dynamically relearning the graph is conceptually sound and, crucially, avoids the pitfalls of existing methods that either assume stationarity or require batch processing. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the related literature and convincingly argue why existing approaches are insufficient. The simulation results, while not exhaustive, are encouraging, showing that LoSST performs competitively with batch methods when the structure is stable and outperforms them when changes occur. The application to US price index data, though brief, adds a layer of practical relevance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The problem itself is novel, and the proposed approach – online structure learning without strong assumptions – is a significant departure from existing work. This is the paper's strongest asset.
*   **Conceptual Soundness:** The idea of tracking sufficient statistics and dynamically adapting the graph is intuitively appealing and logically consistent.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application domain (time-varying systems) is broad and important, with potential applications across numerous fields.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the algorithm's key components.
*   **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors transparently discuss the trade-offs between convergence and diligence, and acknowledge the algorithm's limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Heuristic Nature:** The algorithm relies on several heuristics (e.g., the Mahalanobis distance threshold, the weight update rule, probabilistic relearning schedule) with limited theoretical justification. While heuristics are acceptable, the sensitivity of performance to these parameters is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Limited Validation:** The simulation setup is somewhat simplistic. The choice of two SEMs is reasonable, but the limited number of scenarios and graph sizes restricts the generalizability of the results. Further investigation with more complex graphs, varying degrees of change, and different data distributions would strengthen the validation.
*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention the computational cost of graph relearning, they don't provide a detailed analysis of the algorithm's overall complexity. This is crucial for evaluating its scalability to high-dimensional data.
*   **Parameter Tuning:** The choice of parameters (γ, T, ν) appears somewhat arbitrary. More systematic parameter tuning and a sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
*   **Real-World Application Depth:** The application to US price data is suggestive but lacks a thorough analysis of the learned graphical model's economic interpretation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to a challenging problem.
*   The conceptual foundation is sound, and the algorithm is logically consistent.
*   The simulation results are encouraging, even if not exhaustive.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the research contribution.
*   The problem is of significant interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The algorithm relies heavily on heuristics, and its performance is sensitive to parameter tuning.
*   The validation is limited, and the generalizability of the results is uncertain.
*   The computational complexity is not thoroughly analyzed.
*   The real-world application is superficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the problem and the potential impact of the proposed solution outweigh the methodological limitations. The paper introduces a promising direction for future research and provides a valuable starting point for developing more robust and scalable online structure learning algorithms. I am willing to overlook the incomplete validation and heuristic nature of the algorithm, given the boldness of the approach and the importance of the problem.

**Recommendation:** Accept. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns regarding parameter sensitivity and computational complexity, and consider expanding the validation with more diverse simulation scenarios. A more in-depth analysis of the real-world application would also strengthen the paper.
