This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), an online algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models in non-stationary environments. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics online, weighting recent data more heavily when changes in the underlying structure are detected using a Mahalanobis distance-based change point detection scheme. The algorithm then periodically relearns the graphical model structure using a constraint-based approach (PC algorithm). The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant gap in existing graphical model learning literature which largely assumes i.i.d. data.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Importance:** The problem addressed—online learning of graphical model structure—is genuinely important and under-explored. The assumption of stationarity is often violated in real-world applications, and the ability to adapt to changing structures is crucial.
* **Technical Soundness:** The algorithm appears technically sound. The updates to the sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, sample size) are presented clearly and correctly, building on established statistical principles. The integration of change point detection via Mahalanobis distance and the dynamic weighting scheme is a reasonable approach.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The simulations are well-designed and cover both parameter changes and structural changes. The comparison with a batch-mode learning algorithm (PC) demonstrates the advantage of the online approach when structure changes occur. The inclusion of a real-world application with US price index data adds further credibility.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithm is explained step-by-step, and the mathematical notation is clear. The discussion of convergence vs. diligence is insightful.
* **Consideration of Trade-offs:** The authors explicitly address the trade-off between convergence and diligence, acknowledging the inherent incompatibility of these properties and providing a parameter (γ) that controls this balance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The performance of LoSST is likely sensitive to the choice of several parameters (α, γ, T, ν). While the paper explores some of these (e.g., via simulation), a more systematic sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.  How robust is the performance to changes in these parameters?
* **Computational Cost:** The paper mentions the computational expense of graph structure learning. While the probabilistic relearning schedule helps, the cost of the PC algorithm (especially for higher-dimensional graphs) could be a bottleneck in real-time applications. Alternatives to PC, or approximations thereof, should be discussed. The authors mention this briefly, but more in-depth investigation is required.
* **Mahalanobis Distance Assumption:** The use of the Mahalanobis distance implicitly assumes Gaussian data. While the paper acknowledges applicability to non-Gaussian models, the change detection mechanism relies heavily on this metric. How would the algorithm perform with non-Gaussian data distributions? Are there alternative change detection methods that would be more robust to non-normality?
* **Limited Scope of Real-World Application:** The application to US price index data is a good start, but it is relatively simple.  Exploring more complex datasets with higher dimensionality and more frequent structural changes would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Pooling Method Justification:** The choice of Liptak's method for pooling p-values is not fully justified.  Are there other pooling methods that could be more appropriate, and why was Liptak's method selected?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant gap in the literature on graphical model learning.
* Presents a technically sound and well-motivated algorithm.
* Thoroughly evaluated through simulations and a real-world application.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Provides insights into the trade-offs between convergence and diligence.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Parameter sensitivity could limit practical applicability.
* Computational cost of structure learning might be prohibitive for some applications.
* Reliance on Gaussian assumptions in change detection.
* Real-world application could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The proposed LoSST algorithm offers a practical solution for learning in non-stationary environments. While there are some weaknesses, they are not critical and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding parameter sensitivity, computational cost, and the Gaussian assumption in the change detection mechanism. Specifically, I recommend the authors:

1.  Include a more detailed sensitivity analysis of the key parameters (α, γ, T, ν).
2.  Discuss alternative structure learning algorithms or approximations that could reduce computational cost.
3.  Acknowledge the limitations of the Mahalanobis distance-based change detection and explore potential alternatives for non-Gaussian data.
4.  Consider expanding the real-world application with more complex and high-dimensional datasets.



I rate this paper as a **weak accept**.
