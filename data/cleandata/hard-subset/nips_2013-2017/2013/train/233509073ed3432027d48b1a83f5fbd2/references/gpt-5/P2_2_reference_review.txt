Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, an online heuristic for learning and tracking time-varying graphical model structure, focusing on linear-Gaussian SEMs/DAGs. The key idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an effective sample size) in a single pass, where weights adapt to the recent “fit” of the data via pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances using Liptak’s method. When pooled evidence suggests a change, the algorithm (a) increases weights on recent data (shrinking the effective sample size), and (b) triggers probabilistic “relearning” of the structure using a constraint-based learner (PC). The authors analyze convergence versus diligence trade-offs as a function of hyperparameters (γ, T), provide synthetic experiments with abrupt changes in structure or parameters, and a real-data illustration on U.S. price indices, where LoSST appears to detect macroeconomic regime shifts.

Relation to prior work: The work is positioned between online changepoint detection (CPD), TD learning with exponential forgetting, and batch structure learning for graphical models. It contrasts with Talih & Hengartner (2005; undirected, batch, single-edge changes) and Siracusa & Fisher (2009; Bayesian time-varying directed edges, batch). While related work is discussed, the paper omits several relevant lines: dynamic Bayesian networks; sliding-window or exponentially weighted online structure learning; time-varying GGMs and fused/locally smoothed estimators (e.g., Kolar & Xing; Zhou et al.), which would sharpen the novelty claims and baselines.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The online updates for weighted means and covariances are correct and useful; the effective sample size update is a principled heuristic that achieves the intended behavior (recent data dominate post-change). The use of Hotelling’s T^2/Mahalanobis distance for Gaussian data is appropriate. However:
  - The p-value pooling via Liptak’s method implicitly assumes (approximate) independence of p-values; here they are sequential and correlated via shared sufficient statistics. The paper acknowledges intractability of the full likelihood but does not analyze the impact of dependence on false alarm/false negative rates. Some justification (e.g., robustness results or calibration experiments) would strengthen validity.
  - Convergence/diligence claims are only sketched; conditions on T, γ are intuitive but not fully proved, and the conjecture about time-indexed thresholds lacks empirical or theoretical support.
  - Using PC with weighted covariances is sensible for linear-Gaussian models (partial correlations from Σ suffice). But no discussion is provided of statistical tests under weighted/decayed samples (e.g., degrees of freedom, multiple testing), nor of regularization when effective sample size is small.
- Empirical support: Synthetic experiments are well designed to demonstrate the central claim: LoSST matches batch PC pre-change and adapts post-change where batch PC fails on mixtures. The probabilistic relearning experiment shows reasonable triggering behavior without excessive recomputation. However:
  - Baselines are limited. A strong and fair comparison would include (i) sliding-window PC or exponentially decayed covariance PC with tuned window/decay, (ii) CPD + reinitialization of PC, and (iii) relevant time-varying GGM/DBN baselines where applicable. As is, improvements over batch PC on mixtures are expected and do not fully establish state-of-the-art.
  - No ablations on T, γ, ν; no sensitivity analysis to outliers vs true change; no runtime/memory profiling.
  - Real-data case is illustrative but qualitative; no external ground truth or predictive validation is provided.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized; the derivations for online updates are easy to follow. The narrative around convergence vs diligence is helpful.
- Missing or underspecified elements: a concise algorithm box/pseudocode; explicit details on how conditional independence tests in PC leverage weighted Σ and effective sample size; guidance on hyperparameter selection; discussion of numerical stability/regularization (e.g., covariance inversion under small Sr). Some references are incomplete and a few footnote markers/notation are slightly distracting.

Originality
- The combination of (i) single-pass, weighted sufficient-statistics tracking, (ii) pooled p-value-based adaptive weighting, and (iii) probabilistic relearning of DAG structure appears novel for directed models. The framing of convergence vs diligence is insightful.
- Nonetheless, the idea of adapting to nonstationarity via exponential/adaptive weighting and relearning is related to existing streams (time-varying graphical models, DBNs, sliding-window CI testing). The paper would benefit from deeper differentiation and empirical comparison to these.

Significance
- The problem is important: online structure learning with changing environments is common and underexplored. If reliable, a memory- and computation-efficient approach is valuable.
- Current evidence supports utility over a naive batch baseline but does not yet establish superiority to standard streaming alternatives. The lack of calibration/false alarm analysis and limited baselines reduce immediate impact; still, the approach is promising and likely to stimulate follow-up work.

Pros
- Clear articulation of an important problem; practical single-pass updates with minimal memory.
- Simple, implementable mechanism for adaptive weighting and relearning triggers.
- Insightful discussion of convergence vs diligence trade-offs.
- Synthetic results demonstrate the intended behavior; real-data illustration is plausible.

Cons
- Incomplete theoretical guarantees; reliance on p-value pooling under dependence without calibration.
- Limited baselines; no comparison to sliding-window/decayed PC or CPD+relearn.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity and stability under small effective sample sizes not explored.
- Clarity gaps: algorithm pseudocode, testing details, parameter selection, numerical issues.
- Related work omits key streams (DBNs, time-varying GGMs).

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to sliding-window PC and CPD-triggered relearning; include ablations over T, γ, ν; report computational cost.
- Calibrate the pooled p-value statistic under dependence (simulation-based null distributions) and report false alarm/detection delays.
- Provide pseudocode and precise testing details (partial correlations, df adjustments, regularization).
- Expand related work to dynamic BNs and time-varying GGMs; discuss when LoSST is preferable.
- Consider evaluating predictive performance or intervention recovery as additional metrics.

Overall assessment
- Quality: moderate — sound core updates; heuristic detection; limited baselines and guarantees.
- Clarity: good, with some missing details.
- Originality: moderate to high in combination and framing.
- Significance: moderate — promising direction; needs stronger empirical and theoretical support to be definitive.

Verdict: Leaning borderline/weak accept if space allows for novel framing and practicality; otherwise weak reject pending stronger baselines, calibration, and clarity.
