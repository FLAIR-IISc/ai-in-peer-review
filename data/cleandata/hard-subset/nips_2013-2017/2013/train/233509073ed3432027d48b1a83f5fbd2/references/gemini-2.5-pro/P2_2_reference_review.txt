**Summary:**
This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models in an online fashion from streaming data. The core challenge addressed is that real-world data generating processes are often non-stationary, meaning the underlying graphical structure can change over time. Standard batch-mode structure learning algorithms fail in this setting as they assume i.i.d. data. LoSST tackles this by maintaining and updating weighted sufficient statistics (mean and covariance) for the data stream. The weight assigned to each new data point is dynamically adjusted based on how well it fits the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. To avoid overreacting to single outliers, the algorithm uses a pooled p-value of recent distances to detect persistent changes. When a change is detected, weights for new data are increased, effectively down-weighting past data and allowing the algorithm to adapt. The structure learning itself (using the PC algorithm in this implementation) is triggered probabilistically, with the probability of relearning increasing after a detected change. The paper provides a theoretical discussion of the trade-off between convergence (stability in a stationary environment) and diligence (responsiveness to change), showing how LoSST's parameters can navigate this trade-off. The algorithm's effectiveness is demonstrated through simulations on synthetic data with known changepoints and an application to US economic time-series data.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a critical and practical gap in the graphical model literature. The assumption of i.i.d. data is a major limitation of most structure learning algorithms, and this work proposes a complete, end-to-end solution for the online, non-stationary setting. The approach of combining weighted sufficient statistic tracking with a principled change-detection mechanism (pooled p-values) to dynamically re-learn structure is both novel and well-motivated.

2.  **Technical Depth:** The discussion of the "convergence vs. diligence" dilemma is a major strength. It elevates the paper beyond a purely heuristic proposal by providing a formal understanding of the algorithm's behavior under different parameterizations. The inclusion of proof sketches for the conditions under which LoSST is convergent or diligent adds significant technical substance and demonstrates a deep engagement with the theoretical challenges of online learning.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The simulation study is well-designed and convincing.
    *   It correctly establishes a baseline by showing LoSST performs comparably to a standard batch algorithm (PC) in a stationary setting, demonstrating that there is no performance cost for using the online method when it's not strictly necessary.
    *   It effectively demonstrates the superiority of LoSST over the batch method after a changepoint, both for structural and parametric changes.
    *   The authors are honest and insightful in their analysis, for instance, by highlighting and explaining the temporary increase in edge removal errors when only parameters change. This level of careful analysis inspires confidence in the results.
    *   The simulation with probabilistic relearning validates the efficiency of the proposed triggering mechanism.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The algorithm is developed logically, from tracking sufficient statistics to detecting change and finally triggering relearning. The mathematical formulations are presented clearly, and the high-level intuition is never lost. The real-world application on US price index data serves as a compelling and interpretable example of the algorithm's utility.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm's performance depends on several key hyperparameters, notably the p-value threshold `T`, the maximum weight increase factor `γ`, and the relearning frequency parameter `ν`. The authors state that the chosen values (`T=0.05`, `γ=3`) were determined through "unsystematic investigation." While the results are good, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more systematic sensitivity analysis. How robust is the algorithm to different choices of these parameters? Understanding this would be crucial for practitioners wishing to apply LoSST to new domains.

2.  **Limited Comparative Analysis:** The primary baseline is a batch-mode PC algorithm. While this effectively demonstrates the *need* for a change-aware algorithm, it doesn't compare LoSST to other plausible online strategies. For example, one could combine a generic changepoint detection (CPD) algorithm with a batch learner that is run on data segments identified by the CPD. The authors argue against CPD in the related work, but an empirical comparison would make their case more compelling.

3.  **Dependence on the Base Learner:** LoSST is a wrapper that relies on an underlying structure learning algorithm (PC in this case). The performance is therefore tied to the strengths and weaknesses of that base learner (e.g., PC's assumptions of faithfulness and no latent confounders). While the authors acknowledge that other learners could be used, a brief discussion of how the choice of learner might interact with the tracking mechanism would be beneficial. For instance, would a score-based learner require a different tracking approach? (The authors rightly point this out as future work, but it is a current limitation).

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant and timely contribution to the field. It presents a novel, well-motivated, and thoroughly evaluated algorithm for a challenging and important problem. The theoretical analysis of the convergence/diligence trade-off is particularly impressive and adds a layer of depth not often seen in papers proposing heuristic methods. The weaknesses, primarily related to hyperparameter tuning and the scope of the comparison, are minor in the context of the overall contribution and represent clear avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws. The paper is well-written, the results are convincing, and the ideas are likely to be built upon by other researchers. I recommend acceptance.
