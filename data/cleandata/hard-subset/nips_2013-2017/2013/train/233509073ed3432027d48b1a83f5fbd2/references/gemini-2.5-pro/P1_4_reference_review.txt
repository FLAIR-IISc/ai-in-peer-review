### **Summary**

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel online algorithm for learning graphical model structure from non-stationary data streams. The core problem it addresses is that standard structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data, which fails in dynamic environments where the underlying data-generating process changes over time. The LoSST algorithm works by tracking weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an "effective" sample size) in a single pass. The key innovation is a dynamic weighting scheme where each new data point's influence is determined by how well it fits the current model. This "fit" is measured by the Mahalanobis distance, and to ensure robustness against single outliers, the algorithm uses a weighted pooled p-value (via Liptak's method) over a sequence of data points to detect structural change. When a change is detected (i.e., the data stream becomes unlikely under the current model), the algorithm increases the weights of recent data points, effectively down-weighting the past and adapting to the new regime. The graphical structure is then relearned probabilistically, with the relearning frequency increasing after a detected change. The paper also provides a valuable theoretical discussion on the trade-off between "convergence" (stability on a fixed structure) and "diligence" (rapid adaptation to change), showing how LoSST's parameters can be set to favor one or the other. The algorithm is evaluated on synthetic data with both structure and parameter changes, as well as on a real-world macroeconomic dataset, demonstrating its ability to track changes effectively while performing on par with batch methods in stationary periods.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The core idea of using the Mahalanobis distance and pooled p-values to modulate the learning rate (via data point weights) is clever, well-motivated, and addresses the need to be robust to isolated outliers while remaining sensitive to persistent change. The experimental evaluation is a significant strength, covering different types of change (structure vs. parameter), analyzing the probabilistic relearning mechanism, and validating the approach on a real-world problem.

However, there are a few weaknesses:

1.  **Weak Baseline Comparison:** The experiments compare LoSST only against a standard batch PC algorithm run on the entire dataset. This is a weak baseline, as it is not designed for non-stationary data. A more informative comparison would include other plausible approaches, such as a sliding-window-based learner (i.e., running PC on the last *k* data points) or a two-stage approach combining an existing online changepoint detection algorithm with a batch learner. Without these comparisons, the magnitude of LoSST's contribution over simpler alternatives is unclear.
2.  **Heuristic Justification:** The update rule for the effective sample size (Eq. 3) is presented as a heuristic designed to satisfy two constraints. The justification is brief and not entirely convincing. A more thorough explanation or ablation study showing the impact of this specific formula would strengthen the paper.
3.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The key parameters (`T`, `γ`, `ν`) are chosen based on "unsystematic investigation." The algorithm's performance likely depends heavily on these settings. A sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with these parameters would significantly improve the work's reproducibility and practical utility.
4.  **Proof Sketches:** The proofs for convergence and diligence in Footnotes 4 and 5 are too brief to be properly verified. While space is limited, these claims are central to the paper's theoretical contribution and would be better supported by clearer arguments or a reference to an appendix.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the algorithm is explained logically, and the experiments are easy to understand. The high-level intuition behind each component of the algorithm is conveyed effectively. The use of figures to illustrate the experimental results is excellent.

A minor suggestion for improvement would be to provide more detail on the derivation of the covariance update (Eq. 2) and the effective sample size update (Eq. 3), perhaps in an appendix, for readers who wish to fully reproduce the work.

#### **Originality**

The paper is highly original. While online learning and graphical model learning are established fields, the problem of *online structure learning* in non-stationary environments is relatively under-explored. This work presents a novel and principled approach that elegantly combines ideas from online learning (weighted updates), statistics (Mahalanobis distance, p-value pooling), and graphical models. It clearly distinguishes itself from prior work on time-varying graphs (e.g., Talih & Hengartner, 2005; Siracusa & Fisher III, 2009), which primarily operate in batch mode. The explicit framing and analysis of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off is also a novel contribution in this context.

#### **Significance**

The work is highly significant. The assumption of i.i.d. data is a major limitation for applying graphical models to many real-world domains, such as finance, climate science, and sensor networks, where system dynamics evolve over time. This paper provides a practical, efficient, and effective algorithm to address this limitation. The LoSST algorithm could be a valuable tool for practitioners and a strong foundation for future research in this area. It advances the state of the art by providing the first, to my knowledge, general-purpose online algorithm for graphical model structure learning that can handle unknown, abrupt, or gradual changes in the generating process.

### **Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and practical problem at the intersection of online learning and graphical models.
*   The proposed method (LoSST) is novel, well-motivated, and technically sound.
*   The theoretical discussion of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off adds depth and insight.
*   The empirical evaluation is thorough and demonstrates the algorithm's effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   The paper is clear, well-written, and likely to inspire follow-up work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental comparison is limited to a weak baseline, which may overstate the performance gains.
*   Some key design choices (e.g., the effective sample size update rule) are heuristic and lack rigorous justification.
*   The lack of a parameter sensitivity analysis makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's robustness.

### **Conclusion and Suggestions**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to an important problem. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance. The authors should, however, consider the following suggestions for the final version:

1.  Acknowledge the limitations of the baseline comparison and discuss how LoSST might compare to stronger baselines like a sliding-window approach.
2.  Provide a more detailed justification for the effective sample size update rule (Eq. 3).
3.  If space permits, include a brief discussion on parameter sensitivity or guidance for practitioners on how to set `T`, `γ`, and `ν`.
4.  Consider moving the proof sketches to an appendix and expanding them for clarity.
