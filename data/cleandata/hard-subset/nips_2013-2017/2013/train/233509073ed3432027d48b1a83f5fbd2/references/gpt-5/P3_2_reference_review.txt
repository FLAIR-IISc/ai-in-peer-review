Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical structure in linear-Gaussian SEMs (DAGs). It maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) with per-datum weights ar that adapt to model misfit. Misfit is measured via Mahalanobis distance and pooled over time using Liptak’s combination of p-values, yielding a pooled statistic ρr that both modulates the weights (thus forgetting past data after changes) and triggers probabilistic graph relearning (via PC). The authors analyze parameter regimes that trade off convergence versus diligence, provide simulations comparing LoSST to batch PC under structural and parametric changes, and a brief case study on US price index volatility.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself between online changepoint detection (CPD) methods (e.g., Adams & MacKay) and batch structure learning (PC/score-based), aiming for single-pass online operation without storing long histories. It contrasts with time-indexed structure learning that runs offline (e.g., Siracusa & Fisher), and cites TD methods for gradual adaptation. This fills a practical gap: online tracking of DAG structure under local stationarity. There is additional relevant literature on time-varying graphical models and dynamic network tracking (e.g., Kolar, Song, Xing, AISTATS 2010; kernel or EWMA/CUSUM-based covariance change detection), which should be discussed to sharpen the novelty and baselines.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation: locally stationary SEMs with both structural and parametric change; single-pass online constraints.
- Simple, implementable update rules for means/covariances with adaptive weighting; intuitive use of Mahalanobis distances and pooled evidence to detect sustained misfit.
- Thoughtful discussion of convergence vs diligence, with parameter regimes and qualitative proofs.
- Simulations show LoSST matches batch PC pre-change and adapts post-change, and that probabilistic retraining can greatly reduce compute without large error increases.
- Case study illustrates qualitative sensitivity to known macro phenomena.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
Quality
- Statistical calibration. The pooled p-value ρr relies on Hotelling’s T^2 p-values computed from a covariance estimated from the same (weighted) data used to generate the distances; under adaptive weighting, the null distribution of pr is generally not Uniform(0,1), and the tests across time are highly dependent. Liptak’s method assumes independence (or at least known dependence). This can inflate false alarms or damp detection. A principled alternative is a prequential likelihood or generalized likelihood ratio (GLR) statistic for Gaussian mean/covariance change with exponential forgetting, or an EWMA/CUSUM on log-likelihood residuals; bootstrap calibration or permutation-based thresholds could also help.
- Effective sample size. The update Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1)Sr + 1 is heuristic and does not equal the standard effective sample size for weighted estimators. Since you already track br = Σak and τr = Σa^2k, Kish’s effective sample size n_eff = b^2r/τr is readily available and theoretically grounded. This affects both Hotelling T^2 dof and PC’s Fisher z tests.
- Positive-definiteness and numerical stability. With rapidly increasing weights and small n_eff, Cr can become ill-conditioned. The paper should specify regularization (e.g., ridge on the diagonal) and efficient updates for the inverse covariance (Sherman–Morrison-Woodbury) to compute Mahalanobis distances stably.
- Use of PC with weighted statistics. CI tests in PC assume i.i.d. Gaussian samples; here tests are run on weighted covariances and ambiguous sample sizes. It is unclear whether test thresholds and dof are adjusted appropriately for weights and adaptivity, and whether Type I error is controlled across frequent relearnings and multiple tests.
- Theoretical guarantees. The convergence/diligence sketches are insightful but informal; no bounds on detection delay, false alarm rate, or structural risk under local stationarity are provided. The diligence argument hinges on a bounded effective sample size derived from monotone ar growth, but the link from that to rapid recovery of correct structure via PC is not formalized.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written. However:
  - Precisely state how PC’s CI tests use Sr (or n_eff) and at what α-level; clarify whether partial correlations are computed from the weighted covariance and how conditioning-set size interacts with small n_eff.
  - Provide algorithm pseudocode summarizing state variables and per-step costs.
  - Distinguish clearly the roles of ρr in weight updates and in relearning probability, and describe initialization.
  - Typographical nits and footnotes interrupt the narrative; consider moving proofs to an appendix.

Originality
- The combination of adaptive weighted sufficient statistics with pooled misfit and online PC seems novel for DAG structure tracking. However, related dynamic network estimation and CPD-with-sufficient-statistics literatures offer principled alternatives that deserve a deeper comparison.

Significance
- Practical significance is promising: single-pass tracking with little degradation in stationary regimes. However, the lack of calibrated detection, limited baselines, and heuristic n_eff weaken the generality of the claims. A more rigorous evaluation could make this a valuable contribution.

Experimental evaluation
- Baselines: Comparing LoSST to batch PC on the full dataset after a change is unfavorable to PC. Include stronger baselines:
  - PC on a sliding window or exponentially weighted covariances.
  - CPD + restart PC, or BOCPD with a windowed re-estimation.
  - Time-varying GGM/SEM estimators (e.g., kernel- or fused-lasso-smoothed precision matrices) at each time.
- Report variability (error bars), not just means; include detection delay and false alarm metrics.
- Complexity: Report per-step time and memory, and average cost of relearning with ν; give wall-clock comparisons.
- Real data: Show example graphs over time and discuss interpretability; currently, only misfit signals are shown.

Suggestions
- Replace Sr with n_eff = b^2r/τr; recalibrate tests accordingly.
- Consider a prequential log-likelihood or GLR statistic with EWMA forgetting instead of pooled p-values; or calibrate ρr via parametric bootstrap.
- Add regularization and numerical details for maintaining Cr and its inverse.
- Evaluate against sliding-window and CPD+restart baselines; include ablations of T, γ, ν with calibration guidance.
- Discuss extension to non-Gaussian/discrete models and robustness to outliers (e.g., robust covariance/Mahalanobis).

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored online problem with a practical method.
- Simple, modular algorithm; easy to implement and extend.
- Empirical results show clear benefits under structure change with minimal stationary cost.

Cons for acceptance
- Heuristic statistical components (pooled p-values, effective sample size) lack calibration and may invalidate tests.
- Limited and arguably unfair baselines; no uncertainty quantification.
- Theoretical guarantees are qualitative; no bounds on detection or error rates.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and the engineering is neat, but the statistical underpinnings and evaluation need strengthening. With calibrated detection, principled n_eff, stronger baselines, and clearer PC testing under weights, this could be a solid contribution.
