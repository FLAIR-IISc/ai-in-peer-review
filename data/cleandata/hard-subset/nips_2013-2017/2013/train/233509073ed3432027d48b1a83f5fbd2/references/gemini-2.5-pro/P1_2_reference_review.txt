### Paper Summary

This paper introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel heuristic algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from streaming data where the underlying data-generating process may change over time. The core problem addressed is that standard structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data from a single, static distribution, making them unsuitable for online learning in non-stationary environments.

LoSST's approach is to maintain online, weighted estimates of the sufficient statistics required for structure learning (specifically, for SEMs, the mean, covariance, and effective sample size). The key innovation is the dynamic weighting scheme. Each new data point's "fit" to the current estimated model is measured by its Mahalanobis distance. To be robust to single outliers, the algorithm uses Liptak's method to pool p-values from a sequence of recent Mahalanobis distances. A low pooled p-value (indicating a poor fit and thus a likely structural change) triggers an increase in the weights for subsequent data points, effectively "forgetting" the past and focusing the learning on the new regime. To manage computational costs, the full structure learning algorithm (here, the PC algorithm) is not run at every step but is triggered probabilistically, with a higher probability of relearning after a change has been detected.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis of the algorithm's long-run properties, framing it in terms of the trade-off between "convergence" (stabilizing on a correct model in a stable environment) and "diligence" (quickly detecting and adapting to change). They show how LoSST's parameters can be set to favor one property over the other, or to strike a balance. The algorithm's performance is evaluated on synthetic data, showing that it successfully tracks changes in both graph structure and parameters, significantly outperforming a standard batch-mode PC algorithm applied to the full, non-stationary dataset. In stable periods, its performance is indistinguishable from the batch algorithm. Finally, an application to US price index data demonstrates its ability to detect known macroeconomic shifts.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is well-motivated and the mathematical derivations for the online updates of weighted sufficient statistics are sound. The use of Mahalanobis distance to measure fit is appropriate for the chosen model class (SEMs on continuous variables), and the incorporation of a pooled p-value to provide robustness against isolated outliers is a clever and well-justified design choice. The experimental evaluation is strong, featuring a clear baseline, multiple synthetic data scenarios (structure change vs. parameter-only change), and a thoughtful analysis of the results. The inclusion of a real-world case study adds further credibility. The discussion of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off is a particular strength, adding theoretical depth and demonstrating a nuanced understanding of the challenges in online learning. The authors are also honest about the limitations and heuristic nature of certain components (e.g., the weight update function, the probabilistic relearning schedule).

A minor weakness is the brevity of the proof sketches in the footnotes. While understandable for a conference paper, they are quite compressed and may be difficult for a reader to verify without significant effort. The reliance on several interacting hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) whose optimal setting is not thoroughly explored (the paper notes T=0.05 and γ=3 were found via "unsystematic investigation") introduces a heuristic element that could affect reproducibility and general applicability.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins with a clear motivation, situates the work within the relevant literature, and then logically builds up the components of the LoSST algorithm. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the update equations are presented clearly. The figures are informative and effectively support the paper's claims, especially the plots comparing LoSST's error rates to the batch-mode PC algorithm over time. The probabilistic relearning mechanism and its motivation are also explained well. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to understand the method's core mechanics.

**Originality:**
The paper is highly original. While individual components (online estimation, change-point detection, constraint-based structure learning) are well-known, their synthesis into a coherent framework for online *graphical model structure learning* is novel and addresses a significant gap in the literature. Most prior work on time-varying graphs operates in batch mode [14, 11] or focuses on tracking parameters rather than structure. LoSST provides a practical, real-time solution to a problem that has been largely unaddressed. The framing of the algorithm's behavior in terms of the formal diligence/convergence trade-off [5] is also a novel contribution in this specific context.

**Significance:**
The work is highly significant. The assumption of i.i.d. data is a major limitation of most machine learning algorithms, yet it is frequently violated in real-world applications such as finance, bioinformatics, and social network analysis, where systems evolve over time. This paper provides a principled and effective method for relaxing this assumption for graphical model discovery. The fact that LoSST incurs no performance penalty in stable environments makes it a compelling "drop-in" replacement for batch algorithms, even when one is merely uncertain about the stability of the data source. The ideas presented here are likely to be built upon by other researchers, potentially inspiring new work on online structure learning for different classes of models (e.g., discrete Bayes nets) or exploring more formal, less heuristic approaches to the weighting and relearning schedules. This work meaningfully advances the state of the art in learning from non-stationary data.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Addresses a Novel and Important Problem:** The paper tackles the challenging and highly relevant problem of online graphical model structure learning in non-stationary environments, a significant gap in the current literature.
2.  **Elegant and Effective Solution:** The LoSST algorithm is a clever synthesis of established statistical techniques into a coherent and practical framework. It is well-motivated and intuitive.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The algorithm is thoroughly evaluated on synthetic data, demonstrating its ability to outperform a standard batch-mode baseline after a change point while matching its performance in stable periods. The real-world data application adds further weight to its practical utility.
4.  **Theoretical Depth:** The discussion and formalization of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off provide a solid theoretical foundation for the algorithm's behavior and parameterization.
5.  **High Clarity and Quality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex topic accessible and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Heuristic Components:** The specific functional form for updating weights (Eq. 4) and the probabilistic relearning schedule are heuristic choices. The paper could be strengthened by a sensitivity analysis or a more formal justification for these specific designs.
2.  **Limited Scope of Models:** The current implementation and evaluation are restricted to Structural Equation Models (linear Gaussian models). The extension to other important model classes, such as those for discrete variables, is acknowledged as a significant challenge and left for future work.
3.  **Brief Proof Sketches:** The proofs for the diligence and convergence properties are provided only as very brief sketches in footnotes, which may not be sufficient for rigorous peer review.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance are far stronger. This is an excellent paper presenting a novel, significant, and well-executed piece of research.
