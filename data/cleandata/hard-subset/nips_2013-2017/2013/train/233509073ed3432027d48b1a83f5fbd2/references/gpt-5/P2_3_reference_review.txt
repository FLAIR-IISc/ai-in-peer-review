Summary
The paper addresses online structure learning for graphical models when the data-generating process is time-varying. It proposes LoSST, a heuristic algorithm for linear-Gaussian DAGs (SEMs) that (i) maintains weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size) in a single pass, (ii) adapts weights using a pooled p-value of Mahalanobis distances (via Lipták’s method), and (iii) triggers structure relearning (here via PC) with a probabilistic schedule that increases after suspected changes. The authors analyze a trade-off between “convergence” and “diligence,” give proofs or sketches for limiting cases, provide synthetic experiments where structure or parameters change midstream, and show a small real-world case on US price indices.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The incremental updates for weighted means/covariances are standard and appear correct; the O(p^2) memory footprint is attractive. The learning-rate adaptation via pooled p-values is plausible but statistically uncalibrated: (a) Hotelling’s T^2 distribution presumes i.i.d. samples and a correctly specified covariance; here the covariance is iteratively estimated with nonstationary, uneven weights, so p-values are approximate at best; (b) the independence assumption behind p-value pooling is violated because each Mahalanobis distance depends on the recursively estimated C; (c) the “effective sample size” update Sr+1 = (ar/ar+1) Sr + 1 is chosen to satisfy two desiderata, but it is not the standard ESS for weighted estimators (e.g., (∑w)^2/∑w^2), so downstream tests (e.g., Fisher-z in PC) may have miscalibrated degrees of freedom. The paper acknowledges heuristic choices but does not quantify their impact (e.g., coverage or false positive rates).
- Use within PC: For Gaussian SEMs, conditional independences can be tested from the covariance. However, with weighted, non-i.i.d. statistics and a nonstandard ESS, the nominal p-values used by PC are likely miscalibrated. The paper should clarify exactly which tests are used, how Sr enters, and to what extent Type I/II errors are affected; otherwise, it is hard to assess correctness of recovered structures beyond simulations.
- Theory: The “convergence vs. diligence” discussion is welcome. The diligence/convergence results rest on extreme settings (e.g., T > 1 so weights always inflate) and proof sketches; they illustrate the trade-off but do not characterize the practically relevant regime 0 < T < 1. No rates or finite-sample guarantees are provided. This is acceptable for a heuristic contribution but should be framed more cautiously.
- Experiments: Synthetic results are encouraging and support the intended behavior: LoSST matches batch PC under stationarity and adapts after changes. However, the baselines are weak: comparing to batch PC on all data favors LoSST after a change by design. Missing are sliding-window PC, exponential-forgetting baselines with fixed α, CPD+relearn, and time-varying graphical model estimators (e.g., dynamic GLasso or time-varying networks). An ablation showing the incremental value of pooled p-values versus constant forgetting would be particularly informative. Computational costs (wall-time, number of relearns) are only partially reported; timing/complexity would strengthen the case for online use. The CPI example is interesting but small; no external validation of detected shifts is attempted beyond a qualitative Great Moderation anecdote.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. Derivations are sketched sufficiently for an expert to rederive. That said:
- Provide pseudocode specifying all maintained state and the relearning trigger; some footnote markers are misplaced.
- Clearly state the conditional independence tests used by PC under weights and how Sr is mapped to degrees of freedom.
- Discuss numerical stability and how (C)^−1 is computed/regularized online.
- Hyperparameter guidance (γ, T, ν) is ad hoc; a sensitivity analysis or principled selection strategy would help.

Originality
The combination is novel in this context: online sufficient statistics for SEMs + adaptive forgetting driven by pooled p-values + probabilistic relearning coupled with a standard constraint-based learner. However, related literatures are under-cited: adaptive/variable forgetting in recursive least squares and adaptive filters, CUSUM/Page-Hinkley/EWMA change detection, dynamic Bayesian networks, and time-varying graphical models (e.g., dynamic graphical lasso, kernelized/TV-regularized precision estimation). Positioning relative to these would clarify the contribution.

Significance
Online structure learning under nonstationarity is important. If robustly demonstrated, LoSST could be a practical tool: single-pass, low memory, and compatible with existing learners. As presented, the impact is limited by (i) heuristic statistical calibration, (ii) limited comparative baselines, and (iii) lack of guidance on hyperparameters and compute. Strengthening these would considerably increase significance.

Pros
- Tackles an important, underexplored problem: online DAG structure learning under changing environments.
- Simple, memory-efficient updates; compatible with off-the-shelf structure learners.
- Clear intended behavior; synthetic results support adaptability; insightful discussion of convergence vs. diligence.

Cons
- Heuristic statistical machinery (pooled p-values, nonstandard ESS) without calibration; independence and distributional assumptions unlikely to hold.
- Missing strong baselines (sliding window, fixed EWMA, CPD+relearn, time-varying network estimators) and ablations.
- Unclear mapping of weighted statistics to PC’s testing; potential miscalibration not quantified.
- Limited real-data validation and no computational performance characterization.

Suggestions and questions
- Compare against sliding-window PC and a fixed-α EWMA baseline; add CPD+relearn and a time-varying graphical lasso baseline where appropriate.
- Provide an ablation isolating the gains from Lipták-pooled p-values versus simpler triggers (e.g., recent average MD).
- Clarify the CI testing under weights and report empirical Type I error control on stationary data.
- Consider using a principled ESS for weighted estimators and/or robustifying tests via permutation/bootstrap.
- Report compute time and number of relearns versus ν; discuss inverse updates and regularization.
- Expand related work to adaptive filtering, sequential change detection, and time-varying network estimation.

Overall assessment
Promising idea addressing a timely topic with a neat, implementable algorithm, but currently too heuristic and empirically under-validated against appropriate baselines. I lean toward rejection in its current form; with stronger comparisons, calibration, and clarity on statistical testing, it could be a solid contribution.
