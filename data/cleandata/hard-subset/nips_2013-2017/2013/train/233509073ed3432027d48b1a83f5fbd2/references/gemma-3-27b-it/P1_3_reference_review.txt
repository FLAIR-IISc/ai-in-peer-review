## Peer Review for "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of learning graphical model structure in a non-stationary environment, specifically when data arrives sequentially and the underlying generative model changes over time. Existing structure learning algorithms assume stationarity, and online learning methods lack the capability to adapt graphical model structure. The authors present LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a heuristic algorithm that dynamically tracks sufficient statistics and relearns the graphical model structure as needed. LoSST aims to achieve performance comparable to batch-mode learning when the structure is stable and to effectively adapt to changes when they occur, demonstrating a balance between convergence and diligence. The paper provides simulation results and a real-world application to US price index data, supporting the claims.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of graphical model structure learning. It acknowledges the split between Bayesian/score-based and constraint-based approaches, highlighting their reliance on stationary data.  The discussion of online changepoint detection (CPD) and Temporal Difference Learning (TDL) is relevant, explaining why these methods are insufficient for this particular task (CPD’s memory requirements and TDL’s suboptimality during rapid changes).  The authors also accurately point out the limitations of existing time-indexed graph structure learning approaches ([14, 11]) which operate in batch mode.  The comparison to Talih & Hengartner (2005) and Siracusa & Fisher III (2009) effectively emphasizes the novelty of their online, frequentist approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** Learning dynamic graphical models is crucial in many real-world applications where assumptions of stationarity are violated.
* **Novel approach:** LoSST provides a practical heuristic for adapting graphical models online, filling a gap in the existing literature.
* **Theoretical Considerations:** The discussion of convergence vs. diligence is valuable. Recognizing the inherent trade-off and providing parameters to control it adds nuance to the algorithm.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The simulation results are well-designed and clearly demonstrate the algorithm's ability to track structural changes and maintain performance during stable periods. The application to US price index data provides a compelling real-world example.
* **Clear and well-structured:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the algorithm’s details.
* **Practical Considerations:** LoSST builds upon standard algorithms like the PC algorithm, making it easier to implement and integrate into existing workflows.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Heuristic nature:** The algorithm relies on several heuristic choices (e.g., the Mahalanobis distance threshold, the weight update rule, the probabilistic relearning schedule). While these choices are motivated, a more rigorous analysis of their impact would be beneficial. 
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems sensitive to the parameters γ and T (particularly for the convergence/diligence trade-off). The paper acknowledges this, but a more systematic investigation of parameter tuning would be valuable.  The selection of T=0.05 and γ=3 appears somewhat arbitrary.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the paper highlights the online nature of the algorithm, the computational cost of relearning the graph structure (using the PC algorithm) could still be significant, especially for high-dimensional data. A discussion of scalability and potential optimizations would be useful.
* **Limited Scope of Simulations:** The simulations are focused on relatively simple scenarios with limited variable numbers.  Investigating performance with larger, more complex graphs would increase confidence in the algorithm's generalizability.
* **Lack of Comparison to Online CPD with Structural Learning:** The paper compares to offline methods. A comparison to online changepoint detection algorithms *combined* with a structure learning step after each detected change would be useful.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The algorithm is well-defined, and the simulation results support the claims. While the heuristic nature introduces some uncertainty, the authors provide sufficient justification for their choices. The paper presents a complete piece of work, although acknowledging the limitations.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The mathematical notation is appropriate, and the algorithm is described in sufficient detail. However, the explanation of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off could be further clarified.

**Originality:** The approach of dynamically tracking sufficient statistics and probabilistically relearning the graph structure is novel and represents a significant contribution to the field of dynamic graphical model learning. 

**Significance:** The problem addressed by the paper is important, and the proposed solution has the potential to be widely applicable in various domains. The results demonstrate a promising ability to track changing relationships in real-world data. 



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Fills an important gap in the literature by providing a practical algorithm for online graphical model structure learning.
* Demonstrates strong empirical performance in simulations and a real-world application.
* Addresses the crucial trade-off between convergence and diligence.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on several heuristic choices that could impact performance.
* The computational cost of relearning the graph structure may be a limitation.
* Limited scope of simulations.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and potentially significant contribution to the field of dynamic graphical model learning. While the heuristic nature of the algorithm and the limited scope of the simulations are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I suggest the authors address the weaknesses by:

1.  Providing a more detailed discussion of the parameter sensitivity and exploring alternative parameter tuning strategies.
2.  Discussing the computational complexity of the algorithm and potential optimizations.
3.  Consider adding simulations with more complex graphs and higher dimensionality.
4. Compare to online CPD methods coupled with periodic structural learning.



This work represents a valuable addition to the NIPS program and has the potential to stimulate further research in this important area.
