

# Peer Review: "Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure"

## Summary

This paper proposes the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST) algorithm for online learning of graphical model structures when the underlying structure may change over time. The authors identify a gap in the literature: existing graphical model structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data (batch mode), while change detection algorithms do not handle structure learning. LoSST addresses this by tracking weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances) online, using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values to detect structure changes, and dynamically relearning the graphical model when changes are detected. The paper demonstrates that LoSST performs as well as batch algorithms during stable periods and outperforms them when structures change, with simulations and a real-world application to US price index volatility.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear and well-motivated problem statement, addressing a genuine gap in the literature for online structure learning.
- The technical details of the algorithm are well-described with precise equations and update rules.
- The theoretical discussion of convergence vs. diligence (Section 3.1) is insightful and addresses an important methodological tension in online learning.
- The simulation results are thorough, covering multiple scenarios (structure change, parameter change, probabilistic relearning) with 500 runs per condition.
- The real-world application to US price index volatility demonstrates the algorithm's practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical claims require stronger justification. The paper states LoSST is "provably convergent" or "provably diligent" under certain conditions, but the proofs are sketchy (e.g., "Proof sketch" in footnote 4) and lack mathematical rigor. The convergence proof relies on a condition that may not be practically verifiable.
- The algorithm assumes multivariate Gaussian data (for Mahalanobis distance), which is a significant limitation. The paper doesn't address how the algorithm would perform with non-Gaussian data or discrete variables, despite acknowledging that the method could be extended to Bayesian networks (Section 6).
- The "probabilistic relearning" mechanism (Section 3.1) is ad-hoc. The parameter ν is chosen "unsystematically" (T=0.05, γ=3), with no sensitivity analysis or principled justification for these choices. The noisy-OR gate approach for relearning probability lacks theoretical grounding.
- The comparison with related work is insufficient. While the paper cites Talih & Hengartner (2005) and Siracusa & Fisher III (2009), it doesn't provide a fair comparison when these methods are adapted to online learning. For instance, how would Siracusa & Fisher's Bayesian approach perform if run online with similar parameter settings?
- The real-world application (Section 5) is limited to 6 variables and lacks economic interpretation of the detected changes beyond "Great Moderation." A more detailed analysis of why the detected changes make sense in the context of US economic history would strengthen this section.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to algorithm to results.
- Key equations are well-explained and the algorithm is described step-by-step.
- The simulation results are presented clearly with informative figures.

**Weaknesses:**
- The concept of "pooled p-value" (Section 3) is introduced without sufficient explanation. The paper should clarify why Liptak's method is appropriate here and how it differs from other pooling methods.
- The distinction between "convergence" and "diligence" is theoretically important but not sufficiently elaborated for readers unfamiliar with the literature (e.g., Kummerfeld & Danks' work referenced in [5]).
- The parameter choices (T=0.05, γ=3) are not justified. The paper states these were chosen via "unsystematic investigation" without explaining why these particular values work better than others.
- The paper could better clarify the relationship between the effective sample size (Section 3) and the actual sample size, particularly how the effective sample size relates to the algorithm's ability to detect changes.

## Originality

**Strengths:**
- The core problem (online graphical model structure learning) is well-identified as a gap in the literature.
- The approach of using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values to detect structure changes is novel in this context.

**Weaknesses:**
- The weighted sufficient statistics approach is conceptually similar to TD(0) learning (Section 2), which has been used in other online learning contexts. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how this application differs from previous work.
- The paper doesn't adequately compare with other online structure learning methods. For example, the recent work by [Desobry et al., 2005] on "online kernel change detection" could potentially be adapted for structure learning, but this isn't discussed.
- The use of the PC algorithm as the structure learning component is standard, not novel. While the paper acknowledges this, it doesn't discuss how the algorithm would perform with other structure learning methods (e.g., score-based methods).
- The paper's claim that "none are capable of general, graphical model structure learning" (Section 1) is overstated. The work by Siracusa & Fisher III (2009) could potentially be adapted for online use, though the paper acknowledges this limitation.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The problem addressed is important for real-world applications where data structures change over time (e.g., financial markets, social networks).
- The algorithm provides a practical solution for online structure learning that can be applied to large datasets without requiring batch processing.
- The paper's theoretical discussion of convergence vs. diligence provides valuable insights for online learning researchers.

**Weaknesses:**
- The significance is limited by the algorithm's assumption of "slowly changing" structures. The paper acknowledges this limitation (Section 6), but doesn't provide guidance on how to handle rapidly changing structures, which is a common real-world scenario.
- The real-world application is limited in scope (6 variables) and doesn't convincingly demonstrate the algorithm's superiority over batch methods in a meaningful economic context.
- The paper doesn't provide a clear roadmap for scaling the method to larger, more complex datasets. The computational cost of the PC algorithm for large graphs is not addressed.
- The paper doesn't discuss how LoSST would perform in high-dimensional settings (e.g., thousands of variables), which is increasingly common in modern applications.

## Recommendation

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Addresses an important and underexplored problem in graphical models
- Provides a clear, well-motivated algorithm with thorough simulations
- The theoretical discussion of convergence vs. diligence is valuable
- Practical application to real-world data demonstrates utility

**Con Against Acceptance:**
- Theoretical claims lack sufficient rigor and justification
- Assumptions about data distribution (multivariate Gaussian) are restrictive
- Parameter choices are not well-justified
- Comparison with related work is insufficient
- Real-world application lacks depth and economic interpretation

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on graphical models by addressing the important problem of online structure learning in non-stationary environments. The LoSST algorithm is well-designed and the simulation results are promising. However, the paper has significant weaknesses in theoretical grounding, practical applicability, and comparison with related work that need to be addressed before it can be considered a strong contribution to NeurIPS.

The paper would benefit from:
1. More rigorous theoretical analysis with formal proofs
2. A thorough comparison with related work adapted to online learning
3. A sensitivity analysis of key parameters (T, γ, ν)
4. Discussion of limitations with non-Gaussian data
5. A more detailed real-world analysis with economic interpretation

The paper is on the borderline for acceptance, but with significant revisions, it could be a strong contribution. As it stands, it's a solid technical contribution but lacks the theoretical depth and thoroughness expected at NeurIPS.

## Final Recommendation

**Major Revision Required**

This paper has promising ideas but requires substantial revisions to strengthen its theoretical foundation, improve the comparison with related work, and better justify key design choices. The current version does not fully demonstrate the novelty or significance of the approach in a way that meets NeurIPS standards. With careful attention to the weaknesses identified above, the paper could be significantly improved.
