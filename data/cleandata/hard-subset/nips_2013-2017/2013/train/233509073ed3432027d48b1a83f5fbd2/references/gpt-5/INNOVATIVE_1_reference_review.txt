Summary and context
This paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic online algorithm for tracking time-varying DAG/SEM structure. The core idea is to maintain exponentially reweightable sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective” sample size), where the per-datapoint weight ar adapts to departures from the current model as measured by Mahalanobis distance. To avoid storing long histories, the authors pool per-step p-values using Liptak’s method to form a running “fit” statistic ρr, which simultaneously (i) adjusts the forgetting factor via ar+1 = f(ρr) and (ii) modulates a probabilistic graph-relearning schedule. Structure is relearned using a constraint-based method (PC) driven only by the updated sufficient statistics. The paper sketches conditions under which LoSST is convergent, diligent, or in-between, and presents synthetic experiments with both structural and parametric shifts, plus a CPI volatility case study that aligns with the Great Moderation.

Relative to prior work: batch score- and constraint-based structure learning assumes i.i.d. data; online Bayesian updating also assumes stationarity; classical CPD stores long histories and presumes abrupt changes; prior time-varying graph works (Talih & Hengartner; Siracusa & Fisher) are batch and focus on different graph families or Bayesian posterior over edges. The contribution here is an online, single-pass mechanism that couples adaptive weighting of sufficient statistics with opportunistic relearning of causal structure, tailored to linear-Gaussian SEMs but conceptually extensible.

Strengths
- Novel direction: A simple, general recipe to retrofit online structure learning onto standard batch learners by exposing them to adaptively weighted sufficient statistics. The pooled p-value trigger is creative and memory-light.
- Practicality: Single pass, no need to buffer long histories; the same machinery works in the stationary case without penalty.
- Insightful discussion of convergence vs diligence trade-offs; parameterization offers a knob between robustness and reactivity.
- Empirical evidence: Synthetic studies clearly show advantages over batch PC after changepoints; the probabilistic relearning ablation is sensible. The CPI case study is plausible and interpretable.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Heuristic scaffolding: The effective sample size update (Eq. 3) is ad hoc; a derivation from weighted MLE or survey-sampling notions of effective n would strengthen the method. Likewise, using Hotelling’s T^2 with a time-varying, weighted covariance and small Sr is only approximate; calibration and small-sample behavior are not assessed.
- Dependence in pooled p-values: Liptak’s method assumes independence (or at least weak dependence). Here, successive p-values are highly dependent because both data and the estimated covariance evolve. Please assess calibration (e.g., via PIT histograms) and robustness to dependence; consider alternative change statistics (e.g., GLR/CUSUM/EWMA on Mahalanobis distance) or block-based pooling.
- CI testing details: PC requires conditional independence tests. Using weighted covariances and Sr for degrees of freedom is implied but not specified. Please clarify the test statistic, df, and how conditioning sets are handled with weighted statistics. In high dimensions or low Sr, covariance inversion and partial correlations can be unstable; shrinkage (e.g., Ledoit–Wolf) or ridge regularization would likely help.
- Baselines: Comparison is only to batch PC. Stronger baselines include sliding-window PC, exponentially weighted PC, Bayesian online CPD followed by local relearning, and time-varying GGM approaches adapted to DAGs. Even a simple fixed-size window baseline would contextualize gains.
- Theory: Convergence/diligence results are sketchy and hinge on thresholds (T, γ) without end-to-end guarantees on false alarms or detection delay. A finite-sample bound on detection vs false alarm under simple alternatives would substantially improve the story.
- Clarity issues: Minor inconsistencies (e.g., p=V vs p=N for T^2; “graph2” footnote artifact). Some notation (η, τ) appears abruptly. A brief algorithm box with all updates and the relearning policy would aid reproducibility.

Quality
Methodologically sound as a heuristic framework; empirical evidence matches claims in the evaluated regimes. However, some statistical underpinnings (p-value calibration, effective n, CI tests) need clearer justification and/or robustness checks.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. Adding a consolidated pseudo-code description, clarifying CI testing with weighted stats, and fixing typos would help. Reporting computational costs and wall-clock comparisons would also be useful.

Originality
High. The coupling of pooled fit-statistics with adaptive forgetting and probabilistic relearning for DAG structure is novel and bold. The work goes beyond prior batch time-varying graph papers and standard CPD.

Significance
High potential impact. A lightweight, online structure learner for changing environments is valuable across many application areas, and the framework is likely to inspire follow-up work (e.g., alternative change metrics, regularized CI tests, extensions to discrete BNs and undirected models).

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a fresh, practical approach to an important, under-explored problem (online DAG structure learning).
- Simple, adaptable mechanism likely to be picked up and extended by the community.
- Solid synthetic evidence and a compelling real-data illustration.
- Honest discussion of trade-offs and limitations.

Cons against acceptance
- Heuristic components lack calibration/theoretical support; CI testing details under weighting are underspecified.
- Missing baselines (sliding window, EWMA, CPD+batch) limit external validity of gains.
- Dependence issues in pooled p-values unaddressed.

Questions for authors
- How exactly are conditional independence tests performed with weighted covariances and small Sr? Any regularization?
- How sensitive are results to ν, T, γ, and to covariance shrinkage?
- Can you report false alarm/detection rates and computational costs vs sliding-window PC?
- Did you assess calibration of ρr under stationarity (e.g., uniformity of pooled p-values)?

Recommendation
Accept. Despite heuristic elements and missing baselines, the paper advances a novel and promising direction with clear practical value. With minor clarifications and additional comparisons in the camera-ready, it would make a strong NeurIPS contribution. Confidence: medium.
