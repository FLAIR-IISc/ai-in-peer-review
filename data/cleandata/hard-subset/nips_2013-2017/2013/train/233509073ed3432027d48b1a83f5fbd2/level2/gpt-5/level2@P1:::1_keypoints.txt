- Proposes LoSST: an online heuristic to learn and track time-varying DAG structure under a locally stationary, linear-Gaussian SEM setting.  
- Core idea: single-pass maintenance of weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariance, effective sample size) with weights adapting to recent misfit.  
- Misfit quantified by Mahalanobis distances → p-values → pooled via Liptak’s method to control forgetting and probabilistic relearning (PC).  
- The pooled p-value controls both upweighting of new data (forgetting) and the probability of relearning the graph structure.  
- Paper analyzes a convergence vs. diligence trade-off via parameters T (threshold) and γ (max weight increase) and characterizes regimes.  
- Evaluated on synthetic changepoint/parameter-drift experiments and a small real dataset (US price index volatilities).  
- Positioned relative to batch structure learners, online prediction methods, changepoint detection, and time-varying undirected graphical models; claims novelty for online directed-graph learning with low memory.  

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical problem: online DAG structure learning with limited memory.  
- Elegant, implementable adaptive-forgetting mechanism using pooled p-values and TD-like updates for moments.  
- Clear discussion and intuitive interpretation of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off.  
- Single-pass O(V^2) memory footprint and compatibility with any covariance-based constraint learner.  
- Simulations show parity with batch PC during stationarity and improved adaptation after changes, with reduced computation via probabilistic relearning.  
- Real-data case study highlights robustness to isolated outliers by pooling mildly unlikely points.  

Weaknesses / Suggestions
- Statistical validity of pooled p-values is unclear: p-values are dependent, covariance is adaptively estimated, and null distribution distortion is not addressed.  
- The mapping from pooled p-value to weight and relearning probability is ad hoc and lacks justification or alternatives; only one parameter setting is explored.  
- Insufficient sensitivity analyses and ablations (T, γ, ν; fixed α forgetting vs. adaptive pooling).  
- Limited baselines: lacks sliding-window PC, fixed exponential forgetting, CPD+PC, and time-varying undirected baselines with orientation heuristics.  
- Scalability untested beyond 20 variables; need runtimes, memory vs. V, numerical stability, and use of inverse-update tricks (e.g., Sherman–Morrison).  
- Real-data evaluation is qualitative; add quantitative metrics (rolling predictive log-likelihood, stability) and simple baselines.  
- Algorithmic clarity could improve with a concise pseudocode box and clearer definition/motivation of "effective sample size" (Sr).  
- Convergence/diligence claims would benefit from formal theorem statements and clear assumptions in an appendix.  
- Related time-varying graphical literature (kernel/fused-lasso GGMs) should be discussed more fully to position differences.  

Questions for the authors
- How robust are pooled p-values to dependence among Mahalanobis distances; any empirical false-alarm calibration under stationarity?  
- How are PC independence tests adapted when Sr is small; is Cr regularized (e.g., ridge) before partial correlations?  
- Can T, γ, ν be chosen automatically via calibration (e.g., target average run length under stationarity)?  
- How does LoSST handle gradual drift versus abrupt changes; any controlled experiments on drift rates?  
- Can you compare to a sliding-window PC baseline with matched computational budget?  
- Any preliminary results or observations for online discrete Bayesian network structure learning?  

Pros for acceptance
- Tackles an under-explored, practical problem with a simple, implementable method and low memory requirements.  
- Demonstrates solid synthetic evidence of improved adaptation after changes without harming stationary performance.  
- Thoughtful analysis of convergence vs. diligence trade-offs.  

Cons for acceptance
- Core heuristic relies on unvalidated statistical assumptions in the change/misfit driver.  
- Limited baselines, small-scale experiments, and sparse sensitivity/parameter-selection analysis.  
- Real-data evaluation lacks quantitative rigor and ground truth.  

Overall
- Promising, novel direction with neat ideas but currently borderline due to heuristic assumptions, limited baselines, and scalability/sensitivity gaps.
