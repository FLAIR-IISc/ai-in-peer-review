Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes LoSST, an online heuristic for learning and tracking time-varying graphical structure under a locally stationary assumption. The setting is linear-Gaussian SEMs (DAGs with linear equations and Gaussian noise). The core idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariance matrix, effective sample size) in a single pass, where weights adapt to recent evidence of misfit between incoming data and the current estimated covariance. Misfit is quantified via Mahalanobis distances, transformed into p-values and combined over time using Liptak’s method for weighted pooling. The pooled p-value controls both (i) the rate at which new data are upweighted (increasing effective forgetting) and (ii) the probability of relearning the graph structure via a standard constraint-based method (PC). The paper analyzes the tension between “convergence” and “diligence” via parameters T (threshold) and γ (maximal increase in weight), gives conditions under which LoSST is convergent, diligent, or in-between, and evaluates the algorithm on synthetic data with changepoints in structure and/or parameters and on a small real dataset of US price index volatilities.

The work is positioned relative to batch structure learners (score-based and constraint-based), online prediction methods such as TD(0), and changepoint detection (e.g., Adams & MacKay). It also relates to time-varying graphical models, where undirected graphs are estimated with kernels or sparsity-inducing penalties (e.g., Zhou et al., 2010; Kolar et al., 2010–2012), and to dynamic Bayesian networks. Unlike those, LoSST targets directed acyclic structure learning in real time with minimal memory, and aims to be robust both when the structure is stable and when it changes.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of a practically important problem: online structure learning under changing environments without storing large windows of data.
- Elegant, implementable mechanism to adaptively forget the past using pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances; connections to TD updates for means/covariances are insightful.
- Useful discussion of convergence vs. diligence trade-offs, with parameter regimes characterized and intuitive interpretation.
- Single-pass maintenance of sufficient statistics allows memory O(V^2) and streaming operation; can be coupled with any constraint-based learner that uses covariances.
- Simulation results convincingly show parity with batch PC in stationary periods and substantial gains when structure changes; the probabilistic relearning mechanism appears to reduce computation without hurting accuracy.
- Real data case study is illustrative and highlights why pooling over multiple “mildly unlikely” points is preferable to reacting to isolated outliers.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Statistical validity of the pooled p-value driver is unclear. The method computes p-values from Mahalanobis distances using Hotelling’s T^2 with “effective sample size” Sr and a covariance that is itself adaptively estimated. The distributional assumptions (including independence of p-values implicit in Liptak’s method) are not justified; p-values will be dependent and their null distribution can be distorted by parameter uncertainty and adaptive weighting. A sensitivity analysis or calibration experiment (false alarm rate under known stationarity) would strengthen the claim.
- The transform from pooled p-value to weight ar+1 (Eq. 4) and the relearning probability update are ad hoc. Only one setting (T=0.05, γ=3, ν=.005) is explored. Please provide systematic sensitivity analyses over T, γ, ν (e.g., detection delay/false alarm tradeoffs; structural Hamming distance vs. parameters), and an ablation (e.g., fixed α forgetting vs. adaptive pooling) to isolate the contribution of each component.
- Baselines are limited. The main comparison is to batch PC run on all data (which is known to perform poorly on mixtures). More informative baselines include: sliding-window PC (vary window W), exponential forgetting with fixed α, CPD (Bayesian or frequentist) coupled with resets of PC, and time-varying undirected methods (kernel/fused-lasso precision tracking) followed by DAG orientation heuristics. Even small-scale comparisons would help quantify gains attributable to adaptive weighting vs. generic forgetting or segmentation.
- Scalability is not established. Experiments go up to 20 variables. PC can be costly; repeated relearning plus repeated matrix inversions for Mahalanobis distance may be expensive. Please report runtime and memory vs. V and average degree, and discuss numerical stability (e.g., shrinkage/regularization when effective sample size is small and Cr is ill-conditioned). Are rank-one inverse updates (e.g., Sherman–Morrison) used?
- Real data analysis is qualitative. Without ground truth, add quantitative measures (predictive log-likelihood on a rolling holdout, or stability/consistency metrics across runs) and compare to simple baselines (e.g., rolling window).

Clarity
- The algorithmic description is readable, but a concise pseudo-code box would help readers implement LoSST (state maintained, per-step operations, and triggering of PC).
- Define “effective sample size” more rigorously and motivate Eq. 3 beyond the two constraints. Discuss its interpretation when weights grow rapidly (small Sr) and implications for PC’s conditional independence tests.
- The convergence/diligence statements are interesting; formal theorem statements with assumptions (even in an appendix) would improve rigor.

Originality
- The specific combination—adaptive pooling of misfit statistics to control forgetting and trigger online DAG structure relearning—appears novel. However, related time-varying graphical model literature (e.g., Zhou, Lafferty & Wasserman, 2010; Kolar, Song & Xing, 2010–2012) should be discussed to better position LoSST relative to kernel/fused-lasso approaches for GGMs, highlighting differences (directed vs. undirected; online vs. batch; memory constraints).

Significance
- Potentially useful for practitioners who need online structure tracking with limited memory and no strong prior on change dynamics. However, current evidence is limited to small graphs and a single real dataset. Strengthening baselines, providing parameter selection guidance, and demonstrating scalability would increase impact.

Questions for the authors
1) How robust are the pooled p-values to dependence among Mahalanobis distances? Did you empirically assess false positive rates under strict stationarity?
2) How are independence tests in PC adapted when Sr is small? Do you regularize Cr (e.g., ridge) before computing partial correlations?
3) Can parameters T, γ, ν be chosen automatically via a calibration objective (e.g., target average run length under stationarity)?
4) How does LoSST behave under gradual drift vs. abrupt changes? A controlled experiment varying drift rates would be informative.
5) Could you compare to a sliding-window PC baseline with matched computational budget?
6) Any observations on discrete BN structure learning online, beyond noting difficulty? Even a small-scale demonstration would broaden scope.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and under-explored problem (online DAG structure tracking).
- Simple, implementable method with a clear intuition and small memory footprint.
- Solid synthetic evidence that it outperforms batch learners after changes without sacrificing stationary performance.
- Thoughtful discussion of convergence vs. diligence.

Cons for acceptance
- Heuristic core with unvalidated statistical assumptions in the change/misfit driver.
- Limited baselines and small-scale experiments; unclear scalability.
- Sparse sensitivity analysis and parameter selection guidance.
- Real-data evaluation is qualitative.

Overall, this is a promising direction with a neat idea and clear potential. With stronger empirical comparisons, sensitivity analyses, and clarification of statistical assumptions, it could make a solid contribution. In its current form I lean toward borderline reject, but I encourage the authors to revise along the lines above.
