This paper introduces LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm for tracking changes in graphical model structure in a dynamic, real-time manner. The core idea is to maintain sufficient statistics online and dynamically relearn the graphical model structure when the data no longer fit the current model. The authors provide simulation results demonstrating that LoSST performs comparably to batch-mode learning when the generating structure is stable, and significantly better when it is locally stationary. They also present a compelling application to US price index volatility.

The paper is well-motivated. The problem of learning in non-stationary environments is crucial for many real-world applications, and the assumption of i.i.d. data is often violated. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches and position LoSST as filling a critical gap. The algorithm itself is presented in a clear and detailed manner, and the discussion of convergence vs. diligence is particularly insightful. The connection to TDL and changepoint detection algorithms in the related work is appropriate and helps frame the contribution.  The use of Mahalanobis distance and pooled p-values to detect changes is a sensible approach, and the implementation details regarding the weighting scheme and probabilistic relearning are well-explained. 

The strengths of the paper include its technical soundness, clarity, and novelty. The simulation results are convincing, demonstrating LoSST’s ability to track changing structures and outperform a baseline batch-mode learner. The application to the US price index data provides a practical demonstration of the algorithm’s potential. The discussion of the trade-off between convergence and diligence is a valuable contribution to the field. The algorithm appears well-designed to balance responsiveness to change with stability in stationary environments. The inclusion of the theoretical discussion relating to convergence and diligence strengthens the work.

However, there are several weaknesses. First, the parameter settings (T and γ) used in the simulations and application seem somewhat arbitrary. While the authors mention unsystematic investigation, a more thorough sensitivity analysis of these parameters would be beneficial. How robust is the performance to changes in these settings? A discussion of practical guidelines for setting these parameters would also be helpful. Second, the comparison to the PC algorithm is somewhat limited. While the authors demonstrate that LoSST outperforms PC after a change, it would be valuable to compare the computational cost of LoSST and PC, especially in scenarios where the structure changes frequently.  Third, the authors acknowledge that LoSST performs worse on edge removal errors when only parameters change, and while they provide an explanation, a potential solution to mitigate this issue (e.g., a more sophisticated weighting scheme or a different structure learning algorithm) could be explored.  Fourth, the claim that other standard graphical model structure learning algorithms would perform similarly to PC is potentially weak, and should be supported by a short empirical demonstration or a stronger theoretical argument. Finally, the application to the US price index volatility is intriguing but somewhat brief. A more detailed analysis of the learned graphical models and their implications for understanding the economic data would strengthen the results.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical gap in the literature by providing an algorithm for online structure learning in non-stationary environments.
* Technically sound and well-motivated.
* Clear and detailed presentation of the algorithm and its implementation.
* Compelling simulation results and a practical application demonstrate the algorithm’s effectiveness.
* Insightful discussion of the trade-off between convergence and diligence.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Parameter settings appear somewhat arbitrary and lack a thorough sensitivity analysis.
* Limited comparison to other structure learning algorithms beyond PC.
* Performance on edge removal errors is suboptimal when only parameters change.
* Application to US price index volatility is brief and could be expanded.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The algorithm is novel, well-designed, and supported by compelling empirical evidence. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the parameter sensitivity, computational cost comparison, and potential mitigation of edge removal errors. A more detailed analysis of the application domain could also enhance the paper.
