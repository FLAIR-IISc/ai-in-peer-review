

This paper introduces LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical model structures in real-time. The main claim is that LoSST can effectively learn graphical structure online when the underlying generating process is locally stationary (i.e., changes gradually over time), while maintaining comparable performance to batch-mode learning when the structure is globally stationary.

The paper makes a strong contribution by addressing a significant gap in graphical model learning literature, where most existing methods assume globally stationary structures. The authors correctly identify that current online learning approaches either cannot track structural changes (like Bayesian updating) or require substantial memory (like changepoint detection algorithms). LoSST's approach of tracking sufficient statistics with dynamically weighted data points provides a novel solution.

The experimental evaluation is well-designed, with three simulation sets demonstrating LoSST's effectiveness: (1) when both graph structure and parameters change, (2) when only parameters change, and (3) with probabilistic relearning schedules. The real-world application to US price index volatility successfully demonstrates LoSST's ability to detect meaningful economic shifts (like the Great Moderation). The paper correctly acknowledges limitations regarding rapid structural changes and random-order mixtures.

The algorithm is technically sound with proper theoretical grounding, particularly regarding the convergence vs. diligence trade-off. The derivation of online sufficient statistics updates is elegant and well-explained. The clarity is excellent, with clear mathematical formulations and helpful figures.

However, the paper could strengthen its contribution by more thoroughly comparing against related work on dynamic graphical models. While the authors cite two relevant papers, they don't fully engage with the broader literature on time-varying graphical models. Additionally, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability to larger graphs.

The significance of this work is high, as real-world applications increasingly require online structure learning. The algorithm's ability to operate without batch processing makes it valuable for streaming data applications. The paper's main weakness is insufficient discussion of parameter sensitivity and how to select optimal parameters for different applications.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses a meaningful problem in graphical model learning with solid theoretical and empirical support. With more thorough comparison to related work and additional discussion of practical implementation considerations, this would be an excellent paper for NIPS. The current work is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant acceptance, though minor revisions would improve its impact.
