## Review of “Tracking Time-varying Graphical Structure”

This paper introduces LoSST, a novel heuristic algorithm for online structure learning in graphical models, specifically designed for environments where the underlying generative process is only *locally* stationary. The core idea is to track sufficient statistics and dynamically relearn the graph structure as needed, offering a compelling alternative to traditional batch-mode learning algorithms that assume global stationarity. The motivation is well-articulated, highlighting the limitations of existing methods in real-world dynamic systems.

The paper clearly explains the algorithm's mechanics, focusing on efficient online updates of means, covariances, and sample sizes, alongside a novel weighting scheme based on Mahalanobis distance and pooled p-values to detect structural changes. The convergence vs. diligence discussion is a valuable addition, providing nuanced insight into the algorithm's behavior based on parameter settings. The simulation results convincingly demonstrate LoSST’s performance, showing comparable results to batch learning in stable environments and significant improvements in dynamic scenarios, with insightful error breakdowns. The application to US price index data provides a promising real-world validation.

A key strength of the paper lies in its clear presentation and comprehensive related work section. It accurately positions LoSST within the landscape of existing structure learning and change-point detection techniques, convincingly arguing why current methods are insufficient for the targeted problem. The authors acknowledge limitations, such as sensitivity to rapid changes and potential computational costs with modifications for more complex scenarios. 

However, the heuristic nature of the algorithm, while acknowledged, could be a weakness. The parameter settings (T and γ) appear empirically determined and could benefit from more rigorous justification or adaptive tuning strategies. While the convergence/diligence discussion is valuable, a more formal analysis of these trade-offs would strengthen the theoretical foundation. Furthermore, the reliance on the PC algorithm for structure learning introduces potential bottlenecks, and exploring alternative, more scalable methods could be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant gap in the field of graphical model learning – handling non-stationary environments.
*   Presents a novel and well-explained algorithm with promising simulation results.
*   Clear writing and thorough related work demonstrate a strong understanding of the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Heuristic nature of the algorithm and empirically determined parameters.
*   Limited theoretical analysis of convergence/diligence trade-offs.
*   Potential scalability issues due to reliance on PC algorithm.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field. Despite the minor weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of LoSST warrant acceptance, with encouragement to the authors to explore further theoretical analysis and scalability improvements in future work. A revision addressing the parameter justification and exploring alternative structure learning algorithms would be beneficial.
