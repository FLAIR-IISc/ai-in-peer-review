

This paper introduces LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), an algorithm designed to learn graphical model structure in an online, real-time manner when the underlying generating process is locally stationary (i.e., changes slowly or incrementally over time rather than remaining globally stationary). The authors identify a significant gap in the literature: while most structure learning algorithms assume globally stationary processes, real-world applications often involve gradual structural changes that require rapid detection and adaptation. LoSST addresses this by tracking weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, sample size) online and using Mahalanobis distances combined with pooled p-values to detect structural changes, triggering appropriate model relearning.

The work makes several notable contributions. Theoretically, the paper provides a careful analysis of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off in online learning algorithms, showing that LoSST can balance these properties through parameter choices. Methodologically, the algorithm extends TD(0) learning to graphical model structure learning, using efficient online updates for sufficient statistics and a probabilistic relearning schedule based on pooled p-values. Empirically, the simulations demonstrate that LoSST performs comparably to batch learning in stationary settings while significantly outperforming batch methods in non-stationary scenarios. The real-world application to US price index volatility further validates the approach by detecting known economic shifts (the Great Moderation) while avoiding false positives during transient anomalies.

The paper excels in technical quality and clarity. The algorithm description is precise, with clear update equations and sufficient mathematical detail. The experimental design is robust, testing multiple scenarios including graph structure changes and parameter changes, with appropriate baselines (PC algorithm). The real-world case study provides meaningful validation beyond synthetic data. The writing is well-organized, with a strong related work section that clearly distinguishes LoSST from previous approaches like change point detection algorithms and batch structure learning methods.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a significant contribution by extending online learning concepts to graphical model structure learning, a domain that has been largely overlooked in the literature. The authors properly contextualize their work against relevant prior research, including Talih and Hengartner (2005) and Siracusa and Fisher III (2009), while demonstrating how LoSST differs in key aspects (online operation, directed graphs, frequentist approach).

The significance of this work is substantial. Real-world applications frequently involve non-stationary processes where batch learning is infeasible, making this algorithm potentially valuable across numerous domains including finance, biology, and social sciences. The paper demonstrates practical utility while maintaining theoretical rigor.

Several minor improvements would strengthen the paper. The parameter sensitivity analysis could be expanded to better guide practitioners in selecting T and γ values for different applications. The discussion of the probabilistic relearning schedule would benefit from more detailed justification of the ν parameter's role and optimal values. While the focus on recursive SEMs is reasonable, a brief discussion of potential extensions to other graphical model types (e.g., Bayesian networks with discrete variables) would enhance the paper's scope. The real-world application, while compelling, could be strengthened by discussing computational scalability with larger numbers of variables.

The paper's main limitation is its focus on recursive SEMs; extending the approach to other graphical model types would be a valuable direction for future work. However, this limitation is understandable given the paper's specific focus and the significant technical challenges involved.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field that effectively addresses an important practical problem. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides both theoretical insights and empirical validation. The proposed algorithm fills a clear gap in the literature while maintaining computational efficiency. The results demonstrate clear advantages in non-stationary settings with no significant cost in stationary settings, making it a valuable tool for practitioners dealing with time-varying graphical structures.

The paper meets all four evaluation criteria effectively: high quality through sound methodology and thorough validation, excellent clarity in presentation, significant originality in addressing the online structure learning problem, and substantial significance for both theoretical and applied research. The work represents a meaningful advance over previous methods and should be considered for acceptance at NIPS.
