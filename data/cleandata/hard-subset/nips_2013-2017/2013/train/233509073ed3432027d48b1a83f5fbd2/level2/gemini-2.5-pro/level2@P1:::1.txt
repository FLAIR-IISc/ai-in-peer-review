This paper presents LoSST, a novel, online algorithm for learning the structure of graphical models from data streams where the underlying generating process may be non-stationary. The central challenge addressed is that most structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data from a single, stable generative model, an assumption often violated in real-world scenarios. The proposed LoSST algorithm operates in a single pass by tracking sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, effective sample size) for a set of continuous variables. Its core innovation is a dynamic weighting scheme for incoming data points. The weight assigned to a new data point is determined by how well it "fits" the current estimated model, as measured by its Mahalanobis distance. To distinguish between single outliers and genuine structural changes, the algorithm uses Liptak's method to pool p-values of Mahalanobis distances over time. A sequence of poor fits (low pooled p-value) signals a likely change, causing the algorithm to increase the weights of new data points, effectively down-weighting the influence of past data. The graph structure is then relearned probabilistically, with a higher chance of relearning after a suspected change. The paper situates this work relative to standard batch-mode structure learning, online parameter estimation (like TD-learning), and changepoint detection methods, highlighting its unique ability to perform online *structure* learning.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is well-motivated and the update equations for the weighted sufficient statistics are clearly presented. The use of Mahalanobis distance as a measure of fit for multivariate Gaussian data is standard and appropriate, and its combination with a pooled p-value to provide robustness against isolated outliers is a clever design choice. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using synthetic data to demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness in different scenarios (structure change vs. parameter-only change) and comparing it against a standard batch-mode baseline (the PC algorithm). The results clearly show that LoSST performs comparably to the batch algorithm in stationary environments and significantly outperforms it when the underlying structure changes. The application to real-world US price index data, where the algorithm identifies a known period of economic change (the Great Moderation), serves as a compelling proof of concept.

A particularly strong aspect of the paper is the formal discussion of the trade-off between "convergence" and "diligence." The authors correctly identify that an algorithm cannot simultaneously guarantee convergence to a stable structure in the limit and also guarantee detection of any change within a fixed time window. Providing conditions on LoSST's parameters (γ, T) that lead to convergent, diligent, or mixed-behavior regimes adds theoretical depth to the work.

However, the work has some weaknesses. The algorithm relies on several heuristic choices. The specific functional form for updating the weights (Equation 4) and the noisy-OR gate for scheduling relearning events are justified intuitively but not derived from a more formal principle. The parameter settings for the experiments (e.g., T = 0.05, γ = 3) were chosen via "unsystematic investigation," which raises questions about their generalizability and robustness. A sensitivity analysis for these key parameters would strengthen the paper's claims.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is established clearly in the introduction, and the algorithm is built up step-by-step in Section 3. The use of figures in the results section is effective, clearly visualizing the error metrics over time and demonstrating the performance difference between LoSST and the baseline. The real-world case study is also presented with clear and informative plots. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to understand the method and its implementation.

**Originality:** The paper's contribution is highly original. While learning time-varying graphical models has been explored before (e.g., Talih & Hengartner, 2005; Siracusa & Fisher III, 2009), those methods typically operate in batch mode. The development of a single-pass, online algorithm for this task is a novel and important step. The specific combination of weighted sufficient statistics, pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances, and a probabilistic relearning schedule appears to be a new contribution to the field.

**Significance:** The problem addressed by this paper is of great practical and theoretical significance. The stationarity assumption is a major limitation of many machine learning algorithms, and relaxing it is crucial for applying these methods to real-world data streams in finance, climate science, social networks, and many other domains. LoSST provides a practical and effective solution to this problem for continuous variables. The work opens up several avenues for future research, such as extending the framework to discrete variables, developing more principled parameter-setting procedures, and improving scalability. Other researchers are likely to build upon these ideas.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important and challenging problem of learning graphical model structure in non-stationary environments.
*   Proposes a novel and well-motivated online algorithm, LoSST, that is clearly explained and appears technically sound.
*   The empirical evaluation is strong, showing superior performance over a standard batch-mode baseline on synthetic data and plausible results on a real-world dataset.
*   The paper is very well-written and organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Several components of the algorithm are heuristic, and the choice of key parameters is not well-justified, lacking a sensitivity analysis.
*   The baseline for comparison (batch PC over all data) is a weak one, as it is not designed for non-stationary data. A comparison with a more robust baseline, such as a sliding-window approach, would be more informative.
*   The method's scalability is a potential concern, as it requires a matrix inversion (O(V^3)) at each time step, which may be prohibitive for high-dimensional data. This limitation is not thoroughly explored.

In summary, this is a strong paper presenting a novel and significant contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The strengths—addressing an important problem with a well-designed and clearly explained online algorithm backed by solid experiments—far outweigh the weaknesses, which primarily relate to the heuristic nature of some components and the choice of baseline. I recommend acceptance.
