

This paper introduces LoSST (Locally Stationary Structure Tracker), a novel algorithm for tracking changes in graphical model structure in real-time. The authors address a significant gap in the literature: existing structure learning algorithms assume globally stationary environments (i.i.d. data), but real-world data often comes from locally stationary processes where the generating structure changes over time. The paper is well-motivated, clearly explaining why this problem matters in practical applications, and makes a strong case for the need for online structure learning algorithms.

The main contribution is a heuristic algorithm that tracks sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) in an online fashion, using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values to determine when to relearn the graphical model structure. The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of the algorithm's behavior, showing it can be tuned to balance between convergence (stabilizing on a stable structure) and diligence (responding quickly to structural changes). The simulation results are comprehensive, demonstrating that LoSST performs comparably to batch-mode learning in stable environments, while significantly outperforming batch methods when structure changes occur. The real-world application to US price index volatility further validates the approach.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The related work section effectively distinguishes LoSST from existing methods, particularly highlighting how it differs from online learning algorithms that track environmental features (like TD(0)) and changepoint detection methods (which require storing substantial data). The algorithm description is precise, with clear update equations for the weighted sufficient statistics and the probabilistic relearning mechanism.

Strengths:
- The problem addressed is timely and important, with clear real-world relevance
- The algorithm is well-motivated and theoretically grounded
- The simulation experiments are thorough and well-designed, covering both structural and parameter changes
- The real-world application provides concrete evidence of utility
- The paper properly acknowledges limitations and suggests promising future directions
- The approach is practical, with no additional cost in stable environments

Weaknesses:
- The probabilistic relearning mechanism, while theoretically justified, could benefit from more detailed analysis of how to optimally set the ν parameter
- The paper focuses on recursive SEMs; extending to other graphical model types (e.g., Bayesian networks with discrete variables) would strengthen the claim of broader applicability
- The connection to recent NIPS work could be more explicit; while the authors cite relevant papers, a clearer comparison to other recent structure learning methods would help position the contribution more precisely
- The Mahalanobis distance thresholding strategy, while practical, is somewhat ad hoc; a more principled approach to setting the threshold T would be valuable

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graphical model structure learning by addressing a critical gap in the literature. The problem of learning in non-stationary environments is increasingly important as more applications involve streaming data. The authors have demonstrated that LoSST achieves the best of both worlds: it performs as well as batch methods when structure is stable, while providing substantial improvements when structure changes. This is a meaningful advance that should be of interest to the NIPS community.

For the significance criterion, this work addresses a problem that affects many real-world applications where data is non-stationary (e.g., financial markets, social networks, sensor networks). The ability to track structural changes in real-time without the need for retrospective analysis has clear practical value, and the authors have demonstrated this with both synthetic and real-world data. The method also advances the state of the art by providing a practical solution to a problem that has received limited attention in the literature.

The paper is technically sound, with appropriate theoretical analysis and thorough empirical validation. The authors have carefully considered the trade-offs between convergence and diligence, and have provided a clear characterization of when LoSST performs well and when it might struggle.

To improve the paper, I recommend:
1. Adding a brief discussion of how to select the parameters ν and T in practice, perhaps with some empirical guidance
2. Expanding the discussion of how LoSST could be extended to other graphical model types (e.g., Bayesian networks with discrete variables)
3. Providing a more explicit comparison to recent NIPS papers on structure learning, to better position this work within the field

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of graphical model structure learning. The problem is well-motivated, the solution is novel and well-executed, and the results are compelling. I recommend acceptance.
