

This paper introduces LoSST, a novel algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical model structure in a real-time, online manner. The authors identify a critical gap in the literature: existing structure learning algorithms assume globally stationary data, while real-world data often exhibit locally stationary processes where structure changes gradually or abruptly. LoSST addresses this by tracking sufficient statistics (means, covariances, effective sample size) online, using Mahalanobis distances and pooled p-values to detect structural changes and adjust data weights accordingly.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it clearly articulates the problem and justifies why current approaches are insufficient for online structure learning. The theoretical foundation is solid, with careful derivation of update equations for the sufficient statistics and a thoughtful analysis of the convergence vs. diligence trade-off. The simulation results are convincing, showing LoSST performs comparably to batch methods during stable periods but significantly outperforms them when structure changes occur. The real-world application to US price index volatility demonstrates practical relevance, with LoSST detecting the well-known "Great Moderation" period.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. The related work section inadequately explains why existing online methods (like TDL or changepoint detection) cannot be adapted for graphical model structure learning. The authors mention that Bayesian online methods would require maintaining distributions over superexponential numbers of models, but this argument needs stronger justification. The paper also lacks a clear definition of "locally stationary" in the context of graphical models beyond the i.i.d. assumption at each time point, which is crucial for understanding the algorithm's scope.

The parameter choices (T=0.05, γ=3) for the simulations appear arbitrary without sufficient theoretical or empirical justification. The authors should provide a more systematic exploration of these parameters and their impact on performance. Additionally, the paper assumes multivariate Gaussian data for Mahalanobis distance calculations, but doesn't address how the algorithm would perform with non-Gaussian data, which is a significant limitation given the wide applicability of graphical models.

The real-world application, while promising, is somewhat superficial. The paper shows LoSST detects the Great Moderation period, but doesn't provide detailed analysis of the learned graphical models or their interpretability. The authors could strengthen this section by comparing LoSST's learned structure to domain knowledge or other methods.

The paper also neglects to discuss computational complexity and scalability. Graphical model structure learning is computationally expensive, and the online nature of LoSST may exacerbate this issue. The authors should include complexity analysis and discuss how the algorithm scales with the number of variables and the frequency of relearning.

Regarding significance, the problem is clearly important, but the paper could better articulate how LoSST advances the state of the art beyond incremental improvements. The authors claim their approach is the first to handle online graphical model structure learning, but this claim needs stronger support through more comprehensive comparison with related work.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with clear presentation of simulation results. However, the lack of thorough comparison with related work, the arbitrary parameter choices, and the limited discussion of non-Gaussian data and computational complexity are significant weaknesses that would need to be addressed for acceptance.

Overall, this is an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of graphical models, addressing an important real-world problem. With substantial revisions to strengthen the theoretical justification, provide more thorough experimental comparisons, and address the limitations mentioned above, this paper could make a significant contribution to NIPS. As it stands, the paper has promising ideas but needs more rigorous treatment to fully substantiate its claims. I recommend rejection but would support a resubmission after addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
