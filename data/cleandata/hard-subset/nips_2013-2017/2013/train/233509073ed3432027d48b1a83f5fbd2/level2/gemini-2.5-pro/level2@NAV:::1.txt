This paper presents LoSST, a novel online algorithm for learning graphical model structure from non-stationary data streams. The core idea is to address the common but often violated assumption in structure learning that data is globally i.i.d. LoSST tracks sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, effective sample size) using a dynamic weighting scheme. New data points are weighted based on their fit with the current model, as measured by the Mahalanobis distance. A poor fit suggests an underlying change, leading to higher weights for recent data and a higher probability of triggering a full structure relearning step. This work builds upon the intersection of classical structure learning, online learning, and changepoint detection, but offers a novel, real-time approach that differentiates it from previous batch-mode methods for time-varying graphs.

The paper has several significant strengths.
*   **Significance and Originality:** The problem of learning from non-stationary data is highly relevant and practical. The proposed solution, which combines weighted sufficient statistics with a probabilistic relearning trigger, is an elegant and novel approach to this challenging problem.
*   **Quality:** The technical contribution is sound. The empirical evaluation is thorough, using extensive simulations to demonstrate that LoSST outperforms a standard batch-mode algorithm (PC) in changing environments while remaining competitive in stable ones. The inclusion of a real-world application to economic data successfully showcases the algorithm's utility. Furthermore, the theoretical discussion on the "convergence vs. diligence" trade-off is a sophisticated addition that demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem space.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly motivated. The algorithm's components are explained logically, and the experimental results are presented effectively.

The paper also has minor weaknesses.
*   The algorithm relies on several hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) whose settings are based on "unsystematic investigation." While the chosen values perform well, the paper would be stronger with a brief sensitivity analysis or a more principled discussion on how these parameters could be set in practice.
*   The probabilistic relearning mechanism is effective but heuristic. A brief motivation for choosing the noisy-OR update rule over other alternatives could provide additional insight.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper addresses an important and under-explored problem with a novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated solution. The work is of high quality and significance.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** The reliance on several heuristically chosen parameters is a minor limitation.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. This is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in structure learning for dynamic environments. I recommend acceptance.
