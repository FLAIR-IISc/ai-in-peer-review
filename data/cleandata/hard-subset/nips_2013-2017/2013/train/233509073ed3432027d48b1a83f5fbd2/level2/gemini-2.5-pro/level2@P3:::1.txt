This paper addresses the important and practical problem of learning graphical model structure from data streams where the underlying generating process may be non-stationary. Standard structure learning algorithms typically assume a stationary process, operating in batch mode on a full dataset. This work introduces the Locally Stationary Structure Tracker (LoSST), a novel online algorithm designed to track changes in both the structure and parameters of a graphical model over time.

The core idea of LoSST is to maintain and update a weighted set of sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, and an effective sample size) for a linear Structural Equation Model (SEM). With each new data point, the algorithm assesses the fit of the point to the current model using the Mahalanobis distance. A sequence of poorly fitting points, identified by pooling p-values, signals a likely change in the underlying data distribution. In response, the algorithm increases the weight assigned to new data points, effectively down-weighting past data and allowing the tracked statistics to adapt to the new regime. A standard structure learning algorithm (in this case, the PC algorithm) is then run periodically on the current sufficient statistics to produce an updated graph. The paper also provides a theoretical discussion of the trade-off between convergence to a stable structure and diligence in detecting changes, showing how LoSST's parameters can be tuned to favor one over the other.

The proposed method is evaluated on synthetic data, demonstrating that its performance is comparable to a batch-mode algorithm in a stationary environment and superior when the underlying graph structure or parameters change. An application to U.S. price index data shows the algorithm identifying plausible change points corresponding to known macroeconomic shifts.

**Quality**

The work presents a technically sound approach to a challenging problem. The online update equations for the weighted sufficient statistics are clearly presented. The method for change detection, based on Mahalanobis distance and pooled p-values, is a well-motivated heuristic. The experimental evaluation is solid, using synthetic data with known ground truth to provide a clear comparison against a standard batch-mode baseline. The inclusion of separate experimental conditions for structure change and parameter-only change is a strength, as it reveals more nuanced aspects of the algorithm's performance. The application to real-world data provides a valuable demonstration of the algorithm's potential utility.

A point for consideration is the reliance on the PC algorithm for the structure learning step. Constraint-based methods can be sensitive to errors in conditional independence tests, particularly when the effective sample size is small, which can occur immediately after LoSST detects a change and reduces the effective sample size. A brief discussion of how this sensitivity might interact with the dynamics of the LoSST algorithm could further strengthen the analysis. Additionally, while the paper provides proof sketches for the algorithm's convergent and diligent properties under certain parameter settings, these claims would be stronger with more detailed theoretical support, perhaps in an appendix.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The motivation for the problem is compelling, and the description of the LoSST algorithm is presented logically, from a high-level overview to the specific update equations. The figures are clear and effectively communicate the main results of the simulations.

For potential improvement, the derivation of the update equation for the effective sample size (Equation 3) is justified based on intuition. While the reasoning is plausible, a more formal motivation for this specific functional form would enhance the clarity of the method. Similarly, the parameter settings (e.g., T=0.05, γ=3) were chosen based on "unsystematic investigation." While a full parameter sweep is not expected, providing more guidance or intuition for setting these crucial parameters would increase the paper's utility for practitioners.

**Originality**

The paper makes a novel contribution to the field of structure learning. While online learning and changepoint detection are established fields, their application to the problem of learning the full graphical model *structure* in a real-time, online fashion is relatively unexplored. Previous works on time-varying graphs have typically operated in batch mode. The LoSST algorithm's approach of combining adaptive weighting of sufficient statistics with a periodic structure search is an original and effective synthesis of existing ideas to tackle this new problem. The paper does an excellent job of situating the work within the literature and differentiating it from related methods.

**Significance**

The problem addressed is of high practical and theoretical importance. Many real-world systems, from financial markets to biological networks, are dynamic, and methods that assume stationarity are fundamentally limited in these domains. LoSST provides a practical framework for analyzing such systems. A significant strength is that the algorithm incurs minimal performance cost in stationary settings, making it a potentially powerful general-purpose alternative to traditional batch learners. This work establishes a strong foundation and a valuable baseline for future research in online and non-stationary structure learning.

The current implementation focuses on linear SEMs with Gaussian variables, which is a reasonable starting point but limits immediate applicability to other common data types. The computational cost of inverting the covariance matrix at each time step could also become a bottleneck in very high-dimensional settings. Mentioning these limitations and potential avenues for addressing them would be helpful.

**Summary of Strengths:**
*   Addresses the important and under-explored problem of online structure learning for non-stationary processes.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated algorithm (LoSST) that combines adaptive sufficient statistics tracking with a standard structure learner.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance on both synthetic and real-world data, outperforming a standard batch-mode baseline in non-stationary settings.
*   The algorithm is clearly explained and the paper is well-written.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   Discuss the potential interaction between the sensitivity of the chosen structure learning algorithm (PC) and the dynamics of the effective sample size.
*   Provide more guidance on setting the key hyperparameters (T, γ, ν) that control the algorithm's behavior.
*   Offer a more formal motivation for the effective sample size update rule.
*   Acknowledge the computational complexity of the Mahalanobis distance calculation as a potential limitation in high-dimensional domains.

Overall, this is a strong paper that introduces a novel and significant contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The proposed algorithm is elegant, and the experimental results are convincing. The work opens up a promising direction for future research.
