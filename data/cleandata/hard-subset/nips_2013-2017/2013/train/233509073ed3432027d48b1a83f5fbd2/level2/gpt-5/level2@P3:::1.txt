Summary
The paper proposes LoSST, an online algorithm for tracking time-varying graphical structure in linear-Gaussian DAG-based models (recursive SEMs). The core idea is to maintain locally stationary sufficient statistics—means, covariances, and an effective sample size—via weighted updates. Weights adapt to evidence of distributional mismatch assessed by Mahalanobis distances, whose individual p-values are combined over time using Liptak’s method to obtain a pooled p-value ρ. Low ρ increases the weight given to recent data (and shrinks the effective sample size), which both accelerates adaptation and increases the probability of triggering a structure relearning step. Structure learning is performed by a constraint-based algorithm (PC). The paper analyzes the trade-off between convergence and diligence (fast response to change), showing parameter regimes that guarantee each (or a middle ground). Simulations with synthetic SEMs show LoSST matches batch PC under stationarity and outperforms it after structural changes; an application to U.S. CPI component series indicates detection of macroeconomic regime shifts (e.g., the Great Moderation).

Relation to prior work
The paper clearly situates itself relative to batch learning of DAGs [3,10,12], online change detection [1,4], and prior time-indexed structure learning for undirected graphs [14] and Bayesian dynamic edges [11]. Additional relevant work on time-varying undirected graphical models includes Zhou, Lafferty, and Wasserman (JMLR 2010) and Kolar, Song, Ahmed, and Xing (Annals of Statistics 2010), who use kernel/fused regularization to estimate smoothly evolving Gaussian graphical models. Those methods are batch and undirected, while LoSST targets online learning of directed SEM structure, which differentiates the contribution.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The weighted mean and covariance update equations are reasonable and memory-efficient. The effective sample size recursion is heuristic but motivated by constraints. Using Hotelling’s T^2 for Mahalanobis distances when the covariance is estimated from weighted samples is an approximation; the degrees-of-freedom adjustment via the effective sample size Sr is plausible, but would benefit from a brief justification or sensitivity analysis. The mapping from pooled p-values to weights and the noisy-OR relearning probability are principled heuristics and are analyzed for convergence/diligence trade-offs.
- Inference and tests: When using PC, the conditional independence tests rely on partial correlations derived from the current covariance; it would be helpful to confirm that Type I error is controlled when using weighted sufficient statistics and a time-varying effective sample size, and to describe any regularization when Cr is ill-conditioned (V close to Sr).
- Empirical evaluation: The synthetic studies are thorough across graph sizes and degrees, with clear metrics (edge addition/removal/orientation). The parameter-change-only setting highlights a brief window where LoSST removes edges conservatively—an insightful observation. The probabilistic relearning experiment shows computational savings without accuracy loss. The real-data study is suggestive but would benefit from more quantitative validation (e.g., predictive likelihood or out-of-sample fit).
- Baselines: The main baseline is batch PC on the entire dataset, which is disadvantaged post-change. Reasonable comparators for streaming or locally stationary settings include: (a) sliding-window or exponentially weighted PC; (b) Bayesian online change-point detection [1] coupled with restarts; and (c) for adjacency recovery only, time-varying Gaussian graphical model estimators [Zhou et al. 2010; Kolar et al. 2010], applied in a sliding fashion. Including one or more of these would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally clear and well organized, with explicit update equations and a helpful discussion of convergence vs. diligence. Minor issues: a few typos (e.g., “graph2 relearning”), and the absence of algorithmic pseudocode makes it harder to reproduce details (e.g., exact PC test choices and regularization). A short parameter guide (typical T, γ, ν ranges and their effects) would improve usability.

Originality
The combination of pooled p-value monitoring of Mahalanobis distances, adaptive weighting of sufficient statistics, and probabilistic triggering of constraint-based structure relearning is novel for online DAG/SEM structure learning. The convergence/diligence lens is also a distinctive and insightful framing.

Significance
Real-time structure learning for nonstationary environments is important in many applications. The method is simple, memory-efficient, and broadly compatible with existing structure learners. The empirical benefits under structural change are compelling. Broader significance would be heightened by stronger baselines, larger-scale experiments, and more comprehensive robustness analyses.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation: locally stationary DAG structure tracking with single-pass updates.
- Lightweight, practical algorithm; memory O(V^2) and compatible with standard learners (PC).
- Thoughtful analysis of convergence vs. diligence and parameter regimes.
- Solid synthetic evidence; interpretable real-world case study.

Cons
- Baselines omit common streaming alternatives (sliding windows, EWMA-PC, BOCPD+restart) and time-varying GGM methods for undirected adjacency comparison.
- Statistical calibration of Hotelling’s T^2 with weighted covariances and small effective sample sizes is not fully justified.
- Limited guidance on parameter selection and regularization; no runtime profiling.
- Real-data validation is qualitative; no predictive or intervention-relevant evaluation.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Add at least one streaming baseline (e.g., sliding-window PC or EWMA-PC) and, if feasible, a CPD+restart baseline [1], to quantify gains attributable to the weighting and triggering strategy.
- Provide a brief justification or empirical check for using T^2 with weighted covariances and Sr (e.g., coverage of p-values under stationarity, sensitivity to Sr).
- Include algorithmic details: independence tests and significance levels in PC, regularization (e.g., ridge) when inverting Cr, and handling when V ≥ Sr.

Highly desirable (if time permits):
- Sensitivity analysis over T, γ, ν, with guidance for setting them.
- Runtime and relearning frequency vs. accuracy trade-offs.
- For the CPI case, add quantitative validation: likelihood improvement, stability of discovered edges, or correspondence with documented macro events.

Optional:
- Compare DAG skeleton recovery to time-varying GGM methods (Zhou et al. 2010; Kolar et al. 2010) on synthetic Gaussians.
- Provide pseudocode and release code for reproducibility.

Overall
A timely and practical contribution to online structure learning under nonstationarity, with a clear algorithmic design and promising empirical results. With stronger baselines and modest statistical calibration, the work would be a strong fit for the conference.
