This paper introduces LoSST, a heuristic algorithm for tracking changes in graphical model structure in a dynamic, real-time manner. The motivation is compelling: many real-world systems are not globally stationary, yet standard structure learning algorithms assume i.i.d. data. LoSST aims to bridge this gap by adaptively updating sufficient statistics and dynamically relearning the graphical model structure.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the work within the broader context of graphical model learning. The related work section is reasonably thorough, covering both Bayesian/score-based and constraint-based methods, as well as online changepoint detection and previous attempts at time-indexed graph structures. The description of the LoSST algorithm is detailed and logically presented, with clear explanations of the update equations. The simulation results are convincing, demonstrating that LoSST performs competitively with batch-mode learning when the structure is stable and significantly better when it is locally stationary. The application to US price index volatility provides a compelling real-world example.

A major strength of the paper is its careful consideration of the trade-off between convergence and diligence. The authors explicitly acknowledge this incompatibility and discuss how LoSST can be tuned to favor one over the other. The convergence analysis is a valuable contribution. The algorithm’s design, with weights that adapt to the data fit, and the probabilistic relearning scheme, are both sensible and well-justified. The inclusion of the application to real-world data, even if preliminary, is a positive aspect, demonstrating potential applicability.

However, there are several weaknesses. First, the algorithm relies on a number of heuristics (e.g., the Mahalanobis distance threshold T, the maximum weight γ, the relearning frequency ν) that are not rigorously justified. While the simulations demonstrate good performance with specific parameter settings, it is unclear how sensitive the algorithm is to these parameters and how to choose them optimally in practice. A more systematic exploration of the parameter space would be beneficial.

Second, the complexity of the algorithm is not fully analyzed. The authors mention computational cost but do not provide a detailed analysis of the runtime complexity, particularly as the number of variables increases. This is important for assessing the scalability of LoSST to large-scale problems. Specifically, the repeated application of the PC algorithm, even with a probabilistic schedule, is potentially expensive.

Third, while the comparison to the PC algorithm is reasonable, it would be valuable to compare LoSST to other online structure learning algorithms, even if they are not directly comparable. For instance, comparing its performance to algorithms designed for dynamic Bayesian networks could provide additional insights.

Fourth, the discussion of the application to US price index volatility is somewhat brief. It would be helpful to provide more details about the data preprocessing steps and the specific interpretations of the detected structural changes.  The statement about detecting the “Great Moderation” feels a bit strong without further statistical justification.

Finally, the notation is sometimes inconsistent or could be improved. For example, the meaning of subscript ‘r’ isn’t always immediately clear without referring back to previous definitions.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and largely unexplored problem in graphical model learning: adapting to non-stationary environments.
*   Presents a novel and well-designed heuristic algorithm (LoSST) that demonstrates promising performance in simulations.
*   Provides a careful analysis of the trade-off between convergence and diligence.
*   Includes a motivating real-world application.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relies heavily on heuristics with limited theoretical justification.
*   Lacks a detailed complexity analysis.
*   Limited comparison to other online structure learning algorithms.
*   The real-world application section could be more in-depth.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of graphical model learning. The problem it addresses is important, the proposed algorithm is novel and promising, and the paper is well-written. While there are some weaknesses, they are not fatal. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding parameter sensitivity, complexity analysis, and comparison to other relevant algorithms. Specifically, a more detailed discussion of parameter tuning and computational cost would strengthen the paper considerably.
