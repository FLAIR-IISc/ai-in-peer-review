- Tackles online learning of graphical model structure from non-stationary data via a novel algorithm, LoSST.
- LoSST maintains weighted sufficient statistics (mean, covariance, effective sample size) and updates them online.
- New points are assessed with Mahalanobis distance; pooled p-values detect sequences of poor fits as changepoints.
- On detecting change, LoSST increases weight on new data to adapt statistics to the new regime.
- A standard structure learner (PC algorithm) is run periodically on the current weighted statistics to update the graph.
- Paper analyzes the trade-off between convergence to a stable structure and diligence in detecting changes, with tunable parameters.
- Empirical results on synthetic data: comparable to batch methods in stationary settings and superior under structural/parameter changes.
- Real-world application to U.S. price index data identifies plausible macroeconomic change points.
- Strength: clear, well-motivated algorithm with transparent online update equations and solid experimental design including separate structure/parameter-change conditions.
- Concern: reliance on PC (constraint-based CI tests) may be sensitive when effective sample size is small immediately after detected changes.
- Theoretical claims (convergence/diligence) are supported by proof sketches but would benefit from fuller formal proofs.
- Clarity strong overall, but the effective sample size update (Eq.3) lacks formal derivation and parameter choices (T, γ, ν) were chosen unsystematically.
- Originality: novel synthesis of adaptive sufficient-statistics weighting and periodic structure search for online structure learning.
- Significance: practical and theoretically important problem; LoSST performs well with minimal cost in stationary settings and is a strong baseline for future work.
- Limitations: current focus on linear Gaussian SEMs and potential computational bottleneck from repeated covariance inversion in high dimensions.
- Suggested improvements: discuss interaction between PC sensitivity and effective sample size dynamics, give formal motivation for the ESS update, provide guidance on hyperparameter settings, and note computational scalability concerns.
