Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes LoSST, a heuristic online algorithm for tracking time-varying DAG structures (linear-Gaussian SEMs). The core idea is to maintain weighted sufficient statistics (means, covariances, an “effective sample size”) that adapt to potential changes in the data generating process. Adaptivity is driven by a pooled p-value over Mahalanobis distances (Hotelling’s T^2) of recent data under the current covariance estimate; a low pooled p-value increases the per-sample weight (a forgetting-factor-like mechanism), which both shrinks the effective sample size and triggers more frequent structure relearning via the PC algorithm. The paper provides update equations for weighted means/covariances, a heuristic effective sample size recursion, a relearning policy via a noisy-OR schedule based on pooled p-values, and qualitative statements about convergence vs. diligence trade-offs. Simulations compare LoSST to batch PC in scenarios with single changepoints (structure and/or parameters) and a longer sequence of changepoints; a brief CPI case study illustrates qualitative detection of macroeconomic volatility changes.

Related work is partially covered (batch BN/SEM structure learning; CPD; two time-varying network papers). However, there is substantial prior work on time-varying graphical models not cited or compared against, including sliding-window and kernel-smoothed time-varying GGMs (e.g., Zhou et al., 2010; Kolar, Song, and Xing, 2010), fused graphical lasso and other sparsity-regularized dynamic network estimators, and dynamic Bayesian networks/switching DBNs (e.g., Xuan & Murphy, 2007). The proposed approach is closest in spirit to adaptive/forgetting-factor updating plus periodic structure relearning; explicit comparisons would help situate novelty.

Quality:
- Positive: The online sufficient-statistics updates are clearly described; the pooled p-value mechanism is intuitive and computationally light; the relearning schedule is simple and matches the change-detection signal. Simulations show LoSST matches batch PC in stationary phases and improves markedly when the data are mixtures across structures (as expected).
- Concerns:
  1) Statistical validity of the change signal: Hotelling’s T^2 distributional assumptions hold for i.i.d. Gaussian data with standard (unweighted) estimates. It is unclear that the stated degrees of freedom using the heuristic “effective sample size” Sr are appropriate under time-varying weights and changing covariances. The method is explicitly heuristic, but the consequences (false positives/negatives) are not quantified. Please provide an empirical calibration (e.g., QQ plots, false alarm rates under stationarity) and/or a robustness analysis to mis-specification of the T^2 reference distribution.
  2) P-value pooling: Liptak’s method assumes independent tests under the null; Mahalanobis distances across time are dependent, especially with overlapping/weighted estimates. Some discussion or empirical check of the dependence impact (e.g., via block bootstrap) would strengthen the case.
  3) Baselines: The comparisons use only batch PC. Absent are strong and relevant baselines: (a) sliding-window PC; (b) exponentially weighted PC with fixed forgetting; (c) CPD + segmented PC; (d) time-varying GGM estimators (where applicable to linear-Gaussian settings) with conditional independence tests derived from precision matrices; (e) dynamic BN/DBN methods for discrete/linear-Gaussian models. Without these, it is hard to attribute gains to the specific ρ-driven adaptivity versus any reasonable forgetting strategy.
  4) Hyperparameter sensitivity: T, γ, ν are chosen “unsystematically.” Performance and stability likely hinge on these values. A sensitivity study (curves vs T, γ, ν; grid search on validation) and guidance for practitioners are needed.
  5) Numerical stability: Inverting Cr to compute Mahalanobis distance and running CI tests with small effective sample sizes are risky; singularity and high-variance estimates can occur (Sr ≲ p). No regularization (e.g., ridge/shrinkage covariance, graphical lasso for precision, partial-correlation shrinkage) is discussed. Please address regularization and its effect on both the change signal and PC’s CI tests.
  6) Theory: The convergence/diligence discussion is interesting but the “proof sketches” are insufficient to assess correctness. Formal statements with assumptions (e.g., bounded drift, minimal change size, mixing conditions) and proofs (even in an appendix) would add credibility. In particular, the step from bounded effective sample size to “diligence” merits a precise definition and proof.
  7) Experimental details: The PC parameters (significance level, conditioning set cap) and implementation details are not specified; runtime and relearning frequency vs. ν are not reported; orientation accuracy is reported but identifiability limits under linear-Gaussian models are not discussed.
  8) Real data: The CPI case study is descriptive. Without ground truth, an external validation (e.g., alignment with known regime indices, structural break tests, or out-of-sample predictive gains) would be helpful.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable and well-structured. A few issues: footnotes are hard to follow; some typos (“TDL,” “graph2”), and several references are incomplete. Please define all variables where first used (e.g., Pr(relearn)), state all PC settings, and consolidate the “proof sketches” in an appendix.

Originality:
The combination of adaptive pooled-p-value-driven forgetting with online sufficient statistics and probabilistic relearning for DAG structure is a reasonable and, to my knowledge, not previously presented package. However, components (EWMA/forgetting, windowed structure learning, change detection) are known; novelty rests on the specific pooling mechanism and its integration. Missing comparisons to closely related time-varying network estimators make it difficult to gauge distinctiveness.

Significance:
If validated, a lightweight, general-purpose online structure learner that degrades gracefully to batch performance and adapts to changes would be useful. The current empirical evidence is promising but limited by the choice of baseline and sensitivity analyses.

Suggestions to improve the paper:
- Add baselines: sliding-window PC; fixed forgetting-factor PC; CPD + restart; a time-varying GGM (precision-matrix) method mapped to CI tests.
- Provide sensitivity and calibration: vary T, γ, ν; report stability, change-detection delay, false alarms; analyze the null distribution of Dr under weighting; study dependence in pooled p-values.
- Add regularization for covariance/precision estimation with small Sr; report its effect.
- Detail PC settings and runtime; include complexity and relearning frequency trade-offs.
- Expand related work to include time-varying GGMs (kernel-smoothed and fused penalties) and dynamic BNs/DBNs.
- Strengthen theory with formal definitions and proofs or clearly label as conjectures.
- Improve the real-data validation with external benchmarks or predictive metrics.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear problem formulation; practical online algorithm with low overhead.
- Sensible integration of change detection and relearning; matches intuition.
- Promising simulation results showing substantial gains after changepoints.
- Potentially useful for streaming settings where batch is infeasible.

Cons for acceptance:
- Heuristic statistical underpinnings insufficiently validated.
- Missing strong baselines; unclear comparative advantage over simpler forgetting/window methods.
- Limited hyperparameter guidance; potential instability.
- Theory is sketch-level; real-data validation is qualitative.
- Incomplete related work and references.

Minor comments/typos:
- Fix “TDL” → “TD learning,” and “graph2” footnote artifacts.
- Complete references (years, venues).
- Clarify Hotelling’s T^2 parameterization and the use of Sr; consider an appendix derivation.
- Notation consistency for pooled p-value ρ and updates.
