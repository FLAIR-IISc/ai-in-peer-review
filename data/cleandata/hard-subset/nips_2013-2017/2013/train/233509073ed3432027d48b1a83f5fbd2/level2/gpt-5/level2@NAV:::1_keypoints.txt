- Introduces LoSST: an online heuristic that maintains weighted sufficient statistics and adapts forgetting via pooled p-values of Mahalanobis distances.  
- Relearns structure with PC on a probabilistic schedule driven by the same pooled evidence.  
- Analyzes a convergence–diligence trade-off controlled by parameters T and γ, with many results given as sketches.  
- Empirical evaluation on synthetic SEMs with changepoints and US CPI volatility data shows LoSST matches batch under stationarity and often outperforms when structure/parameters shift.  
- Online sufficient-statistics updates are methodologically sound and pooled-p-value mechanism is a plausible lightweight change detector.  
- Baselines are limited—missing comparisons to sliding-window/exp-weighted PC, BOCPD with restart, dynamic Bayesian networks, and time-varying GGMs (e.g., TV-GLASSO).  
- Real-data case study is qualitative and lacks external validation.  
- Sensitivity analyses for T, γ, ν are acknowledged but unsystematic; statistical significance of improvements is not reported.  
- Notational issues (e.g., Hotelling T2 parameters) and minor footnote/notation glitches need correction.  
- Paper is generally well-written and organized; adding pseudo-code, per-update complexity, and a hyperparameter summary would improve reproducibility.  
- The pooled p-value + online relearning combination is novel within constraint-based learning, and the convergence–diligence framing is interesting.  
- Scope is limited to continuous linear-Gaussian SEMs and PC-based learning, reducing broader applicability.  
- Suggested improvements: add strong baselines, systematic ablations over T/γ/ν, report detection delay/false-alarm trade-offs, consider robust distances and high-dimensional regularization, and release code/data.  
- Arguments for acceptance: clear problem, lightweight practical method, novel adaptive weighting, empirical gains in nonstationary settings.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing strong baselines, incomplete sensitivity/robustness/statistical testing, limited scope and real-world validation, and minor technical issues.
