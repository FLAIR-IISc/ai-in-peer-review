Neurons and their assemblies were inspired by the difficult problem of estimating from calcium imaging recordings. It proposes a "two-level sparse heterogenous hierarchical matrix factorization" through which neurons (i.e., their pixel-related), and the assemblies they belong to (possibly >1 per neuron), are jointly estimated. More generally, it divides an observation matrix into a product of several sparse matrices, where the ranks decrease from lower to higher. This allows a constraint to be imposed on the sparsity at each layer (structural), and is not limited to a hierarchical structure as in previous work (Jenaton et al., 2012; Kim et al., 2012). Furthermore, their method does not require prior assumptions on the exact number of assemblies (although the authors note that the number of assemblies to be assumed depends on optimization parameters, which need to be user-defined).

The method works well on synthetic data:
(i) It recovered the assembly assignment of neurons with more sensitivity than MNMMF (Cichocki and Zdunek, 2006), or KSVDS (Rubinstein et al., 2010), all methods including the proposed method, Adina cell selection started (Diego et al., 2013).
(ii) It identified single-neuron calcium transients with more sensitivity than MNMMF, Adina, or cell selection.

An example is also given from the analysis of real experimental data (calcium imaging in the hippocampus of rats), although the reviewer was not sure what conclusions could be drawn from these statistics (the neurons seem to be less spatially sparse than the initial state obtained from the Adina algorithm).

Parallel estimation of structures at each layer, including the most fundamental layer (dictionary), as well as time courses at each layer, as emphasized throughout the article, is said to be the reason for the more robust identification of neuronal time courses. However, the assumed dictionary (single-neuron image) is never discussed. Such reconstruction images (e.g., Supplementary Figure 6), along with reconstruction from ground truth and other methods, will be much more appealing to the reviewer than images from calcium recordings, where ground truth is unknown (Figure 6).

Quality
This paper is technically correct. It provides a deep description of the proposed cost function, detailed optimization details are available in the supplementary material. Some details may be missing, such as how trade-off parameters can be optimized (or, how they selected and what values they used in their simulations), how adjacency matrices were binarized (to create true and false positives for their evaluation, l. 308), or how sensitive the method was to their tested word level (l.300), but overall, I consider the article a complete work.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written.

Originality
To my knowledge, the proposed method is a new method. The relevant literature is adequately referenced.

Significance
The results are potentially important for any lab performing calcium imaging on cell assemblies. As the authors show, parallel optimization of assembly assignments and time courses of interest at each layer improves the robustness of the assumptions. This method should be extremely valuable for any experiment that can estimate the roughly consistent activity of neurons within an assembly.

Another advantage is the flexibility of imposing prior knowledge on any layer of detailed description. (It is unfortunate that the method did not seem to work well without starting with a dictionary obtained from Adina. In principle, it should be possible to include the knowledge that Adina uses at the 0th layer of the assumption..)

An interesting, technically correct article, valuable for anyone who works with structural data (i.e., calcium imaging of neural assemblies).
