The authors have presented an algorithm for learning multi-layer hierarchical representation. It is modeled as a (pun intended) low-rank matrix factorization problem where the number of factors is typically greater than 2 and each factor represents a different entity in the hierarchy. The factors are estimated using the standard block co-ordinate descent method where each factor is optimized when others are placed first, and a sparsity promoting penalty is used at each step.

The method is discussed in the context of large-scale calcium imaging data analysis where it is assumed that neurons (possibly overlapping) can be collected into a set of neuronal assemblies that create hierarchical pixels --> neurons --> assemblies. The goal is then to estimate the location of each neuron (in terms of pixels), the membership of each neuronal assembly, and the spike of each assembly.

The authors argue that their method performs better than other methods in synthetic (but realistic) data and can correctly identify neural assemblies in real data.

Basics
The concepts presented in this paper are new to my knowledge. The three-way division method is interesting, and I liked that the authors also included a standard two-way division in their model and applied consistency between the two. This additional step could make the algorithm more robust, such as if a neuron in an assembly "misses" a spike, etc.

Quality
My main concern about this paper is efficiency. Large-scale calcium imaging experiments typically involve several thousand pixels and thousands of timesteps. Although multi-layer division significantly reduces the dimension and several steps of the algorithm can be parallelized, I can imagine that the algorithm may still be quite slow and require a large number of iterations. These issues should be discussed.

I am also not satisfied with the use of neural assemblies. It is not clear that this will often be demonstrated in practice. Also, if a subset of neurons has the same spike, it will be reflected in the temporal component of the spatial-temporal matrix. The rank of the matrix will still be small, and I see no direct advantage to including the proposed multi-layer hierarchical division. The authors will probably have to expand on this.

In addition, the various regulators \OmegaU, \OmegaA should be clarified. For example, which convex cell shape was used previously?

The authors leave the user to choose the weight of the regulator. This seems like an important task to me since the number of regulators is large and their use is very important. The authors claim that an accurate prior estimate of the rank is not very important since the algorithm will shrink irrelevant elements to zero. Although this sounds intuitive, I can imagine that this behavior depends heavily on the correct choice of the various sparsity penalties that will shrink irrelevant elements.

Clarity
I think the paper is not written clearly at all and is now only accessible to an expert in calcium imaging data analysis. For example, the authors very briefly mention in the results section how neural spikes are translated into calcium activity through a convolution operator and do not discuss this central aspect of spike deconvolution in their algorithm. In fact, it seems to me that they have missed this problem "by excluding all transients that overlap by more than 20%" (if I understand correctly). This is a subject of active research (e.g. Vogelstein et al. 2010, Grewe et. al. 2010) and I would like to see how the authors relate to that literature.

I found the inclusion of the main equations to be somewhat unusual as an illustration, although this is also a question of style.

Finally, I would prefer a more thorough presentation of the real data experiments. Although the videos cannot be easily presented on paper, the results shown in Figure 6 may be more convincing. In general, Figure 6 needs a better explanation. An interesting method, but the paper needs significant clarification on a few important points.
