This article is inspired by the difficult problem of estimating neurons and their assemblies from calcium imaging recordings. It proposes a "two-level sparse heterogeneous hierarchical matrix factorization" by which neurons (i.e., their pixel-correlations), and the assemblies they belong to (potentially >1 per neuron), are jointly estimated. More generally, it provides a decomposition of an observation matrix into a product of several sparse matrices, whose ranks decrease from low to high. This allows the application of (structured) sparsity constraints at each level, and is not limited to hierarchical structures, as in previous work (Genton et al., 2012; Kim et al., 2012). Furthermore, their method does not require prior knowledge of the exact number of assemblies (although the authors mention that the number of assemblies estimated depends on optimization parameters, which need to be defined by the user).

The method has been shown to work well on synthetic data:

(i) It recovered the assembly assignment of neurons with more sensitivity than the sensitivity of MNMMF (Cichocki and Zdunek, 2006), or KSVDS (Rubinstein et al., 2010), all proposed methods were started with single neuron sorting (Diego et al., 2013).
(ii) It detected calcium transients of single neurons with more sensitivity than MNMMF, single neuron sorting, or cell sorting.

An example is also given from analyzing real experimental data (calcium imaging in the rat hippocampus), although the reviewer was not sure what to conclude from these images (the neurons appear to be less spatially sparse than the initial state obtained from the EDINA algorithm).

The parallel estimation of structures at each level, including the most basic (dictionary), as well as the time courses at each level, is emphasized throughout the article, which is said to be the reason for the strong identification of neuronal time courses. However, the estimated dictionary (images of single neurons) has never been discussed. Images of such reconstructions (for example, Supplementary Figure 6), next to reconstructions from ground truth and other methods, would be much more interesting to the reviewer than images of calcium recordings, where ground truth is unknown (Figure 6).

**Quality**

The paper is technically good. It provides an in-depth description of the proposed cost function, including a description of the optimization available in the supplementary material. Some details may be missing, such as how the trade-off parameters were optimized (or, how they chose and what values they used in their simulations), how the adjacency matrix was made binary (to give true and false positive results for their evaluation, l. 308), or how sensitive the method was to the level of noise it tested (l.300). But overall, I find the article a complete work.

**Clarity**

The paper is written clearly.

**Originality**

To my knowledge, the proposed approach is new. Relevant literature is adequately referenced.

**Significance**

The results are potentially important for any laboratory performing calcium imaging on cell assemblies. As the authors show, parallel optimization of the assembly assignments and time courses of interest at each level improves the robustness of the estimation. For any experiment that can assume a fairly consistent activation of neurons within an assembly - this method should be very valuable.

Another advantage is the flexibility in imposing prior knowledge on the details at any level. (It is a pity that the method does not work well without starting with a dictionary obtained from EDINA. In principle, it should be possible to incorporate the same knowledge used by EDINA into the 0th level estimation..)

An interesting, technically good article, with potential value for anyone working with structured data (i.e., calcium imaging of neural assemblies).
