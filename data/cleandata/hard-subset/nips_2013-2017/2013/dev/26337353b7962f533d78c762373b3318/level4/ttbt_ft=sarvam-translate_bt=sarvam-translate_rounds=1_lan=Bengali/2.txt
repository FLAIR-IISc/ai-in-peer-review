The article was inspired by the difficult problem of estimating neurons and their assemblies from calcium imaging recordings. It proposes a "two-tier sparse heterogeneous hierarchical matrix factorization" through which neurons (i.e., their pixel-related), and the assemblies they belong to (possibly >1 per neuron), are jointly estimated. More generally, it decomposes an observation matrix as a product of several sparse matrices, where the ranks decrease from low to high. This allows for the imposition of a constraint on the (structural) sparsity at each layer and is not limited to hierarchical structures like previous work (Jenaton et al., 2012; Kim et al., 2012). Furthermore, their method does not require a prior assumption on the exact number of assemblies (although the authors note that the number of assemblies assumed depends on optimization parameters, which need to be user-defined).

The method works well on synthetic data:
(i) It has recovered the assembly assignment of neurons with more sensitivity than MNMMF (Cichocki and Zdunek, 2006), or KSVDS (Rubinstein et al., 2010), all methods including the proposed method, Adina cell selection started (Diego et al., 2013).
(ii) It has identified single-neuron calcium transients with more sensitivity than MNMMF, Adina, or cell selection.

An example is also given from the analysis of real experimental data (calcium imaging in the hippocampus of rats), although the reviewer was not sure what conclusions to draw from these figures (the neurons seem to be less spatially sparse than the initial conditions obtained from the Adina algorithm).

The parallel estimation of the structure at each layer, including the most fundamental layer (the lexicon), as well as the time courses at each layer, as emphasized throughout the article, is said to be the reason for the more robust identification of neuronal time courses. However, the assumed lexicon (single-neuron images) is never discussed. Such reconstruction figures (e.g., Supplementary Figure 6), alongside reconstruction from ground truth and other methods, would be much more appealing to the reviewer than figures from calcium recordings, where ground truth is unknown (Figure 6).

Quality
This paper is technically sound. It provides a deep description of the proposed cost function, with detailed optimization details available in the supplementary material. Some details may be missing, such as how the trade-off parameters can be optimized (or, how they selected and what values they used in their simulations), how the adjacency matrices were binarized (to create true and false positives for their evaluation, l. 308), or how sensitive the method was to the level of noise they tested (l.300), but overall, I consider the article a complete work.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written.

Originality
To my knowledge, the proposed method is a new one. The relevant literature is adequately referenced.

Significance
The results are potentially important for any lab performing calcium imaging on cell assemblies. As the authors show, the parallel optimization of assembly assignment and time courses of interest at each layer improves the robustness of the estimates. This method should be extremely valuable for any experiment that can estimate the roughly consistent activity of neurons within an assembly.

Another advantage is the flexibility of imposing prior knowledge on any layer of detail. (It is unfortunate that the method did not seem to work well without starting with a lexicon obtained from Adina. In principle, it should be possible to include the knowledge that Adina uses at the 0th layer of the estimate..)

An interesting, technically sound article, potentially valuable for anyone working with structural data (i.e., calcium imaging of neural assemblies).
