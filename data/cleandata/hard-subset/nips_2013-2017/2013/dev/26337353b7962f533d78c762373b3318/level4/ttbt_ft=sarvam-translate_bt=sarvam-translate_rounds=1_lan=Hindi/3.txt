The authors present an algorithm for learning multi-level sparse representation. It is modeled as a (penal) low-rank matrix factorization problem where the number of factors is typically greater than 2 and each factor represents a distinct unit in the hierarchy. The factors are estimated using standard block coordinate descent methods where each factor is optimized while others are kept at the beginning and a penalty promoting sparsity is used at each step.

The method is discussed in the context of analyzing large-scale calcium imaging data where the assumption is that neurons can be collected into a set of neuronal assemblies that give rise to hierarchical pixels --> neurons --> assemblies. The goal is then to estimate the location of each neuron (in terms of pixels), the membership of each neuronal assembly, and the spikes of each assembly.

The authors argue that their method performs better than other methods in synthetic (but realistic) data and can correctly identify neural assemblies in real data.

Novelty
The ideas presented in this paper are, to my knowledge, novel. The three-way decomposition approach is interesting and I liked the fact that the authors also include a standard two-way decomposition in their model and apply consistency between the two. This additional step can make the algorithm more robust for events like a neuron "missing" a spike in an assembly.

Quality
My main concern about this paper is efficiency. Large-scale calcium imaging experiments typically involve tens of thousands of pixels and thousands of timesteps. Although multi-level decomposition significantly reduces the dimension, and many steps of the algorithm can be parallelized, I can imagine that the algorithm could still be quite slow and require a large number of iterations. These points should be discussed.

I am also not convinced by the use of neural assemblies. It is not clear that this is something that will often appear in practice. Furthermore, if a subset of neurons has similar spikes, this will be reflected in the temporal component of the spatial-temporal matrix. The rank of the matrix will still be small and I see no direct benefit from including the proposed multi-level hierarchical decomposition. The authors will probably need to expand their opinion on this issue.

In addition, the various regularization weights \omega_u, \omega_a should be clarified. For example, what are the prior assumptions of convex cell size that are used?

The authors leave the choice of regularization weights to the user. This seems like an important task because the number of regularization weights is large and their use is significant. The authors claim that accurate prior estimates of the rank are not very important because the algorithm will shrink non-relevant components to zero. Although this seems intuitive, I can imagine that this behavior depends heavily on the correct choice of various sparsity penalties that will shrink non-relevant components.

Clarity
I think the paper is not written clearly at all and is only accessible to an expert in calcium imaging data analysis. For example, the authors very briefly mention in the results section how neural spikes are translated into calcium activity through a convolution operator and do not discuss this central aspect of spike deconvolution at all in their algorithm. In fact, I think they have "omitted all the moments overlapping by more than 20%" (if I understand correctly). This is an active research area (e.g., Vogelstein et al. 2010, Greve et al. 2010) and I would like to see how the authors relate to that literature.

I found the inclusion of the main equations as a figure to be a bit unusual, although this is also a question of style.

Finally, I would prefer a more in-depth presentation of real data experiments. Although video cannot be easily presented in a paper, the results shown in Figure 6 could be more convincing. In general, Figure 6 needs better explanation. An interesting approach, but the paper needs significant clarification on some important issues.
