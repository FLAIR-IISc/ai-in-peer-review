This paper considers a scenario where several relevant linear bandits are connected by underlying graphs. It assumes that the weight vectors of the corresponding nodes are smooth (close to neighbors) on the graph. This seems like a good and sufficiently rich model for recommendations in social networks.

The algorithm runs classical linear bandits on each node (which node is being tried is determined by nature), but then shares this information with other nodes so that they can also appropriately update their model's estimates and confidence widths via Laplace.

Unfortunately, the given regret analysis is disappointing. The authors actually provide an analysis for a different algorithm that they propose and evaluate in the experiment. This might be acceptable, but I couldn't find any reason for it. There is no discussion on how the algorithm, its evaluation, and deployment will change from CB vs. TCB. At least the differences between TCB and CB should be discussed better than using the results from [1]. So it is possible that the TCB-type algorithm does not perform well in the experiment or that it is not possible to guarantee learning for the CB-type.

Perhaps it is better not to include such an analysis.

Regarding the current analysis, it is not clear how large the values of ln|M_t| and 2L can be. How do they behave with T and n? It is needed to get some idea from the given regret bound. And how does the graph's noise affect these quantities?

Furthermore, the [4,9] analyses for linear bandits generally provide at least a guideline for setting the search parameter \alpha. Unfortunately, we get this from the given analysis.

What is the dependence of the upper bound on alpha? Is it hidden to some extent as mentioned above?

On the other hand, the experiments were done very well. The details are very detailed and well explained. It is also great that the authors have done the experiments on real data and described the practical details to make the experiments reproducible.

Finally, the L186 - L190 paper mentions that through the graph, the reward affects other nodes "to a lesser extent", "in some informative way", and "similar updates" are desired. But I don't see any parameter for "in some way" or "similar" in the algorithm. Can this be done by regularizing L by a multiple of I instead of I? In the experimental section, the authors mention that clustering works as a regularizer. Wouldn't not regularizing the Laplacian be a more principled way to achieve this regularization?

Other comments
- Why L(u1,...un) in the regret bound and not the regularized version, since the Laplacian is regularized in the algorithm?
After the protest: Although this is a good job, I think it is misleading to include a theoretical analysis of a modified algorithm despite the fact that there is not a completely clear relationship between the two. I would have increased my score if the theoretical analysis had been removed.

This paper extends the relevant bandits to a setting where different bandits share their weight-vectors through the given graph (useful in the social network setting). Very good experiment, but the given analysis does not seem to be helpful and useful.
