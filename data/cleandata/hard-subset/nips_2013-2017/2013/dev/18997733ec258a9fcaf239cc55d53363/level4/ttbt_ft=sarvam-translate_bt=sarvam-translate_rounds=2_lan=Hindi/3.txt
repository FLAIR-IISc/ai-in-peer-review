This research paper examines a situation where multiple relevant linear bandits are connected by an underlying graph. The assumption is that the weight vectors of the corresponding nodes are smooth on the graph (close to neighbors). This seems like a good and sufficiently rich model for recommendations in social networks.

The algorithm runs the classical relevant linear bandit on each node (which node is being tried is determined by nature), but then it also shares this information with other nodes, so that they can also appropriately update their model estimates and confidence widths through the Laplacian.

Unfortunately, the provided regret analysis is disappointing. The authors have actually provided analysis for a different algorithm, which they propose and evaluate in experiments. This may be acceptable, but I found no reason for it. There is no discussion about how the CB vs. TCB algorithm will change its evaluation and deployment. At least better discussion should be done than using the results of [1] on the difference between TCB and CB. Therefore, it is possible that the TCB-type algorithm does not perform well in experiments or that it is not possible to guarantee learning for the CB-type.

Perhaps it would be better not to include such analysis.

Regarding the current analysis, it is not clear how large the ln|M_t| and 2L terms can be. How do they behave with T and n? It is necessary to get some information from the provided regret limit. And how does graph noise affect these quantities?

Furthermore, the analysis for linear bandits [4,9] generally provides a guideline to set the exploration parameter \alpha to a minimum. Unfortunately, we get this from the provided analysis.

What is the dependence of the upper bound on alpha? Is it hidden in some of the quantities mentioned above?

On the other hand, the experiments are considered very good. The details are very detailed and well explained. It is also very good that the authors conducted experiments on real data and described practical details to reproduce the experiments.

Finally, the L186 - L190 paper mentions that through the graph, the reward is "to a lesser extent", "somewhat informative" and "equally updated" other nodes that are desired. Although I do not see any parameter for "somewhere" or "equally" in the algorithm. Can this be done by regularizing L by I instead of I multiple times? In the experimental section, the authors mentioned that clustering acts as a regularizer. Would not regularizing the Laplacian be a more theoretical way to achieve this regularization?

Other comments
- Why is it L(u1,...un) in the regret limit and not the regularized version, since the Laplacian algorithm is regular?
After rebuttal: While this is a good job, I believe that including a theoretical analysis of a modified algorithm with a completely clear relationship between the two confuses it. I would increase my score if the theoretical analysis is removed.

This paper extends the relevant bandit to a setting where different bandits share their weight-vectors through the provided graph (useful in social network settings). Very good experiments, but the provided analysis is disappointing and does not seem useful.
