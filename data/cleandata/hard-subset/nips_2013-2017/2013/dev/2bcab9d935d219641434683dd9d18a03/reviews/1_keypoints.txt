- Paper compares two fast-inference algorithms (variational and sampling) for estimating linear mixtures of odors in generative olfaction models.  
- Simulations show both algorithms perform well and suggest rapid inference (~100 ms) for detecting present odors.  
- Eliminating false positives takes much longer and becomes difficult when more than two odors are present.  
- Because the inference schemes are fundamentally different, the authors claim experiments could distinguish which strategy the brain uses.  
- Manuscript is well written, mathematically dense but technically sound, and makes realistic problem assumptions.  
- Major concern: timing claim is questionable because the differential equations include nonlinearities (log, exp) that may be infeasible for neural circuits to compute with required precision in the claimed time.  
- Similar timing/implementation concerns apply to the sampling-based algorithm; a realistic neural implementation likely worsens performance beyond the authors’ brief caveat.  
- Unclear how to evaluate or distinguish the two models experimentally, especially if implemented as spike-based networks; the manuscript’s discussion on required measurements is vague.  
- Overall: technically interesting work but doubtful as a convincing explanatory model of olfaction in the brain.  
- Minor comments: improve Figure 1 visualization (use log vertical axis, avoid full-page stretch) and fix typos (“auxillary”, remove full stop before Eq.3.14, “for each odour” before Eq.3.17).
