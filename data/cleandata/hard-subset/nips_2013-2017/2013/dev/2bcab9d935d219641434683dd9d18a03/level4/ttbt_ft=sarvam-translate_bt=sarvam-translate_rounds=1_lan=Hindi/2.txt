This paper explores two competing hypotheses regarding how the olfactory system can estimate the presence and concentration of odors, given the firing rates of olfactory receptor neurons responding to a complex, mixed scene. A simple probabilistic model is defined, and two estimation algorithms are proposed: one based on variance estimation and the other based on sampling. Dynamic update rules are derived to prepare these algorithms in a neuro- प्रशंसनीय manner. The biological feasibility of these two algorithms is then assessed using simulated data. The authors conclude that either the algorithms can theoretically operate within natural time constraints, and that methods can be distinguished based on observed firing patterns.

This paper focuses on a popular topic in the NIPS and broader computational neuroscience community, namely what and how probabilistic reasoning and computation can be performed in neural systems. The authors extend a probabilistic model for olfaction presented in NIPS 2012 (Beck et al. [1]), introducing a spike-and-slab prior on odor concentration, and introduce two new estimation algorithms. The Bayesian estimate in these models is non-trivial, so developing a neuro- प्रशंसनीय implementation of such algorithms is a challenge. The authors use a variety of interesting approaches to the problem of olfactory estimation and develop novel and practical proposals.

The primary conclusion of this paper is that both variance estimation and sampling can theoretically work, but the authors have provided very little corroborative biological evidence to suggest that either is actually employed in real systems, or that the implementation-level requirements imposed by these algorithms are reasonable expectations for biological systems. Another assessment of the computational and physical constraints imposed by these algorithms is left open for future work.

The estimation algorithms proposed here are quite different from other sampling-based approaches and contribute interesting ideas to the growing literature on probabilistic estimation in neural circuits. However, it remains to be seen whether biological or experimental evidence can be found for probabilistic estimation in olfactory systems. This paper builds on existing work by introducing a more appropriate probabilistic model for olfaction and two new estimation algorithms. Despite being technically sound and theoretically interesting, the biological constraints required to realize these algorithms in neural systems are substantial.
