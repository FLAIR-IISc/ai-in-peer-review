- Paper compares two fast inference algorithms (variational inference and sampling) for identifying linear mixtures of odors in olfactory generative models.
- Simulations show both algorithms perform satisfactorily and authors claim inference can be done quickly (≈100 ms), but removing false positives—especially with >2 odors—is harder and slower.
- Because the two schemes are fundamentally different, experimental work could determine which approach the brain uses.
- Manuscript is well-structured and mathematically robust, with realistic assumptions, though the math is dense.
- Major concern: the brain-like timing claim depends on time constants in Eq. 3.10, yet nonlinear operations (log, exp) in the dynamics raise doubts that neural circuits can perform them with required precision and speed.
- Similar timing/implementation concerns apply to the sampling-based algorithm and may be even more severe.
- Unclear how to evaluate or distinguish the models experimentally, particularly for spike-based implementations—discussion lacks specific measurable predictions.
- Overall verdict: technically engaging but its suitability as an explanatory model for olfaction in the brain is questionable.
- Minor comments: use a logarithmic vertical axis and resize Figure 1; correct typo "auxillary" after Eq. 3.11; remove erroneous period before Eq. 3.14; fix spelling/consistency for "odour" before Eq. 3.17.
