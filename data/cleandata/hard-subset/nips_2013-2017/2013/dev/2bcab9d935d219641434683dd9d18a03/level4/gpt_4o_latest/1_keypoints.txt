- Paper studies two rapid-inference algorithms for olfactory generative models: one variational, one sampling-based.  
- Simulations show both methods infer odors quickly (claimed within ~100 ms).  
- Eliminating false positives is much slower and worsens when more than two odors are present.  
- Authors argue experimental data could distinguish whether the brain uses sampling or variational inference.  
- Manuscript is well written; mathematical framework is dense but technically robust and assumptions are appropriate.  
- Primary concern: timing claims rely on chosen neural time constants, but complex nonlinearities (log/exponential) in the dynamics make it doubtful neural systems could compute them with required precision within that timeframe.  
- This timing/implementation concern applies to the sampling algorithm as well and may be more severe than acknowledged.  
- Secondary concern: discussion lacks clarity on specific experimental measurements that would differentiate sampling vs variational implementations, especially for spike-based networks.  
- Conclusion: technically strong work, but its plausibility as an explanation of actual olfactory neural processing is uncertain.  
- Minor comments: figure 1 curves hard to read (suggest log vertical axis and layout changes); three typos—"auxillary"→"auxiliary", remove full stop before Eq. 3.14, "odour"→"odor" before Eq. 3.17.
