This paper considers two competing hypotheses for how firing rates of olfactory neurons can be used to estimate the presence and concentration of complex, mixed odors. A general probabilistic model is defined, and two estimation algorithms are proposed: one based on variational approximation and the other based on sampling. Dynamic update rules are developed to formulate these algorithms in a neural-dependent way. The biological feasibility of these two algorithms is evaluated using simulated data. The authors conclude that both algorithms can potentially work within the constraints of natural time, and the methods can be distinguished based on the observed firing patterns.

This paper focuses on a popular theme in NIPS and the broader computational neuroscience community, namely whether probabilistic reasoning and computation can be performed in neural systems and how it can be done. The authors presented a probabilistic model for olfaction (based on spike-and-slab prior odor concentrations) at NIPS 2012 (Beck et al. [1]) and introduced two new estimation algorithms. Bayesian estimates are non-trivial in these models, so creating a neural-dependent implementation of such algorithms is a challenge. The authors use various interesting methods for the problem of estimating olfaction and offer new and insightful suggestions.

The primary conclusion of this paper is that both variational approximation and sampling can potentially work, but the authors provide very little biological evidence of which is actually used in real systems, or whether the implementation-level requirements imposed by these algorithms are reasonable expectations for biological systems. Further evaluation of the computational and physiological constraints imposed by these algorithms is left open for future work.

The proposed estimation algorithms here are quite different from other sampling-based methods and add interesting ideas to the growing literature on probabilistic estimation in neural circuits. However, it remains to be seen whether biological or experimental evidence is available for probabilistic estimation in olfactory systems. This paper builds on existing work by introducing more reasonable probabilistic models and two new estimation algorithms for olfaction. Although technically sound and theoretically appealing, the biological constraints required to implement these algorithms in neural systems are considerable.
