This study discusses two algorithms for rapid inference in generative models of olfaction. Their goal is to calculate the most likely, linear combination of odors that are integrated by the olfactory stimulus. One of the algorithms uses variational inference, while the other is based on a sampling scheme. Simulations demonstrate that both algorithms perform reasonably well, and the authors claim that rapid inference can be achieved within the first 100 ms, while false positives (identifying odors that are not present in a specific stimulus) take much longer to resolve, and it is difficult when more than two odors are present. Since the inference methods are fundamentally different, it can be concluded that in an experiment, it will be possible to determine which of these options the brain will use.

The manuscript is well-written and touches on an interesting topic. The mathematical section is dense, but technically sound and makes realistic assumptions about the problems the olfactory system must solve.

The most problematic issue is the claim that inference can be achieved in the same short time as in the brain. This is based on the time constant introduced in equation 3.10, which is chosen to match the neural time constant. However, there is a lot of non-linearity (log and exp) on the right side of these DECs - and it is not clear whether a neural system will be able to perform these operations with the required accuracy at the time given by this time constant. Similar comments apply to the second, sampling-based algorithm. The authors state in the discussion that a realistic neural implementation would likely reduce performance, but I think the problems with time will be more serious.

Another issue that is partially unclear is the evaluation of the two models against experimental data, especially if both models are implemented as spike-based networks. Here, the discussion is somewhat vague - what exactly needs to be measured to differentiate between sampling and variational inference hypotheses?

In summary, I think the manuscript is technically very interesting, but it is doubtful whether it provides a suitable method for explaining olfaction in the brain.
