This study examines two algorithms for rapid inference in a generative model of olfaction. Their goal is to calculate the most likely, linear mixture of odors containing a olfactory stimulus. One of the algorithms uses variance estimation, while the other is based on a sampling plan. Simulations show that both algorithms perform reasonably well, and the authors claim that the inference is rapidly made within the first 100 ms, while false positives (detecting odors that are not present in a particular stimulus) take much longer to eliminate, and it is difficult when there are more than two odors present. Since the inference schemes are fundamentally different, it is concluded that in an experiment, it would be possible to determine which of these options is used by the brain.

The manuscript is well-written and touches on an interesting topic. The mathematical section is dense but technically sound, and it makes realistic assumptions about the problem of solving the olfactory system.

The most problematic issue is the claim that the inference can be made within the same short time interval as the brain. This is based on the time constants presented in equation 3.10, which are chosen to match the neural time constants. However, the right-hand side of these DEQs has several non-linearities (log and exp) - and it is unclear whether a neural system will be able to perform these tasks with the required precision in the time given by these time constants. Similar comments apply to the second, sample-based algorithm as well. The authors state in the discussion that a realistic neural implementation would probably reduce performance, but I think the problems will become even more serious over time.

Another issue that is partially unclear is the evaluation of the two models against experimental data, especially if both models are implemented as spike-based networks. Here, the discussion is a bit vague - what exactly should be measured to distinguish between the sample and variance estimation hypotheses?

In summary, I think the manuscript is technically very interesting, but whether it provides a suitable approach to explaining olfaction in the brain is questionable.
