This study discusses two algorithms for rapid estimation in generative models of smell. Their goal is to calculate the most likely, linear combination of smells that are united by a olfactory stimulus. One of the algorithms uses variational estimation, while the other is based on a sample scheme. Simulations show that both algorithms perform reasonably well, and the authors claim that rapid estimation can be achieved within the first 100 ms, while false positives (identifying smells that are not present in a specific stimulus) take much longer to resolve, and it is difficult when more than two smells are present. Since the estimation methods are fundamentally different, it can be concluded that it will be possible to determine which of these options the brain will use in an experiment.

The manuscript is well-written and touches on an interesting topic. The mathematical section is dense, but technically sound and makes realistic assumptions about the problems that need to be solved by the olfactory system.

The most problematic issue is whether estimation can be achieved in the same short time as the brain. This is based on the time constant introduced in equation 3.10, which is chosen to match the neural time constant. However, there is a lot of non-linearity (log and exp) on the right of these DECs - and it is not clear whether a neural system will be able to perform these operations with the required accuracy at the time given by this time constant. Similar comments apply to the second, sample-based algorithm. The authors state in the discussion that a realistic neural implementation would likely reduce performance, but I think the time problems would be more serious.

Another problem that is partially unclear is the evaluation of the two models against experimental data, especially if both models are implemented as spike-based networks. The discussion here is somewhat unclear - what exactly needs to be measured to distinguish between the assumptions of the sample and variational estimation?

In summary, I think the manuscript is technically very interesting, but it is doubtful whether it provides a suitable method for explaining smell in the brain.
