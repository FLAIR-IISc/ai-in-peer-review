- Paper compares variational inference and sampling-based methods for decoding odors from olfactory neural activity.
- Authors propose biologically plausible neural network implementations for both algorithms.
- Introduces a realistic spike-and-slab prior for odor concentrations, addressing a prior gap.
- Both algorithms infer odors within ~100 ms in simulations, matching behavioral timescales.
- Algorithms differ in assumptions about neural connectivity and coding, yielding testable experimental predictions.
- Claim that distinguishing algorithms experimentally could reveal how the brain represents probabilities.
- Strength: mathematically rigorous derivations building on prior work (e.g., Beck et al., 2012).
- Strength: biologically relevant assumptions and network dynamics aligned with ecological timescales.
- Strength: clear, well-organized manuscript accessible to computational neuroscience readers.
- Strength: significant contribution with experimentally testable predictions.
- Weakness: timing claims may be optimistic given nonlinearities (digamma, logs) and noise requirements for sampling.
- Weakness: experimental validation is vague; spike-based network comparisons to data are lacking.
- Weakness: both algorithms have difficulty eliminating false positives in complex mixtures.
- Weakness: figure clarity problems (indistinguishable curves) and minor typographical errors.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address timing claims, experimental comparisons, and presentation issues.
- Arguments for acceptance: technical rigor, biological relevance, novel prior, and testable predictions.
- Arguments against acceptance: questionable timing realism, underdeveloped experimental evaluation, and persistent false-positive issues.
- Overall: meaningful contribution likely to stimulate further research in computational neuroscience.
