- Proposes two approximate inference algorithms (variational and sampling) for odor presence from receptor activations.
- Derives neural implementations of both algorithms using a more realistic prior over odors.
- Main claim: both algorithms can infer correct odors in under 100 ms, making them plausible brain mechanisms.
- Strong theoretical analysis and simulations support the algorithms' effectiveness.
- Provides clear, detailed explanations of neural dynamics and nonlinearities required.
- Simulations show rapid convergence to true odor concentrations and differing predictions about neural activity and probability representation.
- Strength: clear writing, thorough analysis, and detailed comparison of the two algorithms.
- Limitation: assumes a simple generative model that may not capture real-world odor complexity.
- Limitation: requires relatively complicated nonlinearities that may be difficult to realize biologically.
- Limitation: relies on reciprocally connected neurons, which may not be straightforward in real neural networks.
- Novelty: uses a more realistic prior and provides neural derivations that improve on prior work.
- Significance: advances understanding of olfactory inference and could inform artificial olfactory systems and other sensory models.
- Pro-acceptance arguments: well-supported analysis, fast and effective algorithms, new mechanistic insights, potential practical implications.
- Con-acceptance arguments: oversimplified generative model, challenging biological implementation, questionable connectivity assumptions.
- Overall assessment: well-written, well-organized, and valuable contribution despite noted limitations.
