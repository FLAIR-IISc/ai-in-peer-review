- Proposes two biologically plausible algorithms (variational inference and sampling) to infer odors from receptor activity and maps them onto neural dynamics.
- Introduces a spike-and-slab prior to model sparse natural odor distributions, improving ecological realism over smooth priors.
- Both algorithms infer odors within ~100 ms in simulations, matching behavioral timescales and showing similar speed and accuracy.
- Algorithms make different assumptions about neural connectivity and coding, producing experimentally distinguishable predictions about neural activity.
- Biological plausibility is strengthened by mapping mechanisms onto known olfactory features (divisive normalization, reciprocal connectivity).
- Provides clear, testable experimental predictions that could distinguish the two algorithms.
- Simulations are thorough, exploring various conditions (number of odors, time courses) and comparing to human behavior (e.g., false positives).
- Generative model simplifications (linear concentration addition, binary weights, Poisson noise) limit applicability to real olfactory complexity.
- Neural implementations require complex nonlinear computations (e.g., logarithms, digamma functions) that may be biologically challenging to realize.
- Assumes the network already knows odor-to-receptor mappings and priors, not addressing how these are learned.
- Lacks detailed guidance on experimental feasibility and practical steps to identify brain regions for testing predictions.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to better address neural implementation feasibility and provide a clearer experimental roadmap.
- For acceptance: novel problem, plausible algorithms, testable predictions, and simulations aligned with behavior.
- Against acceptance: model simplifications, biologically difficult computations, and omitted learning mechanisms.
- Overall: a valuable contribution likely to stimulate further work on olfactory inference and probabilistic neural coding.
