Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses a fundamental but under-explored question in domain adaptation for visual recognition: how to discover domains when “domain = dataset” is a poor proxy. The authors propose to reshape datasets into latent domains by enforcing two axiomatic properties: (i) maximum distinctiveness, measured as the sum of pairwise distribution discrepancies between domains via RKHS mean embeddings (i.e., an MMD-like distance), and (ii) maximum learnability, operationalized by domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to select the number of domains K. The core optimization maximizes the sum of pairwise squared MMDs over assignments, subject to a label prior constraint (LPC) that preserves per-class proportions within each domain. They further propose “conditional reshaping” of test data to align test latent domains with discovered training domains. Empirical results on Office+Caltech object recognition and IXMAS action recognition show that discovered domains can outperform both original dataset-based domains and the latent-domain model of Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12) [20], and that conditional test reshaping improves cross-view action recognition.

The paper builds on and extends several NIPS-relevant lines: kernel two-sample tests and MMD (Gretton et al., NIPS’07), theoretical perspectives on domain adaptation (Ben-David et al., NIPS’06/’07), multi-source adaptation (Mansour et al., NIPS’08), the practical success of simple feature augmentations (Daumé III, ACL’07) and transfer component analysis (Pan et al., TNN’11). Compared to Hoffman et al. [20], who use a hierarchical GMM to discover latent visual domains, the present work offers a nonparametric, kernel-embedding-based alternative, and adds a learnability-driven model selection criterion.

Quality
Strengths
- Well-motivated objective: Using MMD to encourage domain distinctiveness is sensible and principled, and the LPC is a practical device to discourage degenerate “per-category” clusters.
- Concrete, albeit approximate, optimization: The relaxed quadratic program is simple to implement, uses only kernels and linear constraints, and appears to work in practice on standard benchmarks.
- Sensible model selection: DWCV ties the unsupervised domain discovery to the end task (discriminative performance), and is more aligned with adaptation utility than purely unsupervised criteria.
- Useful test-time extension: Conditional reshaping of test data to align with training domains is a realistic transductive step for many visual adaptation settings.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Formulation clarity and correctness: The relaxation introduces βmk = zmk/Mk with constraints βk ∈ Δ (sum over m equals 1 per domain), and a per-instance bound 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C motivated by Mk ∈ [C, M]. This mapping is awkward and breaks the tight link between β and true assignments. It is unclear whether these bounds are necessary/sufficient to avoid near-empty domains or enforce Mk ≥ C. A more transparent relaxation (e.g., soft assignment variables with ∑k αmk = 1 and explicit group-size constraints) would be preferable.
- Risk of degeneracy and imbalance: The objective monotonically increases with K and, for fixed K, still favors extreme partitions. There is no explicit regularizer for balanced domain sizes beyond the loose bounds. DWCV mitigates K-selection but not within-K imbalance. Reported domain sizes and sensitivity to the bounds (and δ in LPC) are missing.
- LPC realism and tightness: The LPC enforces class-proportion invariance across domains with δ set to 1%. In real visual corpora, class frequencies can legitimately vary across latent domains (e.g., certain viewpoints or environments are correlated with certain categories). Such a tight constraint may bias the discovered domains. A study varying δ and ablations without LPC would clarify its role.
- Optimization tractability and reproducibility: Maximizing an indefinite quadratic subject to linear constraints is NP-hard. The paper mentions using “off-the-shelf solvers” without specifying solver, initialization, stopping criteria, or runtime. Kernel methods on M up to several thousands require care (O(M^2) memory). Approximation strategies (Nyström/randNets) and complexity analysis would strengthen the case.
- Evaluation protocol: 
  - The “oracle” evaluation (max over source domains) is optimistic; while mitigated by ENSEMBLE/MATCHING, more details are needed on aggregation, hyperparameter tuning, and whether target unlabeled data are used for selection. 
  - Conditional reshaping assumes access to all test features (transductive). This should be clearly stated and compared to a non-transductive baseline.
  - Comparisons to [20] should ensure matched K and selection procedures; additional baselines like kernel k-means with class-proportion constraints, spectral clustering in RKHS, or DP mixtures would be informative.
- Sensitivity: Results depend on kernel choice and bandwidth (fixed to 2×median distance). A sensitivity analysis would increase confidence.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well structured, with intuitive motivation and helpful qualitative analyses. However, some notation is confusing (e.g., M′k in Eq. (1)), and the derivation of constraint (5) from Mk bounds is not fully convincing. The conditional reshaping optimization is deferred to supplementary; a brief in-paper summary would help. Please specify classifiers used in DWCV and adaptation baselines, and provide algorithmic pseudo-code.

Originality
The combination of kernel two-sample distances to drive domain distinctiveness, a label prior constraint to avoid trivial per-class clusters, and learnability-driven model selection is novel and well-argued. The conditional reshaping of test data to align to training domains is a useful and original addition. The work is distinct from prior latent-domain discovery [20] by its nonparametric formulation.

Significance
The problem is important: dataset ≠ domain is a pervasive issue in visual adaptation, and tools to discover latent structure can benefit both algorithm design and dataset construction. Empirical improvements on standard benchmarks suggest practical value. Broader impact could be substantial if scalability and robustness are addressed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and refine the relaxation: Consider soft assignment variables with ∑k αmk = 1, explicit constraints/penalties for minimum domain size and class coverage, and an entropy or balance regularizer.
- Analyze sensitivity to kernel choice/bandwidth and δ in LPC; report domain-size distributions.
- Provide optimization details, computational complexity, and scalability strategies (Nyström, random features, block-coordinate ascent).
- Strengthen baselines and fairness: compare to kernel k-means with label-proportion constraints, spectral clustering, and more recent multi-source methods; align K-selection across methods.
- Make the transductive assumption explicit for conditional reshaping; evaluate with and without access to full test set at once; report how well discovered test domains align with ground-truth views.
- Release code and supplements to support reproducibility.

Pros
- Addresses an important gap: discovering latent domains rather than equating datasets with domains.
- Principled nonparametric criterion (MMD) and task-aligned model selection (DWCV).
- Demonstrated benefits on object and action recognition; insightful qualitative analyses.

Cons
- Relaxation and constraints are somewhat ad hoc and under-justified; potential degeneracies not fully ruled out.
- Limited sensitivity analyses and scalability discussion.
- Comparisons and evaluation protocols could be strengthened; conditional reshaping assumes transductive access.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept if the authors clarify the optimization, add sensitivity/scalability analyses, and tighten the evaluation; otherwise borderline. The problem and approach are compelling and likely to stimulate follow-up work.
