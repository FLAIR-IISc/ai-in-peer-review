Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a common but underexplored issue in visual domain adaptation: datasets are often heterogeneous mixtures of conditions (e.g., pose, illumination, background) that do not align with the discrete “domains” most adaptation algorithms assume. The authors propose an unsupervised method to reshape datasets into latent domains that satisfy two axioms: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be as different in distribution as possible) and maximum learnability (each domain should support learning a strong classifier). Distinctiveness is formalized using a kernel two-sample measure (distance between mean embeddings in an RKHS; closely related to maximum mean discrepancy), summed over all domain pairs. The assignment of instances to K domains is posed as an integer program with constraints, including a “label prior constraint” (LPC) enforcing that each domain’s class proportions match the global proportions; this is relaxed to a continuous quadratic program. The number of domains K is selected via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV), choosing K that maximizes a weighted average of within-domain validation accuracies. The method is also extended to “conditional reshaping” of test data by matching latent test domains to discovered training domains via a convex QP.

Empirically, the approach is evaluated on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and cross-view human action recognition (IXMAS). The method’s discovered domains yield higher (oracle and practical) adaptation accuracies than using original dataset groupings and than the latent domain model of Hoffman et al. [20], which uses parametric mixtures. The use of geodesic flow kernel adaptation [4] provides the adaptation mechanism after domain discovery.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Formulation: The distinctiveness objective is well-motivated and grounded in RKHS mean embeddings [21]. The LPC appropriately counteracts the trivial tendency of appearance-based clustering to separate categories rather than factors of nuisance, by preserving global class priors within each domain.
- Optimization: The relaxed problem maximizes a convex quadratic form under linear constraints, which is NP-hard in general. The paper acknowledges this and uses off-the-shelf solvers, but provides limited details about initialization, convergence behavior, or guarantees on solution quality. The transformation from binary zmk to βmk = zmk/Mk is sensible, but after relaxation, constraints such as 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C are heuristic approximations of combinatorial constraints (Mk not directly represented). The impact of these approximations on solutions is not analyzed.
- Model selection: DWCV is a practical criterion for K, but it relies on labeled data and may be sensitive to data size per domain and N-fold choices. The theoretical justification for the K bound Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C} is heuristic.
- Test-time reshaping: The conditional reshaping objective is plausible and computationally tractable, though details are deferred to the supplement.
- Experiments: The method is tested on established benchmarks, with comparisons to original dataset “domains,” to [20], and to simple baselines (UNION/ENSEMBLE). The “oracle” analysis (max over sources) helps isolate the value of the discovered partitions. However, quantitative tables/variances are not included here; the narrative claims improvements are consistent.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The key ideas (distinctiveness via RKHS distances; learnability via DWCV) are communicated effectively. Some derivations could benefit from added rigor/clarification:
- Explicitly connect the distinctiveness metric to MMD (squared vs. unsquared form).
- Provide the exact convex QP for conditional reshaping in the main text (currently in supplement).
- Clarify the derivation and intuition of constraints in the relaxation (Eq. 5) and discuss their tightness.

Originality
The work targets a timely and underexplored problem—unsupervised discovery of latent domains for visual adaptation. Relative to Hoffman et al. [20], the nonparametric RKHS-based objective avoids assuming mixture forms and is likely more robust to complex real-world data. The LPC and DWCV criteria are practical contributions tailored to the supervised learning setting prevalent in vision datasets. Conditional reshaping of test data to match discovered training domains is a useful extension.

Significance
If broadly applicable, the approach can improve domain adaptation by producing more semantically coherent source and target domains without manual metadata. The reported gains over both original dataset splits and prior latent domain methods suggest tangible benefits. The work may also inform dataset design by revealing dominant nuisance factors.

Strengths
- Clear, principled objective for domain distinctiveness using kernel mean embeddings.
- Practical mechanism (LPC) to prevent class-driven clustering.
- Sensible and easy-to-implement selection of K via DWCV.
- Extension to test-time conditional reshaping with convex optimization.
- Empirical validation on both image and video benchmarks with consistent reported improvements over baselines and [20].
- Insightful qualitative analyses indicating discovered factors (pose, background, object appearance).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Optimization: Maximizing a convex quadratic objective is NP-hard; provide more detail on solver choice, initialization strategies, run-time, and sensitivity to local optima. Consider spectral relaxations or difference-of-convex programming as alternatives, and include ablations on initialization robustness.
- Constraints: The relaxation constraints (especially Eq. 5 bounds and δ = 1% in LPC) are somewhat ad hoc; include sensitivity analyses to δ and constraints’ tightness. Discuss scenarios where class priors legitimately shift across domains and how LPC would affect discovery.
- Scalability: The method requires forming the M×M kernel matrix; discuss memory/time costs and possible approximations (random features, Nyström).
- Evaluation breadth: Include quantitative comparisons with variance/confidence intervals, and more baselines (e.g., clustering with/without LPC, spectral clustering on features). An ablation removing LPC would clarify its necessity.
- Generalization: DWCV may favor larger K when labels are abundant; discuss risk of overfitting and whether validation on a held-out target-like domain changes K.
- Theoretical guarantees: Provide any bounds relating TDD(K) and downstream adaptation performance, or at least empirical correlations across tasks/datasets.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to kernel choice and bandwidth? Is the “twice median distance” heuristic consistently good?
- What is the computational complexity and wall-clock time for datasets of different sizes? Any approximations used?
- How often does DWCV select K exceeding the number of original datasets, and how stable is K across random seeds?
- How does the method behave when class priors genuinely vary across latent domains?
- Can the approach be extended to semi-supervised settings with partially labeled data?

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a meaningful gap in domain adaptation by discovering latent domains without parametric assumptions.
- Conceptually simple, grounded in established kernel methods, and practically implementable.
- Demonstrates consistent improvements on standard vision benchmarks, including a novel and useful test-time reshaping step.
- Provides qualitative insights into nuisance factors underlying datasets.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited optimization details and lack of guarantees for the nonconvex relaxation; potential sensitivity to initialization not analyzed.
- Heuristic constraints and DWCV selection may limit robustness and generality; sensitivity analyses are sparse.
- Scalability concerns due to kernel matrix computations not thoroughly addressed.
- Some methodological details (conditional reshaping objective) relegated to the supplement.
