## Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper tackles the crucial problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition, arguing that equating datasets with domains is a weak proxy for real-world variations. The authors propose a novel approach to *automatically discover* latent domains within datasets, guided by the principles of “maximum distinctiveness” and “maximum learnability.” They formulate this as a nonparametric optimization problem and demonstrate its effectiveness on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks. This work builds upon the established field of domain adaptation (e.g., [2, 4, 9, 10]) but shifts the focus from *adapting* between predefined domains to *discovering* meaningful domains within the data itself.  It also relates to recent work on clustering and subspace learning for domain adaptation [20, 27].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of automatically discovering latent domains is a significant contribution.  Most existing work assumes domains are known or manually defined.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The formulation based on maximizing distinctiveness and learnability provides a solid theoretical grounding for the approach.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over baseline methods, including comparisons to Hoffman et al. [20]. The results on reshaping test datasets are particularly compelling.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method. The inclusion of illustrative examples (Figure 1) is helpful.
* **Completeness:** The authors address both the discovery of the number of domains and the practical application of the discovered domains for adaptation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention optimization, the computational cost of the proposed method, especially for large datasets, isn’t thoroughly discussed.  Scaling to very large datasets could be a concern.
* **Kernel Choice:** The performance is sensitive to the choice of kernel and bandwidth.  A more detailed discussion of kernel selection and its impact would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison to [20] is valuable, a more comprehensive comparison to other relevant domain adaptation techniques (e.g., those based on adversarial learning) would strengthen the paper.
* **DWCV limitations:** The DWCV approach for determining K relies on cross-validation accuracy, which can be noisy and may not always reflect true generalization performance.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in domain adaptation.
* The proposed method is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated.
* The results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing approaches.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the method could limit its scalability.
* The sensitivity to kernel choice and the limitations of DWCV are potential concerns.



**Overall:**

I believe this is a strong paper with significant potential. The novelty of the approach and the promising experimental results outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the computational complexity discussion and expanding the comparison to other relevant domain adaptation methods.
