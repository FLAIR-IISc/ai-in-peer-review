This paper tackles the important problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition by challenging the common practice of equating datasets with domains. The authors argue that real-world visual data is influenced by many factors not readily captured by discrete datasets and propose a method to automatically discover latent domains within datasets, based on the principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The approach utilizes a nonparametric formulation and an efficient optimization procedure, evaluated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses a significant weakness in current domain adaptation research – the often-oversimplified assumption of dataset-as-domain. The idea of automatically discovering latent domains is compelling, and the proposed properties of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability provide a principled framework for this discovery. The use of kernel methods and the optimization procedure appear sound, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, showcasing improvements over baseline methods. The inclusion of experiments reshaping *test* datasets is a particularly strong point, as it addresses a more realistic scenario.  The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a thorough review of related work.  The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations and discuss potential future directions.

However, several points require further attention. Firstly, the explanation of the Label Prior Constraint (LPC) feels a bit hand-wavy. While the intuition is understandable, a more rigorous justification or discussion of its potential impact on the discovered domains would be beneficial. What happens when the LPC is violated? Is the optimization sensitive to its parameters? Secondly, while the authors mention the computational complexity of the optimization, a more detailed analysis, specifically relating to the scalability of the method to very large datasets, is missing. How does the runtime scale with the number of data instances (M) and the number of latent domains (K)?  Thirdly, the choice of Gaussian kernel seems somewhat arbitrary.  A brief investigation of other kernel functions or justification for the Gaussian kernel's suitability would be valuable. 

Furthermore, the results, while positive, could be presented with more statistical rigor. Reporting confidence intervals or p-values for the accuracy improvements would strengthen the claims. It’s not always clear if the improvements are statistically significant. The comparison to Hoffman et al. [20] feels slightly superficial. A more detailed comparison of the underlying assumptions, computational costs, and strengths/weaknesses of both methods is needed. Specifically, how does the nonparametric approach address the distributional assumptions inherent in [20]'s Gaussian mixture model?

The ablation studies are also somewhat lacking. While the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the combined approach, it would be beneficial to see the performance of maximizing distinctiveness *without* maximizing learnability, and vice-versa, to better understand the contribution of each component.  Finally, the qualitative analysis of the identified domains (Figure 1) is helpful, but could be expanded. Showing examples of misclassified images *within* each discovered domain would provide further insights into the characteristics and limitations of the approach.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant gap in domain adaptation research by challenging the dataset-as-domain assumption.
*   Proposes a principled framework for discovering latent domains based on maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical results on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, including improvements over baselines and related work.
*   Addresses a more realistic scenario by considering reshaping test datasets.
*   Clear and well-organized writing.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of detailed analysis of the Label Prior Constraint and its potential impact.
*   Missing scalability analysis and discussion of computational complexity.
*   Limited statistical rigor in reporting results (no confidence intervals/p-values).
*   Superficial comparison to related work (Hoffman et al. [20]).
*   Insufficient ablation studies.
*   Limited qualitative analysis beyond a few example images.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the work and increase its impact.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should focus on providing a more rigorous analysis of the LPC, the computational complexity, and the statistical significance of their results, as well as expanding the comparison to related work and the ablation studies.
