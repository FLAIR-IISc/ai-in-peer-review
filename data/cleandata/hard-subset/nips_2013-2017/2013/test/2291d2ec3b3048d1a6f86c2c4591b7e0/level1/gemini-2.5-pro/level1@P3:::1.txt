This paper tackles a fundamental, yet often overlooked, problem in visual domain adaptation: the definition of a "domain". The authors argue that the common practice of equating entire datasets with single domains is a flawed simplification, as datasets are often heterogeneous mixtures of multiple latent domains corresponding to different visual conditions (e.g., lighting, pose, background). The core contribution is a novel, non-parametric method for automatically discovering or "reshaping" datasets into a set of latent domains that are more suitable for adaptation.

The proposed approach is guided by two principled properties that the discovered domains should possess: 1) **Maximum Distinctiveness**, meaning the underlying distributions of the discovered domains should be maximally different from one another, and 2) **Maximum Learnability**, meaning each domain should contain sufficient and coherent data to train a strong discriminative classifier. To achieve distinctiveness, the authors formulate an optimization problem to maximize the sum of pairwise kernel-based two-sample test statistics (specifically, the MMD) between all domains. A crucial component of this formulation is a Label Prior Constraint (LPC), which enforces that the class distribution within each discovered domain matches the global class distribution, cleverly preventing the algorithm from simply clustering the data by category. To determine the number of latent domains, K, the authors propose a "Domain-Wise Cross-Validation" (DWCV) procedure that selects the K which maximizes the average cross-validated classification accuracy within the discovered domains, directly operationalizing the learnability principle. The method is validated on standard object and action recognition benchmarks, demonstrating that adapting from these discovered domains leads to superior performance compared to adapting from the original, monolithic datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper's primary strength lies in its re-examination of the foundational assumptions of domain adaptation. By challenging the "dataset = domain" paradigm, the work opens up a new and important direction for research. This conceptual contribution is highly significant and could influence how the community approaches dataset collection and usage for adaptation tasks.
2.  **Principled and Sound Methodology:** The proposed framework, based on maximizing distinctiveness and learnability, is intuitive and well-motivated. The use of a non-parametric kernel method to measure domain discrepancy is well-suited for complex visual data, avoiding the potentially restrictive assumptions of parametric models like those in Hoffman et al. [20]. The introduction of the Label Prior Constraint is a key technical insight that makes the distinctiveness objective meaningful by preventing a trivial solution of clustering by class.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The authors demonstrate the superiority of their discovered domains over both the original datasets and a competing method on multiple tasks. The "oracle" experiments effectively upper-bound the potential benefit, and the practical multi-source adaptation experiments show that these benefits can be realized. The extension to "conditional reshaping" of the test set is a compelling addition that showcases the full power of the framework, yielding substantial gains in the challenging cross-view action recognition task.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, the technical approach is developed logically, and the experimental results are presented in a clear and easy-to-interpret manner.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Scalability Concerns:** The proposed method relies on computing and operating on an M x M kernel matrix, where M is the total number of data instances. This scales quadratically in both memory and computation, making it infeasible for the large-scale datasets that are now common in computer vision. The authors should explicitly discuss this limitation and could suggest potential avenues for scaling up the approach, such as kernel approximation techniques (e.g., Nyström method, Random Fourier Features).
2.  **Details of the Optimization:** The paper notes that the relaxed optimization problem (maximization of a convex function) is NP-hard in general but can be handled by existing solvers. However, it lacks crucial details about the specific solver used, initialization strategies, typical running times, and convergence properties. Given the non-convex nature of the problem, the quality of the solution could be sensitive to these factors. Including these details would significantly improve the reproducibility and practicality of the work.
3.  **The Label Prior Constraint (LPC) Assumption:** While the LPC is a clever and necessary component of the model, it rests on the assumption that the underlying class priors are constant across all latent domains. This may not always hold true. For instance, a dataset of "animals" might contain a latent "underwater" domain with a high prior for "fish" and a latent "savannah" domain with a high prior for "lion". It would strengthen the paper to discuss the limitations of this assumption and potential scenarios where it might be violated.
4.  **Selection of K:** The DWCV procedure for selecting the number of domains K is a reasonable heuristic. However, the supporting evidence in Figure 2, while promising, is limited. It's interesting that the chosen K often coincides with the number of original datasets mixed together. A more systematic study of how DWCV behaves under different conditions (e.g., varying degrees of domain mixture, different numbers of true underlying domains) would make the method for choosing K more robust and convincing.

**Final Assessment:**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The strengths—a novel problem formulation, a principled technical approach, and a thorough experimental validation—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to scalability and implementation details that can be addressed in future work. The paper is of high **quality**, is clearly written (**clarity**), presents a highly **original** take on a fundamental problem, and is likely to be of high **significance** to researchers in domain adaptation and transfer learning. It challenges existing practices and provides a practical and effective tool for better leveraging existing data. This work is a clear candidate for acceptance.
