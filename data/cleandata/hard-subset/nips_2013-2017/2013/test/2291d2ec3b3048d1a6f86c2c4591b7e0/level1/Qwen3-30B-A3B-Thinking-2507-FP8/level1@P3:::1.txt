

# Peer Review: Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation

This paper addresses a fundamental gap in domain adaptation research: the common practice of equating datasets with domains, which often fails to capture the complex, overlapping factors (lighting, pose, background, etc.) that create domain shifts in visual recognition. The authors propose a novel method to automatically discover latent domains in visual datasets by enforcing two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing distribution differences between domains) and maximum learnability (ensuring domains support strong discriminative models). They formulate a nonparametric approach using kernel-based domain discrepancy measures, develop an efficient optimization procedure, and validate their method on object and action recognition tasks.

The paper relates well to prior work. It correctly identifies the limitation of existing domain adaptation methods (e.g., Saenko et al. [2]) that assume datasets correspond to discrete domains. While Hoffman et al. [20] also addresses latent domain discovery, their parametric Gaussian mixture approach is contrasted with the authors' nonparametric formulation, which avoids restrictive distributional assumptions. The paper also appropriately distinguishes from clustering work (Jegelka et al. [27]) by focusing on domain discovery rather than unsupervised clustering.

**Strengths:**
1. The problem formulation is well-motivated and addresses a genuine limitation in the field. The distinction between "dataset" and "domain" is crucial but often overlooked.
2. The technical approach is sound: the kernel-based domain discrepancy measure (equation 1) is well-established in domain adaptation literature (Gretton et al. [21]), and the relaxation to convex optimization is practical.
3. The DWCV method for determining the optimal number of domains (Section 2.2) is a thoughtful solution to a non-trivial problem, avoiding the common pitfall of choosing K arbitrarily.
4. The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering multiple datasets (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and tasks (object and action recognition), with convincing results showing improvement over both original datasets and [20]'s approach.
5. The qualitative analysis (Figure 1) effectively demonstrates how the identified domains align with meaningful visual factors (pose, color, background), providing valuable insight into the method's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The label prior constraint (LPC) lacks sufficient theoretical justification. While the authors argue that "relative percentages of different classes are approximately in accordance with a prior distribution that is independent of domains," this is often untrue in real-world datasets (e.g., Caltech-256 has different class distributions than Amazon images). The paper would benefit from empirical validation of this assumption or a discussion of its limitations.
2. The nonparametric claim is somewhat misleading: while they avoid parametric distribution assumptions, they still rely on kernel methods with a bandwidth parameter, which is a form of parametric assumption. A more nuanced discussion of this trade-off would strengthen the paper.
3. The computational complexity is not addressed. For large-scale datasets, the optimization problem (equation 4) may become prohibitively expensive, especially with the N-fold cross-validation in DWCV.
4. The comparison with [20] is limited. While they correctly note [20]'s parametric approach, they don't demonstrate cases where their nonparametric method clearly outperforms [20] due to distributional assumptions.

**Significance and Originality:**
The paper makes a significant contribution by shifting the focus from "how to adapt between domains" to "how to discover meaningful domains." This is a novel perspective that addresses a critical flaw in current domain adaptation practice. The method's ability to reshape datasets into more meaningful domains leads to demonstrable performance improvements (Tables 1-3), suggesting practical utility. While the core idea of maximizing domain differences is related to Jegelka et al. [27], the authors' specific application to domain discovery in visual recognition is original.

**Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to domain adaptation research. The problem is well-motivated, the approach is technically sound, and the results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses (particularly regarding the LPC justification), they do not undermine the core contribution. The paper is clearly written and addresses a significant gap in the field.

**Pro-accept arguments:**
- Addresses a fundamental limitation in domain adaptation research (dataset-domain equivalence)
- Proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to latent domain discovery
- Demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing methods
- Provides valuable insights into the factors driving domain shifts

**Con-accept arguments:**
- LPC justification could be stronger
- Nonparametric claim could be more nuanced
- Computational complexity not discussed

Given the significance of the problem, the novelty of the approach, and the solid experimental validation, I recommend acceptance. The paper advances the field by providing a more principled way to handle domain shifts in visual recognition, and the method has clear potential for practical applications in dataset construction and domain adaptation.
