Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a core gap in visual domain adaptation: datasets are often heterogeneous mixtures of latent domains (e.g., pose, illumination, background), but most methods assume domains are known a priori or equate a dataset with a domain. The authors propose to automatically discover latent domains within image/video datasets by imposing two axioms: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. Distinctiveness is operationalized as maximizing the sum of pairwise distribution discrepancies between domains, measured nonparametrically using kernel mean embeddings (MMD). Learnability is enforced by selecting the number of domains K via domain-wise cross-validation accuracy on labeled source data. The discrete assignment is relaxed to a continuous quadratic program with a label-prior constraint to discourage per-class clustering. They also propose “conditional reshaping” of unlabeled test data to align its latent domains to discovered source domains. Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) show that using discovered domains improves adaptation accuracy over treating each dataset as a single domain and over Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12) latent-domain discovery based on a parametric mixture.

This work connects to NIPS literature on domain adaptation and dataset shift (e.g., Ben-David et al., Mansour et al.), nonparametric two-sample tests and kernel embeddings (Gretton et al.), and multi-source adaptation. Relative to Hoffman et al. (latent domain discovery), this approach is nonparametric (no Gaussian mixture assumption), introduces an explicit learnability criterion to choose K, and considers test-time reshaping.

Strengths
- Well-motivated problem with practical impact: identifying latent domains within heterogeneous visual datasets is important for robust adaptation.
- Conceptually clean nonparametric formulation: maximizing pairwise MMD between domain marginals is a reasonable, distribution-free proxy for distinctiveness.
- Practical mechanism to choose K via learnability (domain-wise CV), avoiding overly fragmented domains that hurt discriminative training.
- Conditional reshaping of test data is a useful extension enabling better alignment without labels.
- Empirical gains: consistent improvements over using original dataset partitions and over a prior latent-domain discovery method; both object and action tasks are covered. Qualitative analyses suggest discovered domains correlate with pose/background factors.

Weaknesses and questions
- Optimization and guarantees: The core objective is a nonconvex quadratic maximization with linear constraints; the relaxation and solver choices are described, but there are no approximation or convergence guarantees, nor analysis of sensitivity to initialization or multiple restarts.
- Label prior constraint (LPC): Assuming identical class priors across domains can be violated in real data and may bias the discovery (e.g., when specific views or conditions co-occur with certain classes). An empirical study of robustness to prior mismatch or an alternative regularizer would strengthen the method.
- Risk of per-class clustering: Although LPC discourages gross label imbalance, the distinctiveness objective on P(x) could still partially align with class structure. An ablation showing class-conditional distinctiveness (e.g., using class-conditional MMD or conditioning on y) vs. LPC alone would clarify.
- Scalability: The method requires storing an M×M kernel matrix and solving a QP with O(MK) variables. There is little discussion of computational cost or strategies for larger datasets (e.g., Nyström features, mini-batches).
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Results rely on a Gaussian kernel with bandwidth set by a heuristic; there’s no study of sensitivity to kernel choice/bandwidth or δ (LPC slack).
- K selection via CV: DWCV may overfit to the labeled source; a held-out target-free criterion is fine here, but better justification or regularization on K (e.g., MDL, stability) would help.
- Evaluation breadth: Improvements are shown, but statistical significance and more baselines (e.g., simple balanced clustering, spectral clustering with domain regularizers) would strengthen claims. Details of the “MATCHING” selection metric for target alignment should be explicit in the main text.

Assessment by NIPS review criteria
- Quality: Technically sound overall with appropriate use of MMD and practical algorithms; however, theoretical guarantees and robustness analyses are limited. The experimental section is solid but could be more thorough on sensitivity, scalability, and significance.
- Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The relaxation and constraints (especially the per-instance constraint 1/M ≤ Σk βmk ≤ 1/C and the mechanics of assigning hard domains) are somewhat nonintuitive and would benefit from more explanation and intuition. Explicit pseudocode for training/test reshaping and K selection would aid reproducibility.
- Originality: Good. Nonparametric latent-domain discovery with a learnability-based model selection and conditional test reshaping is a notable advance over prior parametric approaches and standard cross-dataset adaptation.
- Significance: Moderate to high. If adopted, latent-domain reshaping can improve a wide range of visual adaptation pipelines. Open questions about scalability limit immediate impact on very large datasets, but the idea is timely and broadly useful.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem with clear practical benefits.
- Nonparametric, conceptually simple method with demonstrated empirical gains.
- Novel and useful extension to reshape test data conditionally.
- Bridges multiple strands of NIPS-relevant work (kernel methods, domain adaptation, multi-source learning).

Cons against acceptance
- Limited theoretical and robustness analysis of the optimization and K selection.
- Assumptions (shared class priors) may be unrealistic; sensitivity not evaluated.
- Scalability and computational cost are not fully addressed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide computational complexity, memory usage, and runtime for each dataset; discuss kernel approximations (e.g., random features) to scale up.
- Add ablations: without LPC; with class-conditional MMD; varying kernel bandwidth; varying δ. Report stability across random restarts.
- Evaluate robustness when class priors differ across latent domains; consider a soft prior or divergence penalty instead of hard LPC.
- Clarify the target-domain “MATCHING” metric in the main text and compare to additional baselines (e.g., spectral clustering with label-regularization).
- Report statistical significance and confidence intervals.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper presents a novel, practical approach to a meaningful problem with promising results, but would benefit from deeper analysis of assumptions, optimization robustness, and scalability.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
