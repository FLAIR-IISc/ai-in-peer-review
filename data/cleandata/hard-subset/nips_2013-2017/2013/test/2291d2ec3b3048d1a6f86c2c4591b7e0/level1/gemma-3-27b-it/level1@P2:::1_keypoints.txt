- Proposes automatic discovery of latent domains in image datasets by maximizing distinctiveness and learnability using a nonparametric formulation.  
- Motivated by the weakness of equating datasets with domains and by factors like lighting and pose affecting visual data.  
- Novel contribution: discovering latent domains rather than relying on predefined dataset boundaries, extending prior work (e.g., Hoffman et al. 2013).  
- Theoretical formulation is sound, using kernel methods for distribution comparison and cross-validation for assessing learnability.  
- Optimization and algorithm are clearly explained with readable notation.  
- Experimental validation on object and human activity recognition shows consistent improvements over treating original datasets as domains.  
- Ablation study (reshaping test datasets) is a valuable experimental addition.  
- Completeness: includes domain discovery and a procedure to select the optimal number of domains.  
- Main weakness: potential high computational cost and scalability concerns for large datasets.  
- Kernel bandwidth choice (twice the median distance) is arbitrary and needs more systematic analysis.  
- Possible sensitivity to initialization of the optimization is not discussed or evaluated.  
- Limited comparison to strong, modern baselines—missing deep learning–based domain adaptation methods.  
- Figure quality issue: example images in Figure 1 are small and hard to discern.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a fundamental problem, is novel, theoretically grounded, experimentally validated, and well-written.  
- Arguments against acceptance: computational cost, insufficient strong baseline comparisons, and incomplete analysis of some methodological choices.  
- Overall assessment: valuable contribution warranting acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Recommendation and requested revisions: accept with minor revisions to (1) detail computational complexity and scaling, (2) add a strong deep-learning baseline, (3) analyze kernel bandwidth selection, and (4) improve Figure 1 image clarity.
