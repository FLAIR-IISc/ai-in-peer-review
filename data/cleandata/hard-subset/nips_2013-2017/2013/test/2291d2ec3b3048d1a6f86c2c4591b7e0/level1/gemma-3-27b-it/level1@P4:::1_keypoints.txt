- Proposes a method to automatically discover latent domains in image/video datasets to improve domain adaptation.
- Core idea: partition data to maximize both inter-domain distinctiveness and intra-domain learnability.
- Formulates the problem as a non-parametric optimization and uses kernel methods and a label prior constraint (LPC).
- Evaluated on object recognition and human activity recognition, showing improved performance and benefits from reshaping the test set.
- Shifts focus in domain adaptation literature from “how to adapt” to “what constitutes a domain,” challenging the dataset-as-domain assumption.
- Positions itself relative to prior latent-domain discovery work (e.g., [20]) and connections to unsupervised clustering and discriminative learning.
- Strength — Novelty: addresses an important, underexplored problem for visual recognition where domains are ambiguous.
- Strength — Technical soundness: well-defined non-parametric formulation, motivated optimization, and sensible use of kernels and LPC.
- Strength — Experimental validation: comparisons to existing methods demonstrate advantages and insightful analyses (Figures 1–2).
- Strength — Clarity: well-written, organized, and mathematically clear with helpful optimization explanations.
- Weakness — Computational complexity: potential scalability issues for large datasets; scalability not thoroughly addressed.
- Weakness — Parameter sensitivity: kernel bandwidth and LPC deviation parameter δ may be sensitive; selection and impact not fully discussed.
- Weakness — Limited comparisons: lacks broader direct comparison to domain-adaptation methods that do not use explicit domain discovery.
- Weakness — DWCV details: practical bound for Kmax given but rationale for N-fold cross-validation is missing.
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, technically sound approach, strong experimental support, and clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: concerns about scalability, parameter sensitivity, and insufficient comparative evaluation.
- Overall assessment: strong, valuable contribution with minor weaknesses that do not outweigh merits.
- Recommendation: Accept (NIPS 2013); address scalability and parameter-sensitivity concerns in future revisions.
