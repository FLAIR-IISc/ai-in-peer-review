Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles an underexplored prerequisite to domain adaptation: discovering domains themselves when datasets are heterogeneous mixtures of latent factors (pose, illumination, background, etc.). The authors propose to “reshape” visual datasets into latent domains via two principles: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. Distinctiveness is operationalized by maximizing pairwise discrepancies between domain distributions measured nonparametrically via RKHS mean embeddings (akin to MMD), summing across all domain pairs. To prevent trivial per-class partitions, they enforce a label prior constraint (LPC) requiring approximately the same class proportions in each discovered domain as in the pooled data. The resulting 0–1 assignment is relaxed to continuous weights and solved as a constrained quadratic program; hard assignments follow by argmax. The number of domains K is chosen by domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV), seeking the K that yields the highest within-domain supervised accuracy (weighted by domain size). They further introduce conditional reshaping of the test set to match discovered training domains by minimizing RKHS distances, yielding another quadratic program.

Experiments on Office+Caltech-256 and IXMAS compare against treating datasets as domains and against Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12) latent-domain discovery based on hierarchical Gaussian mixtures. Adaptation uses the geodesic flow kernel (GFK). The proposed method discovers domains that yield higher “oracle” adaptation accuracy (best source per target), competitive multi-source adaptation via simple strategies (UNION/ENSEMBLE/MATCHING), and clear gains when reshaping heterogeneous test sets in cross-view action recognition.

This builds on and complements prior domain adaptation research at NIPS and elsewhere (e.g., Daumé, Blitzer et al., Ben-David et al., Pan et al., Gopalan et al.), and is closest to Hoffman et al.’s latent domain discovery; the present work argues for a nonparametric, discriminability-driven criterion with data-driven K selection.

Strengths:
- Clear and important problem formulation: treating “dataset=domain” as undesirable and proposing a principled, data-driven alternative.
- Nonparametric distinctiveness criterion leveraging kernel mean embeddings avoids restrictive parametric assumptions and directly targets distributional separation.
- Practicality: a workable relaxation and an interpretable, label-aware constraint to counter per-class clustering; a simple yet sensible K-selection method (DWCV).
- Demonstrated impact on downstream adaptation across two settings, including a compelling test-set reshaping scenario for cross-view action recognition.
- Empirical comparisons to relevant baselines, including the most related prior work [Hoffman et al.], with consistent improvements.

Weaknesses:
- Optimization lacks theoretical guarantees: maximizing a convex quadratic under linear constraints is NP-hard; reliance on off-the-shelf solvers without discussion of initialization, convergence behavior, or sensitivity raises reproducibility concerns.
- LPC assumption may be unrealistic: enforcing nearly identical class proportions across domains can distort true domain structure, especially when sampling bias or real class-frequency shifts exist; δ=1% seems arbitrary and potentially brittle.
- Scalability is unclear: kernel matrix scales quadratically in M; the method requires sweeping K with repeated QP solves and N-fold CV; no runtime/memory analysis or approximations (e.g., Nyström, random features).
- Limited ablations/sensitivity: little analysis of bandwidth choice, δ, number of folds, or how much each component (distinctiveness, LPC, DWCV) contributes; KMeans/random partitions are mentioned but not shown.
- Some technical details deferred to supplement (e.g., test-set reshaping program) limit self-contained clarity; the relaxation constraints relating βmk across domains appear somewhat ad hoc and merit further justification.

Quality:
The method is well-motivated and empirically supported on two benchmarks with reasonable protocols. However, the technical core rests on a difficult nonconvex program without guarantees, and the relaxation/constraints are not fully justified theoretically. The evaluation is solid but would benefit from more thorough ablations and statistical testing. The choice of adaptation method (GFK) is reasonable for fair comparison, but reporting robustness across different adapters would strengthen claims.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The definition of TDD and its relation to MMD is understandable. More explanation of the relaxation (why the particular bounds on sum_k βmk, implications of Mk ≥ C) and a brief pseudo-code would improve reproducibility. Including key details of the test-set reshaping in the main text would help. A complexity discussion is missing.

Originality:
The combination of (i) nonparametric, label-aware maximization of domain distinctiveness, (ii) automatic K selection via maximum learnability, and (iii) conditional reshaping of test sets is novel relative to prior mixture-model latent-domain work and generalized clustering. The paper clearly differentiates from [20] and related clustering approaches.

Significance:
Discovering latent domains is practically important and likely to influence future domain adaptation pipelines and dataset curation. The gains over treating datasets as domains, and the demonstrated benefit of reshaping test data, suggest real utility. That said, stronger scalability evidence and broader validation would be needed for widespread adoption.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses a meaningful, underexplored prerequisite to domain adaptation.
- Conceptually clean, nonparametric objective with intuitive constraints.
- Empirical improvements over strong, relevant baselines including prior latent-domain work.
- Test-set reshaping contribution is timely and impactful for heterogeneous test distributions.

Cons for acceptance:
- Nonconvex optimization without guarantees; relaxation details feel ad hoc and under-justified.
- LPC assumption and fixed δ may misrepresent domains; no sensitivity analysis.
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability to larger datasets.
- Some key details moved to supplementary; ablations and statistical rigor could be stronger.

Suggestions to improve:
- Provide a principled derivation and justification for the relaxation constraints; discuss initialization strategies and report optimization stability.
- Add runtime/memory benchmarks and, if needed, kernel approximations for scalability; analyze sensitivity to bandwidth and δ with plots.
- Include ablations removing LPC, varying δ, and swapping the distinctiveness metric to show necessity/sufficiency.
- Evaluate with additional adapters (e.g., TCA, CORAL) to demonstrate robustness, and report statistical significance.
- Consider a semi-supervised/unlabeled variant of LPC (e.g., entropy or conditional independence regularizers) to reduce reliance on strict class-prior equality.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper proposes a timely and useful approach with solid empirical evidence, though the optimization and assumptions would benefit from deeper justification and analysis.
