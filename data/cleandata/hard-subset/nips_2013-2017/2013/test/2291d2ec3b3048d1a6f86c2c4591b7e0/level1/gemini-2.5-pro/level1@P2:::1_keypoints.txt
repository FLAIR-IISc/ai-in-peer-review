- Addresses the definition of a "domain" in visual domain adaptation, arguing that treating entire datasets as single domains is flawed.  
- Proposes an unsupervised method to automatically discover latent domains from one or more source datasets.  
- Introduces two guiding principles: Maximum Distinctiveness and Maximum Learnability for defining domains.  
- Achieves distinctiveness by maximizing pairwise distances between domain distributions in an RKHS via a non-parametric formulation.  
- Frames domain discovery as a relaxed quadratic programming problem with a novel Label Prior Constraint (LPC) enforcing consistent class priors across domains.  
- Determines the number of domains K using domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) as a proxy for learnability.  
- Validated extensively on object and action recognition datasets (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam, IXMAS) showing improvements over dataset-level adaptation.  
- Novel extension: reshaping the unlabeled test set into domains that match discovered source domains to further boost performance.  
- Distinct from prior work (Hoffman et al. ECCV 2012) by using a flexible non-parametric approach instead of assuming Gaussian mixtures.  
- High technical quality: elegant MMD-based formulation and principled criteria, with comprehensive experiments including oracle and multi-source settings.  
- Extremely clear and well-written presentation; qualitative figures effectively illustrate discovered domains.  
- Highly original conceptual contribution: defining domains via abstract properties (distinctiveness, learnability) and reshaping test data.  
- Significant impact potential: challenges a core assumption in domain adaptation and could influence future dataset collection and methods.  
- Weakness: optimization is a maximization of a convex quadratic with linear constraints (generally NP-hard) but solver details, convergence, and initialization sensitivity are omitted.  
- Weakness: the Label Prior Constraint (LPC) is a strong assumption whose general validity is not empirically tested.  
- Weakness: scalability concerns from using a kernel matrix that scales quadratically with data size are not addressed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a fundamental problem, proposes a novel principled framework, shows strong empirical gains, has high impact potential, and is well presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks optimization solver details, has scalability questions, and relies on an untested strong LPC assumption.
