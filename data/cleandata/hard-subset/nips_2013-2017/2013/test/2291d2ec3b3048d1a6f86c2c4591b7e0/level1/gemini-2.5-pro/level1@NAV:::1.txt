This paper addresses the fundamental, yet often overlooked, problem in domain adaptation that equates entire datasets with monolithic "domains." The authors convincingly argue that real-world datasets are often heterogeneous mixtures of latent domains (e.g., varying poses, lighting, backgrounds) and that treating them as single entities is a flawed simplification. The core contribution is a novel, non-parametric method to automatically discover these latent domains from one or more source datasets. The proposed framework is built on two well-motivated principles: maximizing the "distinctiveness" between discovered domains, measured via a kernel-based distribution distance, and ensuring their "learnability" by selecting the number of domains (K) that maximizes domain-wise cross-validation accuracy. This work is most closely related to Hoffman et al. (2012), but differentiates itself by using a non-parametric formulation, which avoids strong distributional assumptions like Gaussian mixtures.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its originality and significance. By tackling the definition of a "domain" itself, it addresses a foundational issue that could reshape how researchers approach domain adaptation tasks. The technical quality is high; the formulation is elegant, and the two guiding principles of distinctiveness and learnability are intuitive and sound. The experiments are thorough, demonstrating on both object and action recognition tasks that adapting from the discovered domains yields significant improvements over using the original datasets. The qualitative analysis provides compelling evidence that the method discovers semantically meaningful partitions of the data. Furthermore, the introduction of "conditional reshaping" for the test set is a valuable and novel extension.

Despite these strengths, the work could be improved by addressing a few points. The Label Prior Constraint (LPC) is a strong assumption, positing that class priors are independent of the domains. While intuitive for some scenarios, the authors should discuss cases where this might be violated (e.g., if a specific object class only appears in a certain background) and the potential impact on the method. Secondly, the optimization for domain assignments is a relaxed non-convex problem. A brief discussion on the sensitivity to initialization or the quality of the solutions found would strengthen the paper's technical claims.

Overall, this is a high-quality, original, and significant contribution. It presents a novel and effective solution to a challenging and important problem in unsupervised learning and computer vision. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed in a final version. The ideas presented are likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers in the field.
