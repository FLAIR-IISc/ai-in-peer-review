## Review of “Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation”

This paper tackles the challenging problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition, arguing that equating datasets with domains is a weak proxy for real-world domain shifts. The authors propose an approach to *automatically discover* latent domains within image/video datasets by optimizing for “maximum distinctiveness” and “maximum learnability.” They formulate this as a non-parametric problem with an efficient optimization procedure and demonstrate its effectiveness on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks.

This work builds on a significant body of research in domain adaptation (e.g., [2, 4, 7, 9, 10]) but distinguishes itself by shifting the focus *from* adaptation algorithms *to* the domains themselves. While existing methods often assume domains are given (or equated to datasets), this paper aims to intelligently reshape datasets into more meaningful domains. The connection to related work, particularly Hoffman et al. [20], is well-discussed, highlighting the novelty of their nonparametric approach and extension to test set reshaping. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of automatically discovering latent domains is a fresh perspective on domain adaptation, moving beyond the limitations of dataset-as-domain assumptions.
*   **Soundness:** The proposed properties of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability provide a principled framework for domain discovery. The optimization procedure appears well-formulated and addresses the inherent challenges.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both object recognition and human activity recognition demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines, showcasing the practical utility of the approach.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The inclusion of test set reshaping and analysis of the identified domains (Figure 1) strengthens the evaluation and provides valuable insights.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention efficiency, the computational cost of the optimization, particularly for large datasets, isn't thoroughly discussed. This could be a limitation in real-world applications.
*   **Kernel Choice:** The reliance on Gaussian kernels might limit the method's applicability to datasets where this kernel is not well-suited. Exploring other kernel options or kernel learning could be beneficial.
*   **DWCV Heuristic:** The Domain-wise Cross-Validation (DWCV) for determining the optimal number of domains feels somewhat heuristic and could benefit from further theoretical justification.
*   **Clarity in Section 2:** The mathematical notation in section 2 is dense and could be improved with more explanatory text and potentially breaking down the optimization process into smaller, more digestible steps.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a crucial, often overlooked aspect of domain adaptation – the definition of domains themselves.
*   The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and grounded in sound principles.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational cost of the method is a potential concern.
*   Some aspects of the methodology (DWCV) feel heuristic.



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with significant potential to impact the field of domain adaptation. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty, soundness, and empirical validation outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and discuss computational considerations.
