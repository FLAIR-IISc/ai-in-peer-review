

# Peer Review: Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation

This paper addresses an important but overlooked problem in domain adaptation research: the common practice of treating entire datasets as single domains, when in reality datasets often contain multiple latent domains (e.g., different lighting conditions, camera angles, or background variations). The authors propose a novel approach to automatically discover these latent domains by enforcing two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains should allow for strong discriminative models to be learned from them). They develop a nonparametric formulation using kernel-based distribution measures and an efficient optimization procedure, validated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks.

The paper builds upon domain adaptation work at NIPS (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 4, 20]) but identifies a critical gap: most prior work assumes domains are already defined, while in reality, datasets often contain multiple domains that need to be discovered. The authors' approach differs from Hoffman et al.'s [20] work by using nonparametric methods (avoiding parametric assumptions about distributions) and focusing on the learnability property to determine the number of domains.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-motivated theoretical foundations. The authors properly justify their kernel-based domain distinctiveness measure, and the optimization procedure is carefully derived with appropriate constraints (LPC). The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering both object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human activity recognition (IXMAS) tasks. The authors compare against standard practice (treating datasets as single domains) and the only previous work on latent domain discovery [20], showing consistent improvements. The DWCV method for determining the number of domains is practical and well-justified. However, the paper does not address how their approach would handle completely unlabeled data, as the LPC constraint requires label information, which is a limitation in some real-world scenarios.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement is compelling, and the technical details are presented logically. The figures and tables effectively illustrate the domain discovery process and evaluation results. The mathematical formulations are precise, though the relaxation step (section 2.1) might require careful reading for non-optimization experts. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including kernel bandwidth selection and cross-validation parameters.

**Originality:** This work makes a clear contribution by identifying and addressing the problem of latent domain discovery in visual datasets, which has been largely ignored in prior domain adaptation research. The two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) are novel and well-justified. The nonparametric approach is a significant improvement over Hoffman et al.'s parametric Gaussian mixture model. The paper also provides valuable insights into what factors drive domain boundaries (e.g., object pose, background, color), which is a new contribution beyond just the algorithm.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly significant for real-world visual recognition systems, where datasets often contain multiple latent domains that cause performance degradation. The paper demonstrates that reshaping datasets into the discovered domains leads to substantially better adaptation performance (Table 2), showing improvements of up to 10-15% over standard practice. The method has practical utility for both training and test data reshaping (Table 3), and the insights about domain factors could inform future dataset collection practices.

**Strengths:**
- Identifies a critical but overlooked problem in domain adaptation research
- Proposes two well-motivated, novel properties for domain discovery
- Develops a practical nonparametric method with clear implementation details
- Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and tasks
- Provides insights into what visual factors drive domain boundaries
- DWCV method for determining the number of domains is simple and effective

**Weaknesses:**
- Relies on label information for the LPC constraint, limiting applicability to unlabeled data
- Does not fully explore computational complexity for very large datasets
- The connection between discovered domains and specific visual factors (beyond the examples) could be more thoroughly analyzed
- Does not address how the approach would perform when the number of domains is extremely large

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**
*Pro:* This work addresses a fundamental limitation in domain adaptation research with significant practical implications. The proposed method is technically sound, well-evaluated, and demonstrates clear improvements over standard practice. The problem is timely, and the approach could become a standard component in visual recognition systems.

*Con:* The reliance on label information for the LPC constraint might limit the method's applicability to some real-world scenarios where labels are scarce. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, a discussion of how to handle unlabeled data would strengthen the contribution.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the domain adaptation literature by addressing a critical gap in how we conceptualize and handle domains. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear practical benefits. I recommend acceptance for NIPS. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the label dependency and the computational complexity of the method, but these are minor concerns that do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.
