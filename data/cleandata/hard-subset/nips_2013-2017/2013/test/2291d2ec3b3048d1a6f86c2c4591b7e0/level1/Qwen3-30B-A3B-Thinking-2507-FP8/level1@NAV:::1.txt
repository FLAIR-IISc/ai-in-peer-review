

This paper addresses a critical yet overlooked issue in domain adaptation: the common practice of equating datasets with domains is a weak proxy for real-world data distribution mismatches (lighting, pose, background, etc.). The authors propose a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in visual datasets based on two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing distribution differences between domains) and maximum learnability (ensuring strong discriminative models can be learned from each domain). They develop a nonparametric formulation and efficient optimization procedure for domain discovery, which they evaluate on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human activity recognition (IXMAS) tasks.

The paper demonstrates significant technical quality through rigorous theoretical justification and comprehensive experiments. The authors carefully address limitations of previous work, particularly the related method by Hoffman et al. (2014), showing how their nonparametric approach overcomes the restrictive parametric assumptions of prior work. The experimental results consistently show that their reshaped domains outperform both original datasets and competing methods, with improvements validated through multiple evaluation strategies including oracle accuracy comparisons and practical adaptation scenarios.

The clarity of the paper is excellent, with well-structured organization and sufficient technical detail to allow replication. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. The writing is precise and accessible to the NIPS audience.

Originality is strong - the paper identifies a fundamental conceptual gap in domain adaptation research that has been largely ignored, and proposes a novel solution grounded in two axiomatic properties. While related to clustering work (e.g., Jegelka et al., 2013), the authors clearly distinguish their domain discovery problem from standard clustering by emphasizing the importance of label distributions and discriminative learning.

The significance is high: by enabling more accurate domain discovery, this work has the potential to improve domain adaptation performance across multiple applications and inform better dataset construction practices. The method provides a practical solution to a pervasive problem in visual recognition.

Pro: Novel conceptual framework addressing a fundamental gap, strong theoretical foundation, comprehensive experimental validation, practical utility demonstrated across multiple tasks.
Con: The paper could better discuss computational complexity trade-offs for very large datasets, and the connection to recent deep learning-based domain adaptation methods could be expanded.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the field by addressing a critical conceptual issue in domain adaptation. I recommend acceptance.
