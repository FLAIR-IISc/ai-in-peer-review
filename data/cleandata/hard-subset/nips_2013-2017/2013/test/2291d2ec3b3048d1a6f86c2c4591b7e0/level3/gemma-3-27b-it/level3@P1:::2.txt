This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains within image and video datasets, a critical step for effective domain adaptation. The authors argue that equating datasets with domains, a common practice, is a weak proxy for the complex factors influencing data distribution mismatches. Their method aims to identify domains characterized by maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability, formulating this as a nonparametric optimization problem. The core idea – automatically reshaping datasets into meaningful domains – is a valuable contribution, addressing a relatively underexplored problem in domain adaptation. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and approach. Experimental results on object recognition and human activity recognition demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods, including a recent work on latent domain discovery [20].

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in domain adaptation, acknowledging contributions from areas like computer vision [1, 2, 3, 4], speech processing [5, 6], and statistical learning [7, 8, 9, 10]. It correctly identifies a gap in the literature – the automatic definition of domains – and positions its work accordingly. The connection to related work, such as landmark detection [26] and generalized clustering [27], is thoughtfully discussed, highlighting the nuances of their approach.  The authors also appropriately contextualize their work within the broader landscape of multi-domain adaptation [28, 29, 10].

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. The optimization problem detailed in Section 2 isn’t convincingly demonstrated to directly optimize the original objectives (maximizing distinctiveness and learnability simultaneously). The current formulation treats maximal learnability primarily as a wrapper for model selection (choosing the number of domains K) rather than being integrated directly into the domain partitioning process. An integrated optimization, jointly maximizing both criteria, would be a more compelling approach. The reliance on cross-validation to determine K, while pragmatic, feels somewhat disconnected from the core optimization goal.

Another concern relates to the experimental setup. While the reshaping of test datasets is an interesting idea, its explanation is unclear and implicitly assumes an imputation setting that isn't explicitly stated. How are the test examples assigned to the discovered training domains? More details on this process are crucial for understanding the results. 

A significant methodological weakness is the use of an outdated image representation: a single bag-of-words (BoW) of sparse SURF features with only 800 codewords. This representation is considerably weaker than current standards in computer vision.  The paper would be significantly strengthened by incorporating more sophisticated descriptors such as spatial pyramids of dense SURF, Fisher Vectors, or even deep learning-based features.  Furthermore, the paper doesn’t specify the similarity measure used for comparing histograms; Euclidean distance is inappropriate for this task and should be replaced with a more suitable metric like the chi-squared statistic or intersection kernel [Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronnin ECCV 2010].

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed approach addresses an under-explored and important problem in domain adaptation: automatic domain discovery.
*   **Significance:** The concept of reshaping datasets into latent domains has the potential to improve the performance of domain adaptation algorithms across various applications.
*   **Experimental Results:**  The paper demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods, validating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, approach, and experimental setup.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Optimization Formulation:** The current optimization problem does not fully integrate the two key criteria (distinctiveness and learnability).
*   **Image Representation:** The use of an outdated image representation limits the potential performance and weakens the robustness of the results.
*   **Test Reshaping Explanation:** The method for reshaping the test set is not fully explained and relies on an implicit imputation setting.



**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite its shortcomings, this paper presents a valuable and promising contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The core idea of automatically discovering latent domains is novel and significant.  Addressing the concerns regarding the optimization formulation and image representation would further strengthen the paper. Therefore, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting more detail on the test set reshaping process and a discussion of using more modern image descriptors.
