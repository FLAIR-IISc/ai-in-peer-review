This paper tackles an under-explored but important problem in domain adaptation: how to automatically partition mixed visual datasets into latent domains that are both separable in feature space and discriminatively useful for the original class labels. The authors formulate domain discovery via a nonparametric distinctiveness criterion (kernel mean discrepancy across partitions) and select the number of domains via a learnability criterion (domain-wise cross-validation accuracy). This contrasts with the common “dataset = domain” assumption and relates to prior domain discovery work (Hoffman et al., ECCV’12) and two-sample tests via kernel embeddings (Gretton et al., NIPS’07). Extensive experiments on object and action recognition report clear gains over using datasets as domains and over [19], and the conditional reshaping of heterogeneous test sets is interesting and practically relevant.

Quality: The optimization in Sec. 2 is motivated but not shown to closely optimize the original objectives (2)+(3): maximizing a convex quadratic under linear constraints is NP-hard and no approximation quality or convergence guarantees are provided. Moreover, the two criteria are not integrated—the “maximum learnability” appears only as a wrapper for K selection, not within the partitioning objective. An integrated formulation that jointly balances separability and discriminative power (e.g., a surrogate that couples TDD with a margin or cross-validated risk) would strengthen the methodology. The “conditional reshaping” of test sets effectively assumes a transductive/imputation setting; this is insufficiently specified and risks leakage if not carefully controlled.

Clarity: The paper is well written and organized. It would benefit from more illustrative figures that reveal what visual factors the discovered domains capture, and a clearer description of the test reshaping protocol and assumptions.

Originality: High. Automatically defining domains for adaptation is novel and timely relative to NIPS work on dataset bias and multi-source adaptation.

Significance: The empirical gains over datasets-as-domains and [19] are substantial. However, the image representation is quite outdated (single BoW of sparse SURF, 800 words), which likely underestimates the method’s potential. Please upgrade to stronger descriptors (e.g., spatial pyramid with dense SURF/SIFT, Lazebnik CVPR’06; Fisher Vectors, Perronnin ECCV’10) and use appropriate histogram similarities (χ2 or intersection; see Zhang IJCV’06; Jurie CVPR’05), not Euclidean.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, novel problem formulation; practical impact
- Strong empirical improvements over meaningful baselines and prior domain discovery
- Nonparametric distributional criterion avoids restrictive mixture assumptions

Cons against acceptance:
- Lack of guarantees or tight linkage between relaxed optimization and objectives (2)+(3)
- Separation and learnability not jointly optimized
- Test-set reshaping assumptions underspecified
- Outdated features and missing details on histogram similarity

Verdict: Accept. Despite methodological gaps, the contribution is valuable and promising; addressing the above concerns would make it even stronger.
