This paper tackles the often-implicit assumption in visual domain adaptation that datasets equal domains. It proposes to automatically partition mixed visual datasets into latent domains that are (i) maximally distinctive in distribution and (ii) maximally learnable for discriminative tasks. Distinctiveness is operationalized with a nonparametric kernel two-sample measure (MMD) summed over all domain pairs; learnability is enforced by choosing the number of domains K via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) of within-domain classifiers. The authors also introduce a “conditional reshaping” of heterogeneous test sets to match discovered training domains. They evaluate on object recognition (Amazon, DSLR, Webcam, Caltech-256) and multi-view action recognition (IXMAS), reporting gains over using the original dataset divisions and over a prior latent-domain model based on Gaussian mixtures.

Relative to prior work, this paper is closest to Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12), which assumes a parametric hierarchical mixture to discover domains. Here, the nonparametric RKHS approach avoids parametric distributional assumptions and uses label-aware constraints to prevent degenerate, per-class clusters. The work also connects to generalized clustering via kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.) and to multi-source adaptation and GFK (Gopalan et al., Gong et al.).

Technical approach and contributions:
- Maximally distinctive domains: Given K, assign instances to K domains by maximizing the sum of pairwise MMDs between empirical domain distributions in RKHS. A label prior constraint (LPC) enforces that each domain’s class-label distribution approximates the global label prior, discouraging trivial per-class partitions.
- Optimization: The integer program is relaxed by reparameterizing assignment variables on the simplex, yielding a quadratic objective with linear constraints. Although the paper correctly notes that maximizing a convex quadratic under linear constraints remains NP-hard, the authors rely on off-the-shelf solvers and report satisfactory solutions in practice.
- Maximally learnable domains and model selection: DWCV trains discriminative classifiers within each candidate domain and maximizes the weighted average cross-validated accuracy A(K) over K, selecting K* that balances distinctiveness with learnability.
- Conditional test reshaping: For heterogeneous test data, solve a quadratic program to assign test instances to pseudo-domains so as to minimize distributional mismatch to discovered training domains, then adapt from matched pairs.

Empirical validation:
- On A/D/W/C datasets with BoVW features and GFK for adaptation, the discovered latent domains outperform using original datasets as “domains,” and also beat the Hoffman et al. latent-domain baseline. An “oracle” analysis reports upper bounds by choosing the best single source per target (with target labels), and practical multi-source strategies (union/ensemble/matching) approach these bounds using label-free matching.
- On IXMAS, conditional reshaping of the test set (to match training latent domains) yields notable gains, aligning well with the intuition that view heterogeneity is a primary factor.
- Qualitative analyses suggest the method often recovers interpretable factors (pose, background, color) across datasets.

Quality:
- Strengths: Clear MMD-based formulation; principled K selection via learnability; sensible conditional reshaping; solid improvements on standard benchmarks; useful qualitative evidence.
- Concerns: (1) The LPC assumes class priors are domain-invariant. In many visual settings, latent factors (e.g., viewpoint) correlate with category frequency; enforcing LPC may distort the discovered domains or suppress meaningful prior shifts. An empirical ablation (vary δ, remove LPC, or permit domain-specific priors) would help. (2) The “unsupervised” claim is misleading: the method uses class labels during domain discovery (LPC) and for DWCV. Please present it as supervised or weakly supervised latent-domain discovery. (3) Optimization lacks guarantees; report solver details, typical runtimes, and sensitivity to initialization. (4) The distinctiveness measure depends on kernel bandwidth; provide sensitivity analysis and rationale beyond “twice the median distance.” (5) The method may still gravitate to category-driven partitions without LPC; quantify how LPC mitigates this and its trade-offs.

Clarity:
- The paper would benefit from consistent terminology. “Dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain” are sometimes conflated; define S (original datasets), U (discovered domains), T (targets) early and use consistently.
- Section 4.2’s notation is confusing. You define r(A → B) but later use r(Uk, B) in the max without the arrow; please standardize and ensure equation numbering is consistent. Also clearly explain the “oracle” nature of GOURS/GORIG/GOTHER and the role of target labels.
- Provide the exact quadratic programs for both training-time discovery and conditional test reshaping (currently deferred to supplementary), plus constraints and solver settings.

Originality:
- Using MMD to discover label-regularized latent domains and selecting K by learnability is a novel and appealing combination. The conditional test reshaping is also a useful addition. The contrast to Hoffman et al.’s parametric approach is well-motivated.

Significance:
- The idea that “dataset ≠ domain” is important for vision and DA. The proposed reshaping can improve adaptation pipelines and inform future dataset construction. Broader impact may be limited where class priors legitimately vary across domains or when labels are scarce, but within the labeled-source regime the contribution is meaningful.

Pros for acceptance:
- Nonparametric, label-aware formulation for latent-domain discovery with demonstrated gains.
- Practical K selection grounded in discriminative performance.
- Conditional test reshaping addresses realistic heterogeneous test scenarios.
- Empirical results on standard benchmarks with qualitative insights.

Cons for acceptance:
- Reliance on label information contradicts the “unsupervised” framing.
- LPC assumption may be unrealistic; lack of ablations on δ/LPC.
- Optimization lacks guarantees and computational details.
- Some notational and exposition issues reduce clarity; Section 4.2 needs tightening.

Suggestions to improve the paper:
- Recast the problem setting as supervised/weakly supervised; clarify exactly where labels are used.
- Add ablations: effect of LPC (vary δ, remove), kernel bandwidth, and different base features; report stability across random seeds/initializations.
- Provide complexity and runtime analyses; detail solvers and convergence behavior.
- Expand baselines (e.g., spectral clustering, different divergence measures) and include the omitted K-means/random-partition results.
- Clarify experimental protocol (source/target selection, leave-one-dataset-out setups), and ensure notation/equations are consistent.

Overall recommendation: Promising and relevant contribution with solid empirical evidence, but it requires clearer positioning (not unsupervised), stronger analysis of the LPC assumption and optimization details, and improved exposition. I lean toward a weak accept contingent on addressing clarity and ablations.
