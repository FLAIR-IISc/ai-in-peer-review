Summary
The paper addresses an under-explored but important question in domain adaptation for visual recognition: how to automatically discover latent “domains” within one or more datasets, rather than equating entire datasets with domains. The authors propose two principles for domain discovery: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be distributionally far apart) and maximum learnability (each domain should support strong discriminative models for the category labels). Distinctiveness is operationalized via a kernel two-sample measure (MMD-style distance between RKHS means), summed over all pairs of domains. The assignment of instances to K domains is posed as a constrained optimization (with a label prior constraint), relaxed to a continuous quadratic program. Learnability is used to choose K via domain-wise cross-validation, selecting the K that maximizes weighted per-domain validation accuracy. The method is evaluated on object recognition (Caltech-256 and the Office datasets) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS), showing gains over using datasets as domains and over Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12) latent domain discovery. The paper further explores “conditional reshaping” of unlabeled test sets to align with discovered training domains.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated within domain adaptation (Saenko et al., Gopalan et al., Daumé III, Blitzer et al., Pan et al., Ben-David et al.) and specifically cites kernel two-sample tests (Gretton et al.) to measure distribution gap. It compares to multisource adaptation methods and the closest latent domain discovery approach (Hoffman et al., ECCV’12), which uses parametric mixture modeling; the proposed method is nonparametric and label-aware via the LPC. The notion of maximizing discriminability relates to generalized clustering via kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.). Using the Geodesic Flow Kernel (Gong et al.) for adaptation is appropriate.

Strengths
- Tackles a timely and meaningful problem: discovering latent domains rather than assuming dataset=domain.
- Clear, appealing principles (distinctiveness and learnability) with a nonparametric formulation that avoids mixture-model assumptions.
- Practical model selection (DWCV) to choose K, with sensible bounds.
- Empirical evidence that discovered domains improve adaptation vs. original datasets and outperform the prior latent-domain method.
- Insightful qualitative analysis of discovered factors (pose, background, appearance), potentially useful for dataset design.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Integration of criteria: “maximum learnability” is only used as an outer-loop model selection criterion. The partitioning objective itself optimizes distinctiveness (plus LPC), not learnability. A joint objective that trades off separability and within-domain discriminative performance would be more principled and could avoid over-partitioning driven solely by distributional differences that are irrelevant to classification.
- Optimization gap: The relaxed quadratic maximization is NP-hard with no approximation guarantees; practical solvability is claimed but details (initialization, solver, convergence behavior, sensitivity) are sparse, and it is unclear how closely solutions satisfy the original integer constraints/objective.
- Label prior constraint (LPC): Assuming label proportions are domain-invariant may be unrealistic; the method’s sensitivity to violations of LPC and to δ is not analyzed.
- Test-time reshaping: The conditional reshaping of unlabeled test data is intriguing but under-explained. What is the precise objective and constraint set? What information is assumed available at test time? Is the setting transductive (access to all test samples at once)? How is the method deployed when test samples arrive online or when test distributions shift after training?
- Representation and similarity choices are dated/underspecified:
  - Visual features: single BoW of sparse SURF with 800 words is weak by current standards. Results may understate the approach’s potential.
  - Histogram similarity: the Gaussian kernel on raw BoW implies Euclidean geometry, which is ill-suited for histograms; chi-squared, Hellinger, or intersection kernels would be more appropriate.
- Scalability: The method requires an M×M kernel and solves a quadratic program; scalability to larger datasets is not demonstrated.
- Limited ablations: No study of K sensitivity beyond DWCV curves; no analysis of bandwidth choice, LPC δ, or mismatch between class prior across domains.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, with a reasonable nonparametric discrepancy and a practical selection of K. However, the relaxation/optimization aspects and the interaction between distinctiveness and learnability could be better justified and evaluated. Experimental results are positive and cover two tasks, but features are outdated and there are missing implementation details (kernel choice for histograms, solver).

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The motivation and high-level method are clear. It would benefit from:
- More illustrative figures showing the effect of reshaping, and a schematic of the full pipeline (including test-time reshaping).
- Precise mathematical statement of the conditional reshaping objective for the test set.
- More details on optimization (solver, runtime, initialization) and parameter sensitivity.

Originality
Identifying latent domains for visual adaptation is novel and timely, with clear differences from prior parametric mixture models and from domain-selection/landmark approaches. Using MMD-style distances and an LPC to steer partitions away from per-class clustering is a nice idea.

Significance
If robust, the approach could change how adaptation is done by decoupling domain definition from datasets and potentially boosting performance across heterogeneous collections. The empirical gains over baselines and prior latent domain discovery are encouraging. Stronger features and broader datasets would further bolster impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Integrate learnability into the partitioning objective, e.g., maximize a weighted sum of inter-domain MMD and within-domain discriminative performance (e.g., cross-validated margin/loss), with regularization to prevent tiny domains.
- Provide optimization details and ablations: solver, initialization, complexity, sensitivity to δ and bandwidth, robustness to prior-shift (violations of LPC).
- Clarify test-time reshaping assumptions and objective; discuss transductive vs. inductive use and online scenarios.
- Upgrade descriptors: dense SURF with spatial pyramids (Lazebnik CVPR’06), Fisher Vectors (Perronnin ECCV’10), or modern deep CNN features; report results with histogram-appropriate kernels (chi-squared, intersection) or Hellinger-normalized features.
- Evaluate scalability and include larger-scale results if possible.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-served problem with a clear, nonparametric framework.
- Demonstrates consistent empirical gains over meaningful baselines and prior art.
- Conceptually simple and potentially useful for practitioners.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited integration of learnability into the core objective; potential optimization gap.
- Test-set reshaping under-specified; assumptions unclear.
- Dated and suboptimal feature choices; some missing implementation details.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is the method to LPC violations and δ? Have you tried relaxing/removing LPC?
- What solver and initialization are used for the quadratic program? What are runtimes and scalability limits?
- What exact kernel is used for BoW histograms? Have you tried chi-squared or Hellinger?
- Can you report results with stronger features (e.g., Fisher Vectors or CNNs)?
- Please formalize the conditional reshaping objective and clarify test-time assumptions (transductive vs. inductive).

Overall recommendation
Accept. Despite some methodological and presentation shortcomings, the paper addresses a relevant problem with a novel, nonparametric approach and provides solid empirical evidence that discovering latent domains can substantially improve adaptation. The paper would be strengthened by clearer optimization details, a tighter integration of learnability, and stronger features, but it already offers a valuable and promising contribution.

Reviewer confidence
Medium.
