This paper addresses the important problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition, specifically focusing on the challenge of automatically discovering latent domains within datasets rather than relying on pre-defined dataset boundaries. The authors propose a novel approach based on maximizing inter-domain distinctiveness and intra-domain learnability, formulated as a relaxed quadratic optimization problem. The experimental results on object recognition and action recognition datasets demonstrate promising performance gains over existing methods. Overall, this is a well-written and potentially significant contribution to the field.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation, while complex, is clearly presented and justified. The relaxation of the integer program to a continuous optimization is a sensible approach for tractability. The experimental evaluation is thorough, utilizing multiple datasets and comparison to a relevant baseline. The authors acknowledge limitations and potential areas for improvement. The inclusion of ablation studies, while not extensive, strengthens the conclusions. However, a more rigorous analysis of the computational cost of the optimization would be beneficial.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written. The motivation for the approach is explained reasonably well, although some aspects (detailed below) could be further clarified. The notation is consistent throughout. The discussion of related work is adequate, though could be expanded (see suggested citation). Providing a more intuitive explanation of the kernel-based difference function (Eq. 1) would benefit readers less familiar with kernel methods.

**Originality:** The idea of automatically discovering latent domains, rather than assuming datasets equate to domains, is a novel and important contribution. While related work exists on clustering and domain adaptation, this paper uniquely combines these concepts with a specific focus on maximizing distinctiveness and learnability. The use of a kernel-based difference function and the label-prior constraint are also novel aspects.

**Significance:** The results suggest that automatically reshaping datasets into latent domains can improve domain adaptation performance. This has significant implications for real-world applications where acquiring perfectly labeled and domain-aligned datasets is often impractical. The potential for building more robust and adaptable visual recognition systems is substantial. The work addresses a difficult problem in a demonstrable way and opens avenues for future research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to domain adaptation.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating performance improvements.
*   Clear and technically sound presentation.
*   Addresses a critical challenge in real-world visual recognition.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Motivation for avoiding per-category domains is not fully convincing.
*   Necessity of the label-prior constraint requires further justification.
*   Lack of comparison to unsupervised object discovery baselines.

**Specific Concerns & Questions Addressed (from original notes):**

*   **Motivation:** My concern regarding the motivation—why clustering by appearance yields per-category domains—remains partially unresolved. While the paper explains *that* it happens (citing [20]), it doesn’t fully articulate *why* this is undesirable in all cases. A more detailed discussion of scenarios where per-category clustering is detrimental would be helpful.
*   **Label-Prior Constraint:** The rebuttal provided some justification for the LPC, but its necessity is still unclear. The authors state it encourages label distribution consistency. Results *without* the LPC and a comparison to k-means or other unsupervised clustering methods would provide stronger evidence for its value.
*   **“Maximally Different in Distribution”:** This is adequately clarified in the rebuttal, specifying differences in feature distributions.
*   **β_{mk} on Simplex:** The authors confirmed that β_{mk} as formulated does indeed lie on a simplex.
*   **Constraints Clarification:** The discrepancy in constraints around lines 166 & 182 was explained; the relaxation introduced additional constraints for optimization.
*   **Bound Justification:** The rebuttal offered an intuitive explanation for the bound on line 215 but did not provide a formal proof.
*   **Geodesic Flow Kernel:** The authors indicated the use of an existing implementation of the geodesic flow kernel, which is helpful.
*   **Outperformance Insights:** The rebuttal discussed how the approach handles inter-view variations in IXMAS, offering some insight into the superior performance. A more systematic analysis, perhaps through visualizing the discovered domains, would be beneficial.
*   **Minor Correction:** The correction regarding Eq (1) (M'k -> Mk') has been noted.
*   **Suggested Citation:** The suggestion to include Torralba & Efros (2011) is excellent and provides important context regarding dataset bias.

**Overall Assessment:**

The rebuttal addressed many of my initial concerns, and I now lean slightly towards acceptance. The paper presents a novel and well-executed approach to a challenging problem. While some aspects could be further strengthened with additional experiments and analysis, the contribution is significant and warrants inclusion in NIPS. I recommend acceptance, contingent on incorporating the suggested citation (Torralba & Efros, 2011).
