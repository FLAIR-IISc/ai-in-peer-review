This paper addresses the important and under-explored problem of automatically defining domains for domain adaptation. The standard practice in the field is to treat entire datasets as distinct domains. The authors argue this is a flawed proxy, as a single dataset may contain multiple latent domains defined by factors like lighting, pose, and background. The core proposal is an unsupervised method to partition a collection of images into a set of domains that are both maximally distinctive and maximally learnable. Distinctiveness is measured nonparametrically using a kernel-based, two-sample test statistic (maximum mean discrepancy), ensuring that the feature distributions of the resulting domains are separable. Learnability ensures that a strong discriminative classifier can be trained on the data within each discovered domain, and is used as a criterion for model selection to determine the optimal number of domains. This work is most closely related to Hoffman et al. [20], which also tackles latent domain discovery, but differs by employing a nonparametric approach rather than assuming a parametric (Gaussian mixture) model for the data distribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel formulation of a fundamental problem. The question of "what constitutes a domain" is often glossed over in domain adaptation literature. By proposing a principled, data-driven method to discover latent domains, this work offers a significant conceptual contribution that could change how researchers approach domain adaptation tasks. The idea of moving beyond the "one dataset = one domain" assumption is both insightful and important for real-world applications.
*   **Experimental Results:** The empirical evaluation is extensive, covering both object and action recognition. The experiments convincingly demonstrate that adapting from the automatically discovered domains yields significant performance improvements over adapting from the original datasets. Furthermore, the proposed method shows a clear advantage over the recent competing approach of Hoffman et al. [20], providing strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed formulation.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the core ideas are communicated clearly. The motivation is well-established in the introduction, and the proposed approach is laid out in a logical manner.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Decoupled Optimization Criteria:** A central weakness is the disconnect between the two main criteria: distinctiveness and learnability. The optimization procedure (Sec 2.1) focuses exclusively on maximizing inter-domain distinctiveness (TDD) subject to a label prior constraint. The second criterion, maximal learnability, is not integrated into this optimization. Instead, it is applied only as a post-hoc wrapper (domain-wise cross-validation) to select the number of domains, K. A more principled and potentially more powerful approach would involve a unified objective that jointly optimizes for both separability and learnability. The current formulation feels like a two-step procedure rather than a cohesive model.

2.  **Clarity of Optimization:** The paper relaxes the NP-hard integer programming problem into a continuous optimization of a convex quadratic function. However, it is not clearly shown or argued how well the solution to this relaxed problem approximates the original objectives in equations (2) and (3). A more thorough discussion or analysis connecting the relaxation back to the original problem would strengthen the paper's technical soundness.

3.  **Test Set Reshaping:** The idea of "reshaping" the test set (Sec 4.3) is interesting but is poorly explained and justified. This procedure uses the unlabeled test data to partition it into domains that best match the discovered source domains. This effectively assumes a transductive or imputation setting where the test set is available during the adaptation process, but this assumption is never explicitly stated or discussed. The lack of clarity on the setting and the computational details (referred to the supplementary material) makes this section difficult to fully evaluate.

4.  **Outdated Image Representation:** A major methodological weakness is the choice of image features. The use of a single Bag-of-Words representation based on sparse SURF features with an 800-word codebook is significantly outdated, even by 2013 standards. Modern computer vision has long moved past such weak descriptors. This choice raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings to more powerful, state-of-the-art feature representations. The authors should consider evaluating their method with stronger features like spatial pyramids of dense SIFT (Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006), Fisher Vectors (Perronnin et al., ECCV 2010), or even features from deep networks to demonstrate the robustness of their domain discovery approach.

5.  **Missing Technical Details:** The paper is unclear about the similarity measure used for comparing the BoW histograms within the kernel framework. If a standard Gaussian kernel was used on the histogram vectors, this implies an underlying Euclidean distance, which is known to be a poor choice for comparing histograms. Standard and more appropriate measures like the chi-squared kernel or the intersection kernel should have been used and explicitly mentioned.

**Suggestions:**

*   For future work, the authors should investigate an integrated optimization framework that jointly maximizes domain distinctiveness and learnability within a single objective function.
*   The authors should clarify the connection between the relaxed optimization and the original integer program.
*   The section on test set reshaping needs to be rewritten to clearly state the setting (e.g., transductive learning) and provide more methodological detail in the main paper.
*   To improve the paper's impact and relevance, the experiments should be re-run with more contemporary and powerful feature descriptors (e.g., Fisher Vectors). Relevant citations to consider include Zhang et al. (IJCV 2006), Jurie & Triggs (CVPR 2005), Lazebnik et al. (CVPR 2006), and Perronnin et al. (ECCV 2010).
*   While the paper is well-written, it would benefit from more illustrative figures showing examples of the discovered domains to provide readers with better intuition.

**Overall Verdict:**

Despite the noted shortcomings, particularly the outdated feature representation and the decoupled optimization criteria, this paper makes a valuable and timely contribution. It tackles a novel and important problem in domain adaptation with a well-motivated approach and demonstrates strong empirical results. The core idea of automatically discovering latent domains is promising and opens up an interesting new direction for research in the field. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
