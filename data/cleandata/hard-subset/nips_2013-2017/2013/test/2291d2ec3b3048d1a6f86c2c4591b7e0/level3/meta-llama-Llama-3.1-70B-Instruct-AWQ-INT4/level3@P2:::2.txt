This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in image or video datasets, which is essential for domain adaptation in visual recognition problems. The authors introduce two key properties that latent domains should possess: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. They devise a nonparametric formulation and efficient optimization procedure to discover domains among both training and test data. The approach is extensively evaluated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, showing significant improvements over using datasets as domains and over the recent domain discovery method.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its underlying motivations. The introduction effectively sets the stage for the problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition and highlights the limitations of current practices. The proposed approach is novel and addresses the under-explored problem of automatically defining domains for domain adaptation.

The technical contributions of the paper are substantial. The authors propose a nonparametric formulation to measure domain discrepancies and show how to optimize them to arrive at maximum distinctiveness. They also introduce a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure to identify the optimal number of domains. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in improving adaptation performance on various tasks.

However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement. The proposed optimization is not clearly shown to solve the original objectives closely. The two driving criteria, maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability, are not well integrated, with maximal learnability being applied only as a wrapper for model selection, not as part of the partitioning objective. An integrated optimization that jointly maximizes separability and learnability would be preferable.

Additionally, the image representation used in the experiments is outdated and too weak for current standards. The authors use a single bag-of-words (BoW) of sparse SURF features with 800 codewords, which may not capture the complexities of the data. Upgrading the descriptors to more modern and powerful representations, such as spatial pyramid of dense SURF or Fisher Vectors, would likely improve the performance of the approach.

Furthermore, the similarity measure used for histogram comparison is unclear, and the authors should consider using more appropriate measures, such as the X^2 or intersection kernel.

The use of the test set in the "reshaping" process is also poorly explained and effectively assumes an imputation setting that is not stated. The authors should clarify this aspect and provide more details on how the test set is used in the reshaping process.

Overall, despite these shortcomings, the paper is a valuable and promising contribution to the field of domain adaptation in visual recognition. The authors' approach has the potential to improve the performance of various visual recognition tasks, and the paper provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in image or video datasets.
* The approach is extensively evaluated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, showing significant improvements over using datasets as domains and over the recent domain discovery method.
* The technical contributions of the paper are substantial, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its underlying motivations.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The proposed optimization is not clearly shown to solve the original objectives closely.
* The two driving criteria, maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability, are not well integrated.
* The image representation used in the experiments is outdated and too weak for current standards.
* The similarity measure used for histogram comparison is unclear.
* The use of the test set in the "reshaping" process is poorly explained and effectively assumes an imputation setting that is not stated.

Rating: 8/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address the limitations and areas for improvement mentioned above.
