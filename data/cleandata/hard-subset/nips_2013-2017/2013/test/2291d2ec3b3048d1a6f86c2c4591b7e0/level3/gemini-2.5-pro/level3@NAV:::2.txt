This paper addresses the important and under-explored problem of automatically discovering latent domains within visual datasets for domain adaptation. Instead of the common but problematic practice of equating datasets with domains, the authors propose a method to partition a collection of images based on two criteria: maximizing the feature-space separability between domains and ensuring high "learnability" (i.e., that a strong discriminative classifier can be trained) within each discovered domain. This is a novel contribution that moves beyond most prior work, which assumes pre-defined domains, and offers a more principled, non-parametric alternative to the most related work [20].

The paper’s primary strength is its originality in tackling a fundamental problem. The idea of learning the domains themselves, rather than taking them as given, is significant and could have a substantial impact on domain adaptation research. The experimental results are another strong point, demonstrating significant improvements over both using original datasets as domains and the recent domain discovery method of Hoffman et al. [20]. The paper is also well-written and clearly articulates its motivation and approach.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several weaknesses. The most critical is a disconnect between the stated goals and the proposed optimization. The two driving criteria—separability and learnability—are not well integrated. Maximal learnability is only used as a wrapper for model selection (to find the number of domains, K) and is not part of the primary partitioning objective. A unified optimization that jointly maximizes both criteria would be a more elegant and powerful formulation. Furthermore, it is not clearly shown how the relaxed optimization in Section 2 accurately solves for the original objectives. A major methodological weakness is the use of outdated image features (a single Bag-of-Words of sparse SURF). This representation is far too weak by current standards and may limit the generality of the findings. The authors should consider stronger descriptors like Fisher Vectors or spatial pyramids (e.g., Lazebnik CVPR 2006, Perronnin ECCV 2010). Additionally, key details are missing, such as the similarity measure used for BoW histograms; a Chi-squared or intersection kernel would be more appropriate than the default Euclidean distance. Finally, the "reshaping" of the test set is an interesting idea but is poorly explained and seems to assume an imputation setting that is not explicitly stated or justified.

Overall, despite the outdated feature representation and the decoupled optimization, the core idea is novel, significant, and promising. The paper provides a valuable contribution to a challenging problem and should be accepted.
