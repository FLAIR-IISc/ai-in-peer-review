This paper addresses the important but often overlooked problem of what constitutes a "domain" in visual domain adaptation. The authors argue that the common practice of equating datasets with domains is suboptimal, as a single dataset may contain a mixture of distinct latent domains (e.g., different lighting, viewpoints). The central contribution is a method to automatically discover these latent domains from one or more source datasets. The goal is to partition the training data into K domains that are maximally distinctive from each other while also being "learnable," meaning a strong classifier can be trained on each.

The method defines domain distinctiveness using a kernel-based two-sample test (Maximum Mean Discrepancy) summed over all pairs of domains. The data is partitioned to maximize this distinctiveness via an integer program. This program is relaxed to a continuous quadratic optimization problem. A key constraint enforces that the per-domain class label distribution should match the global prior. The number of domains, K, is chosen via a novel domain-wise cross-validation procedure designed to ensure learnability. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on object and action recognition tasks, showing that adapting from these discovered domains improves performance over using the original datasets as domains and outperforms a prior method for latent domain discovery [20].

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and novel problem. While domain adaptation is a mature field, the question of how to define the domains themselves, especially for complex visual data, is a critical and under-explored area. Automatically discovering a better "basis" of source domains is an elegant idea with significant potential impact for practitioners.
2.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out, the proposed method is described in sufficient detail, and the experiments are well-structured.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The formulation appears technically sound, building on established kernel methods for measuring distribution difference. The proposed optimization and the method for selecting K are reasonable. The performance gains shown in the experiments are encouraging and suggest the method is effective.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

Despite the promising direction, several aspects of the motivation, formulation, and evaluation require clarification and strengthening.

1.  **Motivation and Formulation:** The central motivation is that partitioning the data into "maximally distinctive" domains is beneficial for adaptation. However, it is not entirely clear that clustering by feature appearance (which is what maximizing the kernel distance on feature vectors does) will necessarily yield domains corresponding to nuisance factors like viewpoint or illumination, rather than simply clustering by object category. The paper notes this risk but it is unclear how the model prevents it. The Label Prior Constraint (LPC) seems intended for this, but its effect is not ablated or analyzed. A more explicit discussion of why this formulation avoids discovering per-category clusters would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Necessity of the Label Prior Constraint (LPC) and Baselines:** The LPC is an interesting constraint, but its importance is not experimentally verified. To justify the complexity of the proposed model, it would be very helpful to see an ablation study showing performance *without* the LPC. Furthermore, the claim that simple clustering is insufficient needs to be backed by stronger empirical evidence. I would suggest including comparisons to the following baselines for domain discovery:
    *   Standard k-means clustering on the feature vectors.
    *   A random partitioning of the data into K equal-sized sets.
    *   Perhaps a more sophisticated baseline from the unsupervised object discovery literature.
    These comparisons would more clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed constrained optimization.

3.  **Clarifications Requested:**
    *   The paper states the goal is to find domains that are "maximally different in distribution." Please clarify if this refers to the marginal distribution over features P(x), the conditional P(y|x), the joint P(x,y), or something else. The formulation seems to focus on P(x).
    *   On line 166, the variables β_k are introduced and relaxed to live on a simplex. However, given the definition β_mk = z_mk/M_k, it seems that the vector β_k = (β_1k, ..., β_Mk)^T would already sum to 1 by definition (sum_m z_mk / M_k = M_k/M_k = 1). Could you please clarify the relaxation step here?
    *   The constraints in Eq. (5) (around line 182) appear different from the original integer constraints. Specifically, how is the original constraint `sum_k z_mk = 1` for all m relaxed into `1/M <= sum_k β_mk <= 1/C`? Please provide a derivation for this transformation.
    *   On line 215, a bound is given for the maximum K to check: `K_max <= min{M/(NC), C}`. Could you provide a justification or brief proof for this bound?
    *   For reproducibility, please detail the use of the geodesic flow kernel [4]. Was a publicly available implementation used or was it re-implemented?

4.  **Deeper Analysis:** The results show that the method works, but the paper would benefit from more insight into *why* it works better than the baselines. For instance, showing some qualitative examples where the original dataset partitioning (e.g., Amazon vs. Webcam) leads to adaptation failures that are overcome by the discovered domains would be very insightful.

**Minor Points:**

*   There appears to be a typo in Eq (1). M'k should likely be M_k'.
*   Given the paper's focus on dataset bias, a citation to Torralba & Efros, "Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias" (CVPR 2011) would be highly relevant.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The core idea of automatically discovering latent domains is compelling. However, the paper would be significantly improved by a clearer justification of the modeling choices through ablation studies and stronger baseline comparisons, as well as by addressing the requests for clarification listed above.
