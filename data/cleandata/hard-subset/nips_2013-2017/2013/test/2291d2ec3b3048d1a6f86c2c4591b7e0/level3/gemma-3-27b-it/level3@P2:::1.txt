## Review of “Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation”

This paper proposes a novel approach to domain adaptation by automatically discovering latent domains within datasets, rather than equating datasets with domains as is common practice. The authors argue that this is crucial for visual recognition where underlying factors like pose, illumination, and background significantly impact data distribution and are not easily captured by discrete dataset labels. Their method formulates domain discovery as an optimization problem seeking domains with “maximum distinctiveness” and “maximum learnability,” solved via a nonparametric approach and efficient optimization procedure. The method is evaluated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, demonstrating improved performance compared to existing approaches.

The core idea of discovering latent domains is compelling and addresses a key limitation in current domain adaptation strategies. The motivation for moving beyond treating datasets as single domains is well-articulated, and the problem statement is clearly defined. The formulation leveraging maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability provides a principled framework for domain discovery. The experimental results presented are promising, showing consistent gains in performance on both object recognition and human activity recognition. The paper is generally well-written and presents the technical details in a reasonably clear manner.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need to be addressed. While the authors claim that simply clustering images by appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains, there’s limited empirical evidence presented *within the paper* to substantiate this claim. Showing a comparison with a simple appearance-based clustering method would strengthen this motivation. Similarly, the necessity of the label-prior constraint (LPC) isn’t thoroughly justified.  Results without the LPC, and comparisons to more basic clustering algorithms like k-means or random equal partitioning, would help demonstrate its contribution. It's unclear why the LPC is needed—does it significantly affect the discovered domains, and if so, how?

The definition of “maximally different in distribution” could also be clarified. The paper states that it applies to the distributions of features and labels but doesn’t elaborate on how these distributions are characterized beyond the kernel-based metric.  More discussion on the implications of choosing a particular kernel would be beneficial. Furthermore, line 166 states β_{mk} lies on a simplex, but this seems inherent in its definition; the phrasing is slightly confusing. A clarification would be appreciated.  There's also a minor inconsistency in Eq (1) where M'k should be Mk'.

The choice of K, the number of domains, is determined using domain-wise cross-validation. The paper states a bound for Kmax (line 215) but lacks sufficient justification for this bound. A brief explanation of the reasoning behind this limit would strengthen the methodological section. The description of the geodesic flow kernel adaptation (line 225) is also somewhat sparse. Was a publicly available implementation used, or was it reimplemented for this work? Clarification here is important for reproducibility.  

Finally, while the paper shows performance gains, it would be helpful to include a more detailed analysis of *why* the proposed approach outperforms the baselines. Are there specific failure cases for the baselines that this method overcomes? Demonstrating these scenarios would provide stronger evidence for the method’s efficacy. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to domain adaptation by automatically discovering latent domains.
*   Principled formulation based on maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.
*   Promising experimental results on both object recognition and human activity recognition.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of strong empirical evidence supporting the claim about the pitfalls of simple appearance-based clustering.
*   Unclear justification for the necessity of the label-prior constraint.
*   Ambiguity in the definition of “maximally different in distribution.”
*   Insufficient detail regarding the geodesic flow kernel and Kmax bound.
*   Limited analysis of failure cases and comparative advantages over baselines.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel and promising approach to domain adaptation. While some aspects of the methodology and justification require further clarification and supporting evidence, the core idea is valuable and the experimental results are encouraging. Given the potential impact of this work and the responsiveness of the authors (as demonstrated by the rebuttal), I lean slightly towards acceptance, contingent on minor revisions addressing the concerns outlined above. I recommend the authors address the points raised in the rebuttal to fully convince the community of the significance and robustness of their contribution.
