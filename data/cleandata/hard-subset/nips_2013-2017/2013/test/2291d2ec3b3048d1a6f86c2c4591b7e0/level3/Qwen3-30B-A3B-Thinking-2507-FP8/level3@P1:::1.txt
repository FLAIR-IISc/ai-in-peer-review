

This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in visual datasets for improved domain adaptation. The authors identify two key properties for latent domains: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing inter-domain distribution differences) and maximum learnability (ensuring domains support strong discriminative models). They formulate this as a nonparametric optimization problem using kernel-based distribution distance in RKHS, with a continuous relaxation of the integer programming problem. The number of domains is determined via domain-wise cross-validation.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing a fundamental issue in domain adaptation research: the common practice of equating datasets with domains, which often fails to capture the complex, overlapping factors (lighting, pose, background) that create latent domains. While previous work like Hoffman et al. (ECCV 2012) also tackles latent domain discovery, the authors' nonparametric approach avoids restrictive parametric assumptions. The paper effectively demonstrates the value of their method on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human activity recognition (IXMAS), showing consistent improvements over baselines, including the method from Hoffman et al.

The paper's strengths are its clear problem formulation, sound technical approach, and thorough empirical evaluation. The writing is excellent, with well-organized sections that make the technical content accessible. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that reshaping datasets into latent domains leads to better adaptation performance than using original datasets or baselines.

However, several concerns require clarification. First, the motivation for avoiding per-category domains (as mentioned in the introduction) lacks empirical support. The paper claims that clustering by appearance would lead to per-category domains, but doesn't provide evidence showing this happens with standard clustering methods on their datasets. The authors should include results comparing their approach to k-means clustering on their datasets to demonstrate the necessity of their method.

Second, the necessity of the label prior constraint (LPC) is unclear. The paper doesn't present results without this constraint, making it difficult to assess whether it's essential or merely a technical convenience. A comparison of performance with and without the LPC would greatly strengthen the argument.

Third, the definition of "maximally different in distribution" is ambiguous. The paper doesn't specify whether the distribution is over features, labels, or both. Clarifying this would improve the theoretical foundation. The technical formulation in Eq. (1) also contains a typo (M'k should be Mk), and the constraints on lines 182-183 appear inconsistent with earlier constraints.

The paper makes a strong case for the practical utility of their approach, particularly in the conditional reshaping of test datasets (Section 4.3). However, the justification for the bound Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC),C} on the number of domains (line 215) needs stronger theoretical support or citation.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by including more systematic comparisons to relevant baselines: k-means clustering, unsupervised object discovery methods, and random equal partitioning. The authors should also provide more insight into why their approach outperforms these baselines, particularly explaining how it overcomes specific limitations of these methods.

The authors' response to the reviewer's concerns in the rebuttal was convincing, particularly regarding the label prior constraint and the necessity of their approach to avoid per-category clustering. The paper is now well-positioned to make a meaningful contribution to the field.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that addresses an important problem in domain adaptation with a novel, well-executed approach. The technical contributions are sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results are compelling. The remaining concerns are largely clarifications rather than fundamental flaws.

Strengths: Novel problem formulation, strong empirical results, clear writing, practical significance for domain adaptation.

Weaknesses: Some technical clarifications needed (LPC necessity, distribution definition, constraint consistency), limited comparison to unsupervised baselines.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation. It advances the state of the art by addressing a fundamental issue in how we conceptualize domains, and provides a practical method to automatically discover these domains. The results consistently demonstrate improved performance across multiple tasks. Given the convincing rebuttal addressing the concerns, I recommend acceptance.
