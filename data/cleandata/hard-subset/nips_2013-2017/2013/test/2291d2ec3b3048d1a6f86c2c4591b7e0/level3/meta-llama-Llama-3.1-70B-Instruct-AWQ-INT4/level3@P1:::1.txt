This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in image or video datasets, which is essential for domain adaptation in visual recognition problems. The authors introduce two key properties that latent domains should possess: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. They devise a nonparametric formulation and efficient optimization procedure to discover domains that satisfy these properties. The proposed approach is extensively evaluated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, and the results show that it outperforms the original datasets and other competing methods.

The paper is well-written and appears to be novel. The authors provide a clear motivation for their work, discussing the limitations of current domain adaptation techniques and the need for a more principled approach to discovering latent domains. The proposed method is thoroughly described, and the experiments are well-designed to evaluate its effectiveness.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to identify latent domains that are not apparent from the original datasets. The authors demonstrate that their approach can discover domains that are determined by factors such as object pose, appearance, and background, which are not controlled by the dataset collectors. This is a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation, as it provides a more nuanced understanding of the underlying structure of the data.

The paper also provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed approach, comparing it to other methods and demonstrating its effectiveness on various datasets. The authors use a range of evaluation metrics, including recognition accuracy and domain-wise cross-validation, to assess the performance of their approach. The results show that the proposed method outperforms other methods, including k-means and random partitioning, and that it is able to adapt to new test datasets.

However, there are some concerns and questions that need to be addressed. One of the main concerns is the motivation behind the proposed approach. The authors claim that clustering by appearance yields per-category domains, but there is no evidence to support this claim. Additionally, it is unclear how the model prevents this from happening. The authors should provide more evidence to support their claims and clarify how their approach differs from other methods.

Another concern is the necessity of the label-prior constraint. The authors should provide results without this constraint and compare them to other methods, such as k-means and unsupervised object discovery baselines, and random equal partitioning. This would help to clarify the importance of this constraint and how it contributes to the overall performance of the approach.

There are also some minor corrections and requests for clarification that need to be addressed. For example, the authors should define "maximally different in distribution" and clarify what distribution they are referring to (features, labels, or other). Additionally, they should provide justification or proof for the bound referenced on line 215 and detail the use of the geodesic flow kernel.
