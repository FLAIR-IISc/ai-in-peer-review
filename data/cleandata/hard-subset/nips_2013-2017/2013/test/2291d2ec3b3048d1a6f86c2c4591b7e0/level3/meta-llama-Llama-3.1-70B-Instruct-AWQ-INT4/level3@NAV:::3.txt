This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in image or video datasets. The authors introduce two key properties that latent domains should possess: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. They develop a nonparametric formulation and efficient optimization procedure to identify these domains. The approach is evaluated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, and the results show that the identified domains outperform the original datasets and the domains discovered by a competing method.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its advantages over existing methods. The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. However, there are some issues with the clarity of the paper, particularly in the experimental section. The notation and terminology used in the paper are not always consistent, which can make it difficult to follow.

The novelty of the paper lies in its ability to control class-label distributions in a relaxed convex formulation for latent-domain discovery and selecting the number of domains by checking per-domain classification quality. The authors also provide a clear discussion of the related work and how their approach differs from existing methods.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors claim that the problem is unsupervised, but their method uses semantic class labels, which can be seen as a form of supervision. Additionally, the dataset/source-target selection rationale is not clearly explained, and the experimental results could be improved by providing more details on the qualitative results.

Overall, the paper makes a notable contribution to the field of domain adaptation, but several conceptual and experimental clarifications are needed to make the paper more robust.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to automatic domain discovery, which is a challenging problem in the field of domain adaptation.
* The authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and its advantages over existing methods.
* The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The paper has some clarity issues, particularly in the experimental section.
* The notation and terminology used in the paper are not always consistent.
* The authors claim that the problem is unsupervised, but their method uses semantic class labels, which can be seen as a form of supervision.
* The dataset/source-target selection rationale is not clearly explained.

Rating: 7/10

Recommendation: The paper is a good fit for the conference, but it needs some revisions to address the clarity issues and provide more details on the experimental results. The authors should also clarify the dataset/source-target selection rationale and provide more details on the qualitative results.
