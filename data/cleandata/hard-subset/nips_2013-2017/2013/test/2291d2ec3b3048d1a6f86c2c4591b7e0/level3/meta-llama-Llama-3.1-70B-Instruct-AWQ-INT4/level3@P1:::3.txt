The paper "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation" presents a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in image or video datasets. The authors propose two key properties for the discovered domains: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. Maximum distinctiveness ensures that the underlying distributions of the identified domains are different from each other to the maximum extent, while maximum learnability ensures that a strong discriminative model can be learned from the domain.

The authors devise a nonparametric formulation and efficient optimization procedure to discover domains among both training and test data. They evaluate their approach on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving adaptation performance.

The paper's main contributions are:

1. A convex framework for splitting data into K latent domains with similar within-domain class distributions and maximal pairwise distinctiveness in RKHS.
2. A method for selecting K by maximizing average learnability.
3. A novel approach to controlling class-label distributions in a relaxed convex formulation for latent-domain discovery.

The paper's strengths include:

1. A clear and well-motivated problem statement, highlighting the limitations of current domain adaptation approaches.
2. A thorough evaluation of the proposed approach on multiple datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving adaptation performance.
3. A comparison with existing methods, including a recent approach to latent domain discovery.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses:

1. The terminology used in the paper can be confusing, with inconsistent use of terms such as "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain." Clear definitions and examples would help to clarify these concepts.
2. The experimental section could be improved, particularly in Section 4.2, which is unclear. A more detailed explanation of the experimental setup and results would be helpful.
3. The paper's claim that the problem is unsupervised is misleading, as the method uses semantic class labels. While the approach does not require explicit domain labels, it still relies on labeled data.
4. The paper could benefit from more conceptual and experimental clarifications, such as a clearer explanation of the relationship between the proposed approach and existing methods.

Overall, the paper presents a notable contribution to the field of domain adaptation, but could benefit from some revisions to address the above-mentioned weaknesses.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to domain adaptation.
* The evaluation is thorough and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The paper compares favorably with existing methods, including a recent approach to latent domain discovery.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The terminology used in the paper can be confusing, and clear definitions and examples are needed.
* The experimental section could be improved, particularly in Section 4.2.
* The paper's claim that the problem is unsupervised is misleading, and the approach relies on labeled data.
* The paper could benefit from more conceptual and experimental clarifications.

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address the above-mentioned weaknesses.
