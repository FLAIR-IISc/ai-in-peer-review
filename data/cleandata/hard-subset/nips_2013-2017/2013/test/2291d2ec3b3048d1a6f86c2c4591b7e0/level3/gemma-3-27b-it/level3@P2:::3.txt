This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains within image and video datasets, a crucial step for effective domain adaptation. The authors argue that equating datasets with domains is a weak proxy for the complex factors influencing data distribution, and introduce a framework that seeks domains characterized by both maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. They formulate this as a non-parametric optimization problem, providing an efficient procedure for domain discovery and demonstrating its effectiveness on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks.

The work addresses an important problem in domain adaptation. The common practice of treating entire datasets as single domains often overlooks inherent heterogeneity that can significantly hinder adaptation performance. This paper’s central idea – to actively *reshape* datasets into more meaningful domains – is a valuable contribution. The proposed formulation, leveraging kernel methods to quantify domain distinctiveness and cross-validation to assess learnability, is well-motivated and provides a principled approach to this challenging problem. The method’s ability to automatically determine the number of latent domains (K) using domain-wise cross-validation is a significant strength.

The paper is generally well-written, though some areas could benefit from clarification (discussed below). The experiments are reasonably comprehensive, evaluating the approach on standard datasets and demonstrating improvements over baseline methods. The inclusion of qualitative results (Figure 1) provides valuable insight into the types of factors driving domain discovery. 

However, several weaknesses require attention. The terminology is frequently inconsistent. The terms “dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain” are often used interchangeably, leading to confusion. A clear and consistent definition of each term, along with illustrative examples, is essential. For instance, differentiating between a dataset as a collection of images and a domain as a statistically distinct sub-population within that dataset needs emphasis.

The claim that this is an “unsupervised” approach is somewhat misleading. While the method doesn’t require *explicit* domain labels, it crucially relies on semantic class labels (ym) during the optimization process (specifically, in the label prior constraint). This dependency on labelled data should be acknowledged more explicitly.  The method discovers domains *conditioned* on class labels.

The experimental section (4.2) is not as clear as it could be. The definitions of “GOURS,” “GORIG,” and “GOTHER” are provided, but the rationale behind the dataset/source-target selection isn't fully explained. While the authors mention leaving out one dataset for testing, the logic behind choosing which dataset to leave out (and repeating this process) isn’t elaborated. A more systematic approach, such as a leave-one-dataset-out cross-validation scheme, would strengthen the experimental validation. Moreover, Equation (7) refers to `max_k r(U_k, B)` without prior definition of this notation, which disrupts the flow of understanding.

The relaxation of the integer programming problem, while necessary for computational tractability, introduces approximations. The impact of this relaxation on the quality of the discovered domains should be discussed.  Furthermore, the choice of kernel parameters (specifically, the bandwidth) could influence the results. A sensitivity analysis demonstrating the robustness of the approach to different parameter settings would be beneficial.

Finally, the constraint enforcing similar class distributions within each domain (LPC) could be overly restrictive. While ensuring a reasonable distribution of classes is sensible, it might prevent the discovery of domains where certain classes are naturally more prevalent due to underlying factors (e.g., a domain consisting primarily of images of horses may naturally have fewer cows).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to automatic domain discovery, addressing a critical problem in domain adaptation.
*   Well-motivated formulation based on principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness on standard datasets.
*   The automatic determination of K using DWCV is a strong feature.
*   Provides a principled alternative to equating datasets with domains.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Inconsistent terminology and lack of clear definitions.
*   Misleading claim of “unsupervised” learning.
*   Experimental section lacks clarity and systematic evaluation.
*   Potential limitations of the label prior constraint.
*   Impact of the optimization relaxation and kernel parameter choices not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates promising results. However, several issues related to clarity, experimental rigor, and methodological limitations need to be addressed before the paper can be considered for acceptance. I recommend a **weak accept** contingent on the authors addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding terminology, experimental details, and a more nuanced discussion of the method’s assumptions and limitations. Substantial revisions addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
