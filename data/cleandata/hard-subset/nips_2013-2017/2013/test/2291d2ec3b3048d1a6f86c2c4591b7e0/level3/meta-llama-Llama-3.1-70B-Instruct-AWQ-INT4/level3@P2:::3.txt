This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in image or video datasets, which is essential for domain adaptation in visual recognition problems. The authors introduce two key properties that latent domains should possess: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. They devise a nonparametric formulation and efficient optimization procedure to discover domains among both training and test data.

The paper is well-motivated, and the problem of discovering latent domains is well-defined. The authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of existing approaches and the need for a new method. The proposed approach is technically sound, and the use of a kernel-based method to measure domain discrepancies is a good choice. The optimization problem is well-formulated, and the relaxation into a continuous optimization is a good way to make the problem more tractable.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach. However, there are some clarity issues, particularly in the experimental section. The terminology used is sometimes inconsistent, and the reader may find it difficult to understand the differences between datasets, domains, and latent domains. Additionally, the experimental section could be improved by providing more details on the dataset/source-target selection rationale.

The experiments are adequate, and the results show reasonable gains. However, the qualitative results could be more informative, and the authors could provide more insights into the factors that dominate the identified domains. The authors also claim that the problem is unsupervised, which is misleading since the method uses semantic class labels.

The paper's contribution is notable, but several conceptual and experimental clarifications are needed. The authors should provide clear definitions and examples of datasets, domains, and latent domains. They should also improve the experimental section by providing more details on the dataset/source-target selection rationale and the qualitative results.

The paper is related to previous work on domain adaptation, but the authors provide a clear explanation of the differences between their approach and existing methods. The paper is well-cited, and the authors provide a comprehensive list of references.

In terms of the criteria described in the qualitative evaluation section, the paper scores well on the following:

* Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the proposed approach is well-formulated.
