This paper addresses a fundamental, yet often overlooked, problem in domain adaptation: the definition of a "domain". The standard practice of equating entire datasets with domains is a weak proxy for the underlying data-generating factors (e.g., illumination, pose, background). The authors propose a novel framework to automatically discover latent domains within a collection of datasets. The core idea is to partition the data into K domains based on two principles: 1) Maximum Distinctiveness, where the distributions of the discovered domains are made as different as possible, measured non-parametrically in an RKHS, and 2) Maximum Learnability, where each discovered domain must contain enough information to train a strong classifier. The number of domains, K, is chosen by finding the partition that maximizes a domain-wise cross-validation accuracy (learnability). The discovery process is formulated as a relaxed convex quadratic program. The authors demonstrate on object and activity recognition tasks that adapting from these automatically discovered domains can improve performance over adapting from the original, dataset-defined domains.

This work is well-positioned within the domain adaptation literature. While most prior work (e.g., [2, 4, 8]) assumes pre-defined source and target domains, this paper challenges this premise. The most related work is Hoffman et al. [20], which also aimed to discover latent domains. However, that work relied on parametric Gaussian mixture models, whereas the present work uses a more flexible non-parametric kernel method. The concept of maximizing distinctiveness also relates to generalized clustering methods like [27], but is here tailored for domain discovery using label information.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles the critical and practical problem of defining what constitutes a domain in visual data. Questioning the "dataset = domain" assumption and proposing a principled way to automatically discover more meaningful latent domains is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Principled Technical Approach:** The formulation is elegant. Using a non-parametric kernel-based measure (related to MMD) to enforce maximum distinctiveness is well-motivated, as it avoids making strong distributional assumptions about the data. The subsequent relaxation into a convex optimization problem provides a practical algorithm.

3.  **Learnability Criterion for Model Selection:** The proposal of Domain-Wise Cross-Validation (DWCV) to select the number of domains K is a strong point. It directly links the model selection problem to the ultimate goal of learning effective classifiers, ensuring that the discovered domains are not just statistically distinct but also useful for discrimination.

4.  **Promising Experimental Results:** The experiments show clear benefits of the proposed approach. Reshaping datasets into latent domains improves the "oracle" adaptation performance (Table 1) and also leads to gains in practical multi-source adaptation scenarios (Table 2). The qualitative results in Figure 1 provide valuable insight into what kinds of visual factors the method discovers.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The constraint in Eq. 3, which forces the class-label distribution within each discovered domain to match the global class prior, is a major conceptual concern. While this constraint cleverly prevents the trivial solution of partitioning the data by class, it imposes a very strong and often unrealistic assumption. Many true latent domains (e.g., a specific object pose or background) are naturally correlated with certain object classes. For instance, cars are more likely to be seen on roads, while cows are more likely to be in fields. The LPC actively prevents the discovery of such semantically meaningful domains. The paper should discuss the limitations of this assumption and ideally present an ablation study on its effects.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from several clarity issues that hinder understanding.
    *   The terminology is inconsistent. The terms "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain" are used interchangeably at times. A clear, upfront definition of each term and its role in the framework would be very helpful.
    *   The experimental section, particularly 4.2, is difficult to follow. The description of the evaluation metric `GOURS` and the setup for Table 1 and 2 need to be explained more clearly. For instance, what is the distribution P over which the expectation is taken in Eq. 6, and how is the approximation justified?
    *   There appears to be a typo/unclear notation in the text surrounding Eq. 6. The paper defines `r(A -> B)` but the equation uses `r(Uk, B)`. This should likely be `r(Uk -> B)` for consistency.

3.  **Claim of being "Unsupervised":** The introduction describes the problem as "unsupervised learning". This is misleading, as the proposed method makes critical use of the class labels `y_m` in two places: enforcing the Label Prior Constraint and evaluating learnability via cross-validation accuracy. The method does not use domain labels, but it is not unsupervised with respect to the primary task labels. This should be clarified.

4.  **Experimental Design:** While the results are positive, the rationale for choosing specific dataset combinations for source and target is not explained. A more systematic evaluation, for example, a leave-one-dataset-out protocol where each dataset is held out as a target in turn, would make the empirical claims more robust and generalizable.

### Summary of Arguments

**Pro acceptance:**
*   Addresses a fundamental and important problem in domain adaptation.
*   Proposes a novel and principled framework based on distinctiveness and learnability.
*   The technical formulation is sound (non-parametric, convex relaxation).
*   Demonstrates clear empirical improvements over the standard approach.

**Con acceptance:**
*   The Label Prior Constraint is a strong, potentially flawed assumption that limits the method's applicability.
*   Significant clarity issues in terminology and the experimental section.
*   The claim of being an "unsupervised" method is inaccurate.
*   Experimental protocol could be more systematic and convincing.

Overall, this paper introduces a novel and thought-provoking idea that could have a significant impact on how the community approaches domain adaptation. However, the current manuscript is hampered by a strong conceptual limitation (the LPC) and several clarity issues. I believe the contribution is notable, but I would recommend major revisions to address the weaknesses, particularly by including a thorough discussion and analysis of the LPC's limitations and by improving the clarity of the experimental methodology, before the paper is ready for publication.
