## Review of “Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation”

This paper addresses the crucial problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition, arguing that equating datasets with domains is a weak proxy for the underlying factors causing distribution mismatches. The authors propose a novel approach to *automatically* discover latent domains within image and video datasets, guided by the principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. Their formulation leverages a nonparametric approach and efficient optimization, demonstrated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks.

The motivation is well-presented. Existing domain adaptation literature largely focuses on *how* to adapt, while this work tackles the fundamental question of *what constitutes a domain* in visual data. The argument that manually defining properties is impractical and datasets are often collected without explicit domain awareness is compelling. The connection to prior work, including references to domain adaptation techniques [1, 2, 3, 4] and related areas like speech processing [5, 6] and statistical learning [7, 8, 9, 10], is solid. 

A major strength of the paper lies in its proposed approach. The axiomatic properties of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability provide a sound theoretical foundation. The use of kernel methods to quantify domain differences and the formulation of the optimization problem are well-described. The approach to automatically determine the number of domains via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) is also a valuable contribution.  The experimental results are promising, consistently showing improvements over existing methods and highlighting the benefits of reshaping both training and test datasets. The qualitative analysis in Figure 1 provides intuitive understanding of the discovered domains.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the authors assert that simply clustering by appearance is insufficient, a more thorough empirical comparison against standard clustering algorithms (e.g., k-means) and unsupervised object discovery methods would strengthen their argument. The necessity of the label-prior constraint (LPC) remains unclear, and the paper would benefit from a presentation of results without this constraint.  A clearer definition of "maximally different in distribution" is needed; specifying whether this refers to feature distributions, label distributions, or a combination thereof would increase clarity. 

Several points require clarification. The formulation of β<sub>mk</sub> (line 166) appears to already reside on a simplex, raising a question about its relaxation. The constraints around line 182 appear different from earlier formulations – a reconciliation is needed. The justification for the bound referenced on line 215 needs to be explicitly stated. Details about the implementation of the geodesic flow kernel (e.g., whether it was reimplemented or a publicly available code was used) would improve reproducibility.  Finally, a more in-depth discussion of why the proposed approach outperforms baselines, and specific examples of baseline failures that this method overcomes, would be beneficial.  I noted a minor typo in Equation (1): M'k should be Mk'.

The paper builds upon and extends existing literature, notably referencing Hoffman et al. [20], and acknowledges the related work in generalized clustering [27] and multi-source domain adaptation [28, 29, 10]. I recommend including a citation to Torralba & Efros [36] ("Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias," CVPR 2011) as it provides a valuable perspective on dataset bias in visual recognition. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to a fundamental problem in domain adaptation.
*   Strong theoretical foundation based on maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.
*   Promising experimental results demonstrating improved performance compared to existing methods.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient comparison to simple baselines (e.g., k-means clustering).
*   Lack of clarity regarding the necessity of the label-prior constraint.
*   Some minor technical clarifications needed (definitions, constraints, justifications).



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. While some clarification and additional comparisons would strengthen the manuscript, the novelty of the approach, the soundness of the theoretical foundation, and the encouraging experimental results make it a strong submission.  After reviewing the rebuttal and the authors' response to the concerns, I lean slightly toward acceptance. The authors have addressed my concerns regarding the baselines and provided clarifications on the technical points raised. The work is a substantial contribution and should be of interest to the NIPS community.



**Confidence:** 7/10



**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.
