## Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper tackles the crucial problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition, specifically addressing the limitations of equating datasets with domains. The authors propose a novel approach to *automatically discover latent domains* within training data, aiming to improve generalization to test sets. This is achieved through a kernel-based formulation that maximizes inter-domain differences while ensuring learnability within each domain, formulated as a relaxed quadratic optimization problem. The approach is evaluated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, demonstrating performance gains over a prior domain adaptation method.

The paper is well-written and presents a clear and novel idea. The motivation for automatically discovering domains is sound, given the complexities of real-world visual data and the shortcomings of simply treating entire datasets as single domains. The formulation, while mathematically involved, is presented logically, and the experimental results provide encouraging evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The inclusion of results on both static images and video action recognition strengthens the findings.

However, several concerns remain. The motivation for *why* clustering by appearance alone is insufficient, while stated, isn’t fully convincing. The authors claim it leads to per-category domains, but more evidence or a more detailed explanation of why their method avoids this pitfall would be beneficial. Furthermore, the necessity of the label-prior constraint (LPC) is unclear. The paper needs to demonstrate results *without* the LPC and compare performance to simpler unsupervised clustering methods like k-means, or object discovery baselines, and even random partitioning, to justify its inclusion. 

Several clarifications are needed. The definition of “maximally different in distribution” needs to be more precise – what distribution is being referred to (features, labels, or something else)? Additionally, questions regarding the mathematical formulation remain: does β_{mk} already lie on a simplex, and are the constraints around line 182 consistent with earlier constraints? Justification for the bound referenced on line 215 is also required. More details on the implementation of the geodesic flow kernel (reimplementation or use of publicly available code) would also enhance reproducibility. Finally, providing insights into *why* the proposed approach outperforms baselines and showcasing systematic baseline failures the method overcomes would significantly strengthen the contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Novel approach to a challenging problem, clear writing, promising experimental results.
**Arguments against Acceptance:** Unclear motivation for certain design choices (LPC), lack of comparison to simpler baselines, need for clarifications regarding the mathematical formulation.

Overall, this is a promising paper with a novel contribution. Addressing the concerns raised, particularly regarding the justification of the LPC and comparison to baselines, would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its likelihood of acceptance. I currently lean slightly towards acceptance pending a strong rebuttal.
