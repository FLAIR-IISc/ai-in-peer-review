

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in domain adaptation: the difficulty of manually defining meaningful domains in visual datasets, where factors like pose, lighting, and background interact complexly. The authors propose a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains by enforcing two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should have maximally different distributions from each other in RKHS) and maximum learnability (domains should support strong discriminative models). Their approach formulates the problem as a convex optimization with a label prior constraint (LPC) to ensure consistent class distributions across domains, and uses domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to determine the optimal number of domains K.

The paper relates well to prior work on domain adaptation (e.g., Saenko et al., 2010) and domain discovery (Hoffman et al., 2012). While Hoffman et al. use parametric mixture models, this work advances the field with nonparametric methods that avoid distributional assumptions, making it more suitable for complex visual data. The authors also position their work against related clustering approaches (Jegelka et al., 2011) and multi-domain adaptation methods.

**Strengths:**
1. **Originality**: The dual criteria of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability provide a principled framework for latent domain discovery, distinct from prior clustering approaches. The relaxed convex formulation with LPC is a notable technical contribution.
2. **Significance**: The method addresses a real problem in visual domain adaptation—datasets often contain multiple latent domains that are not captured by simply treating datasets as domains. The empirical results demonstrate meaningful improvements over standard dataset-based approaches.
3. **Quality**: The experimental validation is thorough, showing improvements across object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and action recognition (IXMAS) tasks. The qualitative analysis of identified domains (Fig. 1) provides useful insights into the factors driving domain structure.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Conceptual clarity**: The paper suffers from inconsistent terminology (dataset/domain/latent-domain), which significantly hinders understanding. Clear definitions and examples of these terms are needed. For instance, the distinction between "latent domains" and "datasets" is never explicitly clarified despite being central to the contribution.
2. **Methodological limitations**: The LPC constraint (Eq. 3) forces similar class distributions across domains, which may be limiting for real-world scenarios where class distributions naturally differ (e.g., pose distributions differ between horses and cows). This constraint could artificially merge domains that should be separated.
3. **Experimental design**: The rationale for dataset/source-target selection in Section 4.2 is unclear. The leave-one-dataset-out validation approach would be more convincing than the current setup. The claim that the problem is "unsupervised" is misleading since the method explicitly uses semantic class labels (a critical point that should be clarified).
4. **Presentation errors**: Eq. 7 uses "max_k r(U_k, B)" without defining r(U_k, B). Section 4.2 is confusingly written, making it difficult to understand the experimental protocol.

**Quality**: The technical approach is sound, with a reasonable convex relaxation of the optimization problem. However, the methodological constraint (LPC) and the misleading "unsupervised" claim weaken the technical contribution. The experiments are adequate but would be stronger with more rigorous validation.

**Clarity**: The paper suffers from significant clarity issues that need addressing. The terminology is inconsistent, the explanation of DWCV is unclear, and key notations are undefined (e.g., r(U_k, B)). A more structured presentation with clear definitions would greatly improve readability.

**Originality**: The paper makes a novel contribution in formulating latent domain discovery as a convex optimization problem with explicit constraints on class distributions. While related to Hoffman et al. (2012), the nonparametric approach and DWCV procedure represent meaningful advancements.

**Significance**: The problem addressed is important, and the approach has potential to improve domain adaptation performance. However, the limitations in the method (LPC constraint) and the unclear experimental validation reduce the impact of the contribution.

**Recommendation**: The paper presents a notable contribution to domain adaptation but requires substantial revisions to address clarity, conceptual limitations, and experimental rigor. The authors should:
1. Clarify terminology and explicitly define key concepts
2. Justify the LPC constraint or provide a more flexible alternative
3. Revise the experimental design to include leave-one-dataset-out validation
4. Correct the misleading "unsupervised" claim
5. Define all notation used in equations

The paper has merit but requires significant conceptual and presentation improvements to be accepted. The core idea of latent domain discovery is valuable, but the current formulation has limitations that need addressing. With the suggested revisions, this could be a strong contribution to the field.

**Arguments for acceptance**: Notable technical contribution to latent domain discovery, thorough experimental validation across multiple tasks, meaningful improvements over existing approaches.

**Arguments against acceptance**: Significant conceptual limitations (LPC constraint), unclear terminology, misleading "unsupervised" claim, inadequate experimental validation design.
