This paper tackles the important and often overlooked problem of defining what constitutes a "domain" for visual domain adaptation. Instead of the common practice of treating entire datasets as monolithic domains, the authors propose a novel framework to automatically discover K latent domains within a given data collection. The core idea is to find a partition of the data that maximizes two properties: the pairwise distinctiveness between domains, measured in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS), and the "learnability" of each domain, ensuring that a strong classifier can be trained on it. The number of domains, K, is chosen by maximizing this learnability criterion. This work is most closely related to Hoffman et al. (2012), but distinguishes itself by using a non-parametric, convex formulation rather than assuming a specific generative model like Gaussian mixtures.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality & Significance:** The problem formulation is novel and addresses a fundamental limitation in domain adaptation research. The proposal to jointly optimize for domain distinctiveness while controlling for within-domain class distributions is a significant contribution. The method for automatically selecting the number of domains, K, based on downstream classification performance is also a strong, practical component of the framework.
*   **Technical Approach:** The use of a relaxed convex optimization framework makes the problem tractable and is technically sound. The experimental results, though needing clarification, demonstrate reasonable performance gains over baseline approaches, validating the core idea.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues that impede full understanding and reproducibility. The terminology for "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain" is used inconsistently, creating confusion. For instance, Section 4.2 is particularly difficult to follow. The authors should provide precise definitions and a consistent usage pattern throughout the manuscript. Additionally, a minor but important presentation bug exists: the notation `r(Uk, B)` is used in Equation 6 but is not explicitly defined, leaving the reader to infer its meaning from context.
*   **Conceptual & Experimental Issues:** The claim that the problem is "unsupervised" is misleading, as the formulation explicitly uses semantic class labels to enforce the Label Prior Constraint (LPC). While this constraint is clever in preventing trivial solutions (e.g., domains containing a single class), its core assumption—that class priors are consistent across all latent domains—is a strong one that may not hold for real-world factors like object pose, where distributions can be class-dependent. The experimental rationale for selecting source and target datasets is also unclear; a more systematic evaluation, such as a leave-one-dataset-out protocol, would strengthen the validation.

In summary, this paper presents a notable and promising approach to a challenging problem. However, its acceptance is conditional on major revisions to address the critical issues in clarity, the misleading "unsupervised" claim, and the justification for experimental design.
