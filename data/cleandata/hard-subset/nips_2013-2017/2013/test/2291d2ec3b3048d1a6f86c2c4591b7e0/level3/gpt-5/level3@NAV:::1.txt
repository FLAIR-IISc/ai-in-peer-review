Summary: The paper tackles an important problem in domain adaptation for vision: discovering latent domains within heterogeneous datasets so that adaptation can be performed between meaningful distributions rather than ad hoc “dataset = domain” partitions. The method formalizes “maximum distinctiveness” via a kernel mean embedding difference (essentially MMD) over feature distributions and poses domain assignment as an integer program with (i) single-assignment constraints and (ii) a label-prior constraint (LPC); it relaxes to a continuous quadratic program and selects the number of domains by domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to enforce “maximum learnability.” Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256/A/D/W) and IXMAS multi-view actions show gains over prior latent-domain discovery (Hoffman et al.) and adaptation pipelines (e.g., GFK).

Relation to prior work: The approach is related to two-sample testing via kernel embeddings (Gretton et al.), generalized clustering (Jegelka et al.), and multi-source adaptation, and addresses dataset bias concerns (cf. Torralba & Efros, CVPR’11). Compared to Hoffman et al., this work is nonparametric and explicitly couples discovery with learnability.

Quality: The optimization and evaluation are generally sound, but several clarifications/ablations are needed for rigor and reproducibility:
- Clarify precisely what “maximally different in distribution” means: is TDD defined over Pk(x) only, or over class-conditional Pk(x|y) or joint Pk(x, y)? Currently the objective uses only features, while labels enter via LPC.
- Does βmk, as introduced (βk in a simplex), already lie on a simplex per k? If so, please explain the additional per-instance bounds 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C and how they derive from Mk ≥ C and Mk ≤ M.
- The constraints stated in the relaxed problem differ from the original LPC (exact match) and assignment constraints; please reconcile lines around the two formulations and justify the δ tolerance.
- Provide a justification/proof for the DWCV bound Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C}.
- Necessity of LPC: include ablations without LPC and comparisons to k-means, random equal-size partitioning, and unsupervised object discovery baselines.
- Detail the geodesic flow kernel usage (public code vs. reimplementation, parameters) for reproducibility.
- Offer insights into why the method outperforms baselines, with failure analyses illustrating where appearance clustering collapses to per-category groupings and how LPC/your objective prevent that.

Clarity: Well written overall. Minor: Eq. (1) M′k should be Mk′.

Originality: Novel and thoughtful nonparametric formulation; good positioning vs. ECCV’12 latent domain discovery. Please cite Torralba & Efros (CVPR’11).

Significance: Addressing latent domain structure is impactful; results are promising.

Pros:
- Clear problem and principled objective/selection (DWCV).
- Consistent empirical gains on two domains.

Cons:
- Missing critical ablations/baselines and some theoretical justifications.
- Some constraint/formulation ambiguities.

After rebuttal: The authors addressed most concerns (including ablations and constraint clarifications). I now lean slightly toward accept.
