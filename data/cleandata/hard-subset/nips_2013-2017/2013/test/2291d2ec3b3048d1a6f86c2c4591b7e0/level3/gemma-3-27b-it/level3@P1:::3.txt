## Review of Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation

This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains within visual datasets, aiming to improve domain adaptation performance. The core idea is to reshape datasets into domains that maximize both distinctiveness (difference in distribution) and learnability (ability to learn a strong discriminative model). The authors formulate this as a non-parametric optimization problem with a continuous relaxation, and use a domain-wise cross-validation procedure to determine the optimal number of domains.  Experiments on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

The problem addressed – identifying meaningful domains in visual data without manual annotation – is an important one in domain adaptation. Current practice often equates datasets with domains, which is a weak proxy for the complex factors influencing visual data (lighting, pose, etc.). The paper correctly points out the limitations of this approach and proposes a principled way to discover latent domains directly from the data.  This work builds upon prior work in domain adaptation (e.g., [1, 2, 3, 4]) and unsupervised learning techniques. It also relates to work on dataset bias correction [7] and multi-source domain adaptation [10], although this paper focuses specifically on *discovering* the domains rather than adapting *between* pre-defined ones. The approach of learning domain boundaries is particularly relevant to related work in [20] and [27], and the authors acknowledge this, providing initial comparison. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Formulation:** The proposed formulation, imposing both maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability, is a clever way to approach the challenging problem of latent domain discovery.
* **Non-parametric Approach:** Avoiding parametric assumptions about domain distributions makes the method more flexible and applicable to a wider range of datasets.
* **Automatic K Selection:** The domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure provides an automatic way to determine the optimal number of domains, avoiding the need for manual tuning.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both object recognition and human activity recognition demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The conditional reshaping of the test data is a particularly insightful extension and leads to further improvements.
* **Qualitative Analysis:** The visualization of discovered domains (Figure 1) provides valuable insight into the factors driving domain separation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Terminology & Clarity:** The paper suffers from inconsistent and sometimes confusing terminology.  The distinctions between “dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain” are not always clear and require more precise definitions and illustrative examples.  Section 4.2, in particular, is difficult to follow.
* **Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** While the motivation behind the LPC is understandable, it seems potentially limiting.  Real-world latent factors (e.g., pose) often vary *differently* across different classes. Forcing equal class distributions within each domain may hinder the discovery of truly meaningful domains.
* **"Unsupervised" Claim:**  The claim that the problem is “unsupervised” is misleading, as the method explicitly utilizes semantic class labels during the optimization process. While it does not require manual domain labels, the use of class labels disqualifies it from being truly unsupervised.
* **Experimental Setup:** The rationale for selecting specific source and target datasets in the experiments isn’t fully explained. While the authors use standard datasets, a more systematic evaluation (e.g., a leave-one-dataset-out approach) would strengthen the conclusions. 
* **Missing Definition:** The expression `max_k r(U_k, B)` appears in the text (and is relevant to the discussion of Eq. 7) but is never formally defined.
* **Comparison to Baselines:** While [20] is addressed, more comprehensive comparisons to established unsupervised clustering techniques (e.g., k-means on feature space, spectral clustering) would demonstrate the unique benefits of this approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to latent domain discovery in visual data. The core ideas of maximizing distinctiveness and learnability are well-motivated, and the non-parametric formulation offers flexibility.  The experimental results are encouraging, and the qualitative analysis provides valuable insights. However, several issues regarding clarity, terminology, the LPC constraint, and the claim of being “unsupervised” need to be addressed.  

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The problem is important and well-motivated.
* The proposed approach is novel and technically sound.
* The experimental results are promising.
* The paper has the potential to advance the state-of-the-art in domain adaptation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Clarity issues and inconsistent terminology hinder understanding.
* The LPC constraint may limit the discovery of truly meaningful domains.
* The claim of being “unsupervised” is misleading.
* The experimental setup could be more systematic.




**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should address the clarity issues, provide more precise definitions of key terms, reconsider the potential limitations of the LPC constraint, and clarify the claim of being "unsupervised."  A more detailed explanation of the experimental setup and comparisons to additional baselines would also strengthen the paper. Addressing these points will significantly improve the impact and accessibility of this work.
