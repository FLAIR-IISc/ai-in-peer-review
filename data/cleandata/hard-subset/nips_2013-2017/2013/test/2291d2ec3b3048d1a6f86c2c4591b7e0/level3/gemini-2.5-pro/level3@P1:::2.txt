This paper addresses the important and under-explored problem of defining what constitutes a "domain" in visual domain adaptation. The authors rightly criticize the common practice of equating datasets with domains, arguing that a single dataset may be an agglomeration of several distinct latent domains, and that these latent domains are what adaptation methods should operate on. The core proposal is an unsupervised method to automatically reshape one or more datasets into a set of latent domains. The method is driven by two key criteria: 1) **Maximum Distinctiveness**, where the discovered domains should have maximally different underlying feature distributions, and 2) **Maximum Learnability**, where each discovered domain should contain enough coherent data to train a strong, discriminative classifier.

To achieve this, the authors formulate an optimization problem. Maximum distinctiveness is measured non-parametrically as the sum of pairwise Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) between all domain distributions in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The partitioning is found by maximizing this objective subject to constraints, notably a Label Prior Constraint (LPC) which ensures that class priors are preserved within each new domain. The second criterion, maximum learnability, is used not in the main optimization but as a wrapper for model selection: the optimal number of domains, K, is chosen as the one that maximizes the weighted average of domain-wise cross-validation accuracy. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach on object and action recognition tasks, showing that adapting from the discovered domains yields significant performance improvements over adapting from the original datasets and outperforms the most relevant prior work on latent domain discovery [20].

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and often overlooked prerequisite for domain adaptation. Questioning the "dataset = domain" assumption is a valuable contribution, and proposing a principled, data-driven method to discover more meaningful domains is a novel and significant research direction. This work could influence how researchers construct and utilize datasets for adaptation studies in the future.
2.  **Soundness of Core Idea:** The two proposed criteria, maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability, are intuitive and well-motivated. Forcing domains to be statistically different while ensuring they remain internally consistent enough to be useful for classification is a powerful combination of principles.
3.  **Strong Experimental Results:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed method. The discovered domains consistently lead to better adaptation performance than using the original datasets as source domains. The gains over the competing method of Hoffman et al. [20] are also significant, validating the authors' non-parametric approach. The qualitative analysis in Figure 1 provides interesting insights into what visual factors (e.g., background, object pose) the method discovers.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Methodological Disconnect:** A key weakness is the separation of the two driving criteria. "Maximum Distinctiveness" is formulated as an optimization objective, but "Maximum Learnability" is only used as a post-hoc model selection heuristic (Domain-Wise Cross-Validation, DWCV) to choose the number of domains K. A more elegant and powerful formulation would integrate both criteria into a single, joint optimization objective. This would allow the partitioning process itself to be directly regularized by the need to form learnable domains, rather than simply selecting K from a series of independent partitioning runs.
2.  **Technical Soundness of Optimization:** The paper first presents an integer programming problem (Eq. 2, 3) to find the optimal domain assignments, then correctly states it is NP-hard. It then proposes a continuous relaxation (Eq. 4, 5). However, the connection between the solution of the relaxed problem and the original integer problem is not discussed. It is not clear how well this relaxation approximates the original objective, and the paper simply states that "existing solvers are adequate in yielding satisfactory solutions" without further analysis or justification.
3.  **Outdated Image Representation:** A major weakness lies in the experimental methodology. The use of a standard Bag-of-Visual-Words (BoW) representation with 800 SURF features is significantly outdated, even for 2013 standards. The performance of visual recognition systems is heavily dependent on the quality of the underlying features. The paper would be far more convincing if it employed stronger, contemporary descriptors. For instance, using spatial pyramid matching over dense SIFT (Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006) or, even better, Fisher Vectors (Perronnin et al., ECCV 2010) would provide a much more robust and modern evaluation. Additionally, the paper does not specify the distance metric used for the BoW histograms; if Euclidean distance was used, it is inappropriate, and a chi-squared or intersection kernel would be more suitable.
4.  **Clarity on Test Set Reshaping:** The idea of "conditionally reshaping" the test set in Section 4.3 is interesting but is explained too briefly. The paper relegates details to supplementary material and does not sufficiently explain the assumptions of this setting. This process appears to use the unlabeled test data in its entirety during the reshaping phase, which places it in a transductive setting. This assumption should be stated and justified explicitly in the main paper, as it is a crucial detail for understanding the experimental setup and its practical applicability.

**Overall Assessment**

Despite its weaknesses, particularly the outdated feature representation and the disjointed optimization criteria, this paper makes a valuable and novel contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The core idea of automatically discovering latent domains is compelling, and the proposed principles of distinctiveness and learnability are sound. The strong empirical results validate the promise of this direction. The work is well-written and clearly motivated. I recommend acceptance, as its conceptual contribution is significant enough to overlook the shortcomings in the experimental setup, with the strong suggestion that the authors address these methodological points and, if possible, update the feature representation for the final version.
