Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles the question of how to discover “domains” in visual datasets when traditional domain definitions (e.g., per-speaker, per-language) do not apply. The authors introduce two desiderata for latent domains: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be as different as possible in distribution) and maximum learnability (each domain should support learning a strong discriminative classifier). They operationalize distinctiveness by maximizing pairwise distributional discrepancies between domains using RKHS mean embeddings (i.e., an MMD-style objective), and constrain per-domain label proportions to match the global class prior (a relaxed “label prior constraint”). The discrete assignment is relaxed to continuous weights β on a simplex and solved as a constrained quadratic program; hard assignments are recovered by argmax over β. The number of domains K is selected via “domain-wise cross-validation” (DWCV): build classifiers within each domain and choose K maximizing the weighted average cross-validated accuracy across domains. The same kernel machinery is used to conditionally reshape test sets to align with discovered training domains. Empirically, the method improves adaptation performance on object recognition (Amazon/DSLR/Webcam/Caltech-256) and cross-view human action recognition (IXMAS), comparing favorably to using raw datasets as domains and to the latent-domain GMM approach of Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12).

The work builds on: nonparametric two-sample tests via kernel mean embeddings (Gretton et al., NIPS’07), multi-source/domain adaptation (e.g., Mansour et al., NIPS’09), and recent visual adaptation techniques (e.g., GFK, CVPR’12). The idea of discovering domains is closely related to Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12), who used hierarchical GMMs; here the authors avoid parametric assumptions and add an explicit “learnability” criterion.

Strengths
- Problem is important and timely: dataset≠domain in vision; discovering latent domains is practically useful for adaptation and dataset design.
- Clear formalization of “distinctiveness” via MMD and “learnability” via DWCV; the latter provides a simple, pragmatic model selection criterion for K.
- Nonparametric formulation removes restrictive Gaussian mixture assumptions and naturally handles complex, overlapping factors.
- Conditional reshaping of test data to match training domains is novel and yields convincing gains for cross-view action recognition.
- Qualitative analyses provide intuition about the discovered factors (pose, background, color).
- Empirical results broadly support the claims and include a comparison to the most relevant prior (Hoffman et al.).

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- The method is not truly unsupervised: it assumes labeled instances and explicitly uses labels both in the label prior constraint and in DWCV. The paper occasionally describes the discovery problem as “unsupervised,” which is misleading and should be corrected.
- The label prior constraint assumes class priors are stable across domains and forces each domain to mirror the global label proportions. This may be unrealistic (label shift is common) and could suppress genuine domain factors that correlate with class frequency (e.g., certain poses occurring mainly for some classes). Please analyze sensitivity to violations (δ, missing classes per domain).
- Optimization is described as “a maximization of a convex quadratic function with linear constraints; though in general still NP-hard.” Maximizing a convex quadratic is non-convex; practical solvers may find local optima. The paper should clarify the solver, initialization, restarts, computational complexity, and robustness across runs.
- Notational and presentation issues hinder clarity. The dataset/domain/latent-domain terminology is used inconsistently; a simple glossary and a schematic would help. In Section 4.2, r(A→B) is defined, but later expressions use r(Uk,B); please unify notation and check equation numbering/definitions (e.g., Eq. for GOURS).
- Experimental design details are under-specified. The rationale for source/target splits is not always clear; a leave-one-dataset-out protocol would be more transparent. The “MATCHING” strategy that reportedly achieves near-oracle performance is not fully defined in the main text.
- Potential “double dipping”: DWCV uses labels from the same data used to form domains. A held-out validation or nested CV would strengthen the selection of K and avoid optimistic bias.
- Limited ablations: no study of bandwidth sensitivity, δ values, or removing LPC; no runtime/scalability analysis.

Quality
Technically grounded: the use of MMD for measuring domain discrepancy is sound, and conditional test reshaping via QP is well-motivated. However, the learning objective is non-convex after relaxation, and practical optimization details are missing. The LPC is a strong assumption; empirical and theoretical support for when it helps vs hurts is needed. Experiments are suggestive but could be more rigorous in protocol and ablations.

Clarity
Readable overall but marred by inconsistent terminology and a few notation/numbering lapses. Section 4.2’s definitions and the description of the multi-source strategies (UNION, ENSEMBLE, MATCHING) need to be made precise. Adding a figure that contrasts “dataset,” “true domain,” and “latent domain,” plus pseudocode for DWCV and conditional reshaping, would help.

Originality
Discovering latent domains with (i) a nonparametric distinctiveness criterion and (ii) a learnability-based model selection is a novel and meaningful combination relative to ECCV’12. Conditional reshaping of test sets to match training domains is also a nice addition.

Significance
If clarified, the approach could impact how adaptation is performed on heterogeneous visual corpora and how datasets are constructed. The reported gains are modest to solid and consistent with the thesis. Wider adoption will depend on relieving or validating the LPC assumption and improving optimization transparency.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Clarify the supervised nature of the method and revise claims of “unsupervised” discovery.
- Provide ablations: without LPC; varying δ; robustness to label shift; sensitivity to kernel bandwidth.
- Detail optimization: solver, initialization, restarts, complexity and runtime; report variance across runs.
- Define MATCHING precisely and ensure no target labels are used. Include leave-one-dataset-out protocols.
- Unify notation for r(·→·) and fix equation references; include a terminology glossary/figure.
- Consider a nested CV or held-out split for selecting K to avoid double counting.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and under-explored problem with a principled, nonparametric formulation.
- Combines distinctiveness and learnability in a coherent framework; practical K selection.
- Demonstrates improvements on standard adaptation benchmarks and an interesting test-reshaping scenario.
- Clear relation and improvement over prior latent-domain work.

Arguments against acceptance
- Mischaracterizes supervision; relies on strong LPC that may not hold in practice.
- Non-convex optimization with limited details; potential instability not assessed.
- Experimental protocol clarity and ablations are insufficient; some claims depend on partially specified procedures (MATCHING).
- Notation/presentation issues reduce clarity.

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on clarifying supervision/assumptions, tightening experimental protocols, and improving optimization and notation details. The core idea is sound and promising, and with these improvements the contribution would be valuable to the NIPS audience.
