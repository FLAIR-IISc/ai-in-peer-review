Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles a practical gap in visual domain adaptation: datasets rarely coincide with true “domains,” and naive cross-dataset adaptation conflates multiple nuisance factors (pose, illumination, background, etc.). The goal is to automatically discover latent domains within given training data so that adaptation to a test set improves. The method defines a kernel-based difference between empirical distributions (via RKHS mean embeddings/MMD) and partitions data to maximize inter-domain difference (“maximum distinctiveness”), subject to constraints. The discrete assignment is relaxed to a continuous quadratic program; the number of domains is chosen by a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) criterion favoring “maximum learnability.” Experiments on two settings—static object categories and IXMAS multi-view actions—show gains over a previous latent-domain approach [19]/[20] and over using original dataset partitions. The work relates to MMD/two-sample tests (Gretton et al.), generalized clustering via kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.), and multi-source adaptation, and contributes a nonparametric domain discovery strategy tailored to visual adaptation. It also leverages the Geodesic Flow Kernel (GFK) for adaptation.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and motivation from dataset bias; well-written overall.
- Conceptual clarity: two complementary criteria—distinctiveness (distributional difference) and learnability (predictive utility).
- Nonparametric approach avoids restrictive parametric mixture assumptions used in prior latent domain discovery (e.g., [20]).
- Practical selection of K via DWCV tied to downstream discriminative performance.
- Empirical improvements on object recognition and multi-view action recognition; demonstration that learned domains outperform original datasets and [19]/[20].
- Appreciated analysis of dominant factors in discovered domains and conditional reshaping of heterogeneous test sets.

Weaknesses and main concerns
- Motivation/robustness to per-category clustering: It remains unclear why optimizing distributional distinctiveness over Pk(x) will not simply split by category appearance rather than nuisance factors. The paper mentions this risk but does not show how the model prevents it beyond LPC.
- Necessity of the label-prior constraint (LPC): The paper does not establish whether LPC is essential for preventing per-class clustering, nor quantify its effect. Ablations without LPC are missing in the main text; comparisons to k-means, unsupervised object discovery baselines, and random equal partitions are either absent or reported only anecdotally (“omitted for brevity”).
- Technical clarity: constraints in the integer vs. relaxed formulations are not perfectly aligned; some notation/typos in Eq. (1) are confusing.
- Optimization details: the relaxed problem remains NP-hard in general; practical solver choice, initialization, and convergence behavior need more transparency.

Specific questions and requests for clarification
- What exactly is “maximally different in distribution”? You initially define domains over Pk(x, y), but the distinctiveness is measured using only Pk(x) via RKHS mean embeddings. Please state explicitly that the objective optimizes differences over features only (Pk(x)), not joint distributions, and discuss implications for per-class splits.
- βmk/simplex (line ~166): Since βk lies on the simplex (sum_m βmk = 1 and βmk ≥ 0), what ensures that each data point is dominantly assigned to only one domain other than the final argmax step? Are additional entropy/peakedness incentives applied? Please clarify how the per-instance constraint 1/M ≤ Σk βmk ≤ 1/C interacts with simplex constraints per k.
- Constraint mismatch (around line ~182): The stated constraints in the relaxed problem (Eq. 5) appear different in spirit from the earlier discrete constraints (each point assigned to exactly one domain; LPC). Please reconcile the two formulations and justify the lower/upper bounds 1/M and 1/C on Σk βmk.
- Bound on K (line ~215): Please justify or prove Kmax ≤ min{M/(N C), C} for N-fold CV. Is this ensuring at least N samples per class per domain across folds? Provide a short derivation and clarify whether this bound is sufficient or only heuristic.
- βmk on a simplex: As written, βk ∈ Δ implies Σm βmk = 1 for each k. Combined with the per-instance bound, this can bias solutions toward balanced Mk. Is that intended? Please discuss sensitivity to class imbalance across latent domains.
- GFK usage: Did you reimplement the geodesic flow kernel or use publicly available code? Specify code source, hyperparameters (subspace dimension, PCA preprocessing), and how target subspaces were estimated in the multi-source setting.

Requested experiments/analyses
- Ablation: results without LPC, and sensitivity to δ. Show whether LPC prevents per-category clusters and quantify performance drop without it.
- Baselines: include k-means on x (with and without label balancing), unsupervised object discovery clustering methods, and random equal partitioning, all with the same adaptation pipeline. Report both oracle and practical multi-source adaptation accuracies.
- Failure analysis: Provide concrete insights into why your approach outperforms baselines—e.g., cluster purity w.r.t. nuisance factors (pose/background), class purity within domains, domain-size distributions, cross-domain confusion matrices, and examples where k-means collapses to per-class clusters but your method does not.
- Optimization details: describe the solver, initialization, runtime, and stability across random seeds.

Minor issues
- Eq. (1): M'k should be Mk' (typo).
- Cite Torralba & Efros, “Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias” (CVPR 2011), as it closely frames the motivation and evaluation protocol for cross-dataset/domain issues.

Quality
- Sound core idea leveraging MMD with a learnability-based model selection. Some technical ambiguities (constraints, optimization hardness) and missing ablations weaken the case but are addressable.

Clarity
- Generally well-written. Needs clearer statements on which distributions are compared, precise constraints mapping, solver details, and the role/necessity of LPC.

Originality
- Novel combination: nonparametric latent domain discovery with an explicit distinctiveness objective and a learnability-driven K selection. Differentiates from prior parametric latent-domain work.

Significance
- Addresses a prevalent issue in visual adaptation; potential for broad impact in practice. Reported gains over [19]/[20] and over dataset-level baselines are valuable, especially for heterogeneous test sets.

Pros
- Addresses an important, under-explored problem.
- Principled nonparametric objective; practical K selection.
- Empirical gains on two diverse settings; conditional test reshaping is compelling.

Cons
- Insufficient ablation/baseline comparisons; reliance on LPC left unquantified.
- Some formulation/notation inconsistencies and missing optimization details.

Post-rebuttal note and recommendation
The rebuttal clarified that “maximally different” refers to Pk(x) via MMD, reconciled the constraint forms, and provided additional ablations (no-LPC, k-means, random) showing LPC is beneficial and that k-means tends to split by category. They also clarified the K bound rationale and GFK implementation details. With these addressed and with promised revisions, I now lean slightly toward accept.
