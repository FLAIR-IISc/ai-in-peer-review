Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles an under-explored but important problem in domain adaptation: how to automatically define domains in visual datasets. Rather than equating datasets with domains, the authors propose to partition all images across multiple datasets into latent domains that are (i) maximally distinctive in feature space and (ii) maximally learnable in terms of supporting strong discriminative models for the original class labels. Distinctiveness is formalized nonparametrically using the MMD distance between kernel mean embeddings, summed over all domain pairs (TDD). Learnability is used to select the number of domains K via a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) criterion that aggregates per-domain classification accuracy. The partitioning is formulated first as an integer program with a label prior constraint (LPC) to preserve class priors per domain, then relaxed to a continuous quadratic program. The paper also introduces “conditional reshaping” of the test set: discovering test-time domains that best match the training-side discovered domains. Experiments on Office/Caltech object recognition and IXMAS action recognition show gains over using whole datasets as domains and over the latent-domain discovery method of Hoffman et al. [19]. The work relates to multi-source domain adaptation, MMD-based two-sample tests [21], and generalized clustering [27], but differs by explicitly aiming to discover domains that help train stronger class-discriminative models, not to cluster categories or fit a parametric mixture.

Strengths
- Novel problem framing: Automatic discovery of domains for adaptation, rather than assuming datasets are domains. This is timely and relevant to NIPS.
- Nonparametric distinctiveness: Using kernel mean embeddings/MMD avoids fragile parametric mixture assumptions and is broadly applicable.
- Clear learning objective for K: The DWCV criterion ties domain discovery to downstream discriminative utility.
- Empirical gains: Consistent improvements over “dataset-as-domain” baselines and over [19], and further gains when reshaping the heterogeneous test set in IXMAS.
- Generally well written and easy to follow; the LPC is a reasonable inductive bias.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Disconnect between objectives: The two driving criteria are not integrated. Maximum learnability is only used as an outer-loop model selection wrapper for choosing K, not in the partitioning objective itself. A joint optimization (e.g., maximize TDD subject to, or regularized by, in-domain classification loss/margin) or a bilevel formulation with differentiable surrogate for learnability would better align discovery with downstream use.
- Optimization clarity: The relaxed problem maximizes a convex quadratic subject to linear constraints, which is generally hard. It is not demonstrated that the continuous solution, after rounding to hard assignments, closely satisfies the discrete objectives (2)+(3). Provide guarantees or empirical diagnostics (duality gaps, integrality gaps, sensitivity to δ, stability across random restarts), and ablations of LPC’s tightness (δ = 1% seems very restrictive).
- Use of test set in “reshaping”: Conditional reshaping optimizes test-instance assignments to match training domains. This is interesting but poorly explained. What information from the test set is used (features only vs. any labels)? Does it assume full transductive access to all test instances at once (an imputation-style setting)? Clarify protocol, what is available at training vs. test time, and ensure comparisons keep this assumption consistent across methods.
- Representation is outdated/weak: A single bag-of-words with sparse SURF and 800 codewords is not competitive even by 2013 standards. This likely underestimates the method’s potential and complicates comparisons.
  - Please upgrade to stronger descriptors: spatial pyramid with dense features (Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006) and Fisher Vectors (Perronnin ECCV 2010). Report whether conclusions hold.
- Similarity measure for histograms: It is unclear which distance underlies the Gaussian kernel for histogram features. Euclidean distance on count histograms is inappropriate; use χ2 or histogram intersection kernels (or Hellinger-normalized features) and tune bandwidth accordingly. Report sensitivity to kernel choice and bandwidth.
- Limited integration with adaptation: Experiments rely on GFK; given the discovered domains, it would be useful to also show results with alternative multi-source adaptation methods, to establish robustness.
- Missing figures: The paper would benefit from more illustrative figures of discovered domains and ablation plots for K selection.

Quality
Technically sound overall and well-motivated, with significant empirical validation. However, the optimization–objective gap and the lack of a joint separability–learnability objective reduce rigor. The experimental protocol for test reshaping needs clearer, reproducible detail. Representation choices are dated.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. Add more explanatory figures, make the conditional reshaping procedure and LPC rationale more explicit, and specify the histogram similarity/kernel in detail.

Originality
High. Automatically defining domains for adaptation is novel in vision; the nonparametric separability criterion and test-time conditional reshaping are interesting contributions beyond [19].

Significance
Promising and potentially impactful: the idea of discovering domains that help train strong category classifiers is useful and could influence how adaptation is done. The demonstrated gains over dataset-as-domain and [19] are encouraging. Significance would be stronger with modern descriptors and a clearer reshaping protocol.

Suggestions for improvement
- Formulate and test an integrated objective that jointly maximizes separability and learnability (e.g., TDD − λ classification loss), or a bilevel/alternating scheme.
- Clarify and justify conditional reshaping assumptions; compare against methods with identical transductive access.
- Strengthen features: spatial pyramids of dense SURF/SIFT, Fisher Vectors; cite and compare to Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronnin ECCV 2010.
- Use χ2 or intersection kernels for histogram comparison; report kernel/bandwidth sensitivity.
- Provide analyses on LPC sensitivity, integrality gap, stability across seeds, and computational cost.

Pros
- Addresses an important, under-explored problem; principled nonparametric formulation.
- Clear empirical improvements over baselines and [19].
- Conceptually simple and broadly applicable; writing is mostly clear.

Cons
- Learnability not integrated into the partitioning objective.
- Optimization–objective gap not addressed; little guidance on rounding quality.
- Test reshaping protocol under-specified; transductive assumption not explicit.
- Outdated, weak representations; missing kernel details for histograms.

Overall verdict
Accept. Despite shortcomings, this is a valuable and promising contribution that advances the practice of domain adaptation by automatically discovering useful domains. The paper will be stronger with clearer optimization and test-protocol details and with modern descriptors, but the core idea and empirical gains merit inclusion.
