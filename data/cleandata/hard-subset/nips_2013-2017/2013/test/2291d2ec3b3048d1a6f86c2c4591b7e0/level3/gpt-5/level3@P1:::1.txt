This paper addresses a central but under-explored aspect of domain adaptation for visual data: how to automatically discover latent domains in heterogeneous datasets so that adaptation can be performed between coherent, characteristic domains rather than coarse dataset labels. The authors propose two desiderata for domains—maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability—and realize them via: (i) a nonparametric, kernel-based objective that maximizes pairwise distributional differences between domains, constrained so that each instance is assigned to exactly one domain and the per-domain class-label distribution follows the global label prior; (ii) a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure to pick the number of domains K; and (iii) a conditional reshaping of unlabeled test data to match discovered training domains. They implement the distinctiveness criterion using a kernel two-sample test (MMD) and relax the integer assignment problem into a continuous, constrained quadratic program. Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and cross-view human action recognition (IXMAS) show consistent improvements over treating datasets as domains and over the latent-domain discovery approach of Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12). Adaptation is performed with the Geodesic Flow Kernel (GFK).

Relation to prior work: The paper is well-positioned relative to domain adaptation (e.g., Ben-David et al., Daumé, Blitzer et al., Pan et al., Mansour et al.) and dataset bias (Torralba & Efros, CVPR’11). It differs from prior multi-source adaptation by discovering domains rather than assuming them. Compared to Hoffman et al. (latent domain discovery via Gaussian mixtures), the proposed nonparametric MMD-based formulation avoids restrictive parametric assumptions and extends to conditional reshaping of test sets.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of why “dataset = domain” is a weak proxy in vision, and an algorithmic framework that operationalizes more meaningful domain structure.
- Principled, nonparametric measure of domain distinctiveness via MMD; the DWCV criterion for selecting K is intuitive and tied to downstream utility.
- Empirically strong: discovered domains outperform original datasets and prior latent domain discovery on both images and videos; conditional test reshaping is a useful and underexplored setting.
- Good connection to adaptation practice: discovered domains pair naturally with multi-source adaptation methods and improve over UNION/ENSEMBLE baselines.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Evidence about appearance clustering degenerating to per-category clusters: The paper argues that naive clustering tends to rediscover categories rather than latent nuisance factors. Please substantiate this claim with a systematic experiment: run K-means (and, if possible, a stronger clustering baseline) on features, then report how closely clusters align with labels (e.g., NMI or purity) and how downstream adaptation performs. This will also help isolate which failure modes your method overcomes.
- Role and necessity of the label-prior constraint (LPC): The LPC is the primary mechanism discouraging per-category clusters, but it is not yet clear whether it is necessary or sufficient. Please add an ablation (no LPC) and report both TDD and downstream adaptation performance. Also compare with random equal partitioning and with unsupervised object discovery/segmentation baselines, to ensure gains are not due to regularization alone.
- What distribution is made “maximally different”? The text introduces Pk(x, y) but optimizes a discrepancy over the marginals Pk(x) via MMD. Please clarify that distinctiveness is measured over features only, justify this choice, and discuss whether and how label information could be incorporated (e.g., class-conditional MMD or joint kernels).
- Relaxation and constraints: After introducing βmk = zmk/Mk with βk on the simplex over instances, the relaxed problem (Eq. 4–5) imposes for each instance m the constraint 1/M ≤ Σk βmk ≤ 1/C. This differs from the integer constraint Σk zmk = 1. Please derive these bounds carefully, explain their connection to minimum/maximum domain sizes (Mk ≥ C, Mk ≤ M), and reconcile them with the βk-simplex constraints. The current presentation is confusing and suggests a mismatch between the two formulations.
- Bound on K (Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C}): Please provide a short derivation/justification. The argument seems to rely on per-fold per-class minimum counts; making the assumptions explicit would help reproducibility.
- Optimization and scalability: The method builds and optimizes over an M×M kernel matrix, which may be costly for large M. Please report computational complexity, wall-clock times, memory usage, and whether you used low-rank approximations (e.g., Nyström, random features) or off-the-shelf QP solvers. Discuss sensitivity to kernel bandwidth selection (you set it to 2×median distance); a brief robustness plot would be valuable.
- GFK implementation: Please state whether you used the authors’ public implementation, any parameter choices beyond defaults, and how matching of source/target domains was done without target labels in the non-oracle settings.
- Why does the method win? The qualitative examples suggest the method recovers pose/background/appearance factors. Please add a more systematic analysis: inter-domain MMD vs. intra-domain class-conditional variance, a confusion-matrix shift analysis pre/post reshaping, and a t-SNE/UMAP visualization showing that discovered domains align with nuisance factors rather than categories.

Quality
- Technically sound overall, with correct use of MMD and a reasonable DWCV criterion. However, the relaxation/constraints need clearer derivation and the K bound should be justified. Ablations (LPC on/off) are important to support the design choices.

Clarity
- Generally well-written. Please fix Eq. (1) notation (M′k should be Mk′), ensure consistent notation for distributions over (x, y) vs. x, and clarify the constraint set in the relaxed QP. A brief algorithm box or pseudo-code would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- The combination of nonparametric domain discovery with label-prior constraints, DWCV for K selection, and conditional test reshaping is novel relative to prior ECCV’12 latent domain discovery and generalized clustering via kernel embeddings.

Significance
- If broadly validated, the approach can improve multi-source adaptation and inform dataset construction. The cross-view action recognition gains, especially with test reshaping, underscore practical impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and neglected problem; clear motivation for vision.
- Methodologically principled and generally well-executed.
- Strong empirical results across two modalities; outperforms prior latent domain discovery.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient ablation/diagnostics on LPC and clustering baselines.
- Some ambiguity in the relaxation/constraints and K bound justification.
- Limited discussion of scalability and parameter sensitivity.

Recommendation
- Lean accept. The rebuttal addressed several concerns; with added clarifications, ablations, and minor fixes, this will be a valuable contribution to domain adaptation and dataset design.
