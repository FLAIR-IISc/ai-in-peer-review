

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a fundamental but often overlooked issue in domain adaptation: the mismatch between the standard practice of equating datasets with domains and the reality that datasets often contain multiple latent domains influenced by factors like lighting, pose, background, and resolution. The authors propose a novel approach to automatically discover these latent domains by enforcing two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing distribution differences between domains) and maximum learnability (ensuring domains support strong discriminative models). This work builds on domain adaptation research at NIPS [1,2,3,4] and related work on dataset bias [16,17,18,19], but addresses a critical gap in the field by questioning the very definition of "domains" in visual recognition.

The paper is particularly relevant to NIPS 2013-2017 work on domain adaptation, including the "Geodesic Flow Kernel" [4] and "Multi-domain Adaptation" [28,29] papers. It also connects to earlier work on dataset bias [16] and "Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias" [11], but makes a significant conceptual contribution by shifting the focus from adapting between given domains to discovering meaningful latent domains.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear theoretical foundation. The authors derive a nonparametric formulation for measuring domain distinctiveness using RKHS distances and develop a convex optimization procedure that can be efficiently solved. The DWCV method for determining the number of domains is particularly well-motivated, as it directly relates to the practical goal of building discriminative models from each domain.

However, the claim that this is an unsupervised learning problem is misleading. The method explicitly uses class labels through the label prior constraint (LPC), which requires that class distributions within domains match the overall dataset distribution. This constraint could be limiting, as real-world domain factors (like pose) may naturally cause different class distributions across domains (e.g., horse poses might differ from cow poses in ways that don't align with the label distribution constraint). The paper should clarify this limitation.

The experimental validation is adequate, with reasonable gains over baselines on object recognition and action recognition tasks. However, the rationale for dataset/source-target selection is unclear, and a leave-one-dataset-out validation strategy would have strengthened the claims.

## Clarity

The paper suffers from significant terminology confusion. The inconsistent use of "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain" creates substantial confusion. For example, the paper states "a dataset could be an agglomeration of several distinctive domains" but then later refers to "the original datasets" as domains without clear distinction. The paper would benefit from a clear definition of these terms early on, with concrete examples.

The presentation contains a specific bug: Equation (6) uses max_k r(U_k, B) without defining this notation. This is particularly problematic as it's central to evaluating the "goodness" of identified domains.

Section 4.2 is unclear in its explanation of how the identified domains improve adaptation. The paper states "the MATCHING strategy for adaptation is able to match the oracle accuracies" but doesn't explain what "MATCHING" refers to or how it's implemented.

## Originality

The paper makes a notable contribution to the domain adaptation literature by formalizing the problem of latent domain discovery. While Hoffman et al. [20] also addressed latent domain discovery, their approach uses parametric Gaussian mixture models, whereas this paper's nonparametric formulation is a significant advancement that avoids restrictive distributional assumptions.

The key novelty lies in the convex formulation that controls class-label distributions in a relaxed way, along with the DWCV procedure for automatically determining the number of domains based on learnability. This combination of properties (distinctiveness + learnability) and the practical method for implementing them represent a meaningful advance over previous work.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial because it addresses a fundamental problem in domain adaptation: the mismatch between how we define domains (as entire datasets) and how they actually exist (as latent factors within datasets). By reshaping datasets into more meaningful latent domains, the approach has the potential to improve performance across multiple domain adaptation tasks.

The qualitative analysis in Figure 1 provides useful insights into what factors drive the discovered domains (e.g., object pose and appearance), suggesting practical implications for dataset construction. The method could be valuable for practitioners who want to better understand and utilize the structure within their datasets.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel approach to latent domain discovery with clear theoretical foundation
- Nonparametric formulation avoids restrictive distributional assumptions
- DWCV method provides a principled way to determine the number of domains
- Demonstrates practical utility through multiple domain adaptation tasks
- Qualitative analysis provides useful insights into discovered domain factors

**Weaknesses:**
- Misleading claim of unsupervised learning (class labels are used)
- Terminology confusion (dataset/domain/latent-domain) throughout the paper
- Experimental sections lack clarity, particularly Section 4.2
- Missing definition of key notation (max_k r(U_k, B))
- The label prior constraint may be limiting for real-world domain factors

## Recommendation

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to domain adaptation by addressing the important but overlooked problem of latent domain discovery. The approach is theoretically sound and demonstrates practical value through experiments. However, the terminology confusion, misleading unsupervised claim, and unclear experimental sections prevent it from being fully compelling.

I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The authors should clarify the terminology, explicitly address the limitations of the label prior constraint, define all notation clearly, and provide a more detailed explanation of the experimental methodology. If these issues are addressed, this paper would represent a significant contribution to the field.

**Pro acceptance arguments:** Novel approach to a fundamental problem, technically sound methodology, strong experimental validation, practical insights.

**Con acceptance arguments:** Terminology confusion, misleading unsupervised claim, unclear experimental sections, potential limitation of label prior constraint.
