

# Review: Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation

This paper proposes an approach to automatically discover latent domains in visual datasets to improve domain adaptation performance. The authors identify two key properties that latent domains should possess: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different in distribution from each other) and maximum learnability (domains should allow for strong discriminative models to be learned). The authors formulate this as an optimization problem using kernel-based domain discrepancy measures, relax it to a continuous quadratic optimization problem, and determine the number of domains via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV).

The work builds upon previous domain adaptation research [1,2,3,4] but addresses a critical gap: the common practice of equating datasets with domains is inadequate as datasets often contain multiple latent domains that are not semantically meaningful (e.g., different lighting conditions, viewpoints). The authors argue that simply clustering by appearance (as in [20]) leads to per-category domains rather than characteristic domains, which is undesirable for domain adaptation.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the problem of dataset bias in visual recognition.
- The two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and learnability) provide a principled approach to latent domain discovery.
- The experimental results on two different tasks (object recognition and action recognition) demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines.
- The DWCV approach for determining the number of domains is a practical contribution.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

1. **Motivation Clarity:** The paper claims that clustering by appearance leads to per-category domains, but provides no empirical evidence to support this claim. The authors should include results comparing their method to k-means clustering on the same datasets to demonstrate that their approach avoids this pitfall.

2. **Label-Prior Constraint (LPC) Justification:** The necessity of the LPC is unclear. The authors should provide results without the LPC constraint and compare to unsupervised baselines (k-means, [20]) and random equal partitioning. Without this, it's unclear whether the constraint is driving the performance gains.

3. **"Maximally Different in Distribution" Clarification:** The phrase "maximally different in distribution" needs clarification. The authors should specify over what the distribution is being compared (features, labels, or other aspects).

4. **β_{mk} Constraint:** The question of whether β_{mk} as formulated in line 166 already lies on a simplex should be addressed. If it does, the constraint β_k ∈ Δ is redundant.

5. **Constraint Consistency:** The constraints stated around line 182 appear different from earlier constraints (line 165-166). This inconsistency needs clarification to ensure the optimization problem is well-defined.

6. **Justification for Bound (line 215):** The authors reference a bound on line 215 without providing justification or proof. This should be included to support the theoretical claims.

7. **Geodesic Flow Kernel Details:** The paper uses the geodesic flow kernel but does not specify whether this was reimplemented or used from a public source. Providing this information would improve reproducibility.

8. **Insights into Performance Gains:** The paper should provide more detailed insights into *why* the proposed approach outperforms baselines. A systematic analysis of baseline failures that the proposed method overcomes would strengthen the contribution.

**Minor Corrections:**
- Equation (1) should have M'k as Mk' (as noted in the key points).
- The paper should cite Torralba & Efros, "Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias" (CVPR 2011), which discusses dataset bias in visual recognition.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper addresses an important gap in domain adaptation research by proposing a method to automatically discover latent domains rather than assuming datasets correspond to domains. The theoretical formulation is sound, and the experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over existing approaches. The paper is well-written and makes a novel contribution to the field.

The main concerns relate to the clarity of the motivation, justification of constraints, and lack of comparison to relevant baselines. These issues are important but not fundamental to the core idea. The authors' rebuttal addressed these concerns adequately, providing additional evidence for their claims and clarifying the methodological details.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with clear experimental design, though the theoretical justification for some components could be strengthened.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, though some technical details (LPC, β_{mk}) need clarification.

**Originality:** The problem of latent domain discovery is novel, and the two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and learnability) provide a principled approach not found in previous work.

**Significance:** The method has practical significance for domain adaptation, which is a critical challenge in real-world visual recognition systems. The demonstrated performance improvements over baselines suggest this approach could be widely adopted.

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a valuable contribution to domain adaptation research. The concerns raised are important but addressable, and the authors have provided adequate responses to these concerns in the rebuttal. I recommend acceptance.
