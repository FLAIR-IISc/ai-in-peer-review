## Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper proposes a novel approach to domain adaptation by automatically discovering latent domains within training data. The core idea is compelling: instead of treating entire datasets as single domains (a common but often flawed practice), the authors aim to partition the data to maximize inter-domain distinctiveness and intra-domain learnability. They formulate this as an integer program, relaxed to a continuous quadratic optimization, and use cross-validation to determine the optimal number of domains. The method is evaluated on object recognition and action recognition tasks, demonstrating improvements over a baseline domain adaptation method.

The paper is well-written and presents a technically sound methodology. The motivation for discovering latent domains, rather than relying on pre-defined datasets, is clearly articulated. The formulation using a kernel-based difference function and the subsequent optimization process are presented with sufficient detail. The experimental results, while limited to two datasets, are promising and show a clear performance gain. The inclusion of a domain-wise cross-validation procedure to determine the number of domains is a valuable addition.

However, several concerns remain. The initial motivation, while logical, lacks strong empirical justification. The paper doesn’t adequately demonstrate *why* clustering by appearance leads to per-category domains, and the model’s mechanism for preventing this isn’t fully convincing.  The necessity of the label-prior constraint (LPC) is unclear. It’s crucial to see results *without* the LPC and a comparison to simpler unsupervised clustering algorithms like k-means, or object discovery baselines, to understand its contribution. 

Furthermore, the definition of "maximally different in distribution" requires clarification – what distribution is being referenced (features, labels, or something else)? Several questions regarding the mathematical formulation remain unanswered: does β<sub>mk</sub> lie on a simplex, and are the constraints around line 182 consistent with the earlier formulation?  A justification for the bound referenced on line 215 is also needed. Finally, more details regarding the geodesic flow kernel are required, including whether it was reimplemented or used from a publicly available code base. Providing insights into why this approach outperforms baselines, beyond simply stating that it does, and showing systematic failures of the baselines that the method overcomes, would strengthen the paper significantly.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel approach, technically sound, promising experimental results, clear writing.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Weak justification for motivation, unclear necessity of LPC, lack of comparison to simpler baselines, unanswered questions regarding formulation. 

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. However, addressing the outlined concerns would significantly strengthen the work and increase its impact. I currently lean towards acceptance, pending a thorough response to these points in the rebuttal.
