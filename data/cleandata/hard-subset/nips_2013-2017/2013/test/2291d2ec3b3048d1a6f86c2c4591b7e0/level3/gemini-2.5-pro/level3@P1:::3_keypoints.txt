- Tackles the overlooked problem of defining "domain" in domain adaptation and proposes discovering multiple latent domains within source data.
- Proposes two guiding principles: maximum distinctiveness (separate domains) and maximum learnability (choose K via DWCV).
- Distinctiveness achieved by maximizing pairwise distances in RKHS using a non-parametric MMD formulation.
- Introduces a Label Prior Constraint (LPC) to keep class distributions within discovered domains similar to the overall source distribution.
- Learnability implemented via Domain-Wise Cross-Validation (DWCV) to select the optimal number of latent domains K.
- Technical formulation is a convex quadratic maximization with linear constraints derived from an integer program relaxation.
- Empirical results on object and action recognition show consistent, sometimes moderate, improvements over baselines when adapting from discovered domains.
- Qualitative analysis (example images) convincingly illustrates the semantic factors captured by discovered domains.
- Major conceptual limitation: LPC assumes latent factor distributions are independent of class, which is unrealistic in many real-world cases.
- Clarity issues: ambiguous use of "dataset"/"source domain"/"latent domain" and confusing notation S = {S1,...}, U = {U1,...}.
- Section 4.2 and the definition of G_OURS are hard to parse; G_OURS uses an optimistic oracle-like max_k that is not practically attainable.
- Framing is misleadingly called "unsupervised" despite relying on class labels for LPC and DWCV; should be framed as supervised partitioning.
- Originality: novel combination of kernel MMD and convex relaxation to discover latent domains and a new learnability-based model selection for K.
- Significance: could change how domain adaptation experiments are set up and improve robustness in heterogeneous test streams.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem, technically sound, novel non-parametric approach, supported by experiments and qualitative evidence.
- Arguments for rejection: LPC’s strong assumption limits applicability, significant clarity/reproducibility issues, and incorrect unsupervised framing.
- Recommendation: lean toward acceptance contingent on fixing clarity, explicitly defining terminology, clarifying evaluation metrics, and re-framing the supervised nature of the method.
