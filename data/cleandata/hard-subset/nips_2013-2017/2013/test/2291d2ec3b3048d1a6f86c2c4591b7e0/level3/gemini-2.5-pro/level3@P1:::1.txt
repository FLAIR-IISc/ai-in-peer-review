This paper addresses the problem of discovering latent domains within visual datasets to improve the performance of domain adaptation algorithms. The central argument is that the standard practice of equating entire datasets with domains is flawed, as datasets often contain a mixture of underlying distributions corresponding to factors like pose, illumination, and background. The authors propose a novel, non-parametric method to automatically partition one or more source datasets into a set of latent domains. The proposed formulation is guided by two principles: 1) "maximum distinctiveness," which seeks to find domains whose marginal feature distributions are maximally different from each other, as measured by a sum of kernel-based RKHS distances; and 2) "maximum learnability," which ensures that the discovered domains are useful for training strong classifiers. The partitioning is framed as an integer program, which is then relaxed to a continuous quadratic optimization problem. A key component of the formulation is a "label prior constraint" (LPC), which forces the class distribution within each discovered domain to match the global class distribution. The number of domains is determined automatically via a proposed "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) procedure. The approach is evaluated on object recognition (across Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and multi-view action recognition (IXMAS) tasks. Experiments show that adapting classifiers from the discovered domains yields significant performance gains compared to using the original datasets as domains or using domains discovered by a prior method [20].

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and important problem in domain adaptation. The question of what constitutes a "domain" is often glossed over, and the proposed approach of learning domains directly from data is a novel and promising direction. If successful, this work could significantly influence how researchers set up and evaluate domain adaptation experiments, moving the field beyond simple cross-dataset generalization. The suggested citation to Torralba & Efros, "Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias," CVPR 2011, would be highly relevant here.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated. Using a non-parametric, kernel-based measure for domain distinctiveness is elegant and avoids making strong parametric assumptions about the data distribution, which is a major advantage for complex visual data. The relaxation of the NP-hard integer program to a solvable form is a practical and necessary step.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The core concepts of "maximum distinctiveness" and "maximum learnability" are intuitive and effectively guide the formulation.

4.  **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate clear and consistent performance improvements. The fact that the method outperforms both the original dataset-as-domain baseline and the competing method of Hoffman et al. [20] across multiple tasks and datasets provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Insufficient Motivation and Baselines:** The paper's motivation rests on the claim that naive clustering would fail by simply grouping data by object category. This is a critical point that requires empirical validation.
    *   Why are results for simpler baselines like K-means on the feature representations not included in the main tables? Showing that such a baseline indeed fails as hypothesized would greatly strengthen the motivation for your more complex formulation.
    *   The Label Prior Constraint (LPC) appears to be the key component preventing the algorithm from discovering per-category domains. Its importance should be demonstrated via an ablation study. What happens if you run your optimization without the LPC?
    *   A comparison to a simple random partitioning baseline (that respects the LPC) would also be informative to isolate the gains achieved by the distinctiveness objective itself.

2.  **Clarity of Formulation:** Some technical details could be explained more clearly.
    *   In Section 2.1, you state you want domains Pk(x) and Pk'(x) to be distinctive. Does your distance measure d(k, k') operate on the marginal distribution P(x) or the joint distribution P(x, y)? The formulation seems to only use P(x). Please clarify this and briefly justify the choice.
    *   The constraints in the relaxed optimization (Eq. 5) are not immediately obvious derivations from the integer program constraints. In particular, the constraint `1/M <= sum_k(beta_mk) <= 1/C` should be derived and explained more explicitly for the reader.
    *   The justification for the bound on the number of domains, `K_max <= min{M/(NC), C}` (line 215), is not provided. Please include a brief justification or proof for this bound.

3.  **Experimental Details and Analysis:**
    *   For reproducibility, please clarify whether the geodesic flow kernel [4] was from a public implementation or your own.
    *   The results are strong, but the paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of *why* the method works. The qualitative examples in Figure 1 are insightful but anecdotal. Can you provide a more systematic analysis of what causes the original dataset partitions or the method from [20] to fail in cases where your method succeeds? This would provide more valuable insight than just the final accuracy numbers.

**Minor Point:**
*   There appears to be a typo in Eq (1). `M'k` should likely be `M_k'`.

In summary, this is a strong paper with a novel idea and promising results. The main weaknesses relate to a lack of empirical support for some key motivating claims and the need for stronger baseline comparisons and ablation studies. Addressing the questions above, particularly regarding the baselines and the necessity of the LPC, would significantly strengthen the paper.
