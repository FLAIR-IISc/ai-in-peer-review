- Proposes a framework to discover latent visual domains by enforcing maximum distinctiveness (RKHS distributional differences) and maximum learnability (discriminative models per domain).  
- Formulates the problem as a convex optimization with a label prior constraint to keep class distributions similar across discovered domains.  
- Selects the number of domains K via domain-wise cross-validation and evaluates on object/action recognition, showing improved accuracy over original dataset-as-domain baselines.  
- Builds on domain adaptation literature and addresses the gap that real visual datasets may contain multiple latent, non-semantic domains.  
- Correctly identifies pitfalls of simple appearance-based clustering which can produce undesirable per-category domains.  
- RKHS distance for domain distinctiveness is well-motivated and aligns with recent distributional distance measures.  
- Strength: novel label prior constraint preventing single-class domination and practical cross-validation strategy for K.  
- Strength: extensive quantitative improvements and qualitative analysis linking discovered domains to visual factors (pose, background).  
- Weakness: inconsistent and confusing terminology—unclear distinctions between "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain."  
- Weakness: claim of being unsupervised is misleading because semantic class labels are used in the label prior constraint.  
- Weakness: forcing similar class distributions across domains may be unrealistic and harmful when class-specific domain structures differ.  
- Weakness: experimental section lacks clarity—undefined terms (e.g., "GOURS"/"GOTHER"), undefined notation in Eq.7, and unclear source-target dataset selection.  
- Suggestion: validation should consider leave-one-dataset-out setup; domain-wise cross-validation needs clearer explanation.  
- Overall: technically sound and original contribution but presentation, methodological limitations, and unclear validation undermine impact; substantial revisions required.
