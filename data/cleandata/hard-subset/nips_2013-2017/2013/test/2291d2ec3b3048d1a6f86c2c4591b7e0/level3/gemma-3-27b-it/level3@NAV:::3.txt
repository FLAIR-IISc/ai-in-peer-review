## Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper proposes a novel framework for automatically discovering latent domains within visual datasets to improve domain adaptation performance. The core idea is to split data into K domains that maximize both distinctiveness (measured via an RKHS kernel) and learnability (assessed through cross-validation). The paper tackles a crucial problem—the limitations of equating datasets with domains in domain adaptation—and offers a principled approach to address it. The proposed method builds upon kernel-based distribution comparison and offers an automatic way to determine the optimal number of latent domains, K. 

This work is relevant to the NIPS audience as it presents a new algorithmic approach to a fundamental problem in machine learning: dealing with distribution shift. It builds upon established techniques like kernel methods and domain adaptation, but combines them in a novel way, particularly in the simultaneous optimization for distinctiveness and learnability and the use of cross-validation to determine K. Related work is well-covered, referencing key papers in domain adaptation, transfer learning, and clustering. 

The strengths of this paper lie in its theoretical formulation and the clear presentation of the optimization procedure. The experimental results are adequate, showing improvements on both object recognition and action recognition tasks. The qualitative results (Figure 1) are useful for understanding the types of domains discovered.

However, several weaknesses detract from the overall quality. The terminology is inconsistent—the paper frequently uses "dataset," "domain," and "latent-domain" interchangeably, creating confusion. Clear definitions and examples are needed.  Section 4.2 regarding reshaping test datasets lacks clarity; the connection to the identified training domains could be explained more thoroughly. There is a presentation bug: "max_k r(U_k, B)" is used in Eq. 7 without prior definition. Furthermore, the claim of tackling an "unsupervised" problem is misleading, as the method relies on semantic class labels. While the domain *discovery* is unsupervised, the method is not fully unsupervised learning. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to latent domain discovery with a principled convex formulation.
*   Automatic selection of K based on learnability.
*   Demonstrated improvements in domain adaptation performance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in terminology and experimental details.
*   Misleading claim of being an unsupervised method.
*   Presentation bug in Eq. 7.



**Overall Assessment:** The paper presents a notable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. However, the conceptual and experimental clarifications are critical before acceptance. I recommend a **weak accept** contingent on addressing the clarity issues and correcting the bug. The authors should also revise the claim of unsupervised learning to accurately reflect the method’s reliance on semantic labels.
