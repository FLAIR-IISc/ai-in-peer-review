- Proposes automatic partitioning of images into domains using feature-space separability and per-domain discriminative learnability.
- Tackles an under-explored but important problem for building robust real-world classifiers.
- Method grounded on two principles: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.
- Presents a nonparametric formulation and an efficient optimization procedure to discover domains in training and test data.
- Experiments on object recognition and human activity recognition show significant gains over using datasets-as-domains and a recent domain discovery method.
- Strength: can identify latent domains not aligned with original datasets (e.g., pose, appearance, background).
- Demonstrates practical utility of discovered domains for improving adaptation results.
- Weakness: optimization relaxation (Sec. 2) is not clearly shown to solve the original objectives (2)+(3), leaving impact of relaxation unclear.
- Weakness: the two driving criteria are not well integrated—maximal learnability is used only as a wrapper for model selection, not incorporated into the partitioning objective.
- Weakness: uses outdated image representation (single BoW with sparse SURF, 800 codewords) that is weak by current standards.
- Suggestion: upgrade descriptors to modern representations (e.g., spatial pyramid dense SURF, Fisher Vectors).
- Suggestion: revise and more clearly justify the optimization to show it closely solves the original objectives.
- Suggestion: integrate maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability into a single objective function.
- Suggestion: add more and better-labeled illustrative figures to explain the approach and results.
- Originality: addresses a novel problem and differentiates from prior domain discovery work, but needs broader related-work comparison.
- Overall verdict: promising and valuable contribution with clear writing and strong empirical results, but requires revisions on optimization, criterion integration, and image representation.
