- Proposes a novel nonparametric method to discover latent domains for improved domain adaptation, optimizing for maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.  
- Formulates the problem as a kernel-based distribution distance in RKHS with a continuous relaxation of an integer program; number of domains chosen via domain-wise cross-validation.  
- Addresses the flawed practice of equating datasets with domains and avoids restrictive parametric assumptions used in prior work.  
- Demonstrates consistent empirical improvements on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human activity recognition (IXMAS).  
- Strengths: clear problem formulation, sound technical approach, thorough experiments, and excellent writing making technical content accessible.  
- Shows practical utility in conditional reshaping of test datasets (Section 4.3).  
- Concern: No empirical evidence that standard clustering yields per-category domains; should compare to k-means on their datasets.  
- Concern: Necessity of the label prior constraint (LPC) is unclear; paper lacks an ablation showing performance without LPC.  
- Concern: Ambiguous definition of “maximally different in distribution” — unclear if distribution is over features, labels, or both.  
- Technical issues: typo in Eq. (1) (M'k should be Mk) and apparent inconsistency in constraints on lines 182–183.  
- The bound Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C} (line 215) requires stronger theoretical justification or citation.  
- Needs more systematic baseline comparisons: k-means, unsupervised object discovery methods, and random equal partitioning, with explanations for performance gains.  
- Authors’ rebuttal convincingly addressed some concerns (notably LPC and per-category clustering), strengthening the paper.  
- Overall judgment: high-quality, significant contribution to domain adaptation with recommendation for acceptance after clarifications.
