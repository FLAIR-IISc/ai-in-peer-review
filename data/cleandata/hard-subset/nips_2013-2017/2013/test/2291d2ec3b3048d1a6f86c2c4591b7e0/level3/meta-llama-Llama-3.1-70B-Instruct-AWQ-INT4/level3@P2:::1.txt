**Summary of the Main Ideas**

The paper "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation" proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in visual datasets, which is essential for domain adaptation in visual recognition problems. The authors argue that the standard practice of equating datasets with domains is a weak proxy for the real conditions that alter the statistics in complex ways. They introduce two key properties that latent domains should possess: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The authors devise a nonparametric formulation and efficient optimization procedure to discover domains among both training and test data.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper is related to previous work on domain adaptation, which has been extensively studied in computer vision, speech and language processing, and statistical machine learning. However, the authors argue that most of these approaches are validated by adapting between datasets, which do not necessarily correspond to well-defined domains. The paper is also related to Hoffman et al.'s work, which aims to discover latent domains from datasets using a hierarchical distribution consisting of Gaussian mixtures. However, the authors propose a nonparametric approach, which overcomes the limitation of assuming a specific form of distribution.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The paper has several strengths. First, the authors propose a novel approach to discover latent domains in visual datasets, which is essential for domain adaptation in visual recognition problems. Second, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. Third, the authors provide extensive experimental results on two datasets, which demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. First, the motivation for the approach is not entirely clear. The authors argue that clustering by appearance yields per-category domains, but it is unclear how the model prevents this. Second, the necessity of the label-prior constraint is unclear, and the authors do not provide results without it or comparisons to k-means, unsupervised object discovery baselines, and random equal partitioning.
