Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles a foundational but underexplored question in domain adaptation for visual recognition: how to discover latent domains within heterogeneous datasets so that adaptation is meaningful and effective. The authors propose to reshape datasets into domains that satisfy two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains are as different as possible in distribution) and maximum learnability (each domain supports learning a strong classifier). They operationalize distinctiveness using a kernel two-sample distance (MMD) between marginal feature distributions and formulate domain assignment as an integer program with constraints that each instance belongs to exactly one domain and that per-domain class-label proportions match the global prior (label-prior constraint, LPC). They relax the problem to a continuous quadratic program and choose the number of domains K via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV), selecting K that maximizes a weighted within-domain CV accuracy. They then show improved target performance on object recognition (Caltech-256/Amazon/DSLR/Webcam) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS), including conditional reshaping of test data to match discovered source domains, and comparisons to Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12) who assume parametric mixture models.

The work is well positioned in domain adaptation (e.g., Daumé’07; Blitzer et al.; Ben-David et al.; Saenko et al.; Gopalan et al.; Gong et al. GFK) and related generalized clustering using kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.). The motivation aligns with dataset bias (Torralba & Efros, CVPR’11).

Strengths
- Addresses an important and practical gap: dataset ≠ domain, especially for vision where nuisance factors are intertwined.
- Principled nonparametric formulation using characteristic kernels; avoids strong parametric assumptions of prior latent-domain models.
- Clear use of LPC to discourage trivial per-category partitions; DWCV provides a simple data-driven choice of K.
- Solid empirical gains on two representative settings (objects and cross-view actions); conditional test reshaping is a nice addition.
- Writing is clear and the method is easy to follow; qualitative analyses are informative.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Motivation regarding “clustering by appearance yields per-category domains”: please provide empirical evidence (e.g., show K-means clusters’ label purity) and explain more explicitly how LPC and the objective prevent this failure mode. LPC constrains label proportions, but the distinctiveness objective operates on Pk(x) only; an ablation without LPC would help demonstrate necessity.
- Comparisons and ablations: include results for (i) no LPC, (ii) K-means on features, (iii) random equal partitioning, and (iv) unsupervised object discovery or generic clustering baselines, with the same adaptation pipeline.
- “Maximally different in distribution”: clarify that the discrepancy is between marginal feature distributions Pk(x) via MMD in an RKHS, not the joint Pk(x,y). If any label information influences distinctiveness beyond LPC, please specify.
- Variables and constraints:
  - βmk definition: since βmk = zmk/Mk and βk ∈ Δ with ∑m βmk = 1, βk indeed lies on a simplex by construction. Please confirm and clarify how this interacts with per-example constraints.
  - Constraint mismatch: the relaxed constraints (1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C and the δ-relaxed LPC) look different from the original integer constraints. A short derivation bridging the two would improve clarity. In particular, explain that ∑k βmk = 1/Mk and the bounds imply C ≤ Mk ≤ M.
- Bound on K (Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C}): please justify formally. The M/(NC) factor presumably comes from requiring at least N examples per class for N-fold CV in each domain (Mk ≥ NC), but the C bound is less clear; if the intent is ensuring Mk ≥ C (at least one example per class), that yields K ≤ M/C, not ≤ C.
- GFK usage: please state whether you used public code or a reimplementation, subspace dimensionalities (PCA dims on source/target), and any tuning. Also report sensitivity to the Gaussian kernel bandwidth (chosen as twice the median distance).
- Why it works: deepen the analysis of when/why the proposed objective finds nuisance-factor–aligned domains (pose, illumination, background) instead of semantic categories. The qualitative figures are convincing; adding quantitative diagnostics (e.g., correlation of domain assignments with known view angles on IXMAS; confusion reduction across domains) would strengthen the case.
- Scalability/optimization: maximizing a convex quadratic over linear constraints is NP-hard in general; please report solver, run times, convergence behavior, and M scaling (M and K) for your datasets.

Quality
The formulation is technically sound and grounded in established tools (MMD). The optimization is sensible given relaxations, and the DWCV criterion is practical. However, the paper would benefit from the requested ablations, tighter justification for the K bound, and more details on optimization and adaptation components to ensure reproducibility.

Clarity
Overall well organized and readable. Please clarify the precise distributions compared (marginal vs. joint), the relaxed constraints, and provide the minor fix in Eq. (1) (Mk′ instead of M′k). Adding solver details and parameter settings will further improve clarity.

Originality
Discovering latent domains nonparametrically with an explicit learnability criterion is novel relative to prior mixture-model approaches (Hoffman et al.) and generalized clustering. The conditional test reshaping is also a useful extension.

Significance
The idea is likely to be adopted by practitioners who face heterogeneous data. Results indicate consistent gains over treating datasets as domains and over prior latent-domain discovery. The approach can pair with many adaptation algorithms, increasing its impact.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses a real and common pain point in domain adaptation.
- Principled nonparametric objective with a practical selection of K.
- Demonstrated improvements on multiple benchmarks; clear qualitative insights.

Cons:
- Missing ablations and some baseline comparisons; limited analysis of failure modes.
- Some technical details (constraints, K bound, optimization, GFK setup) need clarification for full reproducibility.
- Sensitivity analyses (kernel bandwidth, K) are light.

Minor comments
- Typo: Eq. (1) should read Mk′ (not M′k).
- Please ensure discussion and citation of Torralba & Efros (CVPR’11) are explicit in the motivation.
- Consider releasing code or pseudo-code for the optimization.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The rebuttal addressed major concerns; with the requested clarifications and ablations included in the final version, this will be a valuable contribution.
