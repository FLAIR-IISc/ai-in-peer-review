

# Review for "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper addresses a fundamental yet underexplored problem in domain adaptation: how to automatically discover latent domains within visual datasets rather than relying on the common practice of equating entire datasets with domains. The authors propose a method that identifies domains based on two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (ensuring domains are maximally different in distribution) and maximum learnability (ensuring strong discriminative models can be trained from each domain). Their approach uses nonparametric kernel-based measures to optimize these properties, with domain discovery performed via continuous relaxation of an integer programming problem.

The work relates to prior domain adaptation research at NIPS, particularly the body of work on cross-dataset adaptation (e.g., Saenko et al. 2010, Hoffman et al. 2012). Unlike most prior approaches that treat datasets as fixed domains, this paper tackles the more challenging problem of identifying the true underlying domains within datasets. The authors correctly note that standard practice equating datasets with domains is problematic, as datasets often contain multiple latent domains (e.g., different camera angles in action recognition datasets).

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear theoretical motivation. The optimization problem is well-formulated, and the nonparametric approach avoids restrictive distributional assumptions. The experiments demonstrate significant improvements over using entire datasets as domains and outperform the recent method by Hoffman et al. (2012). However, the optimization procedure (Section 2.1) does not clearly demonstrate convergence to the original objectives (2)+(3), and the two criteria (distinctiveness and learnability) are not well-integrated into a single optimization framework but rather treated separately (with learnability used only for model selection). The image representation (BoW of sparse SURF features with 800 codewords) is outdated for current standards, and the paper fails to specify the appropriate similarity measure for histogram comparison (X² or intersection kernel should be used instead of Euclidean). This weakens the experimental claims.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear exposition of the method. However, the description of the "reshaping" process for test datasets (Section 4.3) is poorly explained and effectively assumes an imputation setting that isn't clearly stated. More illustrative figures would help clarify how the domains are discovered and how the reshaping process works.

**Originality:** This is a novel contribution that addresses an important gap in domain adaptation research. The problem of automatically discovering latent domains has been underexplored, and the two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) provide a fresh perspective. The paper correctly identifies the limitations of prior approaches like Hoffman et al. (2012) and clearly differentiates its contribution from related work on clustering (Jegelka et al.) and multi-domain adaptation.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is significant, as many real-world visual recognition applications suffer from unaccounted domain variations within datasets. The paper demonstrates practical improvements in domain adaptation performance across object recognition and action recognition tasks. While the image representation is outdated, the core idea of automatically discovering domains rather than assuming them is valuable and could influence future dataset construction and domain adaptation methods.

**Strengths:**
- Correctly identifies the problem of dataset-vs-domain confusion in visual domain adaptation
- Proposes a principled approach with two well-motivated domain properties
- Demonstrates significant empirical improvements over standard practice
- Addresses an important gap in the literature (latent domain discovery)

**Weaknesses:**
- Optimization procedure doesn't clearly solve the original objectives
- Two criteria aren't properly integrated into a single objective
- Outdated image representation weakens experimental claims
- Test set reshaping process poorly explained
- Missing details on histogram comparison similarity measure

**Recommendation:** Despite the methodological weaknesses, the paper presents a valuable and promising contribution to the field. The core idea of automatically discovering latent domains for domain adaptation is important and well-motivated. The experimental results are convincing, showing clear improvements over using datasets as domains and over the recent method by Hoffman et al. (2012). The paper would benefit from updating the image representation (to spatial pyramid of dense SURF or Fisher vectors), clarifying the histogram similarity measure, and better integrating the two domain properties into a unified optimization framework.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Addresses a fundamental problem in domain adaptation that has been overlooked
- Presents a novel, well-motivated approach with clear theoretical foundation
- Demonstrates significant empirical improvements over standard practice
- Has practical implications for dataset construction and domain adaptation

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Outdated image representation undermines experimental validity
- Key methodological details are missing (similarity measure)
- Two criteria not properly integrated into optimization
- Test set reshaping process is poorly explained

Overall, this is a valuable contribution that should be accepted, with the understanding that the authors should address the methodological weaknesses in the camera-ready version. The core idea is important and promising, and the paper makes a clear contribution to the field of domain adaptation.
