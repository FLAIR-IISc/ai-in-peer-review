## Review of “Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation”

This paper tackles the important, and relatively underexplored, problem of automatically defining domains for domain adaptation, moving beyond the common practice of equating datasets with domains. The core idea – to partition datasets into domains based on feature-space separability and per-domain discriminative learnability – is compelling and addresses a fundamental limitation in current domain adaptation approaches. The authors propose a nonparametric formulation and optimization procedure to achieve this, demonstrating improvements over existing methods on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks. This work represents a valuable contribution to the field, particularly as it pushes for a more nuanced understanding of what constitutes a ‘domain’ in visual data.

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear motivation, novelty, and promising experimental results. The authors convincingly argue that treating entire datasets as single domains is often suboptimal and provide a principled approach to address this. The experimental validation, showing significant gains over both dataset-as-domain baselines and the recent domain discovery method [20], is a key strength. The inclusion of reshaping the *test* dataset based on training domain discovery is a particularly interesting aspect, though unfortunately, the explanation of this "reshaping" process feels incomplete and relies on an implicit imputation assumption that isn’t clearly stated.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. While the overall formulation is well-motivated, the link between the proposed optimization (Section 2) and the original objectives (2)+(3) isn’t fully elucidated. The two driving criteria – separability and learnability – aren’t tightly integrated; maximal learnability feels more like a post-hoc model selection wrapper than an integral part of the partitioning objective. An integrated optimization that jointly maximizes both criteria would be more desirable. 

Furthermore, the image representation used – a single bag-of-words of sparse SURF with 800 codewords – is outdated and significantly weaker than current standards. This limits the potential of the proposed method, as more powerful features could lead to better domain discovery and, consequently, improved adaptation performance. The authors should upgrade to descriptors like spatial pyramids of dense SURF, Fisher Vectors, or even learned representations. The similarity measure used for histogram comparison is also unclear; Euclidean distance is inappropriate for histograms and should be replaced with a more suitable metric such as the χ² distance or intersection kernel. 

Finally, while well-written, the paper would benefit from more illustrative figures demonstrating the identified domains and their characteristics.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and important problem addressed.
*   Promising results demonstrating improvements over existing methods.
*   Clear motivation and well-defined objectives.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Weak integration of separability and learnability.
*   Outdated image representation.
*   Unclear explanation of test set reshaping. 



**Overall:** Despite these shortcomings, the paper presents a valuable and promising contribution. I recommend acceptance, with a strong encouragement for the authors to address the mentioned weaknesses in future work.
