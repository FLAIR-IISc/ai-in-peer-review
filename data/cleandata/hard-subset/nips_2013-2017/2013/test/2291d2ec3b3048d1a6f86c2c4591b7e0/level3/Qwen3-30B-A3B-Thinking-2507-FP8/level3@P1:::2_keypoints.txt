- Addresses automatic discovery of latent domains in visual datasets, a critical and underexplored problem in domain adaptation.  
- Proposes defining domains by maximum distinctiveness (distribution differences) and maximum learnability (trainable discriminative models).  
- Novel insight: domains should be defined by discriminative utility rather than dataset boundaries.  
- Nonparametric kernel-based formulation for domain distinctiveness (Eq. 1-2) avoids limitations of parametric clustering.  
- Introduces domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to select the optimal number of domains, balancing distinctiveness and learnability.  
- Empirical results show substantial improvements over treating datasets as domains and over Hoffman et al. [20] on object and activity recognition tasks.  
- MATCHING strategy effectively selects appropriate domains for adaptation, boosting performance.  
- Qualitative analysis indicates discovered domains align with meaningful visual factors (pose, appearance, background).  
- Paper is clearly written and well-structured, accessible to the NIPS audience.  
- Experimental design is sound and ablation studies support the approach.  
- Optimization procedure (Sec. 2.1) does not clearly demonstrate satisfaction of original objectives (Eq. 2-3).  
- The two criteria (distinctiveness and learnability) are not integrated into a single optimization; learnability is used post-hoc.  
- Methodological concern: reshaping uses test set information (Sec. 4.3), implying unrealistic access to target labels and potentially inflating results.  
- Representation is outdated (800 SURF BoVW); should use modern descriptors (SPM, Fisher vectors, or deep features).  
- Similarity measure for histograms is unspecified; Euclidean is inappropriate—prefer X² or intersection kernels.  
- Overall significance is high; strengths (novel formulation, strong empirical validation) outweigh weaknesses, recommendation to accept with minor revisions addressing representation and test-set reshaping.
