Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses the problem of automatically discovering “latent domains” within visual datasets in order to improve domain adaptation. The core idea is to partition labeled examples into K domains that (i) are maximally distinctive from one another in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (via kernel mean embeddings/MMD-style distances), while (ii) remain maximally learnable, meaning each domain supports a strong within-domain classifier. The authors propose a relaxed quadratic program with linear constraints that enforces class-label priors per domain to avoid per-category clustering, and they select K by a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) criterion that maximizes the weighted mean classification accuracy across domains. They further propose conditional reshaping of test data to match training domains. Experiments on object recognition (A/C/D/W) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) report improvements over using the original datasets as “domains” and over a latent-domain GMM baseline [Hoffman et al., ECCV’12]. The approach builds on kernel two-sample testing and domain adaptation (e.g., Gretton et al., MMD; Daumé; Blitzer; Gopalan et al., GFK), and complements multi-source adaptation by discovering the sources rather than taking them as given.

Strengths
- A clear and relevant problem: datasets conflate multiple nuisance factors; discovering latent domains is valuable for adaptation and for understanding dataset bias.
- A simple, generally applicable objective for “distinctiveness” using kernel mean embeddings; leverages nonparametric distances without committing to parametric mixtures.
- A pragmatic mechanism (LPC) to discourage trivial per-class clusters and a data-driven procedure (DWCV) to choose K by learnability.
- Conditional reshaping of test data is practical and shows gains in cross-view action recognition.
- Qualitative analyses are compelling and help interpret discovered factors (pose/background/color).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Supervision: Despite being framed as “unsupervised,” the method uses semantic class labels in both the LPC constraint and in DWCV. This is better described as supervised (or at least weakly supervised) latent-domain discovery.
- Label prior constraint (LPC) assumption: Forcing near-identical class-label proportions per domain prevents single-class domination but may be unrealistic when latent factors correlate with class (e.g., cows vs. horses may have different pose distributions). This risks suppressing genuine domain structure.
- Optimization clarity: The relaxed problem maximizes a convex quadratic subject to linear constraints; maximizing a convex quadratic is non-convex/NP-hard (the paper notes NP-hardness but still calls the relaxation “convex”). The solver details (initialization, multiple restarts, guarantees, sensitivity, runtime) are not specified.
- Notation/clarity: The terminology “dataset/domain/latent-domain” is inconsistent; symbols S, T, U and “domains” are interchanged in Section 4.2, making it hard to follow. There is also a presentation bug: a term of the form max_k r(U_k, B) (Eq. 7 in the text; Eq. 6 in the excerpt) is used while r(·,·) is not defined at that point (later it appears as r(A→B)). Please define consistently.
- Experimental design: While validation is adequate and shows gains, the rationale for source-target selections and the exact protocols (splits, number of restarts, kernel bandwidth sensitivity) are unclear. A leave-one-dataset-out/view-out protocol would be a more transparent and standard evaluation. Oracle analyses in Sec. 4.2 are informative but should be complemented with label-free selection baselines and ablations.
- Missing ablations: No analysis of the effect of LPC (vary δ, remove LPC, minimum-per-class only) or of using conditional distinctiveness (per-class MMD across domains) vs. unconditional MMD.
- K selection by DWCV could overfit; no stability/variance across folds or seeds is reported.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The objective and distances are standard and appropriate. However, the non-convexity of the maximization and the lack of optimization details weaken reproducibility and confidence in global optimality. Claims about “convex” formulations should be corrected or precisely scoped (e.g., the conditional reshaping of test sets appears to be a convex minimization).
- Empirical support: Improvements over using original datasets as domains and over [20] are shown, with helpful qualitative results. Yet stronger baselines (e.g., K-means with label-balance constraint, spectral relaxations, or conditional MMD variants) and ablations would better isolate where gains come from.

Clarity
- Generally readable, but Section 4.2’s notation and the dataset/domain terminology are confusing. Define “dataset,” “(source/target) domain,” and “latent domain” once with consistent symbols and provide a small running example. Clarify how MATCHING is performed without labels.

Originality
- Novel in controlling class-label distributions within a relaxed (partially) convex formulation for latent-domain discovery, and in selecting K by per-domain learnability. Related to, but distinct from, [20]’s parametric GMM view and generalized clustering [27].

Significance
- Useful for practitioners doing domain adaptation who cannot rely on dataset boundaries. The idea of picking K by learnability is pragmatic and may generalize. Impact would increase with clearer optimization details and stronger, standardized evaluation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Recast the problem as supervised/semi-supervised latent-domain discovery; state clearly what labels are required and where.
- Fix notation (define r(A→B)), unify terminology, and expand Section 4.2 with a concrete example of GOURS/GORIG/GOTHER computation.
- Clarify optimization: specify solver, initialization, restarts, runtime, and sensitivity; correct “convex” claims when maximizing a convex quadratic.
- Ablations: vary δ in LPC; remove LPC; replace LPC with softer regularizers (e.g., entropy penalty, minimum-per-class counts) or model P(z|y) to allow class-dependent domain proportions; try conditional distinctiveness (sum_c MMD between P_k(x|y=c)).
- Evaluation: adopt leave-one-dataset/view-out protocols; report variance over seeds/folds; include additional baselines (e.g., K-means with label-balance).
- Detail how MATCHING is implemented without labels and why it tracks the oracle.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, under-explored problem with a principled and practical framework.
- Demonstrates improvements and provides interpretable qualitative insights.
- Nonparametric distances make the approach broadly applicable.

Cons for acceptance
- Mischaracterization as “unsupervised” and strong LPC assumption may limit applicability.
- Optimization non-convexity and missing details reduce confidence and reproducibility.
- Experimental protocol and clarity issues hinder assessment.

Overall, a notable contribution with promising results, but several conceptual and experimental clarifications are needed to meet NeurIPS standards.
