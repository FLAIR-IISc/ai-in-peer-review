- Proposes automatic latent domain discovery using two criteria: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.  
- Formulates discovery as a convex optimization with a label prior constraint (LPC) to enforce similar class distributions across domains.  
- Uses domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to select the optimal number of latent domains K.  
- Advances prior work by using a nonparametric approach that avoids strong distributional assumptions.  
- Relaxed convex formulation with LPC is a notable technical contribution.  
- Empirical results show improvements on object (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and action (IXMAS) recognition tasks.  
- Qualitative analyses (e.g., Fig.1) illustrate meaningful discovered domain factors (pose, lighting, background).  
- Inconsistent terminology (dataset/domain/latent-domain) reduces conceptual clarity and hinders understanding.  
- LPC forcing similar class distributions across domains may be unrealistic and can merge distinct domains improperly.  
- The "unsupervised" claim is misleading because the method uses semantic class labels.  
- Experimental protocol and source-target selection (Section 4.2) are unclear; leave-one-dataset-out would be more convincing.  
- Notation and presentation errors (e.g., r(U_k, B) undefined in Eq.7) and unclear DWCV explanation harm readability.  
- Technical approach is sound overall but weakened by LPC limitation and presentation issues.  
- Paper is original in framing latent domain discovery as convex optimization with DWCV, representing a meaningful advance over parametric mixture models.  
- Significance is high given the practical problem addressed, but impact is reduced by methodological and experimental concerns.  
- Recommended revisions: clarify terminology and definitions, justify or relax LPC, improve experimental validation (leave-one-dataset-out), correct "unsupervised" statement, and define all notation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel technical contribution, nonparametric approach, and thorough experiments showing improvements.  
- Arguments against acceptance: LPC conceptual limitation, unclear terminology, misleading claims about supervision, and insufficient experimental rigor.
