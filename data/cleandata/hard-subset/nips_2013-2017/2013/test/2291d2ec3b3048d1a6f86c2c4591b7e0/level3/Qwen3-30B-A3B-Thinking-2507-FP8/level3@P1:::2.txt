

This paper addresses a critical yet underexplored aspect of domain adaptation: the automatic discovery of latent domains within visual datasets. The authors propose a novel approach that identifies domains based on two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing distribution differences between domains) and maximum learnability (ensuring strong discriminative models can be trained on each domain). This is a significant departure from the common practice of equating entire datasets with domains, which often fails to capture the complex, multi-factor nature of visual data variations (e.g., lighting, pose, background).

The work builds upon recent domain adaptation research (e.g., Saenko et al. [2], Hoffman et al. [20]) but makes a novel contribution by addressing the foundational problem of domain definition itself. The authors' key insight—that domains should be defined by their discriminative utility rather than dataset boundaries—is compelling and well-motivated through clear examples in the introduction. Their nonparametric formulation for measuring domain distinctiveness using kernel-based distribution differences (Eq. 1-2) is theoretically sound and effectively avoids the limitations of parametric clustering approaches. The domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure for determining the optimal number of domains (Sec. 2.2) provides a practical solution to the challenge of balancing domain distinctiveness with learnability.

The paper demonstrates significant empirical improvements over standard practice (treating datasets as domains) and over the recent domain discovery method of Hoffman et al. [20] across both object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human activity recognition (IXMAS) tasks. The results (Tables 1-3) convincingly show that reshaping datasets into the automatically identified domains substantially improves adaptation performance, particularly when using the MATCHING strategy to select the most appropriate domain for adaptation. The qualitative analysis (Fig. 1) effectively reveals that the identified domains correspond to meaningful visual factors like object pose, appearance, and background.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured arguments and a logical flow from problem formulation to evaluation. The writing is precise and accessible to the NIPS audience. However, the paper could benefit from more illustrative figures showing the domain discovery process and the impact of reshaping.

Regarding quality, the experimental design is sound and the results are well-supported. The authors carefully address the challenge of determining the optimal number of domains through DWCV, and the ablation studies provide strong evidence for their approach. A minor weakness is that the optimization procedure (Sec. 2.1) does not clearly demonstrate how it satisfies the original objectives (Eq. 2-3), though the empirical results validate the approach. The two criteria (distinctiveness and learnability) are not well integrated into a single optimization framework; learnability is applied only as a post-hoc selection criterion rather than being incorporated into the domain discovery objective.

The paper's significance is high, as it tackles a fundamental problem in domain adaptation that has been largely overlooked. The ability to automatically discover meaningful domains rather than relying on arbitrary dataset boundaries has substantial implications for building robust visual recognition systems. The method's practical utility is demonstrated through clear improvements across multiple tasks.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses that affect its technical quality. The image representation (bag-of-visual-words with 800 SURF codewords) is outdated and too weak for modern visual recognition tasks. The authors should have used more recent, powerful descriptors like spatial pyramid histograms of dense SURF features, Fisher vectors, or deep features. The paper also fails to specify the similarity measure used for histogram comparison—Euclidean distance is inappropriate for bag-of-visual-words; X² or intersection kernel should be used instead. The authors cite relevant works (Zhang IJCV 2006; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronnin ECCV 2010) but do not incorporate their advances.

A critical methodological issue is the use of test set information in the "reshaping" process (Sec. 4.3). The conditional reshaping of test datasets assumes knowledge of target domain labels, which is not realistic in standard domain adaptation settings. This effectively creates an imputation scenario that is not clearly stated or justified, potentially inflating results.

Despite these weaknesses, the paper makes a valuable contribution to domain adaptation by addressing the foundational problem of domain definition. The core idea is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated. The authors have successfully demonstrated that automatically discovered domains outperform both standard dataset-based domains and the recent method of Hoffman et al. [20]. The paper provides a promising direction for future work, with clear implications for dataset construction and domain adaptation.

The paper's strengths (novel problem formulation, strong empirical results, clear writing) outweigh its weaknesses (outdated representation, incomplete integration of criteria, test set reshaping issue). With minor revisions addressing the representation and test set reshaping concerns, this would be an excellent contribution. As it stands, the paper is a significant advance in domain adaptation and should be accepted.
