This paper proposes to “reshape” visual datasets into K latent domains by optimizing two axioms: maximal distinctiveness (domains should be far apart in RKHS via kernel mean embeddings) and maximal learnability (each domain supports a strong classifier, used to pick K via domain-wise cross-validation). The method enforces a label prior constraint so within-domain label proportions match the global class prior, preventing degenerate single-class domains. The framework uses nonparametric MMD-style distances (Gretton et al.) and is positioned against latent-domain discovery via parametric mixtures (Hoffman et al., ECCV’12) and generalized clustering with kernel embeddings. Experiments on object and action recognition indicate gains over original dataset partitions and a competing latent-domain method, with useful qualitative analyses.

Quality: The modeling of distinctiveness via RKHS mean embeddings is sound and well-aligned with prior theory. The K-selection by per-domain cross-validation is pragmatic. However, (i) the LPC may be too restrictive for real latent factors where class composition genuinely shifts across domains (e.g., pose distributions differing across categories); please discuss/ablate δ and cases where LPC hurts. (ii) The relaxation maximizes a convex quadratic under linear constraints, which remains nonconvex; more details on solver, initialization, and robustness are needed. (iii) The work is not fully “unsupervised” since class labels are used both in LPC and DWCV; the claim should be softened to weakly supervised/semi-supervised.

Clarity: Several notational/terminology issues impede readability. The paper inconsistently uses dataset/domain/latent-domain; provide clear definitions with a small running example. Section 4.2’s notation (S, T, U, r) is hard to follow; explicitly define all symbols. There is a presentation bug: max_k r(U_k, B) appears (Eq. 7) but r(U_k, B) is undefined; reconcile with r(A→B). Experimental setup (source/target choices) needs clearer rationale; consider leave-one-dataset-out protocols.

Originality: Controlling label distributions within domains in a relaxed optimization and selecting K by domain-wise learnability is novel relative to prior RKHS clustering and latent-domain work.

Significance: The idea is timely and practically relevant for domain adaptation and dataset bias. Reported gains are reasonable; further ablations (kernel choice/bandwidth, δ sensitivity, K-selection curves, comparison to simple baselines like class-balanced K-means) would strengthen impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean, nonparametric criterion with practical K selection.
- Addresses an important pain point (unknown domains) and shows gains.
- Useful qualitative insights on discovered factors.

Cons for acceptance:
- Clarity/notation issues and a presentation bug.
- LPC may limit applicability; limited analysis of its impact.
- Optimization details and reproducibility insufficiently specified.
- “Unsupervised” claim is misleading.

Overall: Notable contribution with promising results, but requires conceptual and experimental clarifications before publication.
