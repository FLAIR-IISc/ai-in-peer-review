## Review of “Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation”

This paper tackles the important and under-explored problem of automatically defining domains for domain adaptation, moving beyond the common practice of equating datasets with domains. The core idea – to partition data into domains based on feature-space separability and per-domain discriminative learnability – is compelling and potentially impactful. The authors propose a nonparametric formulation and optimization procedure to achieve this, demonstrating improvements over existing methods on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks. 

The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and approach. The introduction effectively highlights the limitations of current practices and establishes the need for automatic domain discovery. The proposed method, leveraging both distinctiveness and learnability, is a logical and well-reasoned approach. The experimental results are promising, showing significant gains over baseline methods, including a recent domain discovery technique [19]. 

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The optimization process (Sec. 2) and how it directly addresses objectives (2)+(3) isn’t fully elucidated. While the authors state they maximize both distinctiveness and learnability, the integration feels somewhat weak; learnability is primarily used as a wrapper for model selection rather than being directly incorporated into the partitioning objective. An integrated optimization maximizing both criteria simultaneously would be a significant improvement. 

Furthermore, the image representation (single BoW of sparse SURF with 800 codewords) is outdated and limits the potential of the approach. Upgrading to more modern descriptors like spatial pyramids of dense SURF or Fisher Vectors, as suggested in cited works (Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronnin ECCV 2010), would likely yield substantial performance gains. The use of the test set in the “reshaping” process is also poorly explained and effectively assumes an imputation setting that isn’t explicitly stated. The similarity measure used for histogram comparison also lacks clarity; Euclidean distance is inappropriate, and the authors should consider alternatives like the X^2 statistic or intersection kernel. More illustrative figures would also enhance clarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a novel and important problem in domain adaptation.
*   Demonstrates promising results, outperforming existing methods.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Weak integration of the two driving criteria (separability and learnability).
*   Outdated image representation.
*   Unclear explanation of test set usage in reshaping.



**Overall:** Despite these shortcomings, this paper presents a valuable and promising contribution to the field. The core idea is strong, and the initial results are encouraging. I recommend **acceptance** with the expectation that the authors will address the identified weaknesses in a revised version.
