This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains within image and video datasets, addressing a key limitation in domain adaptation research where datasets are often treated as proxies for true domains. The authors argue that datasets frequently contain multiple underlying domains shaped by factors like illumination, pose, and background, and introduce a method to reshape datasets into more characteristic domains based on two key principles: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. They formulate this as a non-parametric optimization problem and demonstrate its effectiveness on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks.

The core idea of automatically partitioning datasets into domains based on intrinsic statistical properties is a significant contribution. Prior work in domain adaptation largely focuses on *how* to adapt, while this paper directly tackles the question of *what constitutes a domain* in visual recognition, a question that has been relatively underexplored. The proposed approach is well-motivated, clearly articulated, and addresses a practical issue in the field. The experimental results demonstrate a clear advantage over treating datasets as single domains and outperform a recent related work [20]. The application to reshaping test datasets to match training domains is a particularly interesting extension.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The optimization process, while described, isn't convincingly shown to directly and closely solve the original objectives outlined in equations (2) and (3). The relationship between the relaxed continuous optimization and the original integer programming problem requires more justification. More critically, the two driving criteria – maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability – feel somewhat decoupled. Maximum learnability is currently implemented as a wrapper for model selection (determining *K*), rather than being integrated into the partitioning objective itself. An integrated optimization that jointly maximizes both criteria would be a significant improvement.

The writing is generally good, but the paper would benefit from more illustrative figures. Visualizations of the discovered domains, perhaps showing representative images from each, would greatly aid understanding. The experimental setup, while thorough, utilizes a relatively outdated image representation – a single bag-of-visual-words (BoW) descriptor based on sparse SURF features with only 800 codewords. Given the significant advances in image descriptors (spatial pyramids of dense SURF, Fisher Vectors, etc.), this limits the potential of the method and weakens the conclusions. The authors should consider cited works such as Zhang et al. (IJCV 2006), Jurie et al. (CVPR 2005), Lazebnik et al. (CVPR 2006), and Perronnin et al. (ECCV 2010). Additionally, the choice of similarity measure for histogram comparison is unclear; Euclidean distance is likely inappropriate and should be justified or replaced with a more suitable metric like the X^2 statistic or an intersection kernel. 

Regarding the experimental section, the test set reshaping process is interesting but poorly explained. It effectively assumes an imputation setting where labels are known on the test set, but this is not explicitly stated. Clarifying this assumption and providing a more detailed explanation of the procedure is necessary.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** Addresses an under-explored problem in domain adaptation: automatic domain discovery.
*   **Significance:**  Provides a valuable tool for improving domain adaptation performance by creating more meaningful domain representations.
*   **Soundness:** The proposed method is theoretically motivated and supported by experimental results.
*   **Experimental Validation:** Demonstrates significant improvements over baseline approaches and a related work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Optimization Details:**  The connection between the relaxed optimization and the original objectives is not fully clarified.
*   **Decoupled Criteria:** Maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability are not integrated in the optimization process.
*   **Outdated Representation:** The image representation is relatively weak by current standards.
*   **Test Set Reshaping Clarity:** The test set reshaping procedure is not well explained and relies on an implicit assumption about label availability.



**Overall:**

Despite these shortcomings, this paper represents a valuable and promising contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The core idea is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results are encouraging.  I believe the paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance. However, I strongly encourage the authors to address the weaknesses regarding the optimization details, descriptor choice, and clarification of the test set reshaping procedure in a revised version.
