

This paper proposes an approach to automatically discover latent domains in visual datasets to improve domain adaptation performance. The authors introduce two key properties for latent domains: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing inter-domain distribution differences) and maximum learnability (ensuring sufficient data per domain for effective classification). They formulate this as an optimization problem using kernel-based distribution difference measures, relaxing it to a continuous quadratic program with label prior constraints, and determine the number of domains via domain-wise cross-validation.

The paper is well-written and presents a novel approach to an important problem in domain adaptation. The experimental results on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and action recognition (IXMAS) demonstrate improved performance over baselines, particularly when using the identified domains for adaptation. The motivation for discovering latent domains rather than relying on dataset boundaries is compelling, and the empirical analysis provides valuable insights into the factors determining the discovered domains (e.g., pose, background, color).

However, several concerns remain. The motivation for avoiding per-category clustering lacks evidence, and the paper does not sufficiently explain how their method prevents this common pitfall. The necessity of the label-prior constraint is unclear, as no ablation study without it is provided. Comparisons to standard clustering baselines (k-means) and random partitioning would strengthen the claims. The paper also requires clarification on what "maximally different in distribution" refers to (features, labels, or both), and the formulation of β_{mk} appears inconsistent with the simplex constraint. The geodesic flow kernel usage needs more detail, and the proof for the bound at line 215 is missing.

Despite these concerns, the authors addressed most points in their rebuttal, particularly providing additional ablation studies and clarifying the constraints. The work makes a meaningful contribution to domain adaptation by addressing the critical problem of latent domain discovery, though the technical justification for key design choices requires strengthening. The paper is technically sound, well-structured, and presents significant empirical results that advance the state of the art in domain adaptation. While some methodological details need clarification, the overall contribution is valuable to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
