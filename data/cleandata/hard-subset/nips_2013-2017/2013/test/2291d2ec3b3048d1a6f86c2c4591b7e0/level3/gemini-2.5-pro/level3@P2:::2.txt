This paper addresses the problem of defining domains for domain adaptation in visual recognition. The authors argue that the common practice of equating datasets with domains is a flawed simplification, as real-world data distributions are affected by a complex mix of factors like lighting, pose, and background. The core contribution is a method to automatically discover latent domains within a collection of image data. The proposed approach partitions the data based on two principles: (1) **Maximum Distinctiveness**, ensuring the discovered domains are as different from each other as possible in their feature distributions, and (2) **Maximum Learnability**, ensuring that each domain provides sufficient data to train a strong discriminative classifier.

Maximum distinctiveness is formulated as an optimization problem that maximizes the sum of pairwise distances between domain distributions, measured non-parametrically using a kernel-based MMD metric. Maximum learnability is used as a criterion for model selection, where the optimal number of latent domains is chosen by evaluating the domain-wise cross-validation accuracy. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach on object and human activity recognition tasks, showing that adapting from these automatically discovered domains yields superior performance compared to using the original datasets as domains or using the domains discovered by a recent competing method [20].

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and under-explored problem in domain adaptation. Questioning the ad-hoc definition of domains as datasets is critical, and this work proposes a principled, data-driven solution. This is a novel contribution that could significantly impact how researchers approach domain adaptation problems, moving beyond simple cross-dataset generalization. The ideas presented could also inform future dataset collection efforts.

2.  **Methodological Contribution:** The formulation based on maximizing domain distinctiveness via a non-parametric measure is elegant and well-motivated. This avoids the limitations of previous parametric approaches (e.g., [20]) which may not be suitable for complex visual data.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed approach. The method shows significant improvements over using the original datasets as source domains and also outperforms the most relevant prior work [20]. The reported oracle and practical accuracies (Tables 1 and 2) provide strong evidence for the utility of the discovered domains.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Technical Soundness and Clarity:**
    *   The optimization procedure in Section 2.1 is not fully justified. The paper presents an integer programming problem (Eq. 2 & 3) but then solves a relaxed continuous version (Eq. 4 & 5). It is not clear how well the solution to the relaxed problem approximates the original objective. A discussion of the relaxation gap or an empirical validation of the approximation quality would strengthen the paper.
    *   The two core principles, distinctiveness and learnability, feel disconnected. Learnability is only used in a post-processing step (a wrapper for model selection) to determine the number of domains, K. A more powerful and unified approach would be to integrate a learnability-based regularizer directly into the partitioning objective. This would allow the optimization to jointly find partitions that are both distinct and useful for classification.
    *   The concept of "reshaping the test datasets" (Section 4.3) is interesting but explained too briefly. The setup is unclear; it seems to be an unsupervised or transductive setting, but the assumptions and procedure need to be detailed more explicitly.

2.  **Quality of Experimental Setup:**
    *   A major weakness is the use of outdated and relatively weak image features (a single Bag-of-Words histogram over sparse SURF with 800 codewords). By 2013 standards, much more powerful representations such as Spatial Pyramid Matching (Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006) with dense SIFT, or Fisher Vectors (Perronnin et al., ECCV 2010) were standard. Using such a weak descriptor makes it difficult to assess how the method would perform in a state-of-the-art system and may limit the generality of the conclusions. Re-running key experiments with stronger features would make the results much more compelling.
    *   The paper does not specify the similarity measure used for comparing the 800-bin BoW histograms. Euclidean distance is generally inappropriate for histograms; metrics like the Chi-squared distance or intersection kernel are standard and should be used. This detail is important for reproducibility and correctness.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, but would benefit from more visualizations. Beyond the exemplar images in Figure 1, a 2D visualization (e.g., via t-SNE) of the feature space showing the original dataset boundaries versus the discovered domain boundaries would provide valuable intuition.

**Summary of evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** The core idea is sound, but there are some weaknesses in the technical justification of the optimization and the experimental setup (outdated features).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is mostly clear, though some key parts (test set reshaping, optimization relaxation) would benefit from more detail.
*   **Originality:** High. The problem formulation is novel and addresses a significant gap in the domain adaptation literature.
*   **Significance:** High. The work has the potential to change the standard protocol for evaluating domain adaptation algorithms and offers a valuable tool for analyzing dataset structure.

**Recommendation:**

Despite the noted weaknesses, particularly the outdated feature representation, the paper makes a valuable and timely contribution. It introduces a novel and important problem—automatic domain discovery—and proposes a sensible and effective initial solution. The originality and potential impact of this work outweigh its current shortcomings. I recommend acceptance. The authors should, however, address the weaknesses mentioned above in the final version, especially by clarifying the methodology and, if possible, strengthening the experiments with more modern features.
