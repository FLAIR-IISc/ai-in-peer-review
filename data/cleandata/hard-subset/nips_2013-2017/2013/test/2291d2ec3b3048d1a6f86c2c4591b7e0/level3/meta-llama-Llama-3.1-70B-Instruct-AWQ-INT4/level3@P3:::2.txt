The paper "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation" proposes an automatic approach to partitioning images across datasets into domains driven by feature-space separability and per-domain discriminative learnability. The authors address the under-explored problem of automatically defining domains for domain adaptation, which is a crucial step in building robust classifiers for real-world applications.

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach, which is based on two key properties: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The authors devise a nonparametric formulation and efficient optimization procedure to discover domains among both training and test data. The experimental results on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showing significant improvements over using datasets as domains and over the recent domain discovery method.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to identify latent domains that are not necessarily aligned with the original datasets. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their approach can discover domains that are determined by factors such as object pose, appearance, and background. The experimental results also demonstrate the practical utility of the identified domains, showing that they can be used to improve adaptation results.

However, there are some criticisms that can be raised. Firstly, the proposed optimization (Sec. 2) is not clearly shown to solve the original objectives (2)+(3) closely. The authors relax the optimization problem into a continuous optimization, but it is not clear how this relaxation affects the solution. Secondly, the two driving criteria (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) are not well integrated, with maximal learnability being applied only as a wrapper for model selection, not as part of the partitioning objective.

Another issue with the paper is the use of outdated image representation (single BoW of sparse SURF with 800 codewords), which is too weak for current standards. The authors should consider upgrading the descriptors to more modern and powerful representations, such as spatial pyramid of dense SURF or Fisher Vectors.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from more illustrative figures to help explain the proposed approach and the experimental results. The writing is clear, but some of the figures are not well-labeled, making it difficult to understand the results.

In terms of originality, the paper addresses a novel problem and proposes a new approach to domain adaptation. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from previous work, including the recent domain discovery method. However, the paper could benefit from more discussion on the related work and how it compares to other approaches in the field.

Overall, despite some shortcomings, the paper is a valuable and promising contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed approach, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. With some revisions to address the criticisms raised above, the paper has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper addresses a novel problem in domain adaptation and proposes a new approach to automatically defining domains.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showing significant improvements over using datasets as domains and over the recent domain discovery method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed approach.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The proposed optimization is not clearly shown to solve the original objectives closely.
* The two driving criteria are not well integrated, with maximal learnability being applied only as a wrapper for model selection.
* The image representation used is outdated and too weak for current standards.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Revise the optimization procedure to clearly show how it solves the original objectives.
* Integrate the two driving criteria (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) into a single objective function.
* Upgrade the image representation to more modern and powerful representations, such as spatial pyramid of dense SURF or Fisher Vectors.
* Add more illustrative figures to help explain the proposed approach and the experimental results.
