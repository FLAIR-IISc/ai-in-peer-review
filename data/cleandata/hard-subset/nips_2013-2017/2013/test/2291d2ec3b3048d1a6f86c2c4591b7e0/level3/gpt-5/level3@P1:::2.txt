This paper tackles the under-explored problem of automatically discovering latent domains within visual datasets to improve domain adaptation. Rather than equating a dataset to a single domain, the authors propose to reshape pooled images (or videos) into multiple domains that are (i) maximally distinctive in distribution and (ii) maximally learnable for the downstream recognition task. Distinctiveness is operationalized via a kernel two-sample distance between empirical distributions (akin to MMD [21]) summed across domain pairs (TDD). Learnability drives model selection over the number of domains via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV), preferring partitions that yield stronger discriminative classifiers. The optimization relaxes an integer assignment to a continuous quadratic program with a label prior constraint to avoid collapsing to per-category clusters. The approach is evaluated on object recognition across Caltech-256/Amazon/DSLR/Webcam [2,4,14] and multi-view IXMAS actions [15], and compared against treating datasets as domains and the latent-domain method of Hoffman et al. [20], as well as multi-source adaptation strategies (UNION/ENSEMBLE and matching).

Relation to prior work: The method builds on kernel mean embeddings (two-sample tests) [21], unsupervised domain adaptation with subspace/geometry (e.g., GFK [4]) and multi-source adaptation [10,28,29]. It contrasts with “dataset bias” work [16–19], which treats datasets as domains, and with [20], which assumes a parametric hierarchical mixture for latent domains. The nonparametric formulation here is a complementary, arguably more flexible alternative.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The core idea—maximize pairwise MMD among domains subject to a label prior constraint, then select K by DWCV—is reasonable and well-motivated. However, the relaxation moves from the original combinatorial problem (Eq. 2–3) to a convex maximization of a quadratic under linear constraints (Eq. 4–5), which remains in general NP-hard. The paper relies on off-the-shelf solvers without guarantees or approximation bounds. It would help to (i) clarify how closely the relaxed solution approximates the discrete assignment objective, (ii) report sensitivity to solver initialization, and (iii) provide runtime/complexity vs. M.
- Integration of objectives: “Maximum learnability” is only used to choose K, not within the partitioning objective. This decoupling risks partitions that are maximally separated in feature space but not optimal for classification. An integrated formulation—e.g., a multi-objective or constrained optimization combining TDD with a surrogate discriminative loss, or a bilevel/alternating scheme that updates domain assignments to improve a validation margin—would likely yield stronger, task-aligned domains.
- Target reshaping: The conditional reshaping of test data to match training domains is interesting and potentially impactful, but the protocol needs to be made explicit. As written, it appears to use unlabeled target data transductively to assign target instances to latent domains. Please clarify: no target labels are used; does the method require access to the full target set at once; is the reshaping done once per target set or uses any cross-validation signal from target? A stricter “no peeking” protocol and a separate setting with transductive access would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The high-level intuition behind maximum distinctiveness/learnability is clear.
- Missing details: specify the kernel used for histogram features. If Gaussian RBF on raw BoW is used, justify this choice; for histograms, chi-square or intersection kernels are typically preferable. Provide bandwidth/normalization details and any feature pre-processing (e.g., L1, power transform).
- Figures: more illustrative examples of discovered domains and failure cases would aid understanding. An ablation on the label prior constraint (δ), and on removing LPC, would be informative.

Originality
- The problem formulation (automatic latent domain discovery for visual datasets) is novel and timely relative to prior NIPS work, which assumed domains were given. Using nonparametric distribution discrepancies for domain partitioning and a DWCV criterion to select K is a fresh combination distinct from [20].
- The conditional reshaping of test sets to align with discovered source domains is also a noteworthy contribution.

Significance
- Empirical results show consistent gains over dataset-as-domain baselines and improvements over [20], both in oracle and practical multi-source settings. The impact is potentially broad: better partitions can benefit any downstream adaptation method.
- That said, the study uses an outdated representation (single BoW of sparse SURF, 800 codewords). In 2010–2013, stronger baselines (dense SIFT with spatial pyramids [Lazebnik CVPR’06], Fisher Vectors [Perronnin ECCV’10], improved vocabularies [Zhang IJCV’06; Jurie CVPR’05]) were standard and often crucial for cross-domain robustness. The choice of weak features may underestimate both baselines and the method’s ultimate impact.

Detailed suggestions
- Integrate learnability: add a term encouraging high within-domain classification margin/accuracy (e.g., hinge loss with shared weights) or perform alternating updates: assign domains to maximize TDD subject to maintaining or improving cross-validated accuracy.
- Revisit LPC: the assumption that label priors are domain-invariant is often violated (label shift). Consider learning per-domain priors with slack penalties, or adding a regularizer that discourages but does not enforce equality.
- Features/kernels: upgrade to dense SIFT with spatial pyramid, Fisher Vectors, or (if feasible) CNN features; use additive kernels (χ2, intersection) or Hellinger on histograms; report sensitivity to kernel bandwidth.
- Evaluation: include runtime and scalability, stability across random seeds, and ablations (effect of K, δ, kernel choice). Clarify the conditional test reshaping protocol and include a setting where only a small unlabeled target sample is available.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation and motivation; novel nonparametric approach to latent domain discovery.
- Strong empirical improvements over dataset-based domains and over [20].
- Practical procedure (DWCV) to select the number of domains.
- Insightful extension to conditionally reshape test sets.

Cons
- Relaxation/optimization lacks guarantees and analysis of approximation quality.
- Learnability is only a model-selection wrapper, not part of the core objective.
- Assumption of domain-invariant label priors may be unrealistic.
- Outdated/weak features and missing kernel details could bias results; limited analysis of scalability.

Recommendation: Accept. Despite methodological gaps and dated features, the paper addresses an important, under-explored question with a principled and empirically effective approach. Tightening the optimization-analysis link, clarifying the test reshaping protocol, and upgrading descriptors would further strengthen the contribution.
