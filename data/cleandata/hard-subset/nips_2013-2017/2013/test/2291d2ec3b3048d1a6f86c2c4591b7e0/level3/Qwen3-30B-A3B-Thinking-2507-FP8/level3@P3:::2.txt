

# Review: Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation

This paper addresses a critical but underexplored problem in domain adaptation: automatically defining meaningful domains within visual datasets rather than relying on the ad hoc practice of equating entire datasets with domains. The authors propose a novel approach to reshape datasets into latent domains that satisfy two key properties: (1) maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different in distribution from each other) and (2) maximum learnability (domains should enable training strong discriminative classifiers for the original categories).

The work builds on the observation that visual datasets often contain multiple hidden domains influenced by factors like pose, lighting, background, and resolution, which are not captured by simply using entire datasets as domains. The authors' key insight is that domains should be defined to maximize both distinctiveness (via a kernel-based measure of distribution difference) and learnability (via domain-wise cross-validation to determine the optimal number of domains). This is a significant departure from previous approaches like [20], which modeled domains using parametric Gaussian mixtures, and from the common practice of treating datasets as domains.

**Strengths:**

*Originality*: The paper makes a clear contribution to a neglected problem in domain adaptation. While domain adaptation techniques are abundant, the problem of automatically defining domains has received little attention. The authors correctly identify this gap and propose a principled approach to address it, making this a novel contribution to the field.

*Significance*: The proposed method demonstrates significant improvements over using datasets as domains and over the recent domain discovery method [20] across multiple object recognition and human activity recognition tasks. The paper convincingly shows that reshaping datasets into latent domains leads to better domain adaptation performance, which has practical implications for real-world applications where domain shifts occur.

*Quality*: The experimental design is comprehensive, with careful evaluation on multiple visual recognition tasks. The authors provide thorough empirical evidence supporting their approach's effectiveness, including comparisons to both naive approaches (K-means, random partition) and a competing method [20]. The DWCV procedure for determining the optimal number of domains is well-motivated.

*Clarity*: The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and presents the core ideas effectively. The problem statement and motivation are compelling, and the technical approach is explained with sufficient mathematical detail.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality*: The paper's most significant weakness is its outdated image representation. Using a single BoW of sparse SURF with 800 codewords is far behind current standards (e.g., spatial pyramid of dense SURF, Fisher Vectors, or deep learning features). This choice limits the paper's ability to demonstrate the full potential of their domain discovery approach. Additionally, the similarity measure used for histogram comparison is unclear—Euclidean distance is inappropriate for histogram comparison (X² or intersection kernel should be used).

*Quality*: The optimization in Section 2 does not clearly integrate the two key criteria (distinctiveness and learnability). Maximum learnability is applied only as a wrapper for model selection (to determine K), not as part of the partitioning objective. The paper does not convincingly show that the proposed optimization solves the combined objectives (2)+(3) closely. An integrated optimization that jointly maximizes both criteria would be preferable.

*Clarity*: While well-written, the paper would benefit from more illustrative figures showing the identified domains and the reshaping process. The explanation of the "reshaping" process for test sets (Section 4.3) is poorly explained and effectively assumes an imputation setting that is not stated.

*Originality*: The paper adequately references related work, though it could better distinguish its contribution from [20] and other clustering approaches like [27].

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The problem addressed is important and underexplored
- The paper provides a principled approach to a critical problem in domain adaptation
- The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over baselines
- The paper is well-structured and clearly written
- The approach has clear practical implications for visual recognition tasks

*Con:*
- The image representation is outdated and limits the paper's impact
- The two key criteria are not properly integrated in the optimization
- The explanation of test set reshaping is unclear
- The paper does not adequately address the similarity measure for histogram comparison

**Overall Verdict:**

Despite the methodological weaknesses in the image representation and the incomplete integration of the two key criteria, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by addressing a significant but neglected problem in domain adaptation. The core idea of automatically discovering latent domains based on feature-space separability and discriminative learnability is promising and well-motivated. With minor revisions to address the methodological shortcomings (particularly upgrading the image representation and clarifying the optimization framework), this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

The paper's main weakness—using outdated image representations—is a technical limitation rather than a conceptual flaw, and the authors could easily address this in a revision. The proposed approach has clear potential to improve domain adaptation performance across many visual recognition tasks.

**Recommendation: Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable and promising contribution to domain adaptation research that addresses an important gap in the literature.
