This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in image or video datasets. The method imposes two key properties on domains: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The authors devise a nonparametric formulation and efficient optimization procedure that can successfully discover domains among both training and test data.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed approach and its advantages over existing methods. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem, including a discussion of the limitations of current methods and the need for a more principled approach to domain discovery.

The experimental results are impressive, with the proposed method outperforming existing methods on several object recognition and human activity recognition tasks. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the identified domains, including the factors that contribute to their distinctiveness and learnability.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The proposed optimization procedure is not clearly shown to solve the original objectives, and the two driving criteria (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) are not well integrated. The authors also use an outdated image representation (single BoW of sparse SURF with 800 codewords) and do not provide a clear explanation of the similarity measure used for histogram comparison.

Despite these limitations, the paper is a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation and should be accepted. The authors' approach to domain discovery is novel and has the potential to improve the performance of domain adaptation algorithms.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to domain discovery that has the potential to improve the performance of domain adaptation algorithms.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem and a clear explanation of their proposed approach.
* The experimental results are impressive, with the proposed method outperforming existing methods on several object recognition and human activity recognition tasks.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The proposed optimization procedure is not clearly shown to solve the original objectives.
* The two driving criteria (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) are not well integrated.
* The authors use an outdated image representation (single BoW of sparse SURF with 800 codewords) and do not provide a clear explanation of the similarity measure used for histogram comparison.

Recommendations for improvement:

* The authors should provide a clearer explanation of the optimization procedure and how it relates to the original objectives.
* The authors should integrate the two driving criteria (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) more effectively.
* The authors should consider using more modern image representations (e.g., spatial pyramid of dense SURF, Fisher Vectors) and provide a clear explanation of the similarity measure used for histogram comparison.
