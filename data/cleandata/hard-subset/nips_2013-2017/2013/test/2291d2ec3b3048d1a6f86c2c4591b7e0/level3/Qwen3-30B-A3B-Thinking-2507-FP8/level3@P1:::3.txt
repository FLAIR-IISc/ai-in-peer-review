

This paper proposes a novel framework for discovering latent domains within visual datasets by imposing two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing distributional differences between domains in RKHS) and maximum learnability (ensuring strong discriminative models can be learned from each domain). The authors formulate this as a convex optimization problem with a label prior constraint to enforce similar class distributions across domains, and select the number of domains K through domain-wise cross-validation. The approach is evaluated on object and action recognition tasks, showing improvements over using original datasets as domains.

The work builds upon domain adaptation research (e.g., Saenko et al. [2], Hoffman et al. [20]) by addressing a critical gap: most domain adaptation research assumes predefined domains, while visual datasets often contain multiple latent domains not semantically defined. The authors correctly identify that simple appearance-based clustering leads to per-category domains, which is undesirable. The RKHS distance measure for domain distinctiveness is well-motivated and aligns with recent distributional distance measures (Gretton et al. [21]).

Strengths include the novel formulation for enforcing similar class distributions across domains through the label prior constraint (preventing single-class domination), the practical solution of using domain-wise cross-validation to determine K, and the extensive evaluation showing consistent improvements in recognition accuracy. The qualitative analysis revealing how discovered domains correspond to visual factors like pose and background is particularly valuable.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses. The terminology is inconsistent and confusing, with unclear distinctions between "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain" throughout. The claim that the problem is unsupervised is misleading since the method explicitly uses semantic class labels to enforce the label prior constraint. Forcing similar class distributions across domains may be limiting for real-world scenarios where different classes have different underlying domain structures (e.g., pose distributions differ between horses and cows).

The experimental section has critical clarity issues. Section 4.2 is particularly confusing, with unclear definitions of terms like "GOURS" and "GOTHER" in Table 1. Equation 7 uses "max_k r(U_k, B)" without definition, which is a serious presentation error. The rationale for source-target dataset selection is unclear, and a leave-one-dataset-out approach would be more appropriate for validation.

The quality of the technical content is sound, with a well-motivated convex optimization approach. The originality is notable in the context of domain adaptation, though it builds on Hoffman et al. [20]. The significance is promising, as better domain representation could improve domain adaptation performance, but the limitations in the approach and unclear validation reduce its immediate impact.

The paper would benefit from: 1) Clearer definitions of key terms, 2) Correction of the unsupervised claim, 3) Discussion of the limitations of forcing similar class distributions, 4) Clarification of experimental methodology, and 5) Better explanation of domain-wise cross-validation.

While the core idea of discovering latent domains with maximum distinctiveness and learnability is promising, the current presentation and methodological limitations prevent it from being a strong contribution. The paper makes a notable contribution to domain adaptation by addressing latent domain discovery, but the unresolved conceptual and presentation issues significantly undermine its scientific quality. With substantial revisions addressing these concerns, this could become a valuable contribution to the field. As written, the paper's weaknesses outweigh its strengths, particularly regarding clarity and methodological soundness.
