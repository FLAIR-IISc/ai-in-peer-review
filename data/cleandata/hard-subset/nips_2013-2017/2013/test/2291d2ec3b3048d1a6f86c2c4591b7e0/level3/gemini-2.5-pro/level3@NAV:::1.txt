This paper proposes a novel method for automatically discovering latent domains within a training dataset to improve domain adaptation performance. The core idea is to partition the data such that the resulting domains are maximally different from each other. This is achieved by defining a kernel-based difference metric between feature distributions and formulating the partitioning as an integer program, which is then relaxed into a continuous quadratic optimization problem. Key constraints ensure each data point is assigned to a single domain and that the per-domain label distribution matches the global prior. The authors evaluate their approach on object and action recognition tasks, demonstrating improved performance over a prior domain adaptation method [19]. This work challenges the common practice of equating datasets with domains, relating to work like [20] but using a non-parametric approach. A relevant, un-cited work is Torralba & Efros (CVPR 2011) on dataset bias.

The paper is well-written, the proposed approach appears novel, and the performance gains are a clear strength. The attempt to move beyond the "dataset-as-domain" paradigm is a significant and welcome contribution to the field.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need to be addressed. The primary concern is the motivation. It is not evident that clustering by appearance to create "maximally different" feature distributions will necessarily yield domains useful for adaptation, as opposed to simply partitioning categories by visual sub-types. The paper needs to provide a stronger justification for how the model avoids this failure mode. Secondly, the evaluation is not fully convincing. The necessity of the label-prior constraint is unclear without an ablation study showing results in its absence. More importantly, the method's performance should be compared against simpler baselines, such as k-means clustering on the features, unsupervised object discovery methods, and even a random equal-partitioning baseline, to properly contextualize its contribution.

For the authors to improve the paper, I would request the following:
1.  Clarify what “maximally different in distribution” refers to (e.g., distribution over features, labels, etc.).
2.  Provide the requested ablation study and baseline comparisons mentioned above.
3.  Justify the bound referenced on line 215 and clarify the constraints around line 182, which appear to differ from earlier ones. It is also unclear if β_{mk} (line 166) already lies on a simplex as formulated.
4.  Provide more insight into *why* the approach outperforms baselines, perhaps with examples of systematic failures of other methods that this approach overcomes.
5.  Include implementation details for the geodesic flow kernel to aid reproducibility.
6.  Correct the typo in Eq (1) (M'k should be Mk').

Overall, this is a promising paper with a novel idea, but its claims require stronger motivational and experimental support.
