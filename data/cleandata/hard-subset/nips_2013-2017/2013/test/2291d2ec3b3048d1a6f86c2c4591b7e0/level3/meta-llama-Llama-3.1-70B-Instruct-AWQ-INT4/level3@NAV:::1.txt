This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in image or video datasets, which is crucial for domain adaptation in visual recognition tasks. The authors introduce two desirable properties for the identified domains: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The former ensures that the distributions of the identified domains are maximally different from each other, while the latter ensures that a strong discriminative model can be learned from each domain.

The authors formulate the problem as a nonparametric optimization problem, which is relaxed into a continuous quadratic optimization problem. They also propose a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) method to determine the optimal number of domains. The approach is extensively evaluated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, and the results show that the identified domains outperform the original datasets and the domains discovered by a competing method.

The paper is well-written and appears novel. The performance gains are appreciated, and the approach has the potential to improve domain adaptation in various visual recognition tasks. However, there are some concerns and questions that need to be addressed.

Firstly, the motivation for the approach is unclear, and there is no evidence that clustering by appearance yields per-category domains. The authors should provide more insights into why their approach is necessary and how it differs from previous methods.

Secondly, the necessity of the label-prior constraint is unclear, and the authors should provide results without it and compare them to k-means, unsupervised object discovery baselines, and random equal partitioning.

Thirdly, the definition of "maximally different in distribution" is unclear, and the authors should specify what distribution they are referring to (features, labels, or other).

Lastly, the authors should provide more details on the use of the geodesic flow kernel and the DWCV method, and clarify some of the constraints and equations in the paper.

Overall, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation, but it needs to address some of the concerns and questions raised above. With proper revisions, the paper could be accepted for publication.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to automatic domain discovery
* Extensive evaluation on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks
* Potential to improve domain adaptation in various visual recognition tasks

Weaknesses:

* Motivation for the approach is unclear
* Necessity of the label-prior constraint is unclear
* Definition of "maximally different in distribution" is unclear
* Lack of details on the use of the geodesic flow kernel and the DWCV method

Recommendations:

* Provide more insights into the motivation for the approach
* Provide results without the label-prior constraint and compare them to other methods
* Clarify the definition of "maximally different in distribution"
* Provide more details on the use of the geodesic flow kernel and the DWCV method
* Clarify some of the constraints and equations in the paper

Rating: 7/10

Acceptance: Leans towards acceptance with revisions.
