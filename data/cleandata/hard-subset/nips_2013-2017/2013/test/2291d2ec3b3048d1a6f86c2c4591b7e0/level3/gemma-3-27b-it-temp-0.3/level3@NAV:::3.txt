## Review of “Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation”

This paper proposes a novel framework for automatically discovering latent domains within visual datasets, aiming to improve domain adaptation performance. The core idea is to split data into K domains that maximize both distinctiveness (in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space) and learnability (measured by per-domain classification accuracy). The authors address the common issue in domain adaptation where datasets are treated as proxies for true domains, and offer a principled approach to uncover underlying data distributions. This work builds upon existing domain adaptation research [1, 2, 3, 4] by focusing on *how* domains are defined, rather than just *how* to adapt across them. The novelty lies in the convex formulation for controlling class-label distributions during latent domain discovery and the use of classification quality to select the optimal number of domains (K).

The paper is generally well-motivated and tackles an important problem. The proposed approach is theoretically sound, leveraging kernel methods and optimization techniques. The experimental validation on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks demonstrates reasonable gains, suggesting the method's effectiveness. The inclusion of qualitative results (Figure 1) provides valuable insights into the discovered domains. 

However, the paper suffers from clarity issues. The terminology surrounding “dataset,” “domain,” and “latent-domain” is inconsistent and requires precise definitions with illustrative examples. Section 4.2, describing the experimental setup, is particularly unclear. A minor presentation bug exists with the undefined `max_k r(U_k, B)` in Eq.7. Furthermore, the claim of being an “unsupervised” method is misleading, as the approach explicitly utilizes semantic class labels. While the method doesn't require labeled *domains*, it relies on labeled *data* within those domains. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to latent domain discovery with a strong theoretical foundation.
*   Demonstrated improvements in domain adaptation performance on multiple tasks.
*   Addresses a critical gap in domain adaptation research – the definition of domains.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Clarity issues regarding terminology and experimental setup.
*   Misleading claim of unsupervised learning.
*   The limitation of the label prior constraint, potentially hindering the discovery of domains with differing class distributions.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a notable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. However, several conceptual and experimental clarifications are needed before it can be considered a strong paper. I recommend **conditional acceptance** pending major revisions addressing the clarity issues, clarifying the unsupervised learning claim, and providing a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the label prior constraint. The authors should also define `max_k r(U_k, B)` or remove the equation.
