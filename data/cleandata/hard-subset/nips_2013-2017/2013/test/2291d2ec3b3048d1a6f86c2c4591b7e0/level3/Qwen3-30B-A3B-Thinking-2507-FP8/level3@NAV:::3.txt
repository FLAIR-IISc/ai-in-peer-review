

This paper proposes a novel framework for automatically discovering latent domains in visual datasets by enforcing two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing pairwise domain dissimilarity in RKHS) and maximum learnability (ensuring each domain supports strong discriminative models). The authors introduce a convex optimization approach that splits data into K domains with similar within-domain class distributions, selecting K via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to maximize average classification accuracy.

The paper makes a notable contribution by addressing the conceptual gap between dataset-level domains and true latent domains in visual recognition. It extends prior work by Hoffman et al. (2012) by using nonparametric methods instead of restrictive parametric assumptions, and it provides a principled way to determine the number of domains. The experimental results on object and action recognition datasets demonstrate consistent improvements over both original dataset divisions and the competing method.

However, several critical issues undermine the paper's contribution. First, the claim that the problem is "unsupervised" is misleading since the method explicitly uses semantic class labels to enforce similar within-domain class distributions via the label prior constraint (LPC). This fundamentally contradicts the unsupervised learning premise. Second, the paper suffers from significant clarity issues: inconsistent terminology (dataset/domain/latent-domain) creates confusion, and Section 4.2's experimental rationale is unclear—particularly the choice of source-target domains without justification like leave-one-dataset-out validation. Third, a presentation error appears in Equation 7 where max_k r(U_k, B) is used without definition.

The method's constraint of enforcing identical class distributions across domains appears problematic for real-world scenarios where class-specific factors (e.g., pose distributions differing between horses vs. cows) are natural latent factors. While the DWCV approach for selecting K is novel, the paper fails to adequately address whether this constraint is necessary or beneficial in practice.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound approach with promising empirical results, but the conceptual misalignment with unsupervised learning and clarity issues significantly weaken its contribution. The authors should clarify the role of class labels, revise terminology, and provide stronger justification for their domain constraints to strengthen this potentially valuable contribution.
