

**Review for "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"**

**Summary:** This paper proposes an approach to automatically discover latent domains in visual datasets, addressing the problem that standard domain adaptation research often equates datasets with domains, which may contain multiple underlying distributions. The authors introduce two key properties for latent domains: maximum distinctiveness (ensuring domains are maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (ensuring strong discriminative models can be learned from each domain). They formulate the problem as a nonparametric optimization with constraints, and use domain-wise cross-validation to determine the optimal number of domains. The approach is evaluated on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human activity recognition (IXMAS multi-view actions), showing improvements over baselines that treat datasets as single domains.

The paper correctly identifies that equating datasets with domains is problematic (as noted in Torralba & Efros, "Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias," CVPR 2011), and this work provides an automatic method for discovering these latent domains. It relates to prior NIPS work on domain adaptation [1-4] and the relevant NIPS 2012 paper by Hoffman et al. [20] on latent domain discovery.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear optimization framework. The relaxation of the integer programming problem to continuous quadratic optimization is well-motivated. The domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure for determining domain count is reasonable. However, the necessity of the label prior constraint (LPC) is not well-justified—experiments without this constraint would strengthen the paper. The claim that the method avoids per-category clustering (as observed in [20]) needs stronger evidence. The paper would benefit from ablation studies showing the contribution of each component (distinctiveness, learnability, LPC).

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The problem statement is clear, and the technical formulation is presented logically. However, several ambiguities need clarification: (1) "maximally different in distribution" should be precisely defined (distribution over features, labels, or both?), (2) the constraints in line 182 appear inconsistent with earlier constraints (lines 162-165), and (3) β_{mk} as defined on line 166 appears to already lie on a simplex, making the relaxation unnecessary. The paper should clarify these points.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by proposing a method to automatically discover latent domains rather than assuming dataset boundaries define domains. While [20] also addresses latent domain discovery, this paper differs in its nonparametric formulation and the addition of maximum learnability. The approach appears sufficiently distinct from prior work, and the paper adequately references related work.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is significant for domain adaptation research, as many methods assume datasets correspond to well-defined domains. The results show consistent improvements over baselines, suggesting practical utility. The paper also provides insights into dominant factors defining domains (pose, appearance, background), which could inform future dataset construction. However, the paper could better articulate how the method advances the state of the art beyond simply improving adaptation performance.

**Strengths:**
- Well-motivated problem addressing a common pitfall in domain adaptation research
- Clear technical formulation with a reasonable optimization procedure
- Extensive evaluation across two different tasks
- Demonstrated consistent improvements over baselines
- Provides useful insights into latent domain structure

**Weaknesses:**
- Motivation for avoiding per-category clustering is not sufficiently supported
- Necessity of label-prior constraint is unclear and requires ablation study
- Several technical ambiguities that need clarification
- Limited comparison to unsupervised clustering baselines (K-means, random partition)
- Insufficient explanation of why the method outperforms baselines

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**
*Pro:* Well-motivated problem, novel approach, consistent performance improvements, well-written, provides useful domain insights.

*Con:* Key technical aspects need clarification, limited justification for design choices, insufficient comparison to relevant baselines.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to domain discovery that addresses a significant gap in domain adaptation research. While the technical formulation has some ambiguities and the motivation for certain design choices needs strengthening, the core idea is sound and the results are promising. The authors' rebuttal addressed most concerns, and the paper now appears to be a solid contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the remaining technical clarifications.

**Specific Recommendations:**
1. Clarify the meaning of "maximally different in distribution" (distribution over features, labels, or both?)
2. Provide ablation studies showing the impact of the label prior constraint
3. Resolve the apparent discrepancy between constraints stated on lines 162-165 and 182
4. Include comparisons to K-means and random partition baselines
5. Explain why the method outperforms baselines, providing insights into systematic failures it overcomes
6. Provide more details about the geodesic flow kernel implementation (reimplementation or public code)
