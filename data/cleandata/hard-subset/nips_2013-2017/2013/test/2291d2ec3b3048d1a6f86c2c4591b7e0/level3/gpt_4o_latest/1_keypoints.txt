- Addresses latent domain discovery for improved classification in domain adaptation by enforcing maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability of domains.  
- Proposes a kernel-based distribution difference measure, partitions data to maximize it, and formulates the task as integer programming relaxed to continuous optimization.  
- Determines the number of domains using a novel domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure.  
- Evaluated on object and human activity recognition tasks, showing significant gains over prior method [19].  
- Strength: Novel, well-motivated approach filling a gap in domain adaptation research.  
- Strength: Nonparametric distinctiveness measure and DWCV are innovative contributions.  
- Strength: Clear writing, organized methodology, detailed experiments, and useful qualitative analysis of discovered domains.  
- Weakness: Limited evidence/explanation for why per-category domain reshaping is suboptimal and how the formulation enforces avoidance of it.  
- Weakness: Label prior constraint (LPC) is insufficiently justified; need ablation without LPC.  
- Weakness: Lacks comparisons to simple baselines (k-means, random partitioning) to justify complexity.  
- Weakness: Ambiguous terms like “maximally different in distribution” and unclear bounds in optimization constraints need clarification.  
- Weakness: No systematic analysis explaining why the method outperforms [19] or examples of baseline failures that are addressed.  
- Weakness: Insufficient implementation details (e.g., geodesic flow kernel usage) harm reproducibility.  
- Minor: Typo in Eq (1) (M'k should be Mk') and should cite “Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias.”  
- Verdict: Leans toward acceptance for originality, clarity, and empirical gains, but would be stronger if the noted weaknesses are addressed.
