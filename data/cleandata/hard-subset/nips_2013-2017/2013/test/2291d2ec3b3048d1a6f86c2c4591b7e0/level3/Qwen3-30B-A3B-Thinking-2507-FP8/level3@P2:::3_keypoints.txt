- Addresses latent domain discovery in visual datasets where datasets contain multiple domains due to factors like lighting, pose, background, resolution.
- Proposes automatic discovery by enforcing maximum distinctiveness (separating domains) and maximum learnability (domains support discriminative models).
- Reframes domain adaptation work by focusing on discovering meaningful latent domains rather than adapting between predefined datasets.
- Develops a nonparametric RKHS-based measure for domain distinctiveness and a convex optimization procedure to find domains.
- Introduces DWCV, a principled method to determine the number of domains based on learnability.
- Method is technically sound with clear theoretical foundations and efficient optimization.
- Claim of unsupervised learning is misleading because the method uses class labels via a label prior constraint (LPC).
- LPC may be limiting in practice since real-world latent domains can have different class distributions (e.g., pose correlated with class).
- Experiments show reasonable gains on object and action recognition but selection of dataset/source-target splits is not well justified.
- A leave-one-dataset-out validation would strengthen experimental claims.
- Terminology is inconsistent and confusing (dataset vs domain vs latent domain); clear definitions and examples are needed.
- Important notation is missing/undefined (e.g., max_k r(U_k, B) in Equation 6), hindering reproducibility.
- Section 4.2 is unclear about how identified domains improve adaptation and what the "MATCHING" strategy entails.
- Novelty: nonparametric formulation and convex label-control formulation advance latent domain discovery beyond prior Gaussian-mixture approaches.
- Combination of distinctiveness + learnability and DWCV represents a meaningful contribution.
- Significance: addresses a fundamental mismatch in how domains are defined and has practical implications for dataset construction and adaptation.
- Qualitative analysis (Figure 1) shows discovered domains align with interpretable factors like pose and appearance.
- Strengths: novel, theoretically grounded, nonparametric, principled domain-counting, and practically useful with insightful qualitative results.
- Weaknesses: misleading unsupervised claim, terminology confusion, unclear experiments, missing notation, and potential limitations from the LPC.
- Recommendation: accept with major revisions — clarify terminology, address LPC limitations, define all notation, and improve experimental methodology/explanations.
