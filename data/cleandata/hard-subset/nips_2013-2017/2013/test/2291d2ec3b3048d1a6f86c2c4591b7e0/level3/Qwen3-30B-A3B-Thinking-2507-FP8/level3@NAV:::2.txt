

This paper proposes an automatic method for partitioning image datasets into domains based on feature-space separability and per-domain discriminative learnability, addressing the under-explored problem of defining meaningful domains for domain adaptation rather than using datasets as proxy domains. The authors introduce two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing inter-domain distribution differences) and maximum learnability (ensuring strong discriminative models can be trained within each domain). They propose a nonparametric optimization approach to achieve these properties, with experimental validation on object and action recognition tasks showing improvements over standard dataset-as-domain approaches and a recent domain discovery method [19].

The paper makes a valuable contribution by tackling a fundamental yet overlooked aspect of domain adaptation. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating significant performance gains across multiple benchmarks. The writing is clear and well-organized, though the paper would benefit from more illustrative figures to visualize domain partitioning.

However, several methodological concerns require attention. The optimization procedure (Sec. 2) does not clearly demonstrate how it achieves the stated objectives (2)+(3), and the two driving criteria are not properly integrated—the learnability criterion is applied only as a model selection wrapper rather than as part of the partitioning objective. An integrated optimization jointly maximizing both criteria would be preferable. Most critically, the image representation is outdated (single BoW of sparse SURF with 800 codewords), which is too weak for current standards. The paper fails to specify the similarity measure used for histogram comparison—Euclidean distance is inappropriate for histogram data, and the authors should use X² or intersection kernels as established in seminal works (Zhang IJCV 2006; Lazebnik CVPR 2006). The authors should upgrade to spatial pyramid of dense SURF or Fisher Vectors and cite relevant literature.

The use of test set data in the "reshaping" process is poorly explained and effectively assumes an imputation setting not stated in the problem formulation. While these are significant methodological weaknesses, the core idea is promising and the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. Despite these shortcomings, the paper's novel approach and strong empirical results warrant acceptance. The authors should address these issues in the camera-ready version, particularly upgrading the image representation and clarifying the domain discovery methodology.
