Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the often-adopted but problematic practice of equating datasets with domains in visual recognition. It proposes to “reshape” one or more labeled visual datasets into latent domains that are (i) maximally distinctive in distribution and (ii) maximally learnable. Distinctiveness is operationalized by maximizing pairwise kernel mean discrepancies (MMD) between inferred domain marginals Pk(x); learnability is enforced by choosing the number of domains K via a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) criterion that measures within-domain discriminative performance.

Technically, the method assigns instances to K latent domains by maximizing the total domain distinctiveness (sum of pairwise squared RKHS mean distances) subject to: (a) each instance is assigned to exactly one domain, and (b) a label prior constraint (LPC) that per-domain class proportions match the global label prior up to a small tolerance. Because the integer program is intractable, the authors relax to continuous variables β with linear constraints that implicitly control domain size (via bounds derived from Mk) and implement a maximization of a convex quadratic form over a polytope. The number of domains K is then selected to maximize A(K), a weighted sum of within-domain cross-validated accuracies.

For testing, the paper also proposes conditional reshaping of heterogeneous test sets into domains matched to the discovered training domains by minimizing distribution discrepancies, yielding a convex quadratic program.

Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and multi-view action recognition (IXMAS) demonstrate that: (1) the discovered latent domains outperform original dataset partitions and a Gaussian mixture–based latent domain discovery method [Hoffman et al., ECCV’12] in an oracle matching evaluation; (2) practical multi-source adaptation strategies (e.g., distribution matching) using the discovered domains approach the oracle; (3) conditional reshaping of the test set improves cross-view action recognition. The approach relates to and builds upon MMD-based two-sample testing [Gretton et al., NIPS’07], multi-source adaptation [Mansour et al., NIPS’08; Duan et al., ICML’12], and generalized clustering via kernel embeddings [Jegelka et al., 2009], while differing from [20] by avoiding parametric mixture assumptions and using label-informed constraints.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and problem formulation: decoupling datasets from domains is well-argued and important for robust domain adaptation.
- Nonparametric distinctiveness criterion grounded in characteristic kernels/MMD; does not assume parametric domain models.
- Practical mechanism for choosing K via DWCV that aligns with the learnability objective.
- Extension to conditional reshaping of test data is novel and practically relevant, with a convex formulation.
- Empirical indications that discovered domains are more useful for adaptation than dataset-level partitions and a parametric LDD baseline; qualitative analysis suggests discovered factors align with pose, background, and appearance.

Weaknesses
- Optimization details and guarantees are insufficient. The relaxed problem is a maximization of a convex quadratic over linear constraints (NP-hard); the paper relies on “existing solvers” without specifying algorithms, initialization, or convergence criteria. No discussion of local optima, scalability, or runtime.
- Strong reliance on the label prior constraint (LPC). Assuming class priors are domain-invariant is often unrealistic in vision; the method may fail or bias assignments when priors shift across domains. No ablation shows behavior without LPC or under prior shift.
- Sensitivity and hyperparameters. The method depends on kernel choice and bandwidth, δ in LPC, and the cross-validation protocol; no sensitivity analysis or guidance is provided.
- Evaluation clarity and completeness. The text references Tables/Figures with numerical results but provides few details in the main body: exact numbers, variances, classifier type for DWCV, N in N-fold CV, and how “MATCHING” is implemented are unclear. Oracle evaluations are informative but should be paired with more comprehensive practical evaluations and ablations (e.g., K-means with balancing, random partitions with LPC, removing LPC).
- Potential degeneracy and balancing. The size constraints 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C are heuristic after relaxation; there is no principled regularization to avoid very small or highly overlapping domains beyond LPC and bounds. The objective could favor splitting by nuisance factors or subtle per-class cues; analysis is limited.
- Limited scope of baselines. More recent or diverse domain adaptation baselines (beyond GF kernel) are not compared; improvements may conflate benefits from the chosen adaptation algorithm.

Quality
- Conceptual framework is sound and leverages established MMD theory. However, the core optimization lacks algorithmic specificity and theoretical analysis. Claims are supported by experiments, but the absence of detailed quantitative results, ablations, and sensitivity analyses makes it difficult to assess robustness and reproducibility.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written with clear intuition. Some formal details need clarification: exact solver and settings; derivation and rationale of constraints in Eq. (5) post-relaxation; precise definition of ENSEMBLE and MATCHING strategies; DWCV protocol (classifier, folds). Include pseudocode for both training and conditional test reshaping.

Originality
- The combination of nonparametric distinctiveness (sum of pairwise MMDs), label-prior regularization, data-driven selection of K via DWCV, and conditional test reshaping appears novel relative to [20] and kernel-embedding clustering work. The work is distinct from multi-source adaptation that assumes known domains.

Significance
- If robust, the method can impact domain adaptation practice by providing a principled way to uncover domain structure in existing datasets and to conditionally reshape test data. The idea is likely to be used or extended. Current evidence is promising but would be strengthened by broader, clearer empirical validation and robustness studies.

Questions for the authors
- What solver is used for the maximization QP, how is it initialized, and what are runtime and scalability with M and K?
- How sensitive are results to kernel choice/bandwidth and δ in LPC? Please provide sensitivity plots.
- How does the method perform when class priors differ across latent domains (violating LPC)? Include an ablation without LPC.
- Specify the classifier and N in DWCV and report variability across folds.
- Precisely define the MATCHING procedure for selecting a source domain without target labels.
- Provide ablations comparing to balanced K-means or spectral clustering with/without LPC.
- Report results on larger datasets and release code for reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important gap (dataset vs. domain), with a clear, general framework.
- Nonparametric formulation and conditional test reshaping are interesting and potentially impactful.
- Empirical results suggest consistent gains over reasonable baselines.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient algorithmic and experimental detail to assess soundness, robustness, and reproducibility.
- Strong LPC assumption may limit applicability; no analysis under prior shift.
- Optimization lacks guarantees and is under-specified; potential for degenerate solutions not analyzed.

Overall, the paper presents a compelling idea with promising initial results but would benefit from stronger methodological and empirical substantiation.
