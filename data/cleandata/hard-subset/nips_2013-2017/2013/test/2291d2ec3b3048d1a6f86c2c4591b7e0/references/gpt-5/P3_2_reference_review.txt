Summary
This paper tackles the important but underexplored problem of discovering latent domains inside visual datasets for the purpose of domain adaptation. The core idea is to “reshape” one or more labeled datasets into K latent domains that (i) are maximally distinctive in marginal feature distribution and (ii) are maximally learnable in the sense that each domain supports training a strong classifier. Distinctiveness is formalized via a nonparametric kernel two-sample discrepancy (essentially MMD in an RKHS) summed over all domain pairs. The combinatorial assignment is relaxed to a continuous quadratic program over βmk=zmk/Mk with label-prior constraints that enforce similar class proportions across domains. Since distinctiveness monotonically increases with K, the number of domains is chosen by domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV), selecting K to maximize held-out accuracy inside domains. The authors also propose “conditional reshaping” of test sets to best match identified training domains by minimizing distribution discrepancies (QP). Experiments on Caltech-256/Amazon/DSLR/Webcam and IXMAS action recognition suggest that discovered domains improve adaptation over treating entire datasets as domains and outperform latent-domain discovery with parametric mixture models [Hoffman et al., ECCV’12], as well as simple K-means and random partitions.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on MMD/two-sample kernel tests for distribution discrepancy.
- Closely related to Hoffman et al. [20], who posit parametric mixture models for latent domains; this paper avoids parametric assumptions via nonparametric kernel embeddings and adds a data-driven criterion for K via DWCV.
- Related in spirit to “discriminability”-driven clustering (Jegelka et al. [27]) but leverages label information differently through label-prior constraints and the learnability criterion.
- Connects to multi-source adaptation by using the discovered domains as sources and to geodesic flow kernel adaptation [4] for the adaptation step.

Strengths
- Problem is timely and practically important: datasets are often heterogeneous, and equating dataset with domain is limiting.
- Methodological novelty: pairing a nonparametric distinctiveness objective with a learnability-based model selection criterion for K is intuitive and useful.
- Sensible constraint design (LPC) to avoid trivial per-class clusters and encourage domain partitions independent of labels.
- Extension to conditional reshaping of test sets is pragmatic and potentially impactful for heterogeneous test-time environments.
- Empirical results (as described) show consistent gains over original dataset boundaries and over [20], and the MATCHING heuristic reportedly approaches oracle performance without test labels.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Objective and optimization:
  - The relaxed problem maximizes a convex quadratic over a polytope, which is non-convex and NP-hard. The paper acknowledges this but does not specify the solver, initialization, or strategies to avoid poor local optima. Without these, reproducibility and reliability are unclear.
  - The constraints are somewhat unintuitive in the relaxed form. The per-sample bounds 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C are used to enforce Mk ∈ [C, M], but this coupling is indirect in the relaxation and may admit inconsistent or degenerate solutions. Clarification and justification are needed.
  - No complexity discussion. The kernel matrix is O(M^2) and the variable size is O(MK); solving the QP may not scale beyond a few thousand points.

- Label prior assumption:
  - LPC assumes class priors are approximately constant across latent domains. In many realistic settings (e.g., web vs. office domains), priors can differ markedly; enforcing LPC could distort the domain structure or bias the discovered domains toward satisfying priors rather than true distributional differences.
  - The method uses labels in constraints and in DWCV to pick K; while that can be acceptable for supervised source reshaping, it would be good to analyze how sensitive the partitions are to violations of LPC or to label sparsity/imbalance.

- Model selection via DWCV:
  - Using the same data to (a) construct domains and (b) perform cross-validation within domains risks overfitting K. A nested cross-validation or a split for domain-discovery vs. evaluation would be more principled.
  - No theoretical justification (e.g., stability or generalization bounds) for DWCV as a proxy for target-domain performance. Empirically, A(K) is shown to correlate with downstream accuracy, but quantitative evidence (curves, sensitivity) should be presented.

- Experimental reporting:
  - Several results are referenced (Tables 1–3, Figures 1–2) but numbers are not visible here; more importantly, the main text says K-means and random partitions are omitted for brevity—these should be in the paper or appendix to assess baselines fairly.
  - Sensitivity analyses are missing: kernel bandwidth, δ in LPC, choice of features, and the impact of K relative to the bound Kmax.
  - Comparisons are limited to [20] and simple baselines. Given the focus on distributional separation, comparisons to spectral clustering with balance constraints, information-theoretic clustering, or other MMD-based partitioning methods would be informative.
  - Runtime and scalability are not reported.

- Conceptual:
  - The distinctiveness objective can favor fragmenting data into small domains; the current mitigation relies on LPC and DWCV. A more integrated objective with explicit size regularization or a penalty for large K could reduce reliance on heuristic model selection.
  - Conditional reshaping of test sets is compelling but under-specified in the main text; without details, it is hard to judge solvability and robustness.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, with appropriate use of kernel two-sample statistics and a clear optimization target. However, key implementation details (solver, initialization, complexity, sensitivity) are under-specified, and some modeling assumptions (LPC) are strong. Empirical evidence appears positive but needs fuller reporting and stronger baselines.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The optimization section would benefit from clearer derivations of constraints in the relaxed form, explicit solver and algorithmic steps, and a brief complexity analysis. The conditional reshaping should have at least a concise formulation in the main paper.

Originality
The combination of nonparametric distinctiveness, label-prior constraints to avoid per-class clustering, and learnability-driven selection of K is novel and interesting relative to [20]. The idea of conditional test reshaping is also a nice contribution.

Significance
If robust and scalable, this approach could alter common practice in domain adaptation by discouraging the “dataset=domain” assumption and providing a tool to mine domains automatically. The empirical gains, especially the ability to approach oracle selection without target labels, suggest practical utility.

Questions for the authors
- What solver/initialization do you use for the concave QP? How sensitive are results to initialization? What are typical runtimes and memory footprints for your largest experiments?
- How sensitive are partitions to δ in LPC and to violations of the equal-prior assumption? Can you show results when priors truly differ across domains?
- Can you provide ablations on kernel bandwidth and feature choices?
- How is MATCHING performed without target labels (e.g., using MMD or proxy A-distance)? Please specify.
- For DWCV, do you use a nested split to avoid optimistic selection of K? If not, how large is the optimism gap?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a meaningful and overlooked problem; method is conceptually sound and broadly applicable.
- Nonparametric formulation avoids strong parametric assumptions; learnability criterion is practical.
- Reported gains over existing practices and [20], including conditional test reshaping.

Cons for acceptance
- Optimization details and scalability are underspecified; reproducibility is at risk.
- Strong LPC assumption; lack of sensitivity analysis.
- Model selection procedure may overfit; limited theoretical support.
- Experimental baselines and reporting could be stronger.

Overall
Borderline accept if the authors provide clearer optimization details, stronger/supplementary baselines, and sensitivity analyses (LPC, bandwidth, K), and include the conditional reshaping formulation. The idea is promising and potentially impactful, but the paper would benefit from tightening the technical and experimental presentation.
