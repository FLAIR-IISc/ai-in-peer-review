- Novelty: Proposes reshaping visual datasets into meaningful domains via maximizing distinctiveness and learnability, shifting focus from how to adapt to what domains are.
- Theoretical grounding: Uses a principled formulation with kernel-based domain discrepancy measures and objectives for distinctiveness and learnability.
- Practical method: Introduces DWCV to automatically determine the number of domains, addressing an important hyperparameter problem.
- Empirical validation: Comprehensive experiments on object recognition and human activity recognition show consistent performance gains.
- Clear presentation: Paper is well-written with helpful illustrations and sufficient methodological detail.
- Comparison to prior work: Builds on domain adaptation and dataset-bias literature, contrasting favorably with parametric approaches like GMMs.
- Computational concern: Optimization may be computationally expensive and scalability to very large datasets is unclear.
- Kernel bandwidth choice: Using twice the median distance is arbitrary; sensitivity to bandwidth is not analyzed.
- LPC parameter (δ): Fixing δ=1% lacks justification and sensitivity analysis of this parameter is missing.
- Limited baselines: Lacks direct comparisons of state-of-the-art domain adaptation algorithms with and without domain reshaping.
- TDD clarity: The TDD objective needs a more intuitive explanation of why maximizing inter-domain differences helps.
- LPC role: Clarify why relaxing the Label Prior Constraint helps and whether it prevents trivial solutions.
- DWCV bound Kmax: The bound Kmax = min{M/(NC), C} appears arbitrary and needs justification.
- Feature choices: Rationale for using SURF and an 800-bin vocabulary is unclear; other representations could change results.
- Matching risk: The effective 'MATCHING' strategy might risk overfitting to the test set.
- Acceptance recommendation: Accept with minor revisions contingent on addressing computational scalability, parameter justifications/sensitivity, and adding direct comparisons to SOTA methods.
