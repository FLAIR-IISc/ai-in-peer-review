This paper addresses the important problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition, arguing that equating datasets with domains is a flawed practice. The authors propose a novel approach to automatically *reshape* datasets into more characteristic domains, guided by the principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. This is a challenging unsupervised learning problem, and the paper offers a principled, nonparametric solution.

The core idea is well-motivated and the paper presents a technically sound approach. The use of kernel methods to quantify domain discrepancies is reasonable, and the formulation of the optimization problem is clear. The inclusion of the label prior constraint (LPC) is a clever addition, acknowledging the expected class distributions within domains. The experimental validation on both object recognition and human activity recognition datasets is comprehensive and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method, consistently outperforming baseline approaches and, importantly, showing improvements even when combined with multi-source adaptation techniques. The analysis of the identified domains, linking them to factors like pose and illumination, adds further insight.  The DWCV approach for determining the optimal number of domains appears effective and practical.  The inclusion of related work, while extensive, appropriately positions the contribution within the field.

However, several weaknesses detract from the overall impact. Firstly, the notation is occasionally dense and could be simplified for improved readability. For example, introducing more descriptive variable names beyond single letters (e.g., `zmk` as `domain_assignment`) would be helpful.  Secondly, the connection between the theoretical motivations (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) and the practical implementation could be further strengthened. While the intuition is clear, a more formal discussion of how the chosen kernel and optimization process directly translate to these properties would be valuable. Thirdly, the ablation studies are limited. While comparisons to [20] are presented, a more detailed analysis of the impact of the LPC and the choice of kernel bandwidth would strengthen the empirical evidence. It would also be helpful to explore the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the regularization parameter (δ) in the LPC.  Fourthly, the figures, particularly Figure 1, are somewhat small and difficult to interpret.  Larger, higher-resolution images showcasing the identified domains would be beneficial.  Finally, the conditional reshaping of the test datasets (section 4.3) is compelling, but the explanation of the optimization process is relegated to the supplementary material. A more concise description within the main paper would improve accessibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of automatically reshaping datasets into latent domains is a significant departure from traditional domain adaptation approaches.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is well-founded in kernel methods and optimization theory.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  Extensive experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines on multiple datasets.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental issue in domain adaptation – the ill-defined notion of a "domain" in visual recognition – and offers a practical solution.
*   **Clarity (Generally):** The paper is reasonably well-written, with a clear overall structure.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Readability:** Dense notation and occasionally convoluted explanations hinder comprehension.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Limited analysis of the individual components of the proposed method.
*   **Figure Quality:**  Figures are small and lack detail.
*   **Test Set Reshaping Detail:**  Insufficient explanation of the test set reshaping optimization within the main text.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and technically sound contribution to the field of domain adaptation. While there are areas for improvement in terms of clarity and experimental analysis, the core ideas and empirical results are compelling. I believe that with revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, this paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS.  I lean towards acceptance with minor revisions.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions.  Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Simplify notation and improve readability.
2.  Provide more detailed ablation studies, focusing on the impact of the LPC and kernel bandwidth.
3.  Include larger, higher-resolution figures illustrating the identified domains.
4.  Expand the description of the test set reshaping optimization within the main paper.
5.  Clarify the connection between the theoretical motivations and practical implementation with more formal discussion.
