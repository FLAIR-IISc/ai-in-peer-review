- Paper tackles discovering latent visual domains for domain adaptation by reshaping labeled datasets into K domains that are distinctive (MMD) and learnable.
- Distinctiveness is measured via a kernel two-sample discrepancy (MMD) summed over domain pairs; assignment is relaxed to a continuous quadratic program over βmk with label-prior constraints (LPC).
- K is chosen by domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) maximizing held-out within-domain accuracy; authors also propose conditional reshaping of test sets via a QP to match training domains.
- Empirical results on object and action datasets show discovered domains improve adaptation versus treating datasets as domains, outperforming Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12), K-means, and random partitions.
- Builds on MMD/two-sample tests and contrasts with parametric mixture models (Hoffman et al.); relates to discriminability-driven clustering and multi-source adaptation.
- Strength: addresses a timely practical problem—dataset≠domain—and offers a nonparametric distinctiveness objective paired with a learnability-based K selection.
- Strength: label-prior constraints help avoid trivial per-class clusters and encourage label-independent partitions.
- Strength: conditional test reshaping is a pragmatic extension potentially useful for heterogeneous test-time scenarios.
- Strength: reported empirical gains and a MATCHING heuristic that approaches oracle performance without test labels.
- Weakness: relaxed objective is a concave QP over a polytope (NP-hard); solver, initialization, and strategies to avoid poor local optima are unspecified, hurting reproducibility.
- Weakness: LPC in relaxed form is unintuitive and may admit degenerate or inconsistent solutions; justification and clarification needed.
- Weakness: scalability concerns—kernel matrix is O(M^2), variables O(MK), no complexity analysis or runtime/memory reporting.
- Weakness: LPC assumes class priors constant across domains, which is unrealistic in many settings and may bias discovered domains; sensitivity to prior violations and label imbalance is not analyzed.
- Weakness: DWCV may overfit K by using the same data to discover domains and evaluate them; no nested-split or theoretical justification provided.
- Weakness: experimental reporting lacks important baselines (K-means/random shown only in text), sensitivity analyses (kernel bandwidth, δ, features, Kmax), and runtime/scalability results.
- Weakness: distinctiveness objective can favor fragmentation into small domains; reliance on LPC and DWCV is heuristic—no integrated size regularization or explicit penalty for large K.
- Weakness: conditional reshaping is under-specified in the main text, making solvability and robustness unclear.
- Quality: method is conceptually sound but key implementation and sensitivity details are under-specified; empirical evidence is promising but needs fuller reporting.
- Clarity: overall paper is well written but needs clearer derivations for relaxed constraints, explicit solver/algorithm steps, and a brief complexity analysis.
- Originality: combination of nonparametric distinctiveness, LPC, and learnability-driven K selection is novel; conditional test reshaping is a useful contribution.
- Significance: if made robust and scalable, the approach could shift practice by enabling automatic domain discovery; reported gains indicate practical utility.
- Questions for authors: which solver/initialization is used and how sensitive are results; runtimes/memory for largest experiments?
- Questions for authors: sensitivity of partitions to δ and to unequal priors; results when priors differ across domains?
- Questions for authors: ablations on kernel bandwidth and feature choices; how MATCHING is performed without target labels?
- Questions for authors: whether DWCV uses nested splits to avoid optimistic K selection and size of any optimism gap.
- Recommendation: borderline accept conditional on authors providing clearer optimization details, stronger/supplementary baselines, sensitivity analyses (LPC, bandwidth, K), and conditional reshaping formulation.
