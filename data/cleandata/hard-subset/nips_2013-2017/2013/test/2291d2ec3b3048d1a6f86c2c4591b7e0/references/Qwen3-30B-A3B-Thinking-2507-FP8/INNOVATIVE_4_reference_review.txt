

# Review: Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation

This paper presents a fundamentally novel perspective on domain adaptation by questioning the very nature of what constitutes a "domain" in visual recognition. The authors make a compelling argument that visual datasets are often aggregates of multiple latent domains, and the common practice of equating datasets with domains leads to suboptimal adaptation. This is a significant conceptual shift from the typical domain adaptation literature, which largely assumes domains are given rather than discovered.

The authors propose a novel approach to "reshape" datasets into latent domains with two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing inter-domain distribution differences) and maximum learnability (ensuring domains contain sufficient data for strong classifiers). Their method uses nonparametric kernel-based measures to quantify domain differences, avoiding the limitations of parametric clustering approaches. The domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure to determine the optimal number of domains is particularly elegant and well-motivated.

The paper makes a strong case for the importance of this work by providing clear examples where treating a dataset as a single domain leads to poor adaptation performance, while reshaping into latent domains improves results. The authors validate their approach on object recognition (using Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, and Webcam datasets) and action recognition (IXMAS multi-view dataset), demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines that treat datasets as single domains or use other clustering approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **High novelty:** This paper introduces a completely new direction in domain adaptation research by addressing the fundamental question of what constitutes a domain rather than just improving adaptation algorithms. The concept of reshaping datasets into latent domains is genuinely novel and represents a significant shift in perspective.

2. **Strong motivation:** The paper clearly articulates why current practices are problematic with concrete examples, making a compelling case for why this research is necessary.

3. **Clear conceptual contributions:** The two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) provide a principled framework for domain discovery that is well-motivated and theoretically sound.

4. **Empirical validation:** The results show consistent improvements over baseline approaches across multiple datasets and tasks, demonstrating practical significance.

5. **Well-structured writing:** The paper is clearly organized and well-written, making the complex ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The computational complexity of the proposed method isn't thoroughly discussed, though the paper mentions they use existing solvers. For large-scale datasets, this could be a practical limitation.

2. While they acknowledge that [20] is the only prior work on latent domain discovery, a more detailed comparison of their approach with [20] would strengthen the paper, especially regarding the nonparametric vs. parametric modeling aspects.

3. The theoretical guarantees for their approach (beyond the empirical results) could be more thoroughly discussed.

**Comparison to prior work:** The paper correctly identifies that [20] is the only prior work on latent domain discovery, and their approach is clearly differentiated by its nonparametric nature and the DWCV procedure. The paper also correctly distinguishes their work from multi-domain adaptation methods, which assume domains are given rather than discovered.

**Overall assessment:** This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution to domain adaptation research by reframing the problem to first identify latent domains within datasets rather than assuming domains are given. The core idea is bold, novel, and addresses a fundamental question that has been overlooked in the literature. The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over baselines, validating the approach.

While the paper has some minor methodological gaps (particularly regarding computational complexity and theoretical guarantees), these are outweighed by the paper's conceptual novelty and clear empirical validation. The authors have identified a genuine gap in the domain adaptation literature and proposed a promising new direction.

Given my review criteria that prioritize novel concepts over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution that merits acceptance at NeurIPS. The paper doesn't just improve existing methods—it fundamentally reframes how we think about domain adaptation, which is exactly the kind of bold, novel approach NeurIPS values.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper introduces a genuinely novel concept that shifts the focus from adapting between domains to discovering the latent domains themselves. This is a significant contribution that will likely influence future work in domain adaptation. The empirical results support the approach, and the conceptual framework is well-motivated and clearly articulated.
