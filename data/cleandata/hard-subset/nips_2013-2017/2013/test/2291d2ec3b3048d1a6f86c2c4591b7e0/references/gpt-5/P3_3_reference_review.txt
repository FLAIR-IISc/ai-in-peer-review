Summary
The paper tackles an important and under-explored problem: datasets used in visual recognition are often agglomerations of heterogeneous “latent domains,” and equating datasets with domains can harm adaptation. The authors propose to automatically reshape datasets into latent domains by enforcing two axioms: (1) maximum distinctiveness of domains, operationalized as maximizing pairwise discrepancies between kernel mean embeddings (an MMD-like measure); and (2) maximum learnability, operationalized via a domain-wise cross-validation score to select the number of domains K. They relax the combinatorial assignment to a continuous quadratic program with label-prior constraints to discourage trivial per-class clustering. They also propose conditional reshaping of heterogeneous test sets to match discovered training domains. Experiments on object (Amazon/DSLR/Webcam/Caltech-256) and IXMAS action datasets suggest performance gains versus treating each dataset/view as a single domain and versus a prior latent-domain approach [Hoffman et al., ECCV].

Relation to prior work
The approach is closest to Hoffman et al. (ECCV) who discover subdomains via a parametric hierarchical GMM; the present work uses nonparametric kernel mean embeddings to avoid strong parametric assumptions. It connects to MMD/two-sample tests (Gretton et al.) and generalized clustering via kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.). The authors position their method as complementary to multi-source adaptation (e.g., GFK, TCA, multi-source theory), using discovered domains as inputs to adaptation.

Strengths
- Clear and timely problem formulation: disentangling domains from datasets is practically important for domain adaptation and cross-dataset generalization.
- Nonparametric discrepancy criterion: using RKHS mean embeddings for domain distinctiveness is principled and flexible, avoiding fragile parametric mixture assumptions.
- Practical mechanism to choose K: the “maximum learnability” criterion via domain-wise cross-validation is simple and operational.
- Conditional test reshaping is a useful addition, especially for multi-view action recognition where test sets are inherently heterogeneous.
- Empirical evidence across two tasks suggests discovered domains can outperform dataset-as-domain baselines and a previous latent-domain method.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical soundness and optimization
- The core optimization maximizes a convex quadratic form over a polytope (sum over pairwise MMDs), which is NP-hard; the paper acknowledges this but provides no algorithmic details (solver type, initialization, complexity, stopping criteria) or guarantees (even local optimality). Since the objective is highly non-convex after the relaxation, the practical behavior and reproducibility are unclear.
- The relaxation introduces βmk = zmk/Mk and constraints 1/M ≤ Σk βmk ≤ 1/C per instance to indirectly bound domain sizes. This is unusual and hard to interpret; it is not clear these constraints faithfully reflect the intended combinatorial problem or avoid degenerate solutions. Please justify and analyze these bounds.
- The discretization rule “assign to argmax βmk” can break the relaxed label-prior constraints post hoc; do you project or re-enforce LPC after discretization?

Use of labels and LPC
- The approach is described as unsupervised domain discovery, yet (i) the LPC enforces per-domain label priors to match the global label prior, and (ii) DWCV uses supervised accuracy. This is effectively supervised or at least label-dependent domain discovery. Please clarify the supervision assumptions and scope (e.g., what fraction of labels is needed? can it work with partial/weak labels?).
- LPC is a strong assumption: per-class proportions are the same across domains up to δ=1%. In many realistic settings (e.g., web vs. office domains), class skew varies substantially. The method may mis-slice or become infeasible when priors truly differ. Sensitivity to δ is not studied.

Objective and risk of class-driven partitions
- Although LPC aims to prevent per-class clustering, in practice strong class-conditional shifts across latent factors can still bias MMD to separate by label. Alternatives such as matching class-conditional means (conditional MMD) or using adversarial class-invariant criteria might better guard against this failure mode.

Model selection via DWCV
- The “maximum learnability” score measures within-domain cross-validated accuracy using the same samples used to construct the domains. This can favor over-segmentation that reduces intra-domain variability but may not improve transfer to unseen target domains. Empirical correlation between DWCV and target performance is shown qualitatively but lacks rigorous support or ablation (e.g., plot A(K) vs. target accuracy under different adaptation methods, random seeds, or noise).
- The bound Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C} is heuristic; more principled criteria (e.g., stability, information criteria) or held-out target validation (if available) would strengthen K selection.

Experimental evaluation
- Tables/figures central to evaluating gains (Tables 1–3) are referenced but not included here; it is hard to judge effect sizes, variance, and robustness. Please include exact numbers, per-target accuracies, and confidence intervals.
- Limited baselines: you compare to [20] and mention K-means/random splits (omitted). Stronger baselines include class-balanced kernel k-means in RKHS, spectral relaxations, or recent subdomain discovery approaches; and testing with multiple adaptation algorithms (not only GFK) to rule out adaptation-specific effects.
- Sensitivity/ablation: no study of kernel choice/bandwidth, δ in LPC, initialization, or runtime/scalability (the M×M kernel scales quadratically; how large can M be?). Reproducibility would benefit from releasing solver settings and code.

Clarity and presentation
- Some key derivations are terse. The TDD sum over all pairs can be simplified to an expression involving Σk ||μk||^2 and the global mean; exposing this may lead to more efficient optimization and better intuition.
- Conditional test reshaping is deferred to the supplement; without details it is difficult to assess novelty and complexity.

Originality and significance
- The framing—discovering latent domains via nonparametric discrepancy and using learnability to choose K—is novel and pertinent. If robust and scalable, this could influence how datasets are curated and how multi-source adaptation is done. However, the heavy reliance on labels and the lack of optimization guarantees or thorough evaluation temper the current significance.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide algorithmic details: initialization, solver, complexity, and convergence behavior. How do solutions vary across runs?
2. How sensitive are results to kernel bandwidth and δ? Any auto-tuning (e.g., multi-kernel)?
3. Can you relax LPC to a softer regularizer (e.g., KL divergence to global priors) and compare?
4. Does DWCV correlate with target-domain accuracy across seeds and adaptation methods? Any counterexamples?
5. How large can M be in practice? Any Nyström/mini-batch approximations to scale kernel computations?
6. After discretization, do you re-check and enforce constraints (e.g., minimum samples per class per domain)?

Pros
- Important problem; clear motivation
- Nonparametric, generally applicable discrepancy
- Practical K-selection idea; conditional test reshaping
- Promising empirical improvements

Cons
- Optimization is NP-hard with no clear algorithmic guarantees; unusual constraints
- Supervision dependence via LPC and DWCV; strong prior-equality assumption
- Incomplete evaluation and missing ablations; scalability unclear
- Some details deferred to supplement hamper reproducibility

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is compelling and potentially impactful, but the paper needs stronger optimization details/guarantees, clearer supervision assumptions, sensitivity analyses, and more comprehensive empirical evaluation to meet the bar. I encourage the authors to address these concerns; with these additions, this could become a valuable contribution.
