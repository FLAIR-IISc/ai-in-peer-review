Summary
The paper tackles an important but often overlooked question in domain adaptation: datasets are typically used as proxies for domains, yet a single dataset may conflate multiple latent domains driven by nuisance factors (pose, illumination, background, etc.). The authors propose to automatically “reshape” annotated visual datasets into latent domains that are (a) maximally distinctive and (b) maximally learnable.

Maximal distinctiveness is operationalized by maximizing the sum of pairwise discrepancies between domain distributions using a kernel two-sample distance (mean embeddings in an RKHS, equivalent to MMD). The assignment of instances to K domains is constrained by (i) exclusivity (each instance in exactly one domain) and (ii) a label prior constraint (LPC): class proportions within each domain match the global class prior (with small tolerance). Because the resulting integer program is NP-hard, the authors propose a continuous relaxation based on variables βmk=zmk/Mk, yielding maximization of a convex quadratic form subject to linear constraints; assignment is recovered by argmax over β for each instance.

Maximal learnability is used to select K by a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV): for each candidate K (starting at 2), fit classifiers within each discovered domain and compute a weighted average of their cross-validated accuracies; pick the K with highest A(K). The paper also proposes “conditional reshaping” of heterogeneous test sets by partitioning them to best match the discovered training domains (again via kernel distribution matching).

Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and multi-view action recognition (IXMAS) show that classifiers adapted from the discovered domains outperform those adapted from the original datasets and those from Hoffman et al.’s latent domain model (Gaussian mixtures). Conditional reshaping of test data further improves cross-view recognition.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on nonparametric domain discrepancy via kernel mean embeddings/MMD (e.g., Gretton et al.), and relates to latent domain discovery via parametric mixture models (Hoffman et al., ECCV). It is also conceptually related to generalized clustering with kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.) and multi-source adaptation methods. The main novelty is a nonparametric, label-regularized latent domain discovery objective coupled with a practical K-selection heuristic and an application to conditional reshaping of test data.

Strengths
- Problem importance: Recognizes and addresses a practical failure mode in domain adaptation when datasets mix multiple domains.
- Conceptual clarity: The “maximum distinctiveness + maximum learnability” principles are intuitive and well-motivated.
- Nonparametric formulation: Avoids restrictive mixture-model assumptions and leverages characteristic kernels; the MMD-based objective has sound statistical footing.
- Practical K selection: DWCV is simple and sensible given labeled sources.
- Broad evaluation: Applies to both images and videos; uses standard features and a well-known adaptation method (GFK); qualitative analyses provide insight about factors captured by domains.
- Conditional test reshaping: The idea to reshape test data to match training domains is novel and practically useful in multi-view scenarios.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality/technical soundness
- Optimization and scalability: The relaxed problem maximizes a convex quadratic under linear constraints—still NP-hard. The paper provides no algorithmic details (solver type, initialization, convergence, runtimes, memory). The method requires the full M×M kernel matrix, which limits scalability. Please report complexity, runtime, and memory usage on your datasets; consider approximations (Nyström/RFF) and discuss initialization sensitivity.
- Constraints and potential degeneracies: LPC enforces class proportions per domain, which can be unrealistic (label shift across domains is common). It also requires labels for all training data. How robust is the method when LPC is relaxed or violated? The objective contains only between-domain mean separation and weak size bounds (via 1/M ≤ sum_k βmk ≤ 1/C), with no explicit within-domain compactness or entropy/size regularization. How do you prevent pathological assignments (e.g., splitting to maximize MMD without forming learnable, cohesive domains)? Did you observe instability across runs?
- Coupling distinctiveness and learnability: Learnability is only used post hoc to choose K rather than influencing the assignments. A joint objective (e.g., augmenting TDD with a surrogate for within-domain discriminability or classifier margin) could be stronger. Any empirical correlation between TDD and within-domain CV accuracy?
- Conditional test reshaping: Details are deferred to the supplement. Please include the explicit optimization, constraints, and how you ensure no label leakage. How do you choose the number of test domains? Is the procedure stable?

Clarity
- Some notational inconsistencies (e.g., d(k,k′) formula and M′k). The derivation of constraints (Eq. 5) from βmk=zmk/Mk is terse and non-intuitive; please expand and motivate these bounds, and clarify how they control domain sizes.
- The manuscript references tables/figures (Tables 1–3, Figs. 1–2), but they are not included here; as written, it is impossible to verify numerical claims.

Originality
- The use of kernel mean embeddings for distributional clustering and the focus on latent domains in visual datasets are timely. Compared to Hoffman et al., the nonparametric, label-regularized approach is a clear differentiation. It would be helpful to contrast more explicitly to generalized clustering with kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.) and to include stronger baselines, e.g., class-balanced K-means/spectral clustering, or clustering to maximize MMD with additional regularization.

Significance
- If the reported gains hold, the approach can materially improve multi-source adaptation pipelines and offers insight into dataset construction. Conditional test reshaping is especially appealing for multi-view recognition. However, the current presentation lacks numerical results, ablations (kernel bandwidth, δ in LPC, effect of removing LPC), variance over runs, and statistical significance, which weakens the case for broad impact.

Suggestions
- Provide full algorithmic details, pseudocode, and implementation choices (solver, initialization), with runtime/memory profiles. Release code if possible.
- Add size/entropy regularization or within-domain compactness terms; consider joint optimization with a discriminative surrogate. Analyze sensitivity to δ and kernel bandwidth.
- Evaluate robustness to label prior shift and partially labeled sources; report performance when LPC is relaxed or replaced by a soft penalty.
- Expand baselines and ablations; include standard deviations and significance tests.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a practically important and underexplored problem with a principled, nonparametric method.
- Clear conceptual contributions; simple, general framework.
- Promising qualitative/claimed quantitative improvements; novel conditional test reshaping.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient algorithmic detail and scalability discussion; potential NP-hardness glossed over.
- Strong LPC assumption and lack of within-domain regularization raise concerns about validity and robustness.
- Missing quantitative tables/figures here; limited ablations and comparisons.

Overall recommendation
Borderline. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper needs stronger empirical evidence (with numbers), clearer optimization details, and a more thorough analysis of assumptions and scalability to meet NeurIPS standards. If these issues are addressed, this could be a valuable contribution.
