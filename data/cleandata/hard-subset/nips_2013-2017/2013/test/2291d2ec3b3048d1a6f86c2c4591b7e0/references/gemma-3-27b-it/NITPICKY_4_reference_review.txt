This paper proposes a method for automatically reshaping visual datasets into domains for domain adaptation, motivated by the observation that standard datasets often conflate multiple underlying domains. The authors identify domains based on two key properties: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability, formalized through a kernel-based approach and a domain-wise cross-validation procedure. While the core idea is interesting and addresses a relevant problem, the paper suffers from numerous methodological and presentation shortcomings that, in my assessment, warrant rejection.

The primary concern is the lack of rigorous justification for several key design choices. The choice of a Gaussian kernel, while standard, is not justified beyond being “characteristic.” A more thorough discussion of alternative kernels and their potential impact would be beneficial. Furthermore, the relaxation of the integer programming problem, while necessary for tractability, introduces approximations whose effects are not adequately analyzed. The choice of δ=1% in the label prior constraint feels arbitrary and lacks sensitivity analysis. The paper claims the optimization is “more accessible with off-the-shelf optimization packages” but fails to specify *which* packages were used and their settings – crucial details for reproducibility.

The experimental evaluation, while extensive, is presented with insufficient detail. The precise parameter settings for the geodesic flow kernel are missing, hindering reproducibility. The choice of 800 visual words for the bag-of-words descriptors is not motivated. While the authors demonstrate improved performance over baselines, the reported improvements are often marginal, raising questions about the practical significance of the proposed method. The comparison to Hoffman et al. [20] is superficial; a more detailed and quantitative comparison, including running time and memory usage, is needed. The claim that the identified domains “coincide with the factors controlled by the dataset collectors” in Figure 1 is speculative and lacks quantitative support. The discussion of Figure 2 feels incomplete; the reasoning behind the observed optimal K values is not fully elaborated.

Clarity is another weakness. The notation is often dense and inconsistent. For example, the definition of zmk is used without clear explanation in the TDD equation. Several sentences are awkwardly phrased and require careful reading to understand. The paper lacks a clear, concise summary of the proposed algorithm, making it difficult to grasp the overall methodology quickly.

Originality is somewhat compromised. While the combination of distinctiveness and learnability is a novel idea, the individual components – kernel-based domain discrepancy measures and cross-validation – are well-established techniques. The contribution lies in the specific combination and application to this domain reshaping problem, but the novelty feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. 

The significance of the work is debatable. While addressing dataset bias is important, the observed performance improvements are not substantial enough to warrant a major shift in domain adaptation practices. The method's reliance on potentially expensive kernel computations and the lack of theoretical guarantees about its convergence and optimality limit its practical applicability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and often overlooked issue in domain adaptation: the conflation of domains within datasets.
*   Presents a novel approach combining distinctiveness and learnability for domain discovery.
*   Demonstrates improvements over baselines on several benchmark datasets.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Lack of rigorous justification for key design choices (kernel, relaxation parameters).
*   Insufficient experimental detail and reproducibility concerns.
*   Marginal performance improvements and limited practical significance.
*   Clarity issues in writing and notation.
*   Incremental novelty.
*   Missing details regarding optimization package selection and configurations.
*   Arbitrary selection of δ=1% in label prior constraint without justification.



In conclusion, while the paper tackles a relevant problem and introduces an interesting idea, the numerous methodological weaknesses, lack of clarity, and limited practical significance lead me to recommend rejection. A major revision addressing the aforementioned concerns – with a stronger emphasis on theoretical analysis, experimental rigor, and clearer presentation – would be required for reconsideration.
