Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the problem of latent domain discovery in visual datasets for domain adaptation. The core claim is that visual datasets often conflate multiple underlying domains (e.g., views, illumination, backgrounds), and treating each dataset as a single domain can hurt adaptation. The authors propose to reshape datasets into latent domains by enforcing two axioms: (1) maximum distinctiveness, operationalized by maximizing pairwise discrepancies between domain distributions via kernel mean embeddings/MMD in RKHS; and (2) maximum learnability, operationalized by choosing the number of domains K through domain-wise cross-validation accuracy (DWCV). The method includes constraints to prevent degenerate solutions, most notably a label prior constraint (LPC) that enforces each discovered domain to follow the global class prior approximately, discouraging trivial per-category clustering.

Optimization proceeds by relaxing a combinatorial assignment to a continuous quadratic program over β variables, with a pairwise sum of MMD-like distances as the objective and linear constraints that encode LPC and weak size controls. The authors also propose “conditional reshaping” of test data to align its latent domains to those discovered in training (convex QP). Experiments on object recognition (Amazon/DSLR/Webcam/Caltech-256) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) show that (i) discovered domains yield higher oracle transfer accuracy than original datasets and a Gaussian-mixture latent domain method [20], (ii) multi-source adaptation strategies using discovered domains (UNION/ENSEMBLE/MATCHING) can match oracle accuracies in some settings, and (iii) conditional reshaping of heterogeneous test sets further improves performance.

This work relates to: prior domain adaptation across fixed datasets [2–4], multi-source adaptation [28–29], latent domain discovery with parametric mixtures [20], kernel two-sample tests/MMD [21], and generalized clustering via kernel embeddings [27]. The paper positions itself as a nonparametric alternative to [20], and adds a procedure to determine K and to reshape test data.

Strengths
- Clear motivation that datasets may comprise multiple latent domains whose discovery can benefit adaptation; aligns with known dataset bias issues.
- Use of kernel mean embeddings to measure domain discrepancy is appropriate, nonparametric, and widely used; summing pairwise discrepancies encourages global separability.
- The LPC is a practical device to avoid degenerate clustering by category; DWCV to select K is simple and task-aligned.
- Conditional reshaping of test sets to match discovered training domains is a useful and underexplored practical scenario.
- Empirical results on two benchmarks indicate consistent improvements over using original datasets and over a parametric latent domain discovery baseline [20]; qualitative examples suggest interpretable discovered factors (pose, background, appearance).

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation

Quality (technical soundness)
- Optimization: The relaxed problem maximizes a convex quadratic (u^T K u with K PSD) under linear constraints. This is non-convex/NP-hard; while acknowledged, no algorithmic details (solver choice, initialization strategy, convergence behavior, multiple restarts) are provided. Practical success appears empirical; reproducibility and robustness are unclear.
- Variable/constraint design: The relaxation introduces βmk = zmk/Mk with domain-wise simplex constraints ∑m βmk = 1, and per-instance bounds 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C. This deviates from standard assignment constraints (e.g., ∑k assignment = 1) and is unusual for soft clustering. The justification given (Mk bounds) is plausible, but the relaxation no longer corresponds to a clear probabilistic assignment and may bias solutions; an ablation or justification would help.
- Assumptions: LPC assumes class priors are approximately invariant across domains. This can be violated in real visual data (e.g., some actions or objects occurring more often under certain conditions). The method’s sensitivity to such violations is not analyzed; δ is fixed at 1% without study.
- Scalability: The method requires an M×M kernel matrix, with O(M^2) memory and O(M^2)–O(M^3) computations. Practical limits and runtime are not reported; it may not scale to modern dataset sizes.
- Theoretical guarantees: While MMD is well-founded, there are no guarantees that maximizing summed pairwise MMD under LPC yields domains that improve target performance; the “maximum learnability” criterion guides K but not the assignment itself.

Clarity
- Overall ideas are well motivated and organized, and related work is appropriately cited. However, several technical details are underspecified: how the quadratic program is solved in practice; initialization; choice of N in DWCV; sensitivity to kernel/bandwidth and δ; the precise metric used for MATCHING without labels; and details of the conditional reshaping objective (relegated to supplement).
- Some notation is terse and may confuse readers (e.g., the per-instance constraint on ∑k βmk and its relation to original integer variables).

Originality
- The nonparametric, MMD-based objective for latent domain discovery with LPC appears novel relative to [20], which uses mixture models. Using DWCV to choose K and conditional reshaping of test sets are also novel in this context.
- Related generalized clustering work [27] is acknowledged; the use of label-informed constraints differentiates the setting.

Significance
- If robust, the approach could influence how datasets are preprocessed for domain adaptation, with potential benefits in multi-source adaptation and heterogeneous test-time scenarios.
- The empirical scope is limited to classical, relatively small benchmarks and BoW features. The lack of scaling studies or tests on modern deep features limits immediate impact.
- The method’s dependence on labeled source data and LPC narrows applicability.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a meaningful and understudied problem (latent domain discovery) with a principled, broadly applicable discrepancy measure.
- Demonstrates consistent empirical gains over treating datasets as domains and over a parametric baseline.
- Introduces a practical framework for choosing K and for reshaping test sets to match training domains.

Cons for acceptance
- Optimization and relaxation choices are under-justified; no algorithmic details or robustness analysis.
- Strong LPC assumption; no sensitivity analysis to class-prior shifts and δ.
- Scalability and computational costs not reported; reliance on full kernel matrix limits applicability.
- Limited empirical breadth; absent ablations (e.g., remove LPC), comparisons to alternative discrepancy measures, or modern baselines.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide algorithmic details: solver, initialization, runtime, memory, and scaling behavior; report stability across random seeds.
- Analyze sensitivity to LPC (vary δ, simulate class-prior shifts) and to kernel/bandwidth choices.
- Clarify the rationale and consequences of the constraint 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C; consider alternative relaxations with assignment constraints and entropy regularization.
- Detail the MATCHING criterion and the conditional reshaping objective/constraints in the main text.
- Include ablations (e.g., remove LPC; replace MMD with other divergences), and experiments with deep features to assess generality.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a well-motivated, nonparametric approach to latent domain discovery with promising empirical results and practical ideas (DWCV, conditional reshaping). However, technical details of the optimization, assumptions (LPC), and scalability are insufficiently substantiated. With stronger experimental analysis and clearer algorithmic exposition, the contribution would be more compelling.
