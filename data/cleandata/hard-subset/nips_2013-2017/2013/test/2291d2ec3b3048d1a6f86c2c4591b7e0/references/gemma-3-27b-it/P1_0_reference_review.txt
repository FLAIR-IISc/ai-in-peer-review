## Peer Review for “Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation”

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper tackles a crucial and often overlooked aspect of domain adaptation: the inherent structure *within* datasets used for training and testing. The authors argue convincingly that standard datasets are often agglomerations of multiple domains, and propose a novel approach to automatically reshape these datasets into more coherent, adaptable domains based on the principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and supported by compelling experimental results. While the writing could be slightly more concise, the paper represents a significant contribution to the field and I recommend **acceptance**.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The paper correctly identifies the common practice in visual domain adaptation of treating entire datasets as single domains, and points out the pitfalls of this approach when datasets are heterogeneous.  Instead, the authors propose a learning method to automatically partition datasets into more meaningful domains.  The core idea revolves around identifying domains that are both maximally different from each other (distinctiveness) and from which strong discriminative models can be learned (learnability).  This is achieved through a nonparametric approach optimizing kernel-based domain discrepancy measures and using cross-validation to determine the optimal number of domains.

The related work section is comprehensive, accurately situating the work within the broader context of domain adaptation, and acknowledging relevant techniques from machine learning, computer vision, and speech processing.  The authors appropriately position their work relative to Hoffman et al. (2013) and Jegelka et al. (2010), highlighting the advantages of their nonparametric approach and the inclusion of label information.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of automatically *discovering* latent domains within datasets is novel and addresses a fundamental limitation of existing domain adaptation techniques.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The concepts of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability provide a strong theoretical grounding for the proposed approach.  The use of kernel methods for measuring domain discrepancy is well-justified and appropriate.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both object recognition and human activity recognition are convincing. The paper demonstrates clear improvements over using the original datasets directly and also outperforms existing latent domain discovery methods.
* **Practical Utility:** The conditional reshaping of test datasets is a particularly valuable contribution, addressing a realistic scenario where the test distribution is also heterogeneous.
* **Clear Problem Definition:**  The paper clearly articulates the problem of dataset-as-domain and provides a compelling argument for why this is a problematic assumption.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The mathematical formulation, particularly around the optimization problem, can be dense and difficult to follow for readers not already familiar with kernel methods and convex optimization. While the authors do attempt to explain the relaxation process, it could be made more accessible.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The choice of kernel bandwidth and the δ parameter in the label prior constraint (LPC) are not discussed in great detail.  A more thorough analysis of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would strengthen the paper.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper briefly mentions the computational cost of solving the optimization problem but does not provide detailed empirical results on scalability. The scalability of the approach to very large datasets would be an important consideration for potential users.
* **Conciseness:** The writing style is somewhat verbose in places, and could benefit from more concise phrasing.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 2.1 – Distinctiveness:**  The choice of the kernel function *k* is mentioned but not justified. Is the Gaussian kernel always the best choice, or are there scenarios where other kernels might be more appropriate?
* **Section 2.2 – Learnability:** The connection between cross-validation accuracy and "learnability" feels somewhat intuitive but could be strengthened with a more formal justification. 
* **Section 4.3 – Test Dataset Reshaping:**  The statement that “the test datasets can also be made of multiple latent domains” feels more obvious in the action recognition setting (multiple viewpoints) than in the object recognition setting.  Could the authors provide more examples or justification for why this assumption is reasonable for object recognition datasets as well?
* **Figures 1 & 2:** These figures are very helpful in illustrating the discovered domains. It would be beneficial to include quantitative metrics (e.g., average domain purity) to further support the visual observations.
* **Discussion of LPC:** The role and impact of the LPC could be further explored.  What happens if the LPC is removed entirely? What is the sensitivity to different values of δ?




**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a fundamental, often-overlooked problem in domain adaptation.
* Novel and theoretically sound approach.
* Strong empirical results on multiple datasets.
* Demonstrates practical utility through test dataset reshaping.
* Significantly advances the state-of-the-art in latent domain discovery.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Mathematical formulation can be challenging to follow.
* Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity.
* Scalability concerns not fully addressed.
* Writing could be more concise.




**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite minor weaknesses, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its drawbacks.  The proposed approach is innovative, well-motivated, and empirically validated.  I believe this work represents a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation and I strongly recommend **acceptance** to NIPS. Addressing the comments regarding parameter sensitivity and clarity would further enhance the paper.
