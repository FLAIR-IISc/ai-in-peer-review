- Tackles the ill-defined notion of “domains” by proposing to reshape datasets into latent domains based on maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.  
- Proposes a non-parametric, continuous-relaxation method with cross-validation to select the number of domains.  
- Shows improved domain-adaptation performance on object recognition and human activity datasets, including reshaping test sets.  

Strengths:
- Identifies a fundamental problem: datasets are often heterogeneous collections of underlying domains.  
- Provides solid theoretical motivation via the axioms of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.  
- Uses a non-parametric formulation that avoids restrictive distributional assumptions.  
- Strong, consistent experimental validation across multiple datasets and tasks.  
- Paper is clearly written and well-organized.  

Weaknesses:
- Optimization appears computationally demanding; no detailed runtime or scalability analysis.  
- Performance likely sensitive to kernel choice/bandwidth; limited sensitivity analysis presented.  
- Limited comparative analysis with related methods (e.g., hierarchical Gaussian mixtures, spectral clustering).  
- Domain-Wise Cross-Validation (DWCV) may overfit the number of domains to validation data; alternative selection strategies not explored.  
- Lacks deeper qualitative analysis and visualization of what defines the discovered domains.  

Evaluation scores:
- Quality: 4/5 (technically sound).  
- Clarity: 5/5 (well-written).  
- Originality: 4.5/5 (novel concept).  
- Significance: 4/5 (useful and broadly applicable results).  

For acceptance:
- Novel, well-motivated approach addressing an important problem with strong empirical support and clear presentation.  

Against acceptance:
- Concerns about scalability, limited related-work comparison, and potential DWCV overfitting.  

Overall:
- Strong paper recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to address computational complexity, broader comparisons, and DWCV limitations.
