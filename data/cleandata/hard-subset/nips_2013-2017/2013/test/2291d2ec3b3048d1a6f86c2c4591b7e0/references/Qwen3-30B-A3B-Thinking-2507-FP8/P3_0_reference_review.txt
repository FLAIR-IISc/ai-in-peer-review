

# Peer Review: "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses a fundamental but overlooked issue in domain adaptation: the common practice of equating datasets with domains, when in reality a single dataset may contain multiple distinct domains. The authors identify that visual recognition datasets often contain multiple latent domains (e.g., different camera angles, lighting conditions) that are not naturally separated, and propose a method to automatically reshape datasets into characteristic domains based on two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains suitable for building strong classifiers).

The authors develop nonparametric methods to measure domain discrepancy using RKHS distances and optimize for maximum distinctiveness. They introduce domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to determine the optimal number of domains K by maximizing classification accuracy across domains. The approach is evaluated on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human action recognition (IXMAS) datasets, demonstrating significant improvements over using original datasets or domains discovered by prior work.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Problem Framing**: The paper correctly identifies a critical conceptual gap in domain adaptation research—datasets are not inherently domains—and provides a compelling argument for why this matters (e.g., the human action recognition example with different camera angles).

2. **Theoretical Contribution**: The two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and learnability) provide a principled foundation for domain discovery. The nonparametric approach avoids the limitations of parametric methods (like Gaussian mixtures in [20]), making it more applicable to real-world visual data.

3. **Practical Impact**: The DWCV method for determining K is both theoretically sound and practically effective, providing a clear way to select the optimal number of domains without requiring prior knowledge.

4. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experiments cover multiple datasets and tasks, with careful comparison to the original datasets and the prior work [20]. The conditional reshaping of test datasets (Section 4.3) is particularly valuable for real-world applications.

5. **Clear Motivation**: The paper effectively explains why simply clustering by appearance (as in [20]) is insufficient, and demonstrates how their approach avoids this pitfall.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Label Dependency**: DWCV requires labeled data to compute domain-specific classification accuracy. This is a significant limitation in unsupervised domain adaptation settings where labels may be unavailable for the target domain. The paper doesn't adequately address how to handle scenarios with limited or no labels.

2. **Computational Complexity**: The optimization problem is relaxed to a convex quadratic program, but the computational cost for large datasets (M=10,000+) is not discussed. For large-scale applications, this could be prohibitive.

3. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper only compares to [20] in related work, but doesn't engage with more recent domain adaptation methods (e.g., [36-38] from the references) that might be relevant to the reshaping approach.

4. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees**: While the method is well-motivated, the paper lacks theoretical analysis of convergence properties or bounds on the domain distinctiveness measure. This is particularly important given the NP-hard nature of the original problem.

5. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper mentions "existing solvers are adequate" but doesn't specify which ones or provide implementation details that would allow reproducibility.

6. **Questionable Assumptions**: The label prior constraint (LPC) assumes that class distributions are approximately consistent across domains, which may not hold in many real-world scenarios (e.g., when domains are created from different sources with different biases).

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to domain adaptation research by addressing a fundamental conceptual problem. The method is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear empirical improvements over existing approaches. The identification of the dataset-domain confusion as a critical issue in the field is particularly valuable.

The paper would benefit from addressing the label dependency of DWCV, providing more details on computational complexity, and comparing to more recent domain adaptation methods. However, these are refinements rather than fundamental flaws.

The work has clear significance for both theoretical and practical aspects of domain adaptation. By enabling better domain discovery, it has the potential to improve a wide range of domain adaptation systems. The approach could also inform future dataset construction practices by revealing the dominant factors in visual domains (e.g., object pose, background, illumination).

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It addresses a fundamental issue in domain adaptation that has been overlooked in the literature, presents a novel and well-justified approach, and demonstrates significant improvements through thorough experiments. While there are some limitations (particularly regarding label dependency), these do not undermine the core contribution and could be addressed in future work.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. It represents a significant step toward more principled approaches to domain adaptation by moving beyond the simplistic dataset-as-domain assumption.

**Overall Rating**: Strong Accept (8/10) - This paper makes a valuable contribution to domain adaptation that will likely influence future research in the area.
