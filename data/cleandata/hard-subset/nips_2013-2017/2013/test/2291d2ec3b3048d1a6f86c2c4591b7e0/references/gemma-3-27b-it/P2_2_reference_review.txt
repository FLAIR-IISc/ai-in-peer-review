## Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a crucial, often overlooked, aspect of domain adaptation: the inherent structure *within* datasets. The authors argue that standard datasets used for visual recognition aren't naturally composed of clearly defined domains, and directly equating datasets to domains can be detrimental to adaptation performance. They propose a novel unsupervised learning approach to automatically *reshape* datasets into latent domains based on two key principles: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.  The method leverages kernel-based distribution discrepancy measures and a domain-wise cross-validation procedure to determine both the domain assignments and the optimal number of domains.  The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, showing improved performance when adapting classifiers learned on the discovered domains.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper identifies a critical limitation in current domain adaptation research – the implicit assumption of well-defined domains within datasets.  Framing the problem as *discovering* these latent domains is a significant contribution.
* **Principled Approach:** The concepts of "maximum distinctiveness" and "maximum learnability" provide a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed method. These aren't arbitrary objectives but directly address the goals of effective domain adaptation.
* **Non-parametric Formulation:** Avoiding strong assumptions about the underlying data distribution through the use of non-parametric methods is a strength. This makes the approach more adaptable to complex real-world data.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach on multiple datasets and tasks, outperforming baseline methods and even achieving performance comparable to oracle domain knowledge in some cases.  The inclusion of reshaping *test* datasets is particularly insightful.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making the proposed method relatively easy to understand. The motivation is clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention optimization challenges, the computational cost of the proposed method (especially the continuous relaxation of the integer programming problem) isn’t fully addressed. Scalability to very large datasets is a concern.  A more detailed analysis of computational requirements would be beneficial.
* **Kernel Choice Sensitivity:** The performance of kernel-based methods is highly sensitive to kernel choice and bandwidth selection.  While the authors use a reasonable strategy for bandwidth selection, a more thorough sensitivity analysis regarding the choice of kernel would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Comparison:**  The comparison to related work (particularly Hoffman et al. [20]) is somewhat brief and appears more in the related work section rather than directly comparing performance metrics in the results. A more direct and quantitative comparison would be valuable.
* **DWCV Practicality:** The DWCV procedure for determining *K* could be computationally expensive, particularly with many data points. The practicality of this method for large-scale datasets needs to be discussed further.
* **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from more detailed error analysis. Understanding *why* the method succeeds or fails in specific cases could provide valuable insights for future research.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are generally well-supported by experimental results. The use of established kernel methods and cross-validation adds to the credibility. The approach *feels* complete, addressing a critical gap in the domain adaptation literature. However, as noted above, the computational complexity and limited comparison to related work detract slightly from the overall quality.
* **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise and informative.  The authors do a good job of motivating their approach and explaining the rationale behind their design choices.
* **Originality:** The problem formulation is novel and the proposed method presents a unique approach to domain adaptation. While it builds upon existing techniques like kernel methods and clustering, the combination of these techniques with the specific objective of discovering latent domains is a significant contribution.
* **Significance:** The results are important because they address a fundamental limitation of existing domain adaptation methods. The ability to automatically discover meaningful domains within datasets has the potential to significantly improve adaptation performance in a wide range of applications. The approach is likely to be used by other researchers and could inspire further work in this area.  The reshaping of test datasets is a particularly valuable contribution.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and important problem formulation.
* Principled and theoretically sound approach.
* Strong experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the method.
* Clear writing and well-organized presentation.
* Potential for significant impact on the field of domain adaptation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity concerns.
* Limited comparison to related work.
* Sensitivity to kernel choice requires further investigation.
* Practicality of DWCV for large datasets needs to be discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation. While there are some minor weaknesses, the novelty of the problem formulation, the principled approach, and the strong experimental results outweigh these concerns.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns raised above, particularly regarding computational complexity, a more thorough comparison to related work, and a discussion of the scalability of the DWCV procedure.  The authors should also consider including a brief error analysis section.
