

**Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"**

This paper addresses a critical yet overlooked issue in domain adaptation research: the common practice of treating entire datasets as well-defined domains, which often conflates multiple underlying domains. The authors argue that visual datasets typically lack clear domain boundaries due to complex, interacting factors like pose, illumination, and background. They propose a novel approach to automatically reshape datasets into characteristic domains based on two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains should support strong discriminative models).

The technical contributions include a nonparametric kernel-based measure for domain discrepancy, an optimization framework to maximize distinctiveness while maintaining label priors, and a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure to determine the optimal number of domains. The authors validate their approach on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human activity recognition (IXMAS) datasets, demonstrating significant improvements in adaptation performance over using original datasets or domains identified by prior work.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper identifies a meaningful conceptual gap in domain adaptation research that has been largely ignored in previous work (e.g., treating datasets as domains without verifying domain structure).
2. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear justification for the axiomatic properties and the use of kernel-based distribution measures.
3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with multiple datasets, tasks, and meaningful comparisons to baselines (including [20] and K-means).
4. The results consistently show significant improvements in adaptation performance, with the proposed method outperforming baselines across multiple settings.
5. The qualitative analysis (Figure 1) provides valuable insights into the factors driving domain structure (e.g., pose, color, background).

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper's claim that previous work treats datasets as domains is somewhat overstated; some prior work (e.g., [2, 3, 4]) explicitly acknowledges dataset boundaries as domains, though they may not be ideal.
2. The relationship between the proposed method and existing multi-domain adaptation approaches (e.g., [28, 29]) is not sufficiently clarified.
3. The relaxation of the optimization problem from integer programming to continuous optimization lacks sufficient justification regarding solution quality.
4. The DWCV procedure's computational complexity is not discussed, which could be a limitation for large-scale applications.
5. The paper does not fully address how the method scales to more than two domains in the test set reshaping scenario.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper identifies a fundamental issue in domain adaptation that has significant implications for both theory and practice.
- The proposed method is technically sound, with clear theoretical motivation and empirical validation.
- The results demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines across multiple tasks and datasets.
- The work has practical utility for improving domain adaptation performance and could inform future dataset construction.
- The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The novelty is somewhat incremental compared to the broader domain adaptation literature, particularly given prior work on latent domain discovery ([20]).
- The paper could better distinguish its approach from existing multi-source adaptation methods.
- Some claims about the limitations of previous work (e.g., "troubling practice") are not fully substantiated by specific citations.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution by addressing a fundamental problem in domain adaptation research that has been overlooked. While the technical approach builds on existing methods (kernel-based domain discrepancy, cross-validation), the specific application to automatically reshaping datasets into characteristic domains is novel and well-justified. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate clear practical benefits.

The paper advances the state of the art by shifting the focus from adapting between datasets to identifying the underlying domains within datasets. This perspective has important implications for both theoretical understanding and practical application of domain adaptation. The quality of the technical content, clarity of presentation, and thorough experimental evaluation support acceptance.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel technical approach and clear empirical validation. It contributes to the field by providing a principled way to identify characteristic domains within datasets, which could lead to more robust and effective adaptation algorithms. The significance of the work is demonstrated by the consistent improvements across multiple tasks and datasets.
