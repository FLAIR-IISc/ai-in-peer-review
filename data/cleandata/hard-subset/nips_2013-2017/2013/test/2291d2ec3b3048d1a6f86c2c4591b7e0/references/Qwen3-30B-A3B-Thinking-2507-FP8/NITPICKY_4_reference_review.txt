

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method to automatically reshape visual datasets into latent domains based on two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing inter-domain distribution differences) and maximum learnability (ensuring domains contain sufficient data for strong discriminative models). The authors claim this approach addresses a critical gap in visual domain adaptation research, where datasets are often incorrectly treated as domains. The method involves nonparametric domain discovery using kernel-based distribution measures and domain-wise cross-validation to determine the optimal number of domains. The approach is evaluated on object recognition and action recognition tasks, showing improvements over using original datasets as domains.

## Strengths
The paper identifies a meaningful gap in visual domain adaptation research: the common practice of treating datasets as domains rather than recognizing that datasets may contain multiple latent domains. The two proposed axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) are well-motivated and address practical concerns in domain adaptation. The experimental evaluation demonstrates improvements over using original datasets as domains, and the method shows promise for practical applications.

## Weaknesses

### Methodological Issues
1. **Incorrect claim about novelty**: The paper states "To our best knowledge, [20] is the first and only work addressing latent domain discovery" despite [20] explicitly addressing latent domain discovery. This misrepresentation of related work is unacceptable in a scientific paper. Section 3 fails to provide a detailed comparison with [20], which is necessary to demonstrate novelty.

2. **Theoretical inconsistencies**: The paper claims "nonparametric approaches" but relies on kernel-based methods (MMD) that implicitly assume a distribution structure. The phrase "without assuming any form of distribution" is misleading since kernel methods require kernel parameter selection and implicitly assume certain distribution properties.

3. **Flawed optimization formulation**: In equation (3), the label prior constraint is written as "1/Mk ∑m zmk ymc = 1/M ∑m ymc" which is dimensionally inconsistent (left side has 1/Mk, right side has 1/M). This error would prevent correct implementation.

4. **Misunderstanding of domain learning**: The paper states "For very large K such that each domain contains only a few examples, A(K) approaches the classification accuracy using the class prior probability to classify." This is incorrect - with few examples per domain, classification performance would degrade significantly, not approach class prior accuracy. The class prior accuracy represents the best possible accuracy with no features, which is unrelated to domain size.

### Experimental Issues
1. **Lack of fair comparison**: The paper claims their approach "outperforms not only the original datasets but also the domains discovered by [20]" but provides no direct comparison with [20] in the experiments. They only mention that they "experimented with Kmeans or random partition" without providing results, and omit the comparison with the most relevant prior work.

2. **Misleading oracle accuracy**: The "oracle" accuracy in Table 1 (GOURS) is presented as an "upper bound" but is actually a best-case scenario that requires target domain labels - which would not be available in practical applications. This mischaracterization undermines the paper's claims about practical utility.

3. **Incomplete reporting**: The experimental results lack standard deviations or confidence intervals (Table 2), making it impossible to assess statistical significance. The paper also omits details about hyperparameter sensitivity, which is critical for reproducibility.

### Formatting and Clarity Issues
1. **Mathematical notation errors**: Equation (1) contains incorrect formatting with "∥∥∥∥∥" instead of a proper norm symbol. The paper uses inconsistent boldface for vectors (e.g., "x" vs. "x" in some places).

2. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper refers to "latent domains" but uses "characteristic domains" interchangeably. The term "latent domains" should be used consistently.

3. **Typos and grammatical errors**: Multiple typos including "re-arranged" (should be "rearranged"), "agglomeration" (should be "agglomeration"), "categorize" (should be "categorize"), and "intuitively" (should be "intuitively").

### Theoretical Justification
The paper lacks theoretical justification for why maximizing the sum of pairwise distances (TDD) is the right objective, rather than alternative measures like maximizing the minimum pairwise distance. The authors also fail to establish a clear connection between their axiomatic properties and the performance improvements they observe.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper addresses a meaningful gap in visual domain adaptation research, the numerous methodological errors, misleading claims, and lack of proper comparison with the most relevant prior work undermine its scientific contribution. The paper contains multiple technical errors that would prevent implementation, and the claims of novelty are incorrect. The experimental evaluation is incomplete and misrepresents the practical significance of the results.

The paper's core idea has potential, but as written, it contains too many fundamental flaws to be considered a solid scientific contribution. The authors need to substantially revise the paper to correct the theoretical errors, provide a proper comparison with [20], clarify the methodology, and properly characterize their results.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper contains critical methodological errors that would prevent reproduction, makes incorrect claims about novelty, and fails to properly compare with the most relevant prior work. While the problem addressed is important, the execution of the paper is insufficient for publication at NIPS.
