Summary
This paper tackles latent domain discovery in visual datasets for domain adaptation. The key idea is to “reshape” heterogeneous datasets into multiple latent domains that (i) are maximally distinctive in distribution (measured via kernel mean embeddings/MMD-style distances), and (ii) are maximally learnable (support strong cross-validated discriminative models). The authors formulate total domain distinctiveness (TDD) as a sum of pairwise RKHS mean discrepancies between domains, propose an assignment optimization with a label-prior constraint (LPC), relax it to a continuous quadratic program by introducing β variables, and choose the number of domains K via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV). Experiments on object recognition (Amazon/DSLR/Webcam/Caltech-256) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) suggest improved adaptation when training data are reshaped into latent domains, and show gains from conditionally reshaping heterogeneous test sets to match discovered training domains.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on kernel two-sample tests / MMD (Gretton et al.), and is closely related to latent domain discovery for multi-source adaptation (Hoffman et al., ECCV’12), and generalized clustering via kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.). It uses GFK (Gopalan et al.) as the downstream adaptation method.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely and under-explored problem: discovering latent domains inside standard visual datasets without human-defined factors.
- Nonparametric use of kernel mean embeddings avoids Gaussian-mixture assumptions in [20].
- Clear intuition: maximize inter-domain discrepancy while maintaining within-domain learnability; pragmatic DWCV procedure to pick K.
- Empirical evaluation on multiple datasets and tasks; qualitative analysis gives insight into discovered factors (pose, background, color).

Weaknesses and technical concerns
1) Optimization formulation and correctness:
- Notational overload and omissions: K denotes both number of domains and the kernel matrix; M′k in Eq. (1) appears but is not defined; “the the” in Eq. (1); use of d(k,k′) not clearly squared vs. not.
- The discrete-to-continuous relaxation is underspecified. Setting βmk = zmk/Mk introduces dependence on Mk (unknown a priori). In the relaxation, βk is put on the simplex ∑m βmk = 1, but the derivation from Eq. (1) to Eq. (4) is not shown. Please provide the exact algebra bridging z to β and how the 1/Mk factor is handled.
- Constraint (5) 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C is unusual. In the integer problem ∑k zmk=1 enforces an assignment; here the relaxed constraint enforces that an instance’s total mass is in [1/M,1/C], which is extremely weak and allows substantial mass across many domains. This does not ensure Mk ≥ C nor “at least one instance per category per domain.” The stated rationale “Mk≥C” is not enforced by (5).
- The LPC constraint uses (1±δ)/M ∑m ymc on the RHS. Since ∑m βmk=1, this forces each domain’s β-weighted class proportions to match the global class prior, regardless of domain size. This seems too restrictive (real domains can have skewed label priors) and can suppress genuine domain-specific label frequency shifts that are common in practice. It may also cause infeasibility for small K or classes with few samples.
- Objective properties: The paper states the relaxed problem is a maximization of a convex quadratic under linear constraints. Maximizing a convex function is intractable in general, and no algorithmic details are provided (which solver? initialization? stopping? multiple restarts?). Without guarantees or careful heuristics, solutions can be poor or unstable.

2) Trivial/degenerate solutions and regularization:
- TDD(K) increases with K; the method relies solely on DWCV to prevent tiny domains. Consider adding explicit regularization on K, minimum domain sizes, or entropy penalties on β to discourage diffuse assignments and avoid near-degenerate splits.
- The instance-level constraint allows ∑k βmk to be small; combined with argmax rounding, assignments can be unstable. No tie-breaking or rounding analysis is provided.

3) Choice of K and DWCV:
- Upper bound Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C} is ad hoc and can be overly restrictive (e.g., limiting K≤C). Why not include K=1 as a baseline? No sensitivity analysis of N or δ.
- DWCV optimizes training-domain CV accuracy; no held-out validation per K is used; risk of overfitting K to training labels.

4) Experimental reporting:
- Critical tables/figures (Tables 1–3, Figs. 1–2) are not included. No standard deviations, number of trials, or significance tests are reported. Without numbers, it is hard to assess effect size or robustness.
- Baselines: only [20] is mentioned; KMeans and random partition were tried but results are “omitted.” They should be reported. Also compare against simple heuristics (e.g., clustering in feature space with class-balance constraints), and against single-domain baseline (K=1).
- Implementation details missing: solver, runtime, memory, scalability with M (O(M^2) kernel), bandwidth selection sensitivity (the “twice the median” heuristic is unusual; justify).
- Features and adaptation method are dated (SURF-BoVW, GFK). While historically plausible, modern features (CNNs) and recent DA methods would better validate the idea’s relevance.

5) Conditional test reshaping:
- The convex QP formulation is deferred to the supplement; core details are missing. How is matching across K domains enforced? Is K shared between train and test? How are domain permutations handled?

6) Claims vs. supervision:
- The method uses labels explicitly in LPC and DWCV; calling the discovery “unsupervised” is misleading. Please clarify the supervision assumptions.

Clarity and presentation issues
- Several typos and inconsistencies; missing definitions (M′k), overloaded notation K, unclear norms (“approaches zero iff Pk=Pk′” holds for population MMD; discuss finite-sample estimators).
- References are incomplete (missing years/pages; many “In CVPR,, , ,” placeholders).
- The paper occasionally reads as a position piece (few algorithmic specifics) rather than a fully reproducible method. Pseudocode/algorithm boxes would help.

Significance and originality
- The problem is important, and the nonparametric angle is reasonable. However, relative to Hoffman et al. (latent domains via mixtures) and kernel-embedding clustering, the methodological novelty is incremental and underdeveloped, and the experimental evidence (as presented) is insufficient to establish clear, robust gains.

Questions for authors
- Provide the full derivation from Eq. (1) to (4), and justify constraint (5). How do you enforce Mk≥C?
- Which solver and initialization do you use? Runtime and memory on each dataset?
- Sensitivity to δ, kernel bandwidth, and Kmax/N?
- Report full quantitative results with variance, and include all baselines mentioned.
- Can LPC be relaxed to allow domain-specific priors?

Pros
- Addresses a relevant, under-explored problem.
- Uses kernel mean embeddings; avoids strong parametric assumptions.
- Sensible high-level criteria (distinctiveness + learnability).

Cons
- Incomplete and potentially flawed optimization formulation; insufficient algorithmic detail.
- Strong, possibly unrealistic LPC; weak assignment constraints.
- Missing empirical details and results; outdated features; limited baselines.
- Presentation/notation/reference issues.

Recommendation: Reject (significant methodological and presentation issues; promising direction but needs clearer, correct formulation, stronger experiments, and complete reporting).

Confidence: High.
