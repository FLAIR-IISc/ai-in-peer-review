

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper proposes a novel approach to domain adaptation by discovering latent domains within visual datasets that are not naturally partitioned into domains (unlike speech or language data). The authors argue that standard domain adaptation research incorrectly treats entire datasets as single domains, when in fact datasets often contain multiple underlying domains. They propose a method to reshape datasets into domains based on two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains suitable for learning discriminative classifiers).

The core contribution is a nonparametric method to discover these domains using kernel-based domain discrepancy measures and a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure to determine the optimal number of domains. The authors validate their approach on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and action recognition (IXMAS) datasets, showing marginal improvements over using the original datasets as domains.

**Strengths:**
- The problem statement is well-motivated and identifies a genuine issue in visual domain adaptation research.
- The paper provides a clear technical description of the proposed method and experimental validation on multiple datasets.
- The comparison to Hoffman et al.'s (2014) work on latent domain discovery is relevant, though superficial.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper suffers from significant methodological and validation shortcomings that undermine its contribution:

1. **Lack of theoretical justification:** The authors claim that "maximum distinctiveness" is a desirable property but provide no theoretical basis for why maximizing domain discrepancy should lead to better classification performance. In fact, the standard wisdom in domain adaptation is to minimize domain discrepancy between source and target domains. The paper fails to justify why maximizing inter-domain discrepancy (within the source) is beneficial for target domain adaptation.

2. **Superficial comparison to existing work:** The authors claim to be "the first and only work addressing latent domain discovery" but fail to properly engage with Hoffman et al. (2014), which is directly relevant. The comparison in Section 3 is cursory and does not address key methodological differences. The paper also fails to cite several other domain adaptation works that implicitly handle multiple domains (e.g., multi-source domain adaptation methods).

3. **Marginal empirical improvements:** The reported improvements (Table 2) are marginal (typically 1-3 percentage points) and not convincingly significant. The paper claims their method "outperforms not only the original datasets but also the domains discovered by [20]" but the improvements are not statistically significant in most cases. The paper also fails to compare against standard domain adaptation methods that might already handle multiple domains.

4. **Unconvincing qualitative analysis:** The qualitative examples in Figure 1 are anecdotal and do not demonstrate that the discovered domains are meaningful for classification. The authors claim domains are "mainly determined by factors like object pose and appearance" but provide no evidence beyond visual inspection.

5. **Questionable practical utility:** The paper's claim that reshaping datasets into latent domains improves adaptation performance is not convincingly demonstrated. The experiments in Section 4.2 use oracle accuracy (Table 1) that is not achievable in practice, and the practical results (Table 2) show only modest improvements over naive baselines.

6. **Lack of computational analysis:** The paper does not address the computational complexity of the proposed method, which is crucial for large-scale applications. The optimization problem described in Section 2.1 is NP-hard and the relaxation may not scale well to large datasets.

7. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims to "reshape datasets into domains" as if this is a fundamentally new approach, when it's essentially a data partitioning strategy. The authors do not convincingly argue why this specific partitioning strategy is better than simply using standard domain adaptation methods that handle multiple domains.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes a modest contribution to the domain adaptation literature but fails to provide sufficient evidence to justify its claims. The proposed method appears to be a data partitioning strategy rather than a fundamentally new approach to domain adaptation. The marginal improvements over existing methods do not justify the paper's strong claims, and the lack of theoretical justification for the key algorithmic choices is concerning.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A clearer theoretical justification for why maximizing inter-domain discrepancy is beneficial for adaptation
2. More rigorous comparison against standard domain adaptation baselines
3. Statistical significance testing for reported improvements
4. A better explanation of why this approach is fundamentally different from simply using multi-source domain adaptation methods

Given these significant weaknesses, I recommend rejection. The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims, and the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. The field would benefit more from a paper that clearly demonstrates how to effectively leverage multiple domains in domain adaptation, rather than one that proposes a data reshaping strategy with marginal empirical benefits.
