

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper addresses a fundamental issue in visual domain adaptation: the common practice of treating entire datasets as single domains, when in reality visual datasets often contain multiple latent domains that are not explicitly defined. The authors propose a method to automatically reshape datasets into characteristic domains based on two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains should allow for strong discriminative models). They develop nonparametric approaches to measure domain discrepancies and optimize for these properties, and introduce domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to determine the optimal number of domains. The authors evaluate their approach on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, showing consistent improvements over both the original datasets and the previous work by Hoffman et al. [20].

The paper builds upon domain adaptation research at NIPS and elsewhere, including the work of Saenko et al. [2], Hoffman et al. [20], and others. It makes a clear distinction between their approach and prior work by focusing on discovering latent domains rather than adapting between pre-specified domains. The authors correctly identify that [20] is the only prior work addressing latent domain discovery, but their nonparametric approach overcomes the limitations of [20]'s parametric mixture modeling.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a clear theoretical foundation. The authors provide rigorous mathematical formulations for measuring domain distinctiveness using kernel-based methods in RKHS, and they properly address the optimization challenges through appropriate relaxation techniques. The DWCV procedure for determining the number of domains is well-justified and practical. The experimental design is comprehensive, with careful evaluation on both object recognition (using Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and action recognition (using IXMAS) tasks. The results consistently show that reshaping datasets into latent domains leads to improved domain adaptation performance, with the authors demonstrating that their approach outperforms both the original datasets and the method from [20]. The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including kernel bandwidth selection, DWCV implementation, and feature extraction methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The problem statement is clearly motivated with concrete examples (e.g., the human action recognition scenario with different view angles). The mathematical formulations are presented logically, and the experimental results are effectively illustrated with tables and figures. The distinction between the theoretical contributions and empirical validation is well-maintained throughout. The authors clearly explain their intuition behind the two axiomatic properties and how they differ from traditional clustering approaches.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution to the field by identifying and addressing the problem of latent domain discovery in visual datasets. The authors correctly note that [20] is the only prior work on this topic, but their nonparametric approach represents a substantial advance over [20]'s parametric method. The concept of "reshaping" datasets rather than treating them as single domains is novel and addresses a critical gap in domain adaptation research. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from related work, including clustering methods and multi-source domain adaptation techniques.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly significant for real-world domain adaptation applications, where datasets often contain multiple latent domains that are not captured by treating the entire dataset as a single domain. The proposed method has the potential to improve performance across a wide range of visual recognition tasks. The paper demonstrates practical benefits through extensive experiments showing consistent improvements in classification accuracy. The authors also note that their approach could inform future dataset construction practices by revealing the main factors determining domain structure (e.g., pose, illumination, background). The work is both theoretically sound and practically valuable.

**Strengths:**
- Clear identification of a fundamental gap in domain adaptation research
- Well-motivated axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and learnability)
- Nonparametric approach that avoids limitations of parametric modeling
- Comprehensive experimental validation on multiple tasks
- Practical DWCV method for determining number of domains
- Strong empirical results showing consistent improvement over baselines

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited theoretical analysis of convergence properties of the optimization procedure
- Computational complexity analysis for large-scale datasets is not provided
- The conditional reshaping of test datasets is described but details are relegated to supplementary material
- More discussion of why the discovered domains correspond to specific visual factors (beyond the examples shown)

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**
Pro: The paper identifies a significant problem in domain adaptation research that has been overlooked, proposes a novel solution with strong theoretical foundation, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing approaches. The work is well-executed, thoroughly evaluated, and has potential to influence both research and practice in domain adaptation.

Con: The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of computational complexity and convergence properties, though these are not critical to the main contribution. The work is not so much limited by these aspects as it is by the need for deeper theoretical analysis, which is common in applied machine learning papers.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation by addressing a fundamental problem that has been overlooked in prior work. The approach is technically sound, well-justified, and empirically validated. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and has the potential to improve domain adaptation performance across multiple visual recognition tasks. The paper is well-written, clear, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. I recommend acceptance.
