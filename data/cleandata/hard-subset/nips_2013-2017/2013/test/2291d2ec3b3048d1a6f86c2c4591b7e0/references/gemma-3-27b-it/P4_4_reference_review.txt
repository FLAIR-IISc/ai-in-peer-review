This paper proposes a method for automatically reshaping visual datasets into domains for improved domain adaptation. The core idea is to identify latent domains based on two principles: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The authors formulate this as an optimization problem and provide a solution, along with a method for determining the number of domains. Experimental results on object recognition and human activity recognition demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a crucial but often overlooked aspect of domain adaptation – the inherent structure within datasets. Existing domain adaptation techniques often treat entire datasets as single domains, which can be suboptimal. The authors clearly articulate this problem and propose a principled approach to address it. The proposed axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) are reasonable and provide a strong foundation for the methodology. The use of kernel methods to measure domain discrepancies is appropriate and well-explained. The DWCV method for determining the optimal number of domains is a practical addition. The experiments are thorough and demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach compared to baseline methods, including a comparison with Hoffman et al. (2013). The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The inclusion of ablation studies and visualizations (Figure 1) strengthens the analysis.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The optimization problem, while formulated, appears complex and potentially computationally expensive. While the authors mention using existing solvers, further details on the scalability of the method to large datasets would be beneficial. The reliance on kernel methods introduces a hyperparameter (kernel bandwidth) that requires careful tuning. The sensitivity of the results to this parameter is not thoroughly explored. The explanation of the label prior constraint (LPC) feels slightly hand-wavy. While the intuition is clear, a more rigorous justification or analysis of its impact would be helpful. The empirical evaluation, while comprehensive, is limited to specific datasets and tasks. Extending the evaluation to more diverse datasets and potentially different modalities would enhance the generalizability of the findings. The connection between the discovered domains and human interpretable concepts is not always clear. While Figure 1 provides some insights, a more systematic analysis of the domain characteristics would be valuable.  The analysis of the factors dominating the identified domains could be expanded to provide more conclusive findings.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a critical problem in domain adaptation.
*   Principled formulation based on sound axiomatic properties.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Comparison to relevant prior work, including Hoffman et al. (2013).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Computational complexity of the optimization problem.
*   Sensitivity to kernel hyperparameters.
*   Limited scalability to very large datasets.
*   Lack of extensive evaluation on diverse datasets.
*   Limited analysis of domain interpretability.



**Overall assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The proposed method for reshaping datasets into domains is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong experimental results. While there are some weaknesses related to computational complexity and scalability, the potential benefits of the approach outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the scalability concerns and providing a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity to kernel parameters.  Additionally, a discussion of potential strategies for improving the computational efficiency of the optimization process would be beneficial.
