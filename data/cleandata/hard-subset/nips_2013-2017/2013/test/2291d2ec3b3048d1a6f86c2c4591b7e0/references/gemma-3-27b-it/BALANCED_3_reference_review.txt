This paper addresses the crucial problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition, specifically focusing on the often-overlooked question of what *constitutes* a domain. The authors argue that datasets are frequently treated as domains, leading to suboptimal adaptation due to the inherent heterogeneity within a single dataset. They propose a novel approach to automatically *reshape* datasets into more meaningful, latent domains based on the principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. This is a compelling and important contribution.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The motivation is strong and the problem is articulated effectively. The core ideas of maximizing distinctiveness and learnability are intuitive and grounded in reasonable assumptions about desirable domain properties. The mathematical formulation, while complex, appears sound, and the use of kernel methods provides a flexible framework. The experimental validation on both object recognition and human activity recognition datasets is thorough and demonstrates significant improvements over existing approaches and baselines, particularly when combined with multi-source adaptation techniques. The analysis of the identified domains provides valuable insights into the factors driving the domain discovery process.  The inclusion of detailed experimental setup and comparison with relevant works (e.g., Hoffman et al. [20]) strengthens the evaluation.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Importance:** The paper tackles a fundamental, yet often ignored, aspect of domain adaptation – the definition and discovery of domains themselves.  This is a significant departure from the standard dataset-as-domain paradigm.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The formulation based on maximizing distinctiveness and learnability provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed approach.
* **Empirical Validation:** Extensive experiments on diverse datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, outperforming existing approaches.
* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.
* **Thorough Comparison:**  The comparison with existing methods, including Hoffman et al. [20], is comprehensive and fair.
* **Practical Relevance:** The ability to automatically discover meaningful domains has significant practical implications for building more robust and generalizable visual recognition systems.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** The optimization process, particularly the continuous relaxation, might be computationally expensive for very large datasets. While the authors mention using existing solvers, the scalability of the approach could be a concern.  More discussion of computational cost and potential optimizations would be beneficial.
* **Kernel Choice:** The choice of the Gaussian kernel appears somewhat arbitrary. While justified, a more thorough investigation of different kernel functions and their impact on performance could be explored.  Is the performance sensitive to the kernel bandwidth parameter?
* **Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The justification for the LPC (equation 3) feels slightly weak. While the authors intuitively explain it, a more rigorous theoretical grounding or ablation study demonstrating its importance would strengthen the argument.  What happens when the LPC is removed or weighted differently?
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors provide some qualitative analysis of the identified domains, a more systematic error analysis identifying cases where the method fails and potential reasons for those failures would be helpful.
* **Conditional Reshaping Details:** While the approach to reshaping the test data is mentioned, the details are relegated to the supplementary material. A more concise explanation within the main body of the paper would improve accessibility.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a critical problem in domain adaptation.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written, clear, and thoroughly evaluated.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of visual recognition.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the approach may limit its scalability to very large datasets.
* Some aspects of the theoretical justification (e.g., LPC) could be strengthened.
* A more detailed error analysis would provide further insights into the limitations of the method.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The authors have identified an important problem, proposed a novel and well-motivated solution, and validated it through extensive experiments. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the concerns regarding computational complexity, LPC justification, and error analysis. Specifically, I suggest the authors provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost, explore the sensitivity of the results to the kernel bandwidth, and conduct a more systematic error analysis to identify the limitations of their approach.
