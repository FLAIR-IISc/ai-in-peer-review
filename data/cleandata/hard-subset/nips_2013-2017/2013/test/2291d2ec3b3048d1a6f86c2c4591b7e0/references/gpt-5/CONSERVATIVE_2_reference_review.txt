Summary
This paper addresses the problem of “latent domain discovery” within visual datasets used for domain adaptation. The authors argue that visual datasets often conflate multiple distinct domains (e.g., pose, illumination), and that treating them as single domains harms adaptation. They propose two principles for reshaping datasets into domains: (1) maximum distinctiveness, implemented by maximizing the sum of pairwise RKHS mean discrepancies (MMD) between latent domains; and (2) maximum learnability, used to select the number of domains K via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV). The main optimization uses a nonparametric kernel formulation with a label prior constraint (LPC) that enforces per-domain class proportions to match the overall prior, with a relaxed quadratic program solved by off-the-shelf solvers. They further propose “conditional reshaping” of test sets to match discovered training domains. Experiments on Office-Caltech object recognition and IXMAS cross-view actions suggest that the discovered domains improve adaptation performance and can outperform Hoffman et al. [20] (GMM-based).

Strengths
- Timely motivation: datasets used as “domains” are often heterogeneous; explicitly discovering latent domains could be useful for adaptation.
- Nonparametric discrepancy measure (MMD) avoids restrictive parametric assumptions; using characteristic kernels is appropriate.
- The label-aware constraint attempts to avoid trivial per-class clustering and aligns with practical supervised settings.
- The idea to pick K via domain-wise cross-validation is pragmatic and easy to implement.
- The “conditional reshaping” of test data is interesting and aligns with transductive unsupervised DA.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Formulation/constraints: The relaxation is under-specified and appears inconsistent. βk is defined on a simplex (sum_m βmk = 1 per k), but the cross-domain consistency for each instance m is not enforced. The first constraint 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C (per m) is unusual and not derived from ∑k zmk = 1; its dependence on C is suspect and may be a typo or conceptual error. This raises concerns about the correctness of the relaxation and whether the assignment step (arg max over k) is principled.
- Degeneracy/overfitting: Maximizing pairwise MMD over partitions, even with LPC, risks carving out small, outlier-driven groups that inflate MMD but harm generalization. The objective is a maximization of a convex quadratic subject to linear constraints (non-convex, NP-hard), yet no guarantees or approximation rationale are provided. Without a regularizer encouraging balanced, stable partitions (beyond LPC), the method may be brittle.
- LPC assumption: Requiring per-domain class proportions to match the global prior is strong and often violated in real multi-domain collections. Many real domains are missing classes or have different priors; enforcing LPC could distort the discovered domains, reduce validity, and limit applicability.
- Choosing K: DWCV optimizes training cross-validated accuracy, which can favor overly fine partitions that fit idiosyncrasies in the training set. There is no stability or model selection analysis (e.g., BIC/MDL, held-out domain performance, perturbation-based stability). The practical bound Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C} seems ad hoc.
- Experimental evidence: The paper reports oracle quantities (max over source domains with target labels) and claims improvements, but concrete numbers are not shown here. Baselines for clustering (kernel k-means, spectral clustering with constraints, supervised mixture models) and alternative DA back-ends (beyond GFK) are missing or only briefly mentioned as non-competitive. It is unclear how robust the findings are to the adaptation method, feature choices, and hyperparameters (kernel bandwidth, δ).
- Conditional reshaping: The test-time convex QP is deferred to the supplement; key details and computational costs are missing. The method is transductive and may exploit test distribution structure; this is acceptable in unsupervised DA, but should be explicitly positioned and compared against standard transductive DA baselines.
- Reproducibility and clarity: The optimization details, solver choices, runtime, and sensitivity analyses are not provided. Several notational inconsistencies (e.g., use of M′k in Eq. 1, constraints in Eq. 5) hinder clarity.

Quality
The technical core is plausible but underdeveloped. The optimization has conceptual issues (constraints), lacks theoretical support (e.g., conditions under which maximizing MMD with LPC recovers meaningful domains), and the empirical section, as presented, does not provide sufficient quantitative evidence or ablations. The reliance on oracle comparisons and limited baselines weakens the validation.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-motivated, but key derivations and constraints need to be corrected and explained. Important algorithmic details (for both training and test reshaping) are deferred or omitted.

Originality
The problem framing is interesting. Using MMD to drive discriminability among latent groups is related to prior work on kernel embedding-based clustering and generalized clustering [27], but the combination with label priors and DA is a novel angle. The overlap with Hoffman et al. [20] is addressed but not deeply compared empirically.

Significance
If sound and robust, such reshaping could be useful for DA and dataset design. However, given the current methodological and empirical gaps, the contribution does not yet convincingly advance the state of the art.

Questions for the authors
1. Please clarify and correct the relaxation constraints. How do you enforce that each instance is assigned to exactly one domain in the relaxed problem? What is the derivation of 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C?
2. How do you prevent degenerate solutions that isolate outliers to inflate MMD? Any balancing regularizer or minimum cluster size constraint?
3. How sensitive are results to kernel choice/bandwidth and δ in LPC? Provide sensitivity plots.
4. How often is LPC violated in real datasets (class priors differ across domains)? What happens if some classes are absent from a domain?
5. Provide comprehensive quantitative results (means, std, significance), more baselines (kernel k-means with label constraints, spectral clustering, [27]), and multiple DA back-ends.
6. Detail the convex QP for test reshaping, its complexity, and ablations showing its incremental benefit over training-only reshaping.
7. Analyze stability of K via DWCV (e.g., under resampling) and compare to alternative criteria.

Pros
- Clear motivation; interesting problem.
- Nonparametric discrepancy measure; practical K selection procedure.
- Applies to training and test reshaping; potentially broadly useful.

Cons
- Formulation/constraint issues; potential methodological flaws.
- Strong and possibly unrealistic LPC assumption.
- Lack of theory and limited empirical validation/ablations.
- Missing details and clarity on optimization and computational costs.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is promising, but the current formulation has apparent inconsistencies, and the empirical evidence and analyses are insufficient to support the claims. Addressing the constraint issues, adding theory or stronger empirical validation with thorough baselines and sensitivity/stability analyses could make this a stronger submission. Confidence: high.
