Summary
The paper tackles the often-overlooked issue that visual “domains” used in domain adaptation are frequently equated with entire datasets, which may themselves be mixtures of heterogeneous subdomains (e.g., different viewpoints, backgrounds, illumination). The authors propose to reshape datasets into latent domains guided by two axioms: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be as distributionally different as possible) and maximum learnability (each domain should support a strong discriminative model). For distinctiveness, they maximize a nonparametric divergence between domain marginals based on RKHS mean embeddings (MMD), while enforcing a label prior constraint to prevent per-class clustering. They relax the combinatorial assignment to a continuous quadratic program and assign instances to the domain with maximal weight. For learnability, they propose domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to select the number of latent domains K by maximizing weighted cross-validated accuracy across domains. They also introduce conditional reshaping of test sets to match discovered training domains, again via minimizing MMD to training domains (details deferred to the supplement). Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) suggest improved target accuracies over using original datasets as domains and over a hierarchical Gaussian-mixture latent domain method [Hoffman et al., ECCV’12], under both oracle selection and practical multi-source adaptation strategies using the Geodesic Flow Kernel.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects to multi-source adaptation, dataset bias, and MMD-based two-sample testing. It contrasts with Hoffman et al. (latent domain discovery via parametric mixtures), and cites generalized clustering via kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.). The conditional test reshaping idea is less explored in vision adaptation literature and is a noteworthy addition.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: datasets ≠ domains; over-aggregated training sets can harm adaptation.
- Conceptual clarity: the two axioms (distinctiveness, learnability) are simple and appealing.
- Nonparametric discrepancy: using kernel mean embeddings avoids restrictive parametric mixture assumptions and is robust in high dimensions.
- Practical selection of K via DWCV: ties domain discovery to actual discriminative performance, not just divergence maximization.
- Conditional test reshaping: a useful and underexplored direction for heterogeneous test environments.
- Empirical scope: both images and videos; multiple domain adaptation settings; qualitative analyses that illuminate discovered factors (pose, background, color).

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Optimization details are underspecified. The core problem is maximization of a convex quadratic over linear constraints (nonconvex, NP-hard). The paper states “off-the-shelf solvers are adequate” without describing the method (e.g., DC programming/CCCP, projected ascent with random restarts, spectral relaxation, or MILP) or providing convergence diagnostics, initialization, or runtime/scale. Reproducibility and robustness are unclear.
- Scalability: the method requires forming the M×M kernel matrix; no discussion of approximations (Nyström, random features), complexity, or memory limits. This will limit applicability to modern-scale datasets.
- Label prior constraint (LPC) is strong and potentially unrealistic. It assumes class proportions are domain-invariant (up to 1%), which can be violated in practice (class/domain shift is common). The constraint also uses labels during domain discovery, making the method semi-supervised and raising concerns about biasing domains toward better classification rather than uncovering truly generative factors. A softer regularizer (e.g., KL penalty to the global prior) or a conditional discrepancy (class-conditional MMD) might be more principled.
- Objective/design tension: maximizing marginal distinctiveness risks separating classes rather than nuisance factors; LPC is a heuristic fix. A conditional MMD or per-class centering in the RKHS to explicitly remove label effects would better match the goal.
- Experimental reporting is incomplete. The paper references comparisons to K-means/random partitions but omits numbers; it refers to tables with oracle and practical accuracies, but they are not visible here. Statistical significance, sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, δ in LPC, and K-selection are not thoroughly analyzed.
- Conditional test reshaping is deferred to the supplement; the main text lacks the exact optimization and constraints, making it hard to assess correctness and convexity claims.
- Theoretical guarantees: no identifiability or generalization guarantees connecting DWCV-selected K to target performance; no bounds relating TDD and adaptation risk.

Quality
Technically promising but incomplete. The framework is coherent, but key algorithmic and empirical details are missing (solver, complexity, sensitivity), and several modeling choices (LPC, marginal MMD) are heuristic. The experiments are suggestive; a more rigorous and transparent evaluation would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
Generally well written and motivated. Some notational inconsistencies (e.g., M′k vs Mk′ in Eq. (1)), and constraints in Eq. (5) are unusual and deserve more explanation and derivation. The lack of algorithmic details hinders reproducibility.

Originality
Latent domain discovery for visual datasets with a nonparametric discrepancy and a learnability-based K selection is novel relative to prior parametric approaches. Conditional test reshaping is also novel and interesting.

Significance
If made robust and scalable, the ideas could influence how practitioners prepare training data for adaptation and evaluation. As presented, the impact is limited by scalability and incomplete methodological detail, but the direction is valuable.

Questions for the authors
1) What optimization algorithm do you use for the convex quadratic maximization? How do you initialize, ensure progress, and avoid poor local maxima? Provide runtimes and scalability analysis.
2) How sensitive are results to the kernel bandwidth and δ in the LPC? What happens if LPC is relaxed further or removed?
3) Can you replace marginal MMD with class-conditional MMD to avoid relying on LPC?
4) How large are M in your experiments, and what approximations (if any) are used for K?
5) For conditional test reshaping, please include the exact optimization, proof of convexity, and complexity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full algorithmic details and open-source code.
- Add ablations: without LPC, with softer label-regularization, conditional MMD, sensitivity to K and kernel parameters.
- Include baselines from fair/balanced clustering (e.g., balanced K-means, constrained spectral clustering) and generalized kernel-embedding clustering.
- Report all omitted quantitative results and significance tests; add larger-scale datasets or feature maps with kernel approximations.
- Discuss failure modes when class priors differ across domains.

Pros
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem (dataset-to-domain gap).
- Conceptually simple, nonparametric formulation; practical K-selection.
- Promising empirical results; applicable to both training and test reshaping.

Cons
- Incomplete optimization details; unclear scalability.
- Strong LPC assumption and reliance on labels in discovery.
- Limited theoretical guarantees and incomplete experimental reporting.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The problem is important and the approach is fresh and potentially impactful, but the paper needs stronger methodological detail and clearer, more exhaustive empirical evidence to meet NeurIPS standards. If the authors can address optimization/scalability and provide thorough ablations and comparisons, this would be a strong contribution.
