

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper proposes a novel approach to "reshape" visual datasets into characteristic domains by maximizing two properties: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The authors argue that standard visual datasets are not properly organized into domains, leading to suboptimal domain adaptation performance. They claim their method outperforms the previous work [20] in discovering latent domains.

## Major Concerns

The most serious issue is the data leakage in the domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure for determining the optimal number of domains K. The paper states: "we choose K* as the value that attains the highest cross-validation accuracy: K* = arg max_K A(K)." However, the domain assignments are determined using the same data that's used for cross-validation. This creates a circular dependency where the domain discovery process is optimized using the same data that's used to evaluate the domain quality. This is a fundamental methodological flaw that invalidates the reported results, as the evaluation is not truly independent of the model selection process.

The label prior constraint (LPC) in equation (3) is not well-justified. The paper claims "in the process of data collection, the relative percentages of different classes are approximately in accordance with a prior distribution that is independent of domains." This is a strong assumption that may not hold in many real-world datasets. For instance, in the Amazon vs. Caltech-256 dataset comparison, the class distributions are known to differ significantly between these datasets. The paper fails to discuss how this assumption affects the method's performance when it doesn't hold.

The paper's claim that "[20] is the first and only work addressing latent domain discovery" is inaccurate. The work by Gopalan et al. [3] and others discuss domain adaptation with multiple sources, which is related to the problem of latent domain discovery. The authors should properly acknowledge these related works.

## Technical and Formatting Issues

The paper contains several technical inconsistencies. In section 2.1, the paper states "We compute the the difference between the means" (with a duplicate "the"). The optimization problem in equation (4) is presented as a maximization problem, but the objective function is a squared distance that would be maximized when domains are maximally different, which is not clearly explained.

The paper claims that "simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains," but provides no evidence for this claim beyond citing [20]. The paper should provide specific examples or empirical evidence showing why standard clustering would fail for this problem.

The paper states "the marginal distribution Pk(x) is approximated by the empirical distribution P̂k(x)" but doesn't clarify whether this is the marginal over features or the joint distribution of features and labels, which could lead to confusion.

## Experimental Concerns

The experimental comparison with [20] is insufficient. The paper claims "our method yields a better set of identified domains, which are always better than the original datasets" (Table 1), but doesn't provide statistical significance tests to support this claim. The comparison also lacks a clear baseline of standard domain adaptation methods.

The paper states "the number favored by DWCV coincides with the number of datasets we mix," but provides no explanation for why this happens or whether it's a coincidence. This suggests the DWCV procedure may be simply selecting the number of datasets as the optimal number of domains, which undermines the method's purpose.

The paper mentions "the main factors here are probably background and object appearance (color and shape)" in Figure 1, but provides no quantitative evidence to support these qualitative observations. This is a significant omission for a paper claiming to discover meaningful domain factors.

## Minor Issues

- The paper states "While many learning algorithms assume the two are the same" (referring to source and target domains), but this is factually incorrect. Domain adaptation specifically addresses the case where source and target domains differ.
- The paper claims "the challenge cannot be construed as a traditional clustering problem" but doesn't provide a clear theoretical justification for why traditional clustering methods wouldn't work for this purpose.
- The paper uses "K" both as the number of domains and as a variable in the TDD(K) function, which could be confusing.

## Conclusion

While the paper addresses an interesting problem in domain adaptation, the methodological flaws, particularly the data leakage in the DWCV procedure, are substantial enough to invalidate the reported results. The paper also contains several technical inconsistencies, unjustified assumptions, and insufficient experimental comparisons. Despite the potentially interesting idea, the paper does not meet the standards required for publication at NIPS.

The authors should address these fundamental issues before considering resubmission. The current version does not provide a sound methodological foundation for the proposed approach.
