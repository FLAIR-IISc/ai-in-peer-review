Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a prevalent but under-examined assumption in visual domain adaptation: that datasets correspond to coherent “domains.” It argues that visual datasets often aggregate multiple latent domains shaped by nuisance factors (pose, illumination, background, etc.), and proposes to automatically reshape datasets into latent domains that are better suited for adaptation. Two desiderata guide the method: maximum distinctiveness (latent domains should be maximally different in distribution) and maximum learnability (each domain should support learning a strong discriminative model). For distinctiveness, the paper maximizes the sum of pairwise discrepancies between kernel mean embeddings (MMD) of domains in RKHS, subject to assignment constraints and a label prior constraint ensuring similar class proportions across domains. The discrete assignment is relaxed to a continuous quadratic program. For learnability, the number of domains K is selected via domain-wise cross-validation accuracy aggregated across domains. The paper also proposes “conditional reshaping” of test sets to match discovered training domains by minimizing kernel discrepancies. Experiments on Office-like object datasets (Amazon, DSLR, Webcam, Caltech-256) and IXMAS action recognition show improved adaptation performance versus treating original datasets or Gaussian-mixture latent domains [Hoffman et al., ECCV’12] as sources, and demonstrate additional gains when reshaping test sets.

The work builds on: (i) kernel two-sample testing and MMD [Gretton et al.], (ii) geodesic flow kernel (GFK) adaptation [Gong et al., CVPR’12], (iii) multisource adaptation, and (iv) latent domain discovery via parametric mixtures [Hoffman et al.]. The nonparametric MMD-based domain discovery with an explicit learnability criterion appears novel relative to [20].

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated problem formulation: the paper convincingly argues that datasets ≠ domains in vision and proposes an unsupervised (w.r.t. domains) way to discover them.
- Principled use of MMD to quantify domain distinctiveness without parametric assumptions; the objective aligns with maximizing between-domain scatter in RKHS.
- Practical and interpretable selection of K via domain-wise cross-validation; connects discovery to downstream utility.
- Thoughtful extension to conditional reshaping of test sets and demonstration on inherently heterogeneous cross-view action recognition.
- Empirical results cover both image and video, show improvement over using original datasets and over a competing latent-domain discovery approach [20], and explore multi-source adaptation strategies.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Optimization clarity and tractability: The relaxed problem maximizes a convex quadratic function subject to linear constraints, which is still NP-hard. The paper states “existing solvers are adequate” but does not specify the algorithm, initialization, stopping criteria, or runtime/memory complexity. Given the O(M^2) kernel and K variables, scalability is a key practical issue; complexity and empirical runtimes should be reported.
- Assumption encoded in label prior constraint (LPC): LPC enforces that class label proportions are approximately equal across latent domains. This is strong and may not hold in many real datasets (e.g., certain categories are naturally more frequent under certain conditions). The approach is therefore not fully unsupervised; it uses labels in discovery and for K selection. An ablation showing performance without LPC (or with relaxed/learned priors) is needed, as well as a discussion of failure modes when priors are domain-dependent.
- Risk of overfitting in choosing K: The DWCV criterion favors within-domain separability on training data. While pragmatic, it could bias the discovered domains to those that are easiest to classify rather than those that transfer best. The paper partially addresses this by showing test performance vs. K, but a more direct validation (e.g., selecting K solely via DWCV and reporting test performance, which seems to be done, plus robustness across folds) would strengthen the claim.
- Limited baselines and reporting: While results versus [20] are reported, KMeans and random partition baselines are said to be uncompetitive but omitted. Including them, and showing that gains are not specific to the chosen adaptation method (e.g., beyond GFK), would bolster significance and generality.
- Notational/technical details: Some expressions (e.g., Eq. (1) uses M′k; constraint 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C) deserve clearer justification and derivation. The conditional reshaping optimization is deferred to the supplement; at least a concise formulation would help readability and reproducibility. Sensitivity to kernel bandwidth is not explored.

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: Technically sound in its use of kernel mean embeddings and constrained assignment; however, the optimization is under-specified and no theoretical guarantees are provided (e.g., on consistency of domain recovery or generalization). Empirical support is promising but would benefit from fuller baselines, runtime/scalability data, and ablations (LPC, bandwidth, without DWCV).
- Clarity: Generally well-written and organized; motivations and high-level ideas are clear. Some notation and optimization details need clarification to enable reproduction.
- Originality: The combination—MMD-based domain discovery with class-prior constraints and a learnability-based K selection, plus conditional test reshaping—is novel relative to prior latent-domain work using parametric mixtures.
- Significance: Addressing dataset-level bias by discovering latent domains is important and likely to influence practice. The demonstrated improvements on standard benchmarks suggest potential impact, especially if optimization and assumptions are clarified.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real and under-served problem with a principled nonparametric approach.
- Empirically improves adaptation over strong and relevant baselines.
- Conceptually simple and potentially applicable beyond the evaluated settings.
- Test-set reshaping is a useful and underexplored addition.

Cons for acceptance
- Optimization tractability and scalability are unclear; limited guidance for practitioners.
- Strong LPC assumption and reliance on labels; limited analysis of when it breaks.
- Missing ablations and omitted baselines reduce confidence in generality.
- No theoretical analysis of when maximizing MMD leads to better transfer.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Specify the solver, initialization strategy, and computational complexity; provide runtime and memory usage for each dataset.
- Provide ablations: without LPC; with learned or domain-specific priors; sensitivity to δ and kernel bandwidth; without DWCV (fixed K).
- Include KMeans/random partition results and at least one additional adaptation method to test generality.
- Clarify Eq. (1) notation and the derivation/intuition for the constraint 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C.
- Discuss robustness when class priors vary across domains, and potential extensions (e.g., soft priors or entropic regularization).

Recommendation: Weak accept
The problem is important and the approach is original and promising, with supportive empirical results. The paper would be strengthened by clearer optimization details, ablations, and discussion of assumptions; if addressed, it would make a valuable NeurIPS contribution.
