## NIPS Review: Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a crucial but often overlooked aspect of domain adaptation (DA) in computer vision: the inherent structure *within* datasets. The authors argue that standard datasets are often ad-hoc collections of sub-domains, and treating them as monolithic entities can hinder adaptation performance. They propose a novel unsupervised learning approach to automatically “reshape” datasets into more characteristic domains, guided by the principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.  The approach leverages kernel methods for measuring domain discrepancies and cross-validation to determine the optimal number of domains.  Empirical evaluation on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks demonstrates the effectiveness of the method, showing improved classification results when adapting classifiers learned on the discovered domains.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader DA landscape, referencing key works in both theoretical foundations ([7, 8, 9, 10]) and application areas ([1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]).  The discussion of existing DA approaches primarily focusing on *adaptation* rather than *domain definition* is well-articulated. The comparison to Hoffman et al. [20] is appropriately deferred to section 3 (though a slightly more detailed initial comparison in the introduction would be helpful).  The connections to generalized clustering [27] and multi-source DA [28, 29, 10] are also relevant and properly highlighted. The authors acknowledge and appropriately situate their work within this existing body of research.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper addresses a fundamental, yet under-explored issue in visual DA: how to properly define domains when they aren’t readily apparent. The framing of this as a dataset reshaping problem is insightful.
* **Principled Approach:**  The proposed method is grounded in well-defined principles—maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability—which provide a clear rationale for the approach.  
* **Non-Parametric Formulation:**  The use of nonparametric methods avoids the limitations of assuming specific distributions, making the approach more flexible and potentially applicable to a wider range of datasets.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on both object recognition and human activity recognition consistently demonstrate the benefits of reshaping datasets into discovered domains. The comparison to existing methods (including Hoffman et al. [20]) and ablation studies (reshaping training *and* testing sets) are particularly convincing.
* **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical derivations are clear and the experimental setup is well-documented.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the paper acknowledges the NP-hard nature of the optimization problem and proposes a relaxation, the computational cost of solving the continuous optimization could be a limiting factor for very large datasets.  A more detailed discussion of the scalability of the method, including runtime analysis for different dataset sizes, would be valuable.
* **Kernel Choice Sensitivity:**  The performance of the method relies heavily on the choice of kernel and bandwidth. While the authors justify their choice of Gaussian kernel, a more comprehensive investigation of the sensitivity to kernel parameters would strengthen the robustness claims.  
* **Limited Analysis of Domain Characteristics:** While Figure 1 provides some qualitative insights into the identified domains, a more systematic analysis of the factors that drive domain discovery would be beneficial. For example, quantifying the contribution of pose, illumination, or background to domain separation.  
* **DWCV can be Expensive:** The domain-wise cross validation is a potentially expensive procedure. Further investigation into more efficient approximations of K might be useful.
* **LPC Assumption:** The Label Prior Constraint (LPC), while intuitively reasonable, is still an assumption. It is unclear how sensitive the algorithm is to violations of this assumption in practice.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and largely overlooked problem in domain adaptation.
* The proposed approach is novel, principled, and well-motivated.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity could be a limitation for large-scale applications.
* Sensitivity to kernel parameters requires careful consideration.
* A more in-depth analysis of domain characteristics would be valuable.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The authors have identified a critical issue, proposed a novel and principled solution, and demonstrated its effectiveness through rigorous experiments. While there are some minor weaknesses related to computational complexity and parameter sensitivity, these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I believe this paper is a good fit for NIPS and recommend acceptance. 

**Rating:**  8.5/10 (Strong Accept)

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the method.
* Investigate the sensitivity to kernel parameters and consider alternative kernel choices.
* Conduct a more systematic analysis of the factors that drive domain discovery (e.g., pose, illumination).
* Discuss the robustness of the approach to violations of the LPC assumption.
*  Consider more efficient approximations to the DWCV process for K selection.
