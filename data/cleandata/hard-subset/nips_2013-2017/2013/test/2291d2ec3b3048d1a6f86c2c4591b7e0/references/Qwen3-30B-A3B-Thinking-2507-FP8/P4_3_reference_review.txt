

**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper addresses a critical gap in domain adaptation research: the common practice of treating datasets as single domains when they may actually contain multiple latent domains (e.g., different lighting conditions, camera angles). The authors propose a method to automatically reshape datasets into latent domains with two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains are maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains contain sufficient data to train strong classifiers). The approach uses kernel-based methods to measure domain differences and introduces a continuous relaxation of the optimization problem. The authors also propose domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to determine the optimal number of domains. The method is evaluated on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human action recognition (IXMAS) datasets, showing significant improvements over using the original datasets or domains discovered by prior work.

**Strengths**

*Quality*: The paper presents a technically sound approach with well-motivated mathematical formulation. The optimization problem is clearly defined with a continuous relaxation that makes it computationally feasible. The DWCV method for determining the number of domains is a thoughtful addition that addresses a key challenge in the problem. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, with results on multiple datasets and tasks demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines.

*Clarity*: The paper is well-organized with clear explanations of key concepts (maximum distinctiveness, maximum learnability). The mathematical formulation is presented with sufficient detail, and the experimental results are clearly presented with tables and figures. The authors effectively contrast their approach with related work, particularly [20], highlighting the distinction between their nonparametric approach and the parametric mixture model used in prior work.

*Originality*: The paper identifies a meaningful gap in the literature and proposes a novel solution. The focus on reshaping datasets into latent domains rather than assuming domains are given represents a significant conceptual shift from previous domain adaptation work. While related to [20], the authors' nonparametric approach is distinct from the parametric mixture model used in prior work, and the DWCV method for determining the number of domains is novel.

*Significance*: The problem addressed has clear practical importance for domain adaptation research, as it impacts how we design and evaluate domain adaptation methods. The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements in recognition accuracy across multiple datasets and tasks, suggesting potential for real-world impact. The approach could also influence future dataset construction practices.

**Weaknesses**

*The paper omits detailed comparison to other clustering methods beyond K-means and random partition. While the authors mention experimenting with these methods, they omit the results, which would strengthen their argument for the superiority of their approach.*

*The discussion of computational complexity is limited. For large-scale applications, the computational requirements of the proposed method might be a significant concern, but the paper does not provide a thorough analysis of this aspect.*

*The paper could provide more detailed discussion of the limitations of their approach, particularly in cases where the number of domains is very large relative to the number of examples per domain.*

**Arguments for Acceptance**

- The paper identifies a meaningful gap in domain adaptation research and proposes a novel, technically sound solution.
- The approach is well-motivated and supported by comprehensive empirical evidence across multiple datasets and tasks.
- The DWCV method for determining the number of domains is a thoughtful contribution that addresses a key challenge.
- The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from prior work, particularly [20], and makes a strong case for the superiority of their nonparametric method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance**

- The omission of comparison results with other clustering methods weakens the empirical argument.
- The limited discussion of computational complexity might be a concern for practical applications.
- The paper could provide more detailed analysis of failure cases and limitations.

**Overall Assessment**

The paper makes a significant contribution to domain adaptation research by addressing the problem of treating datasets as single domains when they may contain multiple latent domains. The proposed approach is technically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated on multiple datasets and tasks. The paper is clearly written and provides a good comparison to related work. While the paper has some minor weaknesses regarding omitted comparisons and computational complexity, these do not significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The problem addressed is important, the solution is novel, and the empirical results are compelling.

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission: it is technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant. I recommend acceptance.
