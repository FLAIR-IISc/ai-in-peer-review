Summary
The paper tackles the important but under-explored problem of discovering latent visual domains within standard datasets to improve domain adaptation. The authors argue that equating datasets with domains is flawed because datasets often aggregate multiple, heterogeneous “domains” (e.g., different poses, illuminations). They propose to reshape datasets into latent domains by optimizing two axioms: (1) maximum distinctiveness—maximize pairwise discrepancies between domain marginal distributions using kernel mean embeddings (MMD) in RKHS—and (2) maximum learnability—choose the number of domains K via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV), favoring partitions that yield classifiers with strong cross-validated accuracy within each domain. The distinctiveness problem is cast as an integer program with a label prior constraint (LPC) to prevent class-proportion shifts across domains; it is relaxed to a continuous quadratic program. They then use the discovered domains in standard adaptation pipelines (e.g., GFK) and also propose “conditional reshaping” of heterogeneous test data to match training domains via a convex QP that minimizes distributional differences. Experiments on Caltech/Amazon/DSLR/Webcam and IXMAS actions suggest gains over using original datasets and over Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12) latent domain discovery.

Quality
- Soundness of the core ideas: Using kernel mean embeddings to measure domain discrepancy (MMD) is principled and well-established. Summing pairwise discrepancies to encourage separation is a reasonable surrogate for “distinctiveness.” The DWCV criterion for selecting K explicitly addresses the trivial increase of distinctiveness with larger K by penalizing domains that cannot support accurate classifiers. This coupling of discrepancy and learnability is a compelling design.
- Assumptions and constraints: The LPC (domain-wise class proportions match the global prior up to δ) is critical to avoid partitions that simply separate classes and inflate distinctiveness. However, it is a strong assumption; real domains often do have label skew. The paper partially justifies LPC via data collection intuition, but this may not hold broadly, and the method’s success could depend on it.
- Optimization and reproducibility: The relaxed problem maximizes a convex quadratic subject to linear constraints—this is non-convex and NP-hard in general. The paper acknowledges this and claims off-the-shelf solvers suffice, but offers no guarantees, convergence diagnostics, or runtime/memory complexity. Given the dependence on an M×M kernel matrix, scalability beyond the reported datasets is unclear. More detail (solver, initialization, stopping) would improve confidence and reproducibility.
- Empirical evaluation: The experiments are well-chosen and show consistent gains. The “oracle” metric (best source per target domain, requiring target labels) is clearly labeled as an upper bound. Practical multi-source strategies (UNION, ENSEMBLE, MATCHING) show that discovered domains can approach oracle performance without target labels. Conditional reshaping of test sets is a nice extension in the IXMAS setting. However, ablations are limited: the paper mentions KMeans/random partitions but omits results; there is little sensitivity analysis to kernel bandwidth, δ, or the impact of removing LPC. Comparisons are primarily to Hoffman et al. [20]; broader baselines (e.g., kernel k-means on features, subspace clustering, or domain-invariant representation learning) would strengthen the case. Statistical significance is not discussed.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well-motivated, with intuitive examples and qualitative visualizations. However, some notation and constraints merit clarification:
  - The relaxation introduces βmk = zmk/Mk, but the first constraint 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C (per m) is nonstandard and its derivation from Mk ≥ C is not obvious. It would help to show the steps leading from integer constraints to these linear bounds and to discuss feasibility when some classes are absent per domain.
  - The role and selection of δ in LPC, and how sensitive results are to δ, deserve more detail.
  - Details of conditional reshaping are deferred to the supplement; a brief formulation in the main text would aid readers.
- Reproducibility would benefit from reporting solver details, complexity, and providing code.

Originality
- Discovering latent domains using a nonparametric discrepancy (MMD) under label-prior constraints, and selecting K by domain-wise learnability, is a novel combination that addresses clear failure modes of naive clustering. The work is distinct from Hoffman et al. (parametric mixture model) and from generalized clustering by kernel embeddings by leveraging supervision and an explicit learnability criterion. The conditional test-set reshaping matched to discovered training domains is also a useful new angle.

Significance
- The paper addresses a practical and impactful problem in domain adaptation: moving beyond dataset-as-domain. If robust and generalizable, the approach could inform how practitioners preprocess source data and handle heterogeneous test data. The improvements on standard benchmarks indicate potential for adoption. That said, reliance on LPC and labels, optimization non-convexity, and limited scalability discussion somewhat temper the immediate applicability to larger, diverse settings.

Pros
- Clear, important problem; well-motivated with concrete vision examples.
- Principled use of MMD for distinctiveness and a sensible, supervised learnability criterion for K.
- Demonstrated gains over original datasets and a prior latent-domain method across two tasks.
- Insightful qualitative analyses and the idea of conditional test reshaping.

Cons
- Strong LPC assumption; unclear robustness when label proportions legitimately vary across domains.
- Non-convex optimization with limited details; potential scalability issues (O(M^2) kernel).
- Limited ablations and baseline breadth; omitted KMeans/random results; no significance testing.
- Some notational/derivational ambiguities in the relaxation constraints.
- Heavy reliance on labels (both in LPC and DWCV); method is supervised latent domain discovery.

Questions for authors
- How sensitive are results to δ in LPC and to kernel bandwidth? What happens when LPC is relaxed or removed?
- Can you clarify and derive the constraint 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C, and discuss feasibility when some classes are absent in a domain?
- What solver/initialization do you use for the QP, and what are the time/memory costs for your largest experiments?
- How does performance change with different adaptation backbones (beyond GFK)?
- In settings where true domain label priors differ, does the method degrade gracefully?

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a timely and interesting approach with solid empirical indications of benefit and a clear conceptual contribution. Addressing the optimization details, providing stronger ablations, and discussing robustness to LPC violations would strengthen it further.
