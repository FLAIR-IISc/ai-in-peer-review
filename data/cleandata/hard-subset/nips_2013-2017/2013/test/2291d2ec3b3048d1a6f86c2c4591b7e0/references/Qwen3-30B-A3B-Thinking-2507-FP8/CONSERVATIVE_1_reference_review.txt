

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper proposes an approach to automatically discover latent domains within visual datasets for domain adaptation, arguing that standard datasets often contain multiple underlying domains that are not properly separated. The authors define two key properties for good domains: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains should contain sufficient data for strong classification). They propose a nonparametric method using kernel-based distribution differences to maximize distinctiveness and a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure to determine the optimal number of domains.

The paper makes a valid point that visual datasets often contain multiple domains that are not properly identified in standard domain adaptation research, where datasets are frequently treated as single domains. This is a recognized issue in the literature, as acknowledged by the authors in their introduction. However, the proposed solution appears to be a straightforward extension of standard clustering techniques rather than a novel methodological contribution.

The key concerns with this work are:

1. **Lack of substantial novelty**: The approach bears strong resemblance to discriminative clustering methods that have been studied extensively in the literature. The "maximum distinctiveness" criterion is similar to the "discriminability" criterion in Jegelka et al. [27], and their DWCV method for determining K is essentially a heuristic that has been used in clustering before. The authors fail to convincingly distinguish their approach from standard clustering techniques that have been shown to work well in similar settings.

2. **Insufficient theoretical justification**: The paper lacks strong theoretical guarantees for their method. The relaxation to continuous optimization is well-motivated, but the paper doesn't address whether the relaxation preserves the optimality of the original integer programming formulation. The DWCV procedure for determining K is presented as an empirical heuristic without theoretical justification.

3. **Limited empirical evidence**: The experimental results show modest improvements over baselines (typically 1-3% accuracy gains), but the paper doesn't compare against the most recent and strongest domain adaptation baselines. For example, the paper doesn't include comparisons to state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods like DANN, MMD, or CORAL, which are standard in the field. The improvements over [20] are not particularly impressive, as [20] is described as "the first and only work" in this area.

4. **Questionable practical utility**: The paper claims their approach "outperforms not only the original datasets but also the domains discovered by [20]," but the practical impact appears limited. The improvements are marginal, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how their approach would be substantially more useful in real-world applications than existing domain adaptation techniques.

5. **Insufficient comparison with established methods**: The paper compares against "K-means or random partition" but doesn't compare against standard clustering techniques that might be more appropriate for this problem, such as spectral clustering or density-based clustering. The paper also doesn't address how their method would perform when applied to datasets that don't contain multiple domains.

The paper is well-written and organized, and the problem statement is clear. However, as a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the contribution to be incremental at best. The authors present a method that appears to be a minor variation of existing clustering techniques with limited empirical evidence of significant improvement over standard approaches.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Providing a more thorough comparison against standard clustering techniques and domain adaptation methods
2. Demonstrating substantial improvements over existing approaches
3. Providing theoretical justification for their DWCV procedure
4. Addressing potential limitations of their approach (e.g., sensitivity to kernel choice, computational complexity)

In summary, while the paper identifies a valid problem in domain adaptation research, the proposed solution lacks sufficient novelty, theoretical grounding, and empirical evidence to justify its publication at NIPS. The contribution appears to be more of an engineering improvement rather than a fundamental methodological advance. The marginal improvements reported in the experiments do not sufficiently demonstrate a significant advance over the state of the art.

Given these concerns, I recommend rejection of this paper. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their contribution by providing more compelling evidence of novelty and significant improvement over existing methods before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
