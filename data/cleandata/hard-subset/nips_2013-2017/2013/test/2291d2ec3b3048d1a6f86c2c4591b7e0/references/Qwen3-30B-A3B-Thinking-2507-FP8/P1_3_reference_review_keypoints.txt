- Problem: Visual datasets often contain multiple latent domains (e.g., camera angle, lighting), and treating a dataset as a single domain harms domain adaptation.

- Proposal: Automatically reshape datasets into latent domains using two axioms—maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability—measured via kernel-based RKHS distances.

- Methodology: Use a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to select the number of domains and relax the optimization to a convex quadratic program; nonparametric approach contrasts with parametric Gaussian-mixture methods like [20].

- Empirical results: Significant improvements in domain adaptation for object recognition and human action recognition versus treating datasets as single domains and versus a related method [20], with comprehensive baselines (K-means, random).

- Strength — Technical soundness: Rigorous mathematical justification, appropriate kernel-based distance, convex relaxation, and well-motivated DWCV validated experimentally.

- Strength — Originality: Novel conceptual contribution identifying datasets ≠ domains and proposing two axiomatic properties and a new DWCV procedure.

- Strength — Significance: Practical relevance for real-world domain adaptation; potential to influence dataset construction and annotation.

- Strength — Clarity: Well-organized, clearly written, with good notation, experimental descriptions, and illustrative figures.

- Weakness — DWCV limitations: Paper does not fully explore DWCV’s limits (e.g., when optimal K is much larger than number of datasets or Kmax is restrictive).

- Weakness — Limited comparisons: Lacks experiments showing how reshaped domains perform when combined with state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods.

- Weakness — Missing factor analysis: No systematic quantification of how identified domains correspond to underlying factors (pose, illumination, background).

- Overall assessment: Valuable, novel, technically sound work with compelling empirical validation; merits acceptance.

- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses fundamental gap, theoretically sound and novel approach, strong empirical gains, practical utility, and thorough presentation.

- Arguments against acceptance: Needs deeper DWCV theory, more comparisons with SOTA when using reshaped domains, and systematic analysis of identified domain factors.

- Recommendation: Accept — the paper advances domain adaptation by focusing on domain discovery within datasets and is well-suited for NIPS.
