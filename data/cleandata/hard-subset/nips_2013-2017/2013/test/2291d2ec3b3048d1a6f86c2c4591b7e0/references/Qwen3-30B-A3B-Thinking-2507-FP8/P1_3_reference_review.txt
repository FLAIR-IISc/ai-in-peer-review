

# Peer Review: "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental yet overlooked problem in domain adaptation: the assumption that entire datasets constitute single, well-defined domains. The authors argue that visual datasets often contain multiple latent domains (e.g., different camera angles, lighting conditions) that are not explicitly labeled, and treating datasets as single domains leads to suboptimal adaptation performance. They propose a novel approach to automatically reshape datasets into latent domains based on two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains should support strong discriminative models). Their method uses kernel-based distribution distance measures and a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure to determine the optimal number of domains. The authors demonstrate significant improvements in domain adaptation performance across object recognition and human action recognition tasks compared to treating datasets as single domains or using a related method [20].

## Strengths

### Quality (Strong)
The paper is technically sound with clear theoretical justification. The authors provide a rigorous mathematical formulation for measuring domain distinctiveness using kernel-based RKHS distance, which is well-supported by prior work [21]. The DWCV procedure for determining the number of domains is well-motivated and properly validated through experiments. The optimization problem is appropriately relaxed to a convex quadratic program, making it computationally tractable. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets and tasks, with appropriate baselines (including K-means and random partitioning, which fail to produce competitive results).

### Originality (Strong)
This work makes a significant conceptual contribution by identifying a critical gap in domain adaptation research: the problematic practice of equating datasets with domains. The two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) are novel and well-motivated. Unlike [20], which assumes a parametric Gaussian mixture model, their nonparametric approach is more flexible and better suited to visual data. The DWCV procedure for determining the optimal number of domains represents a new contribution to the field.

### Significance (Strong)
The problem addressed is highly relevant to real-world domain adaptation, where datasets often contain multiple domains that are not explicitly labeled. The authors demonstrate that reshaping datasets into latent domains significantly improves adaptation performance, as shown in their experiments (Tables 1-3). This work could influence how future datasets are constructed and annotated, and provides a practical tool for improving domain adaptation performance across various visual recognition tasks.

### Clarity (Good)
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement is effectively motivated with concrete examples (e.g., different camera angles in action recognition). The technical details are presented with appropriate mathematical notation. The experimental setup is well-described, and the figures help illustrate the domain identification process. The paper effectively contrasts their approach with related work [20], highlighting key differences.

## Weaknesses

### Limitations of DWCV
While the DWCV procedure is well-motivated, the paper doesn't fully explore its limitations. For example, it's unclear how well the procedure works when the optimal number of domains is much larger than the number of datasets (as suggested by the experimental results where DWCV selects K equal to the number of datasets). The paper mentions a practical bound for Kmax, but doesn't explore cases where this bound might be too restrictive.

### Limited Comparison with Domain Adaptation Methods
The paper focuses on whether reshaping datasets improves adaptation performance, but doesn't demonstrate how their reshaping approach compares to state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods when combined with reshaping. For instance, it would be valuable to see if their reshaped domains, when used with methods like [4], outperform those methods when applied directly to the original datasets.

### Factor Analysis
The paper shows examples of identified domains (Figure 1) but doesn't provide a systematic analysis of how these domains correspond to the underlying factors (e.g., pose, illumination, background). While they mention these factors, they don't quantify the relationship between the identified domains and the factors, which would strengthen their claim about what constitutes "characteristic domains."

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to domain adaptation by addressing a critical yet overlooked problem: the assumption that datasets constitute single domains. The authors propose a novel approach based on maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability, with sound theoretical grounding and compelling empirical results. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a significant problem in the field.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Identifies and addresses a fundamental gap in domain adaptation research
- Proposes a novel, theoretically sound approach with clear axiomatic properties
- Demonstrates significant performance improvements over treating datasets as single domains
- Provides a practical tool that could improve domain adaptation performance across various applications
- Well-motivated and clearly presented with thorough experimental validation

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- DWCV procedure could benefit from deeper theoretical justification
- Limited comparison with state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods when using reshaped domains
- Lacks systematic analysis of how identified domains relate to underlying factors (pose, illumination, etc.)

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant conceptual contribution to domain adaptation by identifying a critical problem (treating datasets as single domains) and providing a novel solution. The results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the significance of the work is clear. While there are some limitations (particularly regarding the DWCV procedure and comparison with domain adaptation methods), these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper advances the field in a demonstrable way and will be of interest to the NIPS community.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it presents a novel theoretical approach with strong empirical validation, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning with clear practical implications. The work is significantly different from previous NIPS papers on domain adaptation, particularly in its focus on domain discovery within datasets rather than adaptation between labeled domains.
