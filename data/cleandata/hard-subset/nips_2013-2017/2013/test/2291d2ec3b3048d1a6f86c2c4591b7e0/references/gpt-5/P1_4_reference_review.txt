Summary
The paper addresses a key but underexplored question in domain adaptation: datasets often conflate multiple latent domains arising from nuisance factors (pose, illumination, background, etc.), and treating a dataset as a single domain can harm adaptation. The authors propose to “reshape” datasets into multiple latent domains that are (1) maximally distinctive in distribution and (2) maximally learnable for building discriminative models. For a fixed K, they define domains by maximizing total domain distinctiveness: a sum of pairwise squared distances between RKHS mean embeddings (i.e., MMD) of the empirical distributions, computed with a characteristic kernel. To avoid trivial per-category clustering, they impose a label prior constraint (LPC) that approximately matches the class-prior distribution in each domain to the global prior. Because the resulting integer program is intractable, they relax to a continuous program over βmk=zmk/Mk and solve a quadratic program with linear constraints. To select K, they propose domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV): split data into K, train classifiers within each domain, and choose K maximizing a weighted average of cross-validated accuracies across domains. They further introduce “conditional reshaping” of test sets, partitioning unlabeled test data into domains that best match the discovered training domains via minimizing kernel discrepancy. Experiments on object recognition (Amazon, DSLR, Webcam, Caltech-256) with GFK adaptation and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) suggest that (i) discovered domains outperform the original dataset partitions and a latent domain mixture model [Hoffman et al., ECCV’12], and (ii) conditional test reshaping further improves performance.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated in domain adaptation (Saenko et al., GFK, TCA) and landmarks (Gong et al.). The distinctiveness criterion uses MMD (Gretton et al.) and relates to generalized clustering via kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.). It contrasts with Hoffman et al.’s parametric Gaussian-mixture-based latent domain discovery by using nonparametric discrepancies and an explicit learnability criterion to pick K. The multi-source adaptation angle is in line with Mansour et al. and others.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The use of characteristic kernel embeddings to measure domain gaps is well-founded; MMD is zero iff distributions match. The DWCV criterion is reasonable and operational. However, the optimization is nonconvex (maximize a convex quadratic under linear constraints), and the paper relies on off-the-shelf solvers without guarantees or much detail (initialization, convergence behavior, runtime). The LPC assumption (per-domain class priors ≈ global prior) is strong; in many realistic settings label shift is a defining property of domains, and the constraint could bias the discovered domains. The method may produce highly unbalanced domains; apart from an implicit lower bound via “at least one per class,” there is no explicit balance or minimum-size constraint. Sensitivity to kernel choice/bandwidth and to the δ tolerance in LPC is not analyzed.
- Empirics: The chosen benchmarks are appropriate; the “oracle” analysis (max over sources for each target) is informative, and the conditional test reshaping experiment on IXMAS is compelling. That said, reported results rely on descriptions; precise numbers, variance across splits, and significance tests are not provided here. Comparisons to simpler baselines (kernel k-means/spectral clustering with LPC; random Fourier features for scalability) are claimed poor but omitted; including them quantitatively would strengthen the case. It would help to show that gains are not tied to GFK by repeating with TCA or KMM-based weighting.
- Reproducibility: Important details are missing: solver choice, initialization strategy, stopping criteria, and computational complexity. An O(M^2) kernel matrix and a QP over M×K variables will not scale; no approximations (Nyström/RFF, mini-batching) are discussed.

Clarity
Overall the paper is clearly written and well-motivated, with intuitive examples and useful qualitative analyses. Some notation and constraints deserve clarification:
- The constraint 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C arises from Mk∈[C,M]; this should be derived explicitly to avoid confusion.
- The default requirement “at least one instance per category per domain” is very restrictive for larger C and may force poor partitions; discuss practical handling.
- Provide pseudocode for both training reshaping and conditional test reshaping, and move key details from the supplement into the main text.
- Discuss computational complexity and practical runtimes.

Originality
Discovering latent domains nonparametrically, decoupling distinctiveness (distributional) from learnability (predictive), and applying conditional reshaping of test sets are original and useful contributions. The MMD-based objective and kernel-embedding perspective relate to prior generalized clustering, but the combination with label-aware constraints tailored to domain discovery and the DWCV model selection is novel relative to [20]. The paper appropriately cites closely related work.

Significance
This work addresses a pervasive issue: datasets are heterogeneous mixtures of latent domains. If robust and scalable, the proposed reshaping could become a standard pre-processing for multi-source adaptation and evaluation, potentially influencing dataset construction. The empirical gains on standard benchmarks suggest practical impact. The lack of scalability analysis and dependence on LPC temper the immediate applicability to larger, more modern datasets.

Pros
- Clear motivation; tackles an important, underexplored problem.
- Well-grounded use of kernel mean embeddings for domain discrepancy.
- Pragmatic and interpretable selection of K via learnability.
- Demonstrates benefits of both training reshaping and conditional test reshaping.
- Improves over prior latent-domain discovery (parametric mixtures).

Cons
- Nonconvex QP without algorithmic details; unclear robustness and runtime.
- Strong LPC assumption; no study of violations (label shift).
- Potential for unbalanced/small domains; no explicit regularization for balance or compactness.
- Limited ablations: kernel/bandwidth sensitivity, adaptation method dependence, and missing quantitative baselines for simple clustering alternatives.
- Scalability to large M not addressed.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide algorithmic details (initialization, solver, runtime) and complexity; consider scalable approximations (Nyström/RFF) and report their effect.
- Add explicit balance constraints or regularization (e.g., lower bounds on Mk, entropy penalties) and study their impact.
- Probe robustness to LPC violations by simulating label shift; consider class-conditional MMD to enforce distinctiveness within classes.
- Conduct sensitivity analysis to kernel bandwidth and δ; explore multi-kernel learning.
- Include quantitative baselines: kernel k-means/spectral clustering with and without LPC, and “mixture of experts” clustering on features.
- Report results with alternative adaptation methods (e.g., TCA, KMM reweighting) to show generality.
- Clarify whether any target data statistics used for matching are also used in selecting K (they should not be).

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The problem is important and the approach is promising and conceptually sound, with encouraging results. However, the paper would benefit from stronger experimental ablations, clarity on optimization and scalability, and an analysis of the LPC assumption. Addressing these points would make the contribution notably stronger. Confidence: medium.
