### Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

**Summary**

This paper addresses a fundamental, yet often overlooked, issue in visual domain adaptation: the assumption that a given dataset represents a single, coherent domain. The authors argue that standard datasets are often heterogeneous mixtures of multiple latent domains (e.g., varying illumination, pose, background). Treating such a dataset as a single source domain can hinder adaptation performance.

The core contribution is a novel unsupervised method to "reshape" one or more source datasets into a set of more coherent, latent domains. The proposed approach is guided by two principles:
1.  **Maximum Distinctiveness:** The discovered domains should be as statistically different from one another as possible. This is formulated as an optimization problem that maximizes the sum of pairwise distances between domain distributions, measured by a kernel-based two-sample test statistic (specifically, the squared Maximum Mean Discrepancy). A crucial Label Prior Constraint (LPC) is introduced to prevent the trivial solution of clustering by class.
2.  **Maximum Learnability:** The discovered domains must be internally consistent enough to train a strong classifier. This principle is used to determine the optimal number of domains, K. The authors propose a "Domain-wise Cross-Validation" (DWCV) scheme, selecting the K that maximizes the average cross-validation accuracy within the discovered domains.

The authors demonstrate experimentally on object and action recognition tasks that adapting from these discovered domains yields superior performance compared to adapting from the original, un-reshaped datasets. They also introduce a "conditional reshaping" method for test sets, further improving results.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)**

1.  **Important and Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper's premise—that datasets are not monolithic domains—is a critical and insightful observation. Challenging this common simplification is a significant conceptual contribution that could influence how the community approaches domain adaptation and dataset collection.
2.  **Novel and Principled Approach:** The formulation based on the dual principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability is elegant and intuitive. Using a non-parametric kernel method to measure distinctiveness avoids restrictive parametric assumptions about the data distribution (a key advantage over prior work like Hoffman et al. [20]). The DWCV method for model selection (choosing K) is a clever and practical solution to a difficult problem.
3.  **Technically Sound Formulation:** The optimization framework is well-defined. The inclusion of the Label Prior Constraint is a crucial and intelligent addition that forces the algorithm to find domain shifts independent of class labels, which is precisely the goal. While the relaxed optimization is still NP-hard, the formulation is standard and allows the use of existing solvers.
4.  **Convincing Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The "oracle" evaluation (GOURS) provides a clear upper bound and effectively demonstrates the quality of the discovered domains compared to the original datasets (GORIG). The subsequent experiments with practical multi-source adaptation methods (e.g., MATCHING) show that these benefits translate to real-world scenarios where target labels are unavailable. The results consistently show significant improvements, validating the paper's core claims.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Scalability Concerns:** The optimization involves a kernel matrix of size M x M, where M is the total number of training instances. This will be computationally prohibitive for large-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet scale). The paper states that "existing solvers are adequate" but does not discuss the computational complexity or runtime, which is a significant practical limitation. Addressing scalability, perhaps through landmark selection or other kernel approximations, would make the method more impactful.
2.  **Hardness of Optimization:** The paper correctly notes that the relaxed problem—maximizing a convex quadratic function over a polytope—is still NP-hard. While the authors found solvers to work well empirically, there is no guarantee of finding a global optimum. A discussion on the quality of the solutions found and the sensitivity to initialization (if any) would strengthen the paper.
3.  **Strong Assumption of the Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The LPC assumes that the class priors are constant across all latent domains. While this is a reasonable heuristic to prevent class-based clustering, it may not hold in all real-world scenarios. For example, certain object classes might naturally appear more frequently in specific domains (e.g., "boats" in "water backgrounds"). The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of this assumption and potential failure cases.
4.  **Clarity on Conditional Reshaping:** The details for the conditional reshaping of the test set are deferred to supplementary material. Given that this is a key component of the results in Table 3, a more detailed summary of the formulation in the main paper would improve its completeness.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high quality. The technical approach is sound and the claims are well-supported by extensive experiments. The work is complete and goes beyond a simple proposal by also addressing model selection (K) and extending the idea to reshaping the test set.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation, and the proposed method is described clearly. The notation is consistent. Figure 1 provides helpful qualitative insight into the discovered domains.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While related to latent domain discovery (Hoffman et al. [20]) and generalized clustering (Jegelka et al. [27]), the specific formulation combining non-parametric distinctiveness with a learnability criterion and the LPC is novel. It moves beyond the standard "source-target" paradigm in a meaningful way.
*   **Significance:** The work is highly significant. It has the potential to shift the methodology of domain adaptation research from a dataset-centric view to a more fine-grained, domain-centric one. The ideas could also inform future dataset collection efforts by providing tools to analyze the latent structure of existing datasets. Researchers in domain adaptation are very likely to build upon these ideas.

**Final Recommendation: Accept**

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel, well-motivated, and effective solution to an important problem in domain adaptation. The conceptual shift from "datasets as domains" to discovering latent domains is significant. The technical approach is sound, and the experimental results are convincing. Despite some concerns about scalability and the strength of the LPC assumption, the paper's strengths in originality, significance, and quality far outweigh its weaknesses. It is a solid contribution that will be of great interest to the NIPS community.
