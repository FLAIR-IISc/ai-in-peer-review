Summary
The paper tackles latent domain discovery for visual recognition. The premise is that common visual datasets are heterogeneous and conflating them as single domains harms adaptation. The authors propose to reshape datasets into latent domains using two principles: (1) maximum distinctiveness, operationalized as maximizing pairwise discrepancies between domain marginals P(x) via kernel mean embeddings (MMD); and (2) maximum learnability, used to select the number of domains K via a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) criterion that favors partitions supporting higher supervised accuracy. The combinatorial assignment is relaxed to continuous variables β with linear constraints, including a “label prior constraint” (LPC) that enforces per-domain label proportions to match the global prior (with tolerance). The approach is evaluated on Office-like object datasets (A, D, W, C) and IXMAS cross-view actions, and extended to “conditional reshaping” of unlabeled test data to match discovered training domains. Results (tables/figures referenced but not included) are reported against original datasets and Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12).

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and under-explored question: what constitutes visual domains, and how to discover them from heterogeneous datasets without explicit factors.
- Use of kernel mean embeddings to measure distribution gaps is principled; Gaussian MMD is a reasonable, nonparametric choice.
- The learnability-based model selection principle (DWCV) is intuitive: domains should support strong supervised learning.
- Conditional reshaping of test sets to align with discovered training domains is a neat idea for scenarios with inherently heterogeneous targets (e.g., cross-view action recognition).
- Empirical narratives suggest discovered domains can outperform naive dataset-based domains and a parametric latent-domain baseline [Hoffman et al.].

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical/methodological:
- LPC assumption is strong and likely violated. Enforcing that per-domain label proportions match the global prior implicitly assumes label shift is zero across domains. In real visual data, class frequency often varies by domain (e.g., views, capture conditions). This constraint can (i) prevent discovering true domains; (ii) encourage per-class stratification; or (iii) dampen distinctiveness. An ablation without LPC and a sensitivity analysis to δ are needed. The choice δ=1% is arbitrary and unjustified.
- Confusion/inconsistency in constraints. The text states “every domain should have at least one instance per category, namely Mk ≥ C”, but Mk ≥ C does not guarantee at least one sample per category; it only constrains total domain size. Moreover, Eq. (5) imposes 1/M ≤ sum_k βmk ≤ 1/C per instance, derived from Mk ∈ [C, M], not from per-class coverage. This is both conceptually and mathematically inconsistent.
- Relaxation is underconstrained. With βk lying on the simplex for each k (sum_m βmk = 1), the per-instance constraint on sum_k βmk does not enforce a one-of-K assignment. The objective encourages separating βk globally but permits fractional assignments per instance. The post-hoc argmax over βmk may produce brittle assignments. There is no entropy or exclusivity regularizer, nor alternating assignment/centroid updates. Please clarify whether additional constraints/penalties were used in practice and provide initialization details; otherwise the relaxation can yield degenerate or highly fractional solutions.
- Objective doubling and notation issues. TDD sums d(k,k’) over all ordered pairs (k≠k’), apparently double-counting. If intentional, say so; otherwise include a 1/2 factor. The notation d(k,k’) overloads k both as kernel function k(·,·) and domain index k; also M′k appears once without definition. Eq. (1) mixes kernel letter k with domain index k in a confusing manner.
- Choice and tuning of kernel. Bandwidth fixed as twice the global median pairwise distance is ad hoc. Since the objective depends critically on the kernel, at minimum include sensitivity analysis and justification for a single global bandwidth across heterogeneous datasets.
- Scalability. The method requires forming the full M×M kernel matrix and solving a nonconvex quadratic program with K(K−1) pairwise terms. Complexity and memory are O(M^2) (per iteration). The paper lacks runtime, solver details, convergence behavior, and any approximation (e.g., Nyström, random features). “Off-the-shelf solvers are adequate” is not reproducible.
- DWCV and K selection. The bound Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C} is unjustified; there is no fundamental reason K ≤ C. DWCV uses supervised accuracy within discovered domains; smaller domains can inflate apparent accuracy via easier classification (risk of overfitting), even with weighting by Mk. What classifier is used, with what hyperparameters, and how is model selection nested with DWCV? What happens when A(2) ≤ prior? The procedure is under-specified.
- Avoiding per-class clusters. The paper claims naive clustering produces per-category domains; the proposed remedy relies primarily on LPC. More principled strategies (e.g., conditional MMD that minimizes divergence in P(x|y) or controls dependence between discovered domains and labels via HSIC) are not considered.
- Conditional reshaping of test data is relegated to the supplement. The main text asserts convexity and performance gains but does not specify the objective, constraints, or matching criterion. Critical details are missing for reproduction.

Empirical evaluation:
- Tables and figures are referenced but not shown here; no numerical results, standard deviations, or statistical tests are presented in the text. Even given space constraints, the main paper should contain at least one representative quantitative table with error bars.
- Baselines are incomplete/under-specified. You compare to [20] and naive K-means/random (omitted), but not to strong nonparametric alternatives (e.g., spectral clustering with kernel embeddings, mixture-of-experts with MMD regularization, conditional MMD-based partitioning). The “MATCHING” strategy appears in the text without being introduced in the multi-source list (only UNION and ENSEMBLE are defined).
- Data protocol lacks key details. For IXMAS, the subset selection (“keep the first five actions… randomly select 20 sequences…”) risks overlap or biased sampling. Specify train/test subjects, repeats, and ensure no leakage. For Office-style datasets, report M (per class), train/test splits, and whether hyperparameters were tuned using target labels (should not).
- GOURS/GORIG use oracle selection over source domains with target labels; that is fine as an upper bound, but the paper claims the unsupervised MATCHING “is able to match the oracle accuracies” without describing the matching criterion or providing numbers.

Clarity and presentation:
- Numerous typos and formatting issues: “the the difference,” “two-sampleproblem,” inconsistent capitalization, missing accents and author names. Many references lack years, venues, or pages; several entries are malformed. Please fix the bibliography.
- Notation is overloaded and occasionally incorrect (kernel vs domain index k, M′k undefined). Some claims are imprecise (Mk ≥ C “means” one per class).
- Important algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement (conditional reshaping) or omitted (solver, initialization), harming reproducibility.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Interesting and relevant problem formulation.
- Principled use of kernel embeddings for nonparametric domain discrepancy.
- Practical K selection idea and extension to test-time reshaping.

Cons:
- Methodological gaps (LPC assumption, relaxation/exclusivity, unspecified optimization).
- Underdeveloped and under-specified experimental protocol; missing quantitative details.
- Clarity/notation/reference issues throughout; several inconsistencies.
- Limited and partially undefined baselines; claims about matching oracle unsupported in the main text.
- Scalability and sensitivity not addressed.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a promising direction, but the current version has too many technical and presentation issues to meet NeurIPS standards. Strengthening the optimization formulation (with clearer constraints and exclusivity), justifying/ablating LPC, detailing and benchmarking the solver and scalability, clarifying K selection, and providing thorough, reproducible experiments with solid baselines and complete results would significantly improve the work.

Minor comments and suggestions
- Consider conditional MMD or class-conditional constraints to decouple domains from labels more cleanly than LPC.
- Add entropy or assignment penalties, or an alternating discrete-continuous optimization, to encourage near one-of-K assignments.
- Provide sensitivity to δ and kernel bandwidth; include runtime and memory usage; consider Nyström approximations.
- Fix notation and references; include at least one main-text quantitative table with error bars.
