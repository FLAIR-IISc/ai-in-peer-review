Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the problem that visual “domains” in practice are often conflated with datasets, even though a dataset may mix several latent domains arising from factors such as pose, illumination, or background. The authors propose to automatically “reshape” one or more annotated visual datasets into multiple latent domains that are better suited for domain adaptation.

The approach is guided by two principles:
- Maximum distinctiveness: domains should have maximally different marginal distributions Pk(x). This is operationalized by maximizing the sum of pairwise kernel mean discrepancies (MMD) between domain assignments in an RKHS (Gretton et al., NIPS’07).
- Maximum learnability: each domain should support learning a strong classifier. The number of domains K is selected via “domain-wise cross-validation” (DWCV), which trains a discriminative classifier within each discovered domain and chooses K to maximize a weighted average of per-domain cross-validated accuracies.

Formally, binary assignment variables zmk indicate which domain each instance belongs to, with an added “label prior constraint” (LPC) enforcing that the per-domain class proportions match the global class prior. The combinatorial optimization is relaxed by introducing βmk ≈ zmk/Mk and optimizing a quadratic objective ∑k≠k′(βk−βk′)⊤K(βk−βk′) with linear constraints, where K is the kernel matrix. Finally, the test set can also be “conditionally reshaped” to match discovered training domains by minimizing distribution discrepancies to training-domain kernel means; the authors state this reduces to a convex QP (details in the supplement).

Experiments on object recognition (Amazon/DSLR/Webcam/Caltech-256) and IXMAS multi-view action recognition show that: (i) discovered domains lead to higher “oracle” adaptation performance than treating each dataset as a domain and outperform the latent domain method of Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12) based on Gaussian mixtures; (ii) practical adaptation strategies using the discovered domains (e.g., matching a source domain to a target domain) achieve accuracies close to the oracle; and (iii) conditionally reshaping heterogeneous test sets improves cross-view action recognition.

The work is related to multi-source domain adaptation (e.g., Mansour et al., NIPS’09), two-sample/MMD methods (Gretton et al., NIPS’07), generalized clustering with kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al., 2009), and latent domain discovery (Hoffman et al., ECCV’12). The paper differentiates itself by a nonparametric discrepancy criterion and a learnability-driven selection of K, and by explicitly reshaping both training and test sets.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The use of MMD as a domain discrepancy measure is appropriate and nonparametric. The LPC helps prevent trivial partitions aligned with class labels. However:
  - The core optimization maximizes a convex quadratic function over a polytope, which is non-convex/NP-hard; the paper acknowledges this but provides few details on the solver, initialization, guarantees, or scalability. With a full kernel, memory/time is O(M^2), which may be limiting; no complexity analysis or runtime is given.
  - The assumption behind LPC (class priors are domain-invariant) is strong; in real visual datasets, priors can vary across latent domains. The effect of violating this assumption is not analyzed.
  - The DWCV criterion uses labels on the same data used to define domains, introducing a risk of circularity/overfitting in selecting K. No held-out validation set or stability analysis is reported.
  - The conditional reshaping of test sets is promising, but the main text omits the objective and constraints; the reader must consult the supplementary material to assess correctness.
- Empirical support: The experiments cover standard benchmarks and show consistent gains over baselines and a relevant prior method. However, the manuscript (as provided) does not include the actual tables/values, confidence intervals, or significance tests; ablations (e.g., without LPC, different kernels/bandwidths, sensitivity to δ) are missing. A comparison to simple constrained clustering baselines (e.g., K-means with per-domain class proportion constraints) would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and clearly motivates the need to disentangle datasets into latent domains. The problem setup and high-level algorithm are understandable. Some notational issues and omissions reduce reproducibility:
- Equation (1) has minor notational inconsistencies (e.g., M′k). It would help to state explicitly that d(k,k′) is the squared MMD.
- The derivation of the constraint 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C is glossed; a brief justification (stemming from 1/Mk with Mk ∈ [C, M]) would aid clarity.
- Critical solver details (optimizer type, initialization, convergence criteria), complexity, and hyperparameter sensitivity (kernel choice/bandwidth, δ) are missing from the main text.

Originality
The combination of (i) nonparametric, label-regularized domain discovery via MMD maximization, (ii) learnability-driven model selection for K, and (iii) conditional reshaping of test sets appears novel relative to prior latent domain discovery approaches that assume parametric mixtures (Hoffman et al., ECCV’12). The idea of enforcing class-prior parity as a soft constraint is a distinctive design choice. The paper also connects the discovered domains to downstream multi-source adaptation in a principled way.

Significance
If robust and scalable, the method addresses a real practical issue: datasets are heterogeneous, and treating them as a single domain can harm adaptation. The demonstrated improvements suggest impact for practitioners working with mixed-condition visual datasets and potentially beyond vision. The conditional reshaping of test sets for cross-view action recognition is a compelling application. Broader significance would be enhanced by theoretical connections (e.g., linking TDD and learnability to adaptation error bounds) and by scaling demonstrations.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem: discovering latent domains within datasets for better adaptation.
- Nonparametric discrepancy via MMD avoids restrictive distributional assumptions.
- Learnability-based selection of K is intuitive and practical.
- Empirical results on standard benchmarks indicate consistent gains over dataset-as-domain baselines and a prior latent-domain method.
- Conditional reshaping of test sets is a practical extension with measurable benefits.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Core optimization is non-convex with limited algorithmic details, no guarantees, and unclear scalability.
- Strong LPC assumption (domain-invariant class priors); no analysis of robustness when priors differ.
- Potential circularity/overfitting in DWCV; no stability or sensitivity analyses.
- Missing quantitative tables/significance tests and limited ablations; K-means or constrained clustering baselines are not reported.
- Theoretical justification linking the proposed criteria to target-domain performance is limited.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full optimization details, complexity/runtime, initialization strategy, and a discussion of scalability to larger M or approximate kernels (e.g., Nyström/Random Features).
- Analyze sensitivity to kernel bandwidth and δ, and include ablations removing LPC and varying constraints.
- Evaluate robustness when class priors differ across domains.
- Add comparisons to constrained clustering baselines and report statistical significance.
- If possible, relate TDD and A(K) to adaptation bounds (e.g., Ben-David et al., NIPS’07) to strengthen theoretical grounding.
- Include the explicit formulation for conditional test reshaping in the main paper.

Overall, the paper presents a compelling idea with promising empirical evidence but would benefit from stronger algorithmic detail and robustness analysis.
