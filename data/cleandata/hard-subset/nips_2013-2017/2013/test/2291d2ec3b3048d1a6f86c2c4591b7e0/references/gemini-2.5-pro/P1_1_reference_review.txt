### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a fundamental and often overlooked problem in visual domain adaptation: the assumption that a given dataset constitutes a single, coherent domain. The authors argue that standard datasets are often agglomerations of multiple latent domains (e.g., different viewpoints, lighting conditions) and that treating them as a single source domain is suboptimal. The core proposal is a novel, unsupervised method to "reshape" datasets by discovering these latent domains.

The proposed approach is built upon two key axiomatic properties that these latent domains should possess:
1.  **Maximum Distinctiveness:** The discovered domains should be maximally different from one another in their data distributions. This is formalized by maximizing the sum of pairwise distances between domain distributions, where the distance is measured using a non-parametric kernel-based metric (the Maximum Mean Discrepancy, MMD). To prevent the trivial solution of clustering by class, the authors introduce a crucial Label Prior Constraint (LPC), which forces the class distribution within each discovered domain to be similar to the overall class distribution.
2.  **Maximum Learnability:** The discovered domains must be useful for training strong classifiers. This principle is used to automatically determine the optimal number of latent domains, `K`. The authors propose a "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) procedure: for a range of `K`, they discover the domains and then compute the weighted average cross-validation accuracy of classifiers trained on each individual domain. The `K` that yields the highest accuracy is selected.

The authors formulate the distinctiveness maximization as an integer programming problem, then relax it to a continuous quadratic program that can be solved with existing tools. The effectiveness of the approach is demonstrated on object and action recognition tasks, showing that adapting from the discovered domains leads to significantly better performance than adapting from the original, monolithic datasets.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Important and Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper tackles a critical and conceptually important problem. The practice of equating datasets with domains is indeed a troubling ad-hoc convention in the domain adaptation literature. Challenging this assumption and providing a principled way to discover more meaningful domains is a significant contribution.

2.  **Elegant and Principled Formulation:** The two proposed properties—maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability—are intuitive and provide a solid conceptual foundation for the method. The use of a non-parametric kernel-based measure for distinctiveness is well-suited for complex visual data, avoiding restrictive parametric assumptions made by prior work like Hoffman et al. [20].

3.  **Technically Sound Approach:** The formulation of the optimization problem is sound. The introduction of the Label Prior Constraint (LPC) is a clever and crucial component that successfully steers the optimization away from simply rediscovering object classes, which is a major potential pitfall. The method for selecting the number of domains `K` via cross-validation (learnability) is also sensible and well-justified.

4.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experiments:** The experimental validation is thorough. The authors not only show improved performance on target domains but also wisely include an "oracle" evaluation (GOURS) that demonstrates the discovered domains have a higher potential for adaptation than the original datasets. The comparisons to baselines and the most relevant prior work [20] are strong. The extension to "conditional reshaping" of the test set is a nice addition that shows the versatility of the core idea. The qualitative analysis in Figure 1 provides compelling visual evidence that the discovered domains correspond to meaningful visual factors like background, pose, and color.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection):**

1.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** The primary weakness is the lack of discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method. The optimization involves a kernel matrix of size M x M, where M is the total number of training instances. This will be computationally prohibitive for large-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet scale). The paper mentions that "existing solvers are adequate" but does not specify which solver was used or discuss the runtime, which is a critical detail for reproducibility and practical application.

2.  **Limitations of the Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** While the LPC is a key strength, it is also a strong assumption. It presumes that the underlying class priors are independent of the domain-defining factors. This may not always hold. For instance, a "beach" domain is inherently more likely to contain "surfboards" than a "kitchen" domain. The paper would be stronger if it acknowledged and discussed the potential limitations of this assumption.

3.  **Clarity on the Optimization:** The paper states that the relaxed optimization problem is a maximization of a convex quadratic function, which is generally NP-hard. While solvers exist for such problems, a brief discussion of the specific type of solver used (e.g., global vs. local optimization) and the quality of the obtained solutions would have improved the paper's clarity and completeness. It is unclear if the solutions found are close to the global optimum.

### Detailed Comments

**Quality:** The paper is of high quality. The technical approach is sound, and the claims are well-supported by strong experimental results on multiple datasets. The work is a complete piece, addressing the discovery of domains, the selection of the number of domains, and their use in adaptation.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is articulated clearly in the introduction, and the proposed approach is described logically. The intuition behind the two core properties is easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While it leverages existing tools like kernel-based distribution measures, the problem formulation—reshaping datasets into latent domains for adaptation—is novel. It clearly distinguishes itself from the most related work [20] by being non-parametric. The two-axiom framework is a fresh perspective on the problem of what constitutes a "good" domain.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. It has the potential to change how researchers approach domain adaptation, encouraging a more critical view of the source data. The proposed method provides a practical tool for improving adaptation performance and could also be used as an analysis tool to better understand the internal structure and biases of existing datasets. The ideas are likely to be built upon by future research in domain adaptation and dataset analysis.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper addresses a significant and fundamental problem with a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound solution. The extensive and convincing experimental results strongly support the authors' claims. The weaknesses concerning scalability and the discussion of optimization are present but do not undermine the core contribution, and can be addressed in a final version.
