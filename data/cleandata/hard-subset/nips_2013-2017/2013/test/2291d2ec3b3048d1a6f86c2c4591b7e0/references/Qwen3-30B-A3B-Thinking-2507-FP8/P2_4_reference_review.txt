

# Review: "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental but overlooked problem in domain adaptation research: visual datasets are not naturally organized into discrete domains as they are in speech or language processing. The authors propose a method to automatically reshape datasets into characteristic domains based on two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains should allow for strong discriminative models). They introduce nonparametric approaches to identify these domains, including a kernel-based domain discrepancy measure and a domain-wise cross-validation procedure to determine the optimal number of domains. The method is validated on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human action recognition (IXMAS) tasks, showing significant improvements over baselines and the previous work by Hoffman et al.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with a well-motivated approach. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation for their optimization problem, including the relaxation to a convex quadratic programming problem that is solvable with standard tools. The experiments are comprehensive, covering two distinct visual recognition tasks with appropriate baselines (including the only previous work on latent domain discovery by Hoffman et al.). The results consistently show that reshaping datasets into the identified domains leads to better domain adaptation performance.

The authors carefully evaluate both strengths and weaknesses by comparing against reasonable baselines and providing an analysis of the identified domains. They demonstrate that their domains correspond to meaningful visual factors like object pose and background, which validates the practical utility of their approach. The DWCV procedure for determining the number of domains is particularly well-justified, as it directly addresses the trade-off between domain distinctiveness and learnability.

## Clarity
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem statement to solution to evaluation. The mathematical formulations are precise, though some sections (particularly the optimization relaxation) may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with convex optimization. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, and the results are presented in an easily digestible manner.

The paper could be improved by more explicitly explaining why "maximum learnability" is a better criterion than simply maximizing TDD (which would lead to more domains). The connection between the theoretical properties and practical performance could also be strengthened. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability.

## Originality
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a problem that has been largely ignored in domain adaptation research. While most prior work assumes domains are given (e.g., datasets as domains), the authors correctly identify that visual datasets are not naturally organized into domains. Their key insight of using "maximum distinctiveness" and "maximum learnability" as domain properties is novel, and their nonparametric approach contrasts with the parametric mixture models used in the only previous work on latent domain discovery (Hoffman et al.).

The paper is substantially different from previous NIPS papers on domain adaptation, which typically focus on adaptation techniques assuming domains are given rather than discovering them. The authors also provide a clear discussion of related work, accurately differentiating their approach from existing methods.

## Significance
The problem addressed is highly significant because it challenges a fundamental assumption in visual domain adaptation research: that datasets correspond to domains. This assumption has led to problematic practices like treating datasets as domains, which can degrade performance when domains are not properly separated. The authors demonstrate that reshaping datasets into the identified domains leads to better adaptation performance, which has clear practical implications for visual domain adaptation.

The approach could also inform future dataset construction by revealing the main factors creating domain differences. The paper's results on identifying meaningful visual factors (pose, background, appearance) provide valuable insights for dataset designers. The method is general and could potentially be applied to other domains beyond visual recognition.

## Strengths and Weaknesses
**Strengths:**
1. Addresses a fundamental but overlooked problem in domain adaptation
2. Proposes a theoretically sound and well-validated approach
3. Provides thorough experimental evaluation across multiple tasks
4. Demonstrates practical utility through improved adaptation performance
5. Offers meaningful insights into the factors creating domain differences

**Weaknesses:**
1. Insufficient explanation of why "maximum learnability" is necessary beyond maximizing TDD
2. Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability
3. No thorough discussion of failure cases or limitations
4. Could strengthen the connection to broader domain adaptation literature

## Recommendation
This paper makes a significant contribution to domain adaptation research by addressing a fundamental problem that has been overlooked in the field. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and rigorously validated. While there are some areas for improvement in clarity and completeness, the paper is technically sound and makes a meaningful advance in the field.

The paper meets all the key criteria for NIPS acceptance. It is of high quality with strong theoretical and experimental support, clear and well-organized writing, original contributions that advance the state of the art, and significant impact for both researchers and practitioners in domain adaptation.

I recommend acceptance. The paper provides a valuable new perspective on domain adaptation that could influence future research in the field, and the method is likely to be widely adopted and extended by the community.
