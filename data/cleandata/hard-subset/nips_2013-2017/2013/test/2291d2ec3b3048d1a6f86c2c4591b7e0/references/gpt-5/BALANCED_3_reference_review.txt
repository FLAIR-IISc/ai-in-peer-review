Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles an important but under-addressed question in domain adaptation for vision: datasets are often heterogeneous mixtures of latent “domains,” and treating each dataset as a single domain can hamper adaptation. The authors propose to automatically reshape datasets into multiple latent domains without manually specifying factors (pose, illumination, background, etc.). Two axioms guide the approach: maximum distinctiveness (latent domains should be distributionally far apart) and maximum learnability (each domain must support learning a good classifier).

For distinctiveness, the paper maximizes a sum of pairwise distribution discrepancies between domains using the RKHS mean embedding/MMD (Gretton et al., NIPS’07). The resulting assignment problem is NP-hard; the authors relax it using per-domain sample weights β and impose a label-prior constraint (LPC) so that class proportions per domain match the global prior, mitigating collapse to per-class clusters. For learnability, the number of domains K is chosen via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV), selecting K that maximizes weighted within-domain classification accuracy. The method is also extended to “conditional reshaping” of heterogeneous test sets by assigning test samples to training domains to minimize distribution discrepancy (claimed to reduce to a convex QP; details in supplement).

Experiments on object recognition (Amazon/DSLR/Webcam/Caltech-256) and IXMAS multi-view action recognition show that domains found by the proposed method yield higher adaptation performance (with GFK and several multi-source strategies) than (a) using original datasets as domains and (b) Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12) latent-domain discovery via parametric Gaussian mixtures. Qualitative analyses illustrate that discovered domains align with interpretable factors (pose, background, color). The paper positions itself against dataset bias work (Torralba & Efros, CVPR’11), adaptation methods (Daumé III; Blitzer et al.; Pan et al.; Mansour et al.; Gopalan et al.; Gong et al.), and generalized clustering (Jegelka et al.), emphasizing a nonparametric, label-aware domain discovery objective.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: dataset-level “domains” are often mixtures; discovering latent domains is practically important for adaptation.
- Conceptual simplicity: uses MMD for nonparametric distribution discrepancy; no mixture-model assumptions.
- Thoughtful constraint: LPC is a pragmatic mechanism to prevent per-class clustering and encourages domains to reflect nuisance factors rather than labels.
- Practical model selection (DWCV): selects K in a data-driven way, tied to the “learnability” axiom.
- Useful extension: conditional reshaping of test sets addresses heterogeneous targets and yields substantial gains for cross-view action recognition.
- Empirical promise: consistent improvements over “dataset = domain” and over the prior latent-domain method [20], with qualitative analysis supporting interpretability.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Optimization clarity: The relaxation is nontrivial and under-specified. The constraints in Eq. (5) (1/M ≤ sum_k βmk ≤ 1/C, with βk on a per-domain simplex) are unusual and do not transparently enforce the one-of-K assignment implied by ∑k zmk=1. It is unclear why these bounds are correct or sufficient; the final argmax assignment is a heuristic. Please justify these constraints and discuss alternative relaxations (e.g., introducing per-sample simplex variables).
- No guarantees: The objective maximizes a convex quadratic (nonconcave) subject to linear constraints; global optimality is not guaranteed. There is no analysis of approximation quality or conditions that avoid degenerate solutions. Some discussion of landscape/initialization and empirical stability is needed.
- LPC assumption: Requiring per-domain class proportions to match the global prior could be unrealistic—real domains often exhibit label shift. This assumption may bias discovery and limit applicability. Please evaluate sensitivity to δ and study scenarios with genuine label-shift across domains (ablations with/without LPC).
- Selection of K: DWCV evaluates within-domain supervised accuracy using the same data used for domain discovery. This can favor over-fragmentation or overfit. A nested CV or data split for discovery vs DWCV would be more principled; at least assess stability across random seeds and report variance.
- Computational complexity: Solving the QP repeatedly for K=2,…,Kmax with an M×M kernel scales poorly for large M. Provide complexity, solver details, runtimes, and any approximations (e.g., Nyström, mini-batching).
- Conditional test reshaping: The formulation is only sketched; constraints, regularization (e.g., minimal domain sizes), and handling of degenerate assignments are unclear. Please include the full optimization, and discuss how you prevent trivial solutions.

Clarity
- Some key pieces are only in the supplement (test reshaping optimization). The main text should at least present the precise objective and constraints.
- The multi-source strategies list omits “MATCHING,” which appears later as a strong performer. Please define MATCHING (e.g., picking the source domain with minimal MMD to target) and detail combination/training.
- Notation and constraints (Sec. 2.1) require tightening; explain the role of βmk=zmk/Mk carefully.
- Provide complete references (years/venues) and ensure tables/figures are fully interpretable with captions.

Originality
- The nonparametric MMD-based objective for domain discovery with a label prior constraint, and the learnability-based K selection, are a novel combination in the adaptation literature. Related to generalized clustering in RKHS but distinct in objectives and supervision.

Significance
- If robust and scalable, the idea of reshaping datasets into more coherent domains could materially improve adaptation pipelines and dataset construction practices. Extending to test-set reshaping is practically valuable, especially in multi-view/action settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and justify the relaxation, constraints, and final assignment; compare to an alternative relaxation with explicit per-sample assignment probabilities summing to 1.
- Provide ablations: effect of LPC (δ=0, larger δ, no LPC), kernel bandwidth sensitivity, and removing DWCV vs fixed K.
- Add stability analyses across random seeds/initializations and report computational costs.
- Evaluate across multiple adaptation backbones (e.g., TCA/CORAL or other subspace/metric methods) to show that the discovered domains are broadly useful.
- Include experiments with synthetic or real label-shift to probe LPC’s validity.
- Fully specify and evaluate conditional test reshaping in the main paper; include size constraints and regularization.

Pros vs cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses an important, under-explored problem with a simple, generally applicable approach.
- Nonparametric formulation avoids restrictive mixture assumptions; qualitative and quantitative gains are promising.
- Practical mechanism for choosing K; useful test-set reshaping extension.

Cons:
- Ambiguities and potential flaws in the relaxation and constraints; no theoretical guarantees.
- Strong LPC assumption; lack of ablations and sensitivity analyses.
- Computational scalability and reproducibility details are insufficient.
- Some experimental details (MATCHING, test reshaping optimization) are missing from the main text.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject
With clearer and better-justified optimization, ablations (especially on LPC), stability/scalability analyses, and fuller specification of test reshaping, this work could be impactful. As it stands, the idea is compelling but the methodological clarity and empirical thoroughness fall short of NeurIPS standards.
