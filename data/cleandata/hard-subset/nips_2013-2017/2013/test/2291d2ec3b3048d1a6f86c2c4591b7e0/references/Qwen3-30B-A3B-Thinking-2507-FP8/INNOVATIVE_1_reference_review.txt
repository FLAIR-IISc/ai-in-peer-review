

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper presents a bold and novel perspective on domain adaptation by challenging the fundamental assumption that datasets inherently correspond to well-defined domains. The authors correctly identify a critical gap in visual domain adaptation research: while speech and language datasets naturally lend themselves to domain definitions (e.g., by speaker or language), visual datasets are typically collected without regard to domain structure, yet researchers often treat entire datasets as single domains. This leads to suboptimal adaptation performance, as datasets often contain multiple latent domains that are blended together.

The paper's core contribution is the introduction of two axiomatic properties for latent domains: maximum distinctiveness (domains maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains that support strong discriminative models). The authors propose a nonparametric approach using kernel-based RKHS distance to measure domain distinctiveness, and introduce domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to determine the optimal number of domains. This is the first work to explicitly address latent domain discovery in visual datasets, as the authors correctly note that [20] is the only prior work on this topic, though it uses restrictive parametric assumptions.

The paper demonstrates significant empirical improvements over using datasets as single domains and over the [20] approach. The results on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and action recognition (IXMAS) show that reshaping datasets into the automatically discovered domains leads to substantially better adaptation performance. The authors also convincingly show that conditional reshaping of test datasets, matching the domains discovered from training data, further improves results.

The most compelling aspect of this work is its conceptual novelty. By shifting the focus from "how to adapt between domains" to "how to properly define domains," the paper opens up an entirely new direction for domain adaptation research. This is exactly the kind of bold conceptual contribution that NIPS values highly. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach not only provides better theoretical foundations for domain adaptation but also leads to practical improvements.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with strong experimental validation across multiple domains and tasks. The authors carefully address the challenges of unsupervised domain discovery (avoiding per-category clustering, handling complex domain factors), and their DWCV method for determining the number of domains is both intuitive and effective.

Minor weaknesses include limited theoretical analysis of why the proposed method works (though the empirical results are strong), and the paper doesn't fully explore the scalability of the approach to extremely large datasets or very high-dimensional feature spaces. However, these are reasonable limitations for a novel conceptual contribution, especially given the paper's clear demonstration of practical utility.

The significance of this work cannot be overstated. By fundamentally questioning how we conceptualize domains in visual recognition, the authors have established a new research direction that has the potential to reshape the entire field of domain adaptation. The approach has clear practical implications for improving adaptation performance in real-world applications, and it opens the door to more sophisticated dataset construction that explicitly considers domain structure.

This paper stands out as a truly novel contribution to the field. While the methodology is not perfectly polished (as expected for a new direction), the conceptual breakthrough is substantial and the empirical results are compelling. The authors have successfully demonstrated that reshaping datasets into latent domains leads to significantly better adaptation performance, and they've provided a principled way to discover these domains.

The paper is clearly within scope for NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel theoretical perspective and strong empirical validation. It advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, and the ideas presented are likely to inspire significant follow-up work.

In summary, this paper makes a bold conceptual contribution to domain adaptation by introducing a new framework for latent domain discovery. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated, with clear practical benefits. While some theoretical aspects could be strengthened, the paper's conceptual novelty and significant empirical improvements outweigh these minor limitations. This is exactly the kind of paper that NIPS should publish - a bold new direction that challenges assumptions and opens up new research avenues.

**Recommendation: Accept**
