Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles the underexplored problem of discovering latent “domains” inside heterogeneous visual datasets to improve domain adaptation. The core idea is that datasets are often agglomerations of multiple, confounded factors (pose, illumination, background, etc.), so treating each dataset as a single domain can hurt adaptation. The authors propose two axioms for latent domains: (1) maximum distinctiveness—domains should be maximally different in marginal feature distribution, quantified nonparametrically via kernel mean embeddings (MMD), and (2) maximum learnability—each domain should support strong discriminative models; they use domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to select the number of domains K. The optimization relaxes a combinatorial assignment into a continuous quadratic program over per-domain simplex variables with a label prior constraint (LPC) that enforces class proportions in each domain to match the global prior.

They evaluate on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS), using the geodesic flow kernel for adaptation. They also propose “conditional reshaping” of test data to match discovered training domains by minimizing MMD, yielding a convex QP. The work relates to: MMD/two-sample tests (Gretton et al.), domain adaptation (Daumé, Blitzer et al., Gopalan et al., Gong et al.), multi-source adaptation (Mansour et al.), selection of landmarks (Gong et al.), and is closest to Hoffman et al. (ECCV) who discover latent domains with parametric Gaussian mixtures. The present approach is nonparametric and uses label-aware constraints and a model-selection criterion for K.

Strengths
- Clear and timely problem formulation: discovering latent visual domains is important and under-addressed.
- Sound nonparametric discrepancy measure: using MMD avoids restrictive parametric assumptions in [20].
- Principled K selection via learnability: DWCV is intuitive and aligns with the goal of training adaptable models.
- Practical contribution: conditional reshaping of test data acknowledges heterogeneous targets and is computationally tractable.
- Empirical breadth: results on both objects and actions; qualitative visualizations help interpret the discovered factors (pose/background/color).
- The method seems to improve over both original dataset partitions and [20], and can integrate with standard adaptation pipelines.

Weaknesses and questions
- Optimization and reproducibility: The core relaxed problem is a maximization of a convex quadratic objective under linear constraints, which is non-convex and in general NP-hard. The paper states off-the-shelf solvers suffice, but gives no details on initialization, solver, stopping criteria, or guarantees. How sensitive are solutions to initialization? Any evidence of local optima or run-to-run variance?
- Reliance on LPC: Enforcing identical class priors across domains is a strong assumption that often fails in practice. It risks forcing assignments that distort genuine domain structure. How sensitive is performance to δ? What happens when priors differ across latent domains (e.g., long-tail categories)?
- Preventing per-class clustering: Without LPC, the distinctiveness term might split by class; with LPC, is per-class fragmentation still possible? Provide ablations: (a) no LPC, (b) weaker regularizers (e.g., entropy or minimum-size), (c) supervision-free constraints.
- Computational scalability: MMD with a full kernel matrix is O(M^2) in memory/time. What are the runtimes and memory footprints on the reported datasets? Any use of Nyström/mini-batching?
- Kernel sensitivity: Using an RBF kernel with bandwidth = 2×median distance is heuristic. How robust are the partitions and A(K) to bandwidth choice? Any automatic bandwidth selection or multiple-kernel variant?
- K selection via DWCV: DWCV uses source labels and may overfit to source learnability rather than target adaptability. Show correlation between A(K) and target accuracy, and cases where they diverge. Compare to alternative criteria (BIC/silhouette/stability).
- Evaluation details: Results reference Tables 1–3 and an oracle upper bound, but absolute numbers, variances, and baselines (e.g., K-means with class-balance constraints, spectral clustering with fairness constraints) are missing here. Please report statistical significance and multiple random splits.
- Scope of “unsupervised”: The method uses labels in LPC and in DWCV; the discovery is “unsupervised” with respect to domain labels but not label-free. Clarify this early to avoid confusion.
- Conditional reshaping of test data: Minimizing MMD to match discovered training domains can be sensitive to covariate shift unrelated to label structure. Provide evidence that conditional reshaping improves not only MMD but also target accuracy consistently. Also clarify whether any target labels are used (they say no), and how the matching to training domains is selected at test time (e.g., via MMD only).
- Theoretical grounding: Beyond the MMD property, there is no analysis linking TDD+LPC maximization to improved downstream adaptation or generalization. Even a simple argument or bound would strengthen the paper.

Quality
The objective (sum of pairwise MMDs) is reasonable, and the conditional test reshaping idea is useful. However, the learning algorithm’s optimization aspects are under-specified, and several design choices (LPC, kernel, K selection) lack ablations. The empirical evidence is promising but needs fuller reporting, stronger baselines, and robustness analyses.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and motivates the problem convincingly. The optimization section would benefit from more detail (derivation of constraints, solver, initialization). Define all adaptation strategies (UNION/ENSEMBLE/MATCHING) precisely in the main text. Include algorithmic pseudocode or a concise procedure.

Originality
Discovering latent domains with a nonparametric discrepancy and selecting K by learnability is a novel combination relative to [20]. Conditional test reshaping is also a useful addition. The ideas build on known tools (MMD, CV) but are applied in a new setting with a clear objective.

Significance
If validated more thoroughly, the approach could influence how adaptation papers treat datasets and encourage dataset construction practices. The practical benefits in multi-source adaptation and heterogeneous targets are relevant to the community.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide optimization details, runtime/memory, and sensitivity to initialization; consider a block-coordinate ascent or CCCP-style algorithm and report convergence behavior.
- Ablate LPC and δ; consider softer fairness constraints (e.g., divergence penalties) or label-free regularizers.
- Analyze kernel choice; try multiple kernels or MK-MMD.
- Compare K selection to alternative criteria; plot target accuracy vs A(K) across tasks.
- Add stronger baselines: class-balanced spectral clustering on kernel features; domain discovery via adversarial representations (if applicable).
- Report means±std across random splits; include effect sizes and significance tests.

Minor comments
- Clarify whether MATCHING uses only unlabeled target data (MMD) and how ties are handled.
- Define Kmax bound rationale more carefully; the current bound seems ad hoc.
- Some references are incomplete; check formatting.

Pro/con list
Pros: Important problem; nonparametric objective; practical K selection; conditional test reshaping; broad experiments; qualitative insights.
Cons: Optimization non-convex and under-specified; strong LPC assumption; limited ablations and robustness; incomplete evaluation details; theoretical guarantees absent.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The problem is important and the approach is promising and practically useful, but the paper needs stronger experimental rigor and more transparency about optimization and assumptions. With added detail and ablations, it would make a solid contribution.
