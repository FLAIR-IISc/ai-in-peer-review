This paper tackles a fundamental and often overlooked problem in domain adaptation: the conventional assumption that a given dataset constitutes a single, coherent domain. The authors argue that datasets are often heterogeneous mixtures of multiple latent domains, and treating them as a single source can hinder adaptation performance. To address this, they propose a novel unsupervised method to "reshape" source datasets into a set of latent domains that are both maximally distinctive and maximally learnable.

The core of the proposed method is an optimization framework that partitions a collection of source data points into `K` domains. The objective has two key components. First, to ensure the discovered domains are meaningful and distinct, the framework maximizes the "Total Domain Distinctiveness" (TDD), defined as the sum of pairwise distances between the empirical domain distributions in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). This is a non-parametric approach that avoids making strong distributional assumptions. Second, to prevent the algorithm from simply clustering by class label, a "Label Prior Constraint" (LPC) is enforced, ensuring that the class distribution within each discovered domain mirrors the overall class distribution of the source data. The optimization problem is formulated as an integer program and then relaxed to a continuous quadratic program for tractability.

To automatically determine the number of latent domains, `K`, the authors introduce the principle of "maximum learnability." They propose a "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) procedure. For a range of `K` values, the data is partitioned, a classifier is trained on each resulting domain, and its performance is evaluated via cross-validation. The optimal `K` is chosen as the one that yields the highest weighted-average cross-validation accuracy, ensuring that the discovered domains are not just distinct but also useful for training effective classifiers. The paper further extends this idea to reshape the unlabeled target set, conditioned on the discovered source domains.

Experiments on standard object recognition (Caltech-256, Office) and action recognition (IXMAS) datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach. The results show that adapting from the discovered domains consistently improves performance over adapting from the original datasets, and also outperforms a prior method for latent domain discovery.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper's main premise—questioning the "dataset-as-domain" paradigm—is insightful and important. It addresses a foundational issue in domain adaptation that could lead to more robust and realistic models. The proposed solution, combining the principles of maximum distinctiveness and learnability, is novel, principled, and elegant.
2.  **Technical Quality:** The use of a non-parametric kernel-based measure (RKHS distance) to quantify domain distinctiveness is well-justified and powerful, as it avoids restrictive parametric assumptions about the data distribution, a key advantage over related work [20]. The Label Prior Constraint is a clever and crucial component that prevents a degenerate solution where domains simply correspond to class clusters.
3.  **Methodological Soundness:** The formulation of the problem is clear. The proposed DWCV procedure for model selection (choosing `K`) is sensible, as it directly optimizes for the ultimate goal: creating domains from which one can learn strong classifiers.
4.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive. The "oracle" evaluation (GOURS) effectively demonstrates the potential of the discovered domains by providing an upper-bound performance. The paper then shows that this potential can be realized in a practical setting using multi-source adaptation techniques. The extension to reshaping the test set is a valuable contribution, showing particularly strong gains on the multi-view action recognition task. The qualitative analysis in Figure 1 provides compelling visual evidence of the meaningful domains discovered by the method.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Optimization Details and Scalability:** The paper mentions that the relaxed optimization problem is still NP-hard in general but can be handled by existing solvers. However, it lacks a discussion on the practicalities of this optimization. How scalable is the method with respect to the number of data points `M`? The kernel matrix `K` is `M x M`, suggesting a quadratic complexity that might be prohibitive for large datasets. A discussion of runtime, convergence, and the quality of the solutions found by the off-the-shelf solvers would strengthen the paper.
2.  **The Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The assumption that class priors are domain-independent is a strong one. While it may hold for the balanced datasets used in the experiments, one can easily imagine real-world scenarios where this is not the case (e.g., a "beach" domain having more instances of "swimsuit" than an "office" domain). A discussion of the limitations of this constraint and potential failure modes would be welcome. Furthermore, the relaxation parameter `δ` is set to 1% without justification; the sensitivity to this hyperparameter is not explored.
3.  **Computational Cost of Model Selection:** The DWCV procedure for selecting `K` appears computationally intensive, as it requires running the entire (and potentially expensive) domain discovery optimization for each candidate value of `K`. This could be a significant practical barrier. Some commentary on the overall computational cost would be beneficial.
4.  **Clarity on Test Set Reshaping:** The computational details for the conditional reshaping of the test set are relegated to the supplementary material. While this is common practice, a more explicit formulation of the objective function in the main paper would improve clarity, as this is an important part of the experimental results.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper identifies and provides a compelling solution to a fundamental problem in domain adaptation. The proposed method is novel, principled, and non-parametric. The experimental results are strong, well-structured, and convincingly demonstrate the benefits of reshaping datasets into latent domains. The work is likely to be influential and inspire future research in this direction.
*   **Against Acceptance:** The primary concerns are practical: the lack of discussion on the scalability and computational complexity of the optimization and model selection procedures. The LPC assumption may also limit the applicability of the method in certain real-world scenarios.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a high-quality, original, and significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The core idea is thought-provoking, and the proposed method is both elegant and effective. It is a solid piece of work that would be of great interest to the NIPS community.
