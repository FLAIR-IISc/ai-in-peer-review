This paper addresses the important problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition, specifically focusing on the often-overlooked issue of how to define “domains” when dealing with visual data where clear semantic properties are absent. The authors argue that existing datasets often represent a mixture of underlying domains (e.g., variations in viewpoint, illumination), and propose a novel method to automatically reshape these datasets into more characteristic domains based on principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. They present a nonparametric approach to measure domain discrepancies and an automatic method to determine the number of latent domains. The method is evaluated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, demonstrating improved performance compared to baseline approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a crucial but under-addressed aspect of domain adaptation – the inherent ambiguity in defining domains for visual data. Recognizing datasets as potentially composed of multiple underlying domains is a significant insight.
* **Novel Approach:** The proposed method of automatically reshaping datasets based on distinctiveness and learnability is novel and provides a principled way to discover latent domains without relying on manual feature engineering or pre-defined properties.
* **Sound Methodology:** The use of kernel-based methods for measuring domain discrepancies and the incorporation of a label prior constraint during domain assignment are well-motivated and contribute to the robustness of the approach. The DWCV method for determining the number of domains is also a sensible approach.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements in performance compared to baseline methods, showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The conditional reshaping of test datasets is a particularly compelling result.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed methodology and experimental setup. The inclusion of illustrative examples (Figure 1) is helpful in understanding the discovered domains.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention relaxing the integer programming problem, the computational cost of the proposed method, particularly for large datasets, is not thoroughly discussed. A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and scalability would be beneficial.
* **Kernel Choice:** The choice of the Gaussian kernel and the method for determining the kernel bandwidth seem somewhat arbitrary. A more thorough investigation of different kernel functions and bandwidth selection strategies could potentially improve performance.
* **Limited Comparison to Recent Methods:** While the paper compares to [20], the field of domain adaptation has advanced significantly in recent years. A comparison to more recent and state-of-the-art domain adaptation techniques (e.g., adversarial domain adaptation methods) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Sensitivity to Hyperparameters:** The performance of the method likely depends on the choice of hyperparameters, such as the deviation parameter δ in the LPC constraint. An analysis of the sensitivity to these parameters and guidelines for their selection would be valuable.
* **Qualitative Analysis Depth:** The qualitative analysis in Figure 1 is a good start, but could be expanded to provide a more detailed understanding of the characteristics of the discovered domains. For example, visualizing the feature distributions within each domain would be informative.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and often overlooked issue in domain adaptation.
* Proposes a novel and principled approach for automatically discovering latent domains.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results on multiple datasets.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity may be a concern for large datasets.
* Limited comparison to recent state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods.
* Lack of detailed sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong empirical results. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper addresses an important problem and presents a compelling solution. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions. 

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

* Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method.
* Include a comparison to more recent and state-of-the-art domain adaptation techniques.
* Conduct a sensitivity analysis of key hyperparameters and provide guidelines for their selection.
* Expand the qualitative analysis to provide a more detailed understanding of the characteristics of the discovered domains (e.g., visualize feature distributions).
* Briefly discuss limitations of the method and potential future research directions.
