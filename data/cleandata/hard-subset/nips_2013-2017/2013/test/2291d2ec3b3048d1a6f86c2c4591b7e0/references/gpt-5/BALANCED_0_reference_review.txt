Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a common but under-examined assumption in visual domain adaptation: datasets are often treated as single “domains,” even though they may mix multiple, heterogeneous data-generating factors (viewpoint, illumination, background, etc.). The authors propose to automatically reshape labeled datasets into latent domains that are (i) maximally distinctive in distribution and (ii) maximally learnable. Distinctiveness is operationalized by maximizing the sum of pairwise distribution discrepancies between domains using RKHS mean embeddings (i.e., MMD). To avoid trivial per-class splits, they impose a label prior constraint (LPC) so that each discovered domain approximately matches the global class prior. The discrete assignment is relaxed to a continuous quadratic program in variables βmk = zmk/Mk. To choose the number of domains K, they introduce domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV): partition the data, train classifiers within each domain, and select K that maximizes the weighted within-domain CV accuracy.

Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam with SURF BoVW) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) show that the discovered domains yield improved adaptation performance, both when used as sources (with GFK) and when conditionally reshaping unlabeled test data to match training domains. They compare against the original dataset partitions and against Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12) on latent domain discovery using parametric mixtures, claiming superior performance. Qualitative examples suggest discovered domains correspond to interpretable factors (pose, background, color).

This work is related to domain adaptation (e.g., Saenko et al.; Gopalan et al.; Gong et al.), latent domain discovery (Hoffman et al., ECCV’12), and kernel two-sample testing (Gretton et al.). It also connects to generalized clustering with discriminability criteria (Jegelka et al.).

Strengths
- Clear and timely problem motivation: datasets ≠ domains; discovering latent domains is useful for adaptation and dataset design.
- Nonparametric formulation avoids strong parametric mixture assumptions and leverages well-established MMD machinery.
- Practical scheme for selecting K (DWCV) and for conditional reshaping of unlabeled test sets.
- Empirical evidence across two tasks with multiple datasets; qualitative analysis indicates interpretable discovered factors.
- Generally well-written and easy to follow at a high level.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality (technical soundness)
- Objective and optimization. The core step maximizes a convex quadratic function under linear constraints; this is NP-hard. The paper acknowledges this but provides no algorithmic details (solver, initialization, convergence, regularization). Without such details, reproducibility and reliability of the solution quality are unclear. Moreover, the M×M kernel matrix scaling raises computational concerns (O(M^2) memory).
- Assumptions in constraints. The LPC assumes the same class prior across domains and forces each discovered domain to approximately preserve the global class proportions. This is often violated in practice (long-tail classes, domain-specific frequencies). The additional implicit requirement Mk ≥ C (each domain contains every class) is restrictive and unrealistic in many settings.
- “Unsupervised” vs. labeled dependence. While the introduction frames the problem as unsupervised, the method relies on labels (LPC; within-domain supervised CV for DWCV). This is, in effect, a supervised (or semi-supervised) domain discovery method on labeled sources; the framing should be corrected.
- Risk of circular model selection. DWCV selects K using the same labeled data used to induce the partitions and to train within-domain classifiers. This can overfit K; there is no outer validation or stability analysis. A nested procedure or complexity penalty would strengthen claims.
- Potential degeneracy and lack of guarantees. Maximizing sum of MMDs can favor splitting by nuisance but also by difficulty/noise or sample size artifacts. Aside from LPC, there is little to prevent pathological partitions. No theoretical characterization (e.g., identifiability, generalization bounds linking TDD and within-domain accuracy) is provided.
- Conditional reshaping of test sets is only sketched; details of the convex QP, the alignment between Ktrain and Ktest, and the matching criterion are not specified.

Clarity
- The β-relaxation and constraints are under-explained. The constraint 1/M ≤ Σk βmk ≤ 1/C is unintuitive; a more direct and interpretable relaxation (e.g., soft assignment with entropy regularization and explicit Mk variables) would help. A complete algorithmic description (objective, constraints, solver, hyperparameters, initialization, stopping) is needed.
- Matching strategy and conditional reshaping require more detail. How are source–target domain matches chosen without labels? Is MMD used directly? How is K chosen for test reshaping?

Originality
- The paper’s main contribution—nonparametric latent domain discovery guided by MMD plus a label prior constraint and DWCV—is a novel and useful perspective relative to Hoffman et al.’s parametric approach. The conditional reshaping of test data is another interesting idea. The overall combination is original in the NeurIPS context.

Significance
- If robust and scalable, the approach could influence how practitioners prepare sources for domain adaptation and prompt re-thinking of “dataset bias.” However, current scalability limitations (kernel matrices), restrictive assumptions (equal class priors, every class in every domain), and incomplete optimization details limit immediate impact. Demonstrations are on relatively small, classic benchmarks; broader validation (including modern large-scale features) would strengthen significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full algorithmic details, complexity, and runtime; consider scalable approximations (Nyström, random Fourier features) and a practical alternating/majorization-minimization scheme with convergence diagnostics.
- Recast the problem as supervised domain discovery; relax LPC to allow class-prior shifts and missing classes (e.g., class-conditional MMD/HSIC or conditional distribution embeddings).
- Use nested validation or stability selection for K; report sensitivity to kernel bandwidth and δ, and ablations (no LPC; different K).
- Expand baselines: constrained/balanced k-means, spectral clustering with label constraints, subspace clustering; compare with more recent latent domain/multi-source methods.
- Clarify and formalize the conditional reshaping optimization and the source–target matching procedure.
- Add theory where possible (e.g., bounds relating TDD and within-domain risk) or at least diagnostic measures to detect degenerate partitions.

Pros
- Motivated problem with practical relevance.
- Nonparametric formulation; simple and flexible.
- Empirical gains and interpretable discovered domains.
- Concept of conditional test reshaping is appealing.

Cons
- Optimization is NP-hard; solver details and scalability are lacking.
- Strong LPC assumptions; unrealistic requirement that each domain contain all classes.
- “Unsupervised” framing is misleading given reliance on labels.
- Potential overfitting in K selection; limited theoretical backing.
- Incomplete details for conditional reshaping and matching.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The ideas are promising and the empirical gains are encouraging, but the paper needs stronger methodological clarity, scalability considerations, and a more careful treatment of assumptions and model selection before it meets the bar for NeurIPS. The authors’ approach could be competitive with these revisions.
