## Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a crucial but often overlooked issue in visual domain adaptation: the inherent lack of clearly defined domains within standard image datasets. The authors argue that treating entire datasets as single domains can be suboptimal when those datasets actually contain multiple, potentially distinguishable domains (e.g., different viewpoints, lighting conditions). They propose a novel unsupervised learning approach to *reshape* datasets into more characteristic domains, guided by two key principles: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.  They formulate this as an optimization problem, relaxing it into a computationally tractable continuous form, and demonstrate its effectiveness on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a very relevant and often implicitly ignored problem in domain adaptation. The assumption of a well-defined domain is frequently violated in practice, and this work provides a principled approach to address this.
* **Novelty:**  The idea of automatically *discovering* and reshaping domains within a dataset, rather than relying on pre-defined or manually-created domains, is novel and constitutes a significant contribution. While [20] touches upon this, the authors clearly differentiate their work, emphasizing the nonparametric approach and the incorporation of “maximum learnability” as a crucial optimization criterion.
* **Sound Methodology:** The proposed approach, based on kernel methods and optimization, appears technically sound. The use of distinctiveness and learnability as guiding principles is intuitive and well-motivated. The relaxation to a continuous optimization problem addresses the computational challenges of the integer programming formulation.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on both object recognition and human activity recognition demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The results consistently outperform baseline methods, including using the original datasets and the approach from [20]. The additional analysis of reshaping *test* datasets is particularly compelling.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated, the method is explained in a logical manner, and the experiments are presented in a comprehensive way.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Sensitivity to Kernel Parameters:** While the authors mention setting the kernel bandwidth, the sensitivity of the results to this parameter is not thoroughly investigated. This is a common challenge with kernel methods, and a more in-depth analysis would strengthen the paper.
* **Computational Cost:** While the relaxation to continuous optimization improves tractability, the computational cost is still likely significant, particularly for large datasets.  A discussion of scalability and potential optimizations (beyond the relaxation) would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to Multi-Domain Adaptation Techniques:** While the authors acknowledge multi-domain adaptation, the comparison is somewhat limited. A more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art multi-domain adaptation algorithms would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the method's benefits.
* **Qualitative Analysis Could Be Deeper:** While the analysis of identified domains is helpful (Figures 1 and 2), a more in-depth qualitative analysis, perhaps with visualizations of the discovered domains and a discussion of failure cases, would add further insight.




**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:**  The paper is technically sound, with a well-motivated approach and strong experimental results. The authors are careful to evaluate the performance of their method and compare it to relevant baselines.  It feels like a complete piece of work.
* **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ reasoning.
* **Originality:** The concept of automatically reshaping datasets into latent domains is novel and represents a significant departure from traditional domain adaptation approaches.  The authors adequately reference related work and clearly differentiate their contribution.
* **Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to impact the field of domain adaptation. The ability to automatically discover and utilize latent domain structure within datasets could lead to more robust and effective adaptation techniques.  Researchers and practitioners are likely to find this work valuable.




**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical, often-overlooked problem in domain adaptation.
* Proposes a novel and technically sound approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results on multiple datasets.
* Clearly written and well-organized.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Sensitivity to kernel parameters requires further investigation.
* Computational cost may be a limitation for very large datasets.
* Comparison to state-of-the-art multi-domain adaptation techniques could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. The authors address an important problem with a novel and well-executed approach. The experimental results are compelling, and the paper is clearly written. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall contribution.  This work has the potential to advance the field of domain adaptation and provide valuable insights for researchers and practitioners alike.



**Confidence:** 9/10
