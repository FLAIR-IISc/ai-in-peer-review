This paper tackles a surprisingly underexplored question in domain adaptation: what *is* a domain, particularly in the context of visual data? The authors argue, compellingly, that equating datasets with domains is a flawed practice and propose a novel approach to automatically reshape datasets into more meaningful, latent domains based on the principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. This is a bold and conceptually important direction, and I find the core idea genuinely exciting. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and technical details.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of actively *discovering* domains within datasets, rather than assuming they are pre-defined, is highly novel. This moves beyond simply adapting *between* datasets and begins to address the fundamental question of domain definition itself.
* **Conceptual Clarity:** The authors clearly articulate the pitfalls of treating datasets as domains and provide a strong rationale for their proposed approach. The axiomatic properties of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability are intuitive and well-defined.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method, based on kernel methods and optimization, appears sound and leverages existing techniques in a creative way. The continuous relaxation of the integer programming problem is a pragmatic and reasonable choice.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both object recognition and human activity recognition demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, showing improved classification results compared to baseline methods. The reshaping of test datasets is particularly promising.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The authors provide a comprehensive overview of relevant work in domain adaptation, clustering, and related fields.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Kernel Sensitivity:** The performance of the method relies heavily on the choice of kernel and its bandwidth. While the authors describe their selection process, a more thorough sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** The optimization problem, even in its relaxed form, could be computationally expensive for very large datasets. The scalability of the approach is not fully addressed.
* **DWCV Heuristic:** The domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) method for determining the number of domains feels somewhat heuristic. A more theoretically grounded approach to K selection would be desirable.  The connection between DWCV accuracy and true domain quality isn’t fully established.
* **Limited Qualitative Analysis:** While the examples provided in Figure 1 are insightful, a more comprehensive qualitative analysis of the discovered domains, perhaps using visualization techniques, would strengthen the paper. Understanding *what* constitutes a domain beyond just improved accuracy would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental, yet largely overlooked, question in domain adaptation.
* The proposed approach is genuinely novel and represents a significant departure from existing methods.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation and technical details.
* The potential impact of this work is high; it could reshape how we think about and approach domain adaptation in visual recognition.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The method relies on kernel methods, which can be sensitive to parameter choices.
* The computational cost could be a limiting factor for large datasets.
* The DWCV heuristic for K selection is not fully justified.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty and significance of the work outweigh the limitations. The authors have identified a critical gap in the field of domain adaptation and proposed a promising approach to address it. While further refinement and scalability analysis are needed, this work has the potential to significantly impact the field. The focus on *discovering* domains, rather than simply adapting between them, is a refreshing and important contribution. I’m willing to overlook the slightly heuristic nature of the DWCV and the potential computational concerns given the boldness of the idea and the strong initial results. This paper is precisely the kind of innovative work NIPS should be showcasing.
