Summary
The paper tackles the common but under-discussed issue that visual “datasets” often conflate multiple underlying domains (e.g., pose, illumination), which harms domain adaptation. The authors propose to “reshape” datasets into latent domains by optimizing two criteria: maximum distinctiveness (maximize pairwise distributional differences between domains using kernel mean embeddings/MMD in RKHS) and maximum learnability (choose the number of domains K via domain-wise cross-validation accuracy). The domain assignment is formulated as a constrained optimization: maximize the sum of pairwise MMD^2 between latent domains, subject to a label prior constraint (LPC) that enforces near-identical class proportions across domains, and per-instance constraints; then relax to a continuous quadratic program. They also propose “conditional reshaping” of heterogeneous test sets to match discovered training domains. Experiments on object recognition (Caltech/Amazon/DSLR/Webcam) and IXMAS action recognition report improvements over using original datasets and over latent domain discovery with a parametric GMM model [Hoffman et al., ECCV’12], especially when combined with a GFK-based adaptation method.

Strengths
- Addresses a real and impactful problem: datasets ≠ domains. The attempt to automate latent domain discovery is valuable to the DA community.
- Uses nonparametric distributional discrepancy (kernel mean embeddings/MMD), avoiding potentially misspecified parametric mixtures.
- A pragmatic procedure for selecting K via “maximum learnability” and a mechanism for test-time conditional reshaping are interesting ideas.
- Empirical results on two standard benchmarks suggest benefits over treating datasets as monolithic domains and over [20].

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strong and potentially unrealistic LPC assumption: enforcing nearly identical class priors across domains (δ=1%) ignores label shift, which is common in practice. This can mislead discovery when domains differ in class frequency. No sensitivity analysis to δ or discussion of failure modes under label shift.
- Objective and optimization: maximizing a convex quadratic subject to linear constraints is nonconvex/NP-hard. The paper acknowledges hardness but provides very limited algorithmic detail (solver used, initialization, restarts, convergence diagnostics). Scalability is unclear (O(M^2) kernel; how large can M be?).
- Risk of degenerate solutions: maximizing pairwise MMD can favor partitions that isolate “easy” or outlier subsets or that inadvertently align with class boundaries. The LPC partly counters per-class collapse, but there is no quantitative analysis showing that discovered domains reflect nuisance factors rather than implicit class structure. Qualitative figures are suggestive but anecdotal.
- Selection of K via domain-wise cross-validation uses labels, i.e., the method is supervised in domain discovery. This weakens the claim of “unsupervised” latent domain discovery; please clarify scope. Furthermore, DWCV may prefer partitions that overfit to easy subsets (no stability analysis across seeds/splits).
- Evaluation limited and partially optimistic:
  - Heavy use of “oracle” best-domain accuracies (requires target labels). The practical “MATCHING” strategy that reportedly approaches the oracle is insufficiently specified. How is source-target matching done without labels, and is any target information used at training time beyond unsupervised features?
  - Baselines are limited. Beyond [20], there is no comparison to simple but competitive alternatives such as balanced spectral clustering/k-means on x with label-balance constraints, subspace clustering, or kernelized clustering with must-link/cannot-link by labels. The statement that k-means/random “are not competitive” without showing numbers is unsatisfactory.
  - Generality to other adaptation algorithms is not established; all results hinge on GFK. It is unclear whether gains persist with TCA, CORAL, SA, or other standard DA methods.
  - Conditional reshaping of the test set is only sketched; key details are deferred to the supplement (objective, constraints, choice of number of test domains), making it hard to assess correctness or reproduce results.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity: no study of kernel choice/bandwidth, δ in LPC, effect of removing LPC, or the impact of K on accuracy and computational cost. Claims about improvements would be more convincing with these analyses.
- Reproducibility: no pseudocode, solver/parameter details, or code release. The exact splits and sampling on IXMAS are nonstandard and may affect comparability.

Quality
Conceptually sound use of MMD for distributional separation, but the optimization and selection procedures are under-specified, and critical assumptions (LPC) are strong. The empirical evidence is suggestive but not conclusive due to limited baselines, optimistic oracle reporting, and missing sensitivity/stability analyses.

Clarity
Generally well written and motivated. However, Section 2 needs more precise exposition: explicit connection to MMD^2, full objective with constraints, algorithmic details (initialization, solver), and complexity. Conditional test reshaping should be described in the main text. Provide pseudocode.

Originality
The combination—MMD-based nonparametric domain partitioning with label-balance constraints and CV-based K selection—is a reasonable variant, but conceptually close to latent domain discovery [20] and generalized clustering with kernel embeddings [27]. The main novelty is incremental rather than fundamental.

Significance
Potentially useful if robust and scalable, but current evidence is insufficient. The method’s reliance on LPC and labels to pick K limits applicability. Without broader baselines, sensitivity/stability, and stronger demonstrations, impact is uncertain.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are results to δ, kernel bandwidth, and K? Please provide ablations (including removing LPC).
2) What solver/initialization do you use for the nonconvex QP? How many restarts? Runtime and memory vs M?
3) How do you prevent partitions that correlate with class labels? Any quantitative measure showing within-domain nuisance homogeneity and between-domain separability beyond class?
4) Precise description of MATCHING and conditional test reshaping (objective, constraints, choice of number of test domains).
5) Can your method handle label shift? If not, can LPC be relaxed or learned?
6) Do gains persist with other DA methods (e.g., TCA, CORAL, SA)? Please report.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real pain point (datasets ≠ domains) with a nonparametric approach.
- Interesting idea of conditional test reshaping.
- Some promising empirical gains.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong assumptions (LPC), limited and optimistic evaluation, under-specified optimization.
- Missing ablations and broader baselines; unclear scalability and robustness.
- Incremental novelty.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising, but the current empirical and methodological evidence does not yet meet the bar for NeurIPS. Strengthening the evaluation, clarifying the optimization, and analyzing assumptions/sensitivity would significantly improve the paper. Confidence: medium.
