Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses an important but under-explored question in domain adaptation: datasets are often agglomerations of multiple, heterogeneous “latent” domains, and treating a dataset as a single domain can hurt adaptation. The authors propose to automatically “reshape” visual datasets into latent domains satisfying two axioms: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different in distribution) and maximum learnability (each domain should support learning a strong classifier). Distinctiveness is operationalized via a kernel mean embedding distance (akin to MMD; Gretton et al., NIPS’07), summed over all domain pairs. The assignment of instances to K domains is formulated as an integer program with constraints, notably a label prior constraint (LPC) requiring each domain’s class proportions to match the global prior. The problem is relaxed to a continuous quadratic program over β variables with linear constraints. To select K, the paper introduces domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV), computing a weighted average of cross-validated accuracies within discovered domains and choosing K to maximize it. The authors also propose “conditional reshaping” of test data to match the discovered training domains by minimizing kernel discrepancies (details in supplement).

Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) indicate that discovered domains improve adaptation performance, measured with geodesic flow kernel (GFK; CVPR’12). The method is compared to latent domain discovery via parametric mixtures (Hoffman et al., ECCV’12) and simple baselines (K-means, random), with favorable results. The paper situates itself within domain adaptation (Saenko et al., ECCV’10; Gopalan et al., ICCV’11; Daumé, ACL’07; Ben-David et al., NIPS’06), dataset bias (Torralba & Efros, CVPR’11), and kernel-embedding clustering (Jegelka et al., 2009), and connects to multi-source adaptation (Mansour et al., NIPS’09).

Strengths
- Problem motivation: Clear articulation that “dataset ≠ domain,” with convincing qualitative examples (pose, illumination, background) and a practical objective for discovering latent domains.
- Methodological idea: Using nonparametric distributional discrepancy (kernel mean embeddings) avoids restrictive parametric assumptions (contrast with [20]), and LPC is an intuitive mechanism to prevent trivial per-class clusters.
- Practical selection of K: DWCV is simple and implementation-friendly, and ties the domain discovery objective to downstream discriminative performance.
- Broad applicability: Approach is agnostic to the downstream adaptation method and demonstrated on both images and videos; conditional reshaping of test data is a useful extension.
- Empirical promise: Reported (oracle and practical) improvements over using original datasets and over [20]; qualitative analyses help interpret discovered factors.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality/technical soundness
- Objective and relaxation: The optimization maximizes a convex quadratic function subject to linear constraints, which is non-convex/NP-hard. The paper relies on “off-the-shelf solvers” without specifying which, initialization, or convergence/stability behaviors. Empirical sensitivity and computational complexity (in M, K, C) are not reported.
- Constraints: The per-instance constraint 1/M ≤ Σk βmk ≤ 1/C is unusual and insufficiently justified. Provide a derivation from integer constraints and explain its effect. The LPC with δ=1% seems strong; many real domains exhibit substantial label shift. How robust is the method when class priors differ across domains? Ablations varying δ and removing LPC would help.
- Risk of overfitting K: DWCV selects K to maximize within-discovered-domain CV accuracy on the same data used to form the domains, which can favor overly fine partitions that inflate in-domain accuracy without improving target performance. Although Figure 2 suggests some alignment, a more principled selection (e.g., stability across resamples, held-out validation, or penalized criteria) or at least an analysis of selection bias is needed.
- Conditional reshaping of test sets: The optimization and constraints are deferred to the supplement. Without those details it is hard to assess identifiability, robustness, and complexity. Clarify how you avoid degenerate matches and whether label priors are assumed at test time.

Clarity
- Several crucial details are missing or terse: solver specifics, algorithm pseudocode, complexity, kernel bandwidth sensitivity, N in N-fold CV, and details of ENSEMBLE/MATCHING strategies. Tables are referenced but not fully described in text (numbers, variance, and statistical significance).
- Notation around β, constraints, and the mapping from relaxed β to hard assignments should be clarified.

Originality
- The combination of MMD-like distinctiveness, label-prior regularization, and domain-wise CV for K is novel in the context of latent domain discovery for adaptation, and plausibly addresses limitations of parametric mixtures (Hoffman et al., 2012). The idea relates to generalized clustering via kernel embeddings but is adapted to supervised settings with label-aware constraints.

Significance
- If reliable and scalable, reshaping datasets could become a useful pre-processing step for multi-source adaptation. However, evidence is limited to relatively small, classical benchmarks and one adaptation method (GFK). Broader validation (other adaptation algorithms, larger/modern datasets, runtime) would strengthen impact. The reliance on an oracle metric for some claims also tempers practical significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full optimization details: explicit objective/constraints for both training and conditional test reshaping, solver choice, initialization, stopping criteria, and complexity. Include runtime/memory as a function of M, K, and kernel evaluations.
- Robustness studies: sensitivity to kernel/bandwidth, δ in LPC, and N in DWCV; ablations removing LPC; performance when class priors vary across domains (violating LPC).
- Stronger baselines: compare to (i) clustering in RKHS with label-dependence penalties (e.g., HSIC to discourage label-driven partitions), (ii) per-class conditional MMD criteria that align p(x|y) rather than enforcing p(y); (iii) modern multi-source/domain adaptation methods. Report statistical significance.
- Avoid oracle-only claims: emphasize practical strategies. Precisely define and evaluate the MATCHING procedure (e.g., distribution-distance-based selection without labels), and compare to UNION/ENSEMBLE across more settings.
- Clarify theory/intuitions: discuss why maximizing pairwise MMD with LPC should yield domains that improve target adaptation, and when it might fail (e.g., when nuisance factors are weak or orthogonal to target shift).
- Release code to improve reproducibility.

Pros
- Addresses a real pain point (“dataset ≠ domain”) with a nonparametric, label-aware approach.
- Simple, general framework; practical K-selection heuristic.
- Empirical indications of benefit on two tasks; interpretable discovered factors.

Cons
- Non-convex optimization with limited algorithmic detail; unclear scalability.
- Strong LPC assumption; limited robustness analysis and ablations.
- Reliance on oracle evaluation; incomplete description of practical strategies and missing experimental details.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The problem is important and the approach is promising, but the current submission lacks sufficient algorithmic clarity, robustness analysis, and breadth of empirical validation to meet a high bar. Addressing the above points would make the work much stronger and potentially impactful.
