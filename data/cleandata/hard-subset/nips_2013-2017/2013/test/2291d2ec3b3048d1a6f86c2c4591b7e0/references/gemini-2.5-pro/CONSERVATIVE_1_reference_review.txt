### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses a fundamental issue in visual domain adaptation research: the common practice of treating entire datasets as single domains. The authors argue that datasets are often heterogeneous collections of multiple latent, underlying domains (e.g., defined by viewpoint, illumination). Treating such a collection as a single source domain can hinder adaptation performance. The paper proposes a novel unsupervised method to "reshape" one or more source datasets into a set of latent domains. The method is guided by two principles: 1) **Maximum Distinctiveness**, where the discovered domains should have maximally different distributions from one another, and 2) **Maximum Learnability**, where each domain should contain sufficient, coherent data to train a strong classifier.

To achieve maximum distinctiveness, the authors formulate an optimization problem that maximizes the sum of pairwise kernel-based distribution distances (specifically, the MMD metric) between latent domains, subject to a "Label Prior Constraint" that enforces a consistent class distribution across domains. To determine the number of domains, K, they propose a "Domain-Wise Cross-Validation" (DWCV) procedure based on the principle of maximum learnability, selecting the K that yields the highest average cross-validation accuracy within the discovered domains. Experiments on object and action recognition tasks show that adapting from these discovered domains can improve performance over adapting from the original datasets.

### Strengths
1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper correctly identifies and articulately describes a significant and often-overlooked weakness in the standard domain adaptation experimental setup. The premise that datasets are not monolithic domains is compelling and motivates the work well.
2.  **Intuitive Principles:** The proposed principles of "maximum distinctiveness" and "maximum learnability" are intuitive and provide a reasonable conceptual framework for what constitutes a "good" set of latent domains.
3.  **Non-Parametric Approach:** The use of a non-parametric, kernel-based method to measure distribution difference is a strength, as it avoids making strong parametric assumptions about the data distribution (e.g., Gaussian mixtures, as in Hoffman et al. [20]), which may not hold for complex visual data.

### Weaknesses
The paper presents a promising idea but falls short in terms of methodological rigor and thoroughness of evaluation, which are critical for acceptance at a top-tier venue like NIPS.

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Clarity:**
    *   **Optimization:** The core optimization problem (Eq. 4) is a maximization of a convex quadratic function, which is NP-hard. The paper dismisses this significant challenge by stating that "existing solvers are adequate in yielding satisfactory solutions." This is insufficient. The authors should provide details on the solver used, the non-triviality of finding a good solution, sensitivity to initialization, and an analysis of the quality of the solutions obtained. Without this, the practicality and robustness of the core algorithm are in question.
    *   **Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The LPC is a very strong assumption. While the intuition is provided, it is easy to conceive of scenarios where domain-specific factors are correlated with class labels (e.g., certain animals only appearing in specific habitats/backgrounds). The paper does not analyze the sensitivity to this constraint or discuss its potential limitations.
    *   **Selection of K:** The DWCV method for choosing K is a heuristic. The justification is that good domains should be "learnable," but high in-domain cross-validation accuracy does not necessarily guarantee that a domain is a good source for adaptation to a *different* target domain. The connection between this metric and out-of-domain generalization performance is assumed rather than established.

2.  **Experimental Rigor and Missing Baselines:**
    *   **Comparison to Prior Art:** The most relevant prior work, Hoffman et al. [20], is only compared against in the "oracle" setting (Table 1). A direct comparison in the practical, non-oracle setting (Table 2) is a critical missing piece of the evaluation. It is unclear if the proposed method's practical utility exceeds this key baseline.
    *   **Simple Baselines:** The authors state that standard clustering methods like K-means yield poor performance and omit the results. This is unacceptable for a rigorous evaluation. A properly constrained baseline (e.g., K-means clustering on features while enforcing the LPC) should be included in the main tables to establish a clear performance floor and properly contextualize the proposed method's contribution.
    *   **Undisclosed "MATCHING" Method:** A key result in Table 2 is that a "MATCHING" strategy achieves performance nearly identical to the oracle. However, the paper provides no details on how this matching is performed, other than stating it does not use target labels. This is a major omission that makes the practical results irreproducible and difficult to evaluate. Is it simply based on minimizing MMD between a source and the target? This crucial component of the experimental pipeline must be explained.
    *   **Details on "Conditional Reshaping":** The interesting idea of reshaping the test set is introduced, but all technical details are deferred to supplementary material. Given that this is central to the results in Table 3, the main paper should at least outline the formulation and how it differs from the source-reshaping problem.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   The paper tackles an important and valid conceptual problem in domain adaptation.
*   The proposed framework is novel in its specific combination of non-parametric distinctiveness and a learnability-based model selection for this problem.
*   The oracle results suggest that the underlying idea of reshaping datasets has merit.

### Arguments for Rejection:
*   Significant weaknesses in methodological rigor, particularly regarding the handling of the NP-hard optimization.
*   The experimental evaluation is incomplete, lacking a practical comparison to the most relevant baseline (Hoffman et al. [20]) and other simple but important baselines (e.g., constrained K-means).
*   Crucial details of the experimental method (the "MATCHING" algorithm) are omitted, hindering reproducibility and a full understanding of the results.
*   Key assumptions (LPC) and heuristics (DWCV) are not sufficiently justified or analyzed for their limitations.

### Recommendation
While the problem addressed is significant and the high-level ideas are appealing, the paper in its current form does not meet the standards for NIPS. The lack of methodological depth regarding the optimization, the insufficient experimental validation against key baselines, and the omission of critical details about the evaluation protocol constitute major flaws. The work shows promise, but it requires a more rigorous and complete treatment before it can be considered a solid scientific contribution. Therefore, I recommend rejection.
