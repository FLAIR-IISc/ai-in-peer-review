

# Peer Review: "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical yet often overlooked issue in visual domain adaptation: the assumption that datasets inherently represent single domains, when in reality they may contain multiple latent domains due to various extraneous factors like pose, illumination, and background. The authors propose a novel framework to automatically reshape datasets into characteristic latent domains based on two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains that enable strong discriminative models). Their approach uses nonparametric kernel-based methods to measure domain differences and a domain-wise cross-validation procedure to determine the optimal number of domains. The authors demonstrate their method's effectiveness on object recognition and human action recognition tasks, showing significant improvements over using original datasets as domains.

This work connects to prior domain adaptation research (e.g., [1,2,3,4]) but critically addresses a fundamental conceptual issue that has been largely ignored in the field. It builds upon but differs significantly from Hoffman et al.'s [20] work on latent domain discovery, which relies on parametric Gaussian mixture models, whereas the authors propose a nonparametric approach that better handles complex visual data distributions.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-motivated approach. The authors clearly define the problem and provide a principled solution based on two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) that are well-justified through empirical examples. The optimization problem is well-formulated, and the relaxation to a continuous optimization problem is appropriate and well-justified.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering both object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human action recognition (IXMAS). The authors compare their approach against the original dataset domains and a competing method [20], showing consistent improvements. The conditional reshaping of test datasets is a particularly valuable contribution that addresses a practical scenario where test data may also contain multiple domains.

However, the paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their approach and its scalability to larger datasets. The DWCV procedure for determining K requires significant computation, and the authors do not provide runtime analysis or comparisons to other methods.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement is compelling and well-motivated, and the technical contributions are presented logically. The experimental setup is clearly described, and the results are presented in a way that makes it easy to understand the key findings.

The authors could improve clarity by providing more intuition about why their approach differs from standard clustering (e.g., why clustering by appearance alone leads to per-category domains, as they mention). A brief example illustrating the difference between their method and simple clustering would be helpful.

The mathematical formulations are well-presented, though some readers may find the relaxation step in Section 2.1 somewhat abrupt. A more intuitive explanation of why this relaxation preserves the essential properties would strengthen the paper.

## Originality

This work makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a fundamental conceptual problem in domain adaptation research that has been largely overlooked. While domain adaptation has been extensively studied, the paper correctly identifies that treating datasets as domains is an ad hoc practice that can lead to suboptimal performance.

The key insight of defining two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) for latent domain discovery is novel and well-justified. The nonparametric approach they propose, as opposed to the parametric methods used in prior work like [20], is particularly valuable for visual data where complex, non-Gaussian distributions are common.

The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, especially Hoffman et al. [20], by emphasizing the nonparametric nature of their approach and its ability to handle complex visual data distributions. The comparison to clustering methods (K-means, random partition) also helps establish the novelty of their approach.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. By addressing a fundamental flaw in how domain adaptation research treats datasets as domains, the authors provide a more principled approach that leads to demonstrable performance improvements across multiple tasks. The ability to reshape both training and test datasets into characteristic domains has practical implications for real-world applications where domain shifts are common.

The paper's findings also have implications for future dataset construction, as the authors suggest examining the main factors of discovered domains to guide more principled dataset collection. This is a particularly valuable contribution that extends beyond immediate technical improvements.

The paper advances the state of the art in domain adaptation by providing a method that consistently outperforms using original datasets as domains, and even outperforms a competing method for latent domain discovery [20]. The improvements are not marginal but substantial, as demonstrated in Tables 1-3.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a fundamental conceptual problem in domain adaptation that has been overlooked
- Proposes a principled solution based on two well-motivated axiomatic properties
- Demonstrates significant performance improvements across multiple tasks
- Provides a method for reshaping both training and test datasets
- Offers practical insights for dataset construction

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited analysis of computational complexity and scalability
- The relaxation step in the optimization could be better motivated
- Some readers may need more intuition about why their approach differs from standard clustering
- The paper doesn't fully address how to handle cases where domains are not perfectly separable

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper at NIPS. It makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a fundamental issue in domain adaptation research that has been largely ignored. The proposed approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear improvements over existing methods. The paper is well-written and makes a strong case for the importance of reshaping datasets into characteristic latent domains.

While there are some areas for improvement (particularly regarding computational complexity and motivation of the relaxation), these do not detract from the overall significance and quality of the work. The paper advances the state of the art in domain adaptation in a demonstrable way and provides valuable insights that will be useful to both practitioners and researchers in the field.

This work represents a substantial contribution to the field of domain adaptation that should be of interest to the NIPS community. It is well-suited for presentation at NIPS as it addresses a fundamental issue with clear practical implications and demonstrates strong experimental results.
