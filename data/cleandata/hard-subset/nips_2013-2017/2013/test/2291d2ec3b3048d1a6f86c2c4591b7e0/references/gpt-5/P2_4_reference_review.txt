Summary
This paper tackles the important but under-addressed problem of discovering latent domains within standard visual datasets, arguing that treating datasets as monolithic “domains” can obscure meaningful, structured variation relevant to domain adaptation. The authors propose two principles to guide the discovery of latent domains: (1) maximum distinctiveness, operationalized by maximizing pairwise discrepancies between domain marginals P(x) via kernel mean embeddings (i.e., MMD in an RKHS); and (2) maximum learnability, operationalized by selecting the number of domains K using domain-wise cross-validation accuracy on labeled source data. They introduce a label prior constraint (LPC) to discourage clustering by class identity, relax an integer assignment problem into a continuous quadratic program with simplex constraints, and then evaluate on object recognition (Caltech-256/Amazon/DSLR/Webcam) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS), including “conditional reshaping” of test sets to match discovered training domains. They report improvements over using the original dataset splits and over Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12) which models latent domains via Gaussian mixtures.

Relation to prior work
- The approach is closely related to kernel two-sample testing and MMD-based distribution matching (Gretton et al.), generalized clustering via kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.), and multisource domain adaptation. The paper distinguishes itself from Hoffman et al.’s latent domain discovery by avoiding parametric mixture assumptions and from prior DA works (e.g., GFK, TCA, landmarks) by focusing on discovering the domains themselves rather than adapting given domains.
- The use of MMD to define a discriminability criterion is consistent with the kernel-embedding literature; the LPC and DWCV components tailor this to labeled visual recognition.

Strengths
- Problem framing is compelling and relevant: datasets often amalgamate heterogeneous conditions (pose, illumination, background), and discovering latent domains is practically useful for downstream multi-source adaptation.
- Nonparametric discrepancy measure (MMD) is well-justified and flexible; avoiding parametric mixtures is a plus given complex image factors.
- The label prior constraint directly addresses a key pitfall (degenerate per-class clusters) and leverages available labels in a simple way.
- The DWCV criterion to pick K is intuitive and practical; bounding K by cross-validation folds and class count is sensible.
- Conditional reshaping of test sets to match discovered training domains is an interesting and practically motivated extension.
- Empirical results (as described) suggest consistent gains over original dataset splits and over [20], and show that simple source–target matching can approach oracle performance.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/methodological:
- Objective targets marginal P(x) distinctiveness; despite LPC, it may still prefer factors correlated with class identity or spuriously easy-to-separate phenomena. A stronger remedy would consider conditional alignment (e.g., discrepancies between P(x|y)), or explicitly penalize within-class cross-domain similarity collapses. An ablation comparing (i) no LPC, (ii) LPC, (iii) conditional-MMD variants would clarify this.
- Optimization details are under-specified. The relaxed problem is a maximization of a convex quadratic with linear constraints—generally NP-hard. The paper reports “off-the-shelf solvers are adequate,” but does not specify solver, initialization, stopping, or guarantees. This raises reproducibility and reliability concerns. There is also notational overload: K denotes both number of domains and the kernel matrix, and Eq. (1) and constraints (5) contain typos/odd bounds (e.g., 1/M ≤ Σk βmk ≤ 1/C).
- Scalability is unclear. The method requires an M×M kernel and solves a constrained QP for each candidate K; complexity and memory for larger datasets are not discussed. Sensitivity to kernel choice/bandwidth is also not examined.
- The “maximum learnability” criterion uses within-domain cross-validation on labeled source data. This may select domains that are easy to classify but not necessarily more transferable to unseen target domains; a variant that directly proxies cross-domain generalization (e.g., reverse validation or leave-one-dataset-out) would be more aligned with the DA goal.
- Conditional reshaping of test data is deferred to the supplement; the main text lacks the exact optimization and constraints, making it hard to assess correctness.

Experimental:
- Tables/figures referenced are not shown here, but the text suggests oracle analyses (max over source domains) and multi-source strategies (UNION/ENSEMBLE/MATCHING). It would help to report variance/error bars and statistical tests; quantify how often matching reaches oracle and how robust it is.
- Comparisons are limited to one latent-domain baseline ([20]) and one DA method (GFK). Including additional DA backbones (e.g., TCA, landmarks, SCL) would strengthen claims that discovered domains help generally.
- No ablations on δ in LPC, kernel bandwidth, or K sensitivity beyond a qualitative DWCV plot. It is important to show the method is not overly sensitive to these choices.
- Qualitative analysis is helpful, but additional diagnostics (e.g., inter/intra-class MMD before/after reshaping, confusion changes) would better support the hypothesized factors driving domains.

Assessment by NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: Conceptually sound and builds on solid kernel machinery; however, optimization hardness, missing details, and limited ablations weaken the rigor. The conditional test reshaping is promising but underspecified in the main text.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and motivated; some notational issues and missing algorithmic details impede reproducibility. Providing a clear algorithm box, solver specifics, and complexity would help.
- Originality: The problem setup and the synthesis (MMD-based distinctiveness + LPC + DWCV + conditional test reshaping) are novel relative to cited work and timely for DA.
- Significance: If robust, the idea could influence how practitioners curate sources and apply multi-source DA. Current evidence on classic benchmarks indicates promise, but broader and deeper evaluations are needed to confirm impact.

Pros
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem.
- Nonparametric, label-aware formulation; clear intuitions.
- Practical K selection; test-time reshaping idea.
- Empirical gains over original splits and a key prior method.

Cons
- Optimization is NP-hard in the relaxed form; solver details and guarantees missing.
- Strong LPC assumption (constant class priors across domains) may be unrealistic; limited validation of its necessity/robustness.
- Scalability and sensitivity to kernel choices not evaluated.
- Limited breadth of baselines and ablations; conditional reshaping details in supplement.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a compelling and original approach to latent domain discovery with encouraging empirical results. To strengthen the contribution, the authors should (i) clarify and justify the optimization procedure and complexity; (ii) add ablations for LPC/δ, kernel bandwidth, and K; (iii) consider conditional MMD or within-class discrepancies; (iv) broaden DA backbones and report variability; and (v) move key test-reshaping details into the main text.
