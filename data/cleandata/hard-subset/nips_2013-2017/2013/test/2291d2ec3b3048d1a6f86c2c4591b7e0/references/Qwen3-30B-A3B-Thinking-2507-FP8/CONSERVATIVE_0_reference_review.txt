

**Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"**

This paper proposes a method to automatically reshape visual datasets into latent domains based on two properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing inter-domain differences) and maximum learnability (ensuring domains contain sufficient data for discriminative models). The authors claim this is the first work addressing latent domain discovery, arguing that visual datasets aren't naturally organized into domains as in speech or language processing.

**Strengths:**
- The paper identifies a genuine problem in visual domain adaptation: datasets aren't naturally partitioned into domains, making standard cross-dataset adaptation problematic.
- The technical approach is sound, using kernel-based domain discrepancy measures that are standard in domain adaptation literature.
- The experimental evaluation is thorough, with results on both object recognition and action recognition datasets.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Questionable novelty:** The paper claims to be the first to address latent domain discovery, but Hoffman et al. [20] (which the authors cite as "the most relevant to ours") also addresses this problem using a hierarchical Gaussian mixture model. The authors dismiss [20] as "assuming an explicit form of distribution" but fail to demonstrate why their nonparametric approach is fundamentally superior. The paper lacks a thorough comparison with [20] or other clustering-based approaches for domain adaptation. The related work section is insufficient, and the novelty claim is overstated.

2. **Insufficient validation:** The paper only compares against the original datasets and [20], but doesn't provide comprehensive comparisons against standard clustering techniques (e.g., K-means, hierarchical clustering) or established domain adaptation baselines. The claim that "neither [K-means or random partition] yields competitive performance" is unsubstantiated without showing actual numbers. The paper should have included these as baselines to properly demonstrate the value of their approach.

3. **Marginal improvements:** The experimental results show modest improvements (typically 1-3%) over using the original datasets. For example, in Table 2, the best improvement is about 2.5% in the object recognition task. Given the complexity of the proposed method (with its kernel-based optimization and DWCV procedure), these marginal gains don't justify the new framework. The paper fails to demonstrate that these improvements are statistically significant or practically meaningful.

4. **Lack of theoretical analysis:** The paper provides no theoretical guarantees for their method. There's no analysis of the convergence properties of their optimization procedure, nor a discussion of how their method scales to larger datasets. The optimization problem is described as NP-hard, but the paper doesn't address how this limitation affects practical application.

5. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims their approach "may also shed light on dataset construction in the future," but the paper doesn't provide any concrete insights about dataset construction beyond the qualitative examples in Figure 1. The factors identified (e.g., object pose, appearance, background) are largely intuitive and don't provide new insights for dataset construction.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection of this paper. While the problem addressed is important, the paper fails to demonstrate that the proposed method is significantly better than existing approaches. The claimed novelty is questionable, the improvements over baselines are marginal, and the validation is insufficient. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparisons against standard clustering techniques and established domain adaptation methods, as well as a more rigorous theoretical analysis.

The field has seen many incremental improvements to domain adaptation, and this paper doesn't make a strong enough contribution to justify publication at NIPS. The authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above before considering resubmission to a venue with more lenient novelty requirements.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**
- Pro: Addresses a real problem in visual domain adaptation; well-written; thorough experimental evaluation.
- Con: Questionable novelty; marginal improvements; limited validation; lacks theoretical analysis.
