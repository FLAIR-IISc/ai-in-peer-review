

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper presents a novel conceptual framework for domain adaptation by addressing a fundamental gap in the field: the implicit assumption that datasets correspond to domains. The authors correctly identify that visual datasets are typically not naturally divided into distinct domains (unlike in speech or language processing), but instead contain latent domains that have been overlooked in previous work. Their key insight is that datasets should be "reshaped" into characteristic latent domains based on two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains from which strong discriminative models can be built).

The authors propose a nonparametric approach to automatically discover these latent domains without requiring manual definition of domain properties. Their method measures domain discrepancy using kernel-based methods in RKHS, then optimizes for maximum distinctiveness while enforcing label prior constraints. They introduce domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to automatically determine the optimal number of domains, which is a clever solution to the problem of determining K without manual specification.

The paper makes a significant contribution by reframing domain adaptation research. Previous work (e.g., Hoffman et al. [20]) has attempted to discover latent domains, but the authors correctly identify that their approach is fundamentally different due to its nonparametric nature and focus on practical domain discovery rather than parametric modeling. The paper thoroughly validates their approach on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and action recognition (IXMAS) datasets, demonstrating that reshaping datasets into the identified latent domains leads to better adaptation performance than using the original datasets or domains discovered by prior methods.

The significance of this work lies in its conceptual shift: it challenges the assumption that datasets equal domains, which has been a foundational (but flawed) premise in domain adaptation research. The authors demonstrate that reshaping datasets into latent domains can lead to substantially better adaptation performance, as shown in Tables 1-3. Their approach has clear potential to improve domain adaptation methods across various applications.

The paper's strengths are its novel conceptual contribution, clear motivation, and thorough experimental validation. The authors successfully distinguish their work from related approaches (particularly Hoffman et al. [20]) and provide strong evidence for their claims. The DWCV method for determining the number of domains is particularly elegant and practical.

Some minor weaknesses include:
- The method requires labeled data for DWCV, which may limit applicability in fully unsupervised settings.
- The relaxation of the optimization problem could benefit from more detailed justification.
- The paper doesn't fully explore how their method would behave with completely unlabeled data.

However, these are minor methodological points that don't undermine the paper's core conceptual contribution. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe these limitations are acceptable for a paper that introduces a fundamentally new perspective on domain adaptation.

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a significant contribution to the field. It addresses a genuine gap in domain adaptation research with a bold, novel approach that has clear potential to impact future work. The experiments are thorough and convincingly demonstrate the superiority of their reshaped domains over existing approaches.

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance rather than an incremental improvement. It fundamentally changes how we think about datasets and domains in domain adaptation research, which is exactly the kind of bold, novel contribution that NIPS values most highly. The authors have successfully identified a critical oversight in the field and proposed a principled solution that is both theoretically sound and practically effective.

**Recommendation: Accept**

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Introduces a novel conceptual framework that reframes domain adaptation research
- Addresses a fundamental, previously overlooked gap in the field
- Provides strong experimental evidence for the effectiveness of the approach
- Proposes a practical, automated method for domain discovery without manual intervention
- Has clear potential to influence future work in domain adaptation

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Requires labeled data for DWCV (though this is a minor limitation)
- Some technical details in optimization could be more thoroughly explained
- Limited exploration of fully unsupervised scenarios

Despite these minor points, the paper's conceptual novelty and significance outweigh these limitations. The authors have made a clear contribution that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. This is the kind of paper that should be celebrated at NIPS for its bold approach to a fundamental problem.
