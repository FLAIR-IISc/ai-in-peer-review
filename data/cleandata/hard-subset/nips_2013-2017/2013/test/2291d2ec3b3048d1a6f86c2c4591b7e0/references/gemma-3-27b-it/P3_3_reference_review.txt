## Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper tackles a crucial and often overlooked problem in domain adaptation – the implicit assumption that datasets *are* domains. The authors convincingly argue that standard datasets are often mixtures of latent domains and propose a novel framework for automatically reshaping them based on principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The technical approach is sound, and the experimental results are promising. However, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of limitations and a clearer articulation of the computational cost. I recommend **accepting** the paper with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper highlights the disconnect between how domains are conceptually defined in fields like speech recognition (discrete properties) and their ad-hoc treatment in visual recognition (often equated with entire datasets).  It argues that treating a dataset as a single domain can hinder adaptation performance when underlying sub-distributions exist. The authors propose a method to *discover* these latent domains, guided by maximizing the difference between them (distinctiveness) and ensuring they are learnable (high classification accuracy).

This work builds upon the growing area of unsupervised domain discovery, with [20] being the most closely related.  The authors correctly position their work as extending beyond [20] by employing a nonparametric approach, avoiding potentially restrictive distributional assumptions.  The connection to kernel-based two-sample tests ([21]) and concepts of discriminability (e.g., [27]) is also well-drawn. The authors accurately situate their contribution within the broader landscape of domain adaptation, acknowledging the numerous existing techniques focused on *adapting* between known domains rather than *discovering* them.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Importance:** The core idea of automatically reshaping datasets into latent domains is genuinely novel and addresses a fundamental issue in DA. The premise that datasets are often mixtures of domains is insightful and likely to resonate with the community.
* **Sound Methodology:** The use of kernel methods to quantify domain discrepancy is well-justified and provides a principled approach to maximizing distinctiveness.  The incorporation of learnability (via cross-validation) is a clever and practical addition, addressing the potential for discovering trivially distinct but useless domains.
* **Strong Experimental Validation:**  The results across both object recognition and action recognition tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The comparison to [20] is favorable, and the ablation studies (reshaping test sets) provide further evidence of the benefits.  The ablation experiment of matching domains between training and test sets to achieve performance close to the oracle is a key contribution.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The core concepts are explained clearly, and the mathematical notation is relatively accessible.
* **Practical Considerations:** The inclusion of the label prior constraint (LPC) is a sensible regularization that reflects real-world data collection processes.

**3. Weaknesses & Areas for Improvement:**

* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention continuous relaxation for optimization, the computational complexity of the proposed approach (especially with large datasets) is not thoroughly discussed.  A more detailed analysis of scalability and runtime performance would be valuable.  How does the kernel matrix scale?  Are there approximations that can be used for larger datasets?
* **Kernel Selection:** The choice of a Gaussian kernel is fairly standard, but the justification for this choice is limited. A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to different kernel parameters or kernel types would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** While the results are generally positive, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of scenarios where the method might fail. For example, what if the underlying domains are highly overlapping? Or if the features are not discriminative enough to separate them?
* **Conditional Reshaping Details:** The description of conditional reshaping of the test set in Section 4.3 feels somewhat brief. More detail on the optimization process and the specific choices made would be helpful.
* **Ablation Study for LPC:**  While the LPC is intuitively appealing, an ablation study demonstrating its impact on performance would be useful.  How significant is the improvement conferred by including this constraint?



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Addresses a fundamental and previously unaddressed problem in domain adaptation.
* Technically sound and novel approach.
* Strong experimental results across multiple datasets.
* Clear and well-written paper.

**Against:**

* Computational cost is not fully discussed.
* Limited analysis of failure cases.
*  Relatively brief description of conditional reshaping.

**5.  Qualitative Evaluation (based on NIPS criteria):**

* **Quality (9/10):** The technical approach is sound, and the claims are well-supported by experimental results. The work is largely complete, although discussing scalability more fully would improve it.
* **Clarity (8/10):**  The paper is generally well-written, but some sections (e.g., conditional reshaping) could be more detailed.
* **Originality (9/10):**  The concept of automatically reshaping datasets into latent domains is highly original and represents a significant departure from existing work.
* **Significance (8/10):** The findings are likely to be impactful, potentially influencing how researchers design experiments and interpret results in domain adaptation.  The approach could be valuable in various applications where datasets are known to be heterogeneous.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend **accepting** the paper with minor revisions. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly concerning the computational cost, kernel selection, and potential failure cases. A more detailed explanation of conditional reshaping would also be beneficial. Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses an important problem in domain adaptation with a novel and effective approach.
