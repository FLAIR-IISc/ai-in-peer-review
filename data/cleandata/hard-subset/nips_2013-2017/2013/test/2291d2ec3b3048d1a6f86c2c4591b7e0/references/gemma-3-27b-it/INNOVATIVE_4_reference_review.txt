This paper tackles a crucial and often overlooked aspect of domain adaptation: the very definition of a ‘domain’ in visual recognition. Instead of blindly equating datasets with domains, the authors propose a method to *reshape* datasets into more meaningful, latent domains based on principles of distinctiveness and learnability. This is a bold and insightful approach, and I find the core idea quite compelling. It moves beyond simply *adapting* between predefined domains to actively *discovering* more suitable domain structures within the data.

The strengths of this paper lie in its novelty and conceptual clarity. The axiomatic properties of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability provide a strong theoretical foundation for their approach. The proposed method, combining kernel-based discrepancy measures with a cross-validation strategy to determine the number of domains, is well-motivated and appears sound. The experimental results, while not overwhelmingly conclusive, demonstrate the potential benefits of this reshaping approach, particularly when combined with multi-source adaptation. The analysis of the discovered domains (Figure 1) is insightful and supports the claim that the method captures meaningful variations in the data.

However, the paper isn't without its weaknesses. The optimization procedure, while relaxed to a continuous form, still seems computationally demanding, and a more detailed discussion of scalability would be beneficial. The empirical evaluation, though promising, could be more extensive. Comparing against simpler baseline domain discovery methods beyond [20] (e.g., a simple k-means clustering) would strengthen the claims. Furthermore, the connection between the discovered domains and human-interpretable factors (pose, illumination) is sometimes tenuous, and a more rigorous qualitative analysis would be valuable. The discussion of the label prior constraint (LPC) feels slightly hand-wavy; a clearer justification for its inclusion and impact would be helpful. Finally, while the conditional reshaping of test datasets is an interesting extension, it feels less fully explored than the training data reshaping.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of automatically reshaping datasets into latent domains is a significant departure from traditional domain adaptation approaches.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability provide a solid foundation.
*   **Potential Impact:** If successful, this approach could lead to more robust and generalizable domain adaptation methods.
*   **Initial Empirical Support:** The results demonstrate a clear trend towards improved performance.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** The optimization procedure might be prohibitive for large datasets.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** More comprehensive comparisons against baselines are needed.
*   **Incomplete Validation:** Some aspects of the method (e.g., LPC) lack a thorough justification.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor flaws, I believe this paper represents a significant step forward in the field of domain adaptation. The core idea is exceptionally novel and has the potential to reshape (pun intended!) how we think about domains in visual recognition. While the methodological details could be more refined and the empirical evaluation more extensive, the paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with a request for minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly a more detailed discussion of scalability and a more thorough comparison against simpler baseline domain discovery methods. The authors have identified a fundamental issue and proposed a promising solution. I am excited to see how this research evolves.
