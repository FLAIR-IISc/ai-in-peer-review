## NIPS Review: Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation

**Overall Assessment:** This paper addresses a critical and often overlooked aspect of domain adaptation – the inherent structure *within* datasets that are commonly treated as monolithic domains. The authors propose a novel and well-motivated approach to automatically reshape datasets into more meaningful domains based on the principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The technical approach is sound, the experimental results are promising, and the paper is generally well-written. While the method isn't a complete solution to all domain adaptation challenges, it represents a significant step forward and offers a valuable perspective on how we conceptualize and work with visual datasets. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly identifies the prevalent practice of equating datasets with domains in visual domain adaptation as a potential pitfall, particularly when datasets are heterogeneous. It argues that blindly applying adaptation techniques across a single, composite dataset can hinder performance. The authors then propose a method to automatically decompose these datasets into more homogeneous "latent domains" based on two key properties: maximizing the difference between domains (distinctiveness) and ensuring each domain is sufficiently informative for learning (learnability).

This work builds upon the growing recognition of the importance of understanding dataset composition. It appropriately cites related work in domain adaptation [1, 2, 3, 4, 9, 10], clustering [27], and latent domain discovery [20]. The distinction from Hoffman et al. [20] is clearly articulated – the authors move beyond Gaussian Mixture Models and leverage nonparametric methods for increased flexibility.  The connection to the concept of “dataset bias” [16, 17, 18, 19] is also well established.  The idea of landmark detection [26] is also appropriately acknowledged.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of automatically discovering latent domains within datasets is a significant contribution.  It challenges the conventional "dataset-as-domain" paradigm and provides a principled method for uncovering hidden structure.
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The formulation based on maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability provides a strong theoretical foundation. The use of kernel methods for measuring domain discrepancy is a solid choice.
* **Sound Methodology:** The optimization problem is well-defined, and the continuous relaxation provides a practical solution. The inclusion of the label prior constraint (LPC) is a clever addition, promoting more meaningful domain assignments.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments on both object recognition and human activity recognition demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, consistently outperforming baseline methods. The results in section 4.3, showing improvements by reshaping *test* datasets, are particularly compelling.
* **Thorough Evaluation:**  The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons to existing methods, ablation studies (implicitly through the comparison with the baseline), and analysis of the identified domains.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it relatively easy to follow the technical details.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the continuous relaxation addresses the integer programming issue, the optimization problem still seems computationally demanding, particularly for large datasets.  More discussion of the scalability of the method would be valuable.
* **Kernel Bandwidth Selection:** The choice of kernel bandwidth (twice the median distance) seems somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of bandwidth selection strategies (e.g., cross-validation) might lead to further improvements.
* **Sensitivity to Kernel Choice:** The paper primarily relies on the Gaussian kernel.  Exploring other kernels and their impact on performance would strengthen the analysis.
* **Limited Discussion of Domain Interpretability:** While Figure 1 provides some qualitative insights, a more in-depth discussion of the characteristics of the identified domains – what underlying factors are driving the domain separations? – would be beneficial.  Relating these factors back to the dataset collection process would enhance understanding.
* **DWCV limitations:** The DWCV method for choosing K relies on cross-validation accuracy, which, as the authors note, isn’t always available in real-world scenarios. Exploring alternative criteria for K selection would be valuable.



**4. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to a critical problem.
* Strong empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the method.
* Well-written and clearly explained.
* Potential to significantly impact the field of domain adaptation by promoting a more nuanced understanding of datasets.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity might limit applicability to very large datasets.
* Limited discussion of domain interpretability.
* Dependence on kernel choice and bandwidth selection.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed method, and discuss potential strategies for improving scalability?
*  Have you considered exploring different kernel functions beyond the Gaussian kernel? If so, what were the results?
*  Could you elaborate on the factors driving the domain separations identified in your experiments?  Are there any surprising or unexpected patterns?
*  Are there alternative criteria for selecting the optimal number of domains (K) besides the domain-wise cross-validation?



**Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.**
