

This paper addresses a critical yet overlooked issue in visual domain adaptation: the conflation of entire datasets with single domains. The authors rightly argue that standard visual datasets (e.g., Caltech-256, IXMAS) often contain multiple latent domains (e.g., differing in pose, lighting, or background) that are mistakenly treated as homogeneous. Their core contribution is a novel method to automatically reshape datasets into *latent domains* based on two axiomatic properties: **maximum distinctiveness** (maximizing distribution differences between domains) and **maximum learnability** (ensuring domains contain sufficient data for effective classification). The method uses kernel-based distribution distance for distinctiveness and a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure to determine the optimal number of domains. The approach is evaluated on object and action recognition tasks, showing improvements over baselines that treat datasets as single domains or use clustering methods.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Novel Problem Framing:** The paper identifies a genuine gap in domain adaptation literature—datasets are not inherently domain-structured, yet most work assumes they are. This reframing is well-motivated and addresses a practical flaw in existing benchmarks.  
2. **Technical Soundness:** The nonparametric approach (avoiding restrictive parametric assumptions like [20]) is well-justified. The kernel-based distinctiveness measure (Eq. 1–2) is theoretically grounded, and DWCV provides a practical, data-driven way to select *K* (the number of domains).  
3. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The results (Tables 1–3) convincingly show that reshaping datasets into latent domains improves adaptation performance (e.g., +5–10% accuracy in cross-view action recognition). The conditional reshaping of test sets (Section 4.3) is particularly insightful and addresses a key practical challenge.  
4. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, with clear definitions (e.g., LPC constraint in Eq. 3) and thorough ablation studies (e.g., comparing K-means vs. proposed method).

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Limited Baseline Comparisons:** The paper compares only to [20] and naive clustering (K-means/random), but omits recent domain adaptation methods (e.g., DANN, MMD, DDC) that *could* benefit from their reshaping step. For instance, it’s unclear whether reshaping *before* applying DANN would yield further gains. This weakens the significance claim.  
2. **LPC Justification Gap:** The label prior constraint (LPC) assumes class distributions are domain-invariant (e.g., "walking" is equally frequent in bright/dark videos). However, in real-world data, this may not hold (e.g., a dataset with only bright-lit videos might have more "walking" clips). The paper lacks empirical evidence that LPC *improves* domain discovery versus dropping it.  
3. **DWCV Theoretical Weakness:** DWCV selects *K* based on domain-wise classification accuracy, but this conflates *domain quality* with *classifier performance*. A domain might be poorly distinct (low TDD) yet yield high accuracy due to easy classes. The paper should clarify this trade-off.  
4. **Computational Cost:** The relaxation to convex optimization (Eq. 4–5) is efficient, but the paper doesn’t discuss scalability to large datasets (e.g., ImageNet). The kernel matrix *K* becomes prohibitively expensive for *M* > 10k.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel problem, strong empirical validation, practical reshaping framework, clear contribution to domain adaptation methodology.  
*Con:* Limited baselines, LPC lacks justification, DWCV lacks theoretical grounding, scalability unaddressed.

**Recommendation:** **Borderline Accept** (with major revisions). The core idea is significant and well-executed, but the paper must strengthen its claims by addressing the weaknesses above.  

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**  
1. **Add Baselines:** Compare reshaped domains with *state-of-the-art adaptation methods* (e.g., DANN, MMD) on the same datasets. Show whether reshaping *before* adaptation consistently improves these methods.  
2. **Justify LPC:** Conduct an ablation study on the LPC constraint (e.g., remove it and measure TDD/accuracy). If LPC is unnecessary, remove it; if essential, provide a stronger justification (e.g., "LPC prevents domain collapse in label-imbalanced data").  
3. **Clarify DWCV:** Discuss the trade-off between TDD and DWCV accuracy. Explicitly state that DWCV *prioritizes learnability over distinctiveness* and provide an example where this leads to better adaptation.  
4. **Address Scalability:** Mention computational complexity (e.g., *O(M²K)* for kernel matrix) and suggest approximations (e.g., Nyström method) for large *M*.  
5. **Contextualize Related Work:** Briefly discuss how [27]’s "discriminability" criterion differs from their "maximum distinctiveness" (e.g., [27] uses labels for clustering, while they do not).  

**Significance Note:** If implemented, this reshaping step could become a standard preprocessing step in domain adaptation, improving reproducibility and performance across benchmarks. The paper’s conclusion about dataset construction (e.g., "examining main factors of discovered domains") hints at broader impact, but this needs more concrete examples (e.g., "Domain II in Amazon primarily reflects object color—suggesting future datasets should standardize color calibration").  

Overall, this is a strong, timely contribution that addresses a real flaw in the field. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The current version is technically sound but requires deeper engagement with the broader adaptation literature to fully justify its significance.
