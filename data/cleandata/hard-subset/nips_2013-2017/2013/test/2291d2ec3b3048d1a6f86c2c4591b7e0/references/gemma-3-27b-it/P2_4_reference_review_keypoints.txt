- Overall: Proposes a novel, well-motivated approach to reshape datasets into latent domains; reviewer recommends acceptance.

- Core problem: Real-world datasets are mixtures of underlying domains; treating a dataset as a single domain harms adaptation.

- Method: Automatically discovers latent domains by maximizing distinctiveness between domains and learnability within domains, using kernel methods and DWCV to choose K.

- Research shift: Moves focus from how to adapt between predefined domains to what the domains should be.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on latent domain discovery and clustering literature but uses a nonparametric, axiomatic approach distinct from prior methods.

- Strength: Strong novelty and motivation addressing an overlooked problem in domain adaptation.

- Strength: Solid theoretical foundation via the two principles and clear optimization formulation.

- Strength: Comprehensive empirical validation on object recognition and activity recognition showing improved adaptation.

- Strength: Clear writing, logical organization, and practical applicability.

- Weakness: Limited discussion of limitations including scalability to very large datasets and sensitivity to kernel bandwidth.

- Weakness: Related work discussion is insufficiently expansive, especially regarding clustering alternatives and why they fail.

- Weakness: Computational cost and runtime performance of the continuous optimization are not thoroughly analyzed.

- Weakness: Parameter sensitivity beyond DWCV (e.g., LPC delta, kernel parameters) needs more analysis.

- Evaluation summary: Quality strong, Clarity good, Originality strong, Significance strong.

- Pros for acceptance: Novel, theoretically sound, empirically validated, addresses an important problem, well-written.

- Cons against acceptance: Incomplete limitations discussion, limited related-work depth, unclear computational costs.
