## Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper tackles a crucial and often overlooked aspect of domain adaptation – the very definition and construction of “domains” in visual data. The authors rightly point out the common practice of equating datasets with domains and highlight the potential pitfalls of this approach. They propose a novel, unsupervised learning framework to automatically reshape datasets into more meaningful domains based on the principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and presents a promising approach with strong experimental validation. However, the method’s reliance on kernel parameters and the limited exploration of the implications of different kernel choices represent a key weakness.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper convincingly argues that standard visual datasets often implicitly contain multiple domains due to variations in factors like pose, illumination, and background.  These variations are often not explicitly modeled, leading to suboptimal performance in domain adaptation scenarios. The authors propose a method to automatically discover these latent domains by optimizing for distinctiveness (maximizing separation between domains in a kernel-induced feature space) and learnability (ensuring each domain contains enough information for effective classification). 

The related work section is thorough, covering key areas in domain adaptation, clustering, and relevant kernel methods. The authors correctly position their work as addressing a gap between existing methods focused on *how* to adapt and their focus on *what* constitutes a domain. They appropriately acknowledge and differentiate their work from Hoffman et al. [20] and other relevant approaches.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Motivation:** The core idea of explicitly discovering latent domains within datasets is compelling and addresses a fundamental issue in visual domain adaptation. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of treating datasets as single domains.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The formulation based on maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability provides a solid theoretical framework. The use of kernel methods to quantify domain discrepancies is appropriate and well-explained.  The label prior constraint (LPC) is a clever addition to improve domain quality.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on both object recognition and action recognition tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The consistently superior performance compared to using original datasets and other domain discovery methods (like [20]) is convincing.  The ablation studies examining reshaping test datasets are particularly valuable.
* **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The technical details are clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-described.  Figures and tables are informative and effectively illustrate the key findings.
* **Practical Relevance:**  The method has practical implications for how visual datasets are collected and utilized, suggesting the need for more structured data acquisition strategies to facilitate domain-aware learning.



**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Kernel Sensitivity:** The performance of the method is heavily reliant on the choice of kernel and its bandwidth. While the authors justify their choice of Gaussian kernel, the sensitivity of the results to different kernel parameters is not thoroughly investigated.  A more systematic evaluation of different kernels and bandwidth selection strategies would strengthen the paper.
* **Computational Cost:**  The optimization problem, even in its relaxed continuous form, could be computationally expensive for large datasets.  The paper briefly mentions using off-the-shelf solvers but does not discuss scalability or provide detailed computational costs for the experiments.
* **Limited Analysis of Discovered Domains:** While the authors provide some qualitative examples of the discovered domains (Figure 1), a more in-depth analysis of the characteristics of these domains would be beneficial.  For example, quantifying the distribution of factors like pose, illumination, or background within each domain would provide further insights into the learned domain representations.
* **Assumption of Unlabeled Data:** While unsupervised domain discovery is valuable, the practical usability is limited by needing a large amount of unlabeled data to get good results, which isn’t always available.



**4.  Specific Questions & Suggestions:**

* **Kernel Selection:**  What other kernels were considered? How much do results vary with different kernels?  Were any kernel learning techniques explored?
* **Scalability:** What are the computational complexities of the proposed algorithm? How does the runtime scale with the number of data points and the number of domains?
* **Domain Interpretation:** Can the discovered domains be interpreted in terms of specific factors (e.g., illumination, pose) beyond the qualitative examples provided?
* **LPC Weighting:** The 1% deviation in the LPC seems arbitrary.  Was this value optimized, or is it a fixed parameter? How sensitive are the results to changes in this parameter?



**5. Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical, often-overlooked problem in domain adaptation.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results on multiple datasets.
* Is clearly written and well-organized.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Reliance on kernel parameters and limited exploration of kernel choices.
* Computational cost could be a limiting factor for large datasets.
* Limited in-depth analysis of the discovered domains.


**6.  Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation, offering a novel and effective approach to addressing the problem of domain discovery. Addressing the weaknesses related to kernel sensitivity and providing a more detailed analysis of the discovered domains would further strengthen the paper. The clarity and strong experimental results make this a valuable contribution to NIPS.
