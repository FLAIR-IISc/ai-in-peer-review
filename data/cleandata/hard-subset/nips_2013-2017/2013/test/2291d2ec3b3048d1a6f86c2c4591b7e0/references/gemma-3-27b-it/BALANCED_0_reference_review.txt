This paper tackles the important problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition by questioning the common practice of equating datasets with domains. The authors argue that visual datasets often contain multiple, naturally occurring domains and propose methods to automatically “reshape” these datasets into more characteristic domains, based on the principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. They present a non-parametric approach for domain discovery, coupled with a cross-validation strategy to determine the optimal number of domains. Experiments on object recognition and human activity recognition demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach, showing improved classification results when adapting classifiers learned on the discovered domains.

The paper addresses a significant issue in domain adaptation – the often-overlooked heterogeneity *within* datasets. The current trend of treating entire datasets as single domains can be suboptimal, and the authors convincingly argue for a more nuanced approach. The concepts of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability provide a solid theoretical foundation for their method. The use of kernel methods for measuring domain discrepancies is appropriate, and the continuous relaxation of the optimization problem makes it tractable. The experimental validation on multiple datasets and tasks is commendable. The comprehensive related work section effectively situates the contribution within the broader context of domain adaptation and clustering. The ablative study in section 4.3 is particularly valuable, demonstrating the benefits of reshaping *both* the source and target datasets. The inclusion of visual examples of the discovered domains (Figure 1) offers insightful qualitative analysis.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. While the motivation is well-articulated, the connection between the theoretical properties (distinctiveness and learnability) and the actual performance gains feels somewhat loose. The paper doesn't fully explain *why* reshaping into these discovered domains leads to better generalization. Is it simply better separation of data points, or is there a more fundamental benefit related to disentangling confounding factors? The explanation of the Label Prior Constraint (LPC) could be more intuitive; the current wording makes it difficult to grasp its importance. The optimization problem, even in its relaxed form, seems computationally intensive. While the authors mention using “off-the-shelf” solvers, providing more detail about the computational cost and scalability would be beneficial, especially for larger datasets. The experimental setup, although thorough, lacks comparison to more recent and sophisticated domain adaptation techniques beyond those mentioned in the related work.  Specifically, recent advances in adversarial domain adaptation or discrepancy-based methods could have provided a stronger benchmark.  The figures, while helpful, are relatively small and could benefit from higher resolution for easier visualization. The discussion on choosing K, while presenting DWCV, is a little light on practical guidance—what should a user do if the DWCV curve is flat or noisy? 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a crucial, often-overlooked issue in domain adaptation.
*   Presents a theoretically sound and novel approach for discovering latent domains.
*   Demonstrates empirical improvements on standard benchmark datasets.
*   Includes a comprehensive related work section and thorough experimental evaluation.
*   The method is broadly applicable to various visual recognition tasks.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The link between theoretical properties and performance gains could be strengthened.
*   The computational complexity of the method is not fully addressed.
*   Lacks comparison to some recent, state-of-the-art domain adaptation techniques.
*   The explanation of the LPC is not sufficiently clear.
*   The practical guidance on choosing the number of domains is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation. While there are some weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, the strong theoretical foundation, and the empirical results warrant a positive evaluation. However, the paper would benefit from addressing the concerns raised above, particularly regarding computational cost, comparison to more recent techniques, and a clearer explanation of the theoretical motivations. 

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly strengthening the discussion around computational complexity and including a more comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods. A minor revision addressing the clarity of the LPC and providing practical guidance for choosing K would also be beneficial.
