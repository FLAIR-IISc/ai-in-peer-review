Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses an underexplored dimension in domain adaptation: rather than taking datasets as pre-defined domains, it proposes to automatically reshape one or more labeled visual datasets into latent domains that are (i) maximally distinctive in distribution and (ii) sufficiently learnable to support robust discriminative models. Distinctiveness is operationalized via a kernel two-sample distance (the squared RKHS distance/MMD between empirical mean embeddings), summed over all pairs of domains. Learnability is used to select the number of domains K via a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) criterion that aggregates per-domain cross-validated accuracy weighted by domain size. The resulting optimization—assigning each instance to one of K domains while preserving per-domain class label priors—is posed as a quadratic program via a relaxation of the integer assignment; at test time, a “conditional reshaping” partitions unlabeled test data into domains that best match the discovered training domains by minimizing kernel distribution discrepancy. Experiments on Caltech-256/Amazon/DSLR/Webcam (object recognition) and IXMAS (multi-view action recognition) suggest the discovered domains outperform treating datasets as domains and improve adaptation with geodesic flow kernels [Gopalan et al. 2011; Gong et al. 2012]. The approach contrasts with Hoffman et al. [ECCV’12], which discovers latent domains via hierarchical Gaussian mixtures; the present paper avoids parametric assumptions by leveraging nonparametric kernel discrepancies (Gretton et al., two-sample test).

Strengths
- Problem framing: Clearly articulates why dataset≠domain in vision and motivates latent domain discovery as a prerequisite to effective adaptation.
- Nonparametric distinctiveness: Using MMD between mean embeddings is a principled, characteristic-kernel criterion that avoids fragile parametric mixture assumptions and is well-aligned with prior adaptation theory.
- Joint attention to learnability: Selecting K by per-domain cross-validated accuracy is pragmatic and ties domain discovery to downstream discriminative performance.
- Test-time conditional reshaping: Matching heterogeneous target mixtures to discovered source domains is practical and often neglected.
- Empirical scope: Applies to both objects and actions; qualitative analyses of discovered factors (pose, background) are informative.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Strong LPC assumption: The label prior constraint requires that each latent domain approximately matches the global class proportions and even defaults to at least one sample per class per domain. In many real settings, domain and class distributions are not independent (e.g., some classes may be absent in certain environments or views). This constraint may force unnatural partitions, suppressing genuinely domain-specific sparsity and hindering discovery quality. Consider adopting softer constraints (e.g., divergence regularization on per-domain label marginals) and evaluating cases with true class imbalance.
- Optimization clarity and guarantees: The relaxed problem maximizes a convex quadratic over a convex polytope, which is NP-hard in general; the paper states “existing solvers are adequate,” but provides no algorithmic details (solver used, initialization, runtime, convergence behavior, sensitivity to local optima). The per-instance constraint 1/M ≤ sum_k βmk ≤ 1/C is unusual and deserves justification; it appears to enforce Mk ∈ [C, M] indirectly but may be redundant or distort the feasible set.
- Risk of spurious distinctiveness: Even with LPC, maximizing pairwise MMD can encourage partitions along high-variance nuisance directions that do not correspond to stable subpopulations and may inflate distinctiveness at the expense of robustness. The learnability filter helps, but DWCV can still favor partitions that cherry-pick easier examples, especially with small domains. An explicit regularizer on domain compactness or a minimum domain size constraint could mitigate this.
- Limited theoretical grounding: There is no analysis connecting the proposed objective to target performance (e.g., via adaptation bounds involving discrepancies and hypothesis class complexity). Even a qualitative discussion aligning TDD and DWCV with known generalization bounds (e.g., Ben-David et al.) would strengthen the case.
- Experimental reporting: The manuscript references Tables 1–3 but omits them here; assuming they exist, the narrative suggests large gains, yet details on variance, statistical significance, and ablations (effect of LPC, δ, kernel bandwidth, K-selection method) are missing. Runtime/scalability with MK variables is also not reported.

Clarity
- Mathematical presentation: Equation (1) uses the RKHS squared norm; clarify whether d is MMD or MMD^2 and use consistent notation. The TDD sum appears to double-count ordered pairs; specify if it is over unordered pairs to avoid ambiguity. Explain the rationale for the per-instance constraint in Eq. (5).
- DWCV procedure: Provide explicit N-fold settings, Kmax derivation, and how ties/plateaus are handled. Clarify whether DWCV uses the same features/classifiers later used for adaptation and ensure no leakage from target data.
- Conditional reshaping: The optimization for test partitioning is relegated to supplemental; a brief main-text formulation would help readers assess tractability and potential degeneracies.

Originality
- The combination of nonparametric discrepancy maximization with label-prior-constrained assignment and a learnability-based K selection appears novel in the context of latent domain discovery for vision. Prior work [Hoffman et al. 2012] uses parametric mixtures; generalized clustering with maximum discriminability [Jegelka et al.] is related but addresses unsupervised clustering without label-aware constraints. That said, the use of MMD and kernel embeddings is well-established; the main novelty is in how it’s operationalized for domain discovery and K selection.

Significance
- If robust and generalizable, discovering subdomains could materially improve multi-source adaptation and inform dataset design. The conditional reshaping of heterogeneous test sets is particularly relevant in multi-view/action settings. However, the strength of the LPC assumption and lack of theoretical and scalability analyses temper the potential impact. Broader comparisons to alternative clustering/spectral methods and recent domain generalization approaches would help substantiate significance.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a real and understudied problem with a principled, nonparametric criterion.
- Practical K-selection via learnability and test-time conditional reshaping are useful contributions.
- Empirical results (as described) suggest clear gains over treating datasets as domains and over [20].

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong, potentially unrealistic LPC that may not hold in practice and may bias partitions.
- Optimization lacks algorithmic detail and guarantees; potential for spurious partitions.
- Missing theoretical analysis and limited ablations/scalability reporting.
- Experimental presentation (as provided) omits key quantitative tables and significance analysis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Relax LPC: Replace hard matching of label priors with a divergence penalty (e.g., KL or chi-square) and allow classes to be absent in some domains; add minimum domain size constraints.
- Provide algorithmic details: Solver, initialization, complexity, convergence diagnostics; include runtime on each dataset; discuss sensitivity to kernel choice/bandwidth and δ.
- Add ablations: No-LPC vs LPC; distinctiveness-only vs +DWCV; different K-selection criteria; robustness to class imbalance.
- Strengthen theory: Connect TDD/DWCV to adaptation error bounds; analyze when partitions improve multi-source adaptation.
- Expand baselines: Kernel k-means/spectral clustering with label-proportion control; mixture-of-experts/domain assignment baselines; recent subdomain adaptation methods.
- Clarify conditional reshaping formulation and guard against degenerate assignments.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The problem is important and the approach is promising, but the current manuscript relies on a strong label prior assumption, lacks optimization and theoretical clarity, and needs more thorough experiments and ablations to meet the NeurIPS standard. I encourage the authors to revise along the lines above. Confidence: moderate.
