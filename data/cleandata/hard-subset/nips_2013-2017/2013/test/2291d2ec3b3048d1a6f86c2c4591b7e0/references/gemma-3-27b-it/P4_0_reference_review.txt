This paper proposes a method for automatically reshaping visual datasets into domains for domain adaptation. The core idea is to discover latent domains based on two principles: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. Distinctiveness is quantified using a kernel-based measure of distribution difference, and learnability is assessed via domain-wise cross-validation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, showing improvements over existing methods.

The paper addresses a crucial and often overlooked aspect of domain adaptation: the inherent structure *within* datasets. The common practice of equating datasets with domains can be limiting, as a single dataset may contain multiple underlying distributions. By proposing an unsupervised method to identify these latent domains, the authors aim to improve adaptation performance. The two proposed properties – maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability – are intuitively appealing and well-motivated. The use of kernel methods for measuring distribution discrepancy is also reasonable. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The problem of automatically discovering latent domains within datasets is relatively unexplored, particularly within the domain adaptation literature.  While [20] addresses a similar problem, the authors clearly articulate the differences and advantages of their nonparametric approach.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The formulation of the problem and the proposed optimization techniques appear theoretically sound. The connection to kernel methods provides a strong foundation.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, proposed approach, and experimental setup. The axioms of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability are clearly articulated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both object recognition and action recognition demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The comparison to baseline methods and [20] is thorough. The supplemental material appears to offer further details.
* **Significance:**  Addressing the implicit structure within datasets is a significant contribution, with the potential to improve the performance of domain adaptation algorithms.  The ability to automatically discover these domains removes a significant burden from practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** The optimization problem, even in its relaxed form, is still computationally demanding, especially for large datasets. The paper does not thoroughly discuss the scalability of the approach.
* **Kernel Choice:** The performance of the method is sensitive to the choice of kernel and bandwidth. While the authors use a standard approach for bandwidth selection, a more detailed analysis of the impact of different kernel choices would be beneficial.
* **Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** While the intuition behind the LPC is understandable, its impact on the results is not fully explored. The choice of δ = 1% seems somewhat arbitrary.
* **DWCV Complexity**: DWCV adds significant computational overhead. Although justified, a comparison against cheaper methods of K selection would be insightful.
* **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates improved performance, a more detailed error analysis would be helpful. Understanding the types of errors that are still being made could provide insights for future work.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a novel and important problem in domain adaptation.
* The proposed approach is theoretically sound and empirically validated.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key concepts.
* The results demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity of the approach may limit its applicability to large datasets.
* The sensitivity to kernel choice and the arbitrary parameter settings require further investigation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The proposed method for automatically reshaping datasets into domains is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses related to computational complexity and parameter sensitivity, these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance.
