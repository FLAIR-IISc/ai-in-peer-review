This paper tackles a surprisingly fundamental and often overlooked issue in domain adaptation: what *is* a domain, and how do we define it when dealing with visual data where explicit domain labels are absent? The authors rightly point out the common, and potentially flawed, practice of equating datasets with domains. Their core idea – to *reshape* datasets into more meaningful, latent domains based on maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability – is genuinely novel and represents a bold step beyond simply adapting between pre-defined datasets. While the methodological details are somewhat dense and the validation isn’t exhaustive, the conceptual contribution is significant and warrants acceptance.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The central premise of discovering latent domains within a dataset is a fresh perspective in domain adaptation. It shifts the focus from adaptation *between* datasets to better understanding the inherent structure *within* them.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) provide a principled, if challenging, framework for domain discovery.  The attempt to formalize these ideas into an optimization problem is commendable.
* **Potential Impact:** If successful, this approach could lead to more robust and effective domain adaptation techniques, particularly in scenarios where clear domain boundaries are lacking.  The idea of reshaping *test* datasets, matching them to learned training domains, is especially insightful.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results, while not overwhelmingly conclusive, demonstrate a clear benefit of reshaping datasets compared to using them directly, and outperform related work. The results on action recognition, where the view-dependent nature of the data benefits from domain reshaping, are particularly compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Methodological Complexity:** The optimization formulation, involving kernel methods and constraints, is complex and might be difficult to implement and tune in practice. The explanation could benefit from more intuitive examples.
* **Kernel Choice:** The sensitivity of the method to kernel choice and bandwidth selection isn't thoroughly discussed. While Gaussian kernels are standard, a more rigorous analysis of the impact of different kernel parameters would be beneficial.
* **Validation Limited:** The experiments primarily focus on relatively standard datasets.  Exploring more complex, real-world scenarios with less controlled variations would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Computational Cost:** The continuous relaxation of the integer programming problem, while necessary for tractability, introduces approximations. The impact of this relaxation on the quality of the discovered domains isn't fully analyzed.
* **Clarity:** While the core idea is clearly presented, the notation and some sections (e.g., the label prior constraint) could be presented more concisely and intuitively.



**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental problem in domain adaptation with a novel and theoretically motivated approach.
* The experimental results provide encouraging evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The idea of reshaping both training and test datasets is a significant contribution.
* The paper opens up new avenues for research in domain adaptation.

**Con:**

* The methodology is complex and may be difficult to apply in practice.
* The validation is limited to relatively standard datasets.
* The impact of the approximations introduced by the continuous relaxation isn't fully understood.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a genuinely novel and potentially impactful approach to domain adaptation. While the methodology is complex and the validation is not exhaustive, the conceptual contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance. I believe the potential benefits of discovering and exploiting latent domain structure within datasets outweigh the current limitations. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges in domain adaptation and offer a promising new direction for future research.  Minor revisions addressing clarity and a more thorough discussion of kernel choice would further strengthen the paper.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
