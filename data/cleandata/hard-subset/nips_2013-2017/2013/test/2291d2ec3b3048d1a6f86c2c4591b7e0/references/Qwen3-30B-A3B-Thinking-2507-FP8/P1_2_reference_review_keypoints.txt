- Paper identifies that visual datasets often contain multiple latent domains (pose, illumination, background) rather than representing a single domain.  
- Proposes a framework to reshape datasets into latent domains using two axioms: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.  
- Uses nonparametric kernel-based measures for domain differences and a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to select the number of domains K.  
- Demonstrates significant improvements on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human action recognition (IXMAS).  
- Method differs from prior latent-domain work (e.g., Hoffman et al.) by using a nonparametric (not Gaussian-mixture) approach suited to complex visual data.  
- Strong technical formulation: well-motivated axioms, principled optimization, and appropriate relaxation to a continuous problem.  
- Experimental evaluation is thorough and compares favorably to original dataset domains and competing methods, including conditional reshaping of test data.  
- Lacks detailed analysis of computational complexity, scalability, and runtime comparisons; DWCV may be computationally heavy.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a clear problem statement and experimental setup.  
- Could provide more intuition on how the approach differs from standard clustering and why appearance-based clustering can produce per-category domains.  
- The relaxation step (Section 2.1) is presented somewhat abruptly and would benefit from more intuitive justification.  
- Original contribution is significant: reframes a fundamental assumption in domain adaptation and introduces novel axiomatic criteria for latent-domain discovery.  
- Nonparametric approach is a notable strength for handling non-Gaussian, complex visual distributions.  
- Work has practical significance for dataset construction and real-world domain-shift scenarios, showing consistent, substantial performance gains.  
- Strengths: addresses a fundamental overlooked problem, principled axioms, strong empirical gains, handles train/test reshaping, informs dataset collection.  
- Weaknesses: limited scalability/complexity analysis, insufficient motivation for relaxation, need clearer contrast with clustering, unclear handling of non-separable domains.  
- Recommendation: accept at NIPS — technically sound, significant, and of clear interest to the community.
