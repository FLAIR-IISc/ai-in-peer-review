### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of discovering latent domains within existing visual datasets. The authors argue that the common practice in domain adaptation of treating entire datasets as single, monolithic domains is flawed, as these datasets are often agglomerations of several distinct underlying data distributions. The proposed solution is to "reshape" datasets into a set of latent domains by optimizing for two key properties: "maximum distinctiveness," ensuring the discovered domains are statistically dissimilar, and "maximum learnability," ensuring each domain is sufficiently large and coherent to train a robust classifier. The distinctiveness is measured non-parametrically using a kernel-based two-sample test metric, summed over all pairs of domains. The number of domains, K, is chosen via a "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) procedure that maximizes classification performance within the discovered domains. Experiments on object and action recognition tasks show that performing domain adaptation from these discovered domains yields better performance than adapting from the original, un-reshaped datasets.

### Strengths

*   **Important Problem:** The paper's core premise—that the "dataset-as-domain" assumption is a significant pitfall in domain adaptation research—is insightful and addresses a fundamental issue in the field. Formulating a method to automatically discover more coherent domains is a valuable contribution.
*   **Principled Formulation:** The two proposed axiomatic properties, maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability, provide an intuitive and sound conceptual basis for the problem.
*   **Non-parametric Approach:** The use of a kernel-based distance (MMD) to measure domain distinctiveness is a major strength, as it avoids making restrictive parametric assumptions about the underlying distributions of the latent domains, a potential limitation of prior work like Hoffman et al. [20].
*   **Novelty:** To my knowledge, this is one of the first works to propose a concrete, non-parametric optimization framework for latent domain discovery, coupled with a practical method for model selection (determining K).

### Weaknesses

Despite the compelling high-level idea, the paper suffers from a significant number of technical ambiguities, strong and poorly-justified assumptions, and a general lack of rigor in its presentation.

**1. Methodological & Technical Clarity:**

*   **Optimization Formulation:** The description of the optimization problem is opaque. In Section 2.1, the relaxation introduces variables `βmk = zmk/Mk`. This is a peculiar choice. The text states `βk` lives on a simplex `∆` where `∑m βmk = 1`. This constraint implies `∑m (zmk/Mk) = (1/Mk) ∑m zmk = Mk/Mk = 1`, which is a tautology and seems to add no value. The derivation of the constraints in Eq. (5) is not explained. For instance, `1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C` is presented without any derivation from the base constraints `C ≤ Mk ≤ M`. While the base constraints are reasonable, the transformation into the final form is non-obvious and should be shown.
*   **NP-Hardness and Solvers:** The paper states that the relaxed problem (maximization of a convex quadratic function) is still NP-hard. This is true, but the objective `∑k≠k' (βk - βk')T K (βk - βk')` is not immediately identifiable as convex in the joint variable `β`. This requires more careful analysis. The claim that "existing solvers are adequate in yielding satisfactory solutions" is insufficient for a top-tier conference paper. Which solvers were used? What are the convergence guarantees, if any? How sensitive is the solution to initialization? This lack of detail makes the work difficult to reproduce and assess.
*   **Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The LPC (Eq. 3) is a very strong assumption. It forces the class distribution within *every* discovered domain to be identical to the global class distribution. The justification provided is anecdotal. It is highly plausible that certain domains (e.g., a "water background" domain) would naturally be biased towards certain classes (e.g., "boats"). This constraint could prevent the discovery of meaningful, naturally-occurring domains. A critical ablation study removing or relaxing this constraint is absent, making it impossible to judge its impact or necessity.
*   **Determining K:** The upper bound for Kmax in the DWCV procedure, `min{M/(NC),C}`, is not clearly justified. Furthermore, the paper does not state which classifier is used for the cross-validation. This is a crucial detail for reproducibility. Is it a simple SVM, or the same geodesic flow kernel method used for the final evaluation?
*   **Conditional Reshaping:** The entire description of conditional reshaping for the test set is relegated to "Suppl. Material." This is a key part of the experimental results in Table 3, and its computational details should be summarized in the main paper.

**2. Presentation and Formatting:**

*   **Citations:** The reference list is improperly formatted. A vast majority of the citations are incomplete, missing venue, year, and page information (e.g., "[1] In CVPR,, , ,"). This pervasive issue suggests a lack of care and professionalism in the manuscript's preparation.
*   **Notation:** The notation in Eq. (1) is ambiguous. `zmk'` seems to be used where `zmk'` should be indexed by `m`, not by `k'`. The text also refers to `M'k`, which is presumably `Mk'`, but this is not defined.
*   **Minor Errors:** There are several minor typos and grammatical errors. For example, names of actors are not capitalized ("alba, andreas..."). In Section 4.4, "K?" appears to be a typo for "K*".

### Decision

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper introduces a novel and important problem, the execution is unfortunately not up to NIPS standards. The core technical sections lack the clarity and detail required for a top-tier publication. The optimization procedure is poorly explained, the Label Prior Constraint is a very strong assumption that is not experimentally justified, and crucial details about the implementation and model selection are missing. These technical shortcomings are compounded by a general sloppiness in presentation, most notably the incomplete and poorly formatted bibliography.

Although the experimental results appear promising, the lack of clarity in the methodology makes it difficult to have full confidence in them. The paper presents a good idea, but it requires significant revision to clarify the technical details, justify its core assumptions through ablation studies, and adhere to basic academic presentation standards. I would be open to reconsidering a future submission that addresses these substantial issues.
