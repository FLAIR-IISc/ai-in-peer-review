

# Review: Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation

This paper proposes a novel approach to discover latent domains within visual datasets by maximizing two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains are maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains contain sufficient data to train good classifiers). The authors argue that standard visual domain adaptation research mistakenly treats datasets as single domains when they may actually contain multiple underlying domains, leading to suboptimal performance. Their approach uses nonparametric kernel-based methods to reshape datasets into characteristic domains.

**Strengths:**
The paper identifies an important conceptual gap in domain adaptation research, recognizing that visual datasets often contain multiple latent domains rather than being naturally partitioned into discrete domains. The proposed method of reshaping datasets into domains based on distributional differences is novel and addresses a real problem in the field. The experimental results on object and action recognition datasets demonstrate that the identified domains lead to improved adaptation performance compared to using the original datasets or domains discovered by prior work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Critical Notational and Conceptual Errors:** The relaxation of the optimization problem in Section 2.1 contains a fundamental error. The authors define βmk = zmk/Mk, but zmk is binary (0 or 1) and Mk is the count of instances assigned to domain k, so βmk would be either 0 or 1/Mk, not a continuous variable as claimed. This invalidates the continuous relaxation approach. The constraint "1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C" is dimensionally inconsistent - βmk is a probability value (0-1), but the bounds are expressed in terms of M (number of instances) and C (number of classes), which makes no sense.

2. **Mischaracterization of Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The paper states that "the label prior constraint (LPC) requires that within each domain, the class labels are distributed according to the prior distribution (of the labels), estimated empirically from the labeled data." However, the constraint as written (equation 3) does not enforce this - it only requires that the domain's class distribution is approximately equal to the overall class distribution, not the prior. In fact, the constraint would be violated if the dataset has imbalanced classes.

3. **Technical Ambiguities in DWCV:** The domain-wise cross-validation procedure (Section 2.2) claims that "for very large K such that each domain contains only a few examples, A(K) approaches the classification accuracy using the class prior probability to classify." This is misleading - the accuracy using class prior would be the accuracy of predicting the most frequent class, which is independent of K. The authors should clarify this relationship.

4. **Missing Implementation Details:** The paper fails to specify critical implementation details for the DWCV procedure, such as how to handle domains with insufficient data (e.g., domains with only one or zero examples of a class), which the authors acknowledge in Figure 2 but don't address in the text.

5. **Inconsistent Terminology:** The paper uses "domain" to refer to both the latent categories discovered by the method and the original datasets (e.g., "the domains identified by our method" vs. "the original datasets"). This creates confusion about whether they're referring to the discovered domains or the original dataset boundaries.

6. **Formatting Inconsistencies:** Multiple typographical errors appear throughout the text, including inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "1Mk" instead of "1/Mk" in equation 1), and inconsistent use of capitalization for terms like "Domain" and "domain."

7. **Lack of Quantitative Analysis:** The paper claims that "the main factors here are probably background and object appearance (color and shape)" based on qualitative examples (Figure 1), but provides no quantitative analysis to support this claim. Without statistical evidence, this remains speculative.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper identifies an important conceptual issue in domain adaptation research and proposes a novel method to address it, the technical execution contains critical flaws that undermine the validity of the approach. The fundamental error in the relaxation of the optimization problem, combined with the missing implementation details and inconsistent terminology, makes it difficult to evaluate the method's true contribution. The experimental results appear promising but are based on a flawed theoretical foundation. The paper would benefit from a complete revision to correct these technical issues before it can be considered for publication.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's technical flaws are substantial and cannot be easily fixed by minor revisions. The fundamental error in the relaxation of the optimization problem invalidates the core methodological contribution, making the experimental results questionable. While the problem addressed is important, the paper does not provide a sound technical solution.
