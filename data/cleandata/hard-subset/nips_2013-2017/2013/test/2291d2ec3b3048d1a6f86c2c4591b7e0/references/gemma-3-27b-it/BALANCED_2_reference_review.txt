This paper addresses the important problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition, specifically focusing on the often-overlooked question of *what constitutes a domain* when dealing with images. The authors argue that standard datasets are often aggregations of multiple, implicitly-defined domains and propose a novel method to automatically “reshape” datasets into more characteristic domains based on the principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The approach uses nonparametric methods to measure domain discrepancies and a domain-wise cross-validation strategy to determine the optimal number of domains. Experiments on object recognition and human activity recognition demonstrate improved performance compared to baseline methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Importance:** The paper tackles a critical and often-overlooked issue in domain adaptation – the definition of a domain itself.  Moving beyond simply treating datasets as domains is a valuable contribution.
* **Clear Motivation and Intuition:** The authors clearly articulate the problem and motivate their approach with a compelling example (viewpoint variations in action recognition). The concepts of “maximum distinctiveness” and “maximum learnability” are intuitive and well-defined.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method, while complex, is well-described and appears technically sound. The use of kernel methods for measuring distribution discrepancies and the optimization strategies are reasonable and appropriately justified.  The relaxation of the integer programming problem is a necessary and standard approach.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both object recognition and action recognition tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparisons to baseline methods, including the related work by Hoffman et al. [20], are thorough.  The ablation study with reshaping the test datasets is particularly valuable.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a comprehensive review of relevant literature in domain adaptation and related areas. The discussion of related works like [20] is particularly thorough and helps position the current contribution.
* **Detailed Experimental Setup:** The experimental section is well-written and provides sufficient detail to reproduce the results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention the use of optimization packages, the computational cost of the proposed method, especially for large datasets and many potential domains, is not fully discussed. The scalability of the approach is a concern. The runtime of the experiments should be included.
* **Sensitivity to Kernel Parameters:** The performance of the method likely depends on the choice of kernel and its bandwidth. A more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would be beneficial.  The justification for the bandwidth selection (twice the median distance) is relatively brief.
* **Limited Exploration of Domain Interpretability:** While the authors provide some qualitative examples of the discovered domains (Figure 1), a more in-depth analysis of *what* these domains represent would be valuable.  Can the identified domains be meaningfully related to real-world factors like lighting conditions, camera angles, or object poses? More in-depth analysis could also explore how domain assignments affect individual instances.
* **Conditional Reshaping Simplification:** The description of conditional reshaping for the test dataset appears somewhat brief. A more detailed explanation of the optimization problem and the implementation details would be helpful.
* **Minor Clarity Issue:** The phrase “Goodness of the identified domains” in Section 4.2 feels slightly awkward. Perhaps “Quality of identified domains” would be a more natural phrasing.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant and previously under-explored problem in domain adaptation.
* The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and well-motivated.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The computational complexity of the method may limit its applicability to large-scale datasets.
* The sensitivity to kernel parameters and the lack of in-depth domain interpretability analyses are potential weaknesses.
* The implementation details of the conditional reshaping strategy are not fully explained.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The authors identify a critical issue – the implicit definition of domains in visual datasets – and propose a novel and effective method to address it. While there are some potential weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

* Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method and its scalability.
* Conduct a sensitivity analysis of the results to the choice of kernel and its bandwidth.
* Expand the analysis of the discovered domains to provide more insights into *what* these domains represent in terms of real-world factors.
* Provide a more detailed explanation of the conditional reshaping strategy for the test dataset.
* Consider refining the phrasing of "Goodness of the identified domains" to "Quality of identified domains".
* Include runtime information for the experiments.
