This paper proposes a novel approach to domain adaptation by automatically *reshaping* datasets into more characteristic domains, rather than treating entire datasets as single domains. The core idea revolves around identifying latent domains that maximize both distinctiveness and learnability, formulated as a non-parametric optimization problem. The authors present a comprehensive evaluation on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, demonstrating improved performance compared to baseline methods and a previous work on latent domain discovery.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of reshaping datasets into latent domains is a fresh perspective in domain adaptation, moving away from the common practice of equating datasets with domains. This addresses a critical, yet often overlooked, issue in the field.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method, based on maximizing distinctiveness and learnability, is well-motivated and mathematically formulated. The use of kernel methods for measuring domain discrepancy is appropriate, and the continuous relaxation of the integer programming problem is a reasonable approach for practical implementation. The DWCV approach for determining the number of domains is sensible and addresses a key hyperparameter tuning challenge.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and motivates the proposed solution. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are accessible.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The authors provide extensive experimental results on multiple datasets and tasks, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach. The comparison with existing methods, including a recent work by Hoffman et al. (2017), is valuable. The ablation studies (reshaping test datasets) provide further insights.
*   **Significance:**  The results indicate a potential for significant improvement in domain adaptation performance by considering the internal structure of datasets.  The idea of reshaping datasets could influence how datasets are designed and utilized in the future.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** While the continuous relaxation mitigates some of the computational challenges, the optimization problem still appears computationally expensive, particularly for large datasets. The paper does not provide detailed analysis of the computational cost. Scalability to very large datasets could be a concern.
*   **Kernel Bandwidth Selection:** The choice of the Gaussian kernel bandwidth, set to twice the median distance, is somewhat arbitrary. While it is a common heuristic, a more principled or data-driven approach to bandwidth selection could improve robustness.
*   **Label Prior Constraint (LPC) Justification:** The LPC is intuitively appealing, but the justification for its use (i.e., preserving label percentages) could be strengthened. It's not immediately clear why enforcing this constraint is crucial for domain discovery. A more thorough analysis of its impact on performance would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on successful applications. It would be helpful to discuss scenarios where the proposed method might struggle or fail and to identify potential limitations.
*   **Reproducibility:** While the paper provides details on the experimental setup, it lacks sufficient information for complete reproducibility. Details regarding the implementation of the optimization algorithms and the specific kernel parameters used would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant and often overlooked problem in domain adaptation: the internal structure of datasets.
*   The proposed approach is novel, technically sound, and well-motivated.
*   The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate clear improvements over baseline methods.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the proposed method could be a limiting factor.
*   The kernel bandwidth selection and the justification for the LPC are somewhat arbitrary.
*   The paper lacks a thorough discussion of failure cases and potential limitations.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The proposed approach of reshaping datasets into latent domains is novel and promising, and the experimental results are convincing. While there are some minor weaknesses related to computational complexity and parameter selection, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above. Specifically, the authors should provide more detailed analysis of computational cost, explore alternative approaches to kernel bandwidth selection, and provide a more thorough justification for the LPC. Details about reproducibility should also be improved.
