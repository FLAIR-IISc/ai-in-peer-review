This paper addresses the critical problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition, specifically focusing on the often-overlooked aspect of *how* domains are defined and formed within datasets. The authors correctly identify the limitations of equating datasets with domains and propose a novel approach to automatically reshape datasets into more characteristic domains based on principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. While the core ideas are interesting and potentially impactful, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, stylistic inconsistencies, and a lack of rigorous experimental detail, ultimately hindering its overall quality.

**Summary of Ideas and Relation to Prior Work:**

The central contribution lies in formulating domain discovery as an optimization problem that seeks domains exhibiting both high inter-domain variance (distinctiveness) and good discriminative performance (learnability). This is a valuable direction, moving beyond simply applying adaptation techniques *assuming* domain definitions. The paper appropriately positions itself within the domain adaptation literature (citing relevant works like [2, 4, 9, 10]) and acknowledges prior work on latent domain discovery [20], promising a detailed comparison later (which, unfortunately, feels somewhat superficial in Section 3). The connection to clustering is also well articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Perspective:** The paper raises a vital question about how we conceptualize domains in visual data, a topic often glossed over in practice.
* **Principled Approach:** Framing domain discovery based on “maximum distinctiveness” and “maximum learnability” provides a solid theoretical foundation.
* **Non-parametric Formulation:** Avoiding strong parametric assumptions about domain distributions is a sensible choice, given the complexity of visual data.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper presents results on both object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, demonstrating some improvement through domain reshaping.

**Weaknesses:**

This is where the review becomes lengthy. The issues are largely *not* major conceptual flaws, but a consistent pattern of carelessness and insufficient detail.

* **Notational Inconsistency:** The use of `zm ∈ [K]` to denote a domain assignment *and* `k ∈ [K]` to index domains is confusing. It would be clearer to use `z_i ∈ {1, ..., K}` for instance assignment and `k` directly.
* **Equation (1):** The notation `d(k, k′)` is defined as a norm but the norm is not explicitly stated (is it L2?). Similarly, the kernel function `k(·, ·)` is only mentioned as “characteristic” without specifying *which* kernel is used in the experiments (Gaussian? Other?).
* **Optimization Details:** The relaxation of the integer programming problem is presented, but the specifics of the solver used and its convergence criteria are missing. The constraint `1/M ≤ ∑ k βmk ≤ 1/C` (Equation 5) feels arbitrary—where does ‘C’ come from? It’s stated as “at least one instance per category,” but that doesn’t logically necessitate this constraint.
* **LPC Motivation:** The justification for the Label Prior Constraint (LPC) is weak. While the authors’ intuition is reasonable, the mathematical formulation appears ad-hoc and its impact on the results is not thoroughly analyzed.  The allowed deviation of 1% (δ = 1%) is also arbitrary.
* **DWCV Implementation:** The details of the cross-validation procedure are skimpy. What is N (number of folds)? What metric is used to evaluate the classifiers within each domain?
* **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental section is surprisingly lacking in detail.  Image representations (SURF features with a 800-bin BoW) are mentioned, but hyperparameter tuning isn’t discussed. The kernel bandwidth for the distribution difference calculation is set to “twice the median distances” without justification. The impact of different kernel choices is unexplored. Tables 1 and 2 have vague captions, and the significance of the improvements is not statistically tested.
* **Comparison to [20]:** The promised detailed comparison to Hoffman et al. [20] is weak. It states their approach’s limitation (Gaussian mixture assumption) but doesn’t provide a side-by-side quantitative comparison on the same datasets with the same evaluation metrics.
* **Figure 1 Caption:** "Domain II all the ‘laptop’ images..." This is grammatically incorrect.
* **Typos and Minor Errors:** Scattered throughout the text (e.g., "surfaces" instead of "surfaces", inconsistent use of commas, unclear phrasing).

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* The paper identifies a crucial, under-explored aspect of domain adaptation.
* The proposed method offers a principled approach to domain discovery.
* The empirical results, while not overwhelmingly convincing, suggest a potential benefit.

**Con:**

* The numerous minor flaws and lack of experimental detail significantly detract from the paper’s credibility.
* The comparison to related work, particularly [20], is inadequate.
* The mathematical formulation feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks thorough justification.
* The writing quality is inconsistent and riddled with minor errors.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the core idea, I lean towards **rejection**. The paper feels incomplete and underdeveloped. The numerous small flaws, while individually minor, collectively create a sense of sloppiness that undermines confidence in the results.  Significant revisions, addressing the points raised above (especially adding more experimental detail, providing a rigorous comparison to [20], and refining the mathematical formulation), would be required for reconsideration. The core idea deserves further exploration, but this submission is not yet up to NIPS standards.
