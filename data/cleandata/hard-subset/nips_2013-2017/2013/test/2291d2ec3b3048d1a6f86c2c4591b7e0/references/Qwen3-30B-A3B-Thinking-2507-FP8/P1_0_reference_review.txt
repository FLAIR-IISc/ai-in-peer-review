

# Peer Review: "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental issue in domain adaptation: the problematic assumption that datasets correspond to well-defined domains. The authors argue that visual datasets often contain multiple latent domains (e.g., due to varying poses, lighting, backgrounds) that are not naturally separable, yet current domain adaptation research treats entire datasets as single domains. They propose a novel approach to automatically reshape datasets into latent domains with two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains containing sufficient data for strong classifiers). Their method uses nonparametric kernel-based measures to quantify domain differences and a domain-wise cross-validation procedure to determine the optimal number of domains. The approach is evaluated on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human action recognition (IXMAS) tasks, showing significant improvements over using original datasets or domains discovered by prior work.

## Strengths

**Originality (High):** The paper identifies a critical gap in domain adaptation research that has been overlooked—datasets often contain multiple latent domains that are not naturally separable. The concept of "reshaping" datasets into characteristic domains is novel and represents a significant conceptual contribution. The two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and learnability) provide a principled framework for domain discovery that differs substantially from prior clustering approaches.

**Significance (High):** The problem addressed is fundamental to domain adaptation, with direct implications for improving practical performance. The results demonstrate clear improvements over existing approaches (including [20], the closest prior work), showing that reshaping datasets leads to better adaptation performance. The insights about what factors dominate latent domains (e.g., object pose, appearance, background) also provide valuable guidance for dataset construction.

**Quality (High):** The technical approach is sound, with well-motivated theoretical foundations and a clear optimization procedure. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets and tasks, and the results robustly support the claims. The DWCV method for determining the optimal number of domains is a particularly strong contribution that addresses a critical practical challenge.

**Clarity (Moderate-High):** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a strong motivation and clear problem statement. The technical details are well-explained, though some aspects of the optimization relaxation could be clarified. The experimental results are presented effectively with appropriate comparisons.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity (Moderate):** While the overall paper is well-written, some technical details in Section 2.1 could be clarified. For instance, the relaxation from integer programming to continuous optimization (Equation 4-5) could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why this relaxation preserves the key properties of the original problem. Additionally, the relationship between the label prior constraint and domain distinctiveness could be better explained.

**Quality (Moderate):** The paper assumes that the test domains are homogeneous, which may not always be the case in practice. While they address heterogeneous test datasets in Section 4.3 (conditional reshaping), the main focus is on reshaping training datasets. The paper doesn't fully address how their approach would handle scenarios where both training and test datasets contain multiple latent domains that aren't aligned.

**Significance (Moderate):** The paper's main contribution is methodological, but it doesn't fully explore the broader implications of their findings for dataset construction. While they mention this in the conclusion, the paper could have provided more concrete suggestions for how dataset creators could use their insights to design better datasets.

## Specific Comments

1. **Technical Clarity:** The relaxation to continuous optimization (Equation 4) is a critical step, but the explanation of why this relaxation preserves the key properties of the original problem is somewhat brief. A sentence or two explaining why the relaxation is reasonable would strengthen the paper.

2. **Experimental Design:** The paper compares their approach to [20] and K-means, but doesn't compare to more recent domain adaptation methods that might incorporate some form of domain discovery. Including a comparison to recent state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods would better demonstrate the practical utility of their reshaping approach.

3. **DWCV Limitation:** The DWCV method requires labeled data for the training domains to estimate domain-wise classification accuracy. This is a significant limitation in scenarios where labels are scarce or unavailable for the training domains. The paper acknowledges this as a limitation but could discuss potential approaches to address it.

4. **Figure 1:** The example images in Figure 1 are helpful, but the paper could provide more detailed analysis of why the identified domains correspond to specific factors (e.g., why Domain II in Amazon has colorful screens). This would strengthen the argument that the discovered domains align with meaningful visual factors.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation by addressing a fundamental problem that has been overlooked in prior work. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear improvements over existing methods. The paper's technical quality is high, and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-executed.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper is a valuable contribution to the field with clear significance and strong technical foundation. The minor weaknesses noted are not sufficient to outweigh the paper's substantial contributions.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro:**
- Addresses a critical, previously overlooked problem in domain adaptation
- Introduces novel axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and learnability) that provide a principled framework for domain discovery
- Presents a well-motivated, technically sound approach with strong empirical results
- Demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods on multiple datasets and tasks
- Provides valuable insights into the factors that define latent domains in visual recognition

**Con:**
- Some technical details in the optimization relaxation could be clarified
- The paper doesn't fully address scenarios with heterogeneous test domains (though this is addressed in Section 4.3)
- The DWCV method requires labeled data, which could limit applicability in some settings

Overall, the paper's strengths far outweigh its weaknesses, and it represents a significant contribution to the field that will likely be of interest to the NIPS community.
