

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper proposes a novel approach to domain adaptation by automatically reshaping visual datasets into characteristic latent domains, based on two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The authors argue that standard visual datasets are not deliberately collected with identifiable domains, and that treating datasets as single domains leads to suboptimal adaptation performance. Their method identifies latent domains by maximizing domain distinctiveness (via kernel-based distribution differences) and learnability (via domain-wise cross-validation).

## Strengths
The paper addresses a meaningful gap in domain adaptation literature by questioning the fundamental assumption that datasets correspond to well-defined domains. The two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) provide a principled approach to domain discovery. The experimental results on object and action recognition datasets demonstrate consistent improvements over using original datasets as domains. The approach is well-motivated and the problem is relevant to the broader domain adaptation community.

## Weaknesses

### Mathematical and Technical Inconsistencies
1. **Equation (1) contains a grammatical error**: "the the difference" should be "the difference."

2. **Notational inconsistencies**: In equation (4), the optimization objective uses (βk - βk')TK(βk - βk'), but βk is a vector of length M, making the subtraction of two vectors dimensionally inconsistent with the kernel matrix K (M×M).

3. **Mischaracterization of label prior constraint**: Equation (3) states "1/Mk ∑m zmk ymc = 1/M ∑m ymc" as the label prior constraint (LPC), but this would require perfect class balance across domains, which is not what the authors describe. The constraint should be proportional to the global class prior, not exactly equal to it.

4. **Incorrect relaxation**: The relaxation from integer variables zmk to βmk = zmk/Mk is mathematically unsound. The authors claim "βmk = zmk/Mk" but then treat βmk as continuous variables on the simplex, which is not a proper relaxation of the original binary constraints.

5. **DWCV implementation ambiguity**: The domain-wise cross-validation procedure (section 2.2) is unclear. The paper states "for each identified domain, we build discriminative classifiers, using the label information and evaluate them with cross-validation," but does not specify how cross-validation is performed within each domain. If the same data is used for training and validation, this constitutes data leakage.

### Methodological Concerns
1. **Kmax bound is incorrect**: The paper claims "Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC),C}" for N-fold cross-validation, but this bound is invalid. The correct bound should be based on the minimum number of samples per class (M/C), not the number of classes C.

2. **Kernel bandwidth selection**: The paper states "we set the kernel bandwidth to be twice the median distances of all pairwise data points," but this is not a standard practice. The bandwidth should be chosen via cross-validation or using a more principled heuristic.

3. **Lack of comparison with standard clustering**: The paper dismisses clustering by appearance as "prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains" but does not compare with more sophisticated clustering methods that incorporate label information (e.g., constrained clustering).

### Experimental and Presentation Issues
1. **Ambiguous terminology**: The paper introduces "GOURS" in Table 1 without defining the abbreviation earlier.

2. **Vague description of MATCHING strategy**: In Table 2, the "MATCHING" strategy is described as "a more sophisticated strategy" but without specifying how matching is performed.

3. **Missing statistical significance**: The paper claims "our method yields a better set of identified domains" but does not provide statistical significance tests for the improvements over baselines.

4. **Inconsistent kernel usage**: The paper states "we use the geodesic flow kernel for adapting classifiers" but does not explain how this relates to the kernel-based domain distinction method described in section 2.1.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper addresses an important problem in domain adaptation and presents a novel approach, the technical flaws in the methodological presentation significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The mathematical inconsistencies and ambiguous descriptions of key components (especially the relaxation and DWCV procedure) make it difficult to assess whether the proposed approach is sound. The experimental results, while promising, are not sufficiently convincing due to these methodological shortcomings.

The paper's contribution is potentially valuable, but the technical execution is flawed. The minor methodological issues identified above are not just "nitpicks" but fundamental to the correctness of the approach. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these flaws substantial enough to recommend rejection, as they prevent the reader from confidently evaluating the paper's claims.

The paper would benefit from a complete mathematical revision, clearer presentation of the DWCV procedure, and more careful comparison with related clustering approaches. Without these corrections, the paper's technical contribution cannot be properly assessed.
