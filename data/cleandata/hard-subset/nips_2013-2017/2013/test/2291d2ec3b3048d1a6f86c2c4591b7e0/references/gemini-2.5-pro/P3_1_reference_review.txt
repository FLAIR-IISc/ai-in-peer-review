**Summary**

This paper addresses a fundamental, yet often overlooked, problem in visual domain adaptation: the assumption that a dataset constitutes a single domain. The authors argue that standard datasets are often heterogeneous collections of multiple latent domains (e.g., defined by viewpoint, illumination, background). Treating such a dataset as a single source domain can hinder adaptation performance. The core contribution is a novel, unsupervised method to "reshape" a dataset by discovering these latent domains. The proposed method is based on two well-motivated principles: 1) **Maximum Distinctiveness**, which seeks a partition of the data such that the resulting domains are maximally different from each other, measured non-parametrically using a kernel-based distribution distance metric. 2) **Maximum Learnability**, which ensures that the discovered domains are useful for training classifiers. This principle is operationalized as a "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) scheme to automatically determine the optimal number of domains. The authors demonstrate on standard object and action recognition benchmarks that performing domain adaptation from these discovered domains significantly outperforms adapting from the original, monolithic datasets.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)**

1.  **Important and Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper's main strength lies in its conceptual contribution. It critically examines and challenges the prevalent "dataset-as-domain" paradigm in domain adaptation research. This is a significant and insightful observation that could influence how future research in this area is conducted, not just in terms of algorithm design but also in dataset collection and analysis.

2.  **Principled and Non-Parametric Approach:** The proposed solution is grounded in the intuitive and appealing axioms of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The use of a kernel-based, non-parametric measure (the MMD) to quantify domain distinctiveness is technically sound and avoids making restrictive parametric assumptions about the data distribution, a key advantage over prior work like Hoffman et al. [20].

3.  **Practical Solution for Model Selection:** The DWCV procedure for automatically selecting the number of domains, K, is a clever and practical contribution. It directly ties the model selection problem to the end goal of discriminative performance, which is more relevant than optimizing a clustering-specific metric.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and comprehensive.
    *   The "oracle" experiment (Table 1) provides a clear upper bound and effectively demonstrates the potential of the discovered domains compared to the original datasets and a competing method.
    *   The paper evaluates practical scenarios using multi-source adaptation techniques (Table 2), showing that the discovered domains provide tangible benefits even when target labels are unavailable.
    *   The introduction and evaluation of "conditional reshaping" for the test set is a valuable extension, particularly relevant for heterogeneous test scenarios like multi-view action recognition.
    *   The qualitative analysis (Figure 1) provides useful insights into what factors the method discovers, confirming that it captures meaningful visual properties like viewpoint, background, and object appearance.

**Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Optimization Challenges:** The relaxed optimization problem (Eq. 4) is a non-convex problem (maximization of a convex quadratic function), which is generally NP-hard. The authors state that "existing solvers are adequate," but this glosses over a significant technical challenge. The quality of the solution can be highly sensitive to initialization, and there is no guarantee of finding a global optimum. A discussion of the solver used, the stability of the solutions, and the computational complexity (especially given the M x M kernel matrix) would significantly strengthen the paper's technical depth.

2.  **The Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The constraint that class priors must be preserved across all discovered domains (Eq. 3) is a very strong assumption. While it serves the important purpose of preventing the algorithm from simply clustering by category, its validity is not universally guaranteed. For example, in a dataset of cars, the "sedan" class might be disproportionately photographed from the side view, while the "sports car" class might have more front-view shots. An ablation study analyzing the impact of this constraint (or its relaxation parameter δ) would be highly beneficial.

3.  **Clarity on the "MATCHING" method:** In Table 2, the "MATCHING" strategy achieves oracle-level performance. The paper states this is done without target labels but provides no detail on how the best source domain Uk is matched to a target domain Tl. One can infer that it is likely done by minimizing a distribution discrepancy measure like the MMD between Uk and Tl, but this crucial detail is omitted. Given its outstanding performance, a clear description of this procedure is necessary for reproducibility and proper evaluation of the claim.

4.  **Robustness of DWCV:** The DWCV curve for selecting K (Figure 2) appears to work well in the presented experiments. However, the criterion "goes up and then drops" might not be robust if the curve has multiple local maxima. A more detailed discussion of the behavior of this curve and the robustness of the selection procedure would be helpful.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation. It is original, technically solid, and addresses a fundamental problem with a well-motivated and elegant solution. The core idea of reshaping datasets into more coherent domains is novel and likely to be influential. The experimental results are strong and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. While there are some weaknesses concerning the details of the optimization and the strength of the LPC assumption, they do not undermine the core contribution. The paper is well-written and clear. It advances the state of the art and provides a new perspective on a mature research area.

I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper is thought-provoking, technically interesting, and presents results of high practical significance for the domain adaptation community.
