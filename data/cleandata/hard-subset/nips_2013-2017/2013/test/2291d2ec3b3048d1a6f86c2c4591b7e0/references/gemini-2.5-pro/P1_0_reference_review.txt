**Summary**

This paper addresses a fundamental, yet often overlooked, problem in visual domain adaptation: the common practice of equating entire datasets with single domains. The authors argue that a single dataset is often an agglomeration of multiple latent domains (e.g., different viewpoints, lighting conditions, backgrounds), and treating it as a monolithic source domain can harm adaptation performance. The core contribution is a novel, unsupervised method to "reshape" one or more source datasets into a set of more coherent latent domains. The proposed approach is based on two principles: 1) **Maximum Distinctiveness**, where the discovered domains should have maximally different distributions from one another, measured non-parametrically using a kernel-based two-sample test statistic (MMD), and 2) **Maximum Learnability**, where the discovered domains should be suitable for training strong classifiers. This second principle is used to automatically determine the optimal number of latent domains (K) via a domain-wise cross-validation procedure. The method is formulated as a quadratic program, relaxed from an integer program, and includes a crucial Label Prior Constraint (LPC) to prevent the method from simply clustering the data by class label.

The work is well-positioned relative to prior art. It is most similar to Hoffman et al. [20], which also sought to discover latent domains, but this paper's non-parametric formulation avoids the potentially restrictive Gaussian mixture model assumptions of that work. The experiments on standard object and action recognition DA benchmarks demonstrate that adapting from the discovered domains significantly outperforms using the original datasets, showing the practical utility of the proposed reshaping framework.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Significant Problem Formulation:** The paper's central premise—that the "dataset-as-domain" assumption is flawed—is insightful and important for the domain adaptation community. By formalizing a method to automatically discover more meaningful domains, this work provides a new and potentially much more effective way to approach multi-source domain adaptation problems. This is a strong conceptual contribution.

2.  **Principled and Flexible Technical Approach:** The formulation based on maximizing inter-domain MMD is elegant. Using a non-parametric kernel method is a major strength, as it avoids making strong assumptions about the underlying distributions of the latent domains, which is a key limitation of prior work like Hoffman et al. [20]. The inclusion of the Label Prior Constraint is a clever and necessary component to guide the discovery process away from trivial, class-based clustering.

3.  **A Novel Method for Model Selection:** The Domain-Wise Cross-Validation (DWCV) procedure for selecting the number of domains K is a practical and well-motivated contribution. It directly ties the unsupervised domain discovery process to the ultimate goal of supervised learning (i.e., finding domains from which one can learn good classifiers), providing a principled way to set a critical hyperparameter.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and comprehensive.
    *   The "oracle" experiments (Table 1) effectively demonstrate the potential of the discovered domains by showing they provide a better source for adaptation than the original datasets.
    *   The practical experiments using multi-source adaptation techniques (Table 2) show that this potential can be realized without access to target labels, with the proposed method outperforming baselines and a competing approach.
    *   The extension to "conditional reshaping" of the test set is a valuable addition, showing significant gains in the challenging cross-view action recognition task.
    *   The qualitative analysis in Figure 1 provides compelling visual evidence that the discovered domains capture meaningful semantic factors like viewpoint, background, and object appearance.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** The optimization problem involves solving a quadratic program where the number of variables scales with M (number of data instances) and K (number of domains). More critically, the formulation requires computing and storing an M x M kernel matrix. This is computationally prohibitive for large-scale datasets (e.g., with M > 10,000). The paper does not discuss the computational complexity or potential approximations (e.g., using random Fourier features or the Nyström method) that would be necessary to apply this method to larger problems, which is a significant limitation in the era of big data.

2.  **The Label Prior Constraint (LPC) Assumption:** The LPC, which enforces similar class priors across all discovered domains, is a very strong assumption. While it serves its purpose of preventing class-based clustering, it may not hold in reality. Certain domains might naturally have different class distributions (e.g., "car" images are more prevalent in "street scene" domains than "office" domains). It would strengthen the paper to discuss the limitations of this constraint and perhaps suggest scenarios where it might fail or could be relaxed.

3.  **Sensitivity to Kernel Choice:** The MMD metric is dependent on the choice of kernel and its hyperparameters (e.g., the bandwidth of the Gaussian kernel). The paper states the bandwidth was set based on the median pairwise distance, which is a common heuristic, but it provides no analysis of how sensitive the discovered domains are to this choice. A brief sensitivity analysis would improve the paper's completeness.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Pros):**
*   Addresses a fundamental and important problem in domain adaptation.
*   The proposed non-parametric method is novel, well-motivated, and more flexible than prior work.
*   The method for selecting the number of domains (K) is principled and practical.
*   The experimental results are strong and clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach across multiple tasks and datasets.

**Arguments for Rejection (Cons):**
*   The method does not scale to large datasets due to the M x M kernel matrix and the nature of the optimization.
*   The Label Prior Constraint is a strong assumption that may not always be realistic.
*   Lack of analysis on the sensitivity to key hyperparameters like the kernel choice.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend acceptance. The paper introduces a novel and valuable perspective on the problem of domain adaptation. The core idea of reshaping datasets is a significant conceptual contribution that could influence how researchers approach DA problems in the future. The technical execution is sound, and the experimental validation is thorough and convincing. While the weaknesses regarding scalability and the LPC assumption are valid, they represent limitations and avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws in the current contribution. The quality, originality, and significance of this work are all high and meet the standards for NIPS.
