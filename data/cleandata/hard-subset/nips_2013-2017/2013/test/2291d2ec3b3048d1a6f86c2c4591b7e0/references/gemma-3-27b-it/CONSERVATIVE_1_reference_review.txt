This paper proposes a method for automatically reshaping datasets into latent domains for domain adaptation, motivated by the observation that standard datasets for visual recognition are often mixtures of distinct domains. The core idea is to identify domains maximizing both distinctiveness and learnability, achieved via a non-parametric optimization process. While the motivation is reasonable and addresses a valid issue in domain adaptation research – the often-artificial nature of equating datasets with domains – the paper suffers from a lack of compelling evidence and overstates its novelty.

The primary weakness lies in the limited experimental validation. While results are presented on object recognition and action recognition, the improvements over baseline methods are not substantial enough to justify the complexity of the proposed approach. The comparison to [20] is superficial, claiming their parametric approach is limiting, without demonstrating a significant advantage in practice. The experiments largely focus on *discovering* domains, rather than demonstrably showcasing the benefits of these discovered domains in a realistic domain adaptation scenario. The conditional reshaping of test datasets, while a potentially valuable contribution, is presented as an afterthought and lacks thorough evaluation; the improvements in Table 3 are marginal and require more robust analysis. 

Furthermore, the optimization problem, while theoretically sound, appears computationally expensive and the relaxation to continuous optimization introduces approximations that are not fully discussed or justified. The parameter sensitivity of this relaxation, and the impact of the δ parameter in the label prior constraint, is not investigated. The analysis of the identified domains (Figure 1) is largely descriptive and does not provide quantitative insight into *why* the algorithm discovers particular domain divisions. 

The paper also overclaims novelty. The concepts of maximizing distinctiveness and learnability, while presented as key insights, are well-established ideas in clustering and machine learning. The kernel-based method for distribution comparison, while standard, is presented as a significant contribution. The connection to existing literature on unsupervised domain discovery and related methods (e.g., [27]) is acknowledged, but the differentiation is overstated.

**Strengths:**

*   The motivation for addressing the mismatch between datasets and true domains is valid and important.
*   The formulation of domain discovery as an optimization problem with distinctiveness and learnability criteria is conceptually sound.
*   The inclusion of a label prior constraint is a sensible addition to the optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited and incremental experimental improvements.
*   Overstated novelty and insufficient comparison to existing work.
*   Computational complexity and lack of sensitivity analysis.
*   Superficial analysis of identified domains.
*   The method heavily relies on kernel parameters, without a thorough exploration of their impact.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and often overlooked issue in domain adaptation.
*   Presents a principled approach to domain discovery.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution and limited experimental validation.
*   Overstated novelty and lack of compelling evidence.
*   Computational complexity and parameter sensitivity.
*   Insufficient comparison to related work and baseline methods.




**Overall Assessment:**

I find this paper to be a reasonable, but ultimately unremarkable, contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The idea of automatically reshaping datasets into latent domains is interesting, but the proposed method does not demonstrate sufficient improvement over existing techniques to warrant acceptance to NIPS. The lack of rigorous validation and the overstated claims of novelty further weaken the paper. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should significantly strengthen their experimental evaluation, provide a more thorough comparison to related work, and address the concerns regarding computational complexity and parameter sensitivity before resubmitting.
