

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper proposes a method for discovering latent domains within visual datasets by maximizing domain distinctiveness and learnability. The authors argue that standard visual domain adaptation research mistakenly treats entire datasets as single domains, when in reality datasets often contain multiple latent domains. They introduce a nonparametric approach using kernel-based distribution differences to identify maximally distinctive domains, and use domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to determine the optimal number of domains.

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses a real problem in domain adaptation research—datasets often contain multiple latent domains that are not properly accounted for. The distinction between "dataset" and "domain" is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear empirical demonstration of the problem. The DWCV approach for determining the number of domains is novel and well-justified. The experimental results on object and action recognition datasets show meaningful improvements over baselines, particularly when using the identified domains for adaptation.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper contains several technical inconsistencies and methodological concerns that undermine its contribution:

1. **Misrepresentation of related work:** The paper claims "[20] is the first and only work addressing latent domain discovery," which is incorrect. Hoffman et al. [20] explicitly addresses latent domain discovery using a hierarchical Gaussian mixture model. The authors should acknowledge this work and clarify the differences between their nonparametric approach and the parametric approach in [20].

2. **Flawed formulation of constraints:** In section 2.1, the "label prior constraint" (LPC) is introduced as "requiring that within each domain, the class labels are distributed according to the prior distribution." However, the constraint in equation (3) is misformulated—it should constrain the proportion of classes within each domain to be approximately equal to the overall proportion, not the proportion of the specific class. The constraint as written is mathematically incorrect.

3. **Inconsistent notation:** The paper defines βmk = zmk/Mk but then uses βk = (β1k, ..., βMk)^T, which is inconsistent with the indexing. The constraint (5) states "1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C" which is nonsensical since βmk is a single variable for instance m and domain k, not a sum over k.

4. **Unjustified assumptions:** The paper claims "the total domain distinctiveness TDD(K) increases as K increases" but provides no theoretical justification for this claim. In reality, with the constraints imposed (particularly the label prior constraint), TDD(K) may not necessarily increase with K, and the authors provide no evidence for this behavior.

5. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims "our method yields a better set of identified domains, which are always better than the original datasets" based on oracle accuracies (Table 1). However, these oracle accuracies require label information from the target domains, which is not available in practice. The practical utility shown in Table 2 is significantly lower than the oracle values, yet the paper presents the oracle results as the main evidence of success.

6. **Formatting and presentation issues:** The paper contains inconsistent citation formats ([1,2,3,4] vs. [1,2,3,4,24,25]), inconsistent use of "domain" vs. "dataset," and a poorly formatted equation (1) with missing subscript H for the RKHS norm.

**Technical concerns:**
The paper's claim that their approach is "nonparametric" is misleading. The kernel-based approach implicitly assumes a structure (RKHS) and the optimization problem is still constrained by the kernel matrix, making it less truly nonparametric than claimed. The DWCV procedure for determining K is not sufficiently detailed—how exactly is the cross-validation performed, and what is the exact formula for A(K)?

**Conclusion:**
While the paper identifies an important problem in domain adaptation and proposes a method with some empirical promise, the technical inconsistencies, misrepresentation of related work, and overstatement of results significantly undermine the contribution. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these issues, particularly by clarifying the relationship with [20], correcting the mathematical formulation of constraints, and tempering the claims about performance improvements.

**Recommendation: Reject** - The paper contains multiple technical flaws that prevent it from making a strong scientific contribution to the field. While the problem addressed is important, the paper fails to properly establish the novelty of the approach or provide a sound theoretical and methodological foundation for the proposed method. The technical inconsistencies and overstatements of results make it difficult to evaluate the true contribution of the work.
