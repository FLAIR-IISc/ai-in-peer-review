

# NIPS Peer Review

## Summary

This paper addresses a critical gap in visual domain adaptation research: the common practice of treating entire datasets as single domains, when datasets often contain multiple latent domains. The authors propose a novel method to automatically reshape datasets into characteristic domains based on two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains should be sufficiently informative for discriminative learning). They formulate a nonparametric optimization approach using kernel-based RKHS distance to maximize domain distinctiveness and propose domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to determine the optimal number of domains. The paper evaluates the approach on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human action recognition (IXMAS) tasks, demonstrating significant improvements over baselines that treat datasets as single domains or use prior domain discovery methods.

The paper builds upon domain adaptation work at NIPS (e.g., [4, 26]) and relates to related work on latent domain discovery [20], though the authors argue their nonparametric approach overcomes limitations of [20]'s parametric Gaussian mixture modeling.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation and Problem Formulation**: The paper effectively identifies a critical but overlooked problem in visual domain adaptation—datasets are not naturally discrete domains as in other modalities. The examples illustrating how treating a dataset with multiple views as a single domain harms performance are compelling and well-justified.

2. **Novel Methodology**: The two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and learnability) provide a principled foundation for domain discovery. The nonparametric approach using RKHS distance avoids the distributional assumptions of prior work [20], making it more applicable to real visual data. The DWCV procedure for determining the number of domains is practical and well-motivated.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper provides thorough experiments across two distinct tasks (object and action recognition) with multiple datasets. The comparison against both the original dataset domains and [20]'s method demonstrates clear improvements. The conditional reshaping of test datasets (Section 4.3) is particularly valuable for real-world applications.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations and intuitive explanations. The figures (particularly Figure 1 showing exemplar images from identified domains) effectively illustrate the method's results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to Recent Domain Adaptation Work**: While the paper cites relevant domain adaptation work, it doesn't sufficiently compare against more recent methods that handle multiple source domains (e.g., [28, 29]), which is the natural follow-up to their domain discovery approach. The paper claims their approach "is natural to apply multi-domain techniques" but doesn't demonstrate this in experiments.

2. **DWCV Dependency on Labeled Data**: The DWCV procedure requires labeled data to compute domain-wise cross-validation accuracy. This is a limitation in unsupervised domain adaptation settings where target domain labels are unavailable. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't explore how to address it.

3. **Computational Complexity**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of their approach, particularly for large-scale datasets. The optimization problem involves solving a convex quadratic program, but the scalability to datasets with millions of images isn't addressed.

4. **Ambiguity in Domain Interpretation**: While Figure 1 shows some examples of domains, the paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of *what* factors define the identified domains (e.g., is it pose, lighting, background?). The qualitative analysis is somewhat superficial compared to the quantitative results.

5. **Comparison with K-means**: The paper mentions they experimented with K-means clustering but didn't report results. It would be valuable to see how their approach compares to simple clustering baselines.

## Recommendation

**Accept** (with minor revisions)

This paper makes a significant contribution to the domain adaptation literature by addressing a fundamental problem in visual domain adaptation—treating datasets as single domains when they contain multiple latent domains. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear empirical improvements over baselines. The problem is important, the approach is novel, and the evaluation is comprehensive.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A more thorough comparison to recent multi-source domain adaptation methods
2. A discussion of the computational complexity and scalability
3. Deeper analysis of what factors define the identified domains
4. A more detailed comparison to simple clustering baselines like K-means

However, these are minor points that don't undermine the paper's core contribution. The paper is technically sound, addresses an important problem, and makes a clear advance over prior work in domain adaptation.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro:**
- Identifies and addresses a critical but overlooked problem in visual domain adaptation research
- Proposes a principled, nonparametric approach to domain discovery that avoids distributional assumptions
- Demonstrates significant improvements over baselines on multiple tasks and datasets
- Provides a practical method (DWCV) for determining the number of domains

**Con:**
- Limited comparison to recent multi-source domain adaptation methods
- DWCV requires labeled data, which limits applicability in unsupervised settings
- Computational complexity not thoroughly discussed
- Qualitative analysis of domain factors is somewhat superficial

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation, with clear potential for impact on both research and practice. The method is sound, well-evaluated, and addresses an important gap in the literature. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points above.
