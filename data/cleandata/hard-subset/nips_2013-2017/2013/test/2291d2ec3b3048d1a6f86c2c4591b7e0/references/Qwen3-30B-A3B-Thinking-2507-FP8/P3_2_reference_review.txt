

# Peer Review: "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental but overlooked question in domain adaptation: what constitutes a "domain" in visual recognition? The authors argue that unlike speech or language processing, visual data doesn't naturally lend itself to discrete domain definitions due to complex factors like pose, illumination, and background. Instead of treating datasets as single domains (a common but problematic practice), they propose a method to automatically reshape datasets into latent domains that satisfy two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains are maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains contain sufficient information for discriminative learning). They develop nonparametric approaches using kernel-based domain discrepancy measures and validate their approach on object recognition and action recognition tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Compelling Problem Statement:** The paper effectively identifies a crucial gap in domain adaptation research. While much work focuses on adapting between given domains, the authors correctly question what constitutes a domain in visual recognition—a fundamental issue that has been overlooked.

2. **Novel Methodology:** The two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) provide a principled framework for domain discovery. The nonparametric approach using kernel-based domain discrepancy measures is well-motivated and avoids the limitations of parametric clustering methods. The paper's distinction from Hoffman et al. (2014) is well-justified.

3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental section is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam, IXMAS) and tasks (object recognition, action recognition). The comparison against original datasets and a prior method is appropriate and convincing.

4. **Interesting Insights:** The qualitative analysis in Figure 1 provides valuable insights into what factors drive domain boundaries in visual data (e.g., object pose, appearance, background), which could inform future dataset construction.

## Weaknesses

1. **Oracle Accuracy Misleading:** The "oracle" accuracy in Table 1 (which assumes target domain labels are known) is not a realistic scenario for practical applications. The paper should clarify how the approach would work in practice without this information. The MATCHING strategy (which selects the best domain for each target) relies on knowing target domain labels, which is not realistic in most applications.

2. **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of the proposed method. The optimization problem involves solving a convex quadratic program, but the paper doesn't discuss how this scales with dataset size or number of domains. For large-scale applications, this could be a significant limitation.

3. **DWCV Robustness:** The domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure for determining K is crucial to the approach, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence of its robustness across different datasets. The claim that "the number favored by DWCV coincides with the number of datasets we mix" suggests the method may be overly sensitive to the number of input datasets.

4. **Overfitting Risk:** The paper doesn't address the risk of overfitting when reshaping the training data, particularly when K is large. The relaxation to continuous optimization might lead to domains that are too fine-grained and don't generalize well.

## Specific Concerns

The paper claims that "the domains identified by our method generally perform well, especially using MATCHING to select the single best source domain to match the target domain for adaptation." However, MATCHING relies on knowing the target domain labels, which is not realistic in practical applications. The paper should clarify how this would work without target labels.

The paper states that "the main factors here are probably background and object appearance (color and shape)" based on qualitative examples. This is not supported by quantitative analysis. It would be stronger to provide statistical evidence of which factors are most important.

The paper cites Hoffman et al. (2014) as the most relevant work but doesn't fully address why their approach is limited. While the paper mentions that Hoffman et al. assume a parametric form, it would be helpful to provide specific examples where their method fails on real visual datasets.

## Conclusion

This paper makes an important contribution to domain adaptation by addressing a fundamental question about what constitutes a domain in visual recognition. The methodology is novel, well-motivated, and the experimental results are promising. The weaknesses identified are important but not fundamental flaws in the approach.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem. It makes a significant contribution by shifting the focus from adapting between given domains to discovering the latent domains themselves. While the paper has some weaknesses, they can be addressed with minor revisions.

## Recommendation
Accept (with minor revisions)

## Pro/Con List for Acceptance

**Pros:**
- Addresses a fundamental but overlooked problem in domain adaptation
- Novel axiomatic framework for domain discovery (maximum distinctiveness and learnability)
- Nonparametric approach avoids limitations of parametric clustering
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple datasets and tasks
- Provides valuable insights into domain boundaries in visual data

**Cons:**
- Oracle accuracy in Table 1 is misleading for practical applications
- Computational complexity not adequately addressed
- DWCV procedure's robustness needs more evidence
- Overfitting risk when reshaping training data not addressed

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The weaknesses identified are important but can be addressed with minor revisions. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a method to discover latent domains, which is a prerequisite for more effective domain adaptation.
