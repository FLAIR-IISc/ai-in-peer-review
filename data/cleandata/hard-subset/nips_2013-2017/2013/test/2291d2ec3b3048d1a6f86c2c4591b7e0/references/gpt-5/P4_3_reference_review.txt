Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses the problem that visual datasets often aggregate heterogeneous “latent domains” (e.g., due to pose, illumination, background), and that treating an entire dataset as a single domain can degrade domain adaptation. The authors propose to automatically reshape labeled datasets into multiple latent domains without predefining semantic factors. Two desiderata guide the approach: (1) maximum distinctiveness: discovered domains should be as distributionally different as possible; and (2) maximum learnability: each domain should support strong discriminative models.

Technically, the paper measures inter-domain discrepancy nonparametrically using the RKHS mean embedding distance (equivalently, MMD) [21] between empirical distributions. It defines a total domain distinctiveness (TDD) as the sum of pairwise squared distances between domain mean embeddings, then seeks assignments of instances to K domains that maximize TDD subject to constraints. Key constraints include: (i) each instance is assigned to exactly one domain; and (ii) a label prior constraint (LPC) requiring each domain’s class label distribution to match the global (empirical) prior, with small tolerance. Because the integer program is NP-hard, the paper relaxes it to a continuous quadratic program by introducing per-domain simplex weights β and writes the objective in kernel matrix form. To determine K, the paper proposes domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV): for each candidate K, train classifiers within each domain and select K maximizing the weighted average cross-validated accuracy across domains.

Empirically, on standard object recognition DA benchmarks (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and IXMAS multi-view action recognition, the paper reports that (i) discovered domains outperform treating original datasets as domains; (ii) the method outperforms Hoffman et al. [20], which models domains via hierarchical Gaussian mixtures; and (iii) conditionally reshaping heterogeneous test sets to match training domains further improves recognition. The approach connects to prior NIPS/ML work on domain adaptation [4,7,9,10,22] and generalized clustering via kernel embeddings [27], but differs by explicitly discovering latent domains using a nonparametric distinctiveness criterion plus supervised constraints, and by choosing K via learnability.

Quality (technical soundness)
Strengths:
- The use of RKHS mean embeddings/MMD to quantify inter-domain discrepancy is principled and avoids strong parametric assumptions [21].
- The LPC is a clear mechanism to guard against trivial “per-class clustering,” which would otherwise be favored by maximizing inter-cluster separability.
- DWCV is a simple, practical criterion to select K that reflects the downstream goal (learnable domains for adaptation).
- The extension to conditional reshaping of test data, formulated as a convex QP (details in supplement), is sensible and addresses heterogeneous test distributions.

Weaknesses/concerns:
- Assumption of invariant class priors across domains: LPC enforces that each latent domain’s class histogram closely matches the global prior. This is strong and often violated in practice (e.g., different environments or views may exhibit different category frequencies). It also implies each domain must contain all classes (Mk ≥ C), which limits applicability to settings where classes are uneven or missing in some domains. Sensitivity to this assumption (and to δ) is not evaluated.
- Supervision dependence: The discovery relies on labels for all training samples (LPC and DWCV), so it is not unsupervised domain discovery. The method’s behavior with partial labels or label noise is not discussed.
- Optimization: The relaxed objective maximizes a convex quadratic over linear constraints, which is in general NP-hard. The paper relies on off-the-shelf solvers without guarantees, and provides no details on initialization, regularization, convergence behavior, or runtime scalability. The kernel matrix requires O(M^2) memory/time, and DWCV multiplies the cost across several K and folds.
- Potential degeneracy and balance: Apart from loose per-sample constraints (1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C), there is no explicit penalty encouraging balanced domain sizes or avoiding overly small domains (beyond what DWCV indirectly penalizes). An entropy or size-regularization term might be warranted.
- Choice of kernel and bandwidth: Only a heuristic (twice median distance) is used; no sensitivity analysis is provided. MMD is known to be sensitive to kernel choice.
- Empirical reporting: While the narrative claims improvements over [20] and original datasets, the main text lacks detailed quantitative tables (e.g., mean ± std over multiple splits), statistical tests, ablations (e.g., without LPC, without DWCV), and computational costs. The “MATCHING” and “ENSEMBLE” procedures are only sketched, making it hard to assess fairness and reproducibility.
- The bound Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C} is justified via N-fold CV feasibility rather than model selection; it may under-explore K when C is small but domains are numerous.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-motivated and readable. The goals of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability are clearly articulated, with intuitive examples (e.g., cross-view actions).
- Some derivations/notations need clarification: the transition from zmk/Mk to βmk and the resulting constraint 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C is non-intuitive and merits a clearer derivation; Eq. (1) would benefit from explicitly writing the empirical MMD estimator and how it reduces to (βk−βk′)^T K (βk−βk′); the “MATCHING” strategy should be defined precisely. Provide algorithmic details, solver choices, complexity, and runtime.
- Conditional test reshaping is deferred to the supplement; including at least the objective in the main text would strengthen clarity.

Originality
- Discovering latent domains by maximizing pairwise MMD with supervised LPC and selecting K via DWCV is a novel combination in the DA context. Compared to [20], the nonparametric MMD-based formulation avoids parametric mixture assumptions. The idea relates to generalized clustering via kernel embeddings [27] but differs by using labels (LPC) and servicing domain adaptation.

Significance
- If robust and scalable, the method could become a useful preprocessing step for multi-source DA by producing more coherent source domains and by reshaping heterogeneous test sets. The qualitative analyses suggest meaningful factors (pose, background) are captured.
- However, the reliance on invariant class priors and full labels, limited scalability discussion, and sparse empirical details temper the immediate impact. Demonstrations on larger-scale/deeper features and broader DA methods would strengthen significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear motivation; addresses a recognized pitfall (dataset ≠ domain).
- Principled, nonparametric discrepancy measure; practical criterion for K.
- Promising empirical gains on standard DA benchmarks and cross-view actions.
- Insightful qualitative analyses of discovered domains.

Cons for acceptance
- Strong LPC assumption (shared class priors, all classes per domain); limited applicability and no sensitivity analysis.
- Optimization is NP-hard in the relaxed form; no algorithmic details, guarantees, or scalability evaluation.
- Dependence on full supervision; lacks discussion of semi-supervised/unlabeled settings.
- Insufficient quantitative reporting (variance, statistical significance, ablations, computational cost); key matching/ensemble procedures under-specified.
- Kernel choice/bandwidth sensitivity not evaluated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full algorithmic details, initialization, solver choices, and complexity analysis; report runtimes and scaling with M.
- Add ablation studies: remove LPC, vary δ, vary kernel bandwidths, compare balanced vs. unbalanced size constraints, and analyze robustness to partial labels.
- Thorough quantitative comparison with [20] under matched settings; include standard errors across multiple splits and significance tests.
- Clarify “MATCHING” and “ENSEMBLE” procedures; ensure fair baselines.
- Discuss extensions when class priors differ across domains and when some classes are missing in some domains (e.g., soft LPC, reweighting).
- Include the objective and constraints for conditional test reshaping in the main paper.
