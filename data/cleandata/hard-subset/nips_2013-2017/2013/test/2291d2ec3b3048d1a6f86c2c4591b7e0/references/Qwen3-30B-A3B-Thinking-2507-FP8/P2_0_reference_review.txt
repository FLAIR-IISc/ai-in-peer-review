

# Review: "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental but overlooked issue in visual domain adaptation: the common practice of treating entire datasets as single domains when they are often "agglomerations of several distinctive domains." The authors propose a novel approach to automatically reshape datasets into characteristic domains by optimizing for two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains from which strong discriminative models can be built). The method uses nonparametric kernel-based measures to quantify domain differences and employs domain-wise cross-validation to determine the optimal number of domains. The paper evaluates this approach on object recognition (using Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human action recognition (IXMAS dataset), demonstrating significant improvements over both original datasets and the closest prior work [20].

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with strong theoretical grounding. The authors clearly articulate the problem with treating datasets as domains and provide a compelling motivation through concrete examples. Their kernel-based domain discrepancy measure (equation 1) is well-justified and properly referenced to [21]. The optimization framework is carefully designed, with appropriate constraints (LPC) to prevent trivial solutions. The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering multiple datasets and tasks, and the results consistently show improvements over baselines. The DWCV procedure for determining the number of domains is particularly well-motivated and practical.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively sets up the problem by contrasting the natural domain structure in speech/language processing with the complex, overlapping factors in visual data. The technical sections are logically structured, with clear mathematical formulations and helpful explanations of key concepts. The experimental results are presented in a logical manner, with appropriate tables and figures that effectively support the claims. The paper is accessible to readers familiar with domain adaptation but also provides sufficient background for those new to the field.

**Originality:** This is a highly original contribution that identifies a critical gap in the field. The authors correctly point out that the common practice of equating datasets with domains is problematic, and they propose a fundamentally different approach to domain discovery. The two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) are novel insights that distinguish this work from traditional clustering approaches. While [20] addresses latent domain discovery, the authors' nonparametric approach without distributional assumptions is a significant improvement, as correctly noted in Section 3. The paper is clearly distinct from prior work, with a fresh perspective on domain adaptation.

**Significance:** Domain adaptation is a major problem in computer vision, and this paper addresses a foundational issue that affects all domain adaptation research. The results demonstrate clear practical improvements in classification accuracy across multiple datasets and tasks. More importantly, the approach could reshape how datasets are constructed and used in future research, moving beyond the current practice of treating datasets as single domains. The paper's insight that "domains are not datasets" is a valuable conceptual contribution that has implications beyond the immediate results.

## Weaknesses

**Quality:** While the method is technically sound, the paper could better address the case where datasets do not contain multiple distinctive domains. The authors assume that reshaping into multiple domains is always beneficial, but it's unclear how the method would behave when the data is homogeneous. The paper also lacks a detailed discussion of computational complexity, which would be important for practical adoption.

**Clarity:** The explanation of the "conditional reshaping" of test datasets (Section 4.3) is somewhat brief compared to the training dataset reshaping. While the paper mentions that this is "more tractable" than training dataset reshaping, a bit more detail would help readers understand how this differs from the training phase.

**Originality:** The paper claims [20] is "the first and only work addressing latent domain discovery," but I should note that related work in "generalized clustering" (e.g., [27]) shares similar concepts of maximizing discriminability. While the paper correctly distinguishes their approach from [27] by noting their focus on domain discovery versus unsupervised clustering, a more thorough comparison would strengthen the originality claim.

**Significance:** The paper demonstrates significant improvements over baselines, but the magnitude of these improvements (e.g., Table 2) could be more explicitly contextualized. How much better are the results compared to state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods? While the paper correctly focuses on reshaping datasets rather than adapting classifiers, a brief comparison to domain adaptation methods that use the identified domains would help readers understand the practical impact.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the domain adaptation literature by addressing a fundamental conceptual issue that has been overlooked in the field. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear improvements over baselines. The insights about the distinction between datasets and domains are valuable and could influence future dataset construction and evaluation practices.

While the paper has minor weaknesses in addressing edge cases and providing computational complexity details, these do not undermine the core contribution. The work represents a substantial advancement in the conceptual understanding of domain adaptation and offers a practical method to improve performance.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the minor weaknesses noted above.

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Novel conceptual insight (datasets ≠ domains)
- Strong theoretical foundation
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation
- Clear practical benefits demonstrated

**Con Acceptance:**
- Slightly limited discussion of edge cases
- Brief explanation of test dataset reshaping
- Need for more contextualization of improvement magnitude

Overall, this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field and advances our understanding of domain adaptation in visual recognition. The work is technically rigorous, well-motivated, and has clear potential for impact on future research.
