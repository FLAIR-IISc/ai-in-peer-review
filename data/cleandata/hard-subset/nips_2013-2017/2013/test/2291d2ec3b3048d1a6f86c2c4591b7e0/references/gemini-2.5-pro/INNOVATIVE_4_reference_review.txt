**Summary**

This paper addresses a fundamental, yet often overlooked, problem in visual domain adaptation: the common practice of equating datasets with domains. The authors argue that a single dataset can be a heterogeneous mixture of multiple latent domains (e.g., defined by viewpoint, illumination, background) and that treating it as a single source for adaptation is suboptimal. The core contribution is a novel, unsupervised method for automatically "reshaping" a dataset into its constituent latent domains. The approach is founded on two intuitive principles: 1) **Maximum Distinctiveness**, where the discovered domains should be maximally different from each other in distribution, measured non-parametrically in an RKHS, and 2) **Maximum Learnability**, where each discovered domain should contain sufficient, coherent data to train a strong classifier, a principle used to automatically select the number of domains. The method is formulated as a relaxed quadratic program with a key "label prior constraint" that prevents the trivial solution of clustering by object class. Experiments on standard object and action recognition benchmarks demonstrate that adapting from these discovered domains significantly improves performance over adapting from the original datasets.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper's primary strength is its conceptual novelty. It challenges a foundational assumption in domain adaptation research and proposes a new, important problem: latent domain discovery. Instead of developing yet another adaptation algorithm, this work provides a new lens through which to view the data itself. This is a bold and significant contribution that could shift the focus of the field from purely algorithmic solutions to a more data-centric perspective. The idea of reshaping datasets *before* adaptation is powerful and likely to inspire a new line of research.

2.  **Principled Formulation:** The proposed method is built on the elegant and well-motivated axioms of "maximum distinctiveness" and "maximum learnability." Using a non-parametric, kernel-based metric for distinctiveness is a strong choice, as it avoids making restrictive parametric assumptions about the data distributions, a key advantage over prior work like Hoffman et al. [20]. The introduction of the Label Prior Constraint is a clever and crucial element that successfully steers the optimization away from simply rediscovering object categories.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Experiments:** The experimental validation is thorough. The authors don't just show that their method works; they provide evidence for *why* it works. The "oracle" experiment (GOURS) convincingly shows that the discovered domains are intrinsically better sources for adaptation. The subsequent experiments show that this potential can be realized with standard multi-source adaptation techniques. Furthermore, the qualitative analysis in Figure 1 is particularly insightful, providing a clear visual confirmation that the method is identifying meaningful latent factors like object pose, background, and color, rather than just artifacts of the optimization. The extension to reshaping the test set is also a valuable addition that shows strong performance gains in a practical setting.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Optimization Details:** The paper is somewhat light on the details of the optimization procedure. It correctly notes that the relaxed problem—maximizing a convex function over a convex set—is generally NP-hard, but then states that "existing solvers are adequate" without specifying the solver, discussing computational complexity, or providing insight into the quality of the local optima found. While the empirical results suggest the solutions are good, a more detailed treatment of this aspect would strengthen the paper's technical completeness.

2.  **Scope of the Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The LPC is justified by the intuition that class priors are often independent of domain shifts. While this is a reasonable assumption for many standard benchmarks, it may not hold in all real-world scenarios. For instance, certain domains could be inherently correlated with the presence or absence of specific object classes. A discussion of the limitations of this assumption and potential failure cases would be welcome.

3.  **Heuristic for Choosing K:** The Domain-Wise Cross-Validation (DWCV) method for selecting the number of domains, K, is a practical and reasonable heuristic. However, it is still a heuristic. The connection between intra-domain cross-validation accuracy and optimal downstream adaptation performance is indirect. While the empirical results in Figure 2 are encouraging, the method lacks a more formal justification.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper presents a highly novel and significant idea that fundamentally reframes the problem of domain adaptation. It moves beyond creating better adaptation algorithms and asks a more foundational question: "What are the domains we should be adapting from?" The proposed solution is elegant, principled, and supported by strong empirical evidence. While there are minor weaknesses regarding the optimization details and the universality of certain assumptions, these are easily outweighed by the boldness and potential impact of the core contribution. This is precisely the kind of thought-provoking, direction-setting work that NIPS should champion. I expect this paper to be influential and to open up new and exciting avenues for research in domain adaptation and dataset analysis.
