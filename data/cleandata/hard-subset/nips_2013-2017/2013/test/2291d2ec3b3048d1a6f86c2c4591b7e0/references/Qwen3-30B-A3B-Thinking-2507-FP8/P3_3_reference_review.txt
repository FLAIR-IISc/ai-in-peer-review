

# NIPS Review: "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental but often overlooked problem in visual domain adaptation: the assumption that datasets naturally correspond to domains. The authors argue that visual datasets often contain multiple latent domains (e.g., different lighting conditions, viewpoints, or backgrounds) that get conflated when datasets are treated as single domains. They propose a novel method to automatically reshape datasets into characteristic domains based on two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains should support strong discriminative models). The authors develop nonparametric methods using kernel-based domain discrepancy measures and introduce domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to determine the optimal number of domains.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation**: The paper clearly articulates a critical gap in domain adaptation research—the conflation of datasets with domains—and provides compelling examples (e.g., different camera views in action recognition) where treating a dataset as a single domain is problematic.

2. **Novel Methodology**: The two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) are well-motivated and represent a significant conceptual advance over previous work. The kernel-based approach to measure domain discrepancy is theoretically sound and addresses limitations of parametric clustering methods.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experimental validation on both object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human action recognition (IXMAS) datasets demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines, including the closest related work by Hoffman et al. (2013). The conditional reshaping of test datasets is particularly insightful for real-world applications.

4. **Practical Utility**: The DWCV procedure provides a principled way to determine the optimal number of domains without requiring domain knowledge, which is a significant practical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Gaps**: While the paper presents a sound optimization approach, it lacks theoretical guarantees. The relaxation to continuous optimization is not fully justified, and there's no discussion of convergence properties or bounds on the quality of the solution compared to the integer programming formulation.

2. **Computational Concerns**: The DWCV procedure is computationally intensive (requires multiple cross-validations for different K values), and the paper doesn't address scalability to large datasets or provide runtime analysis. For large K, the method could become prohibitively expensive.

3. **Limited Generalization**: The paper assumes the test domains are a subset of the latent domains discovered in training. This is a strong assumption; real-world applications often involve test domains that weren't present in training data. The authors should address how their method would handle out-of-distribution test domains.

4. **Kernel Selection Justification**: The choice of Gaussian kernel with bandwidth set to twice the median distance is convenient but not theoretically motivated. The paper doesn't explore how different kernel choices might affect the results, which is crucial for reproducibility.

5. **Comparison with Recent Work**: While they compare with Hoffman et al. (2013), the paper doesn't engage with more recent advances in domain adaptation, such as adversarial domain adaptation methods (e.g., DANN, CDAN) that could provide a more contemporary baseline.

## Significance and Contribution

This paper makes a significant contribution by shifting the focus from adapting between given domains to discovering the latent domain structure within datasets. The approach could substantially improve domain adaptation performance by providing better domain representations. The method's practical utility is demonstrated through consistent performance gains across multiple benchmarks.

The paper also offers valuable insights for dataset construction by revealing dominant factors (e.g., object pose, background) in visual datasets, which could guide future data collection efforts. The authors' observation that domain boundaries often don't align with dataset boundaries (e.g., Caltech-256 and Amazon datasets) is particularly insightful.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that addresses a meaningful gap in domain adaptation research with a well-motivated, technically sound approach. While the theoretical foundations could be strengthened, the practical contributions and experimental validation are compelling.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:
1. Add a brief discussion of computational complexity and scalability
2. Provide more justification for kernel choice
3. Address the out-of-distribution test domain scenario
4. Include a brief comparison with more recent domain adaptation baselines

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field and aligns well with NIPS' focus on rigorous machine learning research with practical impact. It advances the state of the art in domain adaptation by addressing a fundamental but previously overlooked problem in dataset organization.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that should be accepted to NIPS. The work has the potential to influence both theoretical understanding and practical applications of domain adaptation.
