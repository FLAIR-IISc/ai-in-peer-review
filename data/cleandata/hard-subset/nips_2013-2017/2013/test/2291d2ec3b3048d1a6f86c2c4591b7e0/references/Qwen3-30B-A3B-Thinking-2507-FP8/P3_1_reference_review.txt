

# Peer Review: "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental conceptual issue in domain adaptation: the mismatch between "datasets" and "domains" in visual recognition. The authors argue that standard domain adaptation research incorrectly equates datasets with domains, while visual data is affected by complex, overlapping factors (pose, illumination, etc.) that don't naturally define discrete domains. They propose a novel method to automatically reshape datasets into characteristic latent domains based on two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains that support strong discriminative models). The method uses nonparametric kernel-based measures to quantify domain distinctiveness and introduces domain-wise cross-validation to determine the optimal number of domains. The approach is evaluated on object and action recognition tasks, showing consistent improvements over baselines and the closest related work (Hoffman et al.).

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual Contribution:** The paper identifies a crucial, often overlooked conceptual problem in domain adaptation research. The distinction between datasets (collections of images) and domains (underlying distributions) is well-motivated and addresses a real limitation in the field. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could reshape how researchers approach domain adaptation.

2. **Well-Motivated Methodology:** The two properties (distinctiveness and learnability) are well-justified. The use of kernel-based RKHS distances for measuring domain discrepancy is theoretically sound and avoids the limitations of parametric assumptions in prior work. The label prior constraint (LPC) is a thoughtful addition that aligns with realistic data collection scenarios.

3. **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on both object and action recognition tasks demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines. The comparison with Hoffman et al.'s method is particularly valuable, showing that their nonparametric approach outperforms the parametric Gaussian mixture model approach. The conditional reshaping of test datasets (Section 4.3) is a strong practical contribution.

4. **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of treating datasets as domains and presents a principled solution. The theoretical foundations are well-developed, and the method is well-suited to the problem.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Lack of Comparison to Clustering Baselines:** While the paper mentions that clustering by appearance leads to per-category domains (citing [20] and their own experiments), it doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison to other clustering approaches beyond K-means. More detailed comparisons to state-of-the-art clustering techniques (e.g., spectral clustering, deep clustering) would strengthen the argument that their approach is uniquely suited for domain discovery.

2. **Label Prior Constraint (LPC) Limitations:** The LPC constraint assumes that class distributions remain consistent across domains, which may not hold in many real-world scenarios. For example, in datasets where certain categories are more likely to appear in specific lighting conditions or camera angles, the LPC could force unnatural domain divisions. The paper doesn't adequately address these potential limitations or provide examples where LPC might be problematic.

3. **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of their approach. The continuous relaxation and optimization (Equation 4) may become computationally prohibitive for large-scale datasets, which is a significant concern for practical applications. This is especially relevant given the growing scale of visual datasets.

4. **Overstated Claims about Dataset Collection:** The claim that "datasets for visual recognition are not deliberately collected with clearly identifiable domains" is somewhat exaggerated. While many datasets don't explicitly define domains, several (e.g., Office-31, VisDA) are explicitly designed as domain adaptation benchmarks with defined domains. This weakens the paper's motivation.

5. **Lack of Comparison to Recent Domain Adaptation Methods:** The evaluation focuses on comparing the reshaped domains rather than on how the proposed approach integrates with or improves state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods. Including comparisons with recent domain adaptation techniques (e.g., DANN, MMD, CORAL) would better demonstrate the practical impact of their domain discovery method.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a fundamental conceptual issue in domain adaptation. The paper's main strength lies in its clear identification of the dataset-domain mismatch problem and the principled approach to discovering latent domains. The method is well-justified and shows consistent empirical improvements over baselines.

However, the paper would benefit from addressing the computational concerns, providing more nuanced discussion of the LPC constraint, and including more comprehensive comparisons with clustering baselines and recent domain adaptation methods. The claim about dataset collection could also be softened to acknowledge the existence of domain-aware datasets.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable conceptual contribution that advances the field's understanding of domain adaptation. While the weaknesses are valid, they can be addressed with relatively minor clarifications and additional experiments. The core idea of reshaping datasets into characteristic domains is novel, well-motivated, and could significantly influence future domain adaptation research.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Adding a comparison to recent clustering methods beyond K-means
2. Discussing the limitations of the LPC constraint with specific examples
3. Including a brief analysis of computational complexity
4. Softening the claim about dataset collection to acknowledge domain-aware datasets

These revisions would make an already strong paper even more compelling for the NIPS community. The work has the potential to become a foundational contribution to the field of domain adaptation, and I believe it merits publication in NIPS.
