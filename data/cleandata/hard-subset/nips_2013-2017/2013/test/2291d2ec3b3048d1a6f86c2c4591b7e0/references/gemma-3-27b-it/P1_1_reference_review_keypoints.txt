- Summary: Proposes reshaping visual datasets into latent domains using "maximum distinctiveness" and "maximum learnability" with nonparametric methods, automatically discovering domain counts and improving domain adaptation performance.
- Relation to prior work: Positions itself as addressing latent domain discovery (distinct from most DA work that assumes given domains), cites [20] as closest prior and differentiates via nonparametric methods and heterogeneous test handling.
- Novelty & importance: Highlights a novel, important critique that datasets shouldn't be treated as single domains and offers a principled remedy.
- Principled approach: Introduces well-motivated axioms (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) forming a sound theoretical foundation.
- Nonparametric formulation: Uses nonparametric methods to avoid restrictive parametric assumptions suitable for complex real-world data.
- Automatic domain number discovery: DWCV method removes the need to pre-specify the number of domains, a practical advantage.
- Strong empirical results: Demonstrates consistent DA improvements across object recognition and human activity tasks, including reshaping heterogeneous test sets.
- Clear writing and organization: Paper is well-written and easy to follow.
- Computational complexity: Scalability concerns remain due to relaxed integer programming and unclear computational costs on large datasets.
- Kernel selection sensitivity: Reliance on Gaussian kernel and bandwidth choice may influence results and lacks thorough sensitivity analysis.
- Label Prior Constraint (LPC) impact: LPC is proposed but its contribution isn't fully analyzed or ablated.
- Limited baseline comparisons: Comparisons to other domain discovery and clustering-based methods are insufficiently extensive.
- Qualitative analysis depth: Identified domains are qualitatively inspected but lack quantitative characterization (e.g., illumination, pose statistics).
- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses a fundamental DA problem, presents a novel/principled method, strong empirical support, and clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: Scalability concerns, unexplored sensitivity to kernel/LPC, and limited comparisons could weaken claims.
- Overall assessment: Strong, significant contribution recommending acceptance despite areas for improvement.
- Suggestions: Discuss computational complexity and scalability strategies.
- Suggestions: Perform kernel bandwidth sensitivity analysis and explore alternative kernels.
- Suggestions: Conduct ablation studies to quantify LPC's effect.
- Suggestions: Compare against a broader set of domain discovery and clustering baselines.
- Suggestions: Provide more quantitative analyses of the characteristics of discovered domains.
