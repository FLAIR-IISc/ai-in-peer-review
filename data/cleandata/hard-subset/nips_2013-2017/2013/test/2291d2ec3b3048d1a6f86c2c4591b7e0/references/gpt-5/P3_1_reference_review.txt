Summary
The paper tackles an important and under-explored question in domain adaptation: how to discover latent domains within one or more visual datasets, rather than treating each dataset as a monolithic domain. The authors propose two axioms for latent domains: maximum distinctiveness (maximize pairwise distributional differences between domains) and maximum learnability (each discovered domain should support training a strong classifier). They operationalize distinctiveness via a nonparametric RKHS-based two-sample test (MMD/mean embedding distance), formulate an assignment problem over latent domain memberships with label prior constraints, and introduce a continuous relaxation that leads to a quadratic program in β with linear constraints. They select the number of domains K via “domain-wise cross-validation” (DWCV), preferring partitions that yield higher within-domain CV accuracy. They then use multi-source adaptation (geodesic flow kernel) on the discovered domains and additionally propose “conditional reshaping” of heterogeneous test sets to match discovered training domains. Experiments on Caltech/Amazon/DSLR/Webcam (10 classes) and IXMAS multi-view actions show gains over treating datasets as domains and over the parametric latent-domain discovery of Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12).

Relation to prior work
- Closely related to Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12), who posit a hierarchical mixture and infer latent domains parametrically. The present work avoids parametric assumptions via MMD.
- Connects to kernel two-sample testing (Gretton et al.) and to the idea of maximizing between-cluster discrepancy (e.g., generalized clustering via kernel embeddings; Jegelka et al.).
- The use of multi-source adaptation after domain discovery (e.g., GFK) is orthogonal; the contribution is in reshaping datasets into domains.

Strengths
- Clear, compelling motivation: visual datasets often conflate multiple nuisance factors; treating them as single domains can harm adaptation.
- Sound nonparametric criterion for distinctiveness via characteristic kernels; avoids brittle parametric assumptions.
- Explicit mechanism to avoid trivial “cluster-by-class” solutions via label prior constraints.
- Practical procedure to choose K without target labels (DWCV), with a sensible bound on K.
- Empirical gains on both object and action recognition benchmarks; conditional test-set reshaping is a useful addition for scenarios with heterogeneous targets.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strong and potentially unrealistic assumption in the Label Prior Constraint (LPC): per-domain class proportions are constrained to match the global prior (δ=1%). In real scenarios, class priors often vary by domain. This constraint is central to preventing per-class partitions; however, it risks violating feasibility on smaller datasets, and may bias the solution toward artificial balancing that does not reflect true domain structure. There is no sensitivity analysis to δ or discussion of failure cases when priors differ across domains.
- Optimization is only sketched. The relaxed problem maximizes a convex quadratic over a polytope, which is non-convex/NP-hard; the paper acknowledges this but provides no solver details, initialization, or runtime/complexity analysis. The M×M kernel and K domain variables imply at least O(M^2) memory; scalability to larger datasets is unclear.
- The relaxation weakens the per-sample “assign to exactly one domain” constraint to bounds 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C, which enforces domain-size bounds indirectly (Mk ∈ [C, M]) but allows mass to spread across domains. The final hard assignment via argmax βmk is heuristic; the impact of this relaxation vs. enforcing equality constraints is not analyzed.
- Choice of K via DWCV optimizes within-domain supervised accuracy, not transfer performance. While plausible, this objective could favor partitions that are easy to classify but poor for adaptation. There is no theoretical or empirical analysis of correlation between A(K) and target performance beyond anecdotal plots; in some tasks, maximizing in-domain separability can increase inter-domain mismatch to targets.
- Missing details in “conditional reshaping” of test sets are deferred to the supplement; the paper should present the objective, constraints, and solver characteristics in the main text for reproducibility.
- Limited ablations: there is no reported sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, δ, or the necessity of LPC (e.g., without LPC or with looser δ). K-means and random partitions are dismissed without showing numbers.
- Evaluation focuses on traditional small-scale benchmarks with BoVW features; domain sizes, discovered domain characteristics, and runtimes are not reported. Statistical significance or confidence intervals are absent.

Quality
- The core idea is sound and grounded in MMD. However, key modeling assumptions (LPC) are strong; optimization details, complexity, and sensitivity analyses are insufficient for a NeurIPS-standard empirical paper. The conditional test reshaping is promising but under-specified.

Clarity
- Generally well written, with clear motivation and notation. Some notational overload (k as kernel and index; d(k,k′)). Important algorithmic details (solver, initialization, conditional reshaping objective) should be added. Provide pseudo-code for the full pipeline and complexity.

Originality
- The specific formulation—maximizing sum of pairwise MMD under label-prior constraints with a DWCV criterion for K—is novel relative to existing latent domain discovery (parametric mixtures) and generalized clustering. The test-set reshaping to match training domains is a nice addition.

Significance
- The problem is important for robust adaptation. If the approach scales and the constraints are relaxed to handle class prior shift, it could be impactful. As presented, the scope is limited to modest datasets; more convincing evidence and analysis would strengthen significance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace the hard LPC with a softer regularizer that penalizes deviation from global priors (e.g., KL or L2), and allow per-domain priors to vary; analyze sensitivity to δ and failure cases.
- Provide full optimization details: solver (e.g., projected gradient or Frank–Wolfe), initialization, convergence, and runtime; consider scalable approximations (Nyström, random features, block-coordinate updates).
- Analyze connections to kernel k-means/spectral clustering; derive simplified forms of TDD and discuss when the objective reduces to known clustering criteria.
- Justify DWCV: report correlation between A(K) and target adaptation accuracy; consider adding unsupervised transfer proxies (e.g., proxy A-distance/MMD to unlabeled target) into K selection.
- Include ablations: without LPC; varying kernel bandwidth; with/without the lower/upper bounds; compare explicitly to K-means with LPC-like constraints.
- Move the conditional test-reshaping formulation from the supplement to the main text; clarify how matching is done without target labels.

Pros
- Important problem; principled nonparametric criterion; practical K-selection; demonstrated gains; test-set reshaping idea.

Cons
- Strong LPC assumption; under-specified and potentially hard optimization; limited scalability and ablations; missing details for conditional reshaping; weak justification of K criterion vis-à-vis transfer.

Recommendation: Weak accept (borderline). The idea is timely and interesting, and the empirical results are encouraging. However, the paper needs stronger analysis and clearer algorithmic details, especially around LPC, optimization, and K selection, to reach a solid NeurIPS standard.
