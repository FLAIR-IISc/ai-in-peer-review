

# Review: Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation

This paper addresses an important but underexplored aspect of domain adaptation: the common practice of treating entire datasets as single domains, which may actually contain multiple latent domains. The authors propose a novel approach to automatically reshape datasets into latent domains based on two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains should support strong discriminative models). Their method uses nonparametric kernel-based measures for domain discrepancy and introduces domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to determine the optimal number of domains.

The paper relates well to previous work in domain adaptation, particularly citing [20] as the closest related work. However, it differs significantly by using nonparametric methods instead of parametric mixture models, which is a reasonable approach given the complexity of visual data. The work also connects to broader domain adaptation literature [1-4, 24-25] and related clustering work [27].

**Strengths:**

1. The problem addressed is genuinely important and underexplored in domain adaptation research. The paper effectively argues that treating entire datasets as single domains is problematic, as datasets often contain multiple distinct domains that should be handled separately.

2. The proposed method is well-motivated and technically sound. The use of kernel-based domain discrepancy measures is appropriate for nonparametric comparison, and DWCV provides a principled way to determine the number of domains without requiring prior knowledge.

3. The experimental results are compelling. The paper demonstrates significant improvements over using original datasets as domains across multiple object and action recognition tasks. The qualitative analysis in Figure 1 provides valuable insight into what factors the method identifies as domain-defining.

4. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by providing a method that can reshape both training and test datasets into meaningful domains, which is more comprehensive than previous approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited in scope. The paper only uses four image datasets and one video dataset, which doesn't fully demonstrate the generality of the approach. More diverse datasets (e.g., from different visual domains like medical imaging or satellite imagery) would strengthen the claims.

2. The comparison with the closest related work [20] is insufficient. The paper mentions differences in approach (nonparametric vs. parametric), but doesn't provide a thorough experimental comparison on the same datasets to quantify the performance gap. This is a significant weakness for a paper claiming to outperform existing work.

3. The paper doesn't adequately address the practical limitations of the method. The DWCV procedure requires labeled data to determine the optimal number of domains, which might not be available in many real-world domain adaptation scenarios where the target domain is unlabeled.

4. The theoretical analysis is limited. While the authors provide a clear optimization problem, they don't analyze the convergence properties or provide guarantees about the quality of the discovered domains. A more thorough theoretical discussion would strengthen the paper.

5. The paper doesn't sufficiently explore the boundary cases where the method might fail. For example, what happens when the data doesn't contain clear latent domains? How does the method behave when the number of latent domains is very large?

**Recommendation:**

This paper addresses an important problem in domain adaptation with a novel and well-motivated approach. The method is technically sound and the experimental results demonstrate clear improvements over using original datasets as domains. However, the paper's weaknesses—particularly the limited experimental evaluation, insufficient comparison with the closest related work, and lack of theoretical analysis—prevent it from meeting NIPS's high standards for a significant contribution.

I recommend rejection with the following suggestions for improvement:

1. Expand the experimental evaluation to include more diverse datasets and domain adaptation scenarios, particularly those where the domain structure is less obvious.

2. Provide a comprehensive comparison with [20] on the same datasets and tasks, including ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components.

3. Address the practical limitations of the method, particularly the requirement for labeled data in DWCV. Consider exploring semi-supervised or unsupervised approaches to domain discovery.

4. Add a more thorough theoretical analysis of the optimization problem and its properties.

5. Discuss failure cases and boundary conditions where the method might not perform well.

With these improvements, the paper could make a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. As it stands, however, the current version lacks sufficient evidence to demonstrate the significance of the contribution at the level expected for NIPS.
