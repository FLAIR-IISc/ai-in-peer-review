Summary
This paper tackles the important but underexplored question of what constitutes a “domain” in visual recognition and proposes to automatically reshape datasets into latent domains that are both (i) maximally distinctive (in distribution) and (ii) maximally learnable (support strong discriminative models). The approach is nonparametric: distinctiveness is quantified via a kernel mean discrepancy between empirical distributions, summed across pairs of domains (TDD), and learnability is imposed via a domain-wise cross-validation criterion (DWCV) that also selects the number of domains K. The domain assignment is formulated as an integer program with a label prior constraint (LPC) to avoid per-category clustering, and then relaxed to a continuous quadratic program. The paper further proposes “conditional reshaping” of test sets to match discovered training domains. Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) show that the discovered domains outperform original dataset-based domains and a prior latent-domain method [Hoffman et al. ’12], and that conditional reshaping of test data improves adaptation accuracy.

Relation to prior work
The work is closely related to latent domain discovery [Hoffman et al., ECCV’12], but differs by using nonparametric distributional distances (kernel embeddings/MMD) instead of parametric mixture models and by introducing DWCV to set K. It connects to multi-source adaptation (e.g., Mansour et al.; Duan et al.) by supplying latent sources, and to dataset bias and two-sample testing (Gretton et al.). The idea of maximizing inter-group distinctiveness echoes generalized clustering with kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.), but the setting and regularization (labels + LPC) are different.

Strengths
- Novel, clear formulation of domain discovery grounded in two complementary principles: maximum distinctiveness (via MMD) and maximum learnability (via DWCV).
- Nonparametric, label-aware objective that avoids strong distributional assumptions and directly addresses the “dataset ≠ domain” pitfall in visual recognition.
- Practical and impactful idea of conditional reshaping of test sets to match discovered training domains; this is both intuitive and useful for heterogeneous test scenarios (e.g., cross-view action).
- Empirical evidence across object and action datasets suggests meaningful gains over treating datasets as domains and over prior latent-domain discovery.
- The LPC is a simple, effective safeguard against trivial per-class clustering.

Weaknesses and questions
- Optimization details are under-specified. The relaxed problem maximizes a convex quadratic subject to linear constraints, which is nonconvex in general; no guarantees or solver specifics (initialization, stopping) are given. How sensitive are results to initialization? Do multiple runs yield similar partitions?
- The LPC assumes class priors are approximately invariant across domains (δ=1%). In many real settings, class prevalence is domain-dependent. How robust is the method when priors differ? Please provide an ablation with varying δ and with removed LPC.
- Scalability: the method relies on an M×M kernel matrix and solves a QP. What are the computational and memory footprints on larger datasets? Any use of low-rank feature maps, Nyström, or mini-batches?
- Choice of kernel/bandwidth: fixed to twice the median pairwise distance; lack of sensitivity analysis. Does the discovered partition and downstream accuracy vary strongly with kernel choices?
- The constraint 1/M ≤ Σk βmk ≤ 1/C appears ad hoc and stems from βmk=zmk/Mk; please clarify its derivation and practical effect. How does this interact with LPC and with class imbalance?
- Evaluation: Many key numbers are referenced to Tables 1–3; the text claims large improvements, but exact margins, statistical significance, and variance across random seeds are not reported here. Also, some baselines (K-means, random partition) are omitted; even if weak, they help contextualize difficulty. An ablation removing each ingredient (TDD only, DWCV only, LPC, conditional reshaping) would strengthen claims.
- The method uses labels during discovery (LPC, DWCV). This is reasonable for supervised source discovery, but please clarify the scope: is the approach intended only when source labels are available? What happens in semi-supervised or unsupervised settings?

Quality
Technically solid at the conceptual level and empirically promising, but the optimization section needs more rigor and reproducibility detail (solver, complexity, sensitivity). The LPC assumption and kernel dependence deserve analysis. Conditional reshaping is compelling but the exact convex program is deferred to the supplement.

Clarity
Generally well written and well organized. The derivation around equations (3–5) needs clearer justification, and more guidance on hyperparameters (δ, Kmax, kernel bandwidth). Please ensure all tables/figures contain full numerical results with standard deviations and that omitted baselines/ablations are included.

Originality
High. Framing domain discovery via MMD-based distinctiveness plus learnability is a fresh and useful perspective. Conditional reshaping of test sets to match training domains is a novel and practical addition.

Significance
High potential impact. Many vision problems suffer from heterogeneous, unlabeled domains; providing a principled way to tease them apart can benefit a broad swath of adaptation and generalization methods. The gains shown over standard dataset-based domains and over [20] suggest real utility.

Pros
- Conceptually bold and timely; moves the field beyond “dataset = domain.”
- Nonparametric, label-aware discovery with an intuitive model selection criterion.
- Demonstrated across two modalities; test-time reshaping is especially appealing.

Cons
- Limited methodological detail on optimization and scalability.
- Strong LPC assumption; needs robustness and ablation studies.
- Sensitivity to kernel/hyperparameters not analyzed; some comparisons omitted.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning strong). The paper introduces a distinctive and useful direction—automated reshaping of datasets into latent domains with test-time conditional matching—that is likely to influence practice despite some methodological gaps. Strengthening the optimization details, ablations (especially LPC/δ), and sensitivity/scalability analyses would further solidify the contribution.

Confidence
Moderate.
