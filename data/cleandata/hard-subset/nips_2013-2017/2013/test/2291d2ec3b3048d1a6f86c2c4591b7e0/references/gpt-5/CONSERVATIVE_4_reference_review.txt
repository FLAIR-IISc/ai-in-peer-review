Summary
The paper tackles the important and underexplored problem that visual “datasets” are often heterogeneous mixtures of latent domains (e.g., due to viewpoint, illumination), and that treating each dataset as a single domain can harm domain adaptation. The authors propose to reshape datasets into latent domains via two principles: (1) maximum distinctiveness, operationalized by maximizing pairwise discrepancies between empirical domain distributions measured by a kernel mean embedding/MMD-style distance; and (2) maximum learnability, used to choose the number of domains K via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV). The assignment problem is relaxed to a continuous quadratic program with a “label prior constraint” (LPC) that enforces approximately equal class proportions across domains. The discovered domains are then used with standard multi-source adaptation (e.g., GFK) and also to conditionally reshape heterogeneous test sets. Experiments on Caltech/Amazon/DSLR/Webcam and IXMAS suggest improvements over treating datasets as domains and over a prior latent-domain method [Hoffman et al., ECCV’12].

Relation to prior work
The work is related to multi-source domain adaptation, latent domain discovery [Hoffman et al.], kernel two-sample tests/MMD [Gretton et al.], and generalized clustering via kernel embeddings [Jegelka et al.]. The main novelty claimed is a nonparametric domain discovery objective (pairwise MMD maximization) with a supervised criterion to select K and an LPC to avoid per-class clustering.

Strengths
- Problem motivation is compelling and timely; the paper articulates well why datasets may not align with domains.
- Using kernel mean embeddings to quantify distributional separations is reasonable and avoids parametric mixture assumptions in [20].
- The coupling of domain discovery with practical multi-source adaptation pipelines is clear, and the conditional test reshaping idea is natural.
- Reported empirical gains on both objects and actions suggest potential utility; qualitative analyses indicate discovered factors (pose/background) are interpretable.

Weaknesses and concerns
Methodological soundness
- Objective degeneracy and reliance on LPC: Maximizing between-domain MMD can favor trivial partitions unless strongly constrained. The LPC enforces near-equal class proportions per domain (δ=1%), which is a strong and often unrealistic assumption in real domain shift (label shift is common). It also precludes domains where some classes are absent, which contradicts many practical settings. There is no analysis of sensitivity to δ or of performance under label shift.
- Optimization clarity/validity: The paper maximizes a convex quadratic subject to linear constraints, which is in general NP-hard; there is no algorithmic detail (solver, initialization, convergence) nor guarantees on rounding from β to discrete assignments. The per-sample constraint 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C is unusual; without a one-hot or entropy/regularization term, β can be diffuse, and the final argmax rounding is ad hoc. No ablations are provided to show robustness of the continuous relaxation.
- Separation vs. learnability: The assignment objective uses y only via LPC; it does not directly promote discriminative learnability. DWCV selects K but does not influence the assignments for a fixed K. This two-stage design risks discovering partitions that are maximally different in x yet unhelpful for classification, unless LPC happens to suffice.

Experimental validation
- Oracle metrics: GOURS (best source chosen with target labels) is an upper bound and not practically achievable. While “MATCHING” is claimed to approach the oracle without labels, the procedure is underspecified (how is matching done; what similarity; is target unlabeled?). More detail and a careful comparison are needed.
- Baselines: Comparisons omit several strong and simple alternatives, e.g., stratified random partitions matching class proportions, spectral clustering in RKHS with label-proportion regularization, supervised discriminative clustering (e.g., joint assignment and classification loss), or applying [20] with careful model selection. The dismissal of K-means/random is stated but results are “omitted for brevity”.
- Sensitivity and robustness: No studies on δ, kernel bandwidth, K selection stability, multiple random restarts, or scalability (the method uses an M×M kernel and quadratic programming). Runtime and memory are not reported.
- Assumption violations: Since LPC enforces all classes in all domains, it would be important to test scenarios where classes are missing per domain (common in practice) and to evaluate performance under label shift.

Theory and positioning
- Despite references to adaptation theory, there is no analysis connecting the objective to target risk (e.g., HΔH divergence bounds), nor guarantees that maximizing pairwise MMD among sources improves adaptation to unseen targets. The “axioms” are plausible but currently heuristic.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but key details are deferred to the supplement (conditional reshaping optimization) or omitted (matching procedure, solver). The constraints and relaxation would benefit from a more careful derivation and discussion of potential degeneracies.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to δ in LPC and to the kernel bandwidth? What happens if some classes are absent in some domains?
- What solver and initialization are used for the quadratic program? Do you use multiple restarts?
- How exactly is “MATCHING” performed without labels on the target? Does it use the same kernel discrepancy? Is any target data used during training?
- What is the complexity on your largest setting (M, runtime, memory)? Can the method scale beyond a few thousand examples?
- Can you report ablations: without LPC; with weaker LPC (larger δ); with a simple entropy/peakedness regularizer on β?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important practical gap (datasets ≠ domains).
- Nonparametric formulation; interpretable discovered factors.
- Empirical improvements on two tasks; method integrates with existing adaptation pipelines.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on a strong and potentially unrealistic LPC; unclear robustness.
- Objective/relaxation lacks theoretical support and detailed algorithmic treatment; possible degeneracies.
- Experimental validation leans on oracle metrics; baselines and ablations are insufficient; several details are in the supplement or missing.
- Scalability and sensitivity unaddressed.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is interesting and potentially useful, but the current submission lacks methodological rigor (objective/constraints/optimization), relies on restrictive assumptions (LPC), and does not provide sufficiently thorough and reproducible validation against strong baselines. Strengthening the optimization details, relaxing LPC, adding theory/ablations, and broadening empirical comparisons would substantially improve the paper.

Confidence
High.
