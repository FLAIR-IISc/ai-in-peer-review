### Summary
This paper tackles the problem of discovering latent domains within visual datasets for the purpose of improving domain adaptation. The authors argue that the common practice of treating entire datasets (e.g., Caltech-256, Amazon) as single, monolithic domains is flawed, as these datasets are often heterogeneous agglomerations of multiple underlying data distributions (e.g., different lighting, poses, backgrounds). The core proposal is an unsupervised method to "reshape" one or more source datasets into a set of latent domains that are more coherent and useful for adaptation.

The proposed method is guided by two principles: (1) **Maximum Distinctiveness**, where the discovered domains should have maximally different distributions, measured non-parametrically using a kernel-based two-sample test statistic (MMD in an RKHS); and (2) **Maximum Learnability**, where the discovered domains should be suitable for training strong classifiers. This second principle is cleverly used to automatically determine the number of latent domains (K) via a "Domain-Wise Cross-Validation" (DWCV) procedure. The discovery process is formulated as an optimization problem that maximizes the Total Domain Distinctiveness (TDD) subject to a Label Prior Constraint (LPC), which ensures class proportions are maintained across the discovered domains. The authors demonstrate their approach on standard object and action recognition benchmarks, showing that adapting from these discovered domains leads to superior performance compared to adapting from the original datasets.

### Strengths
1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The paper's central premise—that datasets are not domains—is a simple but profound observation that challenges a foundational assumption in a significant body of domain adaptation literature. By framing the problem as one of discovering latent domains, the authors move beyond simply "correcting dataset bias" and towards a more structured understanding of the sources of domain shift. This is a significant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Methodology:** The two guiding principles of "maximum distinctiveness" and "maximum learnability" are intuitive and well-motivated. The use of a non-parametric, kernel-based MMD measure for distinctiveness is theoretically sound and avoids restrictive parametric assumptions about the domain distributions (a key advantage over prior work like Hoffman et al. [20]). The DWCV procedure for selecting K is a pragmatic and clever solution to a difficult model selection problem.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Experimental Validation:** The experiments are well-designed to support the paper's claims.
    *   The "oracle" evaluation (GOURS vs GORIG) in Table 1 provides a clear upper-bound analysis, effectively demonstrating that the discovered domains have more potential for adaptation than the original dataset partitions.
    *   The practical utility is shown in Table 2, where standard multi-source adaptation techniques perform better when applied to the discovered domains. The MATCHING strategy's ability to approach oracle performance is particularly impressive.
    *   The introduction and evaluation of "conditional reshaping" for the test set (Table 3) is a valuable extension, showing that the framework can be applied symmetrically to both source and target data.
    *   The qualitative analysis in Figure 1 provides welcome intuition about what kinds of visual factors (pose, background, color) the method is capturing.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, the method is developed logically in Section 2, and the experiments directly test the hypotheses.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors
1.  **The Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** This is the most significant assumption in the model. The paper justifies it with the intuition that data collection processes are often independent of the domain-defining factors. However, this may not always hold. A domain shift could be correlated with the class prior itself (e.g., a "wildlife" domain will have different animal frequencies than a "domestic pets" domain). The constraint seems to preclude the discovery of such domains. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the limitations of this assumption and potential scenarios where it might be violated or could be relaxed.

2.  **Scalability and Optimization Details:** The paper notes that the relaxed optimization problem (Eq. 4) is a non-convex quadratic program (maximization of a convex function) and is NP-hard, but states that "existing solvers are adequate". For a NIPS audience, more detail would be beneficial. Which solver was used? How does the method scale with the number of data points (M) and the number of domains (K)? Providing runtime figures or complexity analysis would help readers assess the practical applicability of the method to larger datasets like ImageNet.

3.  **Sensitivity to Kernel Choice:** The entire distinctiveness measure relies on the choice of kernel and its parameters (e.g., the bandwidth of the Gaussian kernel). The paper follows a common heuristic for setting the bandwidth. However, the quality of the discovered domains is likely sensitive to this choice. A brief discussion or ablation study on the effect of the kernel parameter would make the empirical results more robust.

4.  **Clarity on "Conditional Reshaping":** The details of the optimization for reshaping the test set are deferred to the supplementary material. While this is understandable for space reasons, it makes a full evaluation of this contribution difficult from the main paper alone. A brief summary of the objective function and constraints in the main text would be helpful.

### Overall Assessment
This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong contribution to the field of domain adaptation. Its primary strength lies in its novel and insightful problem formulation, which could influence how researchers approach domain adaptation problems in the future. The proposed method is principled, technically sound, and backed by comprehensive and convincing experiments. While there are some minor weaknesses related to the strength of the LPC assumption and a lack of detail on computational complexity, these do not detract significantly from the overall impact of the work. The paper is original, significant, and clearly written.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
