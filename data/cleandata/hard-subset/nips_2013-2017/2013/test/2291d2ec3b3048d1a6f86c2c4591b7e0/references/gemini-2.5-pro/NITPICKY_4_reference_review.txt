**Summary**

This paper addresses a foundational problem in visual domain adaptation: the common practice of equating datasets with domains. The authors argue that datasets are often heterogeneous collections of multiple latent domains, and treating them as monolithic entities can hinder adaptation performance. To remedy this, they propose a novel unsupervised method to "reshape" datasets by discovering these latent domains. The discovery process is guided by two principles: 1) "maximum distinctiveness," where domains should be maximally different from each other, quantified by a non-parametric, kernel-based distribution distance measure (sum of pairwise MMDs), and 2) "maximum learnability," where each discovered domain must contain sufficient data to train a robust classifier, a principle used to determine the optimal number of domains via a proposed "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) procedure. The optimization for distinctiveness is formulated as a relaxed quadratic program with a "label prior constraint" (LPC) that enforces similar class distributions across domains. The authors demonstrate experimentally on object and action recognition tasks that adapting from their discovered domains yields superior performance compared to adapting from the original datasets or domains found by a competing method.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Important Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a conceptually important and often-overlooked issue in domain adaptation. Questioning the assumption that "dataset = domain" is a valuable contribution and encourages a more nuanced view of the problem.
2.  **Intuitive Principles:** The guiding principles of "maximum distinctiveness" and "maximum learnability" are well-motivated and provide a sound conceptual basis for the proposed method. The use of a non-parametric measure for distinctiveness is a notable strength, avoiding restrictive distributional assumptions made by prior work [20].
3.  **Promising Empirical Results:** The experiments show that classifiers adapted from the discovered domains consistently outperform those adapted from the original datasets. The gains reported in Tables 1, 2, and 3 are significant and suggest that the proposed reshaping procedure is effective.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the novel ideas, the paper suffers from a substantial number of flaws in its technical presentation, experimental validation, and overall clarity, which collectively undermine its soundness and reproducibility.

1.  **Lack of Technical Clarity and Missing Details:** The manuscript omits several critical methodological details.
    *   The optimization problem in Eq. (4) is a non-concave quadratic program. The authors state that "existing solvers are adequate" but fail to specify which solver was used or discuss the scalability of the approach. The number of variables is M x K, which could be very large. Without these details, the work is not reproducible.
    *   The "MATCHING" strategy, which is shown to achieve oracle performance in Table 2, is never explained. How is the best source domain selected for a given target without using target labels? This is a crucial component of the practical application, and its omission is a major flaw.
    *   The method for "conditional reshaping" of the test set is relegated to supplementary material. This is unacceptable for a core contribution of the paper. The main text must be self-contained.
    *   The justification for the upper bound on K in the DWCV procedure (`Kmax <= min{M/(NC),C}`) is hand-wavy and lacks rigor. A more formal justification is needed.

2.  **Questionable Assumptions and Design Choices:**
    *   The Label Prior Constraint (LPC) is a very strong assumption. It posits that class priors are independent of the domain. This may not hold in many real-world scenarios (e.g., a "beach" domain is more likely to contain "swimmers" than a "boardroom" domain). The potential limitations of this constraint are not discussed.
    *   The choice of `δ = 1%` for the LPC relaxation is arbitrary and presented without justification or sensitivity analysis.

3.  **Insufficient Experimental Rigor:**
    *   The paper mentions that K-means and random partitioning were tried as baselines but dismisses them by stating they did not yield "competitive performance" and omitting the results for brevity. This is poor scientific practice. Quantitative results for simple, standard baselines are essential for contextualizing the performance of the proposed complex method.
    *   The GOURS metric provides a loose upper bound and may be misleading. It is an oracle that rewards finding even a single "pure" domain that matches a test set, but it doesn't penalize the potential fragmentation of the data into many other less useful domains.
    *   The observation in Figure 2 that the optimal K "coincides with the number of datasets we mix" is suspicious. This could suggest the method is merely rediscovering the original, known dataset boundaries. While the qualitative results in Figure 1 suggest this is not the case, this coincidence warrants a much deeper analysis than is provided.

4.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:** The paper exhibits a lack of meticulousness that is unbecoming of a top-tier publication.
    *   The mathematical notation is imprecise at times. For instance, in Eq. (2), the arguments for `d(k, k')` are omitted.
    *   The reference list is egregiously incomplete. Numerous citations (e.g., [1], [2], [4], [11], [20], [21], etc.) are missing conference/journal names, page numbers, or years. This signals a rushed and careless preparation of the manuscript.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper identifies and provides a novel algorithmic solution to a fundamental problem in domain adaptation.
*   The core concepts of maximizing distinctiveness and learnability are elegant and intuitive.
*   The reported performance gains are substantial.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is not self-contained due to critical details about the optimization, matching strategy, and test-set reshaping being either omitted or relegated to supplementary material. This makes the work non-reproducible.
*   The experimental evaluation is incomplete, lacking important baseline comparisons (K-means) and a deeper analysis of key results (coincidence of K with the number of source datasets).
*   The Label Prior Constraint is a strong, potentially unrealistic assumption whose limitations are not discussed.
*   The overall presentation is sloppy, particularly the incomplete reference list, which raises concerns about the care with which the research was conducted and documented.

**Conclusion**

While the paper introduces an interesting and potentially impactful idea, the execution is severely flawed. The lack of crucial methodological details, insufficient experimental rigor, and careless presentation make it impossible to properly assess the technical soundness and reproducibility of the work. The paper falls well short of the high standards expected for NIPS. I therefore recommend rejection. The authors should be encouraged to conduct a more thorough and rigorous validation and to present their work with the clarity and completeness necessary for publication at a premier venue.
