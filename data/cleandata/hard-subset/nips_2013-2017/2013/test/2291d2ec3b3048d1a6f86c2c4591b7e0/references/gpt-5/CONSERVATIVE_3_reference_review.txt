Summary
The paper tackles the observation that visual datasets often bundle heterogeneous “domains” (e.g., pose, background, illumination), and proposes to automatically reshape datasets into latent domains to improve domain adaptation. The core idea has two parts: (1) identify K latent domains by maximizing pairwise distributional distinctiveness among domains using a kernel two-sample measure (MMD) in RKHS, subject to label prior constraints; and (2) select K via “domain-wise cross-validation” (DWCV), which trains discriminative classifiers within each candidate domain and picks the K that maximizes a weighted cross-validated accuracy. A relaxed quadratic program is used for the assignment. The method is evaluated on Office-like object datasets (A/D/W/C) and IXMAS actions, using GFK for adaptation. The authors also propose “conditional reshaping” of test data to match discovered training domains.

Strengths
- Addresses a real and under-discussed issue: datasets ≠ domains. The problem setting is important for practical adaptation.
- Nonparametric distinctiveness criterion (MMD) avoids strong parametric assumptions (contrast to [20]).
- Simple, generally applicable recipe; could plug into different adaptation methods.
- Empirical results suggest that discovered domains can outperform treating datasets as domains and a prior latent-domain method [20]; qualitative analysis illustrates plausible factors (pose, background, color).

Weaknesses
- Reliance on labels: The label prior constraint (LPC) and DWCV make the discovery procedure supervised or at least label-dependent. The LPC assumes identical class priors across domains, which is strong and often violated in practice; it may also inject label information into domain discovery and bias partitions.
- Optimization clarity and rigor: The relaxed objective is a maximization of a convex quadratic over linear constraints, which is non-convex. The paper provides no algorithmic details (solver choice, initialization, convergence behavior), no guarantees, and no discussion of degeneracies. The constraint in Eq. (5) involving ∑k βmk is unusual and needs justification/correction. Scalability is unclear since the method forms an M×M kernel matrix (O(M^2) memory/time).
- Limited baselines and ablations: Beyond [20], there is no comparison to strong, natural alternatives such as spectral clustering in RKHS (with or without label-proportion constraints), kernel k-means with constraints, mixture-of-experts/domain discovery variants, or even established adaptation pipelines (e.g., TCA, SA, CORAL) combined with dataset-as-domain. No ablations for LPC (on/off), δ sensitivity, kernel bandwidth sensitivity, K selection strategy, or adaptation method dependence (all results appear tied to GFK).
- Oracle and practical protocols: “Oracle” accuracies require target labels. The “MATCHING” strategy claimed to approach oracle without labels is not described (distance used? selection rule? any test-time hyperparameters?). The “conditional reshaping” of test data is deferred to the supplement; without details, it is hard to assess soundness.
- No theory or statistical guarantees: No argument that maximizing pairwise MMD under LPC recovers meaningful nuisance domains rather than per-class groupings (indeed LPC is introduced to avoid that, but then the procedure depends on a potentially incorrect assumption). No analysis of K selection beyond empirical curves; risk of overfitting via DWCV is not addressed.

Quality
Technically plausible but underspecified. The objective is reasonable, yet optimization, complexity, and robustness are not convincingly handled. The empirical evaluation suggests improvements, but lacks breadth (few datasets/tasks), statistical rigor (variances, multiple runs), and strong baselines/ablations to isolate where gains come from (e.g., from labels, from K selection, or from the MMD criterion).

Clarity
Generally readable, with motivating examples and clear high-level goals. However, several critical pieces are missing or confusing: the relaxation and constraints (Eq. 5) need careful derivation; solver details and complexity are absent; “MATCHING” and test reshaping are under-specified (relegated to supplemental material).

Originality
Moderate. The idea of using RKHS mean embeddings and MMD for distributional comparison is well-established; maximizing discriminability among clusters appears close in spirit to generalized kernel clustering (e.g., Jegelka et al.). The most novel aspects are the specific combination with LPC and DWCV for K selection. The overlap with [20] is acknowledged; more differentiation and empirical comparison breadth would help.

Significance
Potentially useful if robust and scalable, as domain discovery could benefit many adaptation pipelines. However, given current evidence, the contribution feels preliminary: heavy reliance on labels, limited scale, incomplete baselines, and unclear optimization/scalability dampen impact.

Specific questions for the authors
- Clarify the relaxation: precise derivation of βmk = zmk/Mk, all constraints, and why Eq. (5) has 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C. What guarantees prevent tiny domains (e.g., Mk ≈ C) that trivially maximize TDD?
- Algorithmic details: solver, initialization, runtime, memory footprint, and scalability to larger M. How many restarts? Sensitivity to kernel bandwidth and δ (set to 1%)?
- What happens if LPC is removed or relaxed to allow domain-varying class priors (a realistic setting in dataset shift)?
- Define the “MATCHING” strategy used without target labels. Is it nearest in MMD to source domains, or something else? How is the ensemble constructed?
- Provide ablations: effect of LPC on/off; different K selection criteria; different adaptation methods (TCA/SA/CORAL) to show method-agnostic gains; sensitivity plots for K and kernel bandwidth. Report averages and standard deviations over multiple random seeds.
- Quantify the discovered domains: distribution of Mk, class proportions within domains, and stability across runs.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and practical problem (latent domains in vision datasets).
- Conceptually simple, nonparametric approach; promising empirical results.
- Could stimulate more principled dataset/domain construction.

Cons for acceptance
- Method depends on strong label-based assumptions and lacks theoretical guarantees.
- Optimization and scalability are underspecified; potential non-convexity issues unaddressed.
- Evaluation is not rigorous enough (limited baselines, ablations, and reporting).
- Key methodological details relegated to supplement.

Recommendation
Reject. While the problem is important and the approach is interesting, the current submission lacks the rigor, breadth of evaluation, and methodological clarity needed to justify acceptance. Strengthening optimization details, relaxing label assumptions, broadening baselines/ablations, and providing robustness analyses would substantially improve the paper.

Reviewer confidence
High.
