Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles an important and under-explored question in domain adaptation for vision: datasets often amalgamate multiple latent domains, and treating a dataset as a single domain can harm adaptation. The authors propose to automatically “reshape” datasets into latent domains guided by two principles: maximum distinctiveness (find partitions whose marginal feature distributions differ as much as possible) and maximum learnability (ensure each domain supports learning a strong discriminative model). Distinctiveness is operationalized by maximizing pairwise RKHS mean-discrepancy (MMD) between empirical distributions of domains; learnability is used to select the number of domains K via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV). A label prior constraint (LPC) enforces that each discovered domain preserves the global class proportion to avoid partitions that collapse to per-class clusters. The combinatorial assignment is relaxed to a continuous quadratic program over β variables with linear constraints; instances are assigned by argmax β. The discovered domains are then used with standard adaptation (e.g., Geodesic Flow Kernel). The paper also proposes “conditional reshaping” of heterogeneous test sets to match training domains by minimizing distributional discrepancy.

This builds on and contrasts with Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12) who discover latent domains using hierarchical Gaussian mixtures; here, the authors avoid parametric assumptions via kernel embeddings. It also relates to generalized clustering via kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.) and to multi-source adaptation, but differs in discovering domains rather than assuming them given.

Strengths
- Problem importance and framing: The paper convincingly argues that dataset≠domain in vision and that discovering latent domains can improve adaptation. The “distinctiveness” and “learnability” principles are intuitive and useful.
- Nonparametric criterion: Using MMD-like RKHS mean embedding distances to drive partitioning avoids brittle parametric mixture assumptions.
- Practical constraints and model selection: The LPC cleverly counters trivial per-class partitions, and DWCV ties K-selection to downstream discriminative utility rather than purely unsupervised cluster quality.
- Extensibility to test-time: Conditional reshaping of test data to align with training domains is a practical and interesting addition, especially in cross-view action recognition.
- Empirical scope: Experiments on standard cross-dataset object recognition (A/D/W/C) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) show that discovered domains often outperform original datasets and a prior latent-domain method [20], with “matching” approaching oracle performance.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Optimization/formulation clarity and guarantees: The relaxed problem maximizes a convex quadratic under linear constraints—still non-convex/NP-hard. The paper acknowledges this but provides no algorithmic details (solver, initialization, multi-start strategy, stopping criteria), complexity, or sensitivity. Without these, reproducibility and reliability are unclear. The β reparameterization (βmk = zmk/Mk) leads to constraints such as 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C that are only loosely motivated; the derivation from Mk ∈ [C, M] is not rigorous, and the semantics of ∑k βmk for an instance are non-standard (it no longer sums to 1). This invites concerns about degenerate solutions and sensitivity to constraint choices.
- Risk of tiny or imbalanced domains: The objective increases with K and can prefer very small domains. Apart from crude bounds and ex post K-selection by DWCV, there is no explicit regularization for balanced partitions (e.g., entropy/size penalties). Stability of the discovered domains under resampling or small perturbations is not analyzed.
- Assumption behind LPC: LPC assumes class priors are domain-invariant. This is often violated in practice (e.g., web vs. office images). The method also requires labeled source data and uses labels both in constraints and in DWCV, so it is not “unsupervised” with respect to labels. The paper does not test robustness when priors differ.
- Limited theoretical grounding: There is no analysis linking the maximized distinctiveness or DWCV to adaptation risk (e.g., via domain divergence bounds). As a result, the choice of objective remains heuristic.
- Experimental reporting gaps: Several crucial details are missing: optimization procedure specifics, runtime/memory scaling with M and K, sensitivity to kernel and bandwidth, ablations showing the effect of LPC (with/without), and comparisons against strong modern baselines (e.g., feature-space clustering with deep features). Some evaluations rely on oracle maxima across sources, which are not achievable in practice. The statement that MATCHING reaches oracle without using target labels needs a precise description of the unsupervised matching criterion used. Conditional reshaping details are deferred to the supplement.
- Minor clarity issues: Notation around constraints (Eq. 5) is confusing; it would help to restate the relaxed problem cleanly and justify each constraint. Some references are incomplete.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: The approach is technically plausible and the problem is important, but the optimization is under-specified and lacks guarantees; assumptions (LPC) may be brittle; theoretical justification is limited. Empirical results are promising but need more rigorous and transparent reporting and ablations.
- Clarity: Generally well-written and well-motivated, but the optimization and constraints need clearer derivation and algorithmic details; conditional reshaping should be described in the main text at least at a high level.
- Originality: The combination of nonparametric domain separation via kernel embeddings, a label prior constraint, and DWCV for K-selection is novel relative to prior latent-domain discovery. Conditional reshaping of test sets is also a nice addition.
- Significance: If robust, the ideas could impact multi-source adaptation and dataset curation in vision. The current presentation suggests potential, but missing details and assumptions reduce immediate usability.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses a meaningful and practical gap in domain adaptation.
- Proposes a simple, flexible, nonparametric criterion and a practical K-selection scheme.
- Shows gains over treating datasets as domains and over a prior latent-domain method.
- Introduces test-time conditional reshaping with promising results.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Optimization is non-convex with insufficient algorithmic detail and no guarantees.
- Strong LPC assumption; no robustness analysis to violated class-prior invariance.
- Lack of theoretical connection to adaptation risk.
- Missing ablations and sensitivity analyses; some evaluations use oracles.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full optimization details, initialization strategies, complexity, and empirical convergence; consider multi-start and report stability.
- Add a size/entropy regularizer or explicit balance constraints; explore integrating K-selection into the objective.
- Test robustness to class-prior shift and include ablations for LPC and kernel bandwidth.
- Compare to simpler clustering baselines (e.g., k-means/spectral on strong features) with/without label-aware constraints; add modern adaptation backbones.
- Provide a theoretical discussion connecting distinctiveness to multi-source generalization bounds.

Overall, I find the problem important and the approach promising, but I am concerned about the under-specified optimization and the reliance on a potentially violated LPC assumption. With stronger experimental rigor and clearer optimization details, this could be a solid contribution. As it stands, I lean toward a borderline reject.
