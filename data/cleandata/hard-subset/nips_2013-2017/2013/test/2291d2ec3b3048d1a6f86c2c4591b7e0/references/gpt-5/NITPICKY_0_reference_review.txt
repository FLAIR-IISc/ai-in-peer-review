Summary
The paper addresses the problem of “domain discovery” in visual datasets: instead of equating datasets with domains, it proposes to automatically reshape datasets into latent domains that are (i) maximally distinctive in distribution (via nonparametric kernel mean embeddings/MMD) and (ii) maximally learnable (via domain-wise cross-validation to select K). The method casts domain assignment as an optimization that maximizes the sum of pairwise squared RKHS mean distances across K domains, subject to constraints that each sample belongs to exactly one domain and that each domain roughly matches the global label prior (LPC). The NP-hard integer program is relaxed to a continuous QCQP using βmk = zmk/Mk on simplices. The number of domains K is selected by maximizing a weighted cross-validated accuracy A(K). Experiments on object (Amazon/DSLR/Webcam/Caltech-256) and action (IXMAS) recognition suggest improved adaptation performance over using original datasets and over [20], and the paper also explores “conditional reshaping” of test sets to match discovered source domains.

Strengths
- Targets an important and under-studied subproblem: the mismatch between dataset boundaries and true latent domains.
- Uses a principled discrepancy (MMD via kernel mean embeddings) without parametric assumptions; the label prior constraint is an intuitive way to avoid per-class clustering.
- Proposes a clear operational criterion for selecting K (“maximum learnability”) via DWCV.
- Evaluates both source reshaping and test-time conditional reshaping; qualitative analysis hints at interpretable latent factors (pose, background).
- Potentially general and modular (can be paired with existing adaptation methods like GFK).

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and clarity
- Notation is confusing and sometimes inconsistent:
  - The kernel function and the domain index both use k; in Eq. (1) k(·, ·) and domain index k are overloaded.
  - In Eq. (1), M′k appears but is not defined (likely Mk′). Typo “the the difference.”
  - In (4), “maxβ ∑k≠k′ TDD(K) = ∑k≠k′ (βk − βk′)T K (βk − βk′)” mixes the objective and its name; TDD(K) is already the sum.
- The objective (sum of pairwise MMD2) heavily favors increasing K and can lead to degenerate “tiny-domain” solutions; the only safeguards are (i) LPC and (ii) the a posteriori DWCV for K. There is no within-optimization regularizer for domain balance or complexity (e.g., entropy or size penalties), which makes the formulation brittle.
- The per-sample constraint 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C is unusual and weak. It does not enforce one-domain-per-instance in the relaxation (only bounds the aggregate weight). It follows from C ≤ Mk ≤ M in the hard case, but in the relaxed case it permits substantial mass-splitting. A standard relaxation would use ∑k zmk = 1 with zmk ∈ {0,1}, relaxed to ∑k αmk = 1, αmk ≥ 0, which is not what is done here.
- LPC is very strong: it enforces that each domain matches the global label prior (up to δ). This may not hold in practice and can suppress real domain heterogeneity that correlates with labels. It also contradicts the stated allowance for some classes to be rare/absent: later, A(K) is set to 0 when a domain has ≤1 sample for a class, which suggests violations are expected. How often is LPC infeasible and what happens then?
- The optimization is a nonconvex QCQP (“maximization of a convex quadratic over polytopes”), acknowledged as NP-hard. The paper claims “existing solvers are adequate,” but provides no solver name, initialization strategy, stopping criteria, or run-time/memory complexity. With M up to thousands and K unknown, this is critical for reproducibility.
- Sensitivity is not analyzed: kernel choice/bandwidth (they set σ to twice the median pairwise distance) and δ for LPC could qualitatively change solutions. No ablations.
- DWCV: Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C} is asserted without derivation. The dependence on folds N and class count C is only loosely motivated. Using labels to pick K may bias domain discovery toward class-discriminative partitions rather than truly covariate-based domains.

Experimental evaluation
- Quantitative results are not presented in the text (Tables 1–3 are referenced but not shown here). Claims of “far better” and “significant improvement” cannot be assessed. No effect sizes, variance, or significance tests.
- Comparison to [20] is asserted but details are scarce: implementation, parameter matching, fairness (e.g., both methods using the same features/GFK), and robustness across random seeds are not reported.
- Baselines are limited. Simple alternatives like constrained K-means/GMM with label-proportion constraints, spectral clustering on MMD graphs, or mixture of subspaces/domain-specific subspace discovery are not compared. A random partition baseline is mentioned but omitted.
- The “MATCHING” strategy that allegedly reaches oracle accuracy is not described in the main text (how is matching done without labels? MMD to target? Model selection bias?).
- Conditional test reshaping details are deferred to the supplement (“convex QP”); crucial to the claim but not in the paper. Also unclear if the number of test domains is fixed to K or learned; how are unmatched domains handled?
- Dataset protocols are insufficiently specified: number of images per category per dataset, train/test splits, cross-validation details (N, folds per domain), and how class imbalance is handled. For IXMAS, the selection of “first five actions” and actors is ad hoc; exact counts are not provided.
- No computational performance (time/memory) reported; scalability to modern visual datasets is unclear.

Writing/formatting issues
- Several typos and inconsistencies: “the the difference,” “two-sampleproblem,” “Scholkopf/Schölkopf,” inconsistent use of primes, capitalization, missing years/venues/pages in many references, inconsistent equation punctuation, and overloaded symbols.
- Some technical terms are imprecise (e.g., calling the kernel “positive semidefinite” is fine, but the property needed is “characteristic”).
- Figures 1–2 and tables are critical but not discussed with sufficient quantitative detail; captions/axes not provided here.

Originality and significance
- The use of MMD to separate distributions and a label-proportion constraint is a reasonable combination but incremental. Selecting K by cross-validated accuracy is standard. The novelty relative to [20] (nonparametric vs. parametric mixtures) is clear, but the methodological depth and guarantees are limited.
- Potential impact is interesting (better domain definitions), but current evidence is insufficiently rigorous to support strong claims.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide explicit solver details, complexity, and sensitivity to initialization; report runtimes and M limits.
2. Analyze sensitivity to kernel bandwidth and δ; include ablations.
3. Justify and evaluate LPC: how often is it infeasible? What happens when true domains have different class priors?
4. Clarify the relaxation: why bound ∑k βmk instead of enforcing ∑k αmk = 1 with αmk ≥ 0? Does your current relaxation lead to mass-splitting?
5. Fully specify MATCHING and conditional test reshaping in the main text; report quantitative gains with error bars.
6. Add stronger baselines and statistical tests; release code/splits.

Pros
- Addresses a meaningful gap: dataset ≠ domain.
- Nonparametric discrepancy; avoids Gaussian assumptions.
- Clear, intuitive criterion for K via learnability.
- Qualitative insights on visual factors.

Cons
- Nonconvex QCQP with limited algorithmic detail and no guarantees.
- Strong, potentially unrealistic LPC; unclear feasibility.
- Missing quantitative details/tables/variance; limited baselines.
- Notation/typos/formatting issues; key methods deferred to supplement.
- Sensitivity and scalability not evaluated.

Recommendation: Reject (significant promise, but methodological and experimental rigor are insufficient; requires substantial revisions, clearer algorithms, stronger empirical evidence, and improved presentation).
