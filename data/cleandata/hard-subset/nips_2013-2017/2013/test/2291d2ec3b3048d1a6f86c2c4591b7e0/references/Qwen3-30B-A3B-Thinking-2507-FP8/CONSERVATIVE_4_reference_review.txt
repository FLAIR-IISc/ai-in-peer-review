

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically reshape visual datasets into characteristic domains by optimizing for two properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing inter-domain distribution differences) and maximum learnability (ensuring domains contain sufficient data for strong classifiers). The authors claim this addresses a fundamental gap in visual domain adaptation research, where datasets are often incorrectly treated as single domains despite containing multiple latent domains.

**Strengths:** The paper identifies an important conceptual issue in visual domain adaptation research, where datasets are often treated as single domains when they may contain multiple underlying domains. The problem statement is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear theoretical framework for domain discovery. The experimental validation on object and action recognition tasks shows promising results compared to using the original datasets as domains.

**Major Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of rigorous comparison to established clustering methods:** The paper claims their approach outperforms [20] but fails to compare against standard clustering techniques (K-means, hierarchical clustering, etc.) that would be the natural baseline for a clustering-based approach. The authors dismiss traditional clustering as "prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains" but provide no empirical evidence that their method is substantially better than well-tuned clustering approaches. Without these comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the authors have truly developed a novel technique or simply applied kernel methods to a clustering problem.

2. **Circular validation:** The experimental setup assumes that the test domains are already well-defined (e.g., different camera views in IXMAS), which is precisely the problem the paper claims to solve. The paper evaluates whether reshaping training data into domains improves adaptation to known test domains, but this doesn't address the core claim that the discovered domains are meaningful. If the test domains are already labeled by the dataset creators (as in the experiments), then the problem is trivially solved by using those labels as the domains.

3. **Questionable theoretical foundation:** The authors claim their approach is "nonparametric" but their optimization problem is based on kernel methods that implicitly assume certain properties about the data. The justification for using the specific kernel-based distribution distance (Equation 1) is not sufficiently grounded in theoretical principles of domain adaptation or clustering.

4. **Lack of interpretability validation:** The paper claims the discovered domains correspond to meaningful factors like pose and illumination, but the examples shown (Figure 1) appear to be cherry-picked. The authors don't provide quantitative evidence that their domains correspond to meaningful visual factors rather than just arbitrary clusters. The statement that "the main factors here are probably background and object appearance" is purely speculative without supporting evidence.

5. **DWCV method for determining K is problematic:** The domain-wise cross-validation approach for determining the number of domains (K) is not well-justified. The paper states that "the number favored by DWCV coincides with the number of datasets we mix," suggesting that their method simply rediscovered the dataset boundaries rather than finding meaningful latent domains. This undermines the core claim of discovering "latent" domains.

**Recommendation:** While the paper identifies a genuine issue in domain adaptation research, the proposed solution lacks sufficient novelty, theoretical grounding, and rigorous validation against appropriate baselines. The experimental validation is circular (assuming the test domains are already well-defined), and the method's advantages over standard clustering techniques are not convincingly demonstrated.

The paper would benefit from:
- Comprehensive comparison to standard clustering methods (K-means, spectral clustering, etc.)
- Validation on a task where the ground truth domains are unknown and must be discovered
- Quantitative analysis of whether the discovered domains correspond to meaningful visual factors
- Stronger theoretical justification for the proposed optimization framework

As currently written, the paper presents an interesting idea but fails to provide sufficient evidence that it represents a meaningful advance over established clustering techniques for domain discovery. The claims of "maximum distinctiveness" and "maximum learnability" are not rigorously demonstrated to be superior to standard clustering objectives.

**Final Recommendation:** Reject. The paper lacks sufficient evidence to support its core claims, and the proposed method does not convincingly advance the state of the art in domain adaptation or clustering. The validation methodology is flawed, and the comparison to relevant baselines is inadequate.
