

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper presents a fundamentally novel perspective on domain adaptation by challenging the common practice of treating datasets as domains. The authors correctly identify a critical flaw in current domain adaptation research: visual datasets are typically not naturally segmented into discrete domains, yet researchers often treat entire datasets as single domains. The core insight is that datasets often contain multiple latent domains that are not aligned with dataset boundaries, and these latent domains should be discovered rather than assumed.

The paper's main contribution is the introduction of two axiomatic properties for domain discovery: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different from each other in distribution) and maximum learnability (domains should support strong discriminative models). The authors develop nonparametric methods to measure domain discrepancies using kernel-based RKHS distances, and propose a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure to automatically determine the optimal number of domains. This is a significant departure from prior work, which either assumed domain boundaries (e.g., treating different datasets as domains) or used parametric clustering approaches (like Hoffman et al. [20]) that may not generalize well to real-world visual data.

The paper's novelty is substantial. While domain adaptation has been extensively studied, the conceptual shift from "datasets as domains" to "datasets contain latent domains" represents a genuinely new direction. The authors correctly identify a blind spot in the field that has been overlooked despite years of research. Their critique of current practices - "a troubling practice in visual domain adaptation research is to equate datasets with domains" - is well-founded and supported by empirical evidence in their experiments.

The experimental validation is strong and directly addresses the paper's core hypothesis. On both object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and action recognition (IXMAS) datasets, the authors demonstrate that reshaping datasets into their automatically discovered latent domains leads to significantly better adaptation performance than using the original datasets as domains. The improvement over Hoffman et al.'s [20] method is particularly compelling, as their approach is the only prior work addressing latent domain discovery. The paper's quantitative results (Tables 1-3) clearly show that the authors' method outperforms both the naive approach of using entire datasets as domains and the competing method from [20].

The paper's clarity is excellent. The problem statement is well-motivated, the technical contributions are clearly articulated, and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. The authors carefully distinguish their work from related approaches (particularly [20]) and provide a convincing argument for why their nonparametric approach is better suited for visual data than parametric alternatives.

The only minor weaknesses I note are:
1. The computational complexity of DWCV for very large datasets isn't fully addressed
2. The paper doesn't explore cases where the optimal domain structure might be trivial (e.g., when a dataset is already well-structured into domains)

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I believe these are minor concerns. The paper's conceptual contribution is substantial enough to outweigh these minor limitations. The authors have successfully identified a fundamental flaw in current domain adaptation practices and proposed a novel, principled approach to address it.

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance in domain adaptation research. It challenges a long-standing assumption in the field and provides a new framework for thinking about how to structure visual datasets for adaptation. The experimental results validate the theoretical approach, demonstrating clear performance improvements over existing methods.

I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field and should be accepted. It's not merely an incremental improvement but a bold rethinking of how we conceptualize domains in visual recognition. The authors have identified an important blind spot in the literature and provided a novel solution that could reshape how future domain adaptation research is conducted. This is exactly the kind of conceptual innovation that NIPS should celebrate.

The paper's significance is clear: by shifting the focus from datasets to latent domains, the authors open up new research directions that could lead to more robust and generalizable domain adaptation methods. Their approach has the potential to influence not just how we evaluate domain adaptation, but how we collect and structure visual datasets in the future.

For these reasons, I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a genuine conceptual advance that deserves to be shared with the NIPS community.
