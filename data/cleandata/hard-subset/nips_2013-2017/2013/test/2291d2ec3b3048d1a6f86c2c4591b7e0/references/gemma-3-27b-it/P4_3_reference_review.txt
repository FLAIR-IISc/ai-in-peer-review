This paper proposes a method for automatically discovering latent domains within a visual dataset, aiming to improve domain adaptation performance. The core idea is to reshape the dataset into a set of domains that are both maximally distinct from each other and maximally learnable – meaning they allow for the creation of strong discriminative models. The authors formulate this as an optimization problem, leveraging kernel methods for measuring domain discrepancy and cross-validation for determining the optimal number of domains. Extensive experiments on object recognition and human activity recognition demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, particularly when combined with conditional reshaping of test datasets.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a crucial issue in domain adaptation: the often-ad-hoc way datasets are treated as domains. The authors convincingly argue that datasets frequently contain multiple underlying domains, and explicitly addressing this can lead to improved performance. The proposed method is technically sound, building upon established kernel methods and optimization techniques. The introduction of the "maximum distinctiveness" and "maximum learnability" properties provides a clear and principled framework for domain discovery. The experimental validation is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets and tasks. The inclusion of experiments on reshaping test data is particularly valuable, demonstrating the broader applicability of the approach. The analysis of identified domains and the impact of the number of domains are insightful.

However, several aspects could be strengthened. While the theoretical foundation is solid, the connection between the chosen kernel and the underlying data distribution isn’t thoroughly discussed. Justifying the Gaussian kernel choice, beyond its common use, would be beneficial. The computational complexity of the optimization procedure is not detailed. While the authors mention using “off-the-shelf” solvers, providing a complexity analysis or scalability discussion would be valuable.  The label prior constraint (LPC) is intuitively explained but lacks a rigorous justification. A more detailed discussion of its impact and sensitivity to parameter settings (δ=1%) is needed. The experimental section is extensive, but lacks a clear ablation study focusing on individual components (e.g., impact of the LPC). The choice of parameters for the kernel (bandwidth) is also somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from sensitivity analysis. Finally, while the authors compare with [20], a more detailed comparison addressing specific differences in assumptions, computational cost, and performance under different scenarios would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of automatically discovering latent domains within datasets is a novel contribution to the domain adaptation literature.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is based on established theoretical foundations and well-defined optimization criteria.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on multiple datasets and tasks.
*   **Significance:** Addressing the issue of dataset-as-domain equivalence has the potential to significantly improve domain adaptation performance in real-world applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Computational Complexity:**  Lack of detailed discussion on computational cost and scalability.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** Limited analysis of parameter settings and their impact on performance.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Some aspects, like the LPC and kernel choice, could benefit from further theoretical justification.
*   **Comparison with Related Work:** Comparison with [20] could be more detailed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in domain adaptation with a novel and technically sound approach. The experimental validation is convincing, and the results are promising. While some aspects could be strengthened, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding computational complexity, parameter sensitivity, and theoretical justification.
