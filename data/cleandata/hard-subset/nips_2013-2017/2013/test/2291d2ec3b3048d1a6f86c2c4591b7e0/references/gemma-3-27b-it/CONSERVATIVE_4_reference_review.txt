This paper addresses the important problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition, specifically questioning the common practice of equating datasets with domains. The authors argue that visual datasets often implicitly contain multiple, overlapping domains defined by factors like pose and illumination, and propose methods to automatically reshape datasets into more characteristic domains based on the principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.

The core idea is sound, and the motivation is well-presented. The problem statement is clearly articulated, and the critique of treating entire datasets as single domains is valid. However, my overall assessment is skeptical. While the approach demonstrates promising empirical results, the reliance on heuristics (kernel-based distribution differences, cross-validation for K selection) and the lack of a compelling theoretical justification raise concerns. The proposed methods feel more like engineering solutions than breakthroughs based on fundamental principles. 

A significant weakness lies in the ambiguity surrounding the concept of a “domain” itself. The authors acknowledge the difficulty in defining domains for visual data, yet their method attempts to *discover* them without a more rigorous formalization. This leaves the interpretation of the discovered domains somewhat open to subjectivity, and it's unclear if the resulting domains have a meaningful, interpretable relationship to the underlying factors (pose, illumination, etc.) that motivate the work. The illustrative examples in Figure 1 are helpful, but limited in scope and don’t provide strong evidence of consistently interpretable domain discovery.

The method relies heavily on kernel functions, and the choice of kernel bandwidth is critical. While they follow a standard approach, the sensitivity of the results to this parameter is not adequately discussed. Furthermore, the computational complexity of the optimization problem, even in its relaxed form, is not trivial, and the scalability to very large datasets is questionable.

The experimental evaluation, while extensive, feels somewhat incremental. The comparisons to [20] are favorable, but that work appears to be a relatively recent and limited baseline. More importantly, the paper doesn't sufficiently compare against established domain adaptation techniques *when the true underlying domains are unknown*. The experiments largely focus on demonstrating improvements over treating the entire dataset as a single domain, which is a relatively weak baseline. Presenting performance gains compared to more sophisticated unsupervised domain adaptation methods would significantly strengthen the claims. 

The DWCV approach for determining the number of domains feels somewhat ad-hoc. While the intuition behind maximizing learnability is reasonable, the cross-validation procedure relies on building classifiers within each domain, which introduces a potential bias in the evaluation. The correlation observed between the optimal K and the number of source datasets (Figure 2) is intriguing, but requires further investigation and explanation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and often overlooked issue in domain adaptation – the implicit multi-domain nature of visual datasets.
* Proposes a novel framework for automatically reshaping datasets into more characteristic domains.
* Demonstrates empirically that the identified domains can improve adaptation performance.
* Clear and well-written paper.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Lack of strong theoretical justification for the proposed methods.
* Reliance on heuristics and parameters (kernel bandwidth, DWCV) that may be sensitive and require careful tuning.
* Limited comparison to existing unsupervised domain adaptation techniques.
* The concept of a "domain" remains somewhat ill-defined and open to interpretation.
* Scalability concerns for large datasets.



**Overall:** I lean towards rejection. While the paper presents a reasonable approach to a relevant problem, the lack of strong theoretical grounding, the reliance on heuristics, and the limited comparison to competitive baselines raise significant concerns. The empirical results, while promising, are not sufficiently compelling to outweigh these weaknesses. The work feels more like a proof-of-concept than a substantial contribution to the field. I would encourage the authors to address the theoretical concerns, conduct more thorough comparisons to existing methods, and provide a more rigorous analysis of the identified domains before resubmitting.
