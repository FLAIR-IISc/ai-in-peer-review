**Summary**

This paper challenges the conventional practice in domain adaptation research of treating entire datasets as single, monolithic domains. The authors argue that datasets are often heterogeneous collections of multiple latent domains, and failing to account for this structure can harm adaptation performance. The central contribution is a novel, unsupervised method for "reshaping" datasets by automatically discovering these latent domains. The proposed framework is built upon two intuitive axiomatic properties: "maximum distinctiveness," ensuring that the discovered domains are distributionally as different as possible, and "maximum learnability," ensuring each domain contains sufficient, coherent data to train a strong classifier.

To achieve maximum distinctiveness, the authors propose a non-parametric objective based on maximizing the sum of pairwise kernel-based MMD (Maximum Mean Discrepancy) distances between all latent domains. This optimization is formulated as a relaxed quadratic program with a "label prior constraint" (LPC) that encourages similar class distributions across domains. To determine the optimal number of domains, K, the principle of maximum learnability is operationalized via a "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) procedure, which selects the K that yields the highest internal classification accuracy. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments on object and action recognition that adapting from these discovered domains significantly outperforms adapting from the original, monolithic datasets.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its exceptional novelty and the boldness of its core idea. It steps back from the crowded space of developing ever-more-complex adaptation algorithms and instead questions a fundamental, often implicit, assumption of the entire field: the nature of a "domain" itself. This conceptual shift from *how* to adapt to *what* to adapt from is refreshing and has the potential to significantly influence future work in domain adaptation and dataset curation. This is precisely the kind of high-impact, direction-setting work that NIPS should champion.

The proposed framework is principled and elegant. The two guiding properties—maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability—are well-motivated, intuitive, and provide a solid conceptual foundation. The choice of a non-parametric method using kernel MMD to measure distinctiveness is a major advantage over prior work (e.g., Hoffman et al. [20]), as it avoids restrictive parametric assumptions about the data distribution, making the approach more flexible and widely applicable.

The experimental validation is thorough and convincing. The authors wisely include an oracle experiment (GOURS) which clearly demonstrates that the discovered domains are inherently "better" for adaptation than the original datasets, providing a clean upper-bound analysis. More importantly, they show this theoretical advantage translates into practical gains using standard multi-source adaptation techniques. The qualitative results in Figure 1 are particularly compelling, showing that the automatically discovered domains correspond to semantically meaningful visual factors like object pose, background, and color, which validates the method's effectiveness. The extension to "conditional reshaping" of the test set is also a clever and valuable contribution that further enhances the utility of the framework.

**Weaknesses**

While the core contribution is strong, there are a few aspects that could be refined.

1.  **Optimization:** The paper correctly notes that the relaxed optimization problem (maximization of a convex quadratic function over a polytope) is generally NP-hard. While the authors state that off-the-shelf solvers provided satisfactory solutions, a brief discussion of the specific solver used, its convergence properties for this problem type, and the stability of the solutions would strengthen the paper's technical completeness. However, given the novelty of the formulation, this is a minor point that does not detract from the main contribution.

2.  **Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The LPC is a strong assumption. It posits that the relative frequencies of classes are independent of the domains. While the paper provides a reasonable intuition, one can easily imagine scenarios where this is violated (e.g., a "beach" domain is unlikely to have the same distribution of object classes as an "office" domain). This constraint simplifies the problem significantly but also limits the applicability to cases where domain shift and label shift are largely decoupled. This limitation should be acknowledged more directly.

3.  **Determining K:** The Domain-Wise Cross-Validation (DWCV) method for choosing K is heuristic. While the empirical results in Figure 2 show a correlation between the DWCV score and the final target accuracy, the connection is not formally established. The performance of this heuristic could be sensitive to the choice of the base classifier and cross-validation setup.

**Recommendation**

Despite these minor weaknesses, this is an excellent paper that introduces a significant and thought-provoking new direction for domain adaptation research. Its main strength is its bold reconceptualization of the problem. The methodological approach is sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated. The minor imperfections in the optimization details and the limiting nature of some assumptions are acceptable trade-offs for the high degree of novelty and potential impact. This work is likely to inspire follow-up research on latent domain discovery, multi-source adaptation, and even dataset design. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Novelty and Significance:** Proposes a new, fundamental problem of "reshaping datasets" for domain adaptation, challenging a core assumption in the field.
*   **Principled and Elegant Formulation:** The framework based on maximum distinctiveness and learnability is intuitive, and the non-parametric MMD-based approach is well-suited for complex visual data.
*   **Strong and Comprehensive Experiments:** The results, including oracle, practical, and qualitative analyses, convincingly demonstrate the value of the proposed approach.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The optimization is non-convex, and the paper provides limited details on the stability of the solutions found.
*   The Label Prior Constraint is a strong assumption that may not hold in all real-world scenarios.
