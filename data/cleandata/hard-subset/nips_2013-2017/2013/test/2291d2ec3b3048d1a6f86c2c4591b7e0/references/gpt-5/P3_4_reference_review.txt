Summary
This paper addresses the important but underexplored problem of latent domain discovery in visual datasets. Instead of treating each dataset as a monolithic domain, the authors propose reshaping datasets into multiple latent domains that are (i) maximally distinctive in marginal feature distribution and (ii) maximally learnable in the sense that each discovered domain supports a strong discriminative model. Distinctiveness is operationalized via pairwise RKHS mean embeddings (essentially MMD) between empirical domain distributions and optimized over (relaxed) instance-to-domain assignments with label prior constraints to avoid trivial class-based splits. Learnability determines K (the number of domains) by domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV): compute per-domain CV accuracy and select K maximizing a weighted average. The method is applied to object recognition (Office+Caltech10) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS), typically using GFK for adaptation. They further propose “conditional reshaping” of test data by partitioning it to best match the training domains (again via kernel discrepancies). Experiments report improved adaptation accuracy versus using original datasets or a parametric latent-domain approach [20], and qualitative analyses suggest discovered domains align with pose/background/appearance factors.

Relation to prior work
- Domain adaptation: The work is complementary to adaptation algorithms such as GFK, TCA, and multi-source adaptation; here the focus is discovering domains before adapting. It also connects to dataset bias studies (e.g., Torralba & Efros).
- Latent domain discovery: Closest to Hoffman et al. [20], which uses hierarchical Gaussian mixtures; here a nonparametric approach avoids parametric misfit and explicitly enforces class-prior consistency.
- Kernel two-sample testing: Distinctiveness uses kernel mean embeddings/MMD [21]; the paper’s use in a joint assignment optimization is novel in this context.
- Generalized clustering with discriminability [27] is related in spirit but differs in supervision and objective.

Strengths
- Clear and compelling problem formulation: discovering latent domains is practically important and orthogonal to designing better adaptation algorithms.
- Nonparametric distinctiveness criterion leveraging MMD is well motivated and avoids restrictive distributional assumptions.
- The label prior constraint is a clever way to prevent clusters degenerating into per-class groupings.
- Practical criterion for selecting K via learnability (DWCV); the integration of domain quality with discriminative performance is reasonable and intuitive.
- Conditional reshaping of test sets is a nice extension and seems particularly relevant to cross-view action recognition.
- Qualitative analysis of discovered domains provides interpretability (pose/background/appearance).

Weaknesses and questions
- Optimization details and scalability:
  - The objective is a maximization of a convex quadratic over linear constraints, which is NP-hard; the paper relies on “off-the-shelf solvers” without specifying the algorithm, initialization, or guarantees. With O(M^2) kernels and O(MK) variables, how does this scale beyond the small datasets used? Any use of random features/Nyström approximations, block-coordinate ascent, or CCCP?
  - Relaxation/rounding: Assigning by argmax βmk after solving a relaxed problem can violate the LPC; is there a projection step to enforce label priors post-rounding?
- Assumption encoded by LPC:
  - Enforcing per-domain class priors to match the global prior (δ = 1%) is strong and may be violated in real data (e.g., class imbalance varies across viewpoints or acquisition conditions). How robust is the method to misspecified LPC? Ablations on δ and on removing LPC would be valuable.
  - The text claims “every domain should have at least one instance per category,” but the provided constraints ensure Mk ≥ C, not per-class coverage without LPC; please clarify.
- Distinctiveness vs. usefulness:
  - Maximizing pairwise MMD can reward creating many small “distinct” domains. While DWCV aims to prevent overfragmentation, there is no theoretical link that large inter-domain MMD promotes better adaptation. Could adding explicit size/entropy regularization or a penalty on K improve stability?
- Experimental evaluation:
  - Numerical details are sparse: the exact classifiers for DWCV, solver settings, and runtime are not reported. The tables are referenced but not shown; effect sizes and statistical significance are unclear.
  - Comparison to [20] is under-specified (hyperparameters, number of components, covariance tying, initialization). Please ensure a fair comparison and include additional baselines (e.g., spectral clustering in kernel space with label-regularization; K-means on domain-invariant features).
  - The “MATCHING” multi-source strategy is mentioned in prose but not described alongside UNION/ENSEMBLE; how is matching implemented without target labels (e.g., select source minimizing MMD to target)?
  - Oracle metrics (max over source domains using annotated target domains) are informative upper bounds but not directly actionable; more emphasis on label-free model selection on target (e.g., Reverse Validation, Source-guided Discrepancy) would strengthen the practical story.
- Technical clarifications:
  - The distance d(k,k′) is the squared RKHS mean difference; is the unbiased MMD^2 estimator used? Kernel bandwidth choice (“twice the median”) is heuristic; sensitivity analysis would help.
  - Objective simplification: summing over all pairs may be expressible in closed form via within/between terms; could this enable more efficient optimization?
  - The method operates on Pk(x), not Pk(x|y) or y|x; perhaps consider class-conditional MMD to relax LPC while still discouraging class-driven clustering.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide full algorithmic details (solver, initialization, complexity) and report runtimes and scaling experiments; consider approximations (random features/Nyström, mini-batch updates).
- Add ablations: with/without LPC; varying δ; different K selection strategies; sensitivity to kernel/bandwidth.
- Strengthen baselines: (i) spectral clustering in RKHS with label prior regularization; (ii) domain discovery on learned representations (e.g., PCA, TCA, CORAL, DANN-like features); (iii) more thorough comparison to [20].
- Clarify MATCHING and conditional reshaping optimization; release pseudocode or supplementary derivations.
- Consider a more flexible regularizer than strict LPC, e.g., soft penalties on divergence of per-domain class proportions from the global prior.
- Report target-selection without labels using discrepancy measures, and compare to oracle to quantify the gap.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Some notational issues (Mk′ in Eq. 1; constraints implying per-class coverage) and missing methodological details hinder reproducibility. Explicitly state the classifier used for DWCV, the CV protocol, and optimization settings.

Originality
Moderately high. Using MMD-based nonparametric distinctiveness with label-prior regularization for latent domain discovery and coupling it with a learnability-based K selection is a novel synthesis distinct from prior parametric models.

Significance
Discovering latent domains is impactful for visual recognition under dataset bias and multi-source adaptation. If the method scales and proves robust beyond the examined datasets, it could inform both adaptation pipelines and dataset design.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem with a principled nonparametric approach.
- Empirical improvements and interpretable discovered domains.
- Test-time conditional reshaping is a useful contribution.

Cons for acceptance
- Optimization/scalability and reproducibility details are insufficient.
- Strong LPC assumption may limit applicability; robustness not demonstrated.
- Evaluation lacks detailed baselines, significance analyses, and complete reporting.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The problem is important and the ideas are promising, but the paper would benefit from clearer optimization details, stronger baselines, and robustness analyses. I would lean accept if the authors can address these concerns in a revision. Confidence: high.
