

# Peer Review: "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental yet overlooked issue in visual domain adaptation: the misconception that entire datasets constitute single domains. The authors rightly point out that visual datasets are typically collected without explicit domain boundaries, and treating them as single domains often leads to suboptimal adaptation. They propose a novel approach to automatically reshape datasets into latent domains that satisfy two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing inter-domain distribution differences) and maximum learnability (ensuring domains are suitable for building strong discriminative classifiers). The authors develop nonparametric methods to identify these domains using kernel-based distribution discrepancy measures and introduce a domain-wise cross-validation procedure to determine the optimal number of domains. The approach is evaluated on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human action recognition (IXMAS) tasks, showing significant improvements over using original datasets or domains discovered by a previous method (Hoffman et al.).

The work relates to but significantly extends prior domain adaptation research, which typically assumes domains are already defined (e.g., between datasets), rather than discovering them from raw data. It also builds on recent work on domain adaptation (e.g., Saenko et al., Gong et al.) while addressing a conceptual gap in the field.

## Strengths

**Quality (High):** The paper is technically sound with well-justified claims. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their two axiomatic properties, and the nonparametric approach avoids the restrictive assumptions of parametric clustering methods. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed, comparing against both original datasets and a relevant baseline (Hoffman et al.). The DWCV procedure for determining the number of domains is particularly well-motivated and practical. The results consistently show improvements over baselines across multiple datasets and tasks.

**Originality (High):** This is a novel contribution that identifies and addresses a critical conceptual gap in visual domain adaptation research. While domain adaptation has been extensively studied, the problem of discovering latent domains within datasets has not been properly addressed. The authors' approach to reshaping datasets using maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability is original and represents a significant conceptual advance beyond treating datasets as single domains.

**Significance (High):** The paper addresses a fundamental issue that impacts the validity of much existing domain adaptation research. By providing a principled method for reshaping datasets into meaningful domains, the work has the potential to improve future domain adaptation research and practice. The demonstrated improvements in adaptation accuracy are substantial and consistent across multiple tasks. The work also has implications for future dataset construction by revealing the main factors that define domains in visual data.

**Clarity (High):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear, the motivation is compelling, and the methodology is explained with sufficient mathematical detail. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, and the results are presented in a logical manner. The figures (particularly Figure 1 showing exemplar images from identified domains) effectively illustrate the authors' key insights.

## Weaknesses

**Minor Theoretical Gap:** While the paper introduces two axiomatic properties, it would strengthen the theoretical foundation by more rigorously justifying why these properties lead to better domain adaptation performance. A brief discussion of how these properties relate to known theoretical results in domain adaptation (e.g., bounds on domain shift) would be valuable.

**Practical Limitations:** The paper assumes that the test domain(s) can be reshaped conditionally on the identified training domains (Section 4.3). While this is a reasonable approach, it's worth noting that in some real-world scenarios, the test domains might be completely unknown, making conditional reshaping challenging. The paper could better address this limitation.

**Comparison with Recent Work:** While the paper compares with Hoffman et al. (2013), it doesn't engage with more recent domain adaptation methods that also consider multiple domains (e.g., multi-source adaptation techniques). A brief discussion of how their approach complements or differs from these more recent methods would be beneficial.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Addresses a fundamental conceptual gap in visual domain adaptation research
- Proposes a novel and well-motivated approach with strong empirical validation
- Theoretical foundation is sound and well-justified
- Results consistently show significant improvements over baselines
- Clear, well-organized writing with sufficient detail for reproducibility
- Has clear potential to influence future domain adaptation research

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The theoretical justification for the two axiomatic properties could be strengthened
- The paper doesn't fully address potential limitations in scenarios where test domains are completely unknown
- Could better engage with more recent multi-domain adaptation work

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation by addressing a critical but overlooked problem: the assumption that datasets constitute single domains. The authors' approach to automatically reshaping datasets into latent domains using maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field.

The minor theoretical gaps and practical limitations noted are not substantial enough to detract from the paper's overall contribution. The work has clear significance for both researchers and practitioners in domain adaptation, and the results demonstrate a demonstrable improvement over existing methods.

I strongly recommend acceptance for NIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement
1. Briefly elaborate on why the two axiomatic properties lead to better domain adaptation performance, potentially connecting to known theoretical results.
2. Discuss how the approach relates to or differs from more recent multi-source adaptation techniques.
3. More thoroughly address the limitation where test domains might be completely unknown, making conditional reshaping impossible.
4. Consider adding a brief discussion of computational complexity and scalability to larger datasets.

The paper is already excellent, and these suggestions are minor refinements to an already strong contribution to the field.
