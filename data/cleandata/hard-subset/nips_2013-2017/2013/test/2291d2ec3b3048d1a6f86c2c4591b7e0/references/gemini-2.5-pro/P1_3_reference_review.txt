### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a fundamental, yet often overlooked, issue in visual domain adaptation research: the common practice of equating entire datasets with single domains. The authors argue that datasets are often heterogeneous collections of multiple latent, underlying domains (e.g., varying in pose, illumination, background). Treating such a mixed dataset as a single source domain can harm the performance of adaptation algorithms.

To solve this, the paper proposes a novel, unsupervised method to "reshape" one or more source datasets into a set of coherent, latent domains *before* applying a domain adaptation algorithm. The method is guided by two core principles:
1.  **Maximum Distinctiveness:** The discovered domains should be as statistically different from one another as possible. This is formalized by maximizing the sum of pairwise distances between domain distributions, measured non-parametrically using a kernel-based two-sample test statistic (based on the Maximum Mean Discrepancy, MMD). To prevent the trivial solution of clustering by class, the authors introduce a crucial Label Prior Constraint (LPC), which enforces that the class distribution within each discovered domain mirrors the overall class distribution of the source data.
2.  **Maximum Learnability:** The discovered domains must contain enough data to train a useful classifier. This principle is used to automatically determine the optimal number of latent domains, K. The authors propose a procedure called Domain-Wise Cross-Validation (DWCV), where they find the K that maximizes the weighted average cross-validation accuracy of classifiers trained on each of the K resulting domains.

The authors formulate the domain discovery as a quadratic programming problem, which is relaxed for optimization. They demonstrate their approach on object and action recognition tasks, showing that adapting from their discovered domains yields superior performance compared to adapting from the original, monolithic datasets. They also introduce a "conditional reshaping" method to partition the target data to match the discovered source domains.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The core idea is well-motivated, and the proposed principles of distinctiveness and learnability provide a solid conceptual foundation.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The use of a non-parametric kernel method to measure domain discrepancy is a major strength, as it avoids making restrictive parametric assumptions about the data distributions (a key advantage over prior work like Hoffman et al. [20]).
    *   The Label Prior Constraint (LPC) is a clever and essential component that successfully steers the optimization away from simply clustering by category, which is a plausible and problematic failure mode.
    *   The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The authors wisely evaluate their method from multiple angles: an "oracle" evaluation (GOURS) to show the potential of the discovered domains, practical evaluation using several multi-source adaptation techniques, and an extension to reshaping the test set. The results consistently support the paper's claims.
*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The optimization section (2.1) could be clearer. The transition from the integer program to the relaxed formulation in Eq. (4) involves several steps that are not fully detailed. For example, the definition of `K` in Eq. (4) as the M x M kernel matrix is not explicitly stated. The constraints in Eq. (5) are also somewhat opaque; a more detailed derivation or intuition for the bounds (1/M, 1/C) would improve reproducibility.
    *   The paper states that "existing solvers are adequate" but does not discuss the computational complexity or scalability of the proposed optimization. Given that it involves an M x M kernel matrix, this approach may be computationally expensive for large datasets, which is a relevant concern for NIPS.
    *   The procedure for selecting K (DWCV) is reasonable but feels somewhat heuristic. Its performance may depend on the choice of the underlying classifier used for cross-validation, a point that is not discussed.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction provides a compelling motivation for the problem. The core ideas are explained intuitively before being formalized.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The problem statement is articulated with exceptional clarity, making the paper accessible and its motivation immediately apparent.
    *   The qualitative analysis in Section 4.4, with visualizations in Figure 1, provides excellent intuition about what the discovered domains represent, grounding the abstract method in concrete visual factors like pose, background, and color.
*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   As mentioned under Quality, the optimization section is the least clear part of the paper and would benefit from more detailed explanation.
    *   The "conditional reshaping" of the test set is an important part of the experimental results (Table 3), but its details are relegated to supplementary material. A brief summary of the formulation in the main paper would have been helpful.

**Originality:** The paper makes a highly original contribution. While the tools used (kernel-based distribution measures) are established, their application to the problem of pre-emptively discovering latent domains within datasets for the purpose of improving domain adaptation is novel.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The paper reframes the domain adaptation problem by introducing a new, critical pre-processing step. This challenges the standard "dataset-as-domain" paradigm and could influence how future DA experiments are designed.
    *   It clearly distinguishes itself from the most related work [20] by proposing a non-parametric alternative. It also correctly differentiates its goal from standard multi-source DA (where domains are given) and landmark selection [26] (which is target-specific).

**Significance:** The work is highly significant. It addresses a real and pervasive issue in applied machine learning, where data sources are rarely as clean or homogeneous as assumed.
*   **Strengths:**
    *   The problem of "dataset bias" is widely acknowledged, but this paper offers a constructive and automated method to deal with the internal heterogeneity of datasets, which is a significant step forward.
    *   The proposed method is general and could be applied to any domain adaptation scenario, not just visual recognition. Researchers in other fields could benefit from this idea of reshaping their data before attempting adaptation.
    *   The findings could inform future dataset collection efforts, suggesting that capturing and annotating distinct, internally consistent "domains" of data is more valuable than simply amassing a large, heterogeneous collection of examples.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Novel and Important Problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental and widely ignored problem in domain adaptation research, challenging a flawed but common experimental practice.
2.  **Sound and Well-Motivated Method:** The proposed approach is built on two intuitive principles (distinctiveness and learnability) and implemented with appropriate non-parametric tools, making it both principled and flexible.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough, well-designed, and provide convincing evidence that reshaping datasets with the proposed method leads to significant improvements in adaptation performance.
4.  **High Potential for Impact:** The core idea is simple enough to be widely adopted and could change how researchers approach domain adaptation tasks and evaluate their methods.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  **Clarity on Optimization and Scalability:** The description of the optimization procedure lacks some detail, and the computational cost and scalability of the method are not addressed, which could be a limitation for very large datasets.
2.  **Heuristic for Model Selection:** The method for choosing the number of domains K (DWCV) is a reasonable heuristic but lacks theoretical grounding and its sensitivity to hyperparameters (like the choice of classifier) is not explored.

**Conclusion:** Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, well-motivated, and significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation. It identifies a critical flaw in current practice and provides an elegant and effective solution. The paper is well-written, and its claims are strongly supported by empirical results. I enthusiastically recommend this paper for acceptance.
