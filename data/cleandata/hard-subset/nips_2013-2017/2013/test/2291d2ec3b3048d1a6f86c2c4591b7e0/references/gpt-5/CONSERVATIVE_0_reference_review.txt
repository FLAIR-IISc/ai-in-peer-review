Summary
The paper tackles the problem that commonly used “domains” in visual domain adaptation are often entire datasets that mix multiple, heterogeneous factors (pose, illumination, background, etc.). The authors propose to automatically discover latent domains within datasets by enforcing two axioms: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. Distinctiveness is operationalized by maximizing pairwise discrepancies between marginal feature distributions across domains using a kernel two-sample metric (essentially MMD in RKHS). A label prior constraint (LPC) is added to avoid trivial per-class clustering. The integer program is relaxed via variables βmk=zmk/Mk with linear constraints; the resulting problem is a maximization of a convex quadratic function over a simplex with linear constraints, solved by off-the-shelf solvers. To determine the number of domains K, a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) criterion is used: train classifiers within each discovered domain and select K that maximizes a weighted CV accuracy A(K). The authors also propose “conditional reshaping” of unlabeled test sets to align them to the discovered training domains by minimizing kernel discrepancies. Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon/DSLR/Webcam) and IXMAS multi-view action recognition suggest improved adaptation performance over treating datasets as domains and over a latent-domain method using Gaussian mixtures [Hoffman et al., ECCV’12], with geodesic flow kernel (GFK) as the adaptation backbone.

Strengths
- Addresses a real, under-discussed issue: datasets are not domains; discovering latent domains could benefit adaptation.
- Nonparametric discrepancy (MMD) avoids strong parametric assumptions of prior latent-domain work [20].
- Clear articulation of two desirable properties (distinctiveness and learnability) and a practical K-selection heuristic (DWCV).
- Includes both training-side reshaping and test-side conditional reshaping; the latter is a natural and useful extension.
- Empirical results, as described, indicate gains over naive dataset-as-domain baselines and over [20].

Weaknesses and concerns
- Objective misalignment: The distinctiveness criterion operates solely on P(x). Domain adaptation performance depends on shifts in P(y|x) and label-conditional structure. Maximizing marginal MMD can separate data by factors irrelevant (or even harmful) for classification. The LPC attempts to prevent per-class clustering but enforces an arguably unrealistic assumption that label priors are constant across latent domains.
- LPC assumptions and parameters: Requiring each domain to reflect the global label prior (δ=1%) and have at least one sample per class (Mk≥C) is strong and not justified empirically. Many real latent factors correlate with class prevalence (e.g., actions under different views/contexts). Sensitivity to δ is not analyzed.
- Optimization clarity and guarantees: The relaxation βmk=zmk/Mk is unusual as Mk depends on z. The derivation of constraints like 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C (implying C ≤ Mk ≤ M) is only heuristically motivated. The final problem is still NP-hard; “existing solvers” and satisfactory solutions are claimed without algorithmic detail, initialization, convergence behavior, or sensitivity to kernel bandwidth. No complexity or scalability analysis is provided.
- Choice of K via DWCV: The K-selection relies on supervised CV within the source, not on adaptation performance. There is no statistical justification that maximizing A(K) relates to target performance beyond a qualitative plot. The bound Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C} is ad hoc.
- Evaluation breadth and rigor: Experiments are centered on relatively small, dated benchmarks and a single adaptation method (GFK). It is unclear whether improvements hold across diverse adaptation algorithms (e.g., TCA, CORAL, SA, OCT, more recent deep domain adaptation) or modern features. No ablation of key design choices (LPC on/off, δ values, bandwidth selection, kernel choice) is reported. Oracle metrics requiring target labels (GORIG/GOURS) are informative but optimistic; “MATCHING” that approaches oracle needs clearer description to rule out target leakage.
- Competing baselines: The comparison to [20] is mentioned but details (model settings, number of mixtures, initialization, fairness) are absent here; KMeans/random partitions are dismissed without reporting numbers. Without these, it is hard to assess robustness of the claimed improvements.
- Test-set reshaping: The conditional reshaping formulation is pushed to the supplement; the main text doesn’t provide the precise optimization objective, constraints, or guarantees. Practical deployment aspects (stability, runtime, batch effects) are not discussed.

Quality
Technically, the use of MMD is standard and sound for two-sample testing. However, the formulation mixes several strong, unvalidated constraints (LPC, Mk≥C) and a nonconvex objective with limited optimization detail. The connection between the proposed criteria and adaptation risk (e.g., via discrepancy distance or HΔH bounds) is not established. Empirical evidence suggests potential, but lacks breadth, ablations, and sensitivity analyses expected for a new methodology.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and motivates the problem well. However, key mathematical steps (β-relaxation, constraint derivations), optimization procedures, and the conditional test reshaping formulation need clearer exposition. Important experimental details (numbers in Tables 1–3, baselines’ settings) and sensitivity analyses are missing from the provided text.

Originality
The problem framing is interesting, and combining MMD-based distinctiveness with a K-selection via learnability is a novel angle. Related work on kernel-embedding-based clustering and prior latent-domain discovery are cited. That said, the technical components (MMD, CV) are standard, and the contributions are more in problem setup and heuristic integration than in new theory.

Significance
If robust, tools to reshape datasets into latent domains could be useful to the community and to practitioners. Given the methodological caveats and limited evaluation, the current submission does not yet convincingly demonstrate general, reliable gains or clear guidance for real-world use.

Questions and suggestions
- Justify LPC empirically: do label priors truly remain constant across discovered domains in your data? Provide ablations varying δ, and a variant with class-conditional MMD (per-class MMD averaged across classes) rather than LPC.
- Provide algorithmic details: initialization strategies, solver type, convergence behavior, and runtime/memory for typical M and K.
- Sensitivity analyses: kernel bandwidth, kernel choice, K-selection stability across folds/seeds.
- Tie to adaptation theory: relate your objective to discrepancy measures that bound target risk; consider using label-conditional/discriminative distances.
- Expand baselines: multiple adaptation backbones (e.g., TCA/CORAL/SA), stronger features, and more comprehensive comparisons to [20] with matched settings.
- Clarify “MATCHING”: how is the match selected without target labels? Provide a detailed algorithm and show it does not implicitly use target supervision.
- Conditional test reshaping: include the explicit objective/constraints in the main paper and report empirical stability and overhead.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and underexplored issue in domain adaptation practice.
- Nonparametric approach avoids restrictive parametric assumptions of prior latent-domain models.
- Promising empirical results and qualitative analyses suggesting interpretable latent factors.

Cons for acceptance
- Objective/constraints are heuristic and may not align with adaptation risk; strong assumptions (LPC).
- Limited theoretical grounding and optimization details; potential for brittleness.
- Evaluation lacks breadth, ablations, and modern baselines; reliance on oracle metrics.
- Unclear scalability and reproducibility.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is compelling, but the current paper relies on strong assumptions and heuristic choices without sufficient theoretical or empirical rigor. Strengthening the methodology (e.g., class-conditional criteria), clarifying optimization, and expanding the evaluation would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
