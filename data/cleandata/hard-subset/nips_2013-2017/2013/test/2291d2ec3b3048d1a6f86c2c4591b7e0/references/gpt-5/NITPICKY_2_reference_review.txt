Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses latent domain discovery in visual datasets. The premise is that common practice equates datasets with domains, but a dataset may be an agglomeration of multiple, overlapping “domains” driven by nuisance factors (pose, illumination, background, etc.). The authors propose to reshape datasets into K latent domains guided by two axioms: maximum distinctiveness (domains are distributionally far apart) and maximum learnability (each domain supports training accurate classifiers). Distinctiveness is operationalized via a kernel two-sample discrepancy (squared RKHS mean difference, i.e., MMD^2); learnability selects K via a domain-wise cross-validation criterion that sums per-domain accuracies weighted by domain sizes. They also propose “conditional reshaping” of unlabeled test data to match discovered training domains. Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and IXMAS action recognition suggest gains over using original datasets as domains and over a parametric latent domain model [Hoffman et al. ECCV’12].

This line of work is closely related to: (i) two-sample testing and kernel mean embeddings for measuring distributional differences [Gretton et al.], (ii) domain adaptation with multiple sources [Mansour et al.; Duan et al.; Sun et al.], (iii) prior work by the authors on landmarks [Gong et al. ICML’13], and (iv) generalized clustering with kernel embeddings [Jegelka et al.]. The paper’s nonparametric approach contrasts with [Hoffman et al.], which uses Gaussian mixtures.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: datasets are not monolithic; splitting into latent domains can help adaptation.
- Nonparametric distinctiveness via MMD^2 is a sensible, flexible measure that avoids parametric mis-specification.
- The idea of choosing K by “maximum learnability” is intuitive and practically motivated.
- Consideration of test-time heterogeneity (conditional reshaping) is important and often overlooked.
- Empirical results (albeit partially described) indicate improvements over using original datasets and over [20], plus qualitative analyses of discovered factors.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Methodological/technical
1) Heavy supervision baked into “unsupervised” discovery. The label prior constraint (LPC) requires class proportions within each latent domain to match global priors (within δ). This uses labels and forces each domain to exhibit nearly identical label histograms. In realistic settings, class prior shift across domains is common; enforcing equal priors can prevent discovering semantically meaningful domains (e.g., certain views containing rare classes). Please justify this assumption empirically, and discuss failure modes when priors do differ.

2) Misinterpretation of constraints. The text claims the first constraint encodes “every domain should have at least one instance per category, namely, Mk ≥ C.” Mk ≥ C does not imply at least one instance per class. If that is intended, you need explicit per-class minimum constraints.

3) Notation conflations and ambiguities:
- k denotes both a domain index and the kernel function; K denotes both the number of domains and the M×M kernel matrix. This is confusing (e.g., “TDD(K)” vs “K is the kernel matrix”). Please disambiguate (e.g., use Φ or G for kernel Gram matrix).
- In Eq. (1), using k(·, xm) for the feature map is non-standard; typically φ(xm) ∈ H. Also, M′k appears without definition.
- d(k, k′) is described as “the difference between the means” but Eq. (1) is the squared RKHS norm; please be precise (this is MMD^2).

4) Objective and relaxation correctness. Defining βmk = zmk/Mk and relaxing to β on the simplex with Σm βmk = 1 is reasonable. However, replacing Σk zmk = 1 with instance-wise bounds 1/M ≤ Σk βmk ≤ 1/C is odd: after relaxation, Σk βmk is no longer equal to 1/Mk, so these bounds do not correspond to domain sizes in any exact sense, yet they still drive the solution. The rationale that this encodes C ≤ Mk ≤ M is only valid in the integer case. Please justify this surrogate and its effect after relaxation. Also, the final hard assignment via arg maxk βmk is ad hoc; what guarantees do we have that constraints remain satisfied after rounding?

5) Convex maximization and optimization details. You maximize a convex quadratic over a polytope, which is generally NP-hard; the paper acknowledges this but then states “solvers are adequate” without specifying which solver, initialization, number of restarts, runtime, or scalability (storing K requires O(M^2) memory). Given that M can be large in vision, this is a serious omission. At minimum: report complexity, practical M limits, runtime, and sensitivity to initialization.

6) Alternative and simpler forms. Sum of pairwise squared distances between RKHS means has the identity Σi≠j ||μi−μj||^2 = 2K Σi ||μi−μ̄||^2. This could reduce computation and clarify the geometry. Please discuss.

7) Choice of kernel bandwidth. You fix σ to twice the median pairwise distances. The standard median heuristic uses the median. No tuning or sensitivity analysis is reported, yet σ can materially affect MMD. Please include ablations.

8) Choosing K by learnability. DWCV uses within-domain cross-validation accuracy (with labels) to select K. It is unclear what classifier is used, what hyperparameters, and whether this correlates with target-domain performance. There is no theoretical justification that A(K) proxies adaptation performance, and no variance/error bars or sensitivity to N. The bound Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C} is asserted; the C bound seems arbitrary—why should K ≤ C?

9) Conditional reshaping of test data. You claim a convex QP; however, assigning test instances to K matched domains is inherently discrete. If you relax to soft assignments, you again face rounding issues and potential degeneracy (e.g., empty domains). Constraints to avoid trivial solutions (e.g., minimum cluster sizes) are not detailed.

10) Risk of per-class clustering. The objective ignores y and uses only P(x). The LPC tries to prevent per-class clustering by enforcing identical priors; this is brittle. Consider a conditional version of MMD that aligns P(x|y) across domains or adds a penalty on between-domain divergence within classes.

Empirical/clarity
- The adaptation schemes UNION/ENSEMBLE/MATCHING are not fully defined. MATCHING “uses no target labels”—presumably selects the source with min MMD to target; please specify.
- Experimental tables/figures are referenced but not shown here; results should include means, standard deviations across splits, and clear protocols. Reproducibility is hindered: classifier type for DWCV, solver details, and code availability are missing.
- IXMAS preprocessing: “keep the first five actions performed by [six actors]” and “20 sequences randomly selected per actor per action” is ambiguous; please clarify exact counts and splits.
- Minor typos/formatting: repeated words (“the the difference”), inconsistent capitalization (RKHS H, kernel matrix K), incomplete citations (years/pages missing throughout), inconsistent reference formatting, and undefined symbols (M′k in Eq. (1)). TDD(K) double-counts pairs; if intentional, state so.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant and under-explored problem (latent domain discovery) with a simple, general nonparametric criterion.
- Useful practical angle (test-time reshaping), qualitative insights into discovered factors.
- Potentially broadly applicable beyond vision.

Cons
- Methodologically brittle due to LPC and reliance on labels; questionable relaxation and rounding; lack of optimization/scalability details.
- Insufficient specification of key components (classifier for DWCV, MATCHING, solver), limited ablations/sensitivity.
- Clarity and notation issues; many citation/formatting inconsistencies.

Questions for the authors
1) How does performance change without LPC, or with a weaker regularizer (e.g., entropy, minimum cluster size), or with conditional MMD? Provide ablations.
2) Specify the classifier used in DWCV and its hyperparameters. Does A(K) correlate with target-domain accuracy across seeds?
3) Provide solver details, runtime/memory profile vs M, and initialization strategy. How many restarts?
4) Detail MATCHING and conditional test reshaping optimization and rounding.
5) Sensitivity to kernel bandwidth and δ.

Recommendation
While the high-level idea is appealing and results are promising, the paper has significant methodological and clarity issues: heavy reliance on label priors, unclear and potentially flawed relaxation, missing optimization/scalability details, and underspecified experimental procedures. I recommend rejection in its current form. Strengthening the technical foundation, providing complete algorithmic details and ablations, and tightening the writing/notation would make this a much stronger submission.
