### Paper Summary

This paper challenges the common practice in domain adaptation research of treating entire datasets as single, monolithic domains. The authors argue that datasets are often heterogeneous collections of multiple latent domains, and naively blending them can impair adaptation performance. The core contribution is a novel, unsupervised method to "reshape" datasets by automatically discovering these latent domains. The approach is founded on two intuitive principles: 1) **Maximum Distinctiveness**, where the discovered domains should have maximally different distributions from one another, and 2) **Maximum Learnability**, where each discovered domain must be coherent enough to support the training of a strong classifier.

To achieve maximum distinctiveness, the authors propose maximizing the sum of pairwise distances between domain distributions, measured non-parametrically in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). A crucial "Label Prior Constraint" is introduced to prevent the method from simply clustering by class label, forcing it instead to find domain shifts orthogonal to the classification task. To determine the number of domains, K, the principle of maximum learnability is operationalized via a "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) procedure, selecting the K that yields the highest internal classification accuracy. The authors demonstrate on object and action recognition tasks that adapting from these discovered domains significantly outperforms adapting from the original, un-reshaped datasets.

### Strengths

1.  **High Conceptual Novelty and Significance:** This paper's primary strength lies in its bold and insightful reframing of a fundamental problem. The idea that a source "domain" (i.e., a dataset) is not a single entity but a mixture of latent sub-domains is a powerful one that has been largely overlooked. By proposing a method to automatically discover this structure, the paper moves beyond developing yet another adaptation algorithm and instead offers a new, potentially foundational, pre-processing step for the entire field. This work could inspire a new line of inquiry into the composition of datasets and how that composition affects generalization.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Formulation:** The proposed method is grounded in two clear and well-motivated principles. The use of a non-parametric kernel method (MMD-like) to measure domain distinctiveness is elegant and avoids making restrictive assumptions about the underlying data distributions, a key advantage over prior work like Hoffman et al. [20].

3.  **Insightful Use of Label Information:** The inclusion of the Label Prior Constraint (LPC) is a critical and clever piece of the formulation. Without it, any method aiming to maximize distribution difference would likely just rediscover the object categories themselves. By enforcing a consistent class distribution across the discovered domains, the authors steer the optimization towards finding meaningful, task-independent variations like pose, background, and illumination, which is precisely the goal. This is a subtle but vital contribution.

4.  **Convincing Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the value of the proposed idea. The "oracle" evaluation (GOURS) provides a clear upper bound and shows that the discovered domains are inherently "better" for adaptation than the original datasets. More importantly, the practical utility is shown through multi-source adaptation techniques, where using the discovered domains as input leads to state-of-the-art performance. The extension to reshaping the test set is a compelling addition that further highlights the flexibility and power of the core concept.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Optimization Challenges:** The paper is somewhat brief on the practical aspects of the optimization. The relaxed problem involves maximizing a convex quadratic function subject to linear constraints, which is generally NP-hard. While the authors state that existing solvers provide satisfactory solutions, the work would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the specific solver used, its convergence properties, and its sensitivity to initialization. For a method intended for practical use, these details are important.

2.  **Scalability:** The method relies on constructing and operating on an M x M kernel matrix, where M is the number of data instances. This approach will face significant computational and memory challenges for the large-scale datasets that are now common in the field (e.g., ImageNet). Acknowledging this limitation and suggesting potential avenues for scaling (e.g., using random Fourier features or other kernel approximations) would be beneficial.

3.  **The Label Prior Constraint Assumption:** While the LPC is a key strength, it is also a strong assumption. The paper's intuition that class priors are often independent of domain-specific factors is reasonable, but there may be important real-world scenarios where this does not hold (e.g., certain car models appearing only in street scenes, not showrooms). A discussion of these limitations would provide a more complete picture.

### Recommendation: Accept

I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance. The weaknesses I have identified are relatively minor—issues of methodological detail and scalability that can be addressed in future work—and are far outweighed by the paper's strengths. This is not an incremental paper that offers a small improvement on an existing algorithm. Instead, it presents a bold, novel, and highly significant conceptual contribution that challenges a foundational assumption in domain adaptation. It opens up a new way of thinking about the structure of data and its impact on model generalization. This is precisely the type of thought-provoking, direction-setting research that NIPS should champion. The ideas presented here are very likely to be used and built upon by other researchers.
