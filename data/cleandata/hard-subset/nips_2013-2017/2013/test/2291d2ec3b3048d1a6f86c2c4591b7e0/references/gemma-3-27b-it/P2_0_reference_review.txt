## Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a novel and insightful approach to domain adaptation by focusing on the inherent structure *within* datasets, rather than treating datasets as monolithic domains. The idea of automatically discovering latent domains based on distinctiveness and learnability is compelling and well-motivated. The proposed method is technically sound, supported by experiments on standard benchmarks, and addresses a crucial, often overlooked, aspect of domain adaptation. I believe this paper represents a significant contribution and recommend acceptance.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The authors correctly identify a common pitfall in visual domain adaptation: equating datasets with domains. They argue this is problematic as real-world datasets often contain multiple underlying distributions (domains) arising from factors like viewpoint, illumination, etc.  They propose a method to *reshape* datasets into these latent domains, guided by principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.  The paper effectively connects this work to existing domain adaptation literature (citations 1-10) while highlighting its distinct focus on domain *discovery* rather than adaptation *between* pre-defined domains. The related work section (section 3) is comprehensive, accurately positioning their work alongside Hoffman et al. (20) and generalized clustering approaches (Jegelka et al., 27).

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Insight:** The core idea of automatically discovering domains *within* datasets is a significant departure from the standard paradigm and offers a fresh perspective on domain adaptation.  The authors convincingly argue that this is a crucial step often neglected in practice.
* **Theoretical Grounding:**  The use of kernel methods for measuring domain discrepancy (based on [21]) and the formulation of the optimization problem are well-justified and appear technically sound.  The inclusion of the label prior constraint (LPC) is a clever addition, encouraging domains to maintain reasonable class distributions.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments on object recognition and human activity recognition demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The results consistently outperform baselines, and the ablation studies (reshaping only training, reshaping both training & test) provide valuable insights. Table 2, in particular, is compelling.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly stated, the method is explained in detail, and the experiments are presented in a structured manner.
* **Analysis & Discussion:** The authors provide insightful analysis of the identified domains, revealing the underlying factors driving the domain discovery process (e.g., pose, illumination).  The discussion of the optimal number of domains (K) and the DWCV procedure is clear and well-reasoned.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention relaxation to continuous optimization, the computational cost of the proposed method could be a limitation, especially for very large datasets.  A discussion of scalability and potential approximations would be beneficial.
* **Kernel Choice:** The choice of the Gaussian kernel and the bandwidth parameter is somewhat arbitrary. While the authors follow standard practices, exploring the sensitivity of the results to different kernel choices could strengthen the analysis.
* **Limited Comparison to SOTA:**  While the comparison to Hoffman et al. (20) is fair, a more direct comparison to state-of-the-art domain adaptation techniques (e.g., DANN, CDAN) applied *after* the domain reshaping step would provide a stronger benchmark. Showing the gains from reshaping *in addition* to existing methods would be very impactful.



**4. Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a well-defined problem, a principled approach, and strong experimental validation. The theoretical analysis appears solid, and the authors are careful to discuss the limitations of their work.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The notation is consistent, and the explanations are easy to follow.
* **Originality:** The approach of automatically discovering latent domains within datasets is novel and represents a significant departure from existing methods.
* **Significance:** The results are important and have the potential to impact the field of domain adaptation. The ability to automatically discover meaningful domains could lead to more robust and effective adaptation strategies.  The insights into dataset structure could also inform future data collection efforts.



**5. Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro Acceptance:**

* Novel and insightful approach to domain adaptation.
* Strong theoretical grounding and experimental validation.
* Clear and well-written paper.
* Addresses a crucial, often overlooked, aspect of domain adaptation.

**Con Acceptance:**

* Computational complexity could be a limitation.
* Limited comparison to state-of-the-art domain adaptation methods.




**Final Recommendation:**  **Accept**.  

Despite minor weaknesses, the novelty, technical soundness, and experimental validation of this paper make it a strong contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The authors have identified a significant problem and proposed a compelling solution that has the potential to advance the state of the art.  I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
