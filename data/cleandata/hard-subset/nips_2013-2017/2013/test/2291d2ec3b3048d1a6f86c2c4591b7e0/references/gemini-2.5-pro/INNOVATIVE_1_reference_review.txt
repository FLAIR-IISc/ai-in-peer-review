### Paper Summary

This paper challenges a fundamental practice in visual domain adaptation research: the conflation of datasets with domains. The authors argue that standard datasets are often heterogeneous collections of multiple latent, unobserved domains (e.g., defined by viewpoint, illumination, background), and treating such a dataset as a single source domain is suboptimal. The core contribution is a novel framework to automatically "reshape" a given dataset by discovering these latent domains.

The proposed method is based on two principles: 1) **Maximum Distinctiveness**, where the discovered domains should have maximally different data distributions, and 2) **Maximum Learnability**, where each discovered domain must contain sufficient data to train a robust classifier. To achieve distinctiveness, the authors propose maximizing the "Total Domain Distinctiveness" (TDD), a sum of pairwise distances between domain distributions measured non-parametrically in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). A key "label prior constraint" (LPC) is introduced to prevent the method from simply clustering by object category. To determine the number of domains, K, the authors propose a "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) procedure, selecting the K that maximizes the average classification accuracy within the discovered domains. The authors validate their approach on object and action recognition tasks, showing that adapting from their automatically discovered domains significantly outperforms adapting from the original, monolithic datasets.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** This paper's primary strength lies in its bold and important critique of a standard assumption in the domain adaptation community. The idea that we should not take domains as given, but rather discover them as a latent variable, is a significant conceptual shift. It reframes the problem from "how to adapt from A to B" to "what are the true domains within A, and how can we leverage them to adapt to B?". This kind of work, which questions the problem setup itself, is rare and has the potential to be highly influential, opening up a new direction of research.

2.  **Elegant and Principled Formulation:** The approach is well-grounded in two intuitive axiomatic properties (distinctiveness and learnability). The use of a non-parametric kernel method (MMD-like distance) to measure distinctiveness is particularly elegant, as it avoids making restrictive parametric assumptions about the underlying domain distributions, which is a key advantage over prior work like Hoffman et al. [20]. The formulation as an optimization problem is clear and well-motivated.

3.  **Clever Technical Insight (LPC):** The introduction of the Label Prior Constraint is a crucial and clever component. Without it, any method seeking to maximize distribution difference would likely rediscover the object categories themselves. By forcing the class proportions to be similar across latent domains, the algorithm is guided to find factors of variation (like pose or illumination) that are largely orthogonal to the semantic labels, which is precisely what one would intuitively define as a "domain".

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The empirical validation is strong. The authors not only show that their method works but also carefully design experiments to demonstrate *why* it works.
    *   The "oracle" evaluation (GOURS) effectively establishes an upper bound and shows that the discovered domains are inherently better sources for adaptation than the original datasets.
    *   The experiments with practical multi-source adaptation techniques show that this potential can be realized without access to target labels.
    *   The introduction and evaluation of "conditional reshaping" of the test set is a novel idea in itself and demonstrates a significant performance boost in a realistic scenario (multi-view action recognition), further strengthening the paper's contribution.
    *   The qualitative visualizations provide compelling anecdotal evidence that the discovered domains correspond to semantically meaningful visual factors.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Optimization Complexity:** The paper relaxes the initial integer program into a continuous optimization problem that involves maximizing a convex quadratic function, which is still NP-hard in general. While the authors state that existing solvers provided satisfactory solutions, the paper would be stronger with a more detailed discussion of the optimization's practical aspects. For instance, how scalable is the method to very large datasets? Is the solution sensitive to initialization? A deeper analysis of the optimization landscape or the quality of the solutions would be welcome. However, given the novelty of the overall idea, this is a forgivable omission.

2.  **Strength of the Label Prior Constraint (LPC) Assumption:** The LPC assumes that class priors are constant across all latent domains. While the authors provide a good intuition for this, it might not always hold. In some real-world scenarios, dataset bias might cause a strong correlation between a domain factor (e.g., a specific background) and the frequency of certain object classes. A discussion of the method's potential failure modes when this assumption is violated would improve the paper.

3.  **Indirectness of the Learnability Criterion:** The DWCV method for selecting the number of domains (K) is a reasonable and pragmatic heuristic. It is based on intra-domain discriminability. However, the ultimate goal is inter-domain adaptation. It is conceivable that a value of K that is optimal for cross-validation within the discovered domains is not necessarily the optimal K for adapting to an unseen target domain. While the experimental results suggest it works well in practice, the connection between the selection criterion and the final objective is indirect.

### Recommendation

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

This work addresses a fundamental, overlooked, and important problem in domain adaptation. The proposed direction is bold and novel, moving beyond incremental improvements to existing algorithms and instead questioning the very setup of the problem. While there are minor weaknesses in the discussion of the optimization's complexity and the strength of the LPC assumption, these are far outweighed by the paper's conceptual contribution and significance. The formulation is elegant, the experiments are thorough and convincing, and the work is very likely to inspire a new line of inquiry into discovering latent domain structures. This is precisely the kind of thought-provoking and high-impact research that NIPS should showcase.
