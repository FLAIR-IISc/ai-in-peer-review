## Peer Review for "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and well-motivated approach to domain adaptation in visual recognition, focusing on the often-overlooked issue of how datasets themselves are structured as collections of domains. The authors identify key properties – maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability – for automatically reshaping datasets into more meaningful domains, and demonstrate promising results on both object recognition and human activity recognition tasks. While the approach involves some complexity, the paper is generally well-written and the experiments are comprehensive. I believe this work has the potential to significantly impact the field of domain adaptation and is worthy of acceptance, with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper addresses a crucial, yet often implicit, assumption in domain adaptation: that datasets correspond to meaningful domains. The authors argue that visual datasets, unlike those in speech or NLP, often lack naturally defined domains, and simply treating a dataset as a single domain can hinder performance. They propose a method to automatically discover latent domains within datasets based on maximizing distinctiveness between domains and ensuring each domain is learnable (i.e., contains enough information for good classification). 

This work builds upon existing domain adaptation techniques [1, 2, 3, 4] by shifting the focus from *how* to adapt, to *what* the domains *are*. It connects to work on dataset bias correction [16, 17, 18, 19] but moves beyond simply addressing bias to proactively shaping datasets for better adaptation. The authors rightly position their work in contrast to Hoffman et al. [20], noting the advantages of their nonparametric approach over Gaussian mixture modeling.  They also acknowledge connections to generalized clustering [27] and multi-domain adaptation [28, 29, 10], carefully delineating the unique contribution of their approach.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Importance:** The core idea of explicitly reshaping datasets into domains is novel and addresses a fundamental issue in visual domain adaptation.  The authors correctly highlight the limitations of treating entire datasets as single domains.
* **Theoretical Grounding:**  The formulation based on maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability provides a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The use of kernel methods for measuring domain discrepancies is appropriate and well-motivated.
* **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:**  The authors conduct experiments on both object recognition and human activity recognition, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach in diverse settings. They compare against relevant baselines and showcase the benefits of both domain discovery *and* reshaping test datasets.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the authors provide sufficient detail for understanding their method. The inclusion of illustrative examples (Figure 1) is particularly helpful.
* **Practical Considerations:**  The DWCV procedure for automatically determining the number of domains is a practical and valuable contribution, addressing a non-trivial hyperparameter selection issue.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** The optimization problem, even in its relaxed form, may be computationally expensive for very large datasets. While the authors acknowledge this, a more detailed discussion of computational scalability would be beneficial.
* **Kernel Bandwidth Selection:** The choice of kernel bandwidth (twice the median distance) is somewhat arbitrary. While common, it would be useful to explore the sensitivity of the results to different bandwidth settings.
* **LPC Parameter (δ):** The relaxation of the Label Prior Constraint (LPC) uses a fixed δ=1%. Justification for this specific value and an analysis of its influence on results would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Domain Adaptation Algorithms:** The evaluation primarily focuses on demonstrating the benefits of domain reshaping *after* adaptation. It would be stronger to directly compare the performance of state-of-the-art domain adaptation algorithms *with* and *without* the proposed domain reshaping step.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Page 4 (TDD):**  The discussion of the TDD objective function is a bit dense. A more intuitive explanation of why maximizing the difference between domains is beneficial would be helpful.
* **Page 5 (LPC):**  Why is the LPC constraint helpful, even if violated to a small degree (δ)? Does it prevent trivial solutions?
* **Page 7 (DWCV):**  The bound Kmax = min{M/(NC),C} seems a bit arbitrary. Can you justify this choice?
* **Page 9 (Experimental Setting):**  What is the rationale for using SURF features and a 800-bin vocabulary?  Could other feature representations influence the results?
* **Page 10 (Table 2):**  The 'MATCHING' strategy is particularly effective. Is there a risk of overfitting when matching domains to the test set?



**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* The paper addresses a significant and previously underexplored problem in domain adaptation.
* The proposed approach is theoretically sound and supported by comprehensive experimental results.
* The method demonstrably improves performance on both object recognition and human activity recognition tasks.
* The paper is well-written and clear.

**Against:**

* The computational complexity of the method may be a limiting factor.
* The choice of some parameters (kernel bandwidth, LPC parameter) appears somewhat arbitrary.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art domain adaptation algorithms could be more direct.

**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS, contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, I suggest:

* Providing a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and potential scalability issues.
* Justifying the choices of kernel bandwidth and LPC parameter, and/or performing a sensitivity analysis.
* Including a more direct comparison of state-of-the-art domain adaptation algorithms with and without the proposed domain reshaping step.



I believe that addressing these points will further strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field of domain adaptation.
