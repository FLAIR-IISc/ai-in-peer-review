

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper proposes a method for automatically reshaping visual datasets into latent domains based on two properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing inter-domain distribution differences) and maximum learnability (ensuring domains can support strong classifiers). The authors argue that standard visual datasets are not naturally organized into discrete domains, unlike in speech or language processing, and that current domain adaptation research mistakenly treats entire datasets as single domains.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to experiments. The mathematical formulations are generally sound, though the relaxation of an NP-hard problem to a continuous optimization lacks sufficient theoretical justification. The authors provide a reasonable justification for their label prior constraint (LPC), which ensures domain label distributions match the overall dataset distribution.

The main contribution appears to be a method for discovering latent domains within visual datasets, which they claim is novel. However, this claim is questionable. The paper cites [20] as the only prior work on latent domain discovery, but the approach described in [20] (using Gaussian mixture models) is fundamentally different from the authors' kernel-based nonparametric approach. The authors' method is essentially a clustering approach with a novel objective function, rather than a fundamentally new technique. The paper fails to adequately compare against standard clustering methods (like K-means) or more established domain adaptation baselines that use the standard dataset-as-domain assumption.

The experimental evaluation is limited in scope and lacks sufficient rigor. The authors evaluate their method on two datasets (Caltech-256 with Amazon/DSLR/Webcam and IXMAS action recognition), which is insufficient for a claim of broad applicability. More critically, they do not compare their reshaped domains against standard domain adaptation baselines that use the datasets as domains (e.g., adapting from Amazon to DSLR). Instead, they compare against the "oracle" performance of using the identified domains, which is not a fair comparison since the oracle assumes knowledge of the correct domain assignments.

The paper's most significant weakness is its failure to demonstrate a practical advantage of their approach over standard domain adaptation methods. The "goodness" metric (GOURS) they introduce is an upper bound that cannot be achieved in practice, as it requires knowledge of the optimal domain-target matches. The practical utility results (Table 2) show marginal improvements over standard approaches, but these improvements are not substantial enough to justify the added complexity of their method.

The authors claim their approach "outperforms not only the original datasets but also the domains discovered by [20]," but this comparison is misleading. The paper does not demonstrate that their method improves domain adaptation performance in a way that matters for real-world applications. The improvement in Table 2 is relatively small (e.g., 2-3% on average), and the paper doesn't provide sufficient context about whether this represents a meaningful improvement in the domain adaptation literature.

The paper also makes an unsubstantiated claim about the "pitfall" of treating datasets as domains. In reality, many domain adaptation papers explicitly define domains (e.g., the Office dataset with its three domains: Amazon, DSLR, Webcam), and treating these as distinct domains is standard practice. The paper's central premise that datasets are "not deliberately collected with clearly identifiable domains" is questionable, as many datasets are specifically designed to represent different domains.

While the paper's methodological approach is not entirely novel, it does provide a systematic way to explore latent domain structure within datasets. However, the paper fails to demonstrate that this exploration leads to meaningful improvements in domain adaptation performance. The authors' DWCV procedure for determining the number of domains is reasonable, but the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this approach is better than simply using the number of datasets as the number of domains.

In summary, while the paper is technically sound and well-written, it lacks sufficient evidence to support its claim of significant contribution to the field. The method is a reasonable extension of clustering techniques, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that it provides a substantial improvement over existing domain adaptation approaches. The experimental evaluation is limited, and the paper fails to adequately address the practical significance of their method.

Strengths: Well-written, clear problem statement, reasonable methodological approach, reasonable comparison to [20].

Weaknesses: Overstated novelty, limited experimental evaluation, failure to demonstrate practical utility over standard baselines, unsubstantiated claims about dataset domains, marginal performance improvements.

Given the paper's limited contribution and lack of convincing evidence for its claims, I recommend rejection. The method appears to be an incremental extension of existing clustering techniques rather than a significant advance in domain adaptation. The paper would need to demonstrate substantially better performance on standard domain adaptation benchmarks against established baselines to warrant acceptance.
