Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses the overlooked problem that visual datasets often conflate multiple latent “domains,” and that treating each dataset as a single domain can hinder adaptation. The authors propose to “reshape” datasets by discovering latent domains that are (i) maximally distinctive and (ii) maximally learnable. Distinctiveness is quantified nonparametrically via the RKHS distance between empirical mean embeddings (equivalent to a multi-pair MMD objective), summed over all domain pairs (TDD). The hard assignment to K domains is relaxed to a continuous QCQP with constraints that (a) every point belongs to exactly one domain in the hard limit, and (b) each domain’s label distribution matches the global class prior up to tolerance (LPC). Learnability is enforced through a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure to select K by maximizing the weighted source-domain CV accuracy. The paper also proposes “conditional reshaping” of unlabeled test data to best match the discovered training domains.

Empirical evaluation is on Office-like object datasets (Amazon, DSLR, Webcam, Caltech-256) using SURF BoVW features and IXMAS for cross-view action recognition, with adaptation via the geodesic flow kernel. The authors compare to the latent-domain discovery of Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12), show oracle and practical multi-source adaptation results, and provide qualitative analysis of factors captured by the discovered domains. The work is related to domain adaptation (e.g., Daumé, Blitzer et al., Pan et al., Ben-David et al.), latent domain discovery (Hoffman et al.), MMD/two-sample testing (Gretton et al.), and generalized clustering via kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.).

Strengths
- Addresses an important, under-explored issue: datasets ≠ domains in vision; discovering latent domains can benefit adaptation.
- Nonparametric discrepancy (MMD) avoids fragile parametric mixture assumptions; formulation is conceptually clear.
- Uses labels judiciously to avoid collapsing clusters by class (LPC) and to set K (DWCV).
- Conditional test reshaping is a practical addition for heterogeneous target sets.
- Empirical results (as described) indicate consistent gains over using original datasets and over Hoffman et al.; qualitative analyses are insightful.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strong assumptions: LPC effectively enforces identical class priors across domains and even suggests each domain should contain all classes. In real settings, class prior shift and missing classes per domain are common; the current constraints may distort domain discovery or make the model inapplicable.
- Objective and constraints: Maximizing summed pairwise MMD encourages tiny or imbalanced domains; the current bounds 1/M ≤ Σk βmk ≤ 1/C are weak surrogates for balanced partitions. No explicit lower/upper bounds on Mk or regularization toward balanced domains are provided.
- Optimization/scalability: The relaxed problem remains NP-hard; details on the solver, runtime, convergence, and scalability to large M are lacking. The method requires an M×M kernel matrix; feasibility beyond a few thousands of samples is unclear.
- Sensitivity: No systematic analysis of kernel choice/bandwidth, δ in LPC, or stability across runs; these choices likely impact partitions and performance.
- Label usage: Distinctiveness is computed on P(x) only; without label-conditional discrepancy, discovered domains may still be confounded by class structure despite LPC. A per-class MMD (or conditional MMD) would be a better fit to the stated goal of separating extraneous factors from category structure.
- DWCV: While pragmatic, it may bias K to overfitting the source; the observed coincidence between optimal K and the number of mixed datasets warrants deeper analysis.
- Conditional reshaping: The optimization is only sketched; it appears close to assigning to fixed RKHS centroids (kernel k-means with fixed centers). A clear derivation and comparison to simpler baselines (nearest centroid in RKHS, domain classifier) are needed.
- Evaluation: Modern baselines and features are missing. Even for classical settings, comparisons to label-constrained spectral clustering, kernel k-means with balance/entropy regularization, and other multi-source adaptation methods would strengthen claims. Reproducibility details (hyperparameters, solvers, seeds) are sparse.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, but some modeling choices (LPC, “each domain has at least one sample per class”) are unrealistic and insufficiently justified. Algorithmic details and scalability analysis are insufficient. Experimental evidence is promising but lacks breadth and ablations.

Clarity
The main ideas are clearly presented. However, the relaxation, constraints (especially Eq. 5) and their implications need more careful exposition; the conditional test reshaping derivation is deferred and should be in the main text. Provide solver choices, complexity, and implementation specifics.

Originality
Latent domain discovery for adaptation is timely and relevant; using MMD-based pairwise distinctiveness plus DWCV is a reasonable and somewhat novel combination relative to Hoffman et al.’s parametric mixture. The conditional reshaping of test data is a nice addition, though related to kernel clustering with fixed centers.

Significance
Potentially useful if scalable and robust, as many vision datasets are heterogeneous. Current form likely limits applicability due to assumptions and scalability; stronger empirical and theoretical backing would increase impact.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Tackles a meaningful and general problem (latent domains) with a principled nonparametric approach.
- Simple, intuitive objectives; integrates with existing adaptation pipelines.
- Empirical results suggest practical gains and yield interpretable domains.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Strong, potentially unrealistic LPC and “all classes per domain” requirements.
- Weak balance control and risk of degenerate partitions; limited analysis of sensitivity and stability.
- Scalability and optimization details are missing; reliance on full kernels is limiting.
- Incomplete baselines and ablations; conditional reshaping not fully specified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace LPC with a softer, more realistic regularizer; allow class prior shift and missing classes. Consider label-conditional MMD or a mixture of per-class MMDs.
- Add explicit balance constraints or entropy regularization on β to avoid tiny/imbalanced domains.
- Provide an efficient solver (e.g., block-coordinate ascent, ADMM) and scalability techniques (Nyström, random features) with runtime/memory analysis.
- Include sensitivity and stability studies (kernel bandwidth, δ, initialization).
- Strengthen baselines (balanced kernel k-means with label constraints, spectral methods), and evaluate on larger/deeper features.
- Move the conditional reshaping derivation into the main paper and compare to nearest-centroid/domain-classifier baselines.
- Discuss cases where domains do not share all classes and how to adapt the method.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper presents a promising and relevant idea with encouraging results, but current assumptions, missing details, and limited empirical breadth fall short of the NeurIPS bar. Addressing the above concerns could make this a strong contribution.
