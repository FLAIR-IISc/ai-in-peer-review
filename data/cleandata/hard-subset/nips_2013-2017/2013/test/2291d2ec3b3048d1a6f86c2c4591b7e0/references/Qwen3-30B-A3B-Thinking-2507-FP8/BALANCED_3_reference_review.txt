

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

This paper tackles a critical yet underexplored aspect of domain adaptation: the fundamental question of what constitutes a "domain" in visual recognition. The authors correctly identify that visual datasets are not naturally organized into discrete domains (unlike speaker-specific domains in speech or language-specific domains in NLP), leading to the problematic practice of treating entire datasets as single domains. They propose a novel approach to automatically reshape datasets into characteristic domains based on two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains should support strong discriminative models). The paper makes a compelling case for why this matters with a clear example of how mixing view angles in action recognition leads to suboptimal adaptation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the gap in visual domain adaptation research by explaining why standard datasets don't naturally form discrete domains. The example of view angles in action recognition effectively illustrates the problem with treating entire datasets as single domains.

2. **Novel Approach:** The authors' insight that domains should be characterized by maximum distinctiveness (measured via kernel-based domain discrepancy) and maximum learnability (determined via domain-wise cross-validation) is original. Their nonparametric approach avoids the limitations of parametric methods used in prior work like [20].

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and cover multiple datasets (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam for object recognition; IXMAS for action recognition) and tasks. The comparison against baselines (K-means, random partition) and [20] is thorough, with clear demonstrations of improved performance.

4. **Practical Utility:** The paper effectively shows that reshaping datasets into the discovered domains leads to better adaptation performance, with the MATCHING strategy approaching oracle accuracies even without target domain labels.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical Grounding:** While the optimization is well-formulated, the paper lacks theoretical guarantees for the relaxation approach. The authors don't discuss convergence properties or approximation bounds, which would strengthen the technical contribution.

2. **Label Prior Constraint Justification:** The label prior constraint (LPC) is introduced without sufficient justification. The paper states it "reflects the intuition that in the process of data collection, the relative percentages of different classes are approximately in accordance with a prior distribution," but doesn't explain why preserving label proportions within domains is necessary for discovering characteristic domains. An ablation study showing the impact of relaxing LPC would be valuable.

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze how sensitive the method is to hyperparameters (kernel bandwidth, δ in LPC). This is particularly important given the use of a kernel-based measure.

4. **Qualitative Analysis Depth:** The qualitative analysis in Figure 1 is suggestive but superficial. The paper claims domains correspond to pose and illumination factors but doesn't provide statistical evidence or more systematic analysis connecting identified domains to these factors.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1. Provide theoretical analysis of the relaxation approach, including convergence properties or approximation bounds.
2. Conduct ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of the LPC and analyze its sensitivity to δ.
3. Include a sensitivity analysis for key hyperparameters (kernel bandwidth, δ) across different datasets.
4. Expand the qualitative analysis to more systematically connect identified domains with underlying factors (e.g., by measuring how well domains align with known factors in the data).
5. Discuss potential limitations of the method, particularly for datasets with very complex domain structures or when categories have few examples.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a significant contribution by shifting the focus from adapting between given domains to discovering the latent domains within visual datasets. The problem is well-motivated, the approach is novel and technically sound, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the method. While the theoretical foundations could be strengthened, the practical significance of the work is clear, and the results represent a meaningful advancement in domain adaptation research.

The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in visual domain adaptation that has been overlooked in previous work. By demonstrating that reshaping datasets into the discovered domains leads to better adaptation performance, the authors provide both a theoretical insight and a practical method that could influence future dataset construction and domain adaptation approaches.

**Decision: Accept**
