

# Review: "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental but overlooked issue in domain adaptation: the assumption that datasets correspond to well-defined domains. The authors argue that visual datasets often contain multiple latent domains (e.g., different viewpoints, lighting conditions) that are not explicitly defined in standard dataset collection. They propose a novel approach to "reshape" datasets into latent domains that satisfy two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should have maximally different distributions) and maximum learnability (domains should contain sufficient data to train strong classifiers). The authors develop nonparametric methods using kernel-based RKHS distance to measure domain discrepancies and propose a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure to determine the optimal number of domains. The approach is evaluated on object and action recognition tasks, showing significant improvements over baselines that treat datasets as single domains or use alternative domain discovery methods.

## Strengths

**Quality (Excellent):** The paper presents a technically sound approach with well-justified theoretical foundations. The optimization problem is properly formulated, and the relaxation to a continuous optimization problem is well-motivated. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple datasets (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam, IXMAS) and tasks (object and action recognition). The comparison with Hoffman et al. [20], the only prior work addressing latent domain discovery, is thorough and demonstrates clear improvements. The authors also acknowledge limitations, such as the heuristic nature of DWCV and the subjectivity in interpreting identified domains.

**Originality (Strong):** The paper identifies a crucial but previously overlooked problem in domain adaptation literature. The two axiomatic properties (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) are novel and well-motivated. The nonparametric approach to domain discovery, which avoids assuming parametric distribution forms, represents a significant departure from previous work. The DWCV method for determining the optimal number of domains is also novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work, particularly Hoffman et al. [20], which relies on Gaussian mixture models.

**Significance (High):** The paper addresses a fundamental issue that affects the validity of many domain adaptation studies. The demonstrated improvements in adaptation performance (Table 2) show practical significance for building more robust domain adaptation systems. The insights about the latent domains within standard datasets could also influence future dataset construction practices. The paper's approach to conditional reshaping of test datasets (Section 4.3) is particularly valuable for real-world applications where test data may contain multiple domains.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem statement is effectively motivated with concrete examples. Mathematical formulations are well-explained, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail for reproducibility. Figures 1 and 2 help illustrate the key concepts and results effectively.

## Weaknesses

**Quality (Moderate):** While the experimental results are promising, the paper lacks a thorough theoretical analysis of the proposed method. It doesn't address whether the optimization problem converges to the global optimum or provide guarantees about the quality of the discovered domains. The DWCV method is heuristic and might not generalize well to all datasets. The paper also doesn't discuss computational complexity, which could be a limitation for large-scale applications.

**Originality (Moderate):** The paper doesn't fully address how their approach relates to broader clustering literature (e.g., [27]), though they mention it briefly. While their focus on domain discovery rather than general clustering is valid, a more comprehensive comparison with clustering methods would strengthen the novelty claim.

**Significance (Moderate):** The paper doesn't fully explore the limitations of their approach. For instance, how well would their method work with extremely large datasets or when the number of latent domains is very large? The paper also doesn't discuss how their approach might integrate with state-of-the-art domain adaptation algorithms beyond the basic geodesic flow kernel method.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that addresses an important, overlooked problem in domain adaptation. The authors' novel perspective on reshaping datasets into latent domains represents a significant contribution to the field. The proposed approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing methods.

The main weaknesses relate to the lack of theoretical guarantees and more thorough discussion of limitations. However, these are common challenges in applied machine learning papers and don't undermine the paper's substantial contribution.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should:
1. Add a brief theoretical discussion of the optimization problem's convergence properties
2. Expand the discussion of limitations and potential failure cases
3. Include a more comprehensive comparison with clustering literature

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the domain adaptation literature by fundamentally rethinking how we conceptualize domains and datasets. The insights and methods presented have the potential to significantly improve the robustness of domain adaptation systems in real-world applications.

**Overall Recommendation: Accept**
