This paper addresses the important problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition, specifically focusing on the often-overlooked question of *what constitutes* a domain in visual data. The authors correctly point out the common practice of equating datasets with domains and propose a method to automatically *reshape* datasets into more meaningful domains based on the principles of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. While the motivation is sound and the problem formulation is interesting, I find the paper ultimately lacking in convincing evidence and overly reliant on potentially brittle assumptions.

The core idea – identifying domains based on statistical differences and ensuring they are learnable – is reasonable. The use of kernel methods to measure distribution discrepancy (TDD) is a standard technique, though its scalability to high-dimensional image features isn’t thoroughly discussed. However, the relaxation of the integer programming optimization problem to a continuous one, while necessary for tractability, introduces approximations that could significantly impact the quality of the identified domains. The label prior constraint (LPC) feels somewhat ad-hoc; while intuitively appealing, its justification isn't strong, and the choice of a 1% deviation seems arbitrary. 

My primary concern lies with the experimental validation. The results, presented in Tables 1-3, demonstrate improvements over baseline methods, but the baselines are not always strong enough.  The comparison to Hoffman et al. [20] is brief and doesn't fully address the differences. More importantly, the “oracle” accuracies (Table 1) are presented without sufficient discussion of their practical limitations. While informative, the fact that the proposed method approaches these accuracies *doesn't* automatically translate to real-world utility if the matching procedure requires target domain labels (which is often unavailable).  The multi-source adaptation experiments (Table 2) are more realistic, but the performance gains, while present, are not overwhelming and may be attributable to the reshaping of the test set rather than solely the quality of the identified domains.  Furthermore, the analysis of the identified domains (Figure 1) is descriptive but lacks quantitative rigor. Simply stating that domains correspond to factors like “pose and appearance” isn’t sufficient; a more thorough investigation of the features driving domain separation would be beneficial.

The DWCV approach for determining the number of domains (K) feels somewhat disconnected from the core domain discovery process. While appealing in its attempt to balance distinctiveness and learnability, the dependence on cross-validation accuracy introduces another layer of potential overfitting and sensitivity to the choice of cross-validation parameters.  The observation that the optimal K often coincides with the number of datasets seems almost tautological given the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses an important, often-overlooked question in domain adaptation: the definition of a domain.
*   Proposes a principled framework for discovering latent domains based on maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.
*   Demonstrates some improvement over baseline methods on standard datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited discussion of the approximation introduced by the optimization relaxation.
*   The LPC feels ad-hoc and lacks strong justification.
*   Experimental validation relies on potentially unrealistic “oracle” accuracies and weak baselines.
*   Analysis of identified domains is descriptive and lacks quantitative rigor.
*   DWCV for determining K feels disconnected from the core domain discovery process.
*   Scalability of the kernel-based methods to high-dimensional image features is not discussed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper identifies a novel and important research direction within domain adaptation.
*   The proposed method, while imperfect, provides a starting point for more sophisticated domain discovery techniques.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental validation is not convincing enough to demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.
*   The paper relies on potentially brittle assumptions and lacks a thorough analysis of the limitations.
*   The overall contribution feels incremental and doesn't represent a significant advance in the field.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. While the paper raises interesting questions and proposes a reasonable approach, it falls short of demonstrating sufficient novelty, rigor, and impact to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors need to strengthen the experimental validation, provide a more thorough analysis of the limitations, and address the concerns raised above. A more convincing demonstration of real-world utility, beyond the oracle and relatively weak baseline comparisons, is crucial.
