Summary
This paper tackles the important but underexplored problem of discovering latent “domains” inside visual datasets to improve domain adaptation. The key idea is to reshape annotated source datasets into K latent domains that (i) are maximally distinctive in distribution and (ii) support strong discriminative learning (maximum learnability). Distinctiveness is quantified via kernel mean embeddings (MMD) between domains in an RKHS; the objective sums pairwise squared MMD across domains. The assignment of instances to domains is constrained by a label prior constraint (LPC) that enforces similar class proportions across domains. The resulting combinatorial problem is relaxed to a continuous quadratic program over soft assignment variables β. The number of domains K is chosen by domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV), which trains classifiers within each discovered domain and selects K maximizing weighted CV accuracy. The paper further proposes “conditional reshaping” of heterogeneous test datasets by assigning test instances to match the discovered training domains via minimizing distribution discrepancies. Experiments on Caltech/Amazon/DSLR/Webcam (objects) and IXMAS (actions) report gains, including improved oracle performance and practical multi-source adaptation (GFK) using discovered domains.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on kernel two-sample/MMD ideas [Gretton et al.] and multi-source domain adaptation [Mansour et al.], and is complementary to dataset bias studies (Torralba & Efros). The closest prior is Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12), who discover latent domains parametrically via hierarchical Gaussian mixtures; the current paper’s nonparametric kernel formulation is a notable alternative. The use of learnability to select K relates to discriminative clustering (e.g., Jegelka et al.) but here is tailored to domain discovery.

Strengths
- Timely problem: The observation that dataset boundaries rarely align with latent factors affecting visual domain shift is compelling and practically relevant.
- Nonparametric formulation: Using MMD with characteristic kernels avoids fragile parametric assumptions and is a reasonable way to drive domain distinctiveness.
- Joint criteria: Combining distinctiveness with learnability is a sensible antidote to degenerate solutions (very small domains) and ties the discovery objective to downstream adaptation utility.
- Conditional test reshaping: The idea to partition test data to match discovered training domains is novel and natural for heterogeneous test streams (e.g., multi-view actions).
- Empirical indications: On two benchmarks, discovered domains outperform original datasets and a prior domain discovery method under oracle and practical adaptation strategies.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Optimization correctness/clarity: The objective is a convex quadratic form in β (since K is PSD), and the paper proposes to maximize it. Maximizing a convex function over a convex set is nonconvex/NP-hard; calling the relaxed problem a “convex quadratic program” is inaccurate. The manuscript should clarify the optimization strategy (e.g., DC programming, projected gradient ascent with multiple restarts) and provide convergence behavior and sensitivity to initialization.
- Constraints and derivation: The relaxation from hard assignments zmk to βmk = zmk/Mk is nonstandard. The per-sample constraint 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C is ad hoc and its connection to Mk ≥ C is unclear. As written, it neither guarantees one-domain-per-instance nor one-per-class-per-domain. A principled relaxation (e.g., softmax/entropy regularization with a rounding step, or spectral/kernel k-means–style relaxations) would be preferable.
- Label prior constraint (LPC): Enforcing identical class proportions across domains is a strong assumption, often violated in practice (e.g., different views may systematically skew classes). The paper allows a 1% slack δ, which is too tight for small domains and risks infeasibility or brittle solutions. There is no ablation to show the effect of LPC, δ, or removing the “one-per-class” notion.
- Theoretical support: Beyond stating that characteristic kernels make MMD discriminative, there is no analysis of sample complexity, stability, or consistency of the discovered domains, nor of how DWCV trades off distinctiveness vs. learnability. Even a simple equivalence to between-cluster scatter in RKHS or bounds linking A(K) to generalization would strengthen the contribution.
- Evaluation details: Key tables/values are summarized but not fully specified; it is hard to assess magnitude and statistical significance of gains. The “MATCHING” strategy that nearly attains oracle performance needs precise description (which discrepancy, any target label use?) and comparisons to stronger multi-source baselines (e.g., mixture-of-experts, adaptive weighting, or recent MMD/OT-based source selection).
- Scalability: The method requires an M×M kernel and solves an optimization in O(MK) variables. Runtime, memory, and solver choice are not reported. Applicability to larger, modern datasets is unclear.
- Confounding adaptation effects: Improvements are reported when using GFK. To isolate the benefit of domain discovery, please report non-adapted baselines (e.g., linear SVM on pooled vs. discovered domains) and ablations across different adaptation algorithms.
- Notation and clarity: K is used both as number of domains and kernel matrix; this is confusing. Some steps (conditional reshaping constraints, matching procedure, rounding from β to hard assignments) are in the supplement and should be summarized clearly.

Quality
Technically promising but underspecified in optimization and constraints; empirical results are encouraging yet lack ablations, strong baselines, and scalability analysis. Claims would be more convincing with clearer algorithms and robustness studies.

Clarity
Generally readable with good motivation, but several derivations are opaque; notation overloading and the convexity claim should be fixed. Provide pseudo-code and implementation details.

Originality
Moderate. The combination of MMD-based distinctiveness with learnability-driven model selection and test-time reshaping is novel in this specific form, although related ideas exist in discriminative clustering and multi-source adaptation.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners facing heterogeneous data and for informing dataset construction. Impact depends on robustness and scalability; clearer evidence and comparisons are needed.

Questions for authors
1) Precisely describe the solver, initialization, and rounding; report runtime/memory and convergence statistics.  
2) Justify and derive the constraints in Eq. (5); why 1/M and 1/C? What happens if you replace them with entropy or simplex constraints plus a balancing penalty?  
3) Ablate LPC and δ; how sensitive are results to class imbalance or mis-specified priors?  
4) Detail the “MATCHING” criterion and the conditional reshaping QP (objective, constraints). Do you enforce balanced test-domain sizes?  
5) Provide statistical significance and variance across random seeds/splits.  
6) Compare to kernel k-means (in RKHS) with label-regularization, spectral clustering, and optimal-transport-based source selection.

Pros
- Addresses an important and underexplored problem.  
- Sound nonparametric discrepancy measure; conceptually clean.  
- Practical criterion (DWCV) to choose K.  
- Novel conditional test reshaping and encouraging results.

Cons
- Optimization/formulation issues and missing algorithmic details.  
- Strong, possibly unrealistic LPC; no ablations.  
- Limited baselines and analysis; unclear scalability.

Recommendation
Borderline accept if the authors can clarify the optimization, correct the convexity claim, provide ablations (especially of LPC and K-selection), and strengthen empirical comparisons. Otherwise, the contribution risks being viewed as an interesting idea without sufficient methodological rigor and empirical support.
