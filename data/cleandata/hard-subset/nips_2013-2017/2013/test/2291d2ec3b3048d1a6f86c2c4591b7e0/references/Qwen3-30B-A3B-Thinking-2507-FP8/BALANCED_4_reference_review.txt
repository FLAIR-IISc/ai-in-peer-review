

# Review of "Reshaping Visual Datasets for Domain Adaptation"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental yet overlooked issue in domain adaptation: the assumption that datasets correspond to well-defined domains, when in reality, visual datasets often contain multiple latent domains that are not explicitly identified. The authors propose a novel method to automatically reshape datasets into characteristic domains based on two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different from each other in distribution) and maximum learnability (domains should enable strong discriminative classifiers). Their approach uses nonparametric kernel-based methods to measure domain discrepancies and a domain-wise cross-validation procedure to determine the optimal number of domains. The paper evaluates the approach on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and action recognition (IXMAS) tasks, showing improved adaptation performance compared to using datasets as-is or using the method of [20].

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual Contribution**: The paper identifies an important gap in domain adaptation research that has been largely overlooked - the assumption that datasets correspond to domains. This is a significant conceptual contribution that addresses a fundamental issue in how domain adaptation is currently framed and evaluated.

2. **Novel Methodology**: The proposed approach of automatically discovering latent domains using the two axiomatic properties (distinctiveness and learnability) is well-motivated and distinct from prior work. The label prior constraint (LPC) effectively incorporates label information in a way that avoids per-category clustering, which is a key insight.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The evaluation is thorough, covering multiple datasets and tasks. The comparison to using datasets as-is and to [20] is well-justified, and the demonstration of practical utility through adaptation performance is convincing.

4. **Insightful Analysis**: The qualitative analysis of identified domains (Figure 1) provides valuable insights into what factors drive domain structure in visual datasets (e.g., object pose, appearance, background), which could inform future dataset construction.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Justification**: While the method is well-formulated, the paper lacks sufficient theoretical analysis. The optimization problem is relaxed from integer programming to a continuous problem without justification for the relaxation's quality. The DWCV procedure for determining K lacks theoretical guarantees, and the paper doesn't address how the method would perform when K is large relative to dataset size.

2. **Scalability and Practicality**: The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity or scalability to large datasets. The relaxation to convex quadratic programming is helpful, but the paper doesn't address how the method would perform on datasets with millions of instances, which is common in visual recognition.

3. **Comparison with Baselines**: The paper compares to using datasets as-is and [20], but doesn't include a comparison to using standard domain adaptation methods directly on the original datasets (without reshaping). This makes it difficult to assess whether the improvement comes from reshaping or from the domain adaptation method itself.

4. **Connection Between Properties**: The relationship between maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability could be better explained. The paper states both are necessary properties but doesn't clarify why they work together to produce better domains.

5. **Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the method. For instance, when the optimal number of domains K is large, the DWCV procedure may not be effective, and the paper doesn't discuss how to handle such cases.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable conceptual contribution by identifying a fundamental issue in domain adaptation research that has been overlooked. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear empirical benefits over existing approaches. However, the lack of theoretical analysis and limited discussion of practical limitations prevent it from being a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

## Recommendations

1. **Strengthen Theoretical Analysis**: Provide more theoretical justification for the relaxation of the optimization problem and the DWCV procedure. Discuss the convergence properties of the optimization method and the theoretical guarantees for the domain discovery process.

2. **Expand Evaluation**: Include a comparison of the method with standard domain adaptation methods applied directly to the original datasets (without reshaping) to better isolate the contribution of the reshaping approach.

3. **Address Scalability**: Discuss computational complexity and scalability to large datasets, and provide empirical evidence of performance on larger datasets if possible.

4. **Clarify Connection Between Properties**: Better explain how maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability work together to produce effective domains.

5. **Discuss Limitations**: More thoroughly discuss the limitations of the method, particularly in cases where the optimal number of domains is large relative to dataset size.

## Pro/Con Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Identifies and addresses a fundamental gap in domain adaptation research
- Novel method with clear conceptual contribution
- Comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and tasks
- Provides valuable insights into domain structure in visual datasets

**Con:**
- Lacks sufficient theoretical analysis
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability
- Does not compare to standard domain adaptation methods applied directly to original datasets
- Insufficient discussion of limitations

The paper is promising but requires stronger theoretical grounding and more comprehensive evaluation to meet NIPS standards. I recommend rejection, with the understanding that with the suggested revisions, this could become a strong contribution to the field.
