### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a fundamental, yet often overlooked, problem in domain adaptation research: the assumption that a given dataset represents a single, monolithic domain. The authors argue that standard datasets are often heterogeneous collections of multiple latent domains, defined by factors like viewpoint, illumination, or background. Treating such a collection as a single source domain can hinder adaptation performance.

To address this, the paper proposes a novel unsupervised method to "reshape" one or more source datasets into a set of K latent domains. The core idea is to find a data partition that satisfies two intuitive properties:
1.  **Maximum Distinctiveness:** The discovered domains should be maximally different from each other. This is formalized by maximizing the sum of pairwise distribution distances, measured by the squared Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). To prevent a trivial partitioning by class label, a "label prior constraint" (LPC) is introduced, ensuring that the class distribution within each discovered domain mirrors the overall class distribution.
2.  **Maximum Learnability:** The domains should contain enough data to train effective classifiers. This property is used to determine the optimal number of domains, K. The authors propose a "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) scheme, where for each candidate K, they discover the domains and then compute a weighted average of the cross-validation accuracy of classifiers trained within each domain. The K that maximizes this score is selected.

The resulting optimization problem is a non-convex quadratic program, which the authors solve using standard solvers. The paper's contribution is a principled, non-parametric approach to latent domain discovery. This work is most closely related to Hoffman et al. (2020), but differs by using a non-parametric method for measuring domain discrepancy instead of a parametric Gaussian mixture model. Experiments on object and action recognition tasks demonstrate that adapting from these discovered domains significantly improves performance over adapting from the original, un-reshaped datasets.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed framework is well-motivated and the mathematical formulation is sound. The two guiding principles—maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability—are intuitive and effectively translated into optimization objectives and model selection criteria. The use of a non-parametric kernel method for measuring distribution distance is appropriate, as it avoids making strong assumptions about the underlying domain distributions. The experiments are comprehensive, covering two different computer vision tasks and comparing against sensible baselines, including the original datasets and a competing method. The results consistently show the benefit of the proposed reshaping approach. The extension to reshaping the test set conditionally on the source domains is a nice addition that further strengthens the results.

However, the paper is lacking in crucial details regarding the optimization procedure. The authors state that their relaxed optimization problem is still NP-hard and that "existing solvers are adequate in yielding satisfactory solutions," but they provide no information about which solver was used, its configuration, typical runtimes, or convergence properties. This lack of detail significantly impacts the reproducibility of the work. Furthermore, the scalability of the method is a concern. The formulation involves a kernel matrix of size M x M, making it computationally expensive (at least O(M^2)) for large datasets. This potential limitation is not discussed.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction provides a compelling motivation for the problem, clearly articulating the shortcomings of the standard "dataset-as-domain" paradigm. The proposed approach is explained logically, moving from the objective for a fixed K to the method for selecting K. The notation is consistent and the experimental setup is clearly described. The qualitative analysis in Figure 1, showing example images from the discovered domains, provides valuable intuition about what the method is learning.

A minor point of unclarity is the "MATCHING" multi-source adaptation method used in Table 2. Its implementation is not described, though one can infer it likely involves selecting the source domain with the minimum distribution shift to the target. This should be explicitly stated.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant and original contribution. While Hoffman et al. (2020) previously explored latent domain discovery, this paper's non-parametric formulation based on maximizing MMD is novel and distinct. The combination of maximizing inter-domain distance while enforcing a label-prior constraint to prevent class-separation is a clever and effective formulation. The use of the "maximum learnability" principle, implemented via DWCV, as a model selection criterion for the number of domains is also a novel contribution in this context. This work challenges a common practice in the field and provides a new, principled tool for researchers.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is high. It encourages a more nuanced view of domains and datasets in adaptation research. If the computational challenges can be addressed, the proposed reshaping method could become a standard and valuable pre-processing step for many domain adaptation tasks. The insights gained from analyzing the discovered domains (as in Figure 1) could also inform future dataset collection efforts by highlighting the key factors of variation that define sub-domains within existing benchmarks. The ideas presented are general and could likely be applied to other modalities beyond computer vision.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel, important, and previously under-explored problem in domain adaptation.
*   Proposes a principled and elegant non-parametric framework based on the well-motivated criteria of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.
*   The formulation, particularly the inclusion of the label prior constraint, is clever and effective.
*   Extensive experimental results on multiple benchmarks convincingly demonstrate the practical benefits of the proposed approach.
*   The paper is well-written, clear, and thought-provoking, with the potential to influence future research in the area.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   Crucial implementation details about the optimization solver, runtime, and convergence are missing, which harms reproducibility.
*   The scalability of the method to large datasets is a major concern that is not addressed in the paper. The O(M^2) complexity of the kernel matrix is a significant limitation.

### Constructive Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement

Overall, this is a strong paper with a compelling contribution. The primary weakness is the lack of implementation details, which I strongly encourage the authors to add in the final version.

1.  **Optimization Details:** Please specify which optimization solver was used and provide details on its configuration. A discussion of runtime complexity and empirical runtimes on the datasets used would be invaluable for readers to assess the practical feasibility of the method.
2.  **Scalability:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion on scalability. Can the method be extended to larger datasets? The authors could discuss potential avenues, such as using linear-time MMD approximations or Nyström-based kernel approximations, even if just as future work.
3.  **Clarify Baselines:** Please add a brief description of the "MATCHING" method used in Table 2 to the main text for completeness.
4.  **Ablation on LPC:** The Label Prior Constraint (LPC) is a key component. An ablation study showing what happens if this constraint is removed (e.g., the method simply discovers clusters based on class labels) would further underscore its importance.

Despite the concerns about implementation details, the novelty of the problem formulation and the strength of the core ideas and results make this a valuable contribution to the NeurIPS community. I recommend acceptance, with the strong expectation that the authors will incorporate the suggested details to improve the completeness and reproducibility of their work.
