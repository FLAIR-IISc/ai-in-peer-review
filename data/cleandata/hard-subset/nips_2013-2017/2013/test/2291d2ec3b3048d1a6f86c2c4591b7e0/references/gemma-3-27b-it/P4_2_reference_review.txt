The paper proposes a method for automatically reshaping visual datasets into domains for improved domain adaptation performance. The core idea is to identify latent domains within a dataset based on two principles: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. Distinctiveness is quantified by measuring the distance between distributions of potential domains using a kernel-based method, while learnability is assessed through domain-wise cross-validation. The authors present a continuous optimization approach for discovering these domains and demonstrate its effectiveness on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks.

This work addresses an important and often overlooked aspect of domain adaptation: the inherent structure of datasets themselves. Current domain adaptation techniques frequently treat entire datasets as single domains, potentially overlooking underlying domain variations. This paper directly tackles this issue by proposing a method to *discover* these latent domains, offering a potentially significant improvement over existing approaches. The two proposed properties – distinctiveness and learnability – are well-motivated and intuitively appealing. The authors clearly articulate the problem and position their work within the context of existing domain adaptation research, acknowledging limitations of treating datasets as monolithic domains and referencing relevant work on landmark discovery and latent domain identification (Hoffman et al., ECCV 2013). The experimental setup appears well-designed, employing standard datasets and evaluation protocols.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of automatically discovering latent domains within datasets, rather than assuming they are pre-defined, is a novel contribution.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical framework based on maximizing distinctiveness and learnability is logically sound. The use of kernel methods for measuring distribution differences and cross-validation for assessing learnability are appropriate choices.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method. The notation is reasonably consistent, and the key concepts are presented in a digestible manner.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method can improve domain adaptation performance compared to using the original datasets as domains.  The inclusion of a comparison with Hoffman et al. (2013) is important.
*   **Conditional Reshaping:** The extension to reshaping test datasets is insightful and addresses a practical challenge in domain adaptation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Computational Complexity:** The optimization problem, even in its relaxed continuous form, may be computationally expensive for large datasets. The paper doesn't provide detailed computational cost analysis.
*   **Kernel Choice:** The performance is highly sensitive to the choice of kernel and its bandwidth. While the authors mention using Gaussian kernels and a median-based bandwidth selection, the sensitivity analysis to these parameters is limited.
*   **Label Prior Constraint:** The rationale for the label prior constraint (LPC) is not entirely convincing. The authors acknowledge that it doesn’t restrict the absolute number of labels, but the justification provided feels somewhat ad-hoc.
*   **Limited comparison with Strong Baselines:** While the comparison with Hoffman et al. is present, the paper would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison to established domain adaptation algorithms that *do not* rely on pre-defined domains but learn adaptation mappings directly (e.g., DANN, CDAN). Showing improvements over these methods would provide a stronger validation of the benefit of reshaping the datasets first.
*   **Qualitative analysis depth:** The visualization of identified domains could be expanded to provide more granular insights into the characteristics that differentiate them.  Simply showing representative images may not fully convey the domain structure.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in domain adaptation – the often-unstructured nature of datasets.
*   The proposed method offers a novel and theoretically grounded approach to discovering latent domains.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on multiple tasks.
*   The idea of conditional reshaping of test datasets is a valuable extension.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity of the proposed method could limit its scalability.
*   The sensitivity to kernel choice and bandwidth is a potential concern.
*   The comparison with strong domain adaptation baselines is limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The idea of reshaping datasets into latent domains is novel and well-motivated, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. While there are some weaknesses related to computational complexity and comparison with existing baselines, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the weaknesses section (particularly strengthening the comparison with stronger baselines and providing a more detailed computational analysis).
