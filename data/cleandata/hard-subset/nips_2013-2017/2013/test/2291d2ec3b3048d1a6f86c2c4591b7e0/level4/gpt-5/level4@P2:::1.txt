Summary
The paper proposes an unsupervised method to discover latent domains in visual datasets and then uses these domains within a domain adaptation framework to improve recognition on target data. The key idea is to partition data so that the discovered domains are both maximally distinctive (their underlying distributions are as different as possible) and maximally learnable (each domain supports training a strong classifier). Distinctiveness is operationalized via a kernel-based distance between empirical distributions (akin to MMD) summed over all domain pairs; the assignment is originally posed as an integer program with two constraints: (1) each instance belongs to exactly one domain; and (2) within each domain, the class-label proportions match the global label prior (label prior constraint, LPC). The authors relax the integer program to a quadratic objective with linear constraints and solve it with off-the-shelf solvers. The number of domains K is selected by domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV), weighing per-domain CV accuracies by domain size. Experiments on Saenko et al.’s object datasets [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset [15] show improvements over baselines, including an approach related to dataset bias [19].

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation that targets an important practical issue: dataset ≠ domain, especially in vision where multiple nuisance factors interact.
- Nonparametric formulation for domain distinctiveness avoids potentially misspecified mixture models.
- Joint consideration of distinctiveness and learnability, with a practical DWCV procedure to choose K.
- Empirical gains on two diverse benchmarks; improvements over [19] are encouraging.
- Writing is generally clear and the method is described in sufficient detail to reimplement.

Weaknesses and questions
At submission time I lean slightly toward reject, but I am open to changing my score if the following are addressed:

1) Motivation and avoidance of per-category clustering
- The paper claims (e.g., line 77) that clustering by appearance tends to produce per-category domains. Please provide empirical evidence (e.g., show that K-means over appearance features leads to category-pure clusters and underperforms for adaptation), and clarify in what regimes this occurs.
- Explain concretely how the proposed objective avoids this pitfall. My understanding is that LPC prevents label-imbalanced domains, but then distinctiveness could still be driven by category differences instead of nuisance factors. What element forces domains to reflect nuisance factors (pose, illumination, background) rather than class identity?

2) Necessity and impact of the label prior constraint (LPC)
- Why is LPC required (line 154)? Please give intuition and, if possible, theoretical or empirical justification.
- Report performance without LPC (same optimization but dropping the constraint).
- Include baselines that partition data by: (a) K-means on appearance features; (b) unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al., ICCV’05); (c) random equal-sized partitions. This will help isolate where the gains come from.

Additional comments and clarifications
- Line 82: “Maximally different in distribution” — please state explicitly “distribution over x induced by domain assignments,” and relate to the kernel two-sample test (e.g., MMD).
- Line 166: Given βmk = zmk/Mk, doesn’t β already lie on a simplex? Please clarify the relaxation and any additional bounds you impose.
- Line 182: The relaxed constraints appear to differ from those in line 153 (e.g., bounds like 1/M ≤ Σk βmk ≤ 1/C and the δ-tolerance LPC). Please reconcile and explain the defaults (e.g., why Mk ≥ C).
- Line 215: Provide justification for Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C} in DWCV.
- Line 289: Please detail your use of the geodesic flow kernel [4]: dimensions, bandwidths, and whether you used public code or a reimplementation.
- Lines 313/340: Offer analysis for why your method outperforms baselines. Show typical failure modes of baselines (e.g., when appearance clustering collapses to categories) that your approach overcomes.
- Eq. (1): Typo: M'k should be Mk'.
- Consider adding Torralba and Efros, “Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias” (CVPR 2011) for context on cross-dataset generalization.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses a real and common issue in domain adaptation for vision.
- Conceptually simple, nonparametric, and practical.
- Demonstrated improvements on standard datasets.

Cons:
- Motivation and mechanism for avoiding per-class domains need clearer evidence.
- Dependence on LPC and lack of ablations/baselines weaken the empirical story.
- Some technical details and justifications (constraints, bounds) are underexplained.

Post-rebuttal
The rebuttal satisfactorily addressed the motivation and the role/impact of the label prior constraint. I now lean slightly toward accept.
