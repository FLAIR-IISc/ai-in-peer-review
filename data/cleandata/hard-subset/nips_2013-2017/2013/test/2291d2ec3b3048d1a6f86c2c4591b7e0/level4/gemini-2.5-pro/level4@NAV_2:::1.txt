This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation in visual recognition, arguing that the common practice of equating entire datasets with "domains" is suboptimal. The core idea is to automatically discover latent, more coherent domains within one or more source datasets. The authors propose a novel formulation based on two principles: "maximum distinctiveness," which seeks to partition the data such that the resulting domains have maximally different feature distributions, and "maximum learnability," which ensures that each discovered domain is large and consistent enough to train a strong classifier.

Technically, the method models domain discovery as an optimization problem. It uses a non-parametric, kernel-based measure (the maximum mean discrepancy) to quantify the difference between the distributions of any two domains. The data is then partitioned to maximize the sum of these pairwise differences. The optimization is formulated as an integer program with constraints ensuring each data point is assigned to a single domain and that the class label distribution within each new domain matches the global prior. This problem is then relaxed into a continuous quadratic program. The optimal number of latent domains is determined via a proposed "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) procedure. The approach is validated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, demonstrating that using the discovered domains for adaptation improves performance over using the original datasets and a competing method for latent domain discovery [20].

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality:** The paper tackles a crucial and often-overlooked aspect of domain adaptation: the definition of a domain itself. The proposed formulation, based on the principles of maximum distinctiveness and learnability, is a novel and principled approach to automatically reshaping datasets for this task.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clear, the technical details of the proposed method are explained thoroughly, and the experimental setup is easy to understand.
*   **Significance:** The experimental results are compelling. The method not only outperforms baselines that use the original, pre-defined datasets but also shows significant gains over the most relevant prior work on latent domain discovery [20]. This suggests that the discovered domains are indeed more effective for subsequent adaptation, potentially advancing the state of the art in multi-source adaptation.

**Weaknesses:**
The main weaknesses concern the justification for certain modeling choices and a lack of specific baseline comparisons that would more strongly establish the contribution of the proposed components.

*   **Motivation for the Formulation:** The paper claims that "simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains." This is a key motivation, but it is presented without empirical evidence. Furthermore, it is not immediately obvious how the proposed maximum distinctiveness criterion on its own avoids this same pitfall. A more detailed explanation or a simple illustrative experiment would strengthen this argument.
*   **The Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The LPC is a strong constraint that forces the class distribution in each discovered domain to mirror the global distribution. The rationale for this is not fully justified. In many real-world scenarios, different domains might naturally have different class priors. The inclusion of this constraint requires a more thorough analysis, ideally through an ablation study showing performance with and without it. This would help disentangle the benefits of maximizing distinctiveness from the effects of enforcing a uniform class prior.
*   **Insufficient Baselines:** To demonstrate the value of the complete formulation, it would be beneficial to compare against simpler unsupervised partitioning methods. For example, what is the performance if the data is simply partitioned using k-means on the feature vectors? Or even a random partitioning? These baselines would help clarify how much of the performance gain comes from the sophisticated optimization versus merely splitting the data.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**
1.  The term "distribution" is used throughout the paper (e.g., "maximally different in distribution"). To be precise, could you clarify if this refers to the marginal distribution over features P(x), as the formulation suggests, or the joint distribution P(x, y)?
2.  In Section 2.1, it is stated that the optimization problem for domain assignments is relaxed into a continuous one. However, the constraints in the relaxed formulation (Eq. 5) appear to be different from their counterparts in the integer program (Eq. 3). Could you please elaborate on the connection between these two sets of constraints?
3.  The Domain-Wise Cross-Validation (DWCV) procedure for selecting K includes an upper bound, K_max. Could you provide a more formal justification or proof for this bound?
4.  For reproducibility, please provide more detail on the implementation of the geodesic flow kernel [4]. Specifically, was a publicly available implementation used, and what were the key parameter settings?
5.  The quantitative improvements are impressive. The paper would be even stronger with more qualitative analysis. Could you include some visualizations or examples of failure cases from the baseline methods (e.g., [20] or using the original datasets) that your approach successfully handles? This would provide valuable insight into *why* your method is more effective.
6.  There appears to be a typo in Equation (1). M'_k should likely be M_k'.
7.  A relevant and foundational paper in this area is "Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias" by Torralba and Efros (CVPR 2011). Citing and briefly discussing your work in the context of this paper would be appropriate.
