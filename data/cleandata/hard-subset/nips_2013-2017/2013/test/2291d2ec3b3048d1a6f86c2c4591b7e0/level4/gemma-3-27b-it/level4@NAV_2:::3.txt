This paper proposes a convex framework for partitioning datasets into K subsets, designed such that each subset exhibits similar semantic class distributions and is maximally distinct from others. This distinction, termed “maximum distinctiveness,” is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences between subsets in the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) induced by a chosen kernel. These subsets are then referred to as latent domains. The optimal number of latent domains, K, is identified by maximizing average “learnability” – how well a classifier can be learned – within each domain.

While enforcing similar class distributions within each domain aims to prevent clusters dominated by single classes, it may also be a limiting factor for latent domain discovery in certain applications. For example, when considering pose as a latent domain for animal classification (e.g., horses and cows), class-conditional domain distributions may differ; both horses and cows may be pictured in left/right standing poses, but horses are less frequently depicted in sitting poses compared to cows. 

Although the discovery of latent domains is not entirely novel, the approach of controlling class label distributions to improve latent domain identification within a (relaxed) convex framework is a novel contribution. Identifying the number of latent domains by evaluating classification quality within each domain is also a noteworthy practice, ensuring sufficient samples per class for robust generalization and discrimination.

In several instances, the concepts of dataset, domain, and latent domain lack clarity and risk confusion. These concepts should be clearly defined, ideally with illustrative examples. Section 4.2, in particular, requires clarification; the terms "dataset" and "domain" are used interchangeably (e.g., *S<sub>i</sub>* is referred to as both a dataset and a source domain). Furthermore, *max<sub>k</sub> r(U<sub>k</sub>,B)* is used in equation (7) without prior definition. 

The experimental validation appears adequate, demonstrating reasonable improvements over baseline methods. However, the rationale behind the specific selection of source and target datasets for evaluation is unclear. A leave-one-dataset-out adaptation scheme might provide a more comprehensive assessment. The qualitative results are helpful in visualizing the algorithm’s behavior. 

On line 74, characterizing the problem as an unsupervised learning problem may be misleading, as the method utilizes semantic class labels. The discovery of latent domains, formulated through encouraging similar class distributions within a (relaxed) convex framework, represents a notable technical contribution. However, some concepts within the paper and the experimental validation require clarification.
