

This paper proposes an innovative approach to automatically discover latent domains in visual datasets for domain adaptation, addressing a critical gap in current domain adaptation research. The authors argue that the common practice of equating datasets with domains is inadequate, as real-world visual data is affected by complex, overlapping factors (lighting, pose, background, etc.) that don't lend themselves to discrete domain categorization. Their key insight is that latent domains should possess two properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should have maximally different distributions from each other) and maximum learnability (domains should allow for strong discriminative models to be trained). The authors formulate this as a nonparametric optimization problem with constraints on label distribution preservation, and propose a domain-wise cross-validation method to determine the optimal number of domains.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses a genuine problem in visual domain adaptation. The authors convincingly argue that standard clustering approaches (like k-means) fail to discover meaningful domains because they tend to cluster by category rather than by the underlying visual factors that create distribution shifts. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear definitions of domain distinctiveness using kernel-based distribution comparison in RKHS. The evaluation on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human action recognition (IXMAS) datasets demonstrates consistent improvements over standard domain adaptation approaches that treat datasets as single domains. The results show that reshaping datasets into the discovered latent domains leads to significantly better adaptation performance, particularly when using the MATCHING strategy to select the most appropriate source domain for adaptation.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a principled method for domain discovery that doesn't require prior knowledge of domain structure. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed, with careful attention to the practical utility of the discovered domains through multi-source adaptation strategies. The authors' analysis of the identified domains (Figure 1) provides compelling evidence that the method successfully discovers domains aligned with meaningful visual factors like object pose and background.

I have two main concerns that would need to be addressed to strengthen the paper:

First, the motivation for why standard clustering approaches fail to discover meaningful domains needs more concrete evidence. The paper states that "simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains" (line 77), but provides no experimental evidence to support this claim. The authors should include a simple experiment demonstrating that k-means clustering on appearance features (e.g., visual words) would indeed produce per-category clusters rather than domain-relevant clusters, and explicitly show how their method overcomes this limitation.

Second, the label prior constraint (LPC) is critical to the authors' formulation, but its necessity and impact are not sufficiently justified. The paper states that "within each domain, the class labels are distributed according to the prior distribution" (line 153), but doesn't explain why this constraint is necessary or what happens when it's removed. The authors should provide experimental results comparing their method with and without the LPC, and demonstrate that the constraint is not merely a technical convenience but essential to the approach's success.

Additionally, the authors should include baselines comparing their method to k-means clustering on appearance features and random partitioning of data into domains. These comparisons would help establish whether the performance gains are due to their novel domain discovery approach or simply because they're partitioning data into multiple domains rather than using a single dataset.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its theoretical underpinnings. The evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates the practical value of the approach. The authors make a strong case for the significance of their work by showing that reshaping datasets into latent domains can substantially improve domain adaptation performance.

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions to address the concerns about the motivation for the approach and the necessity of the label prior constraint. The paper makes a valuable contribution to domain adaptation research, and the proposed method has clear potential for practical application. The authors' response to the concerns would be the key factor in determining the final decision.
