This paper aims to automatically identify latent domains within a training set, which is then utilized in a domain adaptation framework to enhance classification performance on a test set. The authors introduce a function that measures the difference between two feature vectors over a specified kernel. The objective is to partition the data points into domains such that the function is maximized across the set of points spanning each pair of domains. This problem is formulated as an integer programming problem with two constraints: each point is assigned to exactly one domain, and the distribution over class labels in each domain must match the input distribution over the entire point set. The problem is then relaxed to a continuous optimization over a quadratic cost with linear constraints. The number of domains is determined via cross-validation.

The approach is evaluated on two datasets: static images of [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset of [15]. Notably, improved performance is demonstrated over the domain adaptation approach of [19].

Strengths: The paper is well-written, and the approach appears to be novel, although I am not an expert in domain adaptation. The performance gains over [19] are also appreciated.

Weaknesses: Initially, I was inclined towards rejection, but after the rebuttal addressed my concerns, I now lean towards acceptance. My primary concerns were:

(i) The motivation behind this paper was unclear. The authors claim that "simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains" (line 77). However, evidence supporting this claim is lacking, and it is unclear how the model formulation in Section 2 addresses this issue.

(ii) The necessity of the second constraint ("label prior constraint") is also unclear. I would like to see the performance without this constraint and a comparison to a baseline where the data is partitioned using k-means clustering or unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al. ICCV '05) over the appearance vectors. Additionally, the performance when the dataset is randomly partitioned into equal sets should be evaluated.
