

This paper proposes a novel method for automatically discovering latent domains in visual datasets, addressing a critical limitation in domain adaptation research where datasets are typically treated as single domains rather than recognizing the multiple underlying domains (e.g., different viewpoints, lighting conditions) present within them. The authors introduce two key criteria for domain discovery: maximum distinctiveness (ensuring domains are maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (ensuring domains can support strong discriminative classifiers). They develop a nonparametric approach to optimize these criteria, along with a domain-wise cross-validation procedure to determine the optimal number of domains. The method is evaluated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, demonstrating significant improvements over standard approaches that treat entire datasets as single domains.

Originality and significance: The problem of automatically discovering latent domains is relatively unexplored, with only [20] addressing a similar problem. This paper makes a significant contribution by introducing a principled approach to domain discovery that better aligns with the underlying data structure. The significance is demonstrated through experiments showing that the identified domains consistently outperform both the original datasets and the domains discovered by [20]. The paper effectively argues that treating datasets as single domains is a weak proxy for the complex real-world conditions affecting visual data, making this work highly relevant to the computer vision community.

Quality: The technical approach is well-motivated and the optimization procedure is logically structured. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness through thorough experiments on multiple datasets and tasks. However, the integration of the two domain criteria could be improved: maximum learnability is primarily used as a post-hoc validation score to determine the number of domains rather than being integrated directly into the domain discovery process. A more integrated approach would better reflect the authors' stated goal of finding domains that simultaneously satisfy both criteria. The optimization method (section 2) would benefit from a clearer explanation of how closely it solves the original problem (2)+(3).

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, though it would benefit from additional visualizations to illustrate the discovered domains and their relationship to underlying factors like viewpoint and lighting. Section 4.3 on reshaping test datasets is particularly confusing and would be greatly improved with a diagram or more explicit explanation of the conditional reshaping process. The paper also lacks sufficient detail about the image representation method, which is critical to the experimental results.

Experimental details: The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines, but the image representation method is outdated. Using a simple bag-of-visual-words with only 800 codebook entries is insufficient for modern computer vision standards. The authors should adopt a more sophisticated representation, such as a spatial pyramid of bag-of-visual-words with dense SURF features or Fisher vectors, as suggested by the references to Zhang (IJCV 2006), Jurie (CVPR 2005), Lazebnik (CVPR 2006), and Perronin (ECCV 2010). The similarity measure used for histogram comparisons is also not specified, though it's implied to be X^2 or an intersection kernel (Euclidean distance would be inappropriate for histogram comparisons).

Strengths:
- Novel problem formulation addressing a critical gap in domain adaptation research
- Strong empirical results showing consistent improvements across multiple tasks
- Clear motivation for the two domain criteria
- Practical utility demonstrated through multiple application domains

Weaknesses:
- Limited integration of the two domain criteria in the optimization process
- Outdated image representation method that undermines the significance of the results
- Insufficient visualizations to illustrate the discovered domains and their properties
- Section 4.3 on test set reshaping is confusingly presented

Overall, this paper makes an important contribution to domain adaptation by addressing the under-explored problem of latent domain discovery. The method is well-motivated, the results are compelling, and the work has clear practical implications. While the paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding the image representation, the core contribution is significant and advances the field. The work represents a valuable step toward more robust domain adaptation methods that better account for the complex structure of visual data. I recommend acceptance.
