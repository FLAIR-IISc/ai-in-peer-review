- Paper tackles domain adaptation by discovering latent domains within datasets rather than treating each dataset as a single domain.  
- Core method driven by two principles: Maximum Distinctiveness (domains are statistically different) and Maximum Learnability (domains allow strong discriminative classifiers).  
- Distinctiveness measured with a non-parametric kernel-based metric; learnability used via domain-wise cross-validation to choose the number of domains.  
- Empirical results show discovered domains used as sources improve object and action recognition versus treating datasets monolithically.  
- Novel and significant conceptual contribution: questions the common "one dataset = one domain" assumption.  
- Guiding principles are intuitive and provide sensible regularization for domain discovery.  
- Method outperforms related work (Hoffman et al. [20]) in experiments, validating effectiveness.  
- Weak integration: distinctiveness and learnability are optimized separately; no unified objective jointly optimizing both.  
- Experimental weakness: uses outdated BoW SURF representation (800 words), raising concern that discovered domains may reflect feature artifacts.  
- Suggest re-evaluation with stronger features (SPM, Fisher Vectors) and histogram-appropriate distance metrics (Chi-squared, intersection).  
- Optimization clarity lacking: relaxes NP-hard integer program to a continuous QP with little discussion on approximation quality or sensitivity.  
- Conditional reshaping of test sets (Section 4.3) is under-explained and implies a transductive setting that should be explicitly stated and justified.  
- Paper clarity could be improved with a pipeline figure and clearer explanation of conditional reshaping; some details relegated to supplementary material.  
- Suggestion to explore incorporating learnability directly into the optimization objective to avoid discovering distinct but unlearnable domains.
