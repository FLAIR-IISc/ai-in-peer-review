Summary
The paper proposes a convex, relaxation-based framework for partitioning data into K latent domains such that (i) each domain has a similar semantic class distribution and (ii) domains are maximally distinct in a reproducing kernel Hilbert space (via pairwise mean differences). The number of domains, K, is determined by maximizing average learnability within each latent domain—i.e., how well a classifier trained on that domain performs—thereby ensuring each domain supports effective class discrimination.

Strengths
- A principled convex formulation for latent domain discovery that explicitly controls class-label distributions across domains, mitigating trivial partitions dominated by single classes.
- Use of RKHS mean differences to promote inter-domain distinctiveness is well motivated and aligns with kernel mean embeddings.
- A practical criterion for selecting K based on learnability, which ties domain discovery to downstream classification quality and helps ensure per-domain sample sufficiency.
- Empirical results show reasonable gains over baselines, and qualitative visualizations aid interpretation of the discovered domains.

Concerns and Limitations
- Assumption of similar class distributions across latent domains: While this prevents degenerate, class-specific clusters, it may be restrictive for tasks where nuisance factors are not evenly present across classes. For example, if pose defines domains in animal recognition (e.g., horses vs. cows), both classes may appear in left/right standing poses, but cows are more frequently depicted sitting than horses. Enforcing similar class distributions across domains could suppress meaningful domain variation and bias the discovery process.
- Conceptual clarity: The manuscript does not clearly distinguish dataset, domain, and latent domain, and the terms appear to be used interchangeably in places (e.g., S_i referred to as both datasets and source domains). This ambiguity makes it difficult to interpret the setup and results, especially in Section 4.2. Clear, formal definitions with illustrative examples are needed early in the paper and should be referenced consistently throughout.
- Notation issues: The quantity max_k r(U_k, B) is used in Eq. 7 but not defined. All symbols in the objective should be introduced and explained in the main text.
- Supervision claim: On line 074, the problem is described as unsupervised, yet the method relies on semantic class labels to enforce distributional constraints and to assess learnability. This is at least semi-supervised or weakly supervised; the claims about supervision should be revised to avoid misleading readers.
- Experimental design: While the validation is generally adequate and shows improvements over baselines, the rationale for the chosen source–target datasets is unclear. A more systematic protocol—e.g., leave-one-dataset-out adaptation across all available sources—would strengthen the evidence and reduce concerns about selection bias. Reporting sensitivity to K and to kernel choice would also help assess robustness.

Requests for Clarification and Suggestions
- Definitions and setup:
  - Provide precise definitions of dataset, (source/target) domain, and latent domain, with a small running example clarifying how S_i relates to each concept.
  - Clearly describe the training and evaluation protocol in Section 4.2, including how latent domains are discovered (from which data, with what labels), how classifiers are trained per domain, and how target performance is measured.
- Objective and notation:
  - Define r(U_k, B) and any other undefined terms; explain the role of B and how it is learned or set.
  - Explicitly state how pairwise mean differences in RKHS are computed (e.g., relation to MMD) and any normalization used to balance domain sizes.
- Model assumptions and ablations:
  - Discuss when enforcing similar class distributions is appropriate and when it may hinder discovery (e.g., class-specific nuisance prevalence). Consider an ablation that relaxes or weights the class-distribution regularizer, and report its effect on both domain distinctiveness and downstream accuracy.
  - Analyze sensitivity to K and describe the stability of the learnability-based selection across random initializations or folds.
  - Report sensitivity to kernel type and hyperparameters; justify the kernel choices.
- Experimental protocol:
  - Justify the selection of source and target datasets. If feasible, include leave-one-dataset-out evaluations to demonstrate generality.
  - Clarify whether qualitative examples are representative and how they were selected.
- Positioning and claims:
  - Situate the work relative to prior latent domain discovery approaches, emphasizing what is novel about the convex relaxation and label-distribution control.
  - Calibrate the supervision claim to reflect the use of class labels in both the discovery and selection criteria.

Minor Comments
- Improve terminology consistency for “dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain.”
- Proofread for small grammatical issues (e.g., “the some concepts…”).
- Ensure all equations include defined terms and that symbols are used consistently.

Overall Assessment
This is a thoughtful and technically solid approach to latent domain discovery that usefully couples a convex relaxation with label-distribution control and a learnability-based selection of K. The empirical gains are encouraging, and the qualitative analyses are informative. However, key conceptual definitions are currently unclear, a notation gap exists (Eq. 7), the supervision claim is overstated, and the evaluation protocol would benefit from a more systematic design. With clarifications and stronger experimental justification—especially around the class-distribution assumption and dataset selection—the paper would make a meaningful contribution.
