Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the problem of discovering latent domains within visual datasets to improve domain adaptation. The key idea is to “reshape” one or multiple datasets into K latent domains that are (i) maximally distinctive in distribution and (ii) maximally learnable for downstream classification. Distinctiveness is enforced by maximizing the sum of pairwise distributional discrepancies between domains measured via kernel mean embeddings (i.e., squared MMD) in an RKHS. Learnability is enforced by selecting K through a domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure that evaluates how well a classifier can be trained within each candidate domain and averages the accuracy across domains.

The optimization includes a “label prior constraint” (LPC) to maintain similar class-label proportions across discovered domains, avoiding trivial clusters dominated by single categories. The discrete assignment is relaxed into a continuous quadratic program. The method is evaluated on Office-Caltech image recognition (10 classes) and IXMAS multi-view action recognition, with comparisons to the explicit parametric latent domain discovery of Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12). The authors also propose conditional reshaping of test sets to align latent test domains with training domains.

The paper is well positioned within domain adaptation research (e.g., Saenko et al. ECCV’10; Gopalan et al. ICCV’11; Gong et al. CVPR’12) and kernel two-sample testing (Gretton et al. NIPS’07). Compared to Hoffman et al.’s GMM-based discovery, the nonparametric formulation and the learnability-based model selection are novel and compelling.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation of “maximum distinctiveness” using MMD, avoiding parametric assumptions on domain distributions.
- Practical, data-driven determination of K via DWCV that guards against overly fine partitions with poor learnability.
- Convincing empirical evidence on object and action datasets, including conditional reshaping of test sets, with improvements over original dataset/domain splits and a prior latent domain discovery method.
- Qualitative visualizations that help interpret the discovered factors (pose, background, appearance).

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
- Assumption behind LPC: Enforcing similar class-label proportions across domains may be inappropriate when latent factors correlate with label frequency (e.g., actions or animal poses that occur unevenly across classes). This constraint could suppress valid domain structure.
- Clarity and notation: The manuscript sometimes conflates dataset, domain, and latent domain. Section 4.2 in particular uses S both as “datasets” and “source domains,” which can confuse the reader. The quantity r(Uk → B) (adaptation accuracy from latent domain Uk to target B) is used but not explicitly defined procedurally (classifier type, adaptation method, hyperparameters).
- Optimization details: The objective is a maximization of a convex quadratic under linear constraints (which is generally nonconvex/NP-hard). The paper should clarify solver choice, initialization, stopping criteria, and practical runtime/memory costs (the M×M kernel matrix can be large). Provide sensitivity to kernel bandwidth and δ in LPC.
- Evaluation design: The choice of source/target splits is not fully motivated. A leave-one-dataset-out protocol (standard in Office-Caltech) or exhaustive source/target rotations would strengthen claims. KMeans/random partitions are mentioned as weak, but results are omitted; they should be reported (at least in the supplement) to complete the empirical picture.
- The “unsupervised” phrasing: The method leverages labels directly via LPC and DWCV. Describing the discovery as unsupervised may be misleading; it is supervised (or semi-supervised) with respect to source labels.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with appropriate use of kernel mean embeddings. However, the relaxation’s nonconvexity and lack of theoretical guarantees (e.g., generalization bounds connecting TDD and target risk) limit the theoretical depth. Practical performance is solid. Additional ablations—MMD-only vs. MMD+LPC, impact of DWCV vs. fixed K—would help isolate contributions.

Clarity
The writing is generally clear, but key concepts and notation require tightening:
- Define “dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain” explicitly and use consistently through the paper.
- In Section 2, be precise about r(A → B), how adaptation is performed (e.g., geodesic flow kernel), and how accuracies are computed.
- In Section 4.2, restate r(Uk → Tl) before Eq. (6) and define the “MATCHING” strategy (e.g., selecting the source with minimal MMD to the target).
- Explain constraints in Eq. (5), especially 1/M ≤ Σk βmk ≤ 1/C; motivate and check feasibility when C is large or classes are imbalanced.

Originality
The nonparametric domain discovery objective and the DWCV criterion for choosing K are novel relative to prior latent domain discovery (Hoffman et al., ECCV’12). Conditional reshaping of test sets is also a valuable addition.

Significance
Discovering latent domains addresses a real pain point in visual adaptation where “dataset = domain” is often a poor proxy. The demonstrated gains and the interpretability of discovered factors suggest the approach could be adopted by practitioners, though scalability and dependence on LPC should be addressed.

Specific suggestions
- Replace the hard LPC with a soft regularizer that penalizes deviations from the global class prior, allowing real shifts when they exist. Analyze sensitivity to class imbalance.
- Provide algorithmic details: solver (e.g., projected gradient, DC programming), initialization (random vs. k-means warm start), runtime/memory, and scalability strategies (e.g., Nyström/random feature approximations to handle large M).
- Add ablations: without LPC; varying δ; effect of kernel bandwidth; DWCV vs. fixed K; KMeans/random partitions (include numbers).
- Strengthen evaluation with leave-one-dataset-out splits and additional multi-source adaptation baselines (e.g., TCA, Landmarks) to complement geodesic flow.
- Move the essential parts of conditional test reshaping from the supplement into the main text (objective, constraints), and clarify how matching is done without target labels.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem (latent domain discovery) with a principled, nonparametric formulation.
- Practical and effective selection of K via learnability.
- Consistent empirical improvements and insightful qualitative analysis.

Cons for acceptance
- LPC assumption may be too restrictive in realistic settings with label–factor correlations.
- Some ambiguity in notation and definitions; missing methodological specifics.
- Limited discussion of optimization hardness, scalability, and sensitivity.

Overall recommendation
A promising and generally well-executed paper with clear contributions beyond prior work on latent domain discovery. With clarifications to definitions, more transparent optimization details, and a few additional ablations/baselines, it would be a strong contribution. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the clarity and evaluation concerns above.
