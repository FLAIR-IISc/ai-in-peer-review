This work introduces a convex framework to partition a dataset into K subsets, referred to as latent domains. The objective is to ensure that each domain maintains a similar semantic class distribution while being maximally distinct from the others. This distinctiveness is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences between the subsets in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). The framework also determines the optimal number of domains, K, by maximizing the average "learnability," which measures how well a classifier can be trained on each individual latent domain.

A potential limitation of the proposed method is its core assumption that class distributions should be similar across latent domains. While this constraint effectively prevents the trivial solution of each domain being dominated by a single class, it may be too restrictive for certain tasks. Consider an animal classification problem where latent domains correspond to poses (e.g., standing vs. sitting). The class distribution of horses and cows might be similar for a "standing" domain, but vastly different for a "sitting" domain, as cows are often depicted sitting while horses are not.

Despite the pre-existence of latent domain discovery methods, the paper's primary novelty is the idea of controlling class label distributions within a (relaxed) convex framework to guide the identification of these domains. Another commendable aspect is the strategy for selecting K by assessing the quality of classification within each potential domain. This approach ensures that the discovered domains are meaningful and contain sufficient per-class samples for effective learning and generalization.

The manuscript could be improved by clarifying the distinction between the concepts of "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain," as their usage is sometimes confusing. Precise definitions, potentially with examples, would be beneficial. Section 4.2, in particular, requires clarification on the experimental setup, where "dataset" and "source domain" (Si) seem to be used interchangeably. Additionally, the term `maxk r(U_k,B)` in Equation 7 is used without a prior definition.

The experimental validation is adequate, and the results show a reasonable improvement over the selected baselines. However, the rationale for choosing the specific source and target datasets for the experiments is unclear. An evaluation using a more standard protocol, such as leave-one-dataset-out adaptation, might offer a more compelling validation. The qualitative results are a helpful addition, providing visual insight into the algorithm's behavior.

Finally, characterizing the problem as "unsupervised" (line 074) may be misleading, as the proposed method relies on semantic class labels. The main technical contribution—the formulation of latent domain discovery with controlled class distributions in a (relaxed) convex framework—is notable. However, the paper's impact would be greater if the conceptual definitions and experimental validation were further clarified.
