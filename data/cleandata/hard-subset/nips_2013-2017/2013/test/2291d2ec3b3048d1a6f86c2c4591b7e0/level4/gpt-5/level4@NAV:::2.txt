Review- This paper proposes a novel approach to domain adaptation for computer vision datasets, which typically vary along several factors (e.g., viewpoint, illumination, clutter, compression). Rather than equating domains with datasets—thereby conflating these factors—the method automatically partitions the union of images from all datasets into domains. The partitioning is guided by two principles: encouraging separation between domains in feature space, and ensuring that each domain supports training a strong discriminative classifier for the original class labels (not for domain discrimination). This increases the likelihood that the discovered domains align with underlying factors rather than with dataset boundaries.

Originality and significance:
There is relatively little prior work on automatically defining domains, and this paper offers a promising idea for automatically discovering useful domains that can enable better classifiers for the original task.
On the downside, I am not fully persuaded by the optimization strategy (section 2), as it is unclear how closely it addresses the initial objective (2)+(3). Furthermore, the two guiding criteria are not yet tightly integrated: the maximal learnability criterion is used primarily as a wrapper around the maximal separability criterion to choose the number of domains. In effect, it serves as a post-hoc validation score assessing the quality of a partition obtained for a fixed number of domains, while the partition itself is determined solely by separability. A more unified approach would directly optimize a single objective that incorporates both criteria to produce the partition.
Despite these issues—perhaps reflecting the exploratory nature of this research direction—I believe the paper moves the field in a positive direction and should be accepted.

Quality and clarity:
The paper is well written but would benefit from figures to illustrate the main concepts.

Experiments:
On the positive side, the experiments show a clear advantage for the domains discovered by the proposed method over treating datasets as domains, and over the recent domain discovery approach [19] (sec. 4.2).
The idea of incorporating the test set into the “reshaping” process is interesting but not explained clearly (sec. 4.3). This appears to correspond to an imputation scenario (i.e., all test data available at once), which should be stated explicitly.
On the negative side, the image descriptor is quite simple and outdated: a single bag-of-words over sparse SURF features for the whole image, with only 800 codebook entries. This is weak by current standards. I recommend using a spatial pyramid bag-of-words on dense SURF features. It is also unclear which similarity measure is used to compare image histograms—ideally X^2 or an intersection kernel; Euclidean distance is not suitable for histograms. As a next step, a Fisher Vector representation could further improve performance and bring the image representation up to modern practice. The following references may help: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010.

Overall, the paper presents interesting, novel ideas on an important problem and achieves good results. There is room for improvement, particularly in the choice of image representation.
