This paper aims to automatically discover latent domains in the training set and then use them within a domain adaptation framework to improve test-time classification. The authors define a kernel-induced dissimilarity between feature vectors and seek a partition of the data into domains that maximizes this measure across every pair of domains. They formulate the partitioning as an integer program with two constraints: (1) each point must be assigned to exactly one domain, and (2) the class-label distribution within each domain must match the global label distribution over the entire dataset. They relax this to a continuous optimization with a quadratic objective and linear constraints, and select the number of domains via cross-validation.

The approach is evaluated on two datasets: static images from [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset [15]. A highlight is the improved performance over the domain adaptation method of [19].

Positives: The paper is clearly written and, to the best of my knowledge, the approach is novel (though I am not a domain adaptation expert). The performance gains over [19] are appreciated.

Negatives: At this point I slightly lean toward reject. I have two main concerns that I would like to see addressed in the rebuttal, which could change my score:
(i) The motivation is not fully clear. On line 77, the paper claims that “simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains.” What evidence supports this claim? And how does the formulation in Section 2 avoid this issue—i.e., what enforces that the learned domains are not simply per-category clusters?
(ii) Relatedly, on line 154, why is the second (“label prior”) constraint necessary? What happens to performance without it? A baseline that partitions data using k-means clustering or unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al., ICCV ’05) on the appearance vectors should be included. Also, what is the performance when the dataset is randomly partitioned into equal-sized subsets?
