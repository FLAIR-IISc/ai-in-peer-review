- Proposes automatic discovery of K latent domains by reshaping datasets to maximize distinctiveness and learnability.  
- Distinctiveness measured via pairwise distribution differences in an RKHS; learnability used to select K through cross-validation.  
- Novel non-parametric, convex formulation that enforces similar class distributions within each latent domain.  
- Builds on prior domain adaptation work but shifts focus from how to adapt to what constitutes a domain.  
- Differs from Hoffman et al. by avoiding parametric Gaussian mixture models and adding a class-distribution constraint.  
- Enforcing similar class distributions is a notable contribution that encourages generalizable domains.  
- The class-distribution constraint may be restrictive or inappropriate for tasks with inherent class–domain correlations (e.g., pose-based animal classification).  
- Terminology (dataset vs. domain vs. latent domain) is used inconsistently and needs clearer definitions and examples.  
- Section 4.2 is unclear about the use of “dataset” and “domain,” and equation (7) references an undefined variable max_k r(U_k,B).  
- Experimental results show improved performance on object and action recognition and useful qualitative visualizations.  
- Rationale for chosen source/target datasets is not fully justified; a more systematic evaluation (e.g., leave-one-dataset-out) is recommended.  
- Characterizing the problem as “unsupervised” is misleading given reliance on semantic class labels.  
- Overall strengths: novel problem framing, technically sound convex formulation, and demonstrated empirical gains.  
- Overall weaknesses: restrictive class-distribution assumption, clarity issues in exposition and experimental choices, and the questionable unsupervised claim.
