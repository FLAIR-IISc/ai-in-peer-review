- Paper goal: automatically discover latent training-set domains and use them in a domain-adaptation framework to improve test-time classification.  
- Method: define a kernel-based discrepancy between feature vectors and find a partition that maximizes inter-domain discrepancy via an integer program with two constraints.  
- Constraints/formulation: (1) each instance assigned to exactly one domain; (2) per-domain label distribution matches global label prior; relaxed to continuous quadratic optimization and domain count chosen by cross-validation.  
- Experiments: evaluated on a static image dataset and IXMAS multi-view action dataset, showing improvements over prior domain-adaptation method [19].  
- Strength: clearly written and (apparently) novel; reported performance gains over [19] are encouraging.  
- Main concern (i) Motivation: claim that appearance-based clustering yields per-category domains lacks empirical/theoretical support and needs explanation of how proposed method prevents category collapse.  
- Main concern (ii) Label-prior constraint: necessity unclear—reviewer requests ablation without this constraint and baselines using k-means, unsupervised object discovery, and random splits.  
- Clarification requests: define “maximally different in distribution” precisely (which variables/features and which kernel/statistic).  
- Technical queries: does β_{mk} already lie on a simplex? reconcile differing constraint formulations around lines 153 vs 182.  
- Proof/request: justify or prove the bound stated on line 215.  
- Implementation details requested: geodesic flow kernel—reimplemented or public code, parameters, deviations.  
- Analysis request: deeper examination of why the method outperforms baselines and what systematic failure modes of baselines are avoided.  
- Minor corrections/suggestions: typo in Eq. (1) (M'k should be Mk'), and consider citing Torralba & Efros (2011) on dataset bias.  
- Post-rebuttal note: rebuttal satisfactorily addressed motivation and label-prior concerns; reviewer now leans slightly toward acceptance.
