

This paper introduces a novel approach to domain adaptation in computer vision, addressing the common issue of treating domains as synonymous with datasets—which conflates multiple underlying aspects (e.g., viewpoint, illumination, clutter, compression). Instead, the authors propose an automatic method to partition all images across datasets into meaningful domains based on feature space structure. The partitioner operates under two principles: (1) creating distinct domains in feature space, and (2) ensuring each domain supports training a strong discriminative classifier for the original task (not for domain separation). This strategy is more likely to align with true underlying aspects rather than arbitrary dataset boundaries.  

**Originality and Significance:**  
The work addresses a notable gap in automatically defining domains, offering a promising direction for discovering useful domain structures to improve classifier performance. While the core idea is valuable, the proposed optimization method (Section 2) lacks clarity on how closely it solves the combined objective (2)+(3). Critically, the two driving criteria are not integrated: the maximal learnability criterion functions merely as a post-hoc validation metric to select the number of domains, while the partitioning itself relies solely on maximal separability. An integrated approach would directly optimize a unified objective combining both criteria, rather than treating learnability as a secondary check. These limitations likely reflect the exploratory nature of the field, but they warrant clarification.  

**Quality and Clarity:**  
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, though it omits illustrative figures to demonstrate the domain partitioning concept.  

**Experiments:**  
The results (Section 4.2) demonstrate a significant advantage of the proposed domains over using datasets as domains and over a recent domain discovery method [19]. However, the inclusion of the test set in the "reshaping" process (Section 4.3) is unclear and assumes an imputation setting (all test data available simultaneously), which should be explicitly stated.  

**Image Representation:**  
A major weakness lies in the image descriptor: a single bag-of-words (BoW) using sparse SURF features (800 codebook entries) for the entire image. This is quite basic and outdated by modern standards. I recommend replacing it with a spatial pyramid BoW computed from dense SURF features. Additionally, the similarity measure for comparing descriptors is unspecified—Euclidean distance is unsuitable for histogram comparisons, so an X² or intersection kernel must be used. For further improvement, Fisher Vectors (e.g., as in Zhang et al., IJCV 2006; Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006; Perronin et al., ECCV 2010) would modernize the representation. The current descriptor significantly undermines the experimental rigor.  

**Overall:**  
The paper presents a compelling, novel perspective on domain discovery for computer vision and achieves strong empirical results. While the image representation and optimization method require substantial revision, the core idea is a meaningful contribution. With improvements to the descriptor and clearer integration of criteria, this work could become a solid foundation for future research. I recommend acceptance pending these revisions.
