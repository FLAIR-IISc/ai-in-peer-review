### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a method to automatically discover latent domains within a training dataset. The discovered domains are then used within a domain adaptation framework to improve classification performance on a separate test set. The core of the method is a function that measures the dissimilarity between pairs of domains, defined over feature vectors using a specified kernel. The objective is to partition the data points into a set of domains that maximizes this total inter-domain dissimilarity.

This objective is formulated as an integer programming problem with two key constraints: 1) each data point must be assigned to exactly one domain, and 2) the class label distribution within each discovered domain must match the global class label distribution of the entire dataset (the "label prior constraint"). To make the problem tractable, the integer program is relaxed into a continuous quadratic optimization problem with linear constraints. The optimal number of domains is determined empirically via cross-validation. The approach is validated on two datasets, showing improved performance over the domain adaptation method of Gong et al. [19].

### **Strengths**

The paper is well-written and the proposed method is clearly explained. To my knowledge, the approach of automatically discovering latent domains by maximizing a dissimilarity metric, constrained by label priors, is a novel contribution to the domain adaptation literature. The empirical results are promising, demonstrating a clear performance improvement over the strong baseline of [19], which is a significant result.

### **Initial Concerns (Addressed in Rebuttal)**

I initially leaned toward rejection due to two primary concerns. However, the authors' rebuttal has satisfactorily addressed these points, and I have consequently raised my score. For the benefit of the authors and the area chair, I will outline my original concerns here.

1.  **Motivation and Problem Formulation:** My first concern was about the core motivation presented on line 77, which argues that "simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains." I was unsure what evidence supported this claim and, more importantly, how the specific formulation in Section 2—particularly the constraints—was designed to prevent this issue. The authors' response clarified the intuition behind this claim and explained how the label prior constraint directly forces the model to find cross-category domains rather than simply rediscovering the object classes themselves.

2.  **Necessity of the Label Prior Constraint:** Related to the first point, I questioned the necessity of the label prior constraint (line 154). I felt the paper would be significantly strengthened by an ablation study showing performance without this constraint. I also suggested that comparisons to simpler baselines would be valuable, such as partitioning the data via k-means clustering on appearance features, using an unsupervised object discovery method, or even using a random partitioning. The rebuttal provided a compelling justification for this constraint and its role in achieving the paper's goal, which has resolved my concern.

### **Conclusion and Recommendation**

The authors' rebuttal has successfully addressed my main reservations regarding the paper's motivation and the justification for the label prior constraint. These clarifications have strengthened my confidence in the contribution of the work. Therefore, I have updated my score and now recommend acceptance.

### **Additional Comments for the Authors**

Below are some minor points and suggestions that could further improve the camera-ready version of the paper:

*   **Clarification of "Distribution" (Line 82):** The phrase "maximally different in distribution from each other" is used throughout the paper. Please clarify precisely what this refers to. Is it the distribution over feature values, or something else?
*   **Constraint on \beta_{mk} (Line 166):** As formulated, it appears that the variable \beta_{mk} is already constrained to a simplex by the other constraints in the problem. Is the explicit mention of it living on a simplex for clarity, or is it a separate, non-redundant constraint?
*   **Consistency of Constraints (Line 182):** The constraints presented around line 182 seem different from those introduced starting on line 153. Please clarify the relationship between these two formulations.
*   **Justification for Bound (Line 215):** The paper introduces a bound on this line. Please provide a brief justification or a pointer to a proof for this claim.
*   **Implementation Details (Line 289):** For reproducibility, please provide more details on the use of the geodesic flow kernel [4]. Was a publicly available implementation used, or was it a re-implementation?
*   **Qualitative Analysis (Lines 313/340):** The quantitative results are strong. To provide deeper insight into *why* the proposed method works better, it would be highly beneficial to include some qualitative analysis. For example, showing some example images from the discovered domains or highlighting systematic failure cases of the baseline methods that your approach overcomes would greatly strengthen the experimental section.
*   **Typo in Eq (1):** M'k should likely be M_k'.
*   **Suggested Citation:** You may find the following paper on dataset bias relevant to your work:
    *   A. Torralba, A. Efros. "Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias." CVPR 2011.
