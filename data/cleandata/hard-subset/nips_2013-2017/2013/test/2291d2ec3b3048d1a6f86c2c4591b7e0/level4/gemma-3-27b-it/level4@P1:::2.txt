This paper presents a new technique for domain adaptation in computer vision, addressing the common issue of distribution mismatches between training and testing data. Instead of treating entire datasets as single domains – which often conflates underlying factors like viewpoint, illumination, and clutter – the proposed approach automatically partitions the combined dataset into more meaningful domains. This partitioning is guided by two key principles: maximizing the distinctiveness between domains in feature space, and ensuring that each domain allows for the training of a strong discriminative classifier for the original task. This approach aims to better capture the underlying data variations compared to simply equating datasets with domains.

**Originality and Significance:**

The work addresses an important and often overlooked aspect of domain adaptation – the definition of domains themselves. There is limited research on automatically discovering useful domains, and this paper presents a compelling idea for achieving this. The approach has the potential to improve the performance of domain adaptation techniques by creating more coherent and discriminative domains.

On the downside, the optimization method described in section 2 is not entirely convincing. It’s unclear how closely the implemented optimization solves the original problem defined by equations (2) and (3). Furthermore, the two guiding criteria – maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability – are not fully integrated. The learnability criterion currently acts as a post-hoc validation step for determining the optimal number of domains, rather than being directly incorporated into the partitioning process. A more integrated approach would aim to maximize a combined objective function encompassing both criteria.

Despite these limitations, which are perhaps inherent to the exploratory nature of this work, I believe the paper represents a valuable step forward for the community and merits acceptance.

**Quality and Clarity:**

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts. However, it would benefit from illustrative figures to visually demonstrate the domain discovery process and the characteristics of the identified domains.

**Experiments:**

The experimental results demonstrate a significant advantage of using the automatically discovered domains compared to treating datasets as domains, and outperform the recent domain discovery technique presented in [20] (Section 4.2).

The idea of incorporating the test set into the domain reshaping process is intriguing, but the explanation in section 4.3 is somewhat unclear. It’s also important to note that this approach assumes an imputation setting – that all test data is available simultaneously – which should be explicitly stated. 

A potential limitation is the relatively simple and somewhat outdated image descriptor used: a single bag-of-words representation based on sparse SURF features, with only 800 codebook entries. Modern approaches often employ more sophisticated representations. I recommend considering a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words computed on dense SURF features. Additionally, it would be beneficial to clarify the similarity measure used for comparing image descriptors; the Euclidean distance may not be appropriate for histograms, and alternatives like the Chi-squared distance or an intersection kernel should be considered. A Fisher Vector representation could also be explored to further improve performance. Relevant papers include: Zhang IJCV 2006, Jurie CVPR 2005, Lazebnik CVPR 2006, and Perronin ECCV 2010.

**Overall:**

The paper presents novel and promising ideas for addressing an important problem in domain adaptation. The results are encouraging, and despite some areas for improvement – particularly regarding the image representation and the integration of the optimization criteria – I believe the paper makes a significant contribution and should be accepted.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to automatic domain discovery.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating improvement over baseline methods.
*   Addresses a key limitation in current domain adaptation approaches.
*   Clear and well-written paper.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Optimization method is not fully justified.
*   Criteria for domain discovery are not fully integrated.
*   Image representation is relatively simple and could be improved.
*   Lack of visual illustrations.
