This paper presents a new technique for domain adaptation in computer vision. It addresses the problem that data distributions often shift due to various underlying factors such as viewpoint, illumination, and background clutter. The authors argue that the common practice of equating entire datasets with domains is a poor proxy, as individual datasets can themselves be heterogeneous mixtures of these factors.

The core contribution is a method to automatically discover and partition a collection of images into a set of latent domains. This partitioning is guided by two principles:
1.  **Maximum Distinctiveness**: The discovered domains should be maximally separable from each other in the feature space.
2.  **Maximum Learnability**: A strong discriminative classifier (for the original object classes) should be learnable from the data within each discovered domain.
The goal is to produce a domain partitioning that better reflects the true underlying data-generating factors, thereby enabling more effective domain adaptation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles the important and relatively unexplored problem of automatically discovering latent domains, moving beyond the simplistic assumption that datasets are domains. This "reshaping" of datasets is a valuable and novel contribution to the field of domain adaptation.
*   **Principled Formulation:** The two guiding principles for domain discovery—maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability—are well-motivated and intuitive. They provide a principled framework for identifying domains that are both statistically different and useful for downstream classification tasks.
*   **Strong Experimental Results:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the benefits of the proposed method. The discovered domains lead to significantly better adaptation performance compared to both using the original datasets as domains and the recent competing method of Hoffman et al. [20].

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Integration of Criteria:** The two core principles, distinctiveness and learnability, are not fully integrated into a unified optimization objective. The "maximum distinctiveness" criterion is used to generate domain partitions for a fixed number of domains K, while "maximum learnability" is used subsequently as a wrapper (via domain-wise cross-validation) to select the optimal K. This feels more like a post-hoc validation step rather than a guiding principle for the partitioning itself. A more compelling approach would be to formulate a single objective function that jointly optimizes for both criteria.
*   **Optimization Relaxation:** The paper formulates the domain assignment problem as an integer program (Eq. 2 + 3) and then relaxes it into a continuous optimization problem (Eq. 4) to make it tractable. While this is a standard technique, the paper does not discuss the quality of this relaxation. It is unclear how closely the solution to the relaxed problem approximates the solution to the original NP-hard problem.
*   **Outdated Image Features:** The experimental validation relies on a simple and somewhat outdated image representation: a single bag-of-words histogram over sparse SURF features with an 800-word codebook. The strength of the paper's claims would be significantly enhanced by using more powerful, modern features.
    *   I strongly recommend the authors use a stronger baseline, such as a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words computed on dense features (e.g., Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006).
    *   The paper does not specify which similarity measure was used to compare these histograms. For histograms, metrics like the Chi-squared distance or an intersection kernel are far more appropriate than the L2 (Euclidean) distance.
    *   To bring the work in line with modern systems, the authors could further strengthen their results by using representations like Fisher Vectors (e.g., Perronin et al., ECCV 2010).
*   **Clarity on Test Set Reshaping:** The idea of reshaping the test set (Section 4.3) is interesting but could be explained more clearly. This procedure assumes that the entire unlabeled test set is available at once, which corresponds to a transductive learning setting. This important assumption should be stated explicitly.
*   **Lack of Illustrations:** While the paper is well-written, it would benefit from figures that visually illustrate the core concepts, for example, by showing the feature space distribution before and after domain discovery.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses noted above, this paper introduces a novel and valuable perspective on domain adaptation. The core idea is compelling, the formulation is interesting, and the initial results are promising. This work represents a solid step in a new and important direction for the community. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors address the concerns raised—particularly regarding the choice of image representation—to further strengthen the paper.
