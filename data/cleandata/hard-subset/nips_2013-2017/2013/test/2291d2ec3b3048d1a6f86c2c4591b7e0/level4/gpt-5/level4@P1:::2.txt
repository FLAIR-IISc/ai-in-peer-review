Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses a key gap in visual domain adaptation: datasets are often treated as domains, even though visual data varies along multiple latent factors (viewpoint, illumination, background, resolution, etc.). The authors propose automatically discovering latent domains across one or more datasets by enforcing two properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different in distribution) and maximum learnability (each domain should support learning a strong discriminative classifier for the original task). Distinctiveness is operationalized nonparametrically via a kernel two-sample criterion, and domain assignment is obtained through a continuous relaxation with a label-prior constraint. Learnability is used via domain-wise cross-validation to select the number of domains. The method can also conditionally reshape heterogeneous test sets to match discovered training domains. Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) show gains over treating datasets as domains and over a recent latent-domain method (Hoffman et al., ECCV 2012), and the approach can be combined with multi-source adaptation (e.g., geodesic flow kernel).

Strengths:
- Clear motivation for decoupling datasets from domains in vision.
- Nonparametric formulation for domain distinctiveness avoids brittle mixture assumptions and is appropriate for complex visual factors.
- Empirical results demonstrate consistent benefit over baselines and over prior latent-domain discovery.
- Conditional reshaping of test sets is a compelling idea for heterogeneous targets.

Weaknesses:
- The optimization in Section 2 is not convincingly tied to the original discrete objective; it is unclear how closely the relaxation solves (2)+(3).
- The two criteria are not integrated: maximum learnability is used only as a wrapper to pick K, not within the partitioning objective. A joint formulation that optimizes a goal including both criteria would be stronger.
- Section 4.3 is not clearly presented; the test-set reshaping assumes access to all test data at once (an imputation/batch setting), which should be stated explicitly.
- The image representation is dated and weak by current standards (single bag-of-words on sparse SURF, 800-word codebook), and the similarity/distance used for histogram comparison is not specified.

Quality:
- The formulation of maximum distinctiveness via a kernel discrepancy is sound and appropriate.
- However, the relaxation and constraints deserve a more rigorous treatment. It is not clear what guarantees (if any) the relaxed solution offers with respect to the integer program, and how sensitive the solution is to the label-prior constraint and to δ.
- The learnability criterion is only used post hoc to choose K; it does not influence the partitioning itself. Integrating learnability into the objective would better align optimization with the end goal.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written.
- It would benefit from more figures illustrating domains discovered across datasets, and a schematic of the training/test reshaping pipeline.
- The conditional reshaping for test data (Section 4.3) should clearly state the assumption that all test data are available simultaneously, and distinguish this setting from online or streaming scenarios.

Originality:
- There is relatively little prior work on automatic latent-domain discovery in vision. Compared to Hoffman et al.’s Gaussian-mixture approach, the nonparametric strategy here is a notable alternative that avoids strong distributional assumptions. The framing via maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability is original and well motivated.

Significance:
- The empirical gains over using datasets as domains and over prior latent-domain discovery suggest this line of work can materially improve adaptation performance. The conditional test reshaping is especially relevant for heterogeneous targets (e.g., multi-view action recognition).

Experimental feedback and suggestions:
- Results show a clear advantage of the discovered domains over dataset-as-domain baselines and over the Hoffman et al. method (Section 4.2).
- The inclusion of the test set in reshaping is interesting but under-explained. Please clarify the optimization, matching procedure, and the batch (imputation-like) assumption.
- The feature representation is outdated. I recommend:
  - Using dense SURF with a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words rather than a single image-level histogram.
  - Employing a similarity appropriate for histograms (e.g., chi-squared or histogram intersection), as Euclidean distance is ill-suited for this purpose.
  - Considering Fisher Vectors to bring the representation in line with modern practice.
  Useful references: Zhang et al., IJCV 2006; Jurie and Triggs, CVPR 2005; Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006; Perronnin et al., ECCV 2010.

Pros for acceptance:
- Well-motivated problem and novel formulation with nonparametric distinctiveness.
- Consistent empirical improvements over strong baselines and prior latent-domain discovery.
- Practical value in both training and test reshaping scenarios.

Cons for acceptance:
- Optimization/relaxation not clearly connected to the original objective with constraints.
- Learnability used only to pick K; lack of a unified objective combining both criteria.
- Outdated features and unspecified histogram metric limit the strength and generality of the empirical evidence.

Recommendation:
Despite the noted shortcomings—some of which reflect the exploratory nature of this research direction—the paper advances an important problem with a thoughtful approach and promising results. I recommend acceptance, and encourage the authors to strengthen the optimization analysis, integrate learnability into the objective, clarify test-set reshaping, and upgrade the visual representation.
