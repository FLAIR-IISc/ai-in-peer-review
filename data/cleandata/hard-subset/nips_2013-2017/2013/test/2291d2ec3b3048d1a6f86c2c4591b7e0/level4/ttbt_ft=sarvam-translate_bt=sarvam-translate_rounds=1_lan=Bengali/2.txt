This paper presents a new technique for domain adaptation for computer vision datasets, which typically represent multiple aspects such as perspective, lighting, degree of clutter, compression, etc. Instead of directly comparing the domain with the dataset, which would mix those aspects together, the proposed technique automatically divides all sets of images in the dataset into domains. The partitioner is driven by two principles: creating separate domains in the feature space and building them in such a way that a good discriminative classifier can be trained on each of them (to classify the main classes, not to separate the domains). This technique is more likely to partition according to the underlying aspects than the dataset itself.

Basics and Significance:
Very little work has been done on automatically defining domains, and this paper proposes a good idea towards automatically discovering useful domains, which will help train a better classifier for the main problem.

The downside is that I am not entirely sure about the proposed optimization method (section 2), as it is not clear how it solves the main problem (2)+(3). Moreover, the two driving criteria proposed are not yet well-integrated: the maximal learnability criterion is only used as a 'wrapper' for the maximal separability criterion, to determine the number of domains. Basically, it acts as a post-hoc validation score that decides how good the learned domain partition is for a specific number of domains, but the partition is only based on the separability criterion. An integrated process would create a partition that maximizes some target function with both criteria.

Despite these shortcomings, which are probably due to the fact that such work is still in a rather exploratory phase, I believe this paper is a step in the right direction for the community and should be adopted.

Quality and Clarity:
The paper is well-written, but there is a lack of illustrations to illustrate the concepts presented.

Experiments:
The positive aspect is that the experiments show a significant advantage in using the proposed method for producing domains compared to using the dataset as a domain and the very recent domain discovery technique [19] (section 4.2).

The idea of including the test set in the 'reshaping' process is interesting, but not clearly presented (section 4.3). Also, it is consistent with an imputation setting (i.e., all test data are available at the same time), but it is not explicitly stated.

The downside is that the image descriptor used is very simple and old: a bag-of-words for the entire image and only 800 codebook entries. This is really weak nowadays. I suggest the authors use a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words calculated on the dense SURF feature. Also, it is not clear what kind of similarity measure is used to compare the image descriptors, hopefully X^2 or an intersection kernel? Euclidean distance histograms are not suitable for comparison. As a next step, a Fisher vector representation could help further and eventually bring the image representation in this paper to the level of modern systems. The following papers may help the authors: Zhang IJCVI 2006; Juri CVPIR 2005; Lazebnik CVPIR 2006; Perronin ICCV 2010.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting new idea on an important problem and achieves good results. The paper can be improved in several ways, especially in the area of the image representation used.
