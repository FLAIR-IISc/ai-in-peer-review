This paper introduces a new technique for domain adaptation in computer vision datasets, which typically vary along multiple factors (e.g., viewpoint, illumination, level of clutter, compression). Rather than equating each dataset with a domain—thereby conflating these factors—the method automatically partitions the full set of images across all datasets into domains. The partitioner is guided by two principles: creating well-separated domains in feature space, and ensuring that a strong discriminative classifier can be trained within each domain to predict the original class labels (not to separate domains). As a result, the partitions are more likely to reflect underlying factors of variation rather than dataset boundaries.

Originality and significance:
There has been little work on automatically defining domains, and this paper advances a promising idea for discovering useful domains that can support training better classifiers for the original task.
On the negative side, I am not fully convinced by the proposed optimization in Section 2; it is unclear how closely it solves the original problem defined by (2)+(3). Moreover, the two guiding criteria are not yet well integrated: the maximal learnability criterion is used only as a wrapper around the maximal separability criterion to choose the number of domains. Essentially, it serves as a post-hoc validation score for a given number of domains, while the partitioning itself is driven solely by separability. A more integrated approach would directly optimize a joint objective that incorporates both criteria to produce the partition.
Despite these shortcomings—which may reflect the exploratory nature of this line of work—I believe the paper is a step in the right direction for the community and should be accepted.

Quality and clarity:
The paper is well written but would benefit from figures to illustrate the key concepts.

Experiments:
On the positive side, the experiments show a significant advantage when using the domains produced by the proposed method, compared with using datasets as domains and with the recent domain discovery technique [19] (Sec. 4.2).
The idea of including the test set in the “reshaping” process is interesting but not clearly presented (Sec. 4.3). This setup corresponds to an imputation setting (i.e., all test data is available simultaneously), but this is not stated clearly.
On the negative side, the image descriptor is very simple and outdated: a single bag-of-words over sparse SURF features for the entire image, with only 800 codebook entries. This is quite weak by current standards. I recommend using a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words computed on dense SURF features. It is also unclear what similarity measure is used to compare image descriptors—hopefully X^2 or an intersection kernel; Euclidean distance is not suitable for comparing histograms. As a next step, a Fisher Vector representation could further improve performance and bring the image representation up to modern standards. The following papers may help: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010.

Overall, the paper presents interesting, novel ideas on an important problem and achieves good results. It can be improved in several ways, especially with respect to the image representation.
