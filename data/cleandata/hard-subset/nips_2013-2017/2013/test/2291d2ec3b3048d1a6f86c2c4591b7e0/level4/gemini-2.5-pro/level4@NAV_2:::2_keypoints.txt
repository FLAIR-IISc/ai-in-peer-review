- Addresses domain adaptation by arguing datasets ≠ true domains and proposes discovering latent domains reflecting generative factors (viewpoint, lighting).
- Proposes partitioning images to maximize distributional distinctiveness between domains and ensure high intra-domain discriminative learnability.
- Aims to find partitions closer to true factors of variation rather than arbitrary dataset boundaries.
- Novel and significant: automatic domain discovery is underexplored and the distinctiveness+learnability idea is valuable.
- Weakness: connection between the relaxed optimization (Eqs. 4–5) and the original integer program (Eqs. 2–3) is not well justified.
- Weakness: the two principles aren’t fully integrated—maximum learnability is used only to select K as post-processing, not in the partitioning objective.
- Suggestion: a unified objective incorporating both distinctiveness and learnability would be more powerful.
- Recommendation: acceptance despite limitations; an important early step for the community.
- Writing is generally clear and readable, but core concepts need illustrative figures for better presentation.
- Experiments show clear advantage over using whole datasets as domains and over Hoffman et al. [20].
- “Conditional reshaping” (including test set in discovery) is interesting but needs clearer presentation and explicit statement that it assumes a transductive/batch setting.
- Significant experimental weakness: use of outdated image representation (sparse SURF BoW with small 800-word codebook) that may not reflect modern performance.
- Recommendation: strengthen results with modern representations (e.g., spatial pyramid with dense features, Fisher Vectors).
- Method detail missing: histogram distance metric is unspecified—Euclidean is inappropriate for histograms; use intersection or chi-squared instead.
