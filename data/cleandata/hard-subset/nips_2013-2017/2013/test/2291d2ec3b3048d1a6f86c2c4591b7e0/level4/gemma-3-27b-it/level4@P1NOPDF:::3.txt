This paper proposes a convex framework for partitioning a dataset (or datasets) into K subsets, with the goal that each subset exhibits a similar semantic class distribution while being as distinct as possible from the others. This distinctiveness, termed “maximum distinctiveness,” is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences of the subsets within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) induced by a chosen kernel. These subsets are referred to as latent domains. The identification of K (the number of latent domains) is accomplished by maximizing average learnability – specifically, how well a classifier can be learned – within each latent domain.

While enforcing similar class distributions within each domain prevents any single domain from being dominated by one class, this could be a limiting assumption for latent domain discovery in certain applications. For example, consider an animal classification task (e.g., horses and cows) where poses represent the latent domains. The class distributions within each latent domain might not be similar. We observe both horses and cows in left-right standing poses, but horses are rarely depicted in a sitting pose, whereas cows are.

Although the discovery of latent domains is not novel, the approach of controlling class label distributions to improve latent domain identification within a (relaxed) convex framework *is* a new contribution. Similarly, identifying the optimal number of latent domains by evaluating classification quality within each domain is a valuable practice, ensuring that each latent domain contains sufficient samples per class for effective generalization and discrimination.

Throughout the paper, the concepts of “dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain” are sometimes unclear and potentially confusing. These terms should be clearly defined, ideally with illustrative examples. Section 4.2, in particular, requires clarification. As I understand it, “dataset” and “domain” are used interchangeably, as Si is referenced as both. Regardless, the experimental setting and concept definitions should be clarified within this section. Additionally, maxk r(U_k,B) is used in equation 7 without prior definition. 

The experimental validation appears adequate, and the results demonstrate reasonable improvement over the baselines. However, the rationale behind the specific selection of source and target datasets for reporting is unclear. A leave-one-dataset-out adaptation strategy might provide a more comprehensive evaluation. The qualitative results are helpful for visualizing the algorithm’s behavior.

On line 74, framing the problem (learning latent domains) as unsupervised learning might be misleading, given that the method utilizes semantic class labels. The discovery of latent domains, achieved by encouraging similar class distributions within each domain and formulated within a (relaxed) convex framework, represents a noteworthy technical contribution. However, some concepts within the paper and the experimental validation require further clarification.
