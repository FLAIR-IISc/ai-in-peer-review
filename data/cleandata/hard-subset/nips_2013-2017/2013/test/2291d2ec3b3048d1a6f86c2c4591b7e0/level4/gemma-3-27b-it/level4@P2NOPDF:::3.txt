This paper introduces a convex optimization framework for partitioning a dataset into *K* subsets – termed ‘latent domains’ – with two key properties: similar semantic class distributions *within* each domain, and maximal distinctiveness *between* domains. This distinctiveness is quantified by maximizing the pairwise mean differences of the subsets in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) defined by a chosen kernel function. The optimal *K* (number of latent domains) is determined by maximizing the average learnability, assessed through classifier performance, within each discovered domain.

While the constraint of maintaining similar class distributions within each latent domain aims to prevent dominance by a single class, this could prove limiting in certain applications. For example, consider an animal classification task focusing on poses (e.g., horses and cows). Though both species might frequently appear in left-right standing poses, horses are less often depicted sitting compared to cows. In such cases, enforcing similar class distributions within each pose-based latent domain might hinder effective domain discovery.

The concept of leveraging distributional control over class labels to improve latent domain identification, formulated within a convex framework, is novel. Furthermore, the approach of using classifier quality *within* each latent domain to determine the appropriate *K* is a valuable practice. This ensures sufficient samples per class in each domain, fostering better generalization and discriminative learning.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in its terminology. The definitions of “dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain” are often conflated, leading to potential confusion. These concepts require precise definitions and illustrative examples. Section 4.2, in particular, needs substantial clarification. Currently, “dataset” and “domain” appear to be used interchangeably (referencing *S<sub>i</sub>* as both), and the experimental setup and associated terminology should be explicitly defined. Additionally, the notation *max<sub>k</sub> r(U<sub>k</sub>,B)* is used in equation 7 without prior definition.

The experimental validation is adequate and demonstrates reasonable improvements over baseline methods. However, the rationale behind the specific choice of source and target datasets is unclear. A more comprehensive evaluation strategy, such as leave-one-dataset-out adaptation, would be more convincing. The qualitative results are beneficial in visualizing the algorithm's behavior.

The framing of the problem as unsupervised learning (line 74) may be misleading, as the method explicitly utilizes semantic class labels. The core technical contribution – discovering latent domains by encouraging similar class distributions within each domain through a relaxed convex formulation – is noteworthy. Nevertheless, greater clarity in the paper’s concepts and a more thorough experimental validation are required.
