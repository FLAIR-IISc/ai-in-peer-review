This paper presents a new technique for domain adaptation in computer vision datasets, which typically exhibit multiple aspects such as viewpoint, illumination, level of clutter, compression, etc. Unlike existing methods that equate domains with datasets, thereby mixing these aspects together, the proposed technique automatically partitions the set of all images across all datasets into domains. The partitioning process is driven by two principles: creating separate domains in feature space and ensuring that a good discriminative classifier can be trained on each domain to classify the original classes, not to separate the domains. This approach is more likely to partition the data according to the underlying aspects rather than the datasets.

Originality and significance:
The paper proposes a novel idea for automatically defining domains, which is a relatively unexplored area in the field. The technique has the potential to support the training of better classifiers for the original problem. However, the optimization method presented in Section 2 is not entirely convincing, as it is unclear how closely it solves the original problem (2)+(3). Furthermore, the two driving criteria are not well integrated, with the maximal learnability criterion being used as a 'wrapper' around the maximal separability criterion to determine the number of domains. Essentially, it serves as a post-hoc validation score to evaluate the quality of the domain partitioning learned for a given number of domains, rather than being an integral part of the partitioning process. A more integrated approach would involve producing a partitioning that maximizes a goal function incorporating both criteria directly.

Despite these limitations, which may be due to the exploratory nature of this type of work, the paper is a step in the right direction for the community and deserves acceptance.

Quality and clarity:
The paper is well-written, but lacks figures to illustrate the concepts presented, which would enhance the reader's understanding.

Experiments:
The experiments demonstrate a significant advantage in using the domains produced by the proposed method over simply using datasets as domains and over the recent domain discovery technique [19] (Section 4.2). The idea of including the test set in the 'reshaping' process is interesting, but its presentation is unclear (Section 4.3). Additionally, this corresponds to an imputation setting, where all test data is available simultaneously, but this is not explicitly stated.

On the negative side, the image descriptor used is overly simplistic and outdated, relying on a single bag-of-words of sparse SURF features for the entire image, with only 800 codebook entries. This is a weak representation by current standards. It is recommended that the authors use a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words, computed on dense SURF features. Moreover, it is unclear what similarity measure is used to compare image descriptors; hopefully, it is X^2 or an intersection kernel, as the Euclidean distance is not suitable for comparing histograms. Consideration of a Fisher Vector representation could further improve the image representation and bring it up to the level of modern systems. The authors may find the following papers helpful: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010.

Overall, the paper presents interesting and novel ideas on an important problem, achieving good results. However, there is room for improvement, particularly in terms of the image representation used.
