This paper introduces a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision, addressing the challenges posed by datasets with varying factors such as viewpoint, illumination, and clutter. Rather than treating datasets as fixed domains, the approach automatically partitions images across datasets into domains based on two core principles: maximizing separation in feature space and ensuring each domain supports the training of an effective discriminative classifier for the original task (not domain separation). This strategy aims to uncover domains aligned with underlying visual aspects, rather than arbitrary dataset boundaries.

Regarding originality and significance, the work addresses a relatively unexplored area – automated domain definition – and proposes a promising approach for discovering domains that can improve classifier performance. 

However, I have some reservations about the proposed optimization method (section 2). It is unclear how faithfully it addresses the combined objectives outlined in equations (2) and (3). Furthermore, the two driving criteria, maximal separability and maximal learnability, are not fully integrated. The learnability criterion currently serves primarily as a post-hoc validation step to determine the optimal number of domains, while the partitioning itself relies solely on separability. A more cohesive approach would directly optimize a unified objective function incorporating both criteria. Despite these limitations, which may be inherent to the exploratory nature of this work, I believe the paper represents a valuable contribution and merits acceptance.

In terms of quality and clarity, the paper is well-written but would benefit significantly from illustrative figures to clarify the presented concepts. 

The experiments demonstrate a clear advantage of using the proposed domain discovery method compared to treating datasets as domains and outperform the recent approach in [19] (sec. 4.2). The inclusion of the test set in the reshaping process (sec. 4.3) is an intriguing idea, though the explanation lacks sufficient detail. It also implicitly assumes an imputation setting—where all test data is available simultaneously—which should be explicitly stated.

On the negative side, the image descriptor used is simplistic and outdated: a single bag-of-words representation of sparse SURF features with only 800 codebook entries. Modern systems typically employ more robust representations. I recommend adopting a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words computed on dense SURF features. Furthermore, the similarity measure used for comparing image descriptors is unclear; ideally, it should be X<sup>2</sup> or an intersection kernel, as Euclidean distance is unsuitable for comparing histograms. Exploring a Fisher Vector representation could further enhance the image representation and bring the paper in line with current state-of-the-art techniques. Relevant papers to consider include Zhang IJCV 2006, Jurie CVPR 2005, Lazebnik CVPR 2006, and Perronin ECCV 2010. 

Overall, this paper presents interesting and novel ideas for an important problem, achieving promising results. While the paper could be improved, particularly concerning the image representation, it represents a solid step forward in the field.
