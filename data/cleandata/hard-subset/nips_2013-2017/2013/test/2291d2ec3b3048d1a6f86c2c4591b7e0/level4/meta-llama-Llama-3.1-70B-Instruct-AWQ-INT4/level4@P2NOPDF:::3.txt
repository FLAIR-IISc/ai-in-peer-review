This paper proposes a convex framework for dividing a dataset into K subsets, referred to as latent domains, such that each subset has a similar semantic class distribution and is as distinct from the others as possible. The distinctiveness of these subsets, termed maximum distinctiveness, is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences of subsets in the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) induced by a selected kernel. The number of latent domains, K, is determined by maximizing the average learnability within each latent domain, which measures how well a classifier can be learned.

Although the proposed approach prevents clusters from being dominated by a single class by forcing similar class distributions within each domain, this assumption may be limiting for certain tasks. For instance, in an animal classification task, the latent domains might correspond to different poses, such as left-right standing or sitting poses. However, the distribution of latent domains within each class may not be similar, as certain poses may be more common for one class than the other.

While the concept of latent domain discovery is not new, the idea of controlling class label distributions to improve the identification of latent domains within a relaxed convex framework is a novel contribution. The approach of determining the number of latent domains by evaluating the quality of classification within each latent domain is also noteworthy, as it ensures that each latent domain has sufficient samples for each class to facilitate better generalization and learning of class discrimination.

However, the paper could benefit from clearer definitions of key concepts, such as dataset, domain, and latent domain, accompanied by supporting examples. The experiments section (4.2) is particularly unclear, and the terminology used is sometimes confusing. For instance, the terms "dataset" and "domain" appear to be used interchangeably, as Si is referred to as both a dataset and a source domain. Clarification of these concepts and the experimental setting is necessary.

Additionally, the notation maxk r(U_k,B) is used in equation 7 without being defined. The experimental validation appears to be adequate, with reasonable improvements over the baselines. However, the selection of source and target datasets for reporting results is not clearly justified, and alternative evaluation methods, such as leave-one-dataset-out adaptation, may be more suitable.

The qualitative results provide a helpful visual representation of the algorithm's performance. However, the problem statement on line 074, which describes learning latent domains as an unsupervised learning problem, may be misleading, as the method relies on semantic class labels. The discovery of latent domains via encouraging similar class distributions in each latent domain, formulated within a relaxed convex framework, is a notable technical contribution. Nevertheless, some concepts in the paper and the experimental validation require further clarification.
