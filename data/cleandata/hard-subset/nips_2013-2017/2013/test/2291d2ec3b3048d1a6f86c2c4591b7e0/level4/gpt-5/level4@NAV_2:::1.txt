The paper aims to automatically uncover latent domains within training data and then use those domains within a domain adaptation pipeline to improve test-time recognition accuracy. The authors introduce a kernel-based discrepancy that measures differences between empirical distributions via mean embeddings in an RKHS, and seek a partition of the data into domains that maximizes the total pairwise discrepancy across domains. They pose this as an integer program with two constraints: (1) each instance is assigned to exactly one domain, and (2) a label prior constraint requiring each domain’s class-label frequencies to match the global label prior. They relax the model to a continuous quadratic objective with linear constraints and determine the number of domains via cross-validation over domains (DWCV). Experiments span two settings: the Saenko et al. image datasets [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset [15]. A notable result is improved performance relative to the approach in [19].

Positives:
- The manuscript is clearly written, and to the best of my knowledge the core idea is novel (I do not consider myself a domain adaptation specialist).
- The empirical improvements over [19] are encouraging.

Negatives:
At this stage I am slightly leaning toward rejection, pending clarification in the rebuttal on two central points that could change my assessment:
(i) Motivation: On line 77, the paper states that “simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains.” What empirical or theoretical evidence supports this claim? Further, how does the Section 2 formulation explicitly prevent collapsing into per-category clusters—i.e., what in the objective/constraints enforces that partitions are not driven primarily by class identity?
(ii) Label prior constraint: Relatedly, why is the LPC (line 154) necessary? Please report performance without this constraint. As ablations/baselines, it would be informative to include partitioning via k-means on appearance features, unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al., ICCV’05), and a random equal-size partition.

Additional comments:
- Line 82: “maximally different in distribution” should be made precise—distribution over x, over (x, y), or another quantity? Clarify the divergence/metric used and what it is defined on.
- Line 166: As defined, doesn’t βmk already lie on a simplex (or collect into βk that lies on a simplex)? Please clarify the role of the simplex constraint relative to the βmk definition.
- Line 182: The constraints appear to differ from those earlier (starting at line 153). Please reconcile and explain the relationship between the two sets of constraints.
- Line 215: Please justify or prove the stated bound on K (or provide a formal reference).
- Line 289: Provide details on the geodesic flow kernel [4] usage—was this a reimplementation or did you use publicly available code?
- Lines 313/340: Offer insight into why the proposed method outperforms baselines. Where do the baselines systematically fail, and which failure modes does your method address?
- Eq. (1): M′k should be Mk′.
- Please consider adding: A. Torralba and A. Efros, “Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias,” CVPR 2011.

The rebuttal satisfactorily addressed my questions about the paper’s motivation and the necessity of the label prior constraint. I now lean slightly toward acceptance.
