This paper proposes a convex framework for partitioning a dataset into K subsets, referred to as "latent domains." The objective is to form subsets that each have a similar semantic class distribution, while simultaneously maximizing the distinctiveness between them. This "maximum distinctiveness" is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences of the subsets in the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) induced by a chosen kernel. The number of latent domains, K, is determined by maximizing the average "learnability"—the performance of a classifier trained within each latent domain.

A potential weakness lies in the assumption that class distributions should be similar within each latent domain. While this constraint effectively prevents clusters from being dominated by a single class, it may be too restrictive for latent domain discovery in certain tasks. For instance, consider an animal classification task (e.g., horses and cows) where different poses represent the latent domains. The distribution of these latent domains (poses) might not be similar across classes. While both horses and cows are commonly observed in a standing pose, cows might be pictured in a sitting pose far more often than horses.

While the concept of discovering latent domains is not new, the paper's core contribution is its novel approach: using a (relaxed) convex framework that explicitly controls class label distributions to guide the identification of these domains. Additionally, the method for determining the optimal number of domains, K, by assessing the classification quality within each potential domain is a notable and practical contribution. This ensures that the identified domains contain a sufficient number of samples per class to enable effective learning and generalization.

The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in its terminology, as the concepts of "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain" are not clearly distinguished and can be easily confused. The authors should provide precise definitions for these terms, preferably supplemented with examples. This ambiguity is particularly problematic in the experiments section (4.2), where the terms "dataset" and "domain" appear to be used interchangeably (e.g., Si is referred to as both a dataset and a source domain). Furthermore, the term `maxk r(U_k,B)` is used in Equation 7 without being defined.

The experimental validation appears adequate, with results showing reasonable improvements over the baselines. However, the rationale for selecting the specific source and target datasets for the reported experiments is not provided. A more standard evaluation, such as leave-one-dataset-out cross-validation, might offer a more robust assessment. On a positive note, the qualitative results are helpful, as they provide a clear visual illustration of what the algorithm achieves.

Finally, the claim on line 074 that the problem is one of "unsupervised learning" could be misleading, given that the method explicitly uses semantic class labels. In summary, the formulation of latent domain discovery as a (relaxed) convex optimization problem that encourages similar class distributions is a significant technical contribution. However, the paper would be substantially strengthened by clarifying key concepts and providing a more thorough justification for the experimental design.
