- Proposes a convex framework to split datasets into K latent domains by making class distributions within subsets similar and subsets maximally distinct.
- Maximizes pairwise mean differences in an RKHS (kernel) to achieve "maximum distinctiveness" between latent domains.
- Identifies the number K of latent domains by maximizing average learnability (how well a classifier can be learned) within each latent domain.
- Novelty: controlling class label distributions in a relaxed convex formulation for latent-domain discovery is new relative to prior domain-adaptation work.
- Novel practice: using classification quality within each latent domain to choose K ensures sufficient class coverage for learning.
- Experimental validation shows reasonable improvements over baselines and useful qualitative visualizations of what the algorithm achieves.
- Unclear why the chosen source/target datasets were reported; leave-one-dataset-out adaptation might be a more appropriate evaluation.
- Terminology and concepts are ambiguous: dataset, domain, and latent domain are used interchangeably and need precise definitions (especially in Sec. 4.2).
- Experimental setting and concept definitions in Sec. 4.2 require clarification to remove confusion.
- The claim that learning latent domains is unsupervised is misleading because semantic class labels are used in the method.
- Technical contribution: convex relaxation and RKHS-based distinctiveness formulation are significant contributions to latent-domain discovery.
- Significance: approach could improve domain adaptation/transfer learning in heterogeneous, multi-domain real-world datasets.
- Overall strengths: new formulation for class-distribution control, principled K selection via learnability, and empirical gains.
- Overall weaknesses: unclear definitions/terminology, unclear dataset selection for experiments, and misleading unsupervised framing.
