This paper introduces a convex optimization approach to partition a dataset into K subsets – termed latent domains – designed to exhibit similar semantic class distributions internally while maximizing distinctiveness between each other. This distinctiveness, quantified as maximum distinctiveness, is achieved by maximizing pairwise mean differences of these subsets within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) defined by a chosen kernel function. The optimal value of K, representing the number of latent domains, is determined by maximizing the average learnability within each identified domain.

While the constraint of similar class distributions within each domain prevents any single class from dominating a cluster, this could potentially limit the discovery of relevant latent domains for certain applications. For example, considering poses as latent domains in an animal classification task (e.g., horses vs. cows), the class distributions within each pose-based domain may naturally differ; horses and cows may both be frequently depicted standing, but sitting poses are more common for cows than horses.

The concept of controlling class label distributions to improve latent domain identification within a convex framework is novel, as is the use of classification performance *within* each latent domain to guide the selection of K, ensuring sufficient samples per class for effective generalization and discrimination. 

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in its terminology. The definitions of “dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain” are often ambiguous and could be easily confused. Clear definitions, ideally with illustrative examples, are needed throughout the manuscript. Section 4.2, in particular, requires substantial clarification; the interchangeable use of “dataset” and “domain” (Si being referred to as both) is problematic. Furthermore, the term “maxk r(U_k,B)” is used in equation 7 without prior definition.

The experimental validation is generally adequate, demonstrating reasonable improvements over baseline methods. However, the rationale for selecting the specific source and target datasets used in the evaluation is unclear. A more comprehensive evaluation strategy, such as leave-one-dataset-out adaptation, might be more informative. The qualitative results are valuable for visualizing the algorithm’s behavior. 

Framing the problem as unsupervised learning on line 74 is potentially misleading, given the method’s reliance on semantic class labels. The formulation of latent domain discovery through encouraging similar class distributions within each domain, implemented within a convex framework, represents a significant technical contribution. However, improved clarity regarding key concepts and a more thorough experimental justification are necessary.
