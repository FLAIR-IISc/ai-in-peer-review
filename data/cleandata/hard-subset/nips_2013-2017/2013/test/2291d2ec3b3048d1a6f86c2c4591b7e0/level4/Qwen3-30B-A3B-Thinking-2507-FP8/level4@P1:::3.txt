

This paper introduces a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains within visual datasets, addressing a critical gap in domain adaptation research. The authors propose two key properties for these latent domains: maximum distinctiveness (ensuring distributions across domains are maximally different) and maximum learnability (ensuring each domain can support strong discriminative models). The method formulates domain discovery as a nonparametric optimization problem, using kernel-based measures in RKHS to quantify domain differences and a convex relaxation to efficiently solve the optimization. The optimal number of domains is determined through domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV), which evaluates classification performance within each candidate domain.

The work builds on previous domain adaptation research but addresses a fundamental limitation: the common practice of equating entire datasets with domains, which fails to capture the complex, overlapping factors (lighting, pose, background, etc.) that actually create domain variations in visual data. While prior work like Hoffman et al. [20] also explores latent domain discovery, this paper distinguishes itself by using nonparametric methods (avoiding restrictive parametric assumptions) and introducing DWCV for determining the number of domains. The authors also extend the problem to include reshaping test datasets conditionally, which is particularly valuable for real-world applications where test data may contain multiple latent domains.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a principled framework for domain discovery that avoids the pitfalls of dataset-as-domain assumptions. Second, the DWCV procedure for determining the optimal number of domains is a practical and well-motivated solution to a challenging problem. Third, the extensive experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human activity recognition (IXMAS) demonstrate clear improvements over baseline approaches, including the original datasets and the method of [20]. The results show that reshaping datasets into the automatically discovered latent domains consistently improves adaptation performance, with the MATCHING strategy achieving near-oracle accuracy without using target domain labels.

The paper is generally well-written and technically sound, with clear mathematical formulations and thorough experimental validation. The authors carefully address the key challenges of domain discovery, including the need to preserve class distributions across domains (via the label prior constraint) and the computational feasibility of the approach. The qualitative analysis in Figure 1 effectively demonstrates how the discovered domains align with meaningful visual factors like object pose and background.

However, the paper would benefit from clearer conceptual distinctions. The terms "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain" are sometimes used interchangeably, which creates confusion. For instance, in Section 4.2, the source domains Sj are referred to as both "datasets" and "source domains," while the identified latent domains Uk are called "domains" without clear differentiation. The paper should explicitly define these terms early on and provide examples to clarify their relationships. The notation in Equation 6 (max_k r(U_k,B)) is also used without sufficient explanation, requiring readers to infer its meaning from context.

Another concern is the class distribution constraint. The authors enforce that each latent domain should have a similar class distribution to the overall dataset (via the label prior constraint), which prevents any single class from dominating a domain. While this is a reasonable assumption for many visual recognition tasks, it may not hold for all applications. For example, if the latent domains correspond to pose variations (e.g., standing vs. sitting), the class distributions within each pose domain might naturally differ (e.g., horses rarely appear sitting while cows do). The paper should acknowledge this limitation and discuss whether the constraint could be relaxed for specific applications.

The experimental validation is strong overall, but the selection of source and target datasets for reporting results lacks justification. The paper reports results on specific dataset pairs (e.g., using Caltech-256 as source and Amazon as target) without explaining why these particular combinations were chosen over more systematic evaluations like leave-one-dataset-out. A more comprehensive evaluation would strengthen the claims about the method's general effectiveness.

The paper's contribution is novel and significant for domain adaptation research. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are well-designed, and the results demonstrate clear practical improvements. The main weaknesses are primarily conceptual (terminology clarity) and methodological (lack of systematic dataset evaluation), which are addressable with minor revisions.

Pros:
- Novel framework for latent domain discovery with two well-motivated properties (distinctiveness and learnability)
- Practical DWCV method for determining the number of domains
- Strong experimental results across multiple visual recognition tasks
- Clear demonstration of the value of reshaping both training and test datasets

Cons:
- Ambiguous terminology (dataset vs. domain vs. latent domain) requires clarification
- Class distribution constraint may not generalize to all domain discovery scenarios
- Lack of justification for specific source/target dataset selections in experiments
- Some technical details (e.g., Equation 6) need better explanation

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to domain adaptation by addressing a fundamental conceptual gap in the field. The method is technically sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results are compelling. With minor clarifications to terminology and experimental design, this work would make an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
