- Goal: Automatically discover latent domains in training data and use them for domain adaptation to improve test-set classification.
- Method: Defines a kernel-based function measuring difference between feature vectors and partitions data to maximize inter-domain differences.
- Formulation: Cast as integer program with constraints: each point assigned to one domain; per-domain label distribution matches overall label prior.
- Optimization: Relaxed to continuous quadratic optimization with linear constraints; number of domains selected by cross-validation.
- Evaluation: Tested on a static image dataset and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset; outperforms domain adaptation method of [19].
- Positive: Paper is well-written and appears novel; reported performance gains are appreciated.
- Main concern (motivation): Claim that clustering by appearance yields per-category domains lacks evidence and clarity.
- Main concern (prevention): Unclear how Section 2’s formulation prevents reshaping into per-category domains.
- Main concern (label prior): Questioning necessity of the label-prior constraint and interest in performance without it.
- Baselines missing: Request results for k-means or unsupervised object discovery clustering, and for random equal partitions as baselines.
- Clarification needed: Explain phrase “maximally different in distribution” — distribution over what?
- Technical questions: Does β_{mk} already lie on a simplex? Are later constraints different from earlier ones?
- Theoretical detail: Request justification or proof for the bound mentioned at line 215.
- Implementation detail: Ask whether the geodesic flow kernel was reimplemented or used from public code.
- Empirical analysis: Need insights into why the proposed method outperforms baselines and examples of systematic baseline failures.
- Minor fixes: Typo in Eq (1) (M'k => Mk') and suggest adding Torralba & Efros (2011) citation.
- Outcome: After rebuttal addressing motivation and label-prior concerns, reviewer now slightly leans toward acceptance.
