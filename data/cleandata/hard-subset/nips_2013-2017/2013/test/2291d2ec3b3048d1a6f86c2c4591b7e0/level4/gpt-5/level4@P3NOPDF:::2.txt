Summary
This paper tackles domain adaptation in vision by moving beyond the common practice of equating domains with datasets. Instead, it proposes to automatically partition the union of images across datasets into meaningful domains that better reflect underlying factors such as viewpoint, illumination, clutter, and compression. The partitioning is guided by two principles: (i) domains should be well separated in feature space, and (ii) each domain should support training a strong discriminative classifier for the original classes. The intent is to discover domains aligned with latent factors rather than dataset boundaries.

Originality and Significance
There has been limited prior work on automatically defining domains, and this paper offers a timely and relevant contribution. The idea of discovering domains that explicitly support improved class discrimination is both novel and practically useful for domain adaptation and multi-source learning. The work addresses an important gap and takes a step toward principled, data-driven domain construction.

Methodological Concerns
While the high-level idea is compelling, the optimization details in Section 2 need clarification. It is not clear how closely the proposed procedure approximates the original objective (2)+(3). More importantly, the two criteria—maximal separability of domains and maximal learnability within domains—are not fully integrated. Learnability is used primarily as a wrapper to select the number of domains, while the actual partitioning is driven only by separability. An integrated objective that directly combines both criteria during partitioning would be more convincing and may yield better, task-aligned domains.

Specific questions and suggestions:
- Provide a clearer derivation or justification for the optimization steps, including assumptions, relaxations, and their impact on the objective gap relative to (2)+(3).
- Report sensitivity to initialization and hyperparameters, and whether the method consistently converges to similar partitions.
- Consider an ablation or an alternative formulation where learnability contributes directly to the partitioning objective, not just to model selection.

Clarity and Presentation
The paper is generally well written, but it would benefit greatly from illustrative figures:
- A schematic of the pipeline showing how datasets are pooled, how domains are discovered, and how classifiers are trained within domains.
- Visual examples of discovered domains, contrasting them with dataset-defined partitions.
- A t-SNE/UMAP or similar embedding to show domain separability and class structure.

Experiments
The empirical results are encouraging. Using discovered domains improves performance relative to treating datasets as domains and outperforms the recent domain discovery approach [19] (Sec. 4.2). This supports the central claim that appropriate domain discovery can enhance downstream classification.

However, the “reshaping” use of test data in Sec. 4.3 is under-explained. It appears to assume access to the full test set during training or partitioning, which is a transductive/imputation-like setting. Please:
- Clearly state the evaluation protocol and whether test instances are all available at once.
- If transductive information is used, provide a strictly inductive baseline for comparison and quantify the benefit from transductive reshaping.
- Ensure that no label information leaks into domain discovery or classifier training.

Image Representation
The current descriptor is quite limited: a single bag-of-words from sparse SURF with an 800-word codebook. This is weak by contemporary standards and could underrepresent the method’s potential. Recommended improvements:
- Use a spatial pyramid bag-of-words and dense feature extraction to capture spatial layout (e.g., Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006).
- Clarify and use appropriate similarity measures for histograms (e.g., chi-square or histogram intersection) rather than Euclidean distance.
- Consider Fisher Vectors or similar high-capacity encodings (e.g., Perronnin et al., ECCV 2010) to achieve more competitive baselines.
- Earlier works such as Zhang et al. (IJCV 2006) and Jurie & Triggs (CVPR 2005) may guide stronger quantization and pooling strategies.

Additional Analysis to Strengthen the Paper
- Domain interpretability: Evaluate whether discovered domains correlate with known factors (viewpoint, illumination, clutter). Qualitative examples would add insight.
- Sensitivity to the number of domains: Provide performance curves and stability analyses.
- Scalability and complexity: Report computational cost and scalability with dataset size and feature dimensionality.
- Robustness across features: Show that gains persist with stronger representations to validate the method’s generality.

Overall Evaluation
This paper introduces a promising approach to discovering domains that are more meaningful than dataset boundaries and empirically beneficial for classification. Despite concerns about the optimization’s faithfulness to the intended objective and the loose integration of separability and learnability, the contribution is novel and relevant. The experimental gains over dataset-based partitioning and recent domain discovery baselines are notable. Presentation can be improved with clearer protocol descriptions and illustrative figures, and the empirical section would be stronger with modern feature representations and appropriate similarity metrics.

Recommendation: Accept (weak accept). The idea is sound and useful, and with the suggested clarifications and feature upgrades, the paper could have substantial impact.
