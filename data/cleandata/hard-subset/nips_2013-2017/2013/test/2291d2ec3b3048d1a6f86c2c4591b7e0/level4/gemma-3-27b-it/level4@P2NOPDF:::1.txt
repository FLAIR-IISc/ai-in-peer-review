This paper introduces a novel approach to domain adaptation by automatically discovering latent domains within a training dataset. The discovered domains are then leveraged to improve classification performance on a test set. The method defines a kernel-based function to measure dissimilarity between feature vectors and aims to partition the data into domains that maximize this dissimilarity across domain pairs. This partitioning is initially formulated as an integer programming problem, subject to constraints ensuring each data point belongs to exactly one domain and that the class label distribution within each domain mirrors the overall dataset distribution. The integer program is then relaxed into a continuous optimization problem involving a quadratic cost function and linear constraints, with the optimal number of domains determined through cross-validation.

The proposed approach was evaluated on two datasets: a static image dataset [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action recognition dataset [15]. Notably, the method demonstrates performance improvements compared to the domain adaptation technique presented in [19].

**Strengths:** The paper is clearly written and, to the best of my knowledge, presents a novel contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The reported performance gains over the baseline method [19] are encouraging.

**Weaknesses:** Currently, I am inclined to recommend rejection. However, I would be open to reconsidering my assessment based on a strong rebuttal addressing the following key concerns:

(i) The motivation for this work remains unclear. The authors claim (line 77) that simple clustering based on appearance is susceptible to creating domains dominated by single categories. I request clarification on the empirical evidence supporting this claim. Furthermore, the paper should explicitly explain how the proposed model formulation (Section 2) mitigates this issue – i.e., what mechanisms prevent the formation of per-category domains?

(ii) The necessity of the “label prior constraint” (line 154) is not adequately justified. I am curious about the performance of the method without this constraint. A crucial addition would be a baseline comparison using simpler partitioning strategies, such as k-means clustering applied to the appearance vectors or unsupervised object discovery techniques (e.g., Sivic et al. ICCV '05). Reporting results with a random partitioning of the dataset into equally sized sets would also be informative.
