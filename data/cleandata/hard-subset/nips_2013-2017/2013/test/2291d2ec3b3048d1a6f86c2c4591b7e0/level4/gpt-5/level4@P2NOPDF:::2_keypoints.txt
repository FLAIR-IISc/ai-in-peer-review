1. Proposes automatic partitioning of images across datasets into domains guided by separability and learnability to recover factors of variation (viewpoint, illumination, clutter, compression).  
2. Novel and promising direction with limited prior work on automatic domain definition; merits acceptance.  
3. Optimization in Section 2 is unclear and may not closely approximate the intended objectives (Eqs. 2 and 3).  
4. The two criteria (maximal separability and maximal learnability) are not tightly integrated; learnability is used mainly as a post-hoc validation for domain count.  
5. Recommend a principled, unified objective that balances separability and learnability during partitioning.  
6. Writing is generally clear but would benefit from figures illustrating key concepts and workflow.  
7. Experiments show clear gains over treating datasets as domains and over the recent domain discovery method [19].  
8. Test-set “reshaping” is interesting but under-explained; it effectively assumes a transductive scenario and this assumption and implications must be stated.  
9. Image representation is outdated: single bag-of-words over sparse SURF with an 800-word codebook is weak by current standards.  
10. Recommend stronger representations and choices: spatial pyramid BOW on dense SURF, specify histogram similarity (chi-square or intersection), or use Fisher Vectors for modern baselines.  
11. Cite relevant prior work on improved representations (Zhang et al. IJCV 2006; Jurie & Triggs CVPR 2005; Lazebnik et al. CVPR 2006; Perronnin et al. ECCV 2010).  
12. Overall assessment: interesting, relevant, and promising results but should integrate criteria, clarify transductive reshaping, and adopt modern image representations and similarity measures.
