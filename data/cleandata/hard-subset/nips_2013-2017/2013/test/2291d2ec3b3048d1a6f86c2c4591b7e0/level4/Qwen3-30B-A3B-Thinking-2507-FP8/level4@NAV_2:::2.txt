

This paper introduces a novel approach to domain adaptation in computer vision by automatically discovering latent domains within datasets, rather than equating domains with datasets as is commonly done. The method partitions image data into domains based on two key principles: maximum distinctiveness (ensuring domains are maximally different from each other in feature space) and maximum learnability (ensuring each domain supports training strong discriminative classifiers for the original recognition task). This approach is designed to separate data according to underlying factors like viewpoint, illumination, and background, rather than simply grouping by dataset boundaries.

Originality and significance:
The paper addresses a critical gap in domain adaptation research, as automatic domain discovery has received limited attention. While the proposed method is promising for improving domain adaptation performance, the optimization approach in Section 2 requires clarification regarding how closely it solves the original problem (Equations 2-3). Additionally, the two driving criteria (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) are not sufficiently integrated—the learnability criterion is used primarily as a post-hoc validation metric to determine the number of domains rather than being incorporated directly into the optimization process. An integrated approach that simultaneously optimizes for both criteria would be more compelling. Despite these limitations, the paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and advances the exploration of latent domain discovery.

Quality and clarity:
The paper is well-written and logically structured, though it would benefit from additional figures to illustrate the domain discovery process and the identified domain characteristics.

Experiments:
The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements in recognition accuracy when using the automatically discovered domains compared to using datasets as domains and to the recent method by Hoffman et al. [20]. The approach of reshaping test datasets to match the discovered training domains is an interesting contribution, though the presentation in Section 4.3 could be clearer regarding the imputation setting assumption (all test data available simultaneously). The image representation used (bag-of-visual-words with 800 SURF features) is notably outdated and simplistic for modern computer vision tasks. The authors should consider using a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words with dense SURF features, appropriate histogram similarity measures (X² or intersection kernel rather than Euclidean distance), and potentially Fisher Vector representations to bring the image representation in line with current state-of-the-art approaches. Relevant references include Zhang (IJCV 2006), Jurie (CVPR 2005), Lazebnik (CVPR 2006), and Perronin (ECCV 2010).

Overall, the paper presents an important and novel approach to domain adaptation that addresses a meaningful problem in the field. While the experimental methodology could be strengthened through improved image representation, the core ideas are compelling and the results demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature and should be accepted.
