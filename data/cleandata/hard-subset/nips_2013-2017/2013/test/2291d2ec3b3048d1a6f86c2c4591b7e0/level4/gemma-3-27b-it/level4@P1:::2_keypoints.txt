- Proposes automatic partitioning of combined datasets into meaningful domains for domain adaptation, rather than treating each dataset as a single domain.  
- Partitioning is driven by two principles: maximizing inter-domain distinctiveness in feature space and ensuring each domain supports a strong discriminative classifier.  
- Addresses an important, underexplored problem — discovering useful domains automatically — with a compelling idea likely to improve adaptation performance.  
- The optimization method (section 2) is not fully convincing; unclear how the implemented solver approximates the original objectives in equations (2) and (3).  
- The two criteria (distinctiveness and learnability) are not fully integrated: learnability is used post-hoc for selecting the number of domains instead of being part of a joint objective.  
- Despite methodological caveats, the work is a valuable exploratory contribution and the reviewer recommends acceptance.  
- Paper is well-written and clear, but would benefit from visual illustrations showing the domain discovery process and resulting domains.  
- Experiments show significant improvement over treating datasets as domains and outperform a recent domain discovery method ([20]).  
- The approach of incorporating the test set into domain reshaping is interesting but poorly explained and assumes an imputation setting (all test data available), which should be stated.  
- Image representation is relatively simple/outdated (single BoW on sparse SURF with 800 codebook entries) and likely limits performance.  
- Reviewer suggests stronger descriptors and similarity measures: spatial pyramid BoW on dense SURF, Chi-squared or intersection kernels for histograms, or Fisher Vectors (cites: Zhang IJCV 2006, Jurie CVPR 2005, Lazebnik CVPR 2006, Perronnin ECCV 2010).  
- Summary: novel and promising idea with strong empirical results, but needs better optimization justification, tighter criterion integration, improved image features, and visual aids.
