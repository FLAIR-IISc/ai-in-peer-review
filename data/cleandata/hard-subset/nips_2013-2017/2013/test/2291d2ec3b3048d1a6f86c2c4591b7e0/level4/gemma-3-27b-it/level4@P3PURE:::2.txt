This paper presents a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision, addressing datasets that exhibit multiple variations such as viewpoint, illumination, clutter, and compression. Rather than treating datasets as domains directly – a practice that conflates these underlying factors – the proposed method automatically partitions the combined image set into meaningful domains. This partitioning is guided by two principles: maximizing separation between domains in feature space and ensuring each domain supports the training of a high-performing discriminative classifier for the original task (not domain separation). This approach aims to partition images based on the underlying variation factors, rather than arbitrary dataset boundaries.

**Originality and significance:**

The existing work on automatic domain definition is limited, and this paper offers a valuable contribution towards automatically discovering useful domains that can improve classifier performance. 

However, I have some concerns regarding the proposed optimization method (section 2). The connection between the optimization and the original objective function (2)+(3) is not fully clear. Furthermore, the two guiding criteria – maximal separability and maximal learnability – are not well integrated. The learnability criterion currently functions as a wrapper around the separability criterion, primarily determining the number of domains through a post-hoc validation process. A more integrated approach would directly optimize a goal function incorporating both criteria simultaneously, leading to a partitioning that inherently balances separability and learnability.

Despite these shortcomings, potentially attributable to the exploratory nature of this work, I believe this paper represents a step forward for the community and is worthy of acceptance.

**Quality and clarity:**

The paper is well-written, but would benefit from figures illustrating the key concepts.

**Experiments:**

The experimental results demonstrate a significant advantage of using the proposed domains over treating datasets as domains and outperform the recent domain discovery technique [19] (sec. 4.2). 

The inclusion of the test set in the ‘reshaping’ process (sec. 4.3) is an interesting idea, but lacks clear explanation. Importantly, this corresponds to an imputation setting – assuming all test data is available concurrently – a fact that should be explicitly stated.

On the negative side, the image descriptor employed is relatively simple and outdated: a single bag-of-words representation of sparse SURF features applied to the entire image, with a limited codebook size of 800 entries. This is a significant weakness given current state-of-the-art methods. I recommend the authors consider a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words features computed on dense SURF features. Additionally, the similarity measure used to compare image descriptors is unclear; ideally, it should be χ² or an intersection kernel, as Euclidean distance is not well-suited for comparing histograms. As a further improvement, exploring a Fisher Vector representation could significantly enhance performance and bring the image representation in line with modern systems. Relevant papers include: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010. 

Overall, the paper presents interesting and novel ideas for an important problem and achieves promising results. While the paper can be improved, particularly in terms of the image representation, the contributions are significant.
