This paper introduces a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision. Datasets in this area often exhibit variations across multiple factors, such as viewpoint, illumination, clutter, and compression artifacts. Rather than equating entire datasets with domains—a method that conflates these underlying factors—the proposed technique automatically partitions the complete set of images from all datasets into new domains. This partitioning is guided by two principles: first, ensuring domains are separable in the feature space, and second, ensuring that an effective discriminative classifier for the original task can be trained within each domain. The authors argue that this approach is more likely to discover partitions corresponding to these underlying generative factors rather than simply replicating the original dataset boundaries.

**Originality and Significance**

Relatively little work has focused on automatically defining domains, and this paper proposes a promising direction for discovering useful domains that can support the training of better classifiers.

On the other hand, I have reservations about the proposed optimization method (Section 2), as it is unclear how closely this method approximates a solution to the original problem defined in equations (2) and (3). Moreover, the two guiding criteria are not well integrated. The maximal learnability criterion is only used as a wrapper for the maximal separability criterion to determine the optimal number of domains. In essence, it functions as a post-hoc validation metric, while the partitioning itself is driven solely by the separability criterion. A more integrated approach would directly optimize a single objective function that incorporates both criteria.

Despite these shortcomings, which are perhaps attributable to the exploratory nature of this research area, I believe the paper is a valuable contribution that moves the community in the right direction and should be accepted.

**Quality and Clarity**

While the paper is well-written, it would benefit from figures to visually illustrate the core concepts presented.

**Experiments**

On the positive side, the experiments demonstrate a significant advantage of using the domains produced by the proposed method over baselines that use datasets as domains, as well as over the recent domain discovery technique from [19] (Section 4.2).

The idea of including the test set in the "reshaping" process is interesting but is not presented with sufficient clarity (Section 4.3). This corresponds to a transductive or imputation setting, a detail that should be stated more explicitly.

On the negative side, the image descriptor used is simplistic and outdated: a single bag-of-words representation of sparse SURF features for the entire image, with a codebook of only 800 entries. This representation is considerably weaker than current state-of-the-art methods. The authors should consider using a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words computed on dense SURF features. Additionally, it is not specified what similarity measure is used for these descriptors; a Chi-squared or intersection kernel would be appropriate, whereas the Euclidean distance is ill-suited for comparing histograms. Furthermore, adopting a Fisher Vector representation would elevate the image features to be on par with contemporary systems. The following papers may be helpful: Zhang et al., IJCV 2006; Jurie & Triggs, CVPR 2005; Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006; Perronnin et al., ECCV 2010.

Overall, the paper presents interesting and novel ideas on an important problem and achieves good results. The work could be improved in several ways, most notably by employing a more modern and powerful image representation.
