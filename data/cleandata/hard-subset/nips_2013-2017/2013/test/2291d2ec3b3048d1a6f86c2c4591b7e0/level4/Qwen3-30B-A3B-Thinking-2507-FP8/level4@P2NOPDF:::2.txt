

This paper introduces a novel approach to domain adaptation in computer vision by automatically partitioning image datasets into meaningful domains based on intrinsic aspects (e.g., viewpoint, illumination, clutter, compression), rather than treating domains as synonymous with datasets. The method partitions all images across datasets into domains that are both separable in feature space and conducive to training discriminative classifiers for the original task (not domain separation). This strategy better aligns partitions with underlying data aspects than dataset-based domain definitions.  

**Originality and Significance:**  
The work addresses a significant gap in domain adaptation literature by proposing an automated method for discovering useful domains. While prior work rarely explores automatic domain definition, this paper offers a promising direction for improving classifier training. However, the optimization method (Section 2) lacks clarity regarding its proximity to solving the combined objectives (2)+(3). Critically, the two proposed criteria—maximal separability (for domain definition) and maximal learnability (to determine domain count)—are not integrated. The learnability criterion functions merely as a post-hoc validation metric for a fixed number of domains, while partitioning itself relies solely on separability. An integrated formulation maximizing a combined objective would strengthen the contribution. These limitations likely stem from the exploratory nature of the problem, but they warrant attention. Despite this, the paper represents a valuable step forward and merits acceptance.  

**Quality and Clarity:**  
The paper is well-written but lacks illustrative figures to clarify the domain partitioning concept and experimental setup.  

**Experiments:**  
The results demonstrate clear advantages: the proposed domains outperform both dataset-based domains and the recent domain discovery method [19] (Section 4.2). The inclusion of the test set in the "reshaping" process (Section 4.3) is conceptually interesting but poorly articulated. Crucially, this approach assumes an imputation setting (all test data available simultaneously), a detail not explicitly stated.  

**Technical Concerns:**  
The image representation is a major weakness. The use of a single bag-of-words (BoW) descriptor with sparse SURF features and only 800 codebook entries is outdated and overly simplistic. Modern approaches require:  
1. A spatial pyramid BoW on dense SURF features (e.g., Jurie et al., CVPR 2005; Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006),  
2. An appropriate histogram similarity measure (e.g., X² or intersection kernel, not Euclidean distance),  
3. Advanced representations like Fisher Vectors (e.g., Perronnin et al., ECCV 2010) to elevate the baseline to state-of-the-art levels.  
The paper’s current representation cannot fairly compete with contemporary methods. The authors should replace it with a more robust descriptor and clarify the similarity metric used.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This paper presents a novel and important perspective on domain discovery, with strong empirical results that validate its core idea. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the value of aspect-based domains over dataset-based or prior domain discovery methods. While the optimization method and image representation require substantial refinement, these are fixable issues that do not undermine the paper’s conceptual contribution. The work provides a compelling foundation for future research in adaptive domain modeling.  

**Recommendation:**  
Accept. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to an underexplored problem, with results that justify its significance. Addressing the technical limitations (especially the image representation) in revision would further strengthen it, but the current work is sufficiently novel and impactful for acceptance.
