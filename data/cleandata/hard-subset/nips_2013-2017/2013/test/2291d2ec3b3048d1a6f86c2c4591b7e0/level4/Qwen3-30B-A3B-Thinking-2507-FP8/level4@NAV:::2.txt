

This paper introduces a novel approach for domain adaptation in computer vision datasets, addressing the challenge of multiple underlying aspects (e.g., viewpoint, illumination, clutter, compression) that are typically conflated when domains are defined solely by datasets. Instead, the proposed method automatically partitions the entire image collection across all datasets into distinct domains, guided by two principles: (1) creating separable domains in feature space, and (2) ensuring each domain supports training effective classifiers for the original task (not domain separation). This strategy better aligns domain partitioning with intrinsic data aspects rather than dataset boundaries.  

Originality and significance:  
The work addresses a notable gap in automated domain definition, proposing a meaningful step toward discovering useful domains for improved classifier training. While the core idea is promising, the optimization method (Section 2) lacks clarity on how closely it solves the combined problem (2)+(3). Furthermore, the two driving criteria are not sufficiently integrated: the maximal learnability criterion functions merely as a post-hoc validation score to select the number of domains, while the partitioning itself relies solely on maximal separability. An integrated approach maximizing a unified objective incorporating both criteria would be preferable. Despite these limitations—likely reflecting the exploratory nature of this emerging direction—the paper represents a valuable contribution and merits acceptance.  

Quality and clarity:  
The paper is well-written but lacks illustrative figures to clarify key concepts.  

Experiments:  
Positive results demonstrate significant gains using the proposed domains over dataset-based domains and a recent domain discovery method [19] (Section 4.2). The inclusion of the test set in the "reshaping" process (Section 4.3) is intriguing but poorly explained and conflates an imputation setting (all test data available simultaneously) with the standard domain adaptation scenario.  
Negative aspects include the use of a notably outdated image descriptor: a single bag-of-words (800 codebook entries) with sparse SURF features applied globally to the entire image. This is insufficient by modern standards; the authors should adopt a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words with dense SURF features. Additionally, the similarity measure for descriptor comparison is unspecified (Euclidean distance is inappropriate for histograms; X² or intersection kernel is recommended). Future work should explore Fisher Vectors for representation (referencing Zhang et al. IJCV 2006; Jurie et al. CVPR 2005; Lazebnik et al. CVPR 2006; Perronin et al. ECCV 2010).  

Overall, the paper advances an important problem with novel ideas and strong empirical results. Key improvements are needed, particularly in the image representation methodology.
