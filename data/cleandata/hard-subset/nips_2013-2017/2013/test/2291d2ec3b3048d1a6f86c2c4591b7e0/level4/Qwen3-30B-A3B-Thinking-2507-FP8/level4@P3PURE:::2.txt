

This paper introduces a novel approach to domain adaptation in computer vision by automatically partitioning images across multiple datasets into meaningful domains based on underlying aspects (e.g., viewpoint, illumination, clutter, compression) rather than treating datasets as monolithic domains. The partitioning is driven by two principles: (1) creating separable domains in feature space, and (2) ensuring each domain supports training a highly discriminative classifier for the original task (not domain separation). This strategy better aligns with the true underlying factors of variation than dataset-based domain definitions.

**Originality and Significance:**  
The work addresses a significant gap in automatically defining meaningful domains, presenting a promising direction for improving classifier performance. While the problem is largely unexplored, the proposed idea is novel and impactful for the community.

**Technical Criticisms:**  
The optimization method (Section 2) lacks clarity regarding how closely it approximates the combined objective (2)+(3). More critically, the two driving criteria are not properly integrated: the maximal learnability criterion functions solely as a post-hoc validation metric to select the number of domains, while the partitioning itself relies exclusively on the maximal separability criterion. An integrated approach would directly optimize a unified objective combining both criteria, rather than using learnability as a secondary validation step.

**Quality and Clarity:**  
The paper is well-written but omits illustrative figures to clarify the domain partitioning concept.

**Experiments:**  
*Strengths:* Results demonstrate significant improvements over using datasets as domains and over the recent domain discovery method [19] (Section 4.2).  
*Weaknesses:* The inclusion of the test set during domain partitioning (Section 4.3) is poorly explained and implies a batch processing setting (all test data available simultaneously), which is not explicitly stated. Crucially, the image descriptor is outdated: a single bag-of-words representation using sparse SURF features with only 800 codebook entries. This is significantly weaker than current standards. The authors should adopt a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words with dense SURF features and clarify the descriptor similarity measure (X² or intersection kernel, not Euclidean distance, which is inappropriate for histograms). For further improvement, Fisher Vectors would modernize the representation. Relevant references include Zhang et al. (IJCV 2006), Jurie (CVPR 2005), Lazebnik (CVPR 2006), and Perronnin (ECCV 2010).

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper presents a valuable and novel perspective on domain adaptation with strong empirical results. While the image representation requires substantial modernization, the core idea is promising. The technical shortcomings are understandable given the exploratory nature of the work, and the paper makes a meaningful contribution. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions addressing the representation and clarity issues.
