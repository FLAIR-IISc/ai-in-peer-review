### Summary

This paper proposes a method to automatically discover latent domains within a training set for the purpose of domain adaptation. The core idea is to partition the training data into a set of domains such that a kernel-based discrepancy measure between all pairs of domains is maximized. This partitioning is formulated as an integer programming problem, subject to two key constraints: (1) each data point must be assigned to exactly one domain, and (2) the class label distribution within each discovered domain must match the global label distribution of the entire training set. The authors relax this problem into a continuous quadratic optimization with linear constraints. The optimal number of domains is determined via cross-validation. The method is evaluated on two datasets, a static image dataset [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset [15], and demonstrates improved classification performance over the domain adaptation technique of [19].

### Strengths

*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   The proposed approach for automatically discovering latent domains appears to be novel, to the best of my knowledge.
*   The reported performance improvements over the strong baseline of [19] are a significant contribution.

### Weaknesses and Major Concerns

My initial recommendation leans toward rejection, but I am open to changing my score if the authors can address the following two major concerns in their rebuttal:

1.  **Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The core motivation for the proposed formulation is not entirely clear. The paper argues (line 77) that "simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains." This claim requires substantiation. First, what is the evidence for this assertion? Second, it is not obvious how the proposed model (Section 2) explicitly avoids this issue. How does the formulation enforce that the discovered domains are not simply proxies for the underlying class categories?

2.  **Necessity of the Label Prior Constraint:** Related to the first point, the rationale for the second constraint (the "label prior constraint" on line 154) is not well-justified. Its impact on the final result is unclear. The paper would be significantly strengthened by an ablation study showing performance without this constraint. Furthermore, to properly evaluate the contribution, additional baselines are needed. Specifically, the authors should compare against partitions generated by:
    *   A standard clustering algorithm like k-means on the appearance features.
    *   An unsupervised object discovery method (e.g., Sivic et al., ICCV 2005).
    *   A simple random partitioning of the data into equally sized sets.

### Questions and Additional Comments

*   **Line 82:** The phrase "maximally different in distribution from each other" is used throughout the paper. Please clarify what distribution this refers to (e.g., the distribution over features, labels, etc.).
*   **Line 166:** As formulated, doesn't the variable β_mk already lie on a simplex? Please clarify why a subsequent projection step is necessary.
*   **Line 182:** The constraints presented here appear to be different from those initially formulated starting on line 153. Please clarify this discrepancy.
*   **Line 215:** Please provide a justification or proof for the bound presented here.
*   **Line 289:** Please provide more implementation details regarding the geodesic flow kernel [4]. Was this a custom re-implementation, or was publicly available source code used?
*   **Lines 313/340:** The analysis of the results would benefit from deeper insights into *why* the proposed method outperforms the baselines. It would be very helpful to include qualitative examples or analysis of systematic failure modes of the baseline methods that are overcome by your approach.
*   **Equation (1):** There appears to be a typo. "M'k" should likely be "Mk'".
*   **Suggested Citation:** The authors may consider citing the following relevant work on dataset bias: "Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias" by Torralba & Efros, CVPR 2011.
