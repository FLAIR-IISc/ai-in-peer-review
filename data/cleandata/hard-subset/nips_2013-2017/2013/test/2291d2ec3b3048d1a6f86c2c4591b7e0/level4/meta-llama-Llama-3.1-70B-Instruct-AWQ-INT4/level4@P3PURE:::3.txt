This paper proposes a convex framework for partitioning datasets into K subsets, referred to as latent domains, such that each subset has a similar semantic class distribution and is as distinct from the others as possible. The distinctiveness, termed maximum distinctiveness, is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences of subsets in the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) induced by a selected kernel. The identification of K, the number of latent domains, is accomplished by maximizing the average learnability within each latent domain, which measures how well a classifier can be learned.

Although forcing the distribution of classes within each domain to be as similar as possible prevents the clusters from being dominated by a single class, this assumption may be limiting for latent domain discovery in certain tasks. For instance, considering poses as latent domains for an animal classification task (e.g., horses and cows), the distribution of latent domains within each class might not be similar. Specifically, both horses and cows are often pictured in left-right standing poses, but horses are rarely depicted in sitting poses, whereas cows are.

While the discovery of latent domains is not new, the idea of controlling class label distributions for better identification of latent domains within a relaxed convex framework is novel. Identifying the number of latent domains by evaluating the quality of classification within each latent domain is also a notable practice, ensuring that each latent domain has a sufficient number of samples for each class to better generalize and learn discrimination between classes.

However, the concepts of dataset, domain, and latent domain are not clearly defined and can be easily confused in several places. These concepts should be explicitly defined, preferably with supporting examples. In particular, the experiments section (4.2) needs clarification. As understood, the terms "dataset" and "domain" are used interchangeably, as Si is referred to as both "datasets" and "source domains." Nevertheless, the experimental setting and concept definitions should be clarified in section 4.2. Additionally, the term "maxk r(U_k,B)" is not defined but is used in equation 7.

The experimental validation appears to be adequate, with the results showing reasonable improvement over the baselines. However, it is unclear why the current selection of source and target datasets is preferred for reporting. For instance, a leave-one-dataset-out adaptation might be a more reasonable evaluation approach. The qualitative results are helpful in visualizing the algorithm's achievements.

The problem statement on line 074, which describes learning latent domains as an unsupervised learning problem, might be misleading since the methods use semantic class labels. The discovery of latent domains via encouraging similar class distributions in each latent domain, formulated in a relaxed convex framework, is a notable technical contribution. However, some concepts in the paper and the experimental validation require clarification.
