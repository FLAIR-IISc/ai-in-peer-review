Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses a common weakness in visual domain adaptation: equating datasets with domains, which collapses distinct nuisance factors (e.g., viewpoint, illumination, background, resolution) into a single source. The authors propose to automatically partition the pooled data across datasets into latent domains guided by two principles: maximum distinctiveness (domains should have maximally different data distributions) and maximum learnability (each domain should support training a strong discriminative classifier for the original labels). The method is nonparametric, measures distributional gaps via kernel mean discrepancies, and relaxes the combinatorial assignment into a continuous quadratic program with a label prior constraint. The number of domains is chosen via domain-wise cross-validation. The approach is also extended to “conditional reshaping” of heterogeneous test data to match discovered training domains. Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) show gains over treating datasets as domains and over a recent latent domain discovery approach based on Gaussian mixtures (e.g., Hoffman et al.).

Strengths:
- Clear motivation: datasets need not coincide with the true underlying factors that cause distribution shift.
- Principled criteria: distinctiveness via kernel two-sample measures and learnability via cross-validated accuracy.
- Nonparametric formulation avoids restrictive mixture assumptions used in prior latent domain discovery work.
- Empirical results show consistent improvements when adapting from discovered domains, and the test-set reshaping idea is promising for heterogeneous targets.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Optimization fidelity: it is not clear how close the relaxed continuous optimization comes to solving the original constrained assignment problem (Eq. 2 + 3). More analysis or bounds would help.
- Limited integration of the two criteria: learnability is only used to choose K in a wrapper-style procedure; the partition itself is driven solely by distinctiveness. A single objective that jointly optimizes distinctiveness and learnability would be more coherent.
- Clarity of conditional reshaping (Sec. 4.3): the description is terse. It effectively assumes an imputation/batch setting where all test data are available at once, but this is not stated or discussed.
- Representation choices: the image descriptor is very weak by current standards—single BoW histograms of sparse SURF with an 800-word codebook. This likely undercuts absolute performance and may affect the generality of conclusions. It is also unclear what similarity/kernel is used for histogram comparison; Euclidean distance is inappropriate for BoW histograms.

Quality and clarity:
- The paper is generally well written. However, more figures would help convey the intuition behind the two principles, illustrate discovered domains, and clarify conditional reshaping.
- Please elaborate on the optimization (practical solvers, convergence behavior, sensitivity to δ in the label prior constraint), and clarify computational complexity.

Originality and significance:
- Automatically discovering useful domains for adaptation has seen limited prior work. The nonparametric distinctiveness criterion and the learnability-based model selection are interesting contributions. If strengthened, this line of work could influence how the community constructs and exploits datasets for domain adaptation.

Experiments:
- Positive: Discovered domains yield significant gains over dataset-as-domain baselines and over a recent latent domain discovery method in Sec. 4.2. Multi-source adaptation strategies using the discovered domains perform well and can approach oracle selection.
- Needs clarification: Sec. 4.3 should clearly define the test-time setting (batch vs. streaming), the matching objective, and any supervision used.
- Representation improvements recommended: adopt spatial pyramids with dense features and appropriate histogram kernels (e.g., chi-square or histogram intersection) rather than Euclidean distances; consider Fisher Vectors to align with modern practice. Useful references include: Zhang et al. (IJCV 2006), Jurie & Triggs (CVPR 2005), Lazebnik et al. (CVPR 2006), Perronnin et al. (ECCV 2010).

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and underexplored problem.
- Sound high-level idea with a nonparametric formulation.
- Demonstrated empirical gains and practical utility.

Cons against acceptance:
- Insufficient integration of distinctiveness and learnability in a single objective.
- Limited analysis of the relaxed optimization and its relation to the original problem.
- Outdated and weak image representation; unclear similarity metrics.
- Conditional reshaping is underexplained and assumes a batch test setting.

Recommendation:
Despite the above limitations—some of which reflect the exploratory nature of this direction—the paper offers a valuable step toward principled domain discovery and shows meaningful empirical benefits. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the optimization and conditional reshaping, and strengthening the experimental setup with modern representations and appropriate histogram kernels.
