This paper proposes a convex framework that partitions the dataset(s) into K subsets (“latent domains”) such that each subset has similar semantic class distributions and the subsets are as distinct from one another as possible. Distinctiveness (“maximum distinctiveness”) is enforced by maximizing the pairwise mean differences of the subsets in the RKHS induced by a chosen kernel. The number of latent domains K is selected by maximizing average learnability within each latent domain, i.e., how well a classifier can be learned.

Although enforcing similar class distributions within each domain prevents clusters from being dominated by a single class, this assumption may be limiting for certain tasks. For example, if poses serve as latent domains in an animal classification task (e.g., horses vs. cows), the pose distribution may not be similar across classes. Both horses and cows appear in left–right standing poses, but horses are rarely shown sitting, whereas cows often are.

While latent-domain discovery is not new, explicitly controlling class-label distributions to improve domain identification within a relaxed convex framework appears novel. Determining K by evaluating within-domain classification quality is also a notable choice, helping ensure each latent domain has sufficient samples per class to generalize and learn class discrimination.

In several places, the notions of dataset, domain, and latent domain are unclear and easily confused. These concepts should be defined clearly, ideally with examples. The experimental setup in Section 4.2 especially needs clarification. As far as I can tell, “dataset” and “domain” are used interchangeably, since S_i is referred to both as datasets and as source domains. The experimental protocol and terminology should be clarified in Section 4.2. Additionally, maxk r(U_k, B) is used in Eq. 7 but is not defined.

The experimental validation appears adequate, with reasonable improvements over baselines. However, the rationale for the chosen source and target datasets is unclear; for instance, a leave-one-dataset-out adaptation protocol might be a more appropriate evaluation. The qualitative results are helpful in visualizing what the algorithm discovers.

On line 074, describing the problem (learning latent domains) as unsupervised may be misleading, since the method uses semantic class labels. Discovering latent domains by encouraging similar class distributions within a relaxed convex framework is a notable technical contribution, but several concepts and aspects of the experimental evaluation require clarification.
