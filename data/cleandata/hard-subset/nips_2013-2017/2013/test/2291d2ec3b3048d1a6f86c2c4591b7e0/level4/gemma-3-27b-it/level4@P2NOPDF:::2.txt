This paper introduces a novel approach to domain adaptation for computer vision, addressing the challenge of varying factors like viewpoint, illumination, and clutter across datasets. Rather than treating entire datasets as single domains, the authors propose an automatic partitioning of images – across datasets – into domains based on two key principles: maximizing separation in feature space and ensuring each domain supports the training of a highly accurate classifier for the original task. This approach aims to discover domains aligned with underlying visual aspects rather than arbitrary dataset boundaries.

**Originality and Significance:**

The work addresses a relatively unexplored area – the automatic definition of domains for adaptation. The proposed method presents a promising direction for discovering domains that improve classifier performance. While the optimization method detailed in Section 2 raises some concerns regarding its direct connection to the stated objective functions (2)+(3), and the integration of the two driving criteria (separability and learnability) currently feels somewhat disjointed, the paper still represents a valuable contribution. Currently, the learnability criterion primarily functions as a post-hoc validation step to determine the optimal number of domains, rather than actively influencing the partitioning process itself. A more tightly integrated approach, directly optimizing a combined objective function, could further enhance the method. Despite these limitations – potentially stemming from the exploratory nature of the work – I believe this paper moves the field forward and warrants acceptance.

**Quality and Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written; however, the inclusion of illustrative figures would significantly improve understanding of the core concepts. Visualizations of the domain partitioning and resulting feature spaces would be particularly beneficial.

**Experiments:**

The experimental results demonstrate a clear advantage of using the proposed domain discovery method compared to both treating datasets as domains and a recent competing technique [19] (Section 4.2). The inclusion of the test set during the ‘reshaping’ process is an interesting idea, though its presentation lacks clarity (Section 4.3). It is crucial to explicitly state that this approach assumes simultaneous availability of all test data, corresponding to an imputation setting. 

However, a significant limitation lies in the simplicity of the image descriptor employed. The use of a single bag-of-words representation with sparse SURF features and only 800 codebook entries is outdated. Modern approaches routinely utilize more powerful representations such as spatial pyramids of bag-of-words computed on dense SURF features. Additionally, the paper does not specify the similarity measure used for comparing image descriptors; I hope it is not Euclidean distance, which is inappropriate for histograms, and instead employs a metric such as Chi-squared distance or an intersection kernel. Further improvements could be achieved by exploring Fisher Vector representations, bringing the image representation in line with state-of-the-art systems. The authors might find the following papers helpful: Zhang IJCV 2006, Jurie CVPR 2005, Lazebnik CVPR 2006, and Perronin ECCV 2010.

**Overall:**

This paper presents interesting and novel ideas for addressing an important problem in domain adaptation and achieves promising results. While improvements, particularly regarding the image representation, are recommended, the work offers a valuable contribution to the field and demonstrates a clear step towards more effective domain adaptation techniques.
