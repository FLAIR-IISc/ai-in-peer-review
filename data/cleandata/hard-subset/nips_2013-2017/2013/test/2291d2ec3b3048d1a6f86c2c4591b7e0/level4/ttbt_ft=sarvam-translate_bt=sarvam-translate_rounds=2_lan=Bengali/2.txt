This paper presents a novel technique for domain adaptation for computer vision datasets, which typically represent multiple aspects such as perspective, lighting, degree of clutter, compression, etc. Instead of directly comparing the domain to the dataset, which would mix those aspects together, the proposed technique automatically divides the entire set of images in the dataset into domains. The partitioner is driven by two principles: creating separate domains in the feature space and building them in such a way that a good discriminative classifier can be trained on each of them (not to separate the main classes, but to classify them). This technique is more likely to partition according to the underlying aspect than the dataset itself.

Basic and Significance:
Very little work has been done on automatically defining domains, and this paper proposes a good idea towards automatically discovering useful domains, which will help train a better classifier for the main problem.

The disadvantage is that I am not entirely sure about the proposed optimization method (section 2), as it is not clear how it solves the main problem (2)+(3). Moreover, the two driving forces proposed are not yet well integrated: the maximum learning criterion is only used as a 'wrapper' for the maximum separation criterion, to determine the number of domains. Basically, it acts as a post-hoc validation score that decides how good the learned domain partition is for a specific number of domains, but the partition is based only on the separation criterion. An integrated process would create a partition that maximizes some target function with both criteria.

Despite these shortcomings, which are probably due to the fact that such work is still rather in the exploratory phase, I believe this paper is a step in the right direction for the community and should be adopted.

Quality and Clarity:
The paper is well-written, but there is a lack of illustrations to illustrate the concepts presented.

Testing:
The positive aspect is that the tests show a significant advantage compared to using the dataset as a domain when using the proposed method for creating domains, and compared to the very recent domain discovery technique [19] (section 4.2).

The idea of including test sets in the 'reshaping' process is interesting, but not clearly presented (section 4.3). Also, it is consistent with an imputation setting (i.e., all test data are available at the same time), but this is not explicitly stated.

The disadvantage is that the image descriptors used are too simple and old: a bag of words for the entire image and only 800 codebook entries. This is really weak these days. I suggest the authors use a spatial pyramid of words calculated on the dense SURF feature. Also, it is not clear what kind of similarity measure is used to compare the image descriptors, hopefully X^2 or an intersection kernel? Euclidean distance histograms are not suitable for comparison. As a next step, a Fisher vector representation could help further and eventually take image representation to the level of modern systems in this paper. The following papers may help the authors: Zhang IJCVI 2006; Jurie CVPIR 2005; Lazebnik CVPIR 2006; Perronin ICCV 2010.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting new idea on an important problem and achieves good results. The paper can be improved in several ways, especially in the field of image representation used.
