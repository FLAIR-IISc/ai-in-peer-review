This paper aims to automatically discover latent domains in a training set, which is then utilized in a domain adaptation framework to achieve improved classification performance on a test set. The authors define a function that measures the difference between two feature vectors over a specified kernel. The objective is to partition the data points into domains such that this function is maximized over the set of points across each pair of domains. This problem is formulated as an integer programming problem with two constraints: each point is assigned to exactly one domain, and the distribution over class labels in each domain must match the input distribution over the entire point set. To make the problem more tractable, it is relaxed to a continuous optimization over a quadratic cost with linear constraints. Finally, the number of domains is determined via cross-validation.

The proposed approach is evaluated on two datasets: static images of [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset of [15]. Notably, the approach achieves improved performance over the domain adaptation method of [19].

The paper has several strengths. It is well-written, and to the best of my knowledge, the approach is novel (although I am not an expert in domain adaptation). The performance gains over [19] are also appreciated.

However, I initially leaned towards rejecting the paper due to two primary concerns that I would like the authors to address in their rebuttal, potentially changing my assessment:

(i) The motivation behind this paper is unclear. On line 77, the authors argue that "simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains." Firstly, what evidence supports this claim? Secondly, how does the model formulation in Section 2 overcome this issue, i.e., how is the reshaping into per-category domains prevented?

(ii) Somewhat related, on line 154, I question the necessity of the second constraint ("label prior constraint"). I am curious to know the performance without this constraint. In fact, a baseline where the data is partitioned using k-means clustering or unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al. ICCV '05) over the appearance vectors should be provided. Additionally, what is the performance when the dataset is randomly partitioned into equal sets?

Some additional comments:

+ Line 82: The phrase "maximally different in distribution from each other" is mentioned throughout the paper. It would be beneficial to clarify what this means, specifically the distribution over what?
+ Line 166: Doesn't \beta_{mk} as formulated already live on a simplex?
+ Line 182: These constraints seem to differ from those formulated earlier (starting on line 153); could the authors clarify this discrepancy?
+ Line 215: What is the justification or proof for this bound?
+ Line 289: Please provide more details on the use of the geodesic flow kernel [4]. Was this a reimplementation or was publicly available source code used?
+ Lines 313/340: It would be helpful to provide insights into the differences in performance, enabling a better understanding of why the proposed approach performs better. It would be beneficial to demonstrate systematic failures of the baselines that the proposed approach overcomes.
+ Eq (1): M'k should be corrected to Mk'.
+ The following citation may be relevant to include:
Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias A. Torralba, A. Efros. IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition (CVPR), 2011.

After considering the authors' rebuttal, which addressed my concerns regarding the paper's motivation and the label prior constraint, I lean slightly towards accepting the paper.
