This paper proposes a convex framework that partitions one or more datasets into K subsets (“latent domains”) such that each subset has similar semantic class distributions, while the subsets are as distinct from one another as possible. Distinctiveness—termed maximum distinctiveness—is enforced by maximizing pairwise mean differences between subsets in the RKHS induced by a chosen kernel. The number of latent domains, K, is selected by maximizing average learnability (i.e., how well a classifier can be trained) within each latent domain.

Encouraging similar class distributions within each domain helps prevent clusters from being dominated by a single class, but it may be a limiting assumption for some tasks. For example, if poses are treated as latent domains in an animal classification task (e.g., horses and cows), the pose distributions may differ by class: both horses and cows are seen in left-right standing poses, but cows are often photographed sitting, whereas horses rarely are. In such cases, enforcing similar class distributions across domains could obscure meaningful latent structure.

While latent domain discovery itself is not new, the idea of controlling class-label distributions to improve latent domain identification within a (relaxed) convex framework is novel. Using within-domain classification quality to select K is also a sensible practice: it helps ensure that each latent domain contains sufficient samples for each class to support generalization and discrimination.

However, the manuscript is sometimes unclear about the notions of dataset, domain, and latent domain, which are easy to conflate. These concepts should be defined precisely, preferably with illustrative examples. In particular, Section 4.2 requires clarification. As far as I can tell, “dataset” and “domain” are used interchangeably, since S_i is referred to both as datasets and as source domains. The experimental setting and the underlying concept definitions should be made explicit in Section 4.2. Additionally, max_k r(U_k, B) is used in Eq. 7 but not defined.

The experimental validation appears adequate, and the method shows reasonable improvements over the baselines. Nonetheless, the rationale behind the chosen source and target datasets is not clearly justified. A leave-one-dataset-out adaptation protocol would be a more natural and informative evaluation strategy. The qualitative results are helpful and provide intuition about what the algorithm is discovering.

Finally, on line 074, framing the problem of learning latent domains as “unsupervised” may be misleading, given that the method uses semantic class labels.

Overall, discovering latent domains by encouraging similar class distributions within a relaxed convex framework is a notable technical contribution. With clearer definitions of key concepts and a more transparent experimental setup—particularly in Section 4.2—the paper would be significantly strengthened.
