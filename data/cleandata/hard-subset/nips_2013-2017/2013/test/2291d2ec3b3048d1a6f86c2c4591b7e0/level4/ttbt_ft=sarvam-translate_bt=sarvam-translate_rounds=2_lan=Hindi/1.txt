The goal of this work is to automatically find embedded domains in training sets, which are used in domain optimization frameworks to achieve better classification performance on test sets. This paper defines a function that measures the difference between two feature vectors on a specified kernel. The goal is to partition the data points into domains in such a way that the function is maximized on each set in each domain. The problem is formulated as an integer programming problem with two constraints: each point is assigned exactly one domain, and the distribution over the class labels in each domain must match the input distribution over the entire set. The problem is relaxed for continuous optimization on a quadratic cost with linear constraints. Finally, the number of domains is determined through cross-validation.

This approach has been evaluated on two datasets: the static images of [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset of [15]. A key finding is that the domain optimization approach of [19] showed better performance.

Pros: The paper is well-written, and as far as I know, the approach is new (although I am not an expert in domain optimization). The performance advantage at [19] is also appreciated.

Cons: At this point, I am a bit skeptical. I have two main concerns that I would like to address in the rebuttal that might convince me to change my score:

(i) The motivation for this paper is not clear to me. The paper argues at line 77 that "the ability to transform the dataset into per-class domains is the only thing that clusters images according to their appearance". First, what is the evidence for this claim? Second, how does the model construction in section 2 address this issue, i.e., how is non-reconstruction applied in per-class domains?

(ii) To some extent related, why is the second constraint ("label prior constraint") needed at line 154? I am curious what the performance would be like without this constraint. In fact, a baseline where the data is divided on appearance vectors using k-means clustering or unsupervised object detection (e.g., Civic et al. ICCV '05) should be shown. Also, what is the performance when the dataset is randomly divided into equal sets?
