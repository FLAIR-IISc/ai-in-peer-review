This paper proposes a convex framework for partitioning a dataset into K subsets, referred to as latent domains, such that each subset has a similar semantic class distribution and is as distinct from the others as possible. The distinctiveness of these subsets, termed maximum distinctiveness, is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences of subsets in the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) induced by a selected kernel. The identification of K, the number of latent domains, is determined by maximizing the average learnability within each latent domain, which measures how well a classifier can be learned.

Although the proposed approach prevents clusters from being dominated by a single class by forcing similar class distributions within each domain, this assumption may be limiting for certain tasks. For instance, in an animal classification task where poses are considered as latent domains (e.g., horses and cows), the distribution of latent domains within each class may not be similar. Specifically, while both horses and cows are often depicted in left-right standing poses, horses are rarely shown in a sitting pose, whereas cows are. This example highlights a potential limitation of the proposed approach.

While the discovery of latent domains is not a new concept, the idea of controlling class label distributions to improve latent domain identification within a relaxed convex framework is a novel contribution. The method of determining the number of latent domains by evaluating the quality of classification within each latent domain is also noteworthy, as it ensures that each latent domain has a sufficient number of samples for each class to facilitate better generalization and learning of class discrimination.

However, the paper could benefit from clearer definitions and distinctions between the concepts of dataset, domain, and latent domain. These concepts are often used interchangeably, which may lead to confusion. For example, in Section 4.2, the terms "dataset" and "domain" are used to refer to the same entity, Si, which is both a dataset and a source domain. Clarifying these concepts with supporting examples would improve the paper's readability. Additionally, the term "maxk r(U_k,B)" is used in Equation 7 without being explicitly defined.

The experimental validation appears to be adequate, with the results showing reasonable improvements over the baselines. However, it is unclear why the authors chose to report on the specific source and target datasets used in the experiments. An alternative evaluation approach, such as leave-one-dataset-out adaptation, may have provided more comprehensive results. The qualitative results, on the other hand, are helpful in visualizing the algorithm's performance.

The problem statement on line 074 refers to learning latent domains as an unsupervised learning problem, which may be misleading since the method relies on semantic class labels. Nevertheless, the discovery of latent domains via encouraging similar class distributions in each latent domain, formulated within a relaxed convex framework, is a notable technical contribution. However, some concepts in the paper and the experimental validation require further clarification to enhance the overall quality of the manuscript.
