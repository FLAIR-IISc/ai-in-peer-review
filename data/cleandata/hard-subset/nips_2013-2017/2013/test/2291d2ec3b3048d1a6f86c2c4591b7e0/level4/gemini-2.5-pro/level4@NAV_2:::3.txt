This paper addresses the problem of discovering latent domains within visual datasets, a crucial but often overlooked step in domain adaptation. The central idea is that standard datasets are often heterogeneous mixtures of underlying data distributions (e.g., due to variations in viewpoint, lighting, background). Treating an entire dataset as a single source domain is therefore suboptimal. The authors propose an automated method to partition one or more source datasets into a set of K latent domains. The proposed framework is guided by two key principles: maximizing the "distinctiveness" between the discovered domains, and ensuring the "learnability" of a strong classifier within each domain. Distinctiveness is formulated as maximizing the pairwise Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) between the discovered domains. Learnability is used as a criterion to select the optimal number of domains, K, by assessing the cross-validated classification performance within each discovered domain. This work is most closely related to Hoffman et al. [20], but differs by employing a non-parametric approach for measuring distribution difference, avoiding the need for specific distributional assumptions like Gaussian mixtures.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles an important and practical problem in domain adaptation. While the idea of latent domain discovery is not entirely new (e.g., [20]), the proposed formulation is novel and compelling. Framing the problem as a constrained optimization that maximizes inter-domain MMD is elegant. The use of a Label Prior Constraint (LPC) to prevent trivial, class-based clustering is a clever way to leverage available label information to guide the discovery of domain structure, rather than class structure.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The formulation of domain discovery as a relaxed convex optimization problem is a significant contribution. The Domain-Wise Cross-Validation (DWCV) procedure for automatically selecting the number of domains K is principled and addresses a key hyperparameter choice.
*   **Clarity and Completeness:** The paper is generally well-written and the proposed approach is clearly explained. The experimental validation is thorough, including comparisons to relevant baselines and an ablation study on the benefits of reshaping both source and target sets. The qualitative results in Figure 1 provide valuable insight into what kinds of latent structures the algorithm is able to discover.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **The Label Prior Constraint (LPC) Assumption:** The LPC, which enforces similar class distributions across all discovered domains, is a strong assumption. While it effectively prevents the algorithm from simply clustering by class, it may be too restrictive for certain real-world scenarios. For example, consider a task of animal classification where pose defines the latent domains. It is plausible that certain classes (e.g., cows) are frequently imaged in a sitting pose, whereas other classes (e.g., horses) are not. In such a case, the true domain-specific class priors would be different, and enforcing them to be similar might hinder the discovery of the correct latent domains. It would strengthen the paper to discuss this limitation and potential scenarios where this assumption might not hold.
*   **Clarity of Terminology and Notation:** The terms "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain" are used somewhat interchangeably, particularly in the experimental section, which can cause confusion. For instance, in Section 4.2, the sets `Si` are referred to as both "datasets" and "source domains." A clearer, more formal definition of these terms early in the paper and their consistent use throughout would improve readability. Furthermore, the notation `r(A → B)` is used in Equation 6 without a formal definition, which should be added.
*   **Experimental Protocol:** The experiments show reasonable improvements over baselines. However, the motivation for choosing the specific combinations of source and target datasets for the experiments is not fully explained. A more systematic evaluation, such as a leave-one-dataset-out protocol (e.g., using A, D, W as source and C as target, and rotating), would provide a more comprehensive and robust validation of the method's effectiveness across different adaptation scenarios.
*   **Characterization as Unsupervised:** The paper describes the domain discovery problem as "unsupervised" (Line 74). While the domain assignments `zm` are indeed learned without direct supervision, the method explicitly uses the class labels `ym` in the Label Prior Constraint. This might be misleading. It would be more accurate to describe the method as being unsupervised with respect to domain labels but supervised with respect to class labels.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and well-motivated framework for automatically discovering latent domains in visual data for adaptation. The technical contribution, centered on a constrained optimization that maximizes domain distinctiveness while ensuring learnability, is significant. While the core assumption of the Label Prior Constraint could be further discussed and the experimental protocol could be more systematic, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The provided suggestions are intended to help clarify and further strengthen the work.
