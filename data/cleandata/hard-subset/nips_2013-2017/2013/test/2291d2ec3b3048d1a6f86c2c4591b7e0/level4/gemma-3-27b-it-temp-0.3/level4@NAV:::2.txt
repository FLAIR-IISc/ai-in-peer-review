This paper introduces a novel domain adaptation technique for computer vision, addressing the common challenge of variations like viewpoint, illumination, and clutter across datasets. The key innovation lies in moving beyond treating datasets as fixed domains and instead automatically partitioning images from all datasets into domains based on inherent visual characteristics. This partitioning is guided by two principles: maximizing separation in feature space and ensuring each resulting domain supports the training of a strong discriminative classifier for the original task. This approach aims to align domain definitions with the underlying sources of variation, rather than arbitrary dataset boundaries.

**Originality and Significance:**

The work addresses a relatively unexplored area – automatic domain definition – and presents a promising approach to discovering useful domains that can improve classifier performance. While the proposed optimization method (section 2) raises some concerns regarding its direct alignment with the combined objectives (2)+(3), the overall concept is valuable. A limitation lies in the current integration of the two driving criteria; the maximal learnability criterion currently functions as a post-hoc validation step for determining the number of domains, rather than being directly incorporated into the partitioning process itself. An integrated approach, optimizing a function that explicitly balances both criteria, would be a beneficial extension. Nevertheless, despite these points, this work represents a positive step for the field.

**Quality and Clarity:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented, though the inclusion of illustrative figures would significantly enhance understanding of the concepts.

**Experiments:**

The experimental results demonstrate a clear advantage of the proposed domain discovery method over both treating datasets as domains and a recent related technique [19] (sec. 4.2). The inclusion of the test set in the reshaping process (sec. 4.3) is an interesting idea, but requires more detailed explanation, particularly regarding the implicit assumption of simultaneous test data availability (an imputation setting). 

However, the current image descriptor is a significant limitation. Relying on a single bag-of-words representation of sparse SURF features with only 800 codebook entries is outdated. I strongly recommend exploring a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words computed on dense SURF features. Furthermore, the similarity measure used for comparing descriptors should be clarified; Euclidean distance is unsuitable for histograms, and a chi-squared distance or intersection kernel would be more appropriate.  A Fisher Vector representation could further improve performance and bring the image representation in line with current state-of-the-art methods. Relevant references include: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010.

**Overall:**

This paper presents novel and interesting ideas for tackling an important problem, achieving encouraging results. While improvements, particularly in the image representation, are recommended, the work is a valuable contribution and should be accepted.
