

This paper addresses the problem of automatically discovering latent domains within visual datasets to improve domain adaptation performance. The authors propose a method that identifies domains based on two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (ensuring domains are maximally different from each other in distribution) and maximum learnability (ensuring each domain can support strong discriminative models). The approach formulates domain discovery as an optimization problem that maximizes the sum of distribution differences across all domain pairs, subject to constraints preserving label distributions within each domain. The number of domains is determined through domain-wise cross-validation. The method is evaluated on object recognition (using Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, and Webcam datasets) and human activity recognition (using IXMAS multi-view action dataset), showing improved performance over baselines that treat datasets as single domains or use naive clustering.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing a fundamental limitation in visual domain adaptation research: the common practice of equating datasets with domains, which fails to account for the complex, overlapping factors (lighting, pose, background, etc.) that create natural domain boundaries within datasets. While previous work has explored domain adaptation between datasets [2, 4, 16-19], this paper is the first to propose a principled method for discovering latent domains within datasets themselves, as noted in the related work section. The authors' approach differs significantly from [20] by using nonparametric methods rather than parametric mixture models, which better handles the complexity of real visual data.

Strengths: The paper is well-written and clearly structured, making a compelling case for the importance of latent domain discovery. The technical approach is sound, with careful consideration of constraints and a well-motivated optimization procedure. The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over treating datasets as single domains (as in standard domain adaptation studies) and over the related work of [20], providing strong empirical evidence for the method's effectiveness. The analysis of identified domains (Figure 1) reveals that the discovered domains correspond to meaningful visual factors like object pose and background, which validates the method's ability to uncover natural domain structures.

Weaknesses: While the paper addresses the problem of domain discovery well, the motivation for why standard clustering (e.g., K-means) fails to discover meaningful domains is not sufficiently supported. The claim that "simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains" (line 77) requires stronger empirical evidence. The paper should include a comparison showing how naive clustering produces per-category domains versus the proposed method. Additionally, the label prior constraint (LPC) is critical to the method's performance, but the paper does not clearly explain why it's necessary or provide results showing performance degradation without it. A baseline comparing the proposed method with and without LPC would strengthen the argument.

The paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of why the proposed approach outperforms baselines. For instance, Figure 2 shows domain-wise cross-validation accuracy, but the paper doesn't explain why the optimal number of domains coincides with the number of input datasets (as noted in the conclusion) when the ideal domain boundaries don't align with dataset boundaries. Providing specific examples where the proposed method overcomes limitations of baselines would enhance the significance of the results.

The approach is theoretically sound and well-executed, but the paper could better justify its claims by addressing these points. The method's practical utility is demonstrated through multiple domain adaptation experiments, but the paper should clarify how the method differs from simply using multiple source domains (which is a standard approach in multi-domain adaptation [28, 29]).

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel approach to latent domain discovery addressing a fundamental limitation in visual domain adaptation
- Strong empirical results showing significant improvements over standard domain adaptation approaches
- Clear theoretical foundation with two well-motivated properties (maximum distinctiveness and learnability)
- Well-written with thorough experimental validation across multiple datasets

Cons for acceptance:
- Insufficient evidence for the claim that standard clustering produces per-category domains
- Lack of analysis showing performance impact of removing the label prior constraint
- Limited explanation of why the proposed method outperforms baselines beyond empirical results
- Some technical details (e.g., the bound for Kmax in Section 2.2) lack justification

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to domain adaptation by introducing a principled method for discovering latent domains within datasets. While the draft review identified some concerns about motivation and the label prior constraint, the paper's core idea is compelling and well-supported by experiments. With minor clarifications addressing the points raised above, this work would be a strong contribution to NIPS. The approach provides a valuable tool for improving domain adaptation performance and could inform future dataset construction by revealing natural domain structures within existing datasets. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
