This paper proposes a convex framework for splitting the dataset(s) into K subsets such that each subset has similar semantic class distributions and as distinct from each other as possible. Distinction, named as maximum distinctiveness, is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences of subsets in the RKHS induced by a selected kernel. Each of these subsets are named as latent domains. Identification of K (number of latent domains) is achieved by maximizing average learnability (how well a classifier can be learned) within each latent domain.

The proposed approach is technically sound, with a clear and well-motivated formulation. The use of a non-parametric method to measure domain discrepancies and the introduction of a relaxed convex optimization problem are notable contributions. The experimental validation appears to be adequate, with reasonable improvements above the baselines.

However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement. The assumption that the distribution of classes within each domain should be similar may not hold for certain tasks, such as animal classification where poses can be considered as latent domains. Additionally, the concepts of dataset, domain, and latent domain are not clearly defined and can be easily confused. The experimental setting and concept definitions should be clarified, particularly in section 4.2.

The experimental validation could also be improved by providing more details on the selection of source and target datasets. For instance, leave one dataset out adaptation might be a more reasonable evaluation. Furthermore, the qualitative results are helpful, but more analysis on the identified domains and the optimal number of domains would be beneficial.

In terms of originality, the idea of controlling class label distributions for better identification of latent domains within a (relaxed) convex framework is new. However, the discovery of latent domains is not new, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on how it differs from previous work.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in domain adaptation, and the proposed approach has the potential to improve the performance of domain adaptation algorithms. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the proposed approach and its potential applications.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed approach is technically sound. However, there are some limitations and areas for improvement that need to be addressed.
