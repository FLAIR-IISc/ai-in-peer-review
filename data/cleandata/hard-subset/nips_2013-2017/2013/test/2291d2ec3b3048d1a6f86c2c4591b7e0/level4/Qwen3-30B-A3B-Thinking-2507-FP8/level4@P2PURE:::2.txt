

This paper introduces a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision datasets, which typically exhibit multiple aspects such as viewpoint, illumination, clutter level, and compression. Rather than equating domains with datasets—which conflates these aspects—the proposed method automatically partitions the entire image set across all datasets into meaningful domains. The partitioner operates under two principles: (1) creating distinct domains in feature space, and (2) ensuring each domain supports training a strong discriminative classifier for the original class labels (not for domain separation). This approach is more likely to partition based on underlying aspects rather than dataset boundaries.  

Originality and significance:  
The paper addresses a significant gap in automatically defining domains, proposing a promising method for discovering useful domains to improve original task classifiers. This represents a meaningful step toward more effective domain adaptation.  

On the negative side, the proposed optimization method (Section 2) lacks clarity on how closely it solves the original problem (Equations 2–3). Furthermore, the two driving criteria are not well integrated: the maximal learnability criterion functions merely as a post-hoc validation score to select the number of domains, while the partitioning itself relies solely on the maximal separability criterion. An integrated approach would directly optimize a goal function combining both criteria, rather than using learnability as a wrapper.  

Despite these limitations—likely reflecting the exploratory nature of this work—the paper advances the field and merits acceptance.  

Quality and clarity:  
The paper is well-written but lacks illustrative figures to clarify key concepts.  

Experiments:  
The experiments demonstrate significant improvements using the proposed domains compared to treating datasets as domains and to the recent domain discovery method [19] (Section 4.2). The inclusion of the test set in the "reshaping" process is intriguing but not clearly explained (Section 4.3). This approach assumes an imputation setting (all test data available simultaneously), which is not explicitly stated.  

On the negative side, the image descriptor is overly simplistic and outdated: a single bag-of-words using sparse SURF features for the entire image with only 800 codebook entries. Modern systems would require a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words on dense SURF features. Additionally, the similarity measure for descriptor comparison is unspecified; it must be X² or an intersection kernel (Euclidean distance is inappropriate for histograms). A Fisher Vector representation would further modernize the approach. Recommended references: Zhang et al. (IJCV 2006), Jurie et al. (CVPR 2005), Lazebnik et al. (CVPR 2006), Perronnin et al. (ECCV 2010).  

Overall, the paper presents novel and important ideas on a key problem, achieving strong results. However, it requires substantial improvement in image representation to align with current practices.
