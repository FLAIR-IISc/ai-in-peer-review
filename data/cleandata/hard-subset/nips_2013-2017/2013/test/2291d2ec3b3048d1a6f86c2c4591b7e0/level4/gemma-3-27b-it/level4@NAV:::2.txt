This paper introduces a novel domain adaptation technique for computer vision, addressing the challenge of variations within datasets such as viewpoint, illumination, and clutter. The approach departs from treating datasets as fixed domains, instead automatically partitioning images from all datasets into domains based on two key principles: maximizing feature space separation between domains and ensuring each resulting domain supports the training of an effective classifier for the original task. This strategy aims to identify domains that align with underlying visual aspects rather than simply dataset boundaries.

**Originality and Significance:**

The work addresses a relatively unexplored area – automatic domain definition – and offers a promising approach to discovering useful domains that can improve classifier performance. While the proposed optimization method (section 2) warrants further scrutiny regarding its fidelity to the original problem formulation (equations 2 & 3), the overall concept is valuable. A limitation lies in the current integration of the two driving criteria. The maximal learnability criterion presently functions as a post-hoc validation step for the number of domains, rather than being directly incorporated into the partitioning process itself. Ideally, a unified objective function encompassing both separability and learnability would yield a more cohesive partitioning strategy. Nevertheless, despite these areas for improvement, this work represents a constructive step forward for the field.

**Quality and Clarity:**

The paper is well-written; however, the inclusion of illustrative figures would significantly enhance understanding of the presented concepts.

**Experiments:**

The experimental results demonstrate a clear benefit to using the proposed domain partitioning method compared to both dataset-as-domain approaches and a recent domain discovery technique [19] (section 4.2). The inclusion of the test set during the ‘reshaping’ process is an intriguing idea, though its presentation could be clarified (section 4.3), specifically noting that it operates within an imputation setting where all test data is simultaneously available. 

A key area for improvement concerns the image descriptor. The current reliance on a single bag-of-words representation based on sparse SURF features (with a limited 800-entry codebook) is outdated.  Employing a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words computed on dense SURF features would be a substantial improvement. Clarification regarding the similarity measure used for descriptor comparison is also needed – ideally, a chi-squared statistic or intersection kernel should be employed, as Euclidean distance is unsuitable for histograms. Future work exploring Fisher Vector representations could further enhance the image representation and bring the paper’s methodology in line with modern computer vision systems. Relevant references include Zhang IJCV 2006, Jurie CVPR 2005, Lazebnik CVPR 2006, and Perronin ECCV 2010.

In conclusion, this paper presents novel and interesting ideas for tackling an important problem, achieving promising results. While further refinements, particularly in the image representation, are recommended, the paper warrants acceptance.
