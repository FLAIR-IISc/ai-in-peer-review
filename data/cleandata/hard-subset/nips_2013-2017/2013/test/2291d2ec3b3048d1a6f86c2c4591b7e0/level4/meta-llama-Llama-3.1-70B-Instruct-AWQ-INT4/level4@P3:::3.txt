This paper proposes a novel approach for automatically discovering latent domains in image or video datasets, which is crucial for domain adaptation in visual recognition problems. The authors introduce two key properties that latent domains should possess: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. They devise a nonparametric formulation and efficient optimization procedure to discover domains that satisfy these properties.

The main idea is to split the dataset(s) into K subsets, named as latent domains, such that each subset has similar semantic class distributions and is as distinct from each other as possible. The distinction, named as maximum distinctiveness, is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences of subsets in the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) induced by a selected kernel. The identification of K (number of latent domains) is achieved by maximizing average learnability (how well a classifier can be learned) within each latent domain.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several domain adaptation tasks for object recognition and human activity recognition. The experimental results show that their method outperforms the original datasets and the domains discovered by a competing method. The qualitative results are also helpful in understanding what the algorithm visually achieves.

However, there are some concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the concepts of dataset, domain, and latent domain are not clearly defined and can be easily confused. These concepts should be clearly defined, and preferably with some supporting examples. Particularly, the experiments section 4.2 needs clarification. As far as I understand, the words dataset and domain are used interchangeably since Si is both named as datasets and source domains. Nevertheless, the experimental setting and the concept definition should be clarified in section 4.2.

Additionally, the notation maxk r(Uk,B) is not defined but used in equation (6). It would be helpful to define this notation clearly. Furthermore, the experimental validation appears to be adequate, but the selection of source and target datasets to report on is not clearly justified. For instance, leave one dataset out adaptation might be a more reasonable evaluation.

Lastly, the problem of learning latent domains is stated as an unsupervised learning problem, which might be misleading since the methods use semantic class labels. The discovery of latent domains via encouraging similar class distribution in each latent domain formulated in a (relaxed) convex framework is a notable technical contribution. However, some concepts in the paper and the experimental validation need to be clarified.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach for automatically discovering latent domains in image or video datasets.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several domain adaptation tasks for object recognition and human activity recognition.
* The qualitative results are helpful in understanding what the algorithm visually achieves.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The concepts of dataset, domain, and latent domain are not clearly defined and can be easily confused.
* The experimental validation appears to be adequate, but the selection of source and target datasets to report on is not clearly justified.
* The problem of learning latent domains is stated as an unsupervised learning problem, which might be misleading since the methods use semantic class labels.

Overall, the paper makes a notable technical contribution to the field of domain adaptation, but some clarifications are needed to improve the paper's quality and readability.
