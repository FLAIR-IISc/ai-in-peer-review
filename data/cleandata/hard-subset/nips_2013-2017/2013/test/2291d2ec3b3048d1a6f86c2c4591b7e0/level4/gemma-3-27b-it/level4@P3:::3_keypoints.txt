- Proposes a novel framework to automatically discover latent domains by partitioning data into K subsets that maximize distinctiveness (RKHS distribution differences) and learnability (cross-validated classifier performance).
- Enforces similar semantic class distributions within each discovered domain via a label prior constraint during optimization.
- Selects the number of domains K by choosing the value that maximizes average classification accuracy across the discovered domains.
- Formulates latent domain discovery as a convex optimization problem — a key technical contribution.
- Balances domain distinctiveness and class-distribution consistency, integrating a learnability criterion to ensure discovered domains are useful for training classifiers.
- Empirical results show improvements over baselines on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, with qualitative analysis illustrating captured factors.
- Main limitation: the assumption of similar class distributions within domains can be unrealistic when domain factors correlate with class presence (e.g., pose differences across animal classes).
- Clarity issues: inconsistent/ambiguous use of “dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain,” notably in Section 4.2; undefined term "maxk r(U_k,B)" in Eq.7.
- Recommendation to add concrete examples and clear definitions to illustrate distinctions between dataset/domain/latent domain.
- Experimental concerns: insufficient justification for chosen source/target datasets; suggests more robust evaluation such as leave-one-dataset-out cross-validation.
- Method’s claim of being unsupervised is misleading because it depends on semantic class labels within the approach.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled and novel approach, technical novelty, learnability-based K selection, and empirical gains justify acceptance.
- Arguments against acceptance: limitations of class-distribution assumption, clarity/explanation weaknesses, limited experimental justification, and only modest improvements over baselines.
- Overall assessment: valuable and novel contribution to domain adaptation; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing clarity and experimental justification.
