This paper introduces a convex framework for partitioning a dataset into *K* subsets, termed “latent domains,” with the goal of achieving both intra-domain semantic consistency and inter-domain distinctiveness. Semantic consistency is enforced by ensuring each latent domain exhibits a similar distribution of class labels, while distinctiveness is quantified by maximizing the average pairwise differences between these domains within a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) defined by a chosen kernel function. The optimal number of latent domains, *K*, is determined by maximizing the average learnability – essentially, the classification performance – within each discovered domain.

The core idea of controlling class label distributions to facilitate latent domain discovery is novel. While latent domain discovery itself is not new, the proposed approach’s combination of a relaxed convex framework with a learnability-based criterion for *K* selection is a valuable contribution. This learnability metric is particularly noteworthy, as it ensures that identified latent domains contain sufficient samples per class to support effective generalization and discriminative learning.

However, the assumption of similar class distributions within each latent domain may be limiting in certain applications. Consider, for example, an animal classification task with “pose” as the latent variable. While both horses and cows might frequently appear in a standing pose, cows are more often depicted sitting, and horses rarely are. Enforcing strict similarity of class distributions could hinder the discovery of such pose-specific latent domains.

The presentation suffers from a lack of clarity in defining and differentiating key concepts. The terms “dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain” are used imprecisely and could easily be confused. Clear definitions, ideally accompanied by illustrative examples, are crucial. Section 4.2, in particular, requires substantial clarification. The interchangeable use of “dataset” and “domain” (where *S<sub>i</sub>* is referred to as both) is problematic. The experimental setup and terminology must be rigorously defined. Additionally, the notation *max<sub>k</sub> r(U<sub>k</sub>,B)* appears in equation 7 without prior definition; this needs to be addressed.

The experimental validation is adequate, and the reported results demonstrate reasonable improvements over baseline methods. However, the rationale for selecting the specific source and target datasets used in the evaluation is unclear. A more comprehensive evaluation, such as a leave-one-dataset-out cross-validation approach, would strengthen the conclusions. The qualitative results are helpful for visualizing the algorithm's behavior.

Finally, stating the problem as an unsupervised learning task on line 74 is potentially misleading, given the method’s reliance on semantic class labels. 

In summary, this paper presents a technically sound contribution in the form of a convex framework for latent domain discovery, driven by class distribution control and learnability maximization. While the proposed approach shows promise, addressing the aforementioned issues – clarifying definitions, expanding the experimental validation, and refining the problem statement – would significantly enhance the paper’s impact.
