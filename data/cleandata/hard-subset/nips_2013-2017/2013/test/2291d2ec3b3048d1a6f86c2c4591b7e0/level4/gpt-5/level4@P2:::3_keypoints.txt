- Paper proposes nonparametric latent domain discovery using maximum distinctiveness (MMD-like RKHS mean differences) and maximum learnability (DWCV), with an LPC to avoid label-driven clusters and a continuous relaxation for optimization.  
- Technical novelty: combines MMD-style distinctiveness, label-prior regularization, and DWCV-based K selection, avoiding parametric mixture assumptions.  
- Empirical claim: consistent improvements over treating datasets as domains and over Hoffman et al. [20], with qualitative insights into discovered factors (pose, background, illumination).  
- Application scope: method supports both partitioning training data and conditional reshaping of heterogeneous test sets for deployment.  
- Major concern — LPC assumption: enforcing near-identical class priors across latent domains can be restrictive and unrealistic; an ablation without LPC and with varying δ is needed to assess sensitivity and failure modes.  
- Misleading “unsupervised” wording: method uses source labels in LPC and DWCV — clarify that setting is supervised on the source side (target unsupervised), not fully unsupervised.  
- Optimization clarity needed: the relaxed quadratic program can be NP-hard in general; provide solver details, initialization, convergence behavior, runtimes, scalability with M and K, and sensitivity to kernel bandwidth.  
- Notation/terminology issues: conflate dataset, observed domain, and latent domain — define these terms consistently and define r(Uk, B) where first used; clarify K* selection and bounds in DWCV.  
- Evaluation protocol critique: justify source/target splits; a leave-one-dataset-out protocol would be more standard and convincing.  
- Oracle metric concern: GOURS requires annotated targets and is an upper bound — include target-agnostic, practically attainable metrics (e.g., multi-source ensemble, automatic matching) as primary results.  
- Baselines and ablations missing: include K-means/random partition baselines (numbers in appendix), stronger baselines, LPC ablation, δ and bandwidth sensitivity analyses, and optimization ablations.  
- Statistical rigor: report results across multiple random seeds and include significance tests.  
- Conditional reshaping clarification: explicitly state that no target labels are used, discuss overfitting risks to test distribution, and detail complexity/practicality for large-scale test streams.  
- Reporting details requested: report chosen K per experiment, provide DWCV curves, and include runtime and memory costs.  
- Overall assessment: promising, novel, and potentially impactful approach if clarifications, stronger evaluations, and analyses (LPC, optimization, notation) are provided; reviewer leans toward acceptance contingent on these fixes.
