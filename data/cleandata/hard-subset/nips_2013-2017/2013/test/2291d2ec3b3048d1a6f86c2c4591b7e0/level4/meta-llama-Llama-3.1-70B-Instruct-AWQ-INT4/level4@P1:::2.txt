This paper presents a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision datasets, which typically exhibit multiple aspects such as viewpoint, illumination, level of clutter, compression, etc. Instead of simply equating domains with datasets, which can lead to mixing these aspects together, the proposed technique automatically partitions the set of all images across all datasets into domains. The partitioning process is driven by two principles: making separate domains in feature space that are maximally distinctive, and making them such that a good discriminative classifier can be trained on each of them to classify the original classes, not to separate the domains. This technique is more likely to partition according to the underlying aspects rather than datasets.

Originality and significance:
There is limited work on automatically defining domains, and this paper proposes a valuable idea towards automatically discovering useful domains that can support training better classifiers for the original problem. The approach is original and significant, as it tackles the challenging problem of domain adaptation in a novel way.

On the negative side, I am not fully convinced of the proposed optimization method (section 2), as it's not clear how closely it solves the original problem (2)+(3). Moreover, the two proposed driving criteria, maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability, are not well integrated yet: the maximal learnability criterion is only used as a 'wrapper around' the maximal separability criterion, in order to determine the number of domains. Essentially, it acts as a post-hoc validation score deciding how good is the domain partitioning learned for a given number of domains, but the partitioning itself is made based on the separability criterion alone. An integrated process would instead produce the partitioning that maximizes some goal function including both criteria directly.

Despite these shortcomings, which might be due to the fact that this type of work is still at quite exploratory stages, I feel that the paper is a step in the right direction for the community and should be accepted.

Quality and clarity:
The paper is well-written, but lacks figures to illustrate the concepts presented. Adding visual aids would enhance the clarity and readability of the paper.

Experiments:
On the positive side, the experiments show a significant advantage in using the domains produced by the proposed method, over just using datasets as domains, and over the very recent domain discovery technique [20] (sec. 4.2).

The idea of including the test set in the 'reshaping' process is interesting, but not clearly presented (sec. 4.3). Also, this corresponds to an imputation setting (i.e., all test data is available at the same time), but this is not stated clearly.

On the negative side, the image descriptor used is very simple and outdated: just one bag-of-words of sparse SURF features for the entire image, and with just 800 codebook entries. This is really weak nowadays. I recommend the authors to use a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words, computed on dense SURF features. Also, it is not clear what similarity measure is used to compare image descriptors, hopefully X^2 or an intersection kernel? The Euclidean distance is not suitable for comparing histograms. As a next step, a Fisher Vector representation could help further and finally place the image representation in this paper at the level of modern systems.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper presents a novel and original approach to domain adaptation.
- The experiments show a significant advantage in using the proposed method.
- The approach has the potential to improve the state-of-the-art in domain adaptation.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The optimization method is not well-justified.
- The image representation used is outdated and weak.
- The paper lacks clarity and visual aids.

Recommendations for improvement:
- Integrate the maximum learnability criterion into the optimization process.
- Use more modern and robust image descriptors.
- Add visual aids to illustrate the concepts presented.
- Clarify the presentation of the test set reshaping process and its implications.
