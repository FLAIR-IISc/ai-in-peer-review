This work introduces a method for automated latent domain discovery in training data, with the aim of improving classification performance on a test set through domain adaptation. The core of the approach is a function designed to quantify the difference between feature vectors using a specified kernel, and the problem is framed as partitioning the data to maximize this difference across domain pairs. This is formally modeled as an integer program, subject to constraints ensuring single assignment of data points to domains and preservation of the original class label distribution within each discovered domain. The integer program is then relaxed into a continuous optimization problem with a quadratic cost and linear constraints, and the optimal number of domains is determined via cross-validation.

The proposed method is evaluated on two datasets: static images ([2]) and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset ([15]). Notably, the results demonstrate performance improvements compared to the domain adaptation technique presented in [19].

**Strengths:** The paper is well-written and, to the best of my knowledge, presents a novel approach to domain adaptation (although I admit I am not a specialist in this area). The observed performance gains over [19] are encouraging.

**Weaknesses:** Currently, my assessment leans towards rejection, but I am open to being persuaded otherwise by a strong rebuttal addressing the following concerns:

(i) The paper’s motivation requires further clarification. The claim on line 77—that simple clustering by appearance leads to per-category domains—needs justification. What evidence supports this assertion? Furthermore, the paper should explicitly explain how the model formulation in Section 2 mitigates this issue and prevents the formation of per-category domains.

(ii) The necessity of the label prior constraint (line 154) is unclear. I would like to see results demonstrating performance without this constraint.  A comparison against baseline partitioning methods, such as k-means clustering or unsupervised object discovery techniques (e.g., Sivic et al. ICCV '05) applied to the appearance vectors, would be highly valuable.  Additionally, reporting performance with a random partitioning of the dataset into equal sets is requested.
