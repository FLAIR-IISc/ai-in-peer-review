The paper aims to automatically uncover latent domains in a training set and then incorporate them into a domain adaptation framework to improve test-set classification performance. It introduces a kernel-based discrepancy function between feature vectors and seeks a partition of the data into domains that maximizes this discrepancy aggregated across points from different domains. The formulation is posed as an integer program with two constraints: each example must belong to exactly one domain, and the class-label distribution within every domain must match the overall label distribution of the entire dataset. The authors relax this to a continuous optimization with a quadratic objective and linear constraints. The number of domains is selected via cross-validation.

The method is evaluated on two datasets: the static images of [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset [15]. A notable result is that it outperforms the domain adaptation method of [19].

Positives: The paper is clearly written and, to the best of my knowledge, the approach is novel (though I am not a domain adaptation specialist). The performance improvements over [19] are welcome.

Negatives: I currently lean slightly toward rejection. I have two primary concerns that I would like addressed in the rebuttal, which could change my assessment:
(i) The paper’s motivation is unclear. On line 77, it claims that “simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains.” What evidence supports this statement? Moreover, how does the formulation in Section 2 prevent this issue—i.e., how is the avoidance of per-category domain reshaping enforced?
(ii) Relatedly, on line 154, why is the second constraint (the “label prior constraint”) necessary? I’m interested in performance without this constraint. Additionally, please include baselines where the partition is obtained via k-means or unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al., ICCV ’05) on the appearance features. Also report performance when the dataset is randomly split into equal-sized partitions.
