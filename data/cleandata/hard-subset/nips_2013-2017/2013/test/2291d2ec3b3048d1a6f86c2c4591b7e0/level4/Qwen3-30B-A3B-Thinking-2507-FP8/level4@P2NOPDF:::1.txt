

This paper proposes an unsupervised method to discover latent domains in training data for improved domain adaptation. The core idea involves partitioning data points into domains such that a kernel-based dissimilarity function between feature vectors is maximized across domains. The problem is formulated as an integer program with two constraints: (1) each point belongs to exactly one domain, and (2) the class label distribution within each domain matches the global training distribution. This is relaxed to a continuous quadratic optimization with linear constraints, and the optimal number of domains is selected via cross-validation. The method is evaluated on static image datasets [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset [15], demonstrating improved performance over the domain adaptation baseline [19].

**Strengths:** The paper is well-written and appears novel within the domain adaptation literature. The performance gains over [19] are a clear contribution.

**Major Concerns (Require Rebuttal):**  
(i) **Motivation and Mechanism:** The paper claims (Line 77) that "clustering images by appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains," but provides no evidence for this claim. Crucially, the paper does not explain *how* the proposed formulation prevents this outcome. The label distribution constraint (Section 2) is intended to address this, but its mechanism for avoiding per-category domains requires clarification. Without evidence supporting the problem statement or a clear explanation of how the model enforces domain separation *beyond* label matching, the motivation remains unclear.

(ii) **Necessity of Label Prior Constraint:** The second constraint (label distribution matching) is central to the formulation, yet its necessity is unproven. The authors should provide ablation studies:  
- Performance without the label prior constraint.  
- Comparison against simpler baselines: k-means clustering on appearance features, unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al. ICCV 2005), and random partitioning into equal-sized sets.  
Without these, it is unclear whether the constraint drives the improvement or merely enforces a baseline behavior.

**Additional Technical Comments:**  
- Line 82: Clarify "maximally different in distribution from each other." What distribution is being compared? (e.g., feature distribution, label distribution?)  
- Line 166: \(\beta_{mk}\) is defined as a simplex variable; confirm if the formulation already enforces this.  
- Line 182: The constraints differ from those in Lines 153–155; reconcile this discrepancy.  
- Line 215: Provide justification or proof for the bound stated.  
- Line 289: Detail the implementation of the geodesic flow kernel [4] (e.g., source code reuse vs. original implementation).  
- Lines 313/340: Explain *why* the proposed method outperforms baselines (e.g., systematic failure modes of [19] or k-means).  
- Eq. (1): Correct notation from \(M'k\) to \(M_k'\).

**Recommendation:**  
The paper addresses a relevant problem with a technically sound approach, but the motivation lacks evidence and the label prior constraint’s necessity is unverified. The proposed baselines (k-means, random partitioning) are critical for assessing the method’s true contribution. Without addressing these concerns in the rebuttal, I lean toward *reject*. If the authors provide compelling evidence for the motivation, ablation results for the constraint, and comparative baselines, I would reconsider toward *accept*. The novelty and clarity of the rebuttal will determine the final score.
