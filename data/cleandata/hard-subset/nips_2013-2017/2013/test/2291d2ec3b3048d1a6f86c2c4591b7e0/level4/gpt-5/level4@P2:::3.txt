Summary
The paper addresses the problem of discovering latent domains within image and video datasets to improve domain adaptation. The authors formalize two principles: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be as distributionally different as possible) and maximum learnability (each domain should support learning a strong classifier). They measure distinctiveness nonparametrically using a kernel two-sample statistic (difference of mean embeddings in an RKHS, akin to MMD), and define a total domain distinctiveness (TDD) objective over all domain pairs. To avoid trivial or label-driven clusters, they add a label prior constraint (LPC) that enforces each latent domain to mirror the global class prior approximately. The resulting integer program is relaxed to a continuous optimization over simplex-constrained variables. The number of domains K is selected via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV), which chooses K to maximize a weighted average of per-domain cross-validated accuracies. Experiments on object recognition (Amazon, DSLR, Webcam, Caltech-256) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) show gains over treating datasets as domains and over a latent-domain baseline based on mixture models [20]. The paper also explores “conditional reshaping” of test data to match discovered training domains.

Relation to prior work
The work is closely related to Hoffman et al. [20], which discovers latent domains via a hierarchical Gaussian mixture model; here, the authors avoid parametric assumptions by using kernel mean embeddings and an MMD-like criterion. It complements multi-source adaptation (e.g., [10, 28, 29]) by discovering the sources rather than assuming them. The adaptation stage leverages geodesic flow kernels [4]. The paper situates itself well within domain adaptation and dataset bias literature (e.g., [2, 16–19]).

Strengths
- Technical novelty: A clear, nonparametric formulation for latent domain discovery based on maximizing pairwise RKHS mean differences, coupled with an LPC to avoid class-driven clustering. The DWCV procedure is a practical way to guard against over-partitioning and to select K.
- Empirical results: Consistent improvements over using original datasets as domains and over [20]. The qualitative analysis helps interpret the discovered factors (pose, background, illumination).
- Scope: The method applies both to partitioning training data and to conditionally reshaping heterogeneous test sets, which is useful in realistic deployment.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Assumption on label priors (LPC): Enforcing near-identical class distributions across domains can be restrictive. In some applications, latent domains correlate with class-specific factors (e.g., pose distributions differing across animal classes: cows may sit more often than horses). Please provide an ablation without LPC and with varying δ to assess sensitivity and failure modes.
- “Unsupervised” claim: The approach uses class labels in LPC and DWCV. Clarify that the setting is supervised on the source side (and unsupervised on the target), rather than fully unsupervised latent domain discovery.
- Optimization details: The relaxed problem maximizes a convex quadratic objective with linear constraints, which is still NP-hard in general. The paper notes using standard solvers, but more detail is needed on the solver, initialization, convergence behavior, and runtime/scalability with M and K. Sensitivity to the kernel bandwidth should also be discussed.
- Terminology and notation: Dataset, (observed) domain, and latent domain are sometimes conflated. In Sec. 4.2, S is called both “datasets” and “source domains”; define these terms clearly once and use consistently. In Eq. (7), r(Uk, B) is used without a prior explicit definition; please define r(·,·) upon first use and specify B. Clarify K* selection and bounds in DWCV in the main text.
- Evaluation protocol: Justify the choice of source/target splits. A leave-one-dataset-out protocol would be a natural and more standard evaluation. The “oracle” metric GOURs requires annotated targets and is an upper bound; include more target-agnostic, practically attainable results (e.g., multi-source ensemble, automatic matching) as primary metrics. If K-means/random partition baselines underperform, include them in the appendix with numbers. Report statistical significance across multiple random seeds.
- Conditional reshaping of test data: Clarify that no target labels are used and discuss risks of overfitting to the specific test distribution. Provide complexity and practicality considerations for large-scale test streams.

Quality
- Soundness: The nonparametric distinctiveness criterion and DWCV are reasonable. Clarify the optimization tractability and provide ablations (LPC, δ, bandwidth).
- Empirics: Improvements are plausible and repeated, but broader protocols, stronger baselines, and significance tests would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, but terminology/notation in Sec. 4.2 should be tightened. Define all quantities when first introduced (e.g., r(Uk, B)), and clearly distinguish datasets from (observed) domains and latent domains.

Originality
- The combination of MMD-style domain distinctiveness, label prior regularization, and DWCV-based K selection appears novel relative to [20] and prior multi-source work.

Significance
- If robust across settings, the approach could impact how visual datasets are used for adaptation and how future datasets are constructed. The conditional test reshaping is particularly interesting for heterogeneous deployment scenarios.

Pros
- Nonparametric, label-aware formulation for latent domain discovery.
- Practical K selection via learnability.
- Demonstrated gains over treating datasets as domains and over [20].
- Qualitative analyses that illuminate discovered factors.

Cons
- Strong LPC assumption may be violated in realistic settings.
- Optimization and scalability details are thin.
- Evaluation could use broader, more standard protocols and more thorough baselines.
- Some notation/terminology and equation definitions need clarification.
- “Unsupervised” characterization is misleading.

Minor comments
- Line 074: Avoid calling the problem “unsupervised” given the use of labels; clarify the supervision available.
- Please include the formal definition of r(Uk, B) where first used and ensure consistent notation in Sec. 4.2.
- Report chosen K values per experiment and provide DWCV curves; include runtime and memory costs.

Overall assessment
A thoughtful and promising approach to latent domain discovery with solid empirical indications of benefit. With clearer terminology, stronger evaluation protocols, and analysis of the LPC assumption and optimization details, this work would make a valuable contribution. I lean toward acceptance contingent on the clarifications and added experiments above.
