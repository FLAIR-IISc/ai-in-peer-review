This paper presents a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision. The core idea is that existing datasets often conflate multiple underlying factors of variation (e.g., viewpoint, illumination, clutter), and thus, treating entire datasets as single domains is suboptimal. Instead, the authors propose a method to automatically partition the combined set of images from all available datasets into a new set of domains. This partitioning is driven by two principles: 1) the resulting domains should be separable in feature space, and 2) a high-performance discriminative classifier for the original task can be trained on each domain. The goal is to discover domains that correspond more closely to the true underlying factors of variation, thereby enabling more effective adaptation.

### Originality and Significance

The problem of automatically discovering domains, rather than taking them as given, has received little attention in the community. This paper makes a valuable contribution by proposing a principled approach to this problem. The idea of discovering domains that are not only separable but also "learnable" is insightful and represents a promising direction for future research in domain adaptation. The paper moves the field beyond the simplistic one-dataset-one-domain assumption and towards a more nuanced understanding of what constitutes a domain.

### Quality and Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and the core concepts are explained clearly. However, the presentation would benefit significantly from the inclusion of figures. Visualizations illustrating the data partitioning, the feature space, or examples of images belonging to the discovered domains would greatly enhance the reader's understanding of the proposed method and its outcomes.

### Strengths

*   **Novelty:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel formulation of the domain adaptation problem, focusing on automatically discovering latent domains from a pool of data.
*   **Methodological Insight:** The two criteria for partitioning—separability and learnability—are well-motivated and provide a solid foundation for the proposed technique.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate a significant performance advantage for the proposed method over two key baselines: using the original datasets as domains and a recent domain discovery technique from [19]. This provides strong evidence for the utility of the approach.

### Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement

Despite its strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed.

1.  **Optimization Procedure:** A primary concern lies with the optimization method detailed in Section 2. It is not immediately clear how the proposed algorithm directly optimizes the objective function defined in equations (2) and (3). The connection between the theoretical objective and the practical algorithm needs to be better explained and justified.

2.  **Integration of Criteria:** The two core principles driving the domain discovery—maximal separability and maximal learnability—are not fully integrated. The current approach uses the separability criterion to generate the partitions, while the learnability criterion is employed only as a post-hoc validation measure to select the optimal number of domains. A more principled and powerful approach would be to formulate a unified objective function that directly incorporates both criteria, allowing the partitioning process itself to be guided by the ultimate goal of learnability.

3.  **Outdated Image Representation:** A significant weakness lies in the choice of image features. The use of a single bag-of-words histogram based on sparse SURF features with a small 800-word codebook is considerably outdated and may limit the performance of the proposed method. The experimental results could be made much more compelling by adopting more robust, modern representations. I strongly recommend the authors consider using a spatial pyramid representation with densely sampled features (e.g., Dense SIFT/SURF). Additionally, the authors should clarify the distance metric used for comparing histograms, as the Euclidean distance is generally unsuitable; metrics like the Chi-squared distance or intersection kernel would be more appropriate. To further strengthen the paper and align it with contemporary systems, exploring Fisher Vector representations would be a valuable next step. The work of Lazebnik et al. (CVPR 2006) and Perronin et al. (ECCV 2010) would be relevant here.

4.  **Clarity on Experimental Setup:** In Section 4.3, the inclusion of the test set in the domain partitioning process is an interesting idea. However, its presentation is unclear. This approach corresponds to a transductive or batch learning setting where all test data is available beforehand. This assumption should be explicitly stated and justified, as it limits the applicability of the method in scenarios where test instances arrive sequentially.

### Conclusion

Overall, this paper introduces interesting and novel ideas on the important problem of domain adaptation. It makes a strong case for moving beyond predefined dataset boundaries to automatically discover more meaningful domains. While the results are promising, the paper could be substantially improved by strengthening the justification of the optimization method, using a more modern image representation, and clarifying the experimental assumptions.

Despite these shortcomings, which are understandable given the exploratory nature of this research direction, I believe the paper's core contribution is a valuable step forward for the community. I recommend acceptance.
