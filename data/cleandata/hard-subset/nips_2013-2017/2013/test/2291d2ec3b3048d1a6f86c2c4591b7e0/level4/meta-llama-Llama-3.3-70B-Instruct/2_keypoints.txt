- Introduces a novel domain-adaptation method that automatically partitions images across datasets into domains based on aspects (viewpoint, illumination, clutter) rather than dataset labels.  
- Domain partitioning is guided by two principles: separable domains in feature space and trainable discriminative classifiers per domain.  
- Contributes to the limited literature on automatic domain definition and proposes a promising concept for discovering useful domains.  
- The proposed optimization (Sec. 2) is unclear and it’s uncertain how well it solves the original problem (Eqs. 2 and 3).  
- The two driving criteria are not well integrated: maximal learnability is used post-hoc to select domain count rather than being incorporated into partitioning.  
- Despite methodological limitations, the work is exploratory, valuable, and warrants acceptance.  
- Manuscript is well-written but would benefit from additional illustrative figures.  
- Experiments show clear gains using the discovered domains over treating datasets as domains and over the recent domain discovery method [19].  
- The idea of including the test set in the “reshaping” process is interesting but poorly explained and effectively assumes access to all test data (an imputation setting).  
- Major drawback: image representation is simplistic/outdated (single bag-of-words of sparse SURF with only 800-codebook entries).  
- Recommendations: use spatial pyramid bag-of-words on dense SURF, clarify similarity measure (prefer X^2 or intersection kernel over Euclidean), and consider Fisher Vectors.  
- Suggests relevant references to improve representation: Zhang (IJCV 2006), Jurie (CVPR 2005), Lazebnik (CVPR 2006), Perronin (ECCV 2010).  
- Overall: innovative and effective results but needs improved image representation and tighter integration of objectives.
