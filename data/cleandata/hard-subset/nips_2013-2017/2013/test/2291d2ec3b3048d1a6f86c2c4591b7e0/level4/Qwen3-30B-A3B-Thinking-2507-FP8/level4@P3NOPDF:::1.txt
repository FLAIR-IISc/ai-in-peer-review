

This paper proposes a method for automatically discovering latent domains in a training set to improve domain adaptation performance. The core idea involves partitioning data points into domains such that a kernel-based dissimilarity measure between feature vectors is maximized across domains. The problem is formulated as an integer program with two constraints: (1) each point belongs to exactly one domain, and (2) the class label distribution within each domain matches the global distribution. The integer program is relaxed to a continuous quadratic optimization, and the number of domains is selected via cross-validation. Evaluation on static image datasets ([2]) and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset ([15]) demonstrates improved performance over the domain adaptation method of [19].

**Positives:**  
The paper is clearly written and appears novel within the domain adaptation literature. The technical approach is well-structured, and the cross-validation procedure for selecting the number of domains is appropriately justified. The performance gains over [19] are meaningful and well-documented.

**Major Concerns:**  
1. **Motivation and Problem Formulation (Lines 77, 154):**  
   The claim that "simply clustering images by appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains" (Line 77) lacks empirical support. The authors must provide evidence (e.g., visualizations, quantitative analysis) demonstrating that standard clustering *does* produce per-category domains on their datasets. Crucially, the paper does not explain *how* the proposed formulation prevents this issue. Specifically, the label prior constraint (Line 154) is intended to address this, but the mechanism for enforcing non-per-category partitioning is unclear. A clear justification is needed: How does the constraint ensure domains are not aligned with category boundaries? Without this, the core motivation remains unsubstantiated.

2. **Necessity of the Label Prior Constraint (Line 154):**  
   The second constraint (label prior matching) is not adequately justified. The authors must evaluate performance *without* this constraint to demonstrate its necessity. Furthermore, a critical baseline comparison is missing: the performance of a standard unsupervised partitioning method (e.g., k-means on appearance features, or unsupervised object discovery like [11]) should be reported. This would isolate the contribution of the label prior constraint. Additionally, the performance of a random partitioning baseline (into equal-sized sets) must be included to establish a lower bound for the proposed method. Without these comparisons, the value of the constraint—and thus the approach—cannot be properly assessed.

**Minor Comments:**  
- Line 82: Clarify "maximally different in distribution." Specify *which* distribution (e.g., "class label distribution" or "feature distribution") and *over what* (e.g., "across domains").  
- Line 166: β_{mk} appears to reside on a simplex by its definition (Eq. 12), yet the paper does not explicitly state this. Clarify the constraint.  
- Line 182: The constraints differ from those in Line 153. Explicitly reconcile this discrepancy.  
- Line 215: Provide justification or proof for the derived bound.  
- Line 289: Detail the implementation of the geodesic flow kernel [4] (e.g., reimplementation vs. public code, key parameters).  
- Lines 313/340: Offer deeper analysis of *why* the proposed method outperforms baselines. Identify specific failure modes of [19] or k-means that the approach overcomes (e.g., via error analysis or case studies).  
- Equation (1): Correct "M'k" to "M_k'".  
- Suggested reference: Torralba & Efros (CVPR 2011) on dataset bias, which is highly relevant to the motivation.

**Recommendation:**  
The paper presents a technically sound method with promising results, but the core motivation and the necessity of the label prior constraint are inadequately supported. Without empirical validation of the per-category clustering claim and rigorous baseline comparisons (k-means, random partitioning), the contribution’s significance is unclear. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper. *If the authors provide compelling evidence in the rebuttal for the motivation and the constraint’s necessity (e.g., via ablation studies and empirical analysis), I would lean toward acceptance.* However, as submitted, the unresolved concerns warrant a **weak reject**. The paper’s novelty and technical execution are promising, but the missing evidence undermines the argument for the proposed framework’s necessity.
