Summary
The paper proposes a method to automatically discover latent domains within a training set and then leverage these domains in a domain adaptation framework to improve test-time classification. The authors define a kernel-based discrepancy function between feature vectors and seek a partition of the data into domains that maximizes inter-domain differences, subject to two constraints: (1) each sample belongs to exactly one domain; (2) the label distribution within each domain matches the overall label prior. The resulting integer program is relaxed to a continuous quadratic optimization with linear constraints, and the number of domains is selected via cross-validation. Experiments on a static image dataset [2] and IXMAS multi-view actions [15] show gains over the domain adaptation method of [19].

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and a principled optimization framework.
- The idea of jointly discovering latent domains and constraining label priors is novel and interesting (to my knowledge).
- Demonstrated improvements over [19] on two datasets, which suggests practical value.
- The presentation is generally clear and well-structured.

Main concerns (pre-rebuttal)
1) Motivation and risk of per-category domains (line 77)
- The paper asserts that naïvely clustering by appearance can reshape datasets into per-category domains. Please provide empirical support (e.g., a quantitative analysis showing high mutual information between clusters and class labels under standard clustering).
- Clarify how the proposed formulation prevents such per-category “collapse.” Intuitively, the label prior constraint should discourage it, but it would help to explicitly explain the mechanism and provide an ablation that shows what happens without the constraint.

2) Necessity and effect of the label prior constraint (line 154)
- Why is the label prior constraint required in practice? What failure modes arise without it?
- Please report performance without this constraint (and with a relaxed/soft version), as well as sensitivity to the tightness of the constraint.
- Baselines: include partitions obtained via k-means on appearance features, unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al., ICCV 2005), and random equal-size partitions. These are important to demonstrate that your approach outperforms simple unsupervised grouping schemes.

Additional comments and questions
- Line 82: “maximally different in distribution.” Please define precisely what distribution is being compared (e.g., distributions over feature embeddings) and which divergence/discrepancy is used (e.g., kernel MMD or another kernel-induced distance). A brief intuition and any properties (e.g., characteristic kernels) would help.
- Line 166: As defined, β_{mk} seems to already lie on the simplex (nonnegative and sums to 1). Please clarify whether any additional normalization or constraints are needed.
- Line 182: The constraints appear to differ from those introduced around line 153. Please reconcile the formulations or explain the change (e.g., from hard to soft constraints or from integer to relaxed variables).
- Line 215: Please provide a justification or proof sketch for the stated bound, including assumptions required (e.g., kernel properties, boundedness of features) and whether it applies to the relaxed or integer formulation.
- Line 289: Geodesic Flow Kernel [4] details. Clarify whether you used public code or a reimplementation. Report key hyperparameters, subspace dimensions, and any preprocessing. If reimplemented, indicate how you validated correctness (e.g., reproducing reported numbers on a standard benchmark).
- Lines 313/340: Provide analysis explaining why your approach outperforms baselines. For example:
  - Per-class accuracies or confusion matrices to identify where gains concentrate.
  - Examples of systematic failures of [19] (or other baselines) that your method mitigates (e.g., cross-view misalignment, illumination-induced drift).
  - How discovered domains align with meaningful nuisance factors (viewpoint, background, acquisition device), without collapsing to categories.
- Computational aspects: Report complexity, runtime, and scalability with respect to number of samples, features, and domains. Discuss initialization sensitivity and whether solutions are stable across random seeds.
- Selection of number of domains: Clarify the cross-validation protocol to avoid leakage from test data. Include sensitivity analysis showing performance versus number of domains.
- Notation: Eq. (1) M'k => Mk' (swap the prime placement as appropriate).
- Related work: Consider adding Torralba and Efros, “Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias,” CVPR 2011, to situate the problem and motivate latent domains as a response to dataset bias.

Experimental suggestions
- Ablation study:
  - Without the label prior constraint.
  - With a soft/penalized version of the constraint.
  - With alternative discrepancy measures or kernels.
- Baselines:
  - k-means on features; random partitions; unsupervised object discovery.
  - Domain discovery methods, if any contemporaneous alternatives exist.
- Robustness:
  - Vary feature types and kernels; report sensitivity.
  - Evaluate stability of discovered domains across runs.
- Qualitative analysis:
  - Visualize discovered domains (e.g., nearest neighbors, exemplar images) and report their label entropy to show they are not category-specific.
  - Show how domain adaptation performance varies when using discovered domains versus naïve partitions.

Post-rebuttal update
The rebuttal clarified the motivation and convincingly explained the role and necessity of the label prior constraint, including evidence that it prevents per-category collapse and improves generalization. With these clarifications, I lean slightly toward acceptance. I still encourage the authors to include the requested ablations, baseline comparisons (k-means, random, unsupervised discovery), and additional analysis of failure modes to strengthen the empirical case and improve the paper’s clarity and impact.

Overall assessment
- Novelty: Good
- Clarity: Good, with room for added precision on discrepancy measures and constraints
- Significance: Promising, supported by empirical gains over [19]
- Recommendation: Lean slightly toward accept, contingent on added ablations and clarifications above.
