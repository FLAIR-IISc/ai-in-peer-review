This paper proposes a convex framework for partitioning a dataset into K subsets, referred to as "latent domains." The objective is to form subsets that each have similar semantic class distributions while being maximally distinct from one another. Distinctiveness is measured by maximizing the pairwise mean differences between the subsets in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) induced by a chosen kernel. The number of latent domains, K, is determined by selecting the value that maximizes the "average learnability," which is the average performance of a classifier trained within each discovered domain.

The core technical contribution is the (relaxed) convex formulation for discovering latent domains by encouraging similar class distributions within each domain. While the general idea of discovering latent domains is not new, this specific approach of controlling class label distributions within an optimization framework is novel. The constraint of forcing similar class distributions cleverly prevents the discovery of trivial domains dominated by a single class. However, this may also be a limiting assumption for certain tasks. For instance, consider an animal classification task (e.g., horses vs. cows) where "pose" is the latent variable. The distribution of poses might not be consistent across classes. While both horses and cows are frequently observed in a standing pose, cows might be pictured in a sitting pose more often than horses. In such a case, the model's assumption of similar class distributions within each latent domain (pose) would be violated.

A further strength of the paper is the method for selecting the number of domains, K. By optimizing for classification quality within the resulting domains, the method ensures that the discovered partitions are not only distinct but also useful for downstream learning tasks, containing enough samples per class to train a generalizable classifier.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity in its terminology. The concepts of "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain" are not always clearly delineated and can be confused. These terms should be precisely defined, ideally with supporting examples. This issue is particularly apparent in the experiments section (4.2), where S_i is referred to as both a "dataset" and a "source domain," making the experimental setting difficult to follow. Additionally, some notation is used without definition, such as `max_k r(U_k, B)` in Equation 7.

The experimental validation appears adequate, with results showing a reasonable improvement over the baselines. However, the paper does not justify the choice of specific source and target datasets for reporting results. A more standard evaluation protocol, such as leave-one-dataset-out adaptation, could offer a more rigorous assessment of the method's capabilities. On a positive note, the qualitative results are insightful and helpful for visualizing what the algorithm achieves.

Finally, a minor point on terminology: on line 074, the problem is stated as being "unsupervised," which may be misleading since the method explicitly uses semantic class labels in its formulation. In summary, while the paper presents a notable technical contribution, its clarity and experimental validation could be significantly improved.
