This paper proposes a method to automatically discover latent domains within a training set for use in a subsequent domain adaptation framework. The goal is to improve classification performance on a test set where the data distribution differs from that of the training set. The core idea is to partition the training data into a set of domains that are maximally dissimilar from one another. This dissimilarity is measured by a kernel-based function that captures the difference between the feature distributions of any two domains. The partitioning is formulated as an integer programming problem, subject to two key constraints: (1) each data point must be assigned to exactly one domain, and (2) the class label distribution within each discovered domain must match the overall class distribution of the training set. This integer program is then relaxed into a continuous quadratic optimization problem with linear constraints. The optimal number of domains is determined via cross-validation.

The approach is evaluated on two datasets: the static image dataset from [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset [15]. Notably, the method demonstrates improved performance over the domain adaptation approach of [19].

**Positives**

The paper is well-written, and the proposed approach appears to be novel, although I am not an expert in the domain adaptation literature. The performance gains over the baseline method of [19] represent a significant contribution.

**Negatives**

Despite these strengths, I currently lean towards recommending rejection. I have two primary concerns that I hope the authors will address in their rebuttal, as this could lead me to reconsider my score:

(i) **Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The motivation for the proposed formulation is not entirely clear. The paper argues (line 77) that "simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains." First, what is the evidence for this claim? Is this a known issue, or is it an observation from the authors' own experiments? Second, it is not obvious how the proposed model (Section 2) specifically prevents this from happening. How does the formulation ensure that the discovered domains are not simply proxies for the class labels?

(ii) **The Label Prior Constraint:** Related to the first point, the necessity of the "label prior constraint" (line 154) is not well-justified. The paper would be much stronger if it included an ablation study showing performance without this constraint. Furthermore, to properly evaluate the domain discovery component, the authors should include baselines that partition the data using standard clustering methods (e.g., k-means on appearance features) or unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al., ICCV 2005). A simple random partitioning of the data into equally sized sets would also serve as a useful sanity check.

**Additional Comments**

*   **Line 82:** The phrase "maximally different in distribution from each other" is used repeatedly. Please clarify what distribution this refers to (e.g., the distribution over features, classes, etc.).
*   **Line 166:** As formulated, doesn't the variable β_{mk} already satisfy the simplex constraint (i.e., it is non-negative and sums to one)? Please clarify why an additional constraint is needed.
*   **Line 182:** The constraints presented here appear to be different from those formulated earlier (starting on line 153). Please clarify this discrepancy.
*   **Line 215:** Please provide a justification or proof for the stated bound.
*   **Line 289:** Please provide more details on the implementation of the geodesic flow kernel [4]. Was a publicly available implementation used, or was it re-implemented by the authors?
*   **Lines 313/340:** The experimental analysis would be more insightful if it explained *why* the proposed approach outperforms the baselines. For example, showing qualitative results or specific failure cases of the baseline methods that are overcome by the proposed approach would greatly strengthen the paper's conclusions.
*   **Eq (1):** Typo: M'k should be Mk'.
*   The authors may consider citing the following relevant work on dataset bias: "Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias" by A. Torralba and A. Efros, CVPR 2011.
