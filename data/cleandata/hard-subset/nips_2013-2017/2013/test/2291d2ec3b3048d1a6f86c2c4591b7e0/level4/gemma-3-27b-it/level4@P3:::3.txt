This paper proposes a novel framework for automatically discovering latent domains within image and video datasets, a crucial step for effective domain adaptation. The core idea is to partition the data into K subsets—termed latent domains—that maximize both distinctiveness (measured by the difference in distributions in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space) and learnability (assessed through cross-validation performance of classifiers trained on each domain). A key component is maintaining similar semantic class distributions within each domain, enforced through a label prior constraint during optimization. The number of domains, K, is determined by selecting the value that maximizes average classification accuracy across the discovered domains.

This work builds upon the growing field of domain adaptation in visual recognition [1, 2, 3, 4], which addresses performance degradation due to distribution mismatches between training and testing data. While prior work often equates datasets with domains [16, 17, 18, 19], this paper argues that datasets typically represent mixtures of underlying domains influenced by factors like lighting, pose, and background. Recent efforts have begun to address latent domain discovery [20], but this paper introduces a new approach with a focus on balancing domain distinctiveness and class distribution consistency. The formulation of this problem as a convex optimization is a notable contribution.

The strengths of this paper lie in its principled approach to latent domain discovery and its technical novelty. The proposed method offers a compelling way to reshape datasets into more meaningful domains, potentially improving the performance of domain adaptation algorithms. The inclusion of a learnability criterion for determining the optimal number of domains is particularly valuable, ensuring that discovered domains are sufficiently informative for training robust classifiers. The experimental results demonstrate improvements over baseline methods on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks. The qualitative analysis provides helpful intuition into the types of factors captured by the discovered domains.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The assumption of maintaining similar class distributions within each domain, while intended to improve robustness, may be limiting for certain tasks. For example, if pose is the primary factor defining latent domains in an animal classification task, the class distributions within each pose-defined domain may naturally differ (e.g., horses are rarely seen sitting, while cows are). This limitation should be acknowledged more explicitly.

Clarity is an area for improvement. The terminology regarding “dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain” is sometimes ambiguous and can be confusing, especially in Section 4.2. Clear definitions and consistent usage are crucial. The undefined term "maxk r(U_k,B)" in the explanation of Equation 7 needs clarification. Providing concrete examples illustrating the distinctions between these terms would greatly enhance understanding. 

The rationale for the specific choice of source and target datasets in the experiments is not fully explained. A more comprehensive evaluation strategy, such as leave-one-dataset-out cross-validation, might provide a more robust assessment of the method’s generalizability.  Furthermore, while the statement that the problem is unsupervised (line 74) is technically correct, it's important to acknowledge the reliance on semantic class labels within the method itself.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to latent domain discovery with a principled formulation.
*   Technical contribution of balancing domain distinctiveness and class distribution consistency.
*   Integration of learnability criterion for determining the number of domains.
*   Empirically validated improvements over baselines.
*   Provides a potentially valuable tool for improving domain adaptation performance.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Potential limitations of the assumption regarding similar class distributions within domains.
*   Lack of clarity in terminology and explanations.
*   Limited justification for the experimental setup and dataset selection.
*   While technically sound, the improvement over baselines, while present, isn’t massive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The proposed method offers a novel and principled approach to latent domain discovery. While some aspects of the paper could benefit from further clarification and a more comprehensive evaluation, the technical novelty and promising results justify acceptance. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the clarity concerns and providing a more detailed justification for the experimental choices.
