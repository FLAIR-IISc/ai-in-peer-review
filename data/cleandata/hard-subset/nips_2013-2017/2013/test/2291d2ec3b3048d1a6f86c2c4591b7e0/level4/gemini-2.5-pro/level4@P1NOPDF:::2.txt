This paper introduces a new technique for domain adaptation in computer vision. The core idea is to move beyond equating domains with source datasets, which often conflates multiple underlying factors of variation (e.g., viewpoint, illumination, clutter). Instead, the proposed method automatically partitions the entire image collection into new domains. This partitioning is guided by two principles: 1) maximizing the separability of the resulting domains in feature space, and 2) ensuring that a high-performing discriminative classifier for the original task can be trained within each discovered domain. The goal is to produce a partitioning that better reflects the true underlying data-generating factors rather than the arbitrary boundaries of the original datasets.

**Originality and Significance:**
The problem of automatically defining domains has received limited attention, and this paper presents a promising approach for discovering domains that are useful for improving classifier performance.

However, I have some reservations. The connection between the proposed optimization method (Section 2) and the original objective function (equations 2 and 3) is not clearly established, making it difficult to assess how well the algorithm solves the stated problem. Furthermore, the two guiding principles—maximal separability and maximal learnability—are not well integrated. The learnability criterion is used only as a wrapper to select the number of domains, acting as a post-hoc validation score for a partitioning generated solely based on the separability criterion. A more unified approach would directly optimize an objective function that incorporates both criteria to produce the final partitioning.

Despite these limitations, which are perhaps understandable given the exploratory nature of this work, the paper addresses an important problem and represents a valuable step forward for the community. I recommend acceptance.

**Quality and Clarity:**
The paper is well-written, although it would benefit significantly from figures to visually illustrate the core concepts and the proposed partitioning process.

**Experiments:**
On the positive side, the experiments demonstrate a clear advantage for the proposed method over two key baselines: treating source datasets as domains and the recent domain discovery technique of [19].

The concept of including the test set in the domain "reshaping" process (Section 4.3) is interesting, but its presentation is unclear. This approach corresponds to a transductive learning setting, where all test data is available during training, and this should be stated explicitly.

On the negative side, a significant weakness of the experimental evaluation lies in the choice of image features. The use of a single bag-of-words descriptor based on sparse SURF features with an 800-word codebook is outdated and substantially weaker than current state-of-the-art methods. The authors are strongly encouraged to use a more powerful representation, such as a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words on dense SIFT/SURF features. Additionally, the paper does not specify the similarity measure used for comparing image descriptors; a metric suitable for histograms, such as the chi-squared distance or an intersection kernel, should be used, as the Euclidean distance is inappropriate. Adopting a Fisher Vector representation would be a valuable next step to bring the image features in line with modern systems. The authors may find the following papers helpful for improving their feature representation: Zhang et al., IJCV 2007; Jurie & Triggs, CVPR 2005; Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006; Perronnin et al., ECCV 2010.

**Overall Recommendation:**
Overall, the paper presents novel and interesting ideas on an important problem and achieves promising results. The work can be improved in several ways, most notably by strengthening the image representation used in the experiments.
