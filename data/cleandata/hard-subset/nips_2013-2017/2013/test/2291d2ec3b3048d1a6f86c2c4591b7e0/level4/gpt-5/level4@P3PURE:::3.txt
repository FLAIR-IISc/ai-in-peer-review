Summary
The paper presents a convex framework for partitioning a dataset (or multiple datasets) into K subsets, termed latent domains. Within each latent domain, the semantic class distributions are encouraged to be similar, while distinctiveness across domains is enforced by maximizing pairwise mean differences in an RKHS induced by a chosen kernel (“maximum distinctiveness”). The number of latent domains K is selected by maximizing average learnability, i.e., how well a classifier can be trained within each latent domain.

Strengths
- Although latent domain discovery itself is not new, the idea of explicitly controlling class-label distributions to aid latent domain identification within a (relaxed) convex framework is novel and technically interesting.
- Choosing K by evaluating within-domain classification quality is a sensible practice that helps ensure each latent domain contains enough samples per class to learn discriminative models that generalize.

Concerns and limitations
- The assumption that class distributions should be similar within each latent domain, while preventing clusters from being dominated by a single class, may be restrictive for some tasks. For example, if poses constitute the latent domains for an animal classification task (e.g., horses and cows), the pose distribution can differ by class: both horses and cows may appear in a left-right standing pose, but cows are frequently pictured sitting while horses rarely are. In such cases, forcing similar class distributions across latent domains could hinder discovery of meaningful domains.
- Conceptual clarity needs improvement. The terms dataset, domain, and latent domain are used in ways that can be confusing. Clear, explicit definitions—ideally with examples—are needed. This issue is particularly evident in Section 4.2, where Si is referred to both as “datasets” and as “source domains,” suggesting interchangeable use of “dataset” and “domain.” The experimental setting and terminology should be clarified.
- A notational issue: max_k r(U_k, B) is used in Equation (7) but not defined.

Experimental evaluation
- The empirical results appear adequate and show reasonable improvements over baselines. The qualitative examples are helpful for understanding the algorithm’s behavior.
- However, the motivation for the chosen source/target dataset pairs is unclear. A leave-one-dataset-out adaptation protocol might provide a more systematic and informative evaluation.

Minor comment
- On line 074, describing latent domain learning as an unsupervised problem may be misleading given that semantic class labels are used.

Overall assessment
The formulation for discovering latent domains by encouraging similar class distributions within each domain in a relaxed convex framework is a notable contribution. At the same time, the paper would benefit from clearer definitions of key concepts (dataset, domain, latent domain), resolution of notational issues (e.g., defining max_k r(U_k, B) in Eq. 7), and a more clearly justified and possibly broader evaluation protocol.
