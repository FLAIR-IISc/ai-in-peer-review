This paper presents a new technique for domain adaptation in computer vision datasets, which typically vary along multiple factors such as viewpoint, illumination, clutter, and compression. Rather than equating domains with datasets—which conflates these factors—the method automatically partitions all images across datasets into domains. The partitioning is driven by two principles: (1) creating domains that are well separated in feature space, and (2) ensuring that a strong discriminative classifier can be trained within each domain to predict the original classes (not to separate domains). This approach is more likely to recover underlying factors of variation rather than merely reflect dataset boundaries.

Originality and significance:
There is relatively little prior work on automatically defining domains, and this paper offers a promising idea for discovering useful domains that support better classifiers for the original task. On the negative side, I am not fully convinced by the proposed optimization in Section 2; it is unclear how closely it solves the original objectives (2) and (3). Moreover, the two driving criteria are not yet well integrated: the maximal learnability criterion is used only as a wrapper around the maximal separability criterion to choose the number of domains. In effect, it serves as a post hoc validation score assessing the quality of the partitioning for a given number of domains, while the partitioning itself is determined solely by separability. A more integrated approach would jointly optimize a single objective that directly incorporates both criteria to produce the partitioning.

Despite these shortcomings—which may reflect the exploratory stage of this line of work—I believe the paper is a step in the right direction for the community and should be accepted.

Quality and clarity:
The paper is clearly written but would benefit from figures to illustrate the main concepts.

Experiments:
On the positive side, the experiments show a significant advantage for the discovered domains over treating datasets as domains, and over the recent domain discovery technique [19] (Sec. 4.2). The idea of including the test set in the “reshaping” process is interesting but not clearly explained (Sec. 4.3). This also corresponds to an imputation setting (i.e., all test data are available at the same time), which should be stated explicitly.

On the negative side, the image descriptor is very simple and outdated: a single bag-of-words of sparse SURF features for the entire image with only 800 codebook entries. This is weak by current standards. I recommend using a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words computed on dense SURF features. It is also unclear what similarity measure is used to compare image descriptors; ideally this should be a χ2 or intersection kernel, as Euclidean distance is not suitable for histogram comparison. As a next step, a Fisher Vector representation could further improve performance and bring the image representation in line with modern systems. The following references may help: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronnin ECCV 2010.

Overall, the paper introduces interesting and novel ideas on an important problem and achieves good results. It can be improved in several ways, especially by adopting a stronger, more modern image representation.
