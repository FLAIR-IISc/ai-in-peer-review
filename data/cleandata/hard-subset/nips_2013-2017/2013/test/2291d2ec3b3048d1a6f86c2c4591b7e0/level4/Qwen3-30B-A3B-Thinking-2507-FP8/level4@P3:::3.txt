

This paper introduces a novel framework for discovering latent domains within visual datasets to improve domain adaptation. The authors propose two key axiomatic properties for these domains: maximum distinctiveness (ensuring domains are as distributionally distinct as possible) and maximum learnability (ensuring each domain contains sufficient data to train strong discriminative models). The approach formulates domain discovery as a nonparametric optimization problem that maximizes pairwise domain differences in RKHS while maintaining balanced class distributions within each domain. The authors determine the optimal number of domains through domain-wise cross-validation, ensuring each domain has enough data for reliable classification.

The work builds upon domain adaptation research at NIPS and elsewhere, addressing a critical gap in the field: the common practice of equating entire datasets with single domains, which fails to capture complex distributional variations. While Hoffman et al. (ECCV 2012) also addressed latent domain discovery, their approach relied on parametric Gaussian mixture models, which the authors rightly argue may not generalize well to real visual data. The authors' nonparametric approach overcomes this limitation and provides a more flexible framework for domain discovery.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical and practical contributions. The technical approach is sound, with a clear optimization procedure that effectively balances domain distinctiveness and learnability. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, showing consistent improvements across multiple object recognition and action recognition tasks. The results demonstrate that reshaping datasets into automatically discovered latent domains significantly outperforms using original datasets as domains and even surpasses the method proposed by Hoffman et al. (ECCV 2012). The qualitative analysis of identified domains (Figure 1) provides valuable insight into the factors driving domain separation (e.g., object pose, background, color).

The paper excels in quality and significance. The methodology is technically sound, with proper theoretical justification for the domain distinctiveness measure. The experiments are well-designed, with appropriate baselines and thorough evaluation across multiple datasets. The significance is clear: by enabling more accurate domain adaptation through better domain identification, this work directly addresses a fundamental challenge in visual recognition systems. The authors demonstrate that their approach leads to measurable performance improvements, making it valuable for practitioners.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though some concepts could be clarified. The distinction between "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain" occasionally becomes blurred, particularly in Section 4.2 where the authors refer to "source domains" while using the term "datasets" interchangeably. This confusion could be resolved with a more precise definition of these terms early in the paper, perhaps with a concrete example. Additionally, the authors should clarify the meaning of "max_k r(U_k,B)" in Equation 6, as this is used without definition.

The paper's main limitation is its reliance on semantic class labels for domain discovery. While the authors acknowledge the need to maintain balanced class distributions within domains (via the label prior constraint), this assumption may not hold for all applications. For instance, in the animal classification example mentioned in the draft review, latent domains (e.g., poses) might not align with class distributions, as different classes (horses vs. cows) may have different pose distributions. The authors could explore this limitation more thoroughly and consider whether their framework could be adapted to cases where class distributions vary across domains.

The paper's originality is notable, particularly in the convex optimization framework for domain discovery and the use of domain-wise cross-validation to determine the optimal number of domains. While domain adaptation is well-studied, this specific approach to latent domain discovery represents a meaningful contribution that advances the field beyond the common practice of treating datasets as single domains.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are thorough, and the results demonstrate clear practical benefits. The minor issues with terminology and the class distribution assumption are manageable with minor revisions. The paper effectively addresses a critical gap in visual domain adaptation research and provides a valuable methodological contribution that should be of interest to the NIPS community.

The authors have made a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation by addressing the critical problem of latent domain discovery. Their framework for automatically reshaping datasets into meaningful latent domains represents a substantial advance over current practices, with clear empirical validation across multiple tasks. The paper should be accepted for presentation at NIPS.
