Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles the problem of discovering latent domains within visual datasets to improve domain adaptation. The authors define domains as distributions over features and labels and propose two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different in distribution) and maximum learnability (each domain should support learning a strong classifier). To enforce distinctiveness, they maximize pairwise discrepancies between domain distributions using a kernel two-sample metric (MMD) with a characteristic kernel. The problem is first posed as an integer program with constraints that each example belongs to exactly one domain and that each domain’s label distribution matches the overall label prior (label prior constraint, LPC). The authors then relax to a continuous quadratic optimization with linear constraints and select the number of domains via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV), which embodies the learnability criterion. They also extend reshaping to the test set via a conditional matching procedure.

Empirical evaluations span object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS). The method improves upon using original datasets as domains and outperforms the latent-domain method of Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12) as well as simple baselines (UNION, ENSEMBLE). The approach relates to prior work on domain adaptation (Saenko et al., Gopalan et al., Gong et al.), dataset bias (Torralba & Efros), generalized clustering via kernel embeddings (Jegelka et al.), and multi-source adaptation.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation: using MMD to quantify domain distinctiveness and DWCV to balance distinctiveness vs. learnability is elegant and well-motivated.
- Nonparametric distributions: avoids potentially misspecified mixture models and is broadly applicable.
- Practical utility: reshaping both training and test datasets shows tangible gains, particularly in cross-view action recognition where heterogeneity is pronounced.
- Thorough experimental setup: comparisons against original dataset-based domains, [20], and multi-source adaptation strategies; analysis of qualitative factors that define discovered domains.
- Relevance to NIPS: contributes to core topics in domain adaptation, representation learning under dataset shift, and nonparametric testing.

Weaknesses and questions
- Clarity on “maximally different”: While Eq. (1) indicates an MMD-style measure, please make explicit early on that “maximally different” means maximizing pairwise squared MMD between empirical distributions P̂k(x), and state the role of characteristic kernels and implications for consistency.
- Risk of per-category clustering: The paper argues that naive appearance clustering can produce per-category clusters. The LPC helps mitigate this, but a small ablation would be informative:
  - Without LPC (or with a looser δ): does the method drift toward per-category domains or degrade adaptation?
  - Baselines: K-means over features, random partitions of equal size, and an unsupervised object discovery baseline (e.g., Sivic & Zisserman, ICCV’05) would contextualize the gains. The text mentions K-means/random were uncompetitive; please include results for completeness.
- Optimization details and constraints:
  - The relaxed objective is a maximization of a convex quadratic under linear constraints, which is in general hard. Please specify the solver, initialization, runtime, and convergence behaviors; report sensitivity to kernel bandwidth and δ.
  - Clarify the transition from the integer constraints to the relaxed constraints. The relaxed constraints in Eq. (5) seem notationally and substantively different from those in the integer formulation (e.g., lower/upper bounds 1/M and 1/C). Please reconcile and justify these bounds.
  - βmk = zmk/Mk naturally lies on a simplex when zmk are 0/1; after relaxation, βk ∈ Δ is imposed—please clarify this step and whether additional regularization is needed to avoid degenerate allocations.
- Choosing K: The DWCV criterion is reasonable, but the bound Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C} deserves justification (derivation or intuition). A brief sensitivity plot of A(K) and target accuracy vs. K is helpful; consider reporting variance across CV folds.
- Practical details:
  - Geodesic flow kernel (GFK) usage: state whether you used public code or a reimplementation, hyperparameter settings, and how you integrated domain reshaping with GFK.
  - Complexity: report per-dataset runtimes for training/test reshaping and adaptation, and how complexity scales with M and K.
- Analysis: Provide insights into systematic failure cases of the baselines that your method overcomes (e.g., when dataset-level domains blend incompatible acquisition factors), and cases where your method may struggle (e.g., when label priors genuinely differ across latent domains).

Quality
- The method is technically sound and grounded in established kernel two-sample testing. The DWCV heuristic is sensible and empirically validated. More algorithmic details (solver, complexity, sensitivity) and ablations would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. Clarify the precise meaning of “maximally different,” reconcile constraint formulations between integer and relaxed problems, and expand on optimization details. Fix minor notational typos (see below).

Originality
- Discovering latent domains nonparametrically via MMD with a learnability-driven model selection appears novel relative to prior mixture-based approaches (Hoffman et al.). Conditional reshaping of test sets is also a useful addition.

Significance
- The gains over using dataset-as-domain and over [20] suggest meaningful impact for practitioners. The approach addresses a real pain point in domain adaptation—unknown, mixed domains—and could influence dataset construction and evaluation protocols.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, principled approach with strong empirical improvements.
- Addresses an important and underexplored aspect of domain adaptation (domain discovery).
- Applicable across image and video recognition; test-time reshaping is particularly compelling.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing ablations (LPC removal, K-means/random, alternative clustering/object discovery baselines).
- Limited optimization details and sensitivity analyses.
- Some notational/constraint inconsistencies reduce clarity.

Minor comments and typos
- Eq. (1): M′k should be Mk′.
- Please ensure the “Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias” citation (Torralba & Efros, CVPR’11) is properly included and discussed (it appears in the references).
- Provide a brief statement on kernel choice and bandwidth selection strategy (currently “twice the median distance”); report robustness to this choice.

Post-rebuttal update
The rebuttal satisfactorily clarified the motivation for avoiding per-category clustering and the role/necessity of the label prior constraint. With these addressed, I lean slightly toward accept, contingent on adding the requested ablations and clarifications (solver/complexity, constraint reconciliation, and GFK details) in the final version.
