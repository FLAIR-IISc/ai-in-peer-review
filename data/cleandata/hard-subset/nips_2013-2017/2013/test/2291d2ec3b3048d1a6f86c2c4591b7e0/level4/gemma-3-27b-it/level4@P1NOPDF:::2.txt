This paper presents a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision, addressing the challenges posed by datasets with multiple varying factors such as viewpoint, illumination, clutter, and compression. Rather than treating datasets as domains, which conflates these distinct factors, the proposed method automatically partitions the combined image set into meaningful domains. This partitioning is guided by two principles: maximizing separation between domains in feature space, and ensuring that a strong discriminative classifier can be trained within each domain (for the original classes, not domain separation). This approach aims to partition the data according to the underlying factors of variation, rather than simply by dataset boundaries.

**Originality and Significance:**

The work on automatically defining domains is limited, and this paper presents a valuable contribution towards discovering useful domains that can improve classifier performance.

However, I have some reservations regarding the proposed optimization method (Section 2). It’s unclear how closely the solution approximates the original objective function (2)+(3). Furthermore, the two driving criteria – maximal learnability and maximal separability – are not fully integrated. The maximal learnability criterion currently acts as a ‘wrapper’ around the maximal separability criterion, primarily determining the optimal number of domains via a post-hoc validation score. Ideally, the partitioning process would directly maximize a combined objective function incorporating both criteria simultaneously. 

Despite these limitations, which may be inherent in this exploratory research area, I believe the paper represents a step forward for the community and warrants acceptance.

**Quality and Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written. However, the inclusion of figures to illustrate the concepts would significantly enhance understanding.

**Experiments:**

The experimental results demonstrate a clear advantage of using the proposed domains over simply using datasets as domains, and over the recent domain discovery technique [19] (Section 4.2). 

The idea of incorporating the test set into the ‘reshaping’ process (Section 4.3) is interesting, though not fully explained. It also implicitly assumes an imputation setting – that all test data is available concurrently – which is not explicitly stated. 

On the negative side, the image descriptor used is quite simple and outdated: a single bag-of-words of sparse SURF features for the entire image, with only 800 codebook entries. This is relatively weak by current standards. I recommend the authors consider using a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words computed on dense SURF features. Additionally, the similarity measure used to compare image descriptors is unclear; I hope it is χ² or an intersection kernel, as Euclidean distance is not suitable for histogram comparison. A Fisher Vector representation could further improve performance and bring the image representation in line with modern systems. Relevant papers to consider include: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010. 

Overall, this paper presents interesting and novel ideas addressing an important problem, and achieves promising results. The work could be strengthened, particularly by employing a more contemporary image representation.
