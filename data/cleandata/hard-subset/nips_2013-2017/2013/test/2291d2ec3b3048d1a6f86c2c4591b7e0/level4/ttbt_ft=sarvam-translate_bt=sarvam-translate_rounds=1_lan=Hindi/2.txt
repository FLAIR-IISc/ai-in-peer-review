This paper presents a new technique for domain adaptation for computer vision datasets, which typically presents multiple aspects such as perspective, lighting, level of clutter, compression, etc. Instead of simply matching the datasets to the domain, which combines those aspects, the proposed technique automatically divides all sets of images across all datasets into domains. The divider is driven by two principles: creating separate domains in the feature space, and creating them in such a way that a good differential classifier can be trained on each (to classify the original classes, not to separate the domains). This technique is more likely to partition according to the underlying aspects rather than the datasets.

Originality and Importance:
Very little work has been done on automatically defining domains, and this paper proposes a good idea towards automatically discovering useful domains, which will help train a better classifier for the original problem.

On the negative side, I am not entirely convinced about the proposed adaptation method (section 2), as it is not clear how closely it solves the original problem (2)+(3). Furthermore, the two proposed driving criteria are not yet well integrated: the maximum learning ability criterion is only used as a 'wrap around' of the maximum separation criterion to determine the number of domains. Essentially, it acts as a post-hoc verification score that determines how well the domain partition has been learned, but the partition itself is created based solely on the separation criterion. An integrated process would instead produce a partition that maximizes some target function including both criteria.

Despite these shortcomings, which may be due to the fact that this type of work is still in quite exploratory stages, I think the paper is a step in the right direction for the community and should be accepted.

Quality and Clarity:
The paper is well-written, but there is a lack of figures to illustrate the presented concepts.

Experiments:
On the positive side, the experiments show a significant advantage in using the domains produced by the proposed method, using only the datasets as domains, and on very recent domain discovery techniques [19] (section 4.2).

The idea of including a test set in the 're आकारing' process is interesting, but not clearly presented (section 4.3). Furthermore, it corresponds to an imputation setting (i.e. all test data is available at the same time), but this is not explicitly stated.

On the negative side, the image descriptor used is very simple and outdated: just a bag of sparse SURF feature words for the entire image, and with only 800 codebook entries. This is really weak these days. I would advise the authors to use a spatial pyramid of the bag of computed word features on dense SURF features. Furthermore, it is not clear what similarity measure is used to compare the image descriptions, hopefully X^2 or an intersection kernel? Euclidean distance is not suitable for comparing histograms. As a next step, a Fisher vector representation could help further and finally place the image representation on the level of modern systems in this paper. The following papers may help the authors: Zhang IJCv 2006; Jury CVPvR 2005; Lazebnik CVPvR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010.

Overall, the paper presents interesting new ideas on an important problem and achieves good results. The paper could be improved in several ways, especially in terms of the image representation used.
