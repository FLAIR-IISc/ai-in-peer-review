This paper introduces a convex framework that partitions one or more datasets into K latent domains such that (i) the class-label distributions within each domain are similar, and (ii) domains are as distinct as possible. Distinctiveness is enforced by maximizing pairwise mean differences in an RKHS induced by a chosen kernel. The number of domains K is selected by maximizing average learnability, i.e., the quality of a classifier trained within each latent domain.

Strengths:
- Controlling class-label distributions during domain discovery within a relaxed convex formulation is a novel and appealing idea. It helps avoid domains that collapse onto a single class.
- Selecting K by evaluating intra-domain learnability is practical and tends to ensure each domain contains sufficient samples per class for effective training.
- Empirical results show reasonable gains over baselines, and qualitative visualizations help interpret what the method discovers.

Concerns and limitations:
- The assumption that class distributions should be similar across domains may be restrictive for some tasks. For example, if latent domains correspond to pose in animal classification, horses and cows may share some poses (e.g., left/right standing), but cows are more often depicted sitting than horses. Enforcing similar class distributions across domains could mask such genuine, class-dependent domain structure.
- Throughout the paper, the terms dataset, domain, and latent domain are conflated. This is most evident in Section 4.2, where S_i are referred to both as datasets and as source domains. Clear, formal definitions with simple examples are needed to avoid confusion.
- In Equation (7), the term max_k r(U_k, B) is used but not defined. Please define r(·) and all variables (U_k, B), and explain the role of this quantity in the optimization.
- The experimental protocol needs clearer justification. The choice of specific source–target pairs is not well motivated. A leave-one-dataset-out adaptation protocol (treating each dataset as target in turn) would provide a more systematic evaluation and reduce concerns about selection bias.

Clarifications requested:
- Precisely define dataset vs. (source/target) domain vs. latent domain, and how these relate in your setup. Provide a small schematic or toy example illustrating how data from multiple datasets are partitioned into latent domains.
- Detail the kernel and RKHS used to compute distinctiveness, and discuss sensitivity to kernel choice.
- Explain how K and other hyperparameters are selected in practice (e.g., validation strategy, stability across runs).
- Revisit the claim that the approach is “unsupervised.” Since class labels are explicitly used to encourage similar distributions, the method is more accurately described as weakly supervised or semi-supervised domain discovery.

Suggestions:
- Consider relaxing the class-distribution similarity constraint into a tunable regularizer, allowing the model to discover domains with modest but meaningful class-imbalance differences when warranted by the data.
- Report additional diagnostics, such as per-class domain composition, to show whether the constraint unduly homogenizes domains.
- Provide ablations isolating the effect of label-distribution control vs. the RKHS distinctiveness term.

Overall assessment:
The paper presents an interesting and technically sound formulation that combines label-distribution control with an RKHS-based distinctiveness objective and a learnability-driven selection of K. The empirical gains are promising. However, stronger conceptual clarity (terminology, definitions, and missing equation components), a more transparent evaluation protocol, and a nuanced discussion of the supervision assumptions are necessary to fully validate and position the contribution.
