- Proposes automatically partitioning pooled datasets into domains to better capture underlying factors (viewpoint, illumination, clutter) for improved domain adaptation.
- Partitioning is driven by two principles: domains should be separable in feature space and each domain should be learnable by a discriminative classifier.
- Original and significant: addresses the underexplored problem of discovering domains rather than assuming one-dataset-one-domain.
- Insightful formulation: the combined separability-and-learnability criteria move the field toward more realistic domain definitions.
- Paper is generally well-written and clear, but would benefit from more figures and visualizations of partitions, feature spaces, and example images.
- Strength: novel problem formulation that challenges the dataset-as-domain assumption.
- Strength: well-motivated methodological criteria (separability and learnability) as a foundation for domain discovery.
- Strength: experiments show significant improvements over using original datasets as domains and over a recent domain discovery baseline.
- Weakness: unclear optimization—algorithmic procedure in Section 2 does not clearly or convincingly optimize the stated objectives (eqs. 2–3).
- Weakness: separability and learnability are not integrated—learnability is used only for model selection rather than as part of a unified partitioning objective.
- Weakness: outdated image representation (sparse SURF bag-of-words with 800 codebook) likely limits performance; recommend dense features, spatial pyramids, better histogram metrics (Chi-squared/intersection), Fisher Vectors, and cite Lazebnik/Perronin.
- Weakness: unclear experimental setup in Section 4.3—using the test set in partitioning implies a transductive/batch assumption that must be explicitly stated and justified.
- Conclusion: novel and promising contribution that warrants acceptance after addressing optimization justification, modern representations, and clearer experimental assumptions.
