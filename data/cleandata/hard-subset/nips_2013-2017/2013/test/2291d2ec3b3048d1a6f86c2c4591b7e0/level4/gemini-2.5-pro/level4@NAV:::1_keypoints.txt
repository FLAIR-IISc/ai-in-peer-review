- Proposes automatic discovery of latent domains in training data and uses them in a domain adaptation framework to improve target test accuracy.  
- Introduces a kernel-based dissimilarity function and seeks partitions that maximize dissimilarity across domain pairs.  
- Formulates the problem as an integer program with constraints: each point assigned to one domain and each domain’s label distribution matches the global distribution.  
- Relaxes the integer program to a continuous quadratic optimization with linear constraints; selects the number of domains by cross-validation.  
- Evaluated on a static image dataset [2] and IXMAS multi-view action dataset [15]; shows improvement over domain adaptation method [19].  
- Positives: manuscript is clearly written, technique appears novel, and performance gain over [19] is notable.  
- Initial decision: weak reject, contingent on addressing two major concerns in rebuttal.  
- Major concern (i): Motivation unclear — claim that appearance clustering yields per-category domains lacks evidence and unclear how the formulation prevents class-based clustering.  
- Major concern (ii): Necessity of the label-prior constraint is not justified — request experiments removing the constraint and comparisons to simple baselines (k-means, random partitions) and an unsupervised method like Sivic et al. (ICCV ’05).  
- Clarification requested on what “maximally different in distribution” refers to (features vs. labels).  
- Question whether β_mk is already simplex-constrained by formulation.  
- Noted discrepancy between constraints presented around lines 153 and 182; request clarification.  
- Request justification/proof for the bound stated at line 215 and correction of typo (Eq (1): M'k should be Mk').  
- Ask for details on geodesic flow kernel implementation (public implementation or re-implemented).  
- Request deeper analysis of performance differences, reasons for superiority, and illustrative failure cases of baselines that the method fixes.  
- Suggest adding citation: Torralba & Efros (2011), “Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias.”  
- Final outcome: authors’ rebuttal satisfactorily addressed main concerns; reviewer updated recommendation to weak accept.
