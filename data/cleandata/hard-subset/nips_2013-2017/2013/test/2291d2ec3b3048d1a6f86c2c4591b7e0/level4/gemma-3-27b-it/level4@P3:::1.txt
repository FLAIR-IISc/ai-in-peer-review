The goal of this work is to automatically discover latent domains within a training dataset, subsequently leveraging these domains in a domain adaptation framework to improve classification performance on a test set. The paper introduces a method for measuring the difference between feature distributions using a kernel function, and aims to partition data points into domains that maximize this difference. The problem is formulated as an integer program with constraints ensuring each point belongs to one domain and that label distributions within each domain match the overall input distribution. This is then relaxed into a continuous optimization problem. Finally, the optimal number of domains is determined via cross-validation.

The approach is evaluated on standard datasets: images from Saenko et al. [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset [15]. Notably, the method demonstrates performance gains over the domain adaptation approach of Gong et al. [19].

**Strengths:** The paper is clearly written and the proposed approach appears novel, representing a valuable contribution to the domain adaptation literature. The demonstrated performance improvements over a strong baseline are encouraging. The authors address a crucial, often overlooked aspect of domain adaptation – the definition and discovery of domains themselves, rather than assuming they align with existing datasets. The inclusion of both object recognition and action recognition experiments strengthens the generalizability of the results.

**Weaknesses:** While the paper presents a promising approach, several areas require further clarification and investigation. The initial motivation for the work could be more strongly articulated. The claim that simple clustering leads to per-category domains (line 77) lacks supporting evidence and needs to be substantiated. More importantly, a clearer explanation of how the proposed formulation avoids this pitfall is needed. The necessity of the “label prior constraint” (line 154) is not fully justified, and its impact on performance should be analyzed. Finally, a comparison with simpler baseline domain discovery methods, such as k-means clustering or unsupervised feature learning (e.g., Sivic et al. ICCV '05), is essential to establish the benefits of the proposed method. 

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Line 82:** “maximally different in distribution from each other” – This phrase is frequently used. Specifying *what* aspects of the distribution are being compared (e.g., mean, variance, higher-order moments) would improve clarity.
*   **Line 166:** The statement that β<sub>mk</sub> already lives on a simplex appears correct, raising a question about the purpose of the relaxation.
*   **Line 182:** The constraints presented here seem to differ from those initially formulated on line 153. Clarification is needed.
*   **Line 215:** The justification or proof for the bound on *K<sub>max</sub>* requires more explanation.
*   **Line 289:** Provide details regarding the implementation of the geodesic flow kernel [4]. Was it a reimplementation or utilized from publicly available code?
*   **Lines 313/340:**  A deeper analysis of performance differences is needed. Identifying specific failure cases of the baselines that the proposed approach overcomes would provide valuable insights.
*   **Eq (1):** Typo: *M’<sub>k</sub>* should be *M<sub>k</sub>’*.
*   **Related Work:** Consider including Torralba & Efros [2011] as it provides a relevant discussion on dataset bias.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel approach to domain discovery, moving beyond the common practice of equating datasets with domains. The combination of maximizing distinctiveness and learnability is a unique contribution.

**Significance:**  The problem of automatically discovering meaningful domains is important for improving the robustness and generalization ability of domain adaptation algorithms.  If the method proves effective, it could have a significant impact on various computer vision applications. The potential to address the limitations of existing domain adaptation techniques by better characterizing the underlying data distributions is a valuable contribution.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear mathematical formulation and well-defined optimization procedure.  The experimental evaluation is reasonably thorough, though the inclusion of additional baselines (as suggested above) would strengthen the analysis.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to domain discovery.
*   Clear and technically sound formulation.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements over existing methods.
*   Evaluated on multiple datasets and tasks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Motivation could be stronger and more thoroughly justified.
*   The necessity of the label prior constraint is unclear.
*   Lack of comparison to simpler baseline domain discovery methods.




**Overall Assessment:** I initially leaned towards rejection, but the clarity of the writing and the novelty of the approach are compelling. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding the motivation, label prior constraint, and baseline comparisons, would significantly strengthen the paper. I now lean slightly towards acceptance, contingent on a strong rebuttal that addresses these points.
