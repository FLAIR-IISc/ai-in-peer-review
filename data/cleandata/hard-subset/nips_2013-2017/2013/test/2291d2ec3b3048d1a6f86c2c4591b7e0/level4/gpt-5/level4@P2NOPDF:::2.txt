This paper proposes a new approach to domain adaptation for computer vision datasets, which often vary along multiple factors such as viewpoint, illumination, clutter, and compression. Rather than equating domains with datasets (which conflates these factors), the method automatically partitions all images across datasets into domains. The partitioning is guided by two principles: (1) creating well-separated domains in feature space, and (2) ensuring that a strong discriminative classifier can be trained within each domain to predict the original classes (not to separate domains). This strategy aims to recover domains aligned with underlying factors of variation rather than dataset boundaries.

Originality and significance:
There is limited prior work on automatically defining domains. This paper offers a promising idea for discovering domains that can support training more effective classifiers for the primary task.

That said, I remain unconvinced by the optimization procedure in Section 2; it is unclear how closely it approximates the intended objective in Eqs. (2) and (3). In addition, the two driving criteria are not tightly integrated. The “maximal learnability” criterion is used mainly as a wrapper around the “maximal separability” criterion to select the number of domains. In effect, it serves as a post-hoc validation score to evaluate a partition found using separability alone, rather than participating in a joint objective. A more principled design would directly optimize a unified objective that balances separability and learnability during partitioning.

Despite these limitations—which may reflect the exploratory nature of this line of work—the paper points the community in a valuable direction and, in my view, merits acceptance.

Quality and clarity:
The writing is generally clear, but the exposition would benefit from figures to illustrate the key concepts and workflow.

Experiments:
On the positive side, the experiments show clear gains from using the discovered domains compared to treating datasets as domains and relative to the recent domain discovery method in [19] (Sec. 4.2).

The idea of incorporating the test set into the “reshaping” process is interesting but insufficiently explained (Sec. 4.3). This setup effectively assumes an imputation or transductive scenario in which all test data are available at once; this assumption should be stated explicitly and its implications discussed.

On the negative side, the image representation is outdated. The use of a single bag-of-words over sparse SURF with an 800-word codebook is weak by current standards. I recommend employing a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words computed on dense SURF features. The similarity measure for comparing histograms is also unspecified; chi-square or histogram intersection would be more suitable than Euclidean distance. As a stronger baseline and a natural next step, a Fisher Vector representation could substantially improve performance and align the paper’s representation with modern practice. Relevant references include Zhang et al., IJCV 2006; Jurie and Triggs, CVPR 2005; Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006; and Perronnin et al., ECCV 2010.

Overall assessment:
The paper introduces novel and relevant ideas for an important problem and reports promising results. It could be strengthened by integrating the two selection criteria into a single optimization, clarifying the transductive test-time reshaping, and adopting a more modern image representation and similarity measure.
