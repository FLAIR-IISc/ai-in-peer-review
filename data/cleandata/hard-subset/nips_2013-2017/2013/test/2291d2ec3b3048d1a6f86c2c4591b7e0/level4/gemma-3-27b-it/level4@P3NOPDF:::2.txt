This paper introduces a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision, addressing the challenge of varying factors such as viewpoint, illumination, and clutter that often exist across datasets. Rather than treating datasets as monolithic domains, the authors propose an automatic partitioning of images – across datasets – into domains guided by two key principles: maximizing feature space separation between domains and ensuring each domain supports the training of a strong discriminative classifier for the original task. This approach aims to uncover domains aligned with underlying visual aspects, rather than arbitrary dataset boundaries.

**Originality and Significance:**

The work addresses an important and relatively unexplored problem – automatic domain definition. The proposed approach represents a valuable step towards discovering meaningful domains that can improve classifier performance. The idea of automatically learning domain structure, instead of relying on pre-defined dataset labels, is a significant contribution. 

However, the optimization method described in Section 2 warrants further scrutiny. The connection between the proposed optimization and the objective function comprising equations (2) and (3) is not entirely clear. Furthermore, the two guiding criteria – maximal separability and maximal learnability – currently lack tight integration. The learnability criterion appears to function primarily as a post-hoc validation mechanism for selecting the optimal number of domains, determined after the partitioning is performed based solely on separability. A more cohesive approach would directly incorporate both criteria into a unified objective function during the partitioning process. Despite this limitation, which may be inherent in the exploratory nature of this work, the paper offers a promising direction for the field.

**Quality and Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and the concepts are articulated clearly. However, the lack of illustrative figures hinders comprehension. Visual examples demonstrating the domain partitioning process and the resulting feature space arrangements would significantly enhance the reader’s understanding.

**Experiments:**

The experimental results demonstrate a clear advantage of the proposed domain discovery method compared to using datasets as domains and outperform the recent technique described in [19] (Section 4.2). The inclusion of the test set in the “reshaping” process (Section 4.3) is an interesting concept, but its presentation could be improved for clarity. The implicit assumption of an imputation setting—where all test data is available simultaneously—should be explicitly stated.

A critical weakness lies in the choice of image descriptor. The reliance on a single bag-of-words representation of sparse SURF features, with only 800 codebook entries, feels outdated. Modern computer vision systems leverage more powerful and nuanced representations. I strongly recommend the authors explore more sophisticated approaches, such as a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words computed on dense SURF features. Furthermore, the paper does not specify the similarity measure used to compare image descriptors. Euclidean distance is not appropriate for comparing histograms; the authors should clarify whether a chi-squared distance or an intersection kernel was employed. Future work could benefit from adopting a Fisher Vector representation to further enhance the image representation and align with current state-of-the-art methods. Relevant papers to consider include Zhang et al. (IJCV 2006), Jurie et al. (CVPR 2005), Lazebnik et al. (CVPR 2006), and Perronin et al. (ECCV 2010). 

**Overall:**

This paper presents innovative ideas addressing an important problem in domain adaptation and achieves compelling results. While several aspects, particularly the image representation and the integration of the optimization criteria, could be improved, the work represents a significant step forward. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns raised regarding the image descriptor and potentially clarifying the optimization process.
