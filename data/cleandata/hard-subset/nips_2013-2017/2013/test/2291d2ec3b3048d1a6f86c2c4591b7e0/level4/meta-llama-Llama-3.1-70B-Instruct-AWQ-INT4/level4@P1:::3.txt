This paper proposes a convex framework for splitting the dataset(s) into K subsets, referred to as latent domains, such that each subset has similar semantic class distributions and is as distinct from each other as possible. The distinction, named as maximum distinctiveness, is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences of subsets in the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) induced by a selected kernel. Each of these subsets is named as a latent domain. Identification of K (number of latent domains) is achieved by maximizing average learnability (how well a classifier can be learned) within each latent domain.

While the proposed framework is novel, the assumption that the distribution of the classes within each domain should be as similar as possible may be limiting for certain tasks. For instance, if we consider poses as the latent domains for an animal classification task (e.g., horses and cows), then the latent domain distribution within each class might not be similar. Specifically, we may observe both horses and cows in left-right standing poses, but horses are not often pictured in a sitting pose, whereas cows are. This suggests that the proposed framework may not be suitable for all tasks, and the assumption of similar class distributions within each domain should be carefully evaluated.

Although the discovery of latent domains is not new, the idea of controlling class label distributions for better identification of latent domains within a (relaxed) convex framework is a notable contribution. Identifying the number of latent domains through checking the quality of classification within each latent domain is also a valuable practice, ensuring that the latent domain has enough samples for each class to better generalize and learn discrimination between classes.

However, the paper could benefit from clearer definitions and distinctions between the concepts of dataset, domain, and latent domain. These concepts are often used interchangeably, which may lead to confusion. For example, in section 4.2, the terms "dataset" and "domain" are used to refer to the same entities, which may cause confusion. Providing clear definitions and examples would help to alleviate this issue.

Additionally, some notations and equations could be clarified. For instance, the notation maxk r(Uk,B) is not defined but used in equation (6). Providing clear explanations for these notations and equations would improve the readability of the paper.

The experimental validation appears to be adequate, with reasonable improvements above the baselines. However, the choice of source and target datasets for reporting results is not clearly justified. For instance, a leave-one-dataset-out adaptation approach might be a more reasonable evaluation strategy. The qualitative results are helpful in visualizing what the algorithm achieves.

Finally, the problem of learning latent domains is stated as an unsupervised learning problem, which might be misleading since the methods use semantic class labels. A more accurate description would be that the discovery of latent domains is formulated as a supervised learning problem, where the goal is to encourage similar class distributions in each latent domain within a (relaxed) convex framework. This is a notable technical contribution, but some concepts in the paper and the experimental validation need to be clarified.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel convex framework for discovering latent domains.
* The idea of controlling class label distributions for better identification of latent domains is a valuable contribution.
* The experimental validation shows reasonable improvements above the baselines.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The assumption of similar class distributions within each domain may be limiting for certain tasks.
* The paper could benefit from clearer definitions and distinctions between the concepts of dataset, domain, and latent domain.
* Some notations and equations could be clarified.
* The choice of source and target datasets for reporting results is not clearly justified.
* The problem statement could be more accurate, reflecting the use of semantic class labels.
