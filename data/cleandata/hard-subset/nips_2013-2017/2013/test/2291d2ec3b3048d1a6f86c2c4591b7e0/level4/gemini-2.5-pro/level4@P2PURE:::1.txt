This paper proposes a method to automatically discover latent domains within a training set, which are then used in a domain adaptation framework to improve classification performance. The core idea is to partition data points into domains by maximizing a kernel-based distance function between pairs of domains. This objective is formulated as an integer programming problem with two key constraints: (1) each data point must be assigned to exactly one domain, and (2) the class label distribution within each discovered domain must match the global label distribution of the entire dataset. The authors relax this problem into a continuous quadratic optimization with linear constraints and use cross-validation to determine the optimal number of domains.

The method is evaluated on two datasets, the static image dataset of [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset [15], demonstrating improved performance over the domain adaptation technique of [19].

**Strengths:**
*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
*   The proposed approach for automatic domain discovery appears to be novel, to the best of my knowledge.
*   The empirical performance gains over the strong baseline of [19] are a significant contribution.

**Major Concerns:**

Initially, my inclination is towards rejection. My score may be raised pending the authors' response to the following two primary concerns:

1.  **Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper's core motivation is not sufficiently justified. The authors claim (line 77) that "simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains." First, this claim requires substantiation or evidence. Second, it is not clear how the proposed model formulation in Section 2, particularly the objective function, explicitly prevents this from occurring. How is the model enforced to discover domains that are not simply aligned with class categories?

2.  **Necessity of the Label Prior Constraint and Baselines:** The rationale for the second constraint, the "label prior constraint" (line 154), is unclear. An ablation study showing performance without this constraint would be highly informative to understand its impact. Furthermore, to properly contextualize the contribution, the experiments should include comparisons against simpler domain discovery baselines. For instance, what is the performance when domains are formed by applying k-means clustering on appearance features, using an unsupervised object discovery method (e.g., Sivic et al., ICCV 2005), or even by randomly partitioning the data into sets of equal size?

**Additional Comments:**
*   **Line 82:** The phrase "maximally different in distribution from each other" is used throughout the paper. The authors should clarify what distribution this refers to (e.g., the distribution over features, labels, etc.).
*   **Line 166:** Is the explicit simplex constraint on \beta_{mk} necessary? It seems that \beta_{mk} as formulated already lies on a simplex.
*   **Line 182:** The constraints presented here appear to be different from those introduced starting on line 153. Please clarify this discrepancy.
*   **Line 215:** A justification or proof for the bound presented here is needed.
*   **Line 289:** Please provide more implementation details regarding the use of the geodesic flow kernel [4]. Was this a re-implementation, or was publicly available code used?
*   **Lines 313/340:** The paper reports superior performance but lacks a qualitative analysis. To better understand the advantages of the proposed method, it would be beneficial to include insights or examples of systematic failure cases for the baseline methods that are overcome by this approach.
*   **Eq (1):** There appears to be a typo. "M'k" should likely be "Mk'".
*   **Citation:** The authors might consider citing the following relevant work on dataset bias: A. Torralba, A. Efros, "Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias," CVPR 2011.

**Post-rebuttal Update:**
The rebuttal has addressed my concerns regarding the paper's motivation and the label prior constraint. I am now leaning slightly towards acceptance.
