Summary
The paper addresses the problem of discovering latent domains within visual datasets to improve domain adaptation. The authors propose to split one or more labeled datasets into K latent domains that satisfy two properties: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. Distinctiveness is enforced by maximizing pairwise differences between domain distributions measured in an RKHS via a kernel two-sample statistic (i.e., mean embeddings/MMD). To prevent trivial “per-class” clusters, a label prior constraint enforces that the class label proportions within each latent domain approximately match the global label prior. The resulting assignment problem is relaxed into a (continuous) optimization over simplex variables. The number of latent domains, K, is chosen by domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV): train classifiers within each latent domain and select K that maximizes the weighted average validation accuracy, thus encouraging domains that are both distinct and predictive.

Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) demonstrate that reshaping training datasets into latent domains improves adaptation to target sets, including scenarios where test datasets are themselves heterogeneous. The method is compared to using original datasets as domains and to prior latent domain discovery (e.g., Hoffman et al.), and qualitative examples illustrate the visual characteristics captured by the discovered domains.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The distinctiveness objective is well motivated by nonparametric two-sample testing in RKHS, and the label prior constraint is a practical way to avoid degeneracy. The optimization is presented as a relaxed (continuous) problem with linear constraints; while maximizing a convex quadratic is generally hard, the authors report satisfactory solutions with standard solvers.
- Empirical support: Results show consistent improvements over baselines, and the DWCV procedure is sensible for selecting K. The qualitative analyses help interpret what factors (pose, appearance, background) the latent domains capture.
- Caveat: The paper refers to the problem as unsupervised in places, yet the method uses class labels via the label prior constraint and DWCV. This characterization can be misleading; the approach is more accurately semi-supervised or supervised in the source.

Clarity
- The concepts of dataset, domain, and latent domain need clearer, consistent definitions, ideally with examples up front. In Section 4.2, S is called both “datasets” and “source domains,” which can confuse the reader. Please standardize terminology throughout (e.g., datasets are collections, domains are distributions; original domains are the provided datasets; latent domains are the discovered splits).
- Notation: The quantity max_k r(U_k, B), used in Eq. 7, is not defined when first introduced. Please define r(A → B) and related notation before use.
- The claim that the problem is “unsupervised” (e.g., around line 074) should be revised to reflect reliance on class labels for both the LPC and DWCV.

Originality
- Discovering latent domains is not entirely new (e.g., Hoffman et al., ECCV 2012), but this work’s combination of (i) a nonparametric, kernel-based distinctiveness measure, (ii) an explicit label prior constraint to prevent per-class clustering within a relaxed optimization framework, and (iii) selecting K via domain-wise learnability is novel and practically appealing.

Significance
- The ability to automatically reshape datasets into more coherent domains is valuable for domain adaptation, especially in vision where “domains” are usually proxies like datasets or camera views. The empirical gains over original datasets and prior latent domain discovery indicate that these ideas could be adopted by practitioners. The conditional reshaping of test sets is also a useful extension for heterogeneous targets.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The label prior constraint, while effective at avoiding per-class splits, may be too restrictive in some applications. For example, if latent domains correspond to poses in an animal classification task (e.g., horses and cows), the per-class pose distributions may differ substantially across classes (e.g., cows are often photographed sitting; horses rarely are). Enforcing similar label distributions across domains could hinder discovery of such meaningful latent factors.
- Experimental choices: The selection of source–target pairs is not fully justified. A leave-one-dataset-out adaptation protocol would be a more transparent and standard evaluation for cross-dataset adaptation.
- Reporting: While qualitative visualizations are helpful, clearer explanations of the experimental setup in Section 4.2 (terminology, notation, and the role of DWCV) would improve readability.

Pros
- Clear, principled objective for domain distinctiveness using kernel mean embeddings/MMD.
- Practical label prior constraint to avoid trivial per-class domains.
- Sensible, data-driven method (DWCV) to select the number of latent domains.
- Demonstrated improvements over baselines and prior latent domain discovery; informative qualitative analyses.
- Extension to conditional reshaping of test sets for heterogeneous targets.

Cons
- The label prior constraint may suppress real, class-dependent domain structure (e.g., pose distributions varying by class).
- Ambiguity between “dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain,” and missing notation definitions (e.g., in Eq. 7).
- The “unsupervised” characterization is misleading given the use of labels.
- Experimental protocol choices could be better justified; leave-one-dataset-out would strengthen the claims.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify terminology (dataset vs. domain vs. latent domain) early, and ensure consistent use throughout; revise Section 4.2 accordingly. Define r(·) and related notation before Eq. 7.
- Revisit the “unsupervised” wording to accurately reflect label usage.
- Discuss the limitations of the label prior constraint and, if possible, provide a variant or sensitivity analysis (e.g., varying δ) for cases with class-specific domain factors.
- Justify the selection of source/target configurations, and consider adding a leave-one-dataset-out evaluation to strengthen empirical validation.

Overall, the paper presents a useful and well-motivated approach to latent domain discovery with solid empirical benefits, but would benefit from clearer exposition and a more transparent evaluation protocol.
