This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in a training set, which is then used in a domain adaptation framework to yield improved classification performance on a test set. The key idea is to partition the data points into domains such that the difference between the distributions of each pair of domains is maximized. This is achieved through a non-parametric formulation that imposes two desirable properties on the identified domains: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.

The paper is well-written, and the approach is novel. The performance gains over previous domain adaptation approaches are appreciated. However, there are some concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the motivation for this paper is not entirely clear. The claim that simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains is not supported by evidence. Furthermore, it is not clear how the model formulation overcomes this issue. A more detailed explanation of how the proposed approach avoids reshaping into per-category domains would be helpful.

Secondly, the necessity of the label prior constraint (LPC) is not entirely clear. The performance of the approach without this constraint should be evaluated, and a comparison with a baseline that partitions the data using k-means clustering or unsupervised object discovery would be informative. Additionally, the performance when the dataset is randomly partitioned into equal sets should be reported.

Some minor comments include the need for clarification on what is meant by "maximally different in distribution from each other." The distribution over what is not explicitly stated. Furthermore, the formulation of βmk already lives on a simplex, so the relaxation to a continuous optimization may not be necessary. The constraints formulated on lines 182-183 appear to be different from those formulated earlier, and the justification for the bound on line 215 is not provided.

In terms of experimental evaluation, more details on the use of the geodesic flow kernel would be helpful. It is not clear whether this is a reimplementation or publicly available source code was used. A more in-depth analysis of the differences in performance between the proposed approach and the baselines would be informative. It would be helpful to show systematic failures of the baselines that the proposed approach overcomes.

Overall, the paper presents a novel approach to domain adaptation, and the performance gains are promising. However, addressing the concerns mentioned above would strengthen the paper and provide a clearer understanding of the proposed approach.
