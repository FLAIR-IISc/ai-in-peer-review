This paper proposes a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision datasets, which typically exhibit multiple aspects such as viewpoint, illumination, level of clutter, compression, and others. Unlike existing approaches that equate domains with datasets, thereby confounding these aspects, the proposed method automatically partitions the set of all images across datasets into domains. This partitioning is driven by two principles: separating domains in feature space and ensuring that a good discriminative classifier can be trained on each domain to classify the original classes, rather than distinguishing between domains. As a result, this technique is more likely to partition based on the underlying aspects rather than datasets.

Originality and significance:
The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field by proposing a method for automatically defining domains, a topic that has received limited attention. This approach has the potential to facilitate the training of better classifiers for the original problem.

However, there are some concerns regarding the proposed optimization method (Section 2), as it is unclear how closely it approximates the original problem (2)+(3). Furthermore, the two driving criteria are not well integrated, with the maximal learnability criterion serving only as a post-hoc validation score to determine the optimal number of domains. The maximal separability criterion alone is used for partitioning, whereas an integrated approach would ideally produce a partitioning that maximizes a goal function incorporating both criteria directly.

Despite these limitations, which may be attributed to the exploratory nature of this research, the paper represents a step in the right direction for the community and warrants acceptance.

Quality and clarity:
The paper is well-written, but lacks figures to illustrate the presented concepts, which would enhance clarity.

Experiments:
The experiments demonstrate a significant advantage of using the proposed method to produce domains, outperforming the use of datasets as domains and the recent domain discovery technique [19] (Section 4.2). The idea of incorporating the test set in the 'reshaping' process (Section 4.3) is interesting, although its presentation is unclear. Additionally, this approach corresponds to an imputation setting, where all test data is available simultaneously, but this is not explicitly stated.

However, the image descriptor used is overly simplistic and outdated, relying on a single bag-of-words of sparse SURF features for the entire image, with only 800 codebook entries. This representation is weak by current standards. The authors are advised to employ a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words, computed on dense SURF features, and consider using a Fisher Vector representation to further enhance the image representation. The following papers may provide guidance: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010. Furthermore, it is unclear what similarity measure is used to compare image descriptors; ideally, it should be X^2 or an intersection kernel, as Euclidean distance is not suitable for comparing histograms.

Overall, the paper presents novel and interesting ideas on an important problem, achieving good results. However, there is room for improvement, particularly in terms of the image representation used.
