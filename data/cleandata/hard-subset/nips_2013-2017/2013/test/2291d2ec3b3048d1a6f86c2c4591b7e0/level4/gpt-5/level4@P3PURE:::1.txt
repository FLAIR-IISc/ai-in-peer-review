Summary:
The paper aims to automatically discover latent domains within a training set and then leverage these domains in a domain adaptation framework to improve test-time classification performance. The method defines a function that measures the difference between pairs of feature vectors under a specified kernel. The objective is to partition the dataset into domains such that this function is maximized across points belonging to different domains. The problem is initially posed as an integer program with two constraints: (1) each data point is assigned to exactly one domain, and (2) the class-label distribution within each domain matches the overall label distribution of the entire dataset. The authors relax this to a continuous optimization problem with a quadratic objective and linear constraints, and select the number of domains via cross-validation. The approach is evaluated on two datasets (the static images dataset of [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset of [15]), and shows improved performance over the domain adaptation method of [19], which is a notable highlight.

Strengths:
- The paper is clearly written.
- As far as I can judge (I am not an expert on domain adaptation), the approach appears novel.
- The empirical gains over [19] are appreciated and suggest practical impact.

Main concerns (pre-rebuttal):
I was initially leaning slightly toward rejection, primarily due to two concerns that I asked the authors to address:

1) Motivation and avoidance of per-category domains (line 77):
- The paper states that “simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains.” What is the evidence for this claim?
- More importantly, how does the model in Section 2 prevent such per-category reshaping in practice? Please make explicit how the formulation enforces or encourages discovery of domains that are not simply aligned with object categories.

2) Necessity and impact of the “label prior constraint” (line 154):
- Why is the second constraint (matching each domain’s label distribution to the global label distribution) needed?
- What is the performance without this constraint?
- Please include baseline partitions obtained by simple methods such as k-means clustering or unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al., ICCV 2005) on the appearance features.
- Also report performance when the dataset is randomly partitioned into equal-sized subsets.

Additional comments and requests for clarification:
- Line 82: The phrase “maximally different in distribution from each other” appears multiple times. Different with respect to what distribution? Please clarify precisely what distribution is being compared (e.g., distribution of features, labels, kernel-induced distances, etc.) and how this is estimated.
- Line 166: As written, doesn’t β_{mk} already lie on a simplex? Please clarify.
- Line 182 vs. line 153: The constraints stated here seem to differ from the earlier formulation. Please reconcile or explain the relationship between these sets of constraints.
- Line 215: Please provide a justification or proof sketch for the stated bound.
- Line 289: Please provide more implementation details on the use of the geodesic flow kernel [4]. Was this a reimplementation or did you use publicly available code? If the latter, please cite the source and specify any deviations.
- Lines 313/340: Please discuss why the proposed approach outperforms the baselines. What systematic failures of the baselines does your method overcome? Providing qualitative or quantitative analyses of such failures would strengthen the paper.
- Eq. (1): The notation M'k should be corrected to Mk'.
- Suggested related work to cite: A. Torralba and A. Efros, “Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias,” CVPR 2011.

Post-rebuttal assessment:
The rebuttal adequately addressed my main concerns regarding the motivation and the role of the label prior constraint. With those clarifications, I now lean slightly toward acceptance.
