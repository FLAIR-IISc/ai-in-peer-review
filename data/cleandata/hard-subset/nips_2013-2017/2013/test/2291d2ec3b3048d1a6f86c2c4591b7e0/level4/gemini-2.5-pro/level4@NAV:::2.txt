This work introduces a novel method for domain adaptation in computer vision, addressing datasets that encompass various factors such as viewpoint, illumination, clutter, and compression artifacts. Rather than conflating domains with entire datasets—a common approach that merges these underlying factors—the authors' technique automatically partitions the complete image collection across all datasets into distinct domains. This partitioning process is guided by two core principles: ensuring that the resulting domains are well-separated in the feature space, and that each domain is amenable to training an effective discriminative classifier for the primary classification task (i.e., not for domain separation). Consequently, this method is poised to generate partitions that reflect the true underlying visual aspects instead of merely the dataset of origin.

**Originality and Significance:**
The automatic definition of domains is an under-explored area, and this paper offers a valuable contribution by proposing a promising direction for discovering domains that facilitate the training of more robust classifiers for the target task.

On the other hand, some concerns arise regarding the proposed optimization method (Section 2). Specifically, its fidelity in solving the objective function defined in equations (2) and (3) is not sufficiently demonstrated. Furthermore, the two guiding criteria—maximal learnability and maximal separability—appear to be loosely coupled. The learnability criterion is primarily employed as a post-processing step, or a "wrapper," to select the optimal number of domains, rather than being integral to the partitioning process itself. It functions more as a post-hoc validation metric to evaluate partitions generated for a given number of domains, while the partitioning is driven exclusively by the separability criterion. A more unified approach would directly optimize a single objective function that incorporates both criteria to generate the final partition.

Notwithstanding these limitations, which are perhaps characteristic of the exploratory nature of this research area, this work represents a positive advancement for the field and warrants acceptance.

**Quality and Clarity:**
The manuscript is clearly written, although it would benefit significantly from the inclusion of figures to visually illustrate the core concepts.

**Experiments:**
The experimental results are a strength of the paper, demonstrating a clear performance improvement when using the discovered domains compared to both using entire datasets as domains and the recent method of [19] (Section 4.2). The concept of incorporating the test set during the "reshaping" phase (Section 4.3) is intriguing, though its presentation lacks clarity. This setup implies an imputation setting where all test data is available concurrently, a condition that should be explicitly stated.

A major weakness, however, lies in the choice of image representation, which is simplistic and outdated by current standards. The use of a single bag-of-words histogram from sparse SURF features with a small 800-word codebook is insufficient for modern computer vision tasks. I strongly suggest the authors adopt a more powerful representation, such as a spatial pyramid model using bag-of-words computed on densely sampled SURF features. Additionally, the paper does not specify the similarity metric used for comparing image descriptors; a Chi-squared distance or an intersection kernel would be appropriate, as Euclidean distance is ill-suited for histogram comparison. To further elevate the work, employing a Fisher Vector representation would align the paper's image encoding with state-of-the-art systems. The authors may find the following references helpful: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010.

In summary, this paper introduces compelling and novel ideas for a significant problem, supported by strong empirical results. There are several avenues for improvement, most notably regarding the modernization of the image representation pipeline.
