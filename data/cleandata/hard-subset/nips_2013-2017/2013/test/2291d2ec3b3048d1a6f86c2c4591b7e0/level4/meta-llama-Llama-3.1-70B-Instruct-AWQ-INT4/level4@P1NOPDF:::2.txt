This paper presents a new technique for domain adaptation in computer vision datasets, which typically exhibit multiple aspects, such as viewpoint, illumination, level of clutter, compression, and more. Unlike existing methods that equate domains with datasets, thereby mixing these aspects together, the proposed technique automatically partitions the set of all images across all datasets into domains. The partitioning process is driven by two key principles: creating separate domains in feature space and ensuring that a good discriminative classifier can be trained on each domain to classify the original classes (not to separate the domains). This approach is more likely to partition the data according to the underlying aspects rather than the datasets.

Originality and significance:
There is limited work on automatically defining domains, and this paper proposes a promising idea for automatically discovering useful domains that can support the training of better classifiers for the original problem.

On the negative side, I am not fully convinced by the proposed optimization method (section 2), as it is unclear how closely it solves the original problem (2)+(3). Furthermore, the two proposed driving criteria are not well integrated: the maximal learnability criterion is only used as a 'wrapper around' the maximal separability criterion to determine the number of domains. In essence, it acts as a post-hoc validation score to evaluate the quality of the domain partitioning learned for a given number of domains, but the partitioning itself is based solely on the separability criterion. A more integrated process would produce a partitioning that maximizes a goal function incorporating both criteria directly.

Despite these shortcomings, which may be due to the exploratory nature of this type of work, I believe that the paper is a step in the right direction for the community and should be accepted.

Quality and clarity:
The paper is well-written, but it lacks figures to illustrate the concepts presented.

Experiments:
On the positive side, the experiments demonstrate a significant advantage in using the domains produced by the proposed method over simply using datasets as domains and over the recent domain discovery technique [19] (sec. 4.2).

The idea of including the test set in the 'reshaping' process is interesting, but not clearly presented (sec. 4.3). Additionally, this corresponds to an imputation setting (i.e., all test data is available at the same time), but this is not stated explicitly.

On the negative side, the image descriptor used is very simple and outdated: a single bag-of-words of sparse SURF features for the entire image, with only 800 codebook entries. This is suboptimal by today's standards. I recommend that the authors use a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words, computed on dense SURF features. Moreover, it is unclear what similarity measure is used to compare image descriptors; hopefully, it is X^2 or an intersection kernel, as the Euclidean distance is not suitable for comparing histograms. As a next step, a Fisher Vector representation could further improve the image representation and bring it up to the level of modern systems. The authors may find the following papers helpful: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010.

Overall, the paper presents interesting novel ideas on an important problem and achieves good results. The paper can be improved in several ways, particularly in terms of the image representation used.
