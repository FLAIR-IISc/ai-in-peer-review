This paper introduces a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision datasets, which typically exhibit multiple aspects such as viewpoint, illumination, level of clutter, compression, and more. Unlike existing methods that equate domains with datasets, thereby entangling these aspects, the proposed technique automatically partitions the entire set of images across all datasets into distinct domains. This partitioning process is guided by two key principles: creating separate domains in feature space and ensuring that a robust discriminative classifier can be trained on each domain to classify the original classes, rather than distinguishing between domains. This approach is more likely to partition based on the underlying aspects rather than the datasets themselves.

Originality and significance:

The paper proposes a valuable idea for automatically discovering useful domains, which can lead to training better classifiers for the original problem. There is limited work on automatically defining domains, making this contribution noteworthy.

However, some concerns exist. The proposed optimization method (section 2) is not entirely convincing, as it is unclear how closely it solves the original problem (2)+(3). Furthermore, the two driving criteria are not well-integrated; the maximal learnability criterion is used as a 'wrapper' around the maximal separability criterion to determine the number of domains. Essentially, it serves as a post-hoc validation score to evaluate the quality of the domain partitioning learned for a given number of domains, whereas the partitioning itself is based solely on the separability criterion. A more integrated approach would yield a partitioning that maximizes a goal function incorporating both criteria directly.

Despite these limitations, which may be attributed to the exploratory nature of this work, the paper is a step in the right direction for the community and warrants acceptance.

Quality and clarity:

The paper is well-written but lacks figures to illustrate the presented concepts.

Experiments:

The experiments demonstrate a significant advantage in using the domains produced by the proposed method over using datasets as domains and over the recent domain discovery technique [19] (sec. 4.2). The idea of including the test set in the 'reshaping' process is intriguing but not clearly presented (sec. 4.3). Additionally, this corresponds to an imputation setting, where all test data is available simultaneously, but this is not explicitly stated.

On the negative side, the image descriptor used is outdated and overly simplistic: a single bag-of-words of sparse SURF features for the entire image with only 800 codebook entries. This is no longer suitable for modern systems. It is recommended that the authors employ a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words, computed on dense SURF features. Moreover, the similarity measure used to compare image descriptors is unclear; hopefully, it is X^2 or an intersection kernel, as the Euclidean distance is not suitable for comparing histograms. Consideration of a Fisher Vector representation could further enhance the image representation and bring it in line with modern systems. The authors may find the following papers helpful: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010.

Overall, the paper presents interesting novel ideas on an important problem and achieves good results. The paper can be improved in several ways, particularly in terms of the image representation used.
