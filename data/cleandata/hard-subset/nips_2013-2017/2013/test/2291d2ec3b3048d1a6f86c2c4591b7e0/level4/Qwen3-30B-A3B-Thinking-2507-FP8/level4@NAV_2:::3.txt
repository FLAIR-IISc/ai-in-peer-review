

This paper introduces a novel nonparametric framework for discovering latent domains within visual datasets by enforcing two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing inter-domain distribution differences via RKHS mean discrepancy) and maximum learnability (determining the optimal number of domains K through domain-wise cross-validation). The authors formulate domain discovery as an optimization problem that avoids parametric assumptions, contrasting with prior work like [20] that relies on Gaussian mixture models. The method is evaluated on object and activity recognition tasks, demonstrating improved adaptation performance over both original datasets and [20]'s approach.

Strengths: The technical contribution is significant, particularly the nonparametric formulation for domain distinctiveness and the DWCV procedure for determining K, which ensures domains contain sufficient data for discriminative learning. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of equating datasets with domains—a common practice in visual domain adaptation—and provides compelling empirical evidence for the utility of latent domain discovery. The qualitative analysis (Fig. 1) effectively illustrates how discovered domains align with real-world factors like pose and background.

Weaknesses: 
- **Clarity**: The manuscript conflates "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain" throughout (e.g., Section 4.2 refers to "datasets" as source domains S₁, S₂, etc., while Eq. 6 uses "domains" interchangeably). This ambiguity requires precise definitions with examples, especially since the core contribution is domain discovery *within* datasets. The term "latent domain" should be consistently used for the discovered subsets.
- **Originality**: While the DWCV approach for determining K is novel, the paper understates the limitation of enforcing similar class distributions per domain (via the label prior constraint). This constraint may be inappropriate for tasks where domain factors (e.g., pose) correlate with specific classes (e.g., cows appearing in sitting poses but horses rarely do), potentially biasing domain discovery. The claim that "domain discovery is not new" [20] is accurate but should be contextualized by explicitly contrasting the *methodology* (nonparametric vs. parametric) and *goal* (general latent discovery vs. landmark-based adaptation).
- **Significance**: The experimental validation is adequate but lacks a systematic evaluation strategy. The choice of source/target domain splits (e.g., "Caltech vs. Amazon") appears arbitrary; a leave-one-dataset-out evaluation would better demonstrate robustness. The oracle accuracy in Table 1 (Eq. 6) is not defined in the text, causing confusion.

The paper addresses a critical gap in visual domain adaptation by moving beyond dataset-as-domain assumptions. However, clarifying the terminology, justifying the class distribution constraint, and strengthening the evaluation methodology would significantly enhance its impact. The technical approach is sound, but the current presentation risks confusing readers about the relationship between datasets and latent domains.
