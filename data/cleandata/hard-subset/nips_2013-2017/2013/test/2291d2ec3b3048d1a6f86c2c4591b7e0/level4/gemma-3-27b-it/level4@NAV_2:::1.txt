The goal of this work is to automatically discover latent domains in a training set, which is subsequently used in a domain adaptation framework to yield improved classification performance on a test set. The paper defines a kernel-based function to measure the difference between distributions, and proposes a method to partition the data into domains that maximize this difference. The problem is formulated as an integer programming problem subject to constraints ensuring each instance belongs to one domain and that label distributions within each domain mirror the overall label distribution. This problem is relaxed to a continuous optimization, and the number of domains is determined via cross-validation. 

The approach is evaluated on object recognition using datasets from [2] and on human activity recognition with the IXMAS dataset [15]. A key result is improved performance compared to the domain adaptation method of [19]. 

Positives: The paper is well-written and the approach appears novel, although I am not a specialist in domain adaptation. The reported performance gains over [19] are encouraging. The inclusion of both object recognition and action recognition tasks strengthens the evaluation.

Negatives: I currently lean towards rejection, but am open to being persuaded by a strong rebuttal. My primary concerns are centered around motivation and justification of key design choices.

(i) The motivation for this work is not fully convincing. The paper argues that simple clustering by appearance leads to per-category domains (line 77). However, there is little evidence presented to support this claim. Furthermore, it's unclear how the proposed model formulation in Section 2 avoids this issue—a more explicit explanation of how the formulation prevents collapsing into per-category domains is needed.

(ii) Relatedly, the necessity of the “label prior constraint” (LPC) on line 154 is unclear. What is the performance impact of removing this constraint? A comparison against simpler baselines would be valuable. Specifically, performance should be contrasted with partitioning the data using k-means clustering or unsupervised feature learning-based object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al. ICCV '05) applied to the appearance vectors. Additionally, the performance with a random partitioning of the data into equal sets should be reported.

Additional comments: 

+ Line 82, “maximally different in distribution from each other”: This statement is repeated throughout the paper. It requires clarification. What is meant by “distribution” in this context – distribution over features, labels, or something else?

+ Line 166: The variables β<sub>mk</sub> are already defined as residing on a simplex, so the statement “relaxing them to live on the simplex” is slightly misleading.

+ Line 182: The constraints presented here appear different than those originally formulated on line 153. This requires clarification.

+ Line 215: What is the justification or theoretical basis for the stated bound on K<sub>max</sub>?

+ Line 289: More detail is needed regarding the use of the geodesic flow kernel [4]. Was a public implementation used, or was it reimplemented for this work?

+ Lines 313/340: The discussion of performance differences could be strengthened. Providing more insightful analysis of *why* the proposed approach outperforms the baselines – identifying systematic failure modes of the baselines that the proposed approach addresses – would be beneficial.

+ Equation (1): Typo – M'<sub>k</sub> should be M<sub>k</sub>'.

+ Relevant citation: Torralba & Efros, "Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias," CVPR 2011. This paper addresses related issues of dataset bias and domain generalization.
