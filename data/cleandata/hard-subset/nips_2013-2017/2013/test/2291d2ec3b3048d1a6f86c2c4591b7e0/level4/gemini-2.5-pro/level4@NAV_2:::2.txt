This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation in computer vision, where standard practice often equates datasets with domains. The authors argue this is a flawed proxy for the true underlying factors (e.g., viewpoint, lighting) that cause distribution shifts. They propose a novel method to automatically discover latent domains within a collection of images by partitioning the data according to two principles: maximizing the distributional distinctiveness between domains and ensuring that a strong discriminative classifier can be learned within each domain (maximum learnability). This approach aims to find partitions that correspond more closely to the true generative factors of variation rather than arbitrary dataset boundaries.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Originality and Significance:** The problem of automatically defining domains has seen relatively little exploration, and this paper introduces a compelling approach to discovering useful latent structures that can improve subsequent adaptation performance. The core idea of defining domains based on distinctiveness and learnability is a valuable contribution.

However, there are weaknesses in the proposed formulation. The connection between the relaxed optimization (Eq. 4-5) and the original integer programming problem (Eq. 2-3) is not thoroughly justified, making it difficult to assess how closely the proposed solution approximates the intended objective. Furthermore, the two guiding principles are not fully integrated. Maximum learnability is used only as a post-processing step to select the number of domains, K, effectively serving as a model selection criterion. The domain partitioning itself is driven solely by the distinctiveness objective. A more powerful approach might integrate both criteria into a single, unified objective function.

Despite these limitations, which are perhaps expected in the early stages of exploring this problem, the paper presents an important step in a promising direction for the community. I recommend acceptance.

**Quality and Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, the presentation of the core concepts could be significantly enhanced by the inclusion of illustrative figures.

**Experiments:** The experimental evaluation demonstrates a clear advantage of using the domains discovered by the proposed method compared to both the baseline of using entire datasets as domains and the recent domain discovery method of Hoffman et al. [20].

The idea of including the test set in the domain discovery process ("conditional reshaping" in Sec. 4.3) is interesting. However, this section is not presented with sufficient clarity. The authors should explicitly state that this assumes a transductive or batch setting where all test data is available at once.

A significant weakness of the experimental evaluation lies in the choice of image representation. A standard bag-of-words descriptor with sparse SURF features and a small 800-word codebook is outdated and may not be representative of modern performance. The authors are strongly encouraged to strengthen their results by using more powerful representations, such as a spatial pyramid model with dense features (e.g., Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006) or a Fisher Vector encoding (Perronin et al., ECCV 2010). Additionally, the paper does not specify the distance metric used for the BoW histograms; Euclidean distance is a poor choice for histograms, and an intersection or chi-squared kernel would be more appropriate.

In summary, the paper presents novel ideas for an important problem and demonstrates promising results. The work could be substantially improved, particularly by employing a more modern image representation.
