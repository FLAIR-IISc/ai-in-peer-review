- Paper goal: automatically discover latent domains in training data and incorporate them into domain adaptation via a kernel-based discrepancy and data partitioning.  
- Formulation: integer program with per-example single-domain and per-domain label-distribution-matching constraints, relaxed to a continuous quadratic program; number of domains chosen by cross-validation.  
- Evaluation: tested on two datasets (static images and IXMAS multi-view actions) and reported outperforming prior method [19].  
- Positive: paper is clearly written and the approach appears novel; reported performance gains over [19] are meaningful.  
- Main concern 1 (motivation): unclear evidence for the claim that clustering by appearance creates per-category domains and unclear how the proposed formulation prevents category-based domain reshaping.  
- Main concern 2 (label prior constraint): necessity of the label-prior constraint is unclear — reviewer requests ablation without it and comparisons to simple baselines (k-means on appearance, unsupervised object discovery like Sivic et al. ICCV ’05, and random equal splits).  
- Clarification request: what does “different in distribution from each other” refer to precisely (distribution over what)?  
- Technical query: does β_{mk} already lie on a simplex as defined (line 166)?  
- Constraint question: are the constraints at line 182 different from those introduced earlier around line 153?  
- Theoretical request: justification or proof for the bound stated at line 215 is missing.  
- Implementation question: clarify details on the geodesic flow kernel use (own reimplementation or public code).  
- Empirical explanation: need intuition for why the proposed method outperforms baselines and examples of systematic failure modes of baselines that this method avoids.  
- Minor issues: typo in Eq. (1) (M'k => Mk') and suggested citation: Torralba & Efros, CVPR 2011 on dataset bias.  
- Final stance update: after rebuttal addressing motivation and label-prior constraint, reviewer now leans slightly toward acceptance.
