This paper proposes a convex framework for partitioning a dataset (or datasets) into K subsets, designed such that each subset exhibits a similar semantic class distribution while being as distinct as possible from the others. This distinction, termed maximum distinctiveness, is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences of subsets within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) induced by a chosen kernel. These subsets are referred to as latent domains. The identification of K – the optimal number of latent domains – is accomplished by maximizing average learnability (the ease with which a classifier can be learned) within each domain.

While enforcing similar class distributions within each domain prevents any single domain from being dominated by a single class, this could be a limiting assumption for latent domain discovery in certain applications. For example, consider using poses as latent domains for an animal classification task (e.g., horses and cows). The class distributions within each pose-based latent domain might not be similar; both horses and cows are commonly depicted in left-right standing poses, but horses are rarely shown in a sitting pose, whereas cows frequently are.

Although latent domain discovery is not novel, the idea of controlling class label distributions to improve latent domain identification within a (relaxed) convex framework *is* a new contribution. Similarly, identifying the number of latent domains by evaluating classification quality within each domain is a notable practice, ensuring sufficient samples per class to promote generalization and discrimination. 

Throughout the paper, the concepts of “dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain” are not always clearly differentiated and are potentially confusing. These concepts should be rigorously defined, preferably with illustrative examples. Section 4.2, in particular, requires clarification. As currently presented, “dataset” and “domain” appear to be used interchangeably, as Si is described as both a dataset and a source domain. The experimental setting and concept definitions in Section 4.2 should be clarified accordingly. Additionally, the term `maxk r(U_k,B)` is used in equation 7 without prior definition.

The experimental validation appears adequate, with results demonstrating reasonable improvements over the baselines. However, the rationale behind the selection of specific source and target datasets for evaluation is unclear. A leave-one-dataset-out adaptation strategy might offer a more comprehensive evaluation. The qualitative results are helpful in visualizing the algorithm’s behavior. 

The framing of the problem on line 74 as an unsupervised learning problem may be misleading, as the method utilizes semantic class labels. The discovery of latent domains through encouraging similar class distributions within each domain, formulated within a (relaxed) convex framework, represents a noteworthy technical contribution. However, several concepts within the paper and the experimental validation require further clarification.
