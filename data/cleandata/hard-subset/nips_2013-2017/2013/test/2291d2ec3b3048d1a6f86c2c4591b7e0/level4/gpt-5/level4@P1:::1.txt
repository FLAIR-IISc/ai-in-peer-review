Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles the problem of discovering latent domains within visual datasets to improve domain adaptation. The authors propose two principles for defining domains: maximum distinctiveness (domains should differ as much as possible in distribution) and maximum learnability (each domain should support a strong discriminative model). They operationalize distinctiveness using a kernel two-sample metric (difference of mean embeddings in an RKHS, akin to MMD), and pose domain assignment as an integer program with two constraints: each instance is assigned to exactly one domain, and each domain’s label distribution matches the overall label prior. They relax the problem to a continuous quadratic program and assign domains by taking the maximum βmk. The number of domains K is selected using domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV), maximizing a weighted average of within-domain accuracies. They also consider “conditional reshaping” of the test data to align discovered test domains with training domains. Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) show improvements over baselines and a prior latent-domain method [20], with adaptation via the geodesic flow kernel [4]. The paper is related to dataset bias work (e.g., Torralba and Efros), multi-source adaptation, and latent-domain discovery with parametric mixture models [20]; here the approach is nonparametric.

Quality
The formulation is sensible and uses a well-founded distributional distance. The relaxation is standard for this class of problems, and the DWCV heuristic for K is reasonable. Empirical results are promising on two tasks, including the conditional test reshaping scenario. That said, some methodological choices and claims need clearer justification and ablation:
- Evidence is needed that naive appearance clustering collapses into per-category clusters, and an explanation of how the proposed objective avoids this failure mode.
- The necessity and impact of the label prior constraint should be quantified.
- Additional baselines (k-means on appearance; random partition; unsupervised object discovery) should be included.

Clarity
Overall the paper is clearly written and well organized. A few places would benefit from clarification:
- Specify explicitly whether “maximally different in distribution” refers to Pk(x) or Pk(x, y). The current objective measures differences in Pk(x) via kernel mean embeddings, while Pk(x, y) is introduced but not directly used in the distance.
- Align the notation and constraints consistently between the integer and relaxed formulations, and clarify the role of βmk and its constraints.

Originality
To my knowledge, the combination of nonparametric distributional distinctiveness (via RKHS mean embeddings), a relaxed quadratic program with a label prior constraint, and DWCV for K is novel for latent domain discovery in vision. Prior work [20] uses parametric mixtures; this paper’s nonparametric approach is a meaningful alternative.

Significance
If broadly validated, automatic latent domain discovery could make adaptation more robust when “datasets ≠ domains,” a common situation in vision. The conditional reshaping of test data is also practically relevant for heterogeneous test conditions (e.g., arbitrary views in action recognition). The improvements over baselines and over [20] are encouraging, though stronger ablations would solidify the contribution.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated problem setup; practical algorithm with nonparametric distributional distance.
- Sensible relaxation and heuristic for selecting K via DWCV.
- Empirical gains on two tasks, including a useful conditional test reshaping scenario.
- Outperforms prior latent-domain approach [20] and improves over a dataset-bias baseline [19].

Cons / questions for rebuttal
(i) Motivation for avoiding naive appearance clustering (line 77):
- What empirical evidence shows that simple clustering yields per-category domains? Please provide quantitative results supporting this claim (e.g., cluster purity with respect to class labels).
- How does your objective prevent per-category clustering? Is the prevention due mainly to the label prior constraint, or does maximizing distinctiveness over P(x) suffice? A synthetic or controlled experiment would help.

(ii) Necessity of the label prior constraint (line 154):
- Why is this constraint needed? Please report performance without it.
- Include baselines: (a) k-means on appearance features; (b) unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al., ICCV’05) as a clustering step; (c) random equal-size partitions. This will contextualize the gains.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Clarify “maximally different in distribution”: explicitly state whether the distance is on P(x) only, although P(x, y) is introduced.
- Line 166: As defined, βmk form vectors βk that already live on the simplex; please make this explicit and reconcile with the constraints.
- Line 182: The constraints appear to differ from those at line 153. Please align the two presentations and explain the relaxation step carefully (including the bounds 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C).
- Line 215: Please justify the bound Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC), C} for N-fold CV.
- Line 289: Provide implementation details for the geodesic flow kernel [4]: reimplementation vs. public code; hyperparameters; any deviations from the original.
- Lines 313/340: Offer insight into why your domains work better: analyze failure cases of the baselines (e.g., when mixture modeling misfits or when naive clustering conflates pose with category), and highlight factors your method captures (pose, background, illumination).
- Eq. (1): Use Mk′ (not M′k) for consistency in the second domain’s sample size.
- Suggested citation: Torralba and Efros, “Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias,” CVPR 2011.

Post-rebuttal update
The rebuttal satisfactorily addressed my concerns about the paper’s motivation and the role of the label prior constraint. I now lean slightly toward acceptance.
