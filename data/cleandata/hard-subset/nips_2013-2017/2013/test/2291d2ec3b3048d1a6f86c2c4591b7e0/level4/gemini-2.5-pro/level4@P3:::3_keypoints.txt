- Problem: Datasets often contain mixtures of underlying distributions (lighting, pose, background), so treating an entire dataset as a single domain is a flawed simplification.  
- Proposal: Automatically partition one or more source datasets into K latent domains by optimizing for "maximum distinctiveness" and "maximum learnability."  
- Distinctiveness: Use kernel-based Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) in an RKHS as a non-parametric measure to maximize distributional differences between discovered domains.  
- Learnability / model selection: Choose K by selecting the partition that yields highest domain-wise cross-validation accuracy ("maximum learnability").  
- Optimization: Formulate domain discovery non-parametrically and relax the NP-hard integer program into a convex quadratic program for tractable optimization.  
- Empirical validation: Experiments on object and action recognition show discovered domains improve adaptation versus monolithic datasets and outperform Hoffman et al.  
- Relation to prior work: Extends Hoffman et al. (2012) by avoiding Gaussian mixture assumptions via a non-parametric approach and connects to Jegelka et al.'s discriminability objective while incorporating class labels.  
- Strength — problem formulation: Elegant, well-motivated framework grounded in maximizing distinctiveness and learnability for downstream adaptation.  
- Strength — technical soundness: Appropriate non-parametric kernel MMD measure and convex relaxation make the approach principled and computationally feasible.  
- Strength — practical model selection: Domain-wise cross-validation for K is a sensible, practical solution ensuring domains are trainable and coherent.  
- Strength — empirical evidence: Strong quantitative gains and qualitative insights (e.g., domains based on background/appearance) support the method's effectiveness.  
- Weakness — Label Prior Constraint (LPC): LPC assumption that class priors are independent of domain may be too restrictive and could prevent discovering semantically meaningful groupings (e.g., class-specific pose distributions).  
- Weakness — clarity/terminology: Inconsistent use of "dataset"/"domain"/"latent domain" and undefined notation (e.g., r(A→B)) reduce clarity, especially in experiments.  
- Weakness — experimental justification: Choice of source/target dataset combinations lacks full rationale; more systematic protocols (e.g., leave-one-dataset-out) would strengthen evaluation.  
- Weakness — "unsupervised" claim: Calling the task "unsupervised" is misleading because the method relies on semantic class labels (unsupervised for domain labels but supervised for classes).  
- Overall verdict: High-quality, valuable contribution with strengths outweighing weaknesses and likely to inspire further work.  
- Acceptance arguments: Addresses a fundamental problem, offers a novel non-parametric formulation, practical K selection, and demonstrates significant performance improvements.  
- Rejection arguments: LPC may limit generality and experimental/terminology clarity needs improvement.
