- Paper proposes discovering latent domains by partitioning training data via a kernel-based pairwise difference function, then using those domains for domain adaptation to improve test-time classification.
- Formulates domain discovery as an integer program with constraints: each point assigned to one domain and each domain’s label distribution matches the global label distribution; relaxed to a quadratic program and number of domains chosen by cross-validation.
- Evaluated on two datasets (static images [2] and IXMAS multi-view action [15]) and shows empirical improvements over prior domain adaptation method [19].
- Strength: clearly written and appears novel to the reviewer.
- Strength: empirical gains over [19] suggest practical impact.
- Main concern 1: unclear evidence that naive appearance clustering yields per-category domains and unclear how Section 2’s model prevents per-category alignment in practice.
- Main concern 1 request: make explicit how the formulation enforces discovery of domains not simply aligned with object categories.
- Main concern 2: unclear necessity and impact of the label-prior constraint that matches domain label distributions to the global distribution.
- Main concern 2 request: report performance without the constraint, include baselines from simple partitions (k-means, unsupervised object discovery), and random equal-sized partitions.
- Clarify what “maximally different in distribution” refers to (which distribution and how it is estimated).
- Clarify whether β_{mk} lies on a simplex and reconcile differing constraint statements between line 182 and line 153.
- Provide justification or proof sketch for the bound stated at line 215.
- Provide implementation details for the geodesic flow kernel use (reimplementation vs. public code, cite source, note deviations).
- Explain why the proposed method outperforms baselines and analyze systematic failures of those baselines, with qualitative/quantitative evidence.
- Correct notation (M'k to Mk') in Eq. (1) and consider citing Torralba & Efros “Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias” (CVPR 2011).
- Post-rebuttal: the authors’ rebuttal addressed motivation and label-prior concerns, leading the reviewer to now lean slightly toward acceptance.
