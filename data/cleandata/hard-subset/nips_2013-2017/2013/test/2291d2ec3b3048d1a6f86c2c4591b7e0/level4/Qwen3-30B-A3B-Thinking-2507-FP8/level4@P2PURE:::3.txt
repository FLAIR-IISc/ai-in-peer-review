

This paper introduces a convex optimization framework for partitioning datasets into K subsets (termed "latent domains") with two key properties: (1) each subset exhibits similar semantic class distributions, and (2) subsets are maximally distinct from one another. Distinctiveness is quantified by maximizing pairwise mean differences of subsets in the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) induced by a chosen kernel. The number of latent domains K is determined by maximizing the average learnability—i.e., the quality of classification within each latent domain.  

While constraining class distributions to be similar within each latent domain prevents cluster dominance by single classes, this assumption may limit applicability for certain tasks. For example, in an animal classification task where poses define latent domains (e.g., horses and cows), the class distribution *within* each pose domain may not be similar. Specifically, both horses and cows appear in left-right standing poses, but horses rarely appear in sitting poses while cows frequently do.  

Although latent domain discovery is not novel, the approach of controlling class label distributions within a (relaxed) convex framework for improved identification is new. Similarly, determining K by evaluating classification quality within each latent domain—which ensures sufficient samples per class for generalization and discrimination—is a notable contribution.  

Key conceptual ambiguities require clarification. The terms "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain" are inconsistently defined and conflated throughout the paper, particularly in Section 4.2. For instance, the experimental setup refers to "Si" as both "datasets" and "source domains," creating confusion. These terms must be rigorously defined with examples. Additionally, Section 4.2 lacks clarity on the experimental setup and terminology, and Equation 7 references an undefined term "maxk r(U_k,B)."  

The experimental validation is adequate, with results showing reasonable improvement over baselines. However, the rationale for selecting specific source/target datasets for evaluation is unclear; leave-one-dataset-out adaptation would provide a more robust assessment. Qualitative results effectively illustrate the algorithm's visual outcomes.  

At line 74, describing the latent domain discovery problem as unsupervised is misleading, as the method leverages semantic class labels. Despite this, the formulation of latent domain discovery—encouraging similar class distributions via a (relaxed) convex framework—represents a notable technical contribution. However, certain concepts and experimental validation require clarification to strengthen the paper.
