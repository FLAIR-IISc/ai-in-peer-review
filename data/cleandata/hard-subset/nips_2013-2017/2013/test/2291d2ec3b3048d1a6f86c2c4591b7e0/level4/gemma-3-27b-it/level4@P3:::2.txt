This paper presents a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision, addressing the common issue of distribution mismatches between training and testing data. Instead of treating entire datasets as single domains – a practice that often conflates underlying factors like viewpoint, illumination, and clutter – the authors propose an automated approach to partition data into latent domains. This partitioning is guided by two key principles: maximizing distinctiveness between domains in feature space, and ensuring each domain supports the training of a strong discriminative classifier for the original task. This approach aims to discover domains aligned with the underlying data-generating factors, rather than artificial dataset boundaries.

**Originality and Significance:**

The work tackles an important and often overlooked aspect of domain adaptation: defining what constitutes a domain in the first place. While domain adaptation research focuses heavily on *how* to adapt, this paper directly addresses the *what* – the composition of domains themselves. There is limited work on automated domain discovery, making this contribution valuable. The idea of learning domains directly from data, driven by both statistical distinctiveness and discriminative power, is promising and could significantly impact how domain adaptation problems are formulated. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel Approach:** The paper proposes a fresh perspective on domain adaptation by focusing on automatic domain discovery.
*   **Principled Formulation:** The two proposed criteria – maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability – provide a strong theoretical foundation for the approach.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** Experiments demonstrate a clear advantage of using the discovered domains compared to treating datasets as single domains and outperform a recent related work [20].
*   **Conditional Reshaping:** The extension to reshape test datasets based on learned training domains is a valuable and insightful idea.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Optimization Concerns:** The optimization method described in Section 2 is complex, and it’s not entirely clear how effectively it solves the original problem formulation (equations 2 and 3). The interplay between the two criteria feels somewhat decoupled; maximal learnability currently serves as a post-hoc validation step for the number of domains, rather than being integrated directly into the partitioning process. A more unified objective function incorporating both criteria could be beneficial.
*   **Limited Feature Representation:** The use of a simple bag-of-words representation with sparse SURF features (800 bins) feels dated. More modern feature descriptors (e.g., spatial pyramids of bag-of-words, dense SURF features, or Fisher Vectors) would likely improve performance and bring the image representation in line with current state-of-the-art methods. (See suggestions in original review: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010.) The similarity measure used for comparing image descriptors should also be specified (Euclidean distance is likely suboptimal for histograms).
*   **Clarity of Test Reshaping:** Section 4.3, describing the reshaping of test datasets, lacks clarity. The assumption of simultaneous access to all test data (an imputation setting) should be explicitly stated.
*   **Lack of Visualizations:** The paper would benefit significantly from visualizations illustrating the discovered domains. Showing example images from each domain would help to understand the types of factors the algorithm is identifying.

**Quality and Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. However, the lack of illustrative figures hinders understanding of the discovered domains. The mathematical notation is clear, but further clarification of the optimization process would be beneficial.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental, yet often neglected, problem in domain adaptation: domain definition.
*   The proposed approach is novel and theoretically sound.
*   Empirical results are promising, demonstrating an improvement over existing methods.
*   The paper’s ideas are likely to stimulate further research in this area.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The optimization method could be more clearly explained and potentially integrated to better balance the two core criteria.
*   The use of a relatively simple image representation limits the potential performance and makes comparisons to more recent work less compelling.
*   The description of the test reshaping process needs further clarification.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The authors have identified an important problem and proposed a novel and promising solution. While improvements could be made to the optimization process, feature representation, and clarity of explanation, the core ideas and empirical results warrant acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper, with a request for the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above in a revised version.
