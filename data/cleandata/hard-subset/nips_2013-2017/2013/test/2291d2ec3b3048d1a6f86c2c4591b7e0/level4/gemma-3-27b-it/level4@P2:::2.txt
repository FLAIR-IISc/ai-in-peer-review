This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation in computer vision, moving beyond the common practice of equating datasets with domains. The authors propose a novel technique to automatically partition image datasets into latent domains, guided by two key principles: maximizing distinctiveness between domains and ensuring each domain supports learning a strong discriminative classifier. This approach aims to uncover underlying factors (e.g., viewpoint, illumination) influencing image variations, rather than simply grouping images by dataset origin.

**Originality and Significance:** The work tackles an important and often overlooked aspect of domain adaptation – the definition of domains themselves. Existing literature largely focuses on *how* to adapt, while this paper explores *what* constitutes a domain in visual data. The proposed method represents a valuable step towards automatically discovering meaningful domains, potentially leading to improved classifier performance. The idea is well-motivated and addresses a limitation of current practices.

**Quality:** The paper is generally technically sound, presenting a clear formulation of the problem and a proposed solution. However, the optimization method described in Section 2 could benefit from further clarification regarding its approximation of the original problem formulation (equations 2 & 3). The integration of the ‘maximum distinctiveness’ and ‘maximum learnability’ criteria feels somewhat sequential; the latter primarily serves as a validation metric for the former rather than being directly incorporated into the partitioning process. A more unified objective function combining both criteria could potentially improve the results.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow. However, the inclusion of illustrative figures would significantly enhance understanding, particularly when explaining the domain discovery process and the characteristics of identified domains.

**Experiments:** The experimental results demonstrate a clear advantage of using the automatically discovered domains over treating datasets as domains and outperform the recent approach of Hoffman et al. [20]. The extension to include the test set in the reshaping process (Section 4.3) is an interesting direction, though its presentation could be improved by explicitly acknowledging the imputation setting (i.e., assuming complete access to test data). 

A significant weakness lies in the image representation used: a basic bag-of-words model with sparse SURF features and a limited codebook size (800). This representation is outdated and limits the potential of the proposed method. Modern techniques like spatial pyramids of bag-of-words (e.g., Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006), dense SURF features, or Fisher Vector representations (e.g., Perronin et al., ECCV 2010; Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005) would be more appropriate and could significantly improve performance. The paper also lacks clarity on the similarity measure used for comparing image descriptors – the use of Euclidean distance for histograms is questionable; a chi-squared or intersection kernel would be more suitable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and under-explored problem in domain adaptation.
*   Proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to automatically discover latent domains.
*   Demonstrates significant performance gains over existing methods.
*   Experiments are thorough and validate the effectiveness of the proposed technique.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The optimization method lacks sufficient detail and clarification.
*   The integration of the two core criteria (distinctiveness and learnability) could be improved.
*   The reliance on a simple and outdated image representation limits the potential of the approach.
*   The presentation of the test set reshaping process (Section 4.3) could be clearer.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the identified weaknesses, this paper presents valuable contributions to the field of domain adaptation. The proposed method offers a promising approach to automatically discovering meaningful domains, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. While improvements are possible, particularly concerning the image representation and the optimization details, I believe the paper represents a worthwhile step forward and should be **accepted**. I recommend the authors address the comments regarding the image representation and optimization method in a revision.
