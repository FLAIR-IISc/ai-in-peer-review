This paper proposes a method for automatically discovering latent domains within visual datasets. The core idea is that standard datasets may be heterogeneous mixtures of different data distributions (e.g., due to lighting, viewpoint), and explicitly identifying these sub-domains can improve domain adaptation performance. The authors formulate this discovery process as an optimization problem guided by two principles: "maximum distinctiveness," where domains should have maximally different feature distributions, and "maximum learnability," where each discovered domain must contain enough data to train a strong classifier.

To achieve this, the authors propose maximizing the total pairwise distribution difference between domains, measured using a kernel-based metric. This is formulated as an integer programming problem, which is then relaxed into a continuous quadratic program with linear constraints. A key constraint enforces that the class label distribution within each discovered domain should match the global prior. The optimal number of domains is determined automatically using a proposed "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) procedure. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on object and action recognition tasks, showing that adapting from these discovered domains improves performance compared to adapting from the original, monolithic datasets or domains found by a previous method [20].

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Formulation:** The problem of automatically discovering latent domains for the purpose of domain adaptation is important and relatively underexplored. The proposed formulation, based on the principles of maximizing distinctiveness and learnability, is novel and well-motivated.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough and demonstrate a clear benefit. The approach shows significant performance gains over adapting from the original datasets and outperforms the most relevant prior work [20] on discovering latent domains.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Rebuttal:**

My primary concerns, which lead me to lean towards rejection at this time, are related to the motivation and the necessity of a key constraint. I would be open to reconsidering my score if these points can be adequately addressed.

1.  **Motivation and Comparison to Clustering:** The paper argues (L77) that "simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains." This is a central motivation, but it lacks empirical support.
    *   Could the authors provide evidence for this claim? For instance, showing that standard k-means clustering on the feature space results in clusters that are highly correlated with class labels rather than underlying domain factors.
    *   How does the proposed formulation explicitly prevent this failure mode? It is not immediately obvious how maximizing distribution distinctiveness avoids collapsing into per-category domains. The label prior constraint (LPC) seems to be the primary mechanism for this, which leads to my second concern.

2.  **Justification for the Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The LPC (L154) requires the label distribution in each discovered domain to match the global prior. This seems like a very strong assumption and its necessity is not fully justified.
    *   What is the intuition behind this constraint? It seems plausible that different domains (e.g., different viewpoints) could have different class priors.
    *   What is the performance of the method without the LPC? An ablation study is crucial to understand its impact.
    *   Without a clear justification and ablation study, the LPC makes the comparison to standard clustering methods feel incomplete. The paper should include baselines that partition the data using k-means on the feature vectors or even a random partition, to establish a clear performance floor and highlight the contribution of the proposed objective function versus the constraints.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions:**

*   **Clarity on "Distribution":** The phrase "maximally different in distribution" is used frequently. Please clarify early in the paper that this refers to the distribution over features, P(x).
*   **Relaxation Details (L166, L182):**
    *   On L166, it is stated that `βmk` are relaxed to live on the simplex. Given the definition `βmk = zmk/Mk` and `∑m zmk = Mk`, doesn't `∑m βmk = 1` hold by definition? Please clarify the relaxation step.
    *   On L182, the constraints in the relaxed problem appear different from the original ones. Could you please provide a more detailed derivation of how the constraints in (5) are derived from those in (3)?
*   **Bound on K (L215):** The paper provides an upper bound for the number of domains to test: `Kmax ≤ min{M/(NC),C}`. Please provide a justification or proof for this bound.
*   **Reproducibility (L289):** For the geodesic flow kernel [4], was the implementation from the authors or a public implementation used? Please provide details to aid reproducibility.
*   **Qualitative Analysis (L313/340):** The quantitative results are strong, but the paper would benefit from more qualitative analysis. Could you provide examples of systematic failures of the baseline methods that your approach corrects? This would provide deeper insight into *why* the proposed method performs better.
*   **Typo:** In Eq. (1), `M'k` should be `Mk'`.
*   **Suggested Citation:** The work of Torralba and Efros, "Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias" (CVPR 2011), seems highly relevant to the paper's motivation and would be a valuable citation to include and discuss.
