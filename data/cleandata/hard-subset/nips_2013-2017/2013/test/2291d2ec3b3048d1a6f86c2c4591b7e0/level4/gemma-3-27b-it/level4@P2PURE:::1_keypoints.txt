- Proposes automatic discovery of latent domains via a kernel-based dissimilarity and domain partitioning to aid domain adaptation for classification.  
- Formulates partitioning as an integer program with constraints: each point in exactly one domain and per-domain class label distribution matching the overall dataset.  
- Relaxes the integer program to a continuous quadratic optimization with linear constraints; selects number of domains by cross-validation.  
- Evaluated on two datasets (static images [2] and IXMAS multi-view actions [15]) and outperforms the domain adaptation method in [19].  
- Strength: paper is well-written and presents a novel approach with empirical gains over [19].  
- Reviewer initially leans toward rejection but is open to changing decision based on rebuttal.  
- Concern 1: Motivation unclear—insufficient evidence that appearance-based clustering produces per-category domains and unclear how the model avoids category-based partitions.  
- Concern 2: Necessity of the label prior constraint is not justified; requests ablation without the constraint and comparisons to k-means, unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic ICCV ’05), and random equal partitions.  
- Requests clarification of the repeated phrase “maximally different in distribution” — define what “distribution” means here.  
- Notes β_mk (line 166) appears to already lie on a simplex.  
- Points out inconsistency: constraints on line 182 differ from those on line 153.  
- Requests justification or proof for the bound claimed on line 215.  
- Asks for details on geodesic flow kernel implementation or source code used (line 289).  
- Requests deeper analysis/examples explaining why the proposed method outperforms baselines and examples of baseline failures (lines 313/340).  
- Minor correction: Eq. (1) M’k should be Mk’.  
- Suggests adding Torralba & Efros (2011) “Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias” as a relevant citation.  
- Rebuttal effectively addressed concerns about motivation and the label prior constraint, causing the reviewer to slightly favor acceptance.
