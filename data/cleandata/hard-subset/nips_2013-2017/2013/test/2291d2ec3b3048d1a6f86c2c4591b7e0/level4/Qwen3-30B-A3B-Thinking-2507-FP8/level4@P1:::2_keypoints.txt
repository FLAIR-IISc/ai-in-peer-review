- Problem: Datasets are often wrongly treated as single domains; real distribution shifts arise from overlapping factors (viewpoint, illumination, background).  
- Proposal: Automatic discovery of latent domains by optimizing for maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability.  
- Two criteria: Maximize inter-domain distribution differences and ensure each domain supports strong discriminative classifiers.  
- Method: Nonparametric kernel-based domain discovery with convex relaxation to address NP-hard assignment.  
- Model selection: Domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) used to determine the optimal number of domains.  
- Evaluation: Tested on object (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and action (IXMAS) recognition, outperforming dataset-as-domain baselines and prior methods.  
- Originality: Novel focus on automatic latent-domain discovery; nonparametric formulation avoids Gaussian-mixture assumptions (contrast to Hoffman et al. 2012).  
- Quality/clarity: Technical framework is sound and well-motivated, but visualizations and some kernel/matrix details need clearer justification.  
- Strength: Strong empirical gains, especially with conditional reshaping for cross-view action recognition.  
- Strength: The distinctiveness+learnability criteria are well-justified and improve over simple clustering baselines.  
- Strength: Practical and scalable approach addressing a pervasive real-world problem in domain adaptation.  
- Weakness: Convex relaxation (Eq.4) lacks rigorous connection to original discrete assignment (Eq.3).  
- Weakness: Learnability is used post-hoc for domain count rather than jointly optimized with distinctiveness.  
- Weakness: Use of basic bag-of-words SURF representation (800 bins) is outdated; better feature representations and kernel choices should be used.  
- Recommendation: Accept — valuable, novel, and experimentally convincing despite some methodological and representation refinements needed.
