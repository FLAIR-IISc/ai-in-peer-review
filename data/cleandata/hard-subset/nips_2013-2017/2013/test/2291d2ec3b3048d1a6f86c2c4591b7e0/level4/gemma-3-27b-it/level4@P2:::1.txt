This work addresses the challenge of automatically discovering latent domains within image and video datasets, a crucial step for effective domain adaptation. The core idea is to partition data into domains maximizing both distinctiveness – ensuring significant distributional differences between domains – and learnability – guaranteeing strong discriminative models can be learned within each domain. This is formulated as an optimization problem, initially as an integer program with constraints enforcing single assignment per instance and preservation of label distributions, then relaxed to a continuous form solvable with standard optimization techniques. The optimal number of domains is determined via a domain-wise cross-validation procedure. The approach is evaluated on object recognition (using datasets from [2]) and human activity recognition (IXMAS dataset [15]), demonstrating performance improvements over a previous domain adaptation method [19].

The paper is clearly written and proposes a novel approach to a significant problem in domain adaptation. The reported performance gains are encouraging. The method’s focus on *discovering* domains, rather than relying on pre-defined datasets as proxies for domains, is a valuable contribution. 

However, I initially leaned towards rejection due to concerns regarding motivation and the necessity of certain components. After considering the rebuttal, I now lean slightly towards acceptance. My primary concerns revolved around the following:

**(i) Motivation and Per-Category Domain Avoidance:** The initial motivation for avoiding simple clustering (line 77) felt unsupported. The claim that clustering inherently leads to per-category domains requires stronger justification. Furthermore, the mechanism by which the proposed formulation prevents this outcome wasn't clear. The rebuttal effectively clarified this point by explaining how the *distinctiveness* objective actively discourages such a partitioning, favoring domains that capture variations *within* categories rather than simply separating categories themselves. This explanation significantly strengthened the paper’s rationale.

**(ii) Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The necessity of the label prior constraint (line 154) wasn't immediately obvious. I questioned its impact on performance and requested a comparison to simpler baselines. The rebuttal persuasively argued that the LPC acts as a regularizer, guiding the domain discovery process towards more meaningful and adaptable partitions, and provided empirical results showing a slight performance improvement with the constraint included.

**Additional Points & Clarifications (addressed by the rebuttal or now less critical):**

*   **Distinctiveness Definition (line 82):** The rebuttal clarified that “distribution” refers to the joint distribution of features and labels, providing a clearer understanding of the distinctiveness criterion.
*   **Simplex Variable Definition (line 166):** The rebuttal confirmed that the variables were already defined on a simplex.
*   **Constraint Discrepancy (line 182):** The rebuttal pointed out a typo in my notes, confirming the consistency of the constraints.
*   **Kmax Bound (line 215):** The justification for the Kmax bound, related to ensuring sufficient data per domain for meaningful cross-validation, was satisfactory.
*   **Geodesic Flow Kernel (line 289):** The rebuttal confirmed the use of publicly available code for the geodesic flow kernel, addressing concerns about reimplementation.
*   **Performance Insights (lines 313/340):** The rebuttal offered a more nuanced explanation of the performance differences, highlighting the approach's ability to capture subtle variations within datasets that simpler methods missed.
*   **Typo Correction:** The correction of "M'k" to "Mk'" in Eq. (1) is appreciated.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach to unsupervised domain discovery is innovative and addresses a key limitation of existing domain adaptation methods.
*   **Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over a relevant baseline [19].
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the concepts are explained clearly, particularly following the rebuttal clarifications.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical formulation and optimization approach appear solid.

**Arguments Against Acceptance (now significantly weaker):**

*   **Complexity:** The optimization formulation is relatively complex, potentially hindering reproducibility.
*   **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While a comparison to [19] is provided, additional baselines (e.g., k-means clustering, unsupervised feature discovery) would have further strengthened the evaluation. (The rebuttal addressed this concern to some extent).



**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The proposed method for automatically discovering latent domains is novel, well-motivated (following the rebuttal), and demonstrates promising results. While some minor points remain (such as the optimization complexity), the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses, and I now recommend acceptance.
