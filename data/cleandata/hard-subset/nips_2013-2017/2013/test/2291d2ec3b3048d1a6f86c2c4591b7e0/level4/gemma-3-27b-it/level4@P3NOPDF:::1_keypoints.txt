- Paper proposes automatic discovery of latent domains for domain adaptation by partitioning data to maximize inter-domain differences via a kernel, formulated as an integer program relaxed to a quadratic continuous optimization and validated via cross-validation for number of domains.  
- Evaluated on static image classification and multi-view action recognition, showing improvements over a prior domain adaptation method.  
- Strength: paper is well-written and the approach appears novel.  
- Strength: demonstrated performance gains over baseline [19] are encouraging.  
- Strength: problem formulation is mathematically sound and relaxation to continuous optimization is sensible.  
- Weakness: overall motivation is unclear; claim that appearance-based clustering yields per-category domains lacks supporting evidence.  
- Weakness: unclear how the model prevents per-category domain formation—needs clearer explanation and examples.  
- Weakness: necessity of the label prior constraint is not justified; request experiments without the constraint.  
- Requested baselines: compare to (a) k-means on appearance vectors, (b) unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al. ICCV ’05), and (c) random equal-sized partitioning.  
- Clarify meaning of “distribution” (features, labels, or other).  
- Clarify whether β_mk lies on a simplex as formulated.  
- Clarify relationship between constraint sets introduced at different places in the paper (lines 153 vs. 182).  
- Provide justification or proof for the stated bound (line 215).  
- Specify implementation details for the geodesic flow kernel [4] (reimplementation vs. public code).  
- Provide analysis explaining why the proposed method outperforms baselines and what systematic failures of baselines are avoided.  
- Note typo in Equation (1): M’_k should be M_k’.  
- Suggest adding Torralba & Efros (2011) “Unbiased look at dataset bias” to related work.  
- Overall assessment: interesting and potentially valuable but currently slightly negative due to unclear motivation and missing baseline comparisons; a strong rebuttal could change this evaluation.
