This paper proposes a method to automatically identify latent domains within a training dataset. These discovered domains are then utilized within a domain adaptation framework to enhance classification accuracy on a target test set. The authors introduce a function, based on a specified kernel, to measure the dissimilarity between feature vectors. The core objective is to partition the data into domains such that this dissimilarity function is maximized across all pairs of the resulting domains. This task is framed as an integer programming problem with two key constraints: first, each data point must be assigned to a single domain, and second, the class label distribution within each domain must match the global label distribution of the entire dataset. To make the problem tractable, it is relaxed into a continuous quadratic optimization with linear constraints. The optimal number of domains is determined via cross-validation.

The method is evaluated on the static image dataset from [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset from [15]. A significant result is the demonstrated performance improvement over the domain adaptation technique of [19].

**Positives:**
The manuscript is clearly written and the proposed technique appears to be novel, although I am not a specialist in domain adaptation. The superior performance compared to the work of [19] is a notable contribution.

**Negatives:**
My initial recommendation is a weak reject. My decision could be swayed if the authors address the following two major concerns in their rebuttal:

(i) The core motivation is not entirely convincing. The paper claims (line 77) that "simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains." First, what evidence supports this assertion? Second, it is not explicitly explained how the proposed formulation in Section 2 prevents this specific outcome. How does the model enforce the discovery of domains that are not simply class-based clusters?

(ii) Related to the first point, the necessity of the "label prior constraint" (line 154) is not well-justified. What is the impact on performance if this constraint is removed? The authors should include experiments with simpler baselines for domain discovery, such as partitioning the data using k-means clustering on appearance features or a random partitioning into equal-sized sets. A comparison to an unsupervised discovery method like Sivic et al. (ICCV '05) would also be informative.

The authors' rebuttal has satisfactorily addressed my main concerns regarding the motivation and the label prior constraint. I have updated my recommendation to weak accept.
