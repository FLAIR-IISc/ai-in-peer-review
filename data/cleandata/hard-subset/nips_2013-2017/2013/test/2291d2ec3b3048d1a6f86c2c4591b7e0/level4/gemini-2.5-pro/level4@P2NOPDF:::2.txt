This paper introduces a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision. The core idea is to move beyond the conventional approach of equating domains with datasets. Since datasets often conflate multiple underlying factors—such as viewpoint, illumination, clutter, and compression artifacts—the authors propose a method to automatically partition a collection of images from multiple sources into more meaningful domains. The partitioning is guided by two principles: 1) maximizing the separability of the resulting domains in the feature space, and 2) ensuring that a high-performing discriminative classifier (for the original object classes) can be trained within each discovered domain. This approach is designed to discover domains that correspond more closely to the true underlying generative factors in the data rather than to the arbitrary boundaries of datasets.

**Originality and Significance:**

The problem of automatically defining domains has received limited attention in the literature, and this paper makes a valuable contribution by proposing a principled approach to discover them. The idea of identifying domains that are not only distinct but also conducive to learning is a promising direction for improving domain adaptation and generalization.

However, there are some weaknesses in the proposed formulation. I am not fully convinced by the optimization method presented in Section 2, as its connection to the original objective function in equations (2) and (3) is not clearly established. It is difficult to assess how closely the proposed algorithm approximates a solution to the stated problem.

Furthermore, the two guiding principles—maximal separability and maximal learnability—are not well integrated. The learnability criterion is used only as a wrapper to select the number of domains, K. In essence, the partitioning for a given K is driven solely by the separability criterion. The learnability score then acts as a post-hoc validation metric to choose the best K. A more powerful, integrated approach would jointly optimize an objective function that directly incorporates both criteria, allowing the learnability goal to influence the partitioning process itself.

Despite these shortcomings, which are perhaps expected in the exploratory stages of a new research direction, the paper represents a conceptual step forward for the community and warrants acceptance.

**Quality and Clarity:**

The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow. However, its clarity could be significantly improved by the inclusion of figures. Visualizations illustrating the partitioning process or the characteristics of the discovered domains would help build intuition for the proposed method and its results.

**Experiments:**

The experimental evaluation provides compelling evidence for the proposed method. The results in Section 4.2 show that using the automatically discovered domains yields a significant performance advantage over both the baseline of using datasets as domains and the recent domain discovery technique of [19]. The idea of including the unlabeled test set in the domain discovery process (Section 4.3) is also interesting. However, this aspect is not presented clearly. The paper should explicitly state that this corresponds to a transductive or imputation setting, where all test data is available at once during training.

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its choice of image representation. The use of a single bag-of-words histogram based on sparse SURF features with an 800-word codebook is outdated and significantly limits the potential performance of the system. I strongly recommend that the authors adopt more robust, contemporary feature representations. Immediate improvements could be gained by using a spatial pyramid model with densely sampled features. The paper also fails to specify the similarity measure used to compare image descriptors; for histograms, the Euclidean distance is inappropriate, and alternatives like the Chi-squared or intersection kernel should be used. For future work, a Fisher Vector representation would further strengthen the experimental results and bring the image representation pipeline up to the standard of modern systems. The authors may find the following works helpful: Lazebnik et al. (CVPR 2006) for spatial pyramids, and Perronnin et al. (ECCV 2010) for Fisher Vectors.

**Overall Assessment:**

In summary, this paper presents novel and interesting ideas on the important problem of domain discovery. The proposed method is well-motivated and achieves strong empirical results despite its methodological limitations. The paper can be substantially improved, particularly by strengthening the image representation used in the experiments. Nevertheless, its conceptual contribution is significant, and I recommend acceptance.
