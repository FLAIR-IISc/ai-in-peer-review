Summary
This paper addresses a core limitation in visual domain adaptation: datasets are often used as proxies for domains, even though they mix multiple latent factors (e.g., viewpoint, illumination, background, resolution). The authors propose to automatically discover latent domains within and across datasets by enforcing two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. Distinctiveness is operationalized via a nonparametric kernel two-sample measure (mean embeddings in an RKHS; akin to MMD), optimized to partition data into distributions that are maximally different. Learnability is enforced via domain-wise cross-validation used to select the number of domains K, favoring partitions that support strong discriminative classifiers on the original labels. The resulting method can reshape both training and test sets; for test reshaping, a conditional variant aligns latent test partitions to discovered training domains. Experiments on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS) demonstrate improved transfer performance over treating datasets as domains and over Hoffman et al. [20], which uses parametric Gaussian mixture models for latent domain discovery. The approach is compatible with downstream multi-source adaptation (e.g., GFK) and yields strong empirical gains.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well positioned with respect to domain adaptation in vision and learning [2–4, 9, 10, 22, 23], nonparametric two-sample testing [21], and multi-source adaptation [28, 29]. Its most direct comparator is Hoffman et al. [20], which also discovers latent domains but assumes a parametric mixture model; the present work uses a nonparametric discrepancy measure and an optimization with label prior constraints, making fewer distributional assumptions. The work also connects conceptually to generalized clustering via kernel embeddings [27], but focuses on domain discovery for transfer rather than unsupervised clustering.

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear, principled formulation of “distinctiveness” using kernel mean embeddings and a sum over pairwise distances between empirical domain embeddings.
- Sensible practical mechanism for “learnability” via domain-wise cross-validation to choose K, with informative plots linking A(K) to target performance.
- Solid empirical evaluation on both object and action recognition, with ablation against dataset-as-domain baselines, K-means/random partitions, and the latent-domain method of [20].
- Practical extensions: conditional reshaping of test sets, and multi-source adaptation strategies (UNION, ENSEMBLE, MATCHING), where the discovered domains often approach oracle performance.

Weaknesses:
- Optimization gap: the discrete assignment problem maximizing total domain distinctiveness (TDD) with label prior constraints is relaxed to a nonconvex quadratic program. The paper relies on off-the-shelf solvers but does not characterize the approximation quality, sensitivity to initialization, or solution stability across runs. Empirical evidence of robustness (e.g., multiple restarts, variance bars) would strengthen the technical case.
- The two principles are only loosely coupled. Learnability influences K via an outer cross-validation loop, but not the partition itself. A unified objective trading off distinctiveness and within-domain classification performance would be conceptually cleaner and might yield better partitions.
- Assumptions and settings: conditional reshaping of test data presumes all test examples are available at once (a transductive/imputation setting). This should be stated explicitly, with discussion of feasibility in online or streaming scenarios.
- Methodological details need clarification: kernel choice for histogram descriptors (Gaussian on Euclidean distance is suboptimal for histograms); bandwidth selection sensitivity; impact of the label prior constraint δ; and constraints that bound per-domain sizes.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. The conceptual framing (why datasets ≠ domains) is convincing. However:
- The conditional reshaping procedure for test sets (Section 4.3) is underexplained in the main text; a brief, self-contained description would help readers assess assumptions and complexity without relying on supplemental material.
- A few illustrative figures (beyond the qualitative examples) depicting how partitions evolve with K, or t-SNE/RKHS-mean visualizations of discovered domains, would make the method more intuitive.

Originality
The paper offers a novel nonparametric approach to latent domain discovery guided by two principled criteria, and extends discovery to test-time reshaping with conditional alignment. While it builds on known tools (kernel mean embeddings, cross-validation), the overall formulation and its use for domain adaptation are original relative to prior work [20, 27].

Significance
Discovering meaningful latent domains has practical impact: it makes multi-source adaptation more effective and improves transfer accuracy. The empirical gains (often matching oracle selection) suggest utility for practitioners dealing with heterogeneous visual corpora. The approach is general and could extend to other modalities.

Experimental setup and suggestions
- Visual features: the representation (global BoW of sparse SURF with an 800-word codebook) is outdated for 2013 standards. Stronger baselines would likely amplify the method’s benefits and enhance credibility. Consider dense descriptors with spatial pyramids [Lazebnik CVPR 2006], χ2 or histogram intersection kernels for histograms, or Fisher Vectors [Perronnin ECCV 2010]. Earlier BoW work [Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie & Triggs CVPR 2005] provides guidance on robust pipelines.
- Report similarity measures explicitly; Euclidean distance on histograms is suboptimal.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for kernel bandwidth, δ in the LPC, number of restarts, and K selection bounds; ablate the LPC to show its effect on partitions and downstream accuracy.
- Discuss runtime and scalability with respect to M (kernel matrix costs) and potential approximations (e.g., Nyström, random features).

Pros
- Addresses an important and underexplored problem: discovering domains rather than assuming them.
- Principled nonparametric formulation with clear intuitions.
- Consistent empirical improvements over strong baselines and a recent competing method.
- Practical utility demonstrated with multi-source adaptation and test-set reshaping.

Cons
- Relaxation–original objective gap is not analyzed; potential instability is unquantified.
- Learnability is not integrated into the partitioning objective.
- Conditional reshaping assumes full test-set availability; needs explicit framing.
- Use of dated, weak visual features and potentially suboptimal kernels for histograms.

Recommendation
Overall, this is a thoughtful and promising contribution that advances domain adaptation beyond dataset-as-domain assumptions. Despite some methodological gaps and an outdated feature pipeline, the core ideas are sound and empirically validated. I recommend acceptance, with revisions to clarify the optimization and test-time assumptions and to strengthen the experimental setup with modern image representations and appropriate histogram kernels.
