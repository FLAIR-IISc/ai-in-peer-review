- Proposes a novel convex framework to split data into K "latent domains" with similar within-domain class distributions and maximal distinctiveness between domains.
- Achieves maximum distinctiveness by maximizing pairwise mean differences in an RKHS induced by a chosen kernel.
- Determines the number K of latent domains by maximizing average learnability (how well a classifier can be learned) within each latent domain.
- Novelty: controlling class label distributions in a relaxed convex formulation for latent-domain discovery.
- Novelty: identifying K via per-domain classification quality to ensure adequate samples per class for learning.
- Limitation: assumption that class distributions within each domain should be similar may not hold for all tasks (example: pose-based domains for animals).
- Example concern: some classes may be absent in certain poses, violating the similar-within-domain-distribution assumption.
- Clarity issue: dataset, domain, and latent domain are used interchangeably and need explicit definitions and examples (confusing usage in Sec. 4.2).
- Notation issue: some symbols/equations are unclear or undefined (e.g., maxk r(Uk,B) in Eq. 6).
- Experiments: show reasonable improvements over baselines and useful qualitative visualizations.
- Experimental design concern: choice of source/target datasets and evaluation protocol is not well justified; leave-one-dataset-out may be more appropriate.
- Conceptual framing issue: paper calls discovery "unsupervised" despite using semantic class labels—should be framed as supervised.
- Overall acceptance arguments: novel convex framework, useful control of class-label distributions, and empirical improvements.
- Overall rejection arguments: limiting assumption on class distributions, clarity and notation problems, unjustified dataset choices, and inaccurate problem statement.
