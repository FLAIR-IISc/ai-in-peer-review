### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a fundamental but often-overlooked problem in domain adaptation: the conventional practice of treating entire datasets as single, discrete domains. The authors argue that this is a flawed proxy, as datasets are often heterogeneous collections of multiple latent domains defined by complex factors like lighting, pose, and background. To address this, the paper proposes a novel, unsupervised method for automatically discovering these latent domains from one or more source datasets.

The core of the proposed approach rests on two principles that the discovered domains should satisfy: "maximum distinctiveness" and "maximum learnability." Maximum distinctiveness is achieved by partitioning the data such that the distributions of the resulting domains are maximally different from one another. This difference is measured non-parametrically using a kernel-based method in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). This objective is formulated as an integer programming problem, which is then relaxed into a solvable continuous quadratic program. A key component of this formulation is a "label prior constraint" (LPC), which ensures that the class distribution within each discovered domain mirrors the overall class distribution of the source data. Maximum learnability is used to automatically determine the optimal number of domains, K. The authors propose a "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) procedure where K is chosen to maximize the weighted average of classification accuracies on the discovered domains.

The method is evaluated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks. The experiments demonstrate that using classifiers adapted from these automatically discovered domains yields superior performance compared to adapting from the original, monolithic datasets. The approach also outperforms the most relevant prior work by Hoffman et al. [20].

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and important problem. The assumption that one dataset equals one domain is a major simplification in domain adaptation research, and this work provides a principled, data-driven approach to move beyond it. The proposed concepts of "maximum distinctiveness" and "maximum learnability" are intuitive and provide a solid foundation for the method.
*   **Technical Quality:** The technical formulation is sound. Using a non-parametric kernel method to measure distribution difference is well-motivated, as it avoids making strong parametric assumptions about the underlying domain distributions, which is a key limitation of related work [20]. The optimization procedure, while a relaxation, is a practical way to solve an otherwise NP-hard problem.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is well-articulated, the proposed method is explained systematically, and the experiments are described in sufficient detail.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The experiments convincingly show the value of reshaping datasets into latent domains. The performance gains over using the original datasets and the method of Hoffman et al. [20] are significant and validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**

My primary concerns relate to the motivation for certain modeling choices and the need for stronger baseline comparisons to fully appreciate the contribution. Addressing these points could significantly strengthen the paper.

1.  **Motivation Against Simpler Clustering:** The paper argues (line 77) that "simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains." This is a central claim motivating the complexity of the proposed method, but it is not substantiated with evidence.
    *   How does the "maximum distinctiveness" objective function inherently avoid this problem? It seems the Label Prior Constraint (LPC) is the primary mechanism preventing this collapse. Is this correct?
    *   To validate this claim, it would be highly beneficial to include baseline results from a standard clustering algorithm like k-means applied to the feature vectors. This would clearly demonstrate whether simpler methods fail as hypothesized and would highlight the necessity of the proposed formulation.

2.  **Justification of the Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The LPC (Section 2.1, constraint 2) is a critical component of the model, but its impact is not fully explored.
    *   What would the performance be without this constraint? An ablation study removing the LPC is essential to understand its contribution to the final result. Does the optimization produce trivial (e.g., category-based) domains without it?
    *   It would also be useful to compare against a baseline where the data is randomly partitioned into K sets of equal size (while still respecting the LPC). This would help isolate the benefits derived from the maximization of the TDD objective itself, separate from the benefits of simply partitioning the data.

3.  **Clarifications on Technical Details:**
    *   **Distribution Definition:** Throughout the paper, the term "maximally different in distribution" is used. Please clarify if this refers to the marginal distribution P(x) or the joint distribution P(x, y). The formulation appears to focus on P(x).
    *   **Relaxation Formulation:** In Section 2.1, the variables βmk are introduced as zmk/Mk and are then described as living on a simplex where they sum to 1 over *m*. This seems inconsistent, as summing zmk/Mk over *m* for a fixed *k* would equal 1 by definition (sum of zmk over m is Mk). Please clarify the definition of β and the corresponding constraints.
    *   **Constraint Discrepancy:** The constraints in Eq. (5) seem to differ from the conceptual constraints described starting on line 153. Could you please reconcile these descriptions?
    *   **Bound on K:** In Section 2.2, a practical bound for Kmax is given. Could you provide a brief justification for this specific bound?
    *   **Reproducibility:** For the geodesic flow kernel [4], was a publicly available implementation used, or was it re-implemented? Providing this detail is important for reproducibility.

4.  **Deeper Analysis of Results:**
    *   The results in Tables 1 and 2 show *that* the proposed method works better, but a deeper qualitative analysis of *why* would be more insightful. For instance, could you show some systematic failure cases of the baselines (e.g., UNION or [20]) that your approach successfully overcomes? Visualizing some examples of mis-clustered domains from baselines versus correct ones from your method would be very compelling.

**Minor Comments:**

*   There appears to be a typo in Equation (1): M'k should likely be Mk'.
*   The authors may consider citing "Unbiased Look at Dataset Bias" by Torralba and Efros (CVPR 2011), as it is highly relevant to the paper's motivation regarding dataset-level analysis.

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a novel and valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The idea of automatically discovering latent domains is compelling and well-executed. While the results are strong, the paper would be improved by providing stronger empirical justification for key modeling choices through ablation studies and comparisons with simpler baselines. I have several questions regarding the motivation and technical details that I would like to see addressed in the author rebuttal. Currently, I lean slightly towards rejection but would be willing to raise my score if these concerns are adequately addressed.
