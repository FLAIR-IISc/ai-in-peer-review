This paper proposes a convex framework for splitting a dataset (or multiple datasets) into K subsets, referred to as latent domains. The goal is to create subsets that each have similar class distributions, while simultaneously being as distinct from one another as possible. This "maximum distinctiveness" is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences between the subsets in an RKHS induced by a chosen kernel. The optimal number of latent domains, K, is determined by maximizing the "average learnability," defined as the classification performance within each discovered domain.

While the concept of latent domain discovery is not new, the paper's approach of controlling class label distributions within a (relaxed) convex framework to guide this discovery is a novel and valuable contribution. Furthermore, the method for selecting K by evaluating the classification performance within each discovered domain is a notable strength. This approach helps ensure that the resulting domains are not only distinct but also contain sufficient data per class to train a generalizable classifier.

However, the constraint that forces similar class distributions across all discovered domains may be a limiting assumption for certain tasks. While this prevents the discovery of trivial solutions (e.g., domains dominated by a single class), it might hinder the identification of more natural groupings. For instance, consider an animal classification task (e.g., horses vs. cows) where the latent domains correspond to different poses. While both horses and cows may be observed in a standing pose, horses are rarely photographed sitting down, whereas cows might be. In such a scenario, the distribution of poses (latent domains) is inherently different for each class. Forcing the class distributions within each pose-based domain to be similar could prevent the algorithm from discovering these meaningful, pose-related domains.

The paper could also be improved by clarifying several key concepts. The distinction between 'dataset,' 'domain,' and 'latent domain' is unclear in several places and could lead to confusion. These concepts should be defined precisely, ideally with supporting examples. Section 4.2, in particular, requires clarification, as it appears 'dataset' and 'domain' are used interchangeably (e.g., Si is referred to as both). Additionally, the term `max_k r(U_k, B)` is used in Equation 7 but appears to be undefined.

The experimental validation is adequate, with results showing a reasonable improvement over the baselines. The qualitative results are helpful for visualizing the algorithm's output. However, the justification for the specific choice of source and target datasets used in the experiments is not clear. A leave-one-dataset-out evaluation, for instance, might provide a more comprehensive assessment of the method's generalization capabilities.

Finally, on line 74, the authors describe latent domain discovery as an unsupervised problem. This may be misleading, as the proposed method explicitly uses class labels to guide the discovery process.

In summary, the discovery of latent domains via a (relaxed) convex framework that encourages similar class distributions is a notable technical contribution. However, the paper would be stronger if the authors addressed the limitations of this core assumption, clarified key terminology, and provided a clearer justification for the experimental design.
