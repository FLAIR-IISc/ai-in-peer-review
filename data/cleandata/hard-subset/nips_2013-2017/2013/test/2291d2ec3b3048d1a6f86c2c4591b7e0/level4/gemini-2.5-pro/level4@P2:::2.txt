### Review

**Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation in computer vision, where standard practice often equates entire datasets with distinct domains. The authors argue this is a flawed assumption, as visual datasets are typically heterogeneous mixtures of various latent factors like viewpoint, illumination, and background. The core contribution is a novel method to automatically discover and partition these latent domains from a collection of source datasets. The proposed approach is guided by two principles: 1) **Maximum Distinctiveness**, ensuring that the discovered domains have maximally different feature distributions, and 2) **Maximum Learnability**, ensuring that a strong discriminative classifier can be trained on the data within each discovered domain. The authors formulate this as an optimization problem, propose a non-parametric method to measure domain distinctiveness, and use a cross-validation procedure based on learnability to determine the optimal number of domains. Experiments on object and activity recognition tasks demonstrate that adapting from these discovered domains yields superior performance compared to adapting from the original, monolithic datasets.

**Strengths (Originality and Significance)**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its originality and the significance of the problem it tackles. The domain adaptation community has largely focused on developing algorithms that assume pre-defined source and target domains. This paper challenges that premise by addressing the more fundamental question of "what constitutes a domain?" in complex visual data.

1.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** To my knowledge, there is very little prior work on automatically discovering latent domains from visual data. This paper provides a principled formulation for this task, moving the field beyond the convenient but often inaccurate "dataset-as-domain" paradigm. This is a conceptually important and valuable contribution.

2.  **Intuitive Guiding Principles:** The proposed criteria of "maximum distinctiveness" and "maximum learnability" are well-motivated and provide an intuitive foundation for the partitioning process. The idea is not just to find clusters in the data, but to find partitions that are both statistically separable and useful for the downstream classification task.

3.  **Promising Empirical Results:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the value of the proposed approach. The method consistently outperforms baselines that use the original datasets as domains and also shows advantages over the recent related work of Hoffman et al. [20]. This provides strong evidence that reshaping datasets according to their latent structure is a promising direction for improving domain adaptation.

**Weaknesses (Quality and Clarity)**

While the core idea is strong, there are several weaknesses in the methodology and experimental validation that should be addressed.

1.  **Decoupled Optimization Criteria:** The two guiding principles, distinctiveness and learnability, are not well-integrated into a unified objective. The domain partitioning is driven solely by maximizing distinctiveness (TDD). The learnability criterion is only used as a post-hoc model selection step to choose the number of domains, K. A more compelling approach would be to formulate a single objective function that directly trades off between these two criteria, thereby allowing learnability to influence the partitioning itself, not just the number of partitions.

2.  **Optimization Relaxation:** The paper formulates the partitioning as an integer programming problem (Eq. 2 & 3) and then relaxes it into a continuous optimization problem (Eq. 4 & 5). While this is a common technique for dealing with NP-hard problems, the paper provides no analysis of the quality of this relaxation. It is unclear how closely the solution of the relaxed problem approximates the optimal solution to the original formulation.

3.  **Outdated Visual Features:** A significant weakness is the use of a simple bag-of-visual-words representation with 800 sparse SURF features. This feature representation is outdated and known to have limited descriptive power compared to modern alternatives. The conclusions drawn from the experiments would be far more convincing if validated with stronger, contemporary features. The authors are strongly encouraged to adopt more powerful representations like spatial pyramid matching (Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006), representations based on dense features (Jurie & Triggs, CVPR 2005), or Fisher Vectors (Perronin et al., ECCV 2010). Furthermore, the paper does not specify the distance metric used for comparing histograms (e.g., Euclidean, Chi-squared, intersection kernel), which is a crucial detail for reproducibility.

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:**
    *   The paper would benefit greatly from figures illustrating the core concepts, such as a diagram showing how a heterogeneous dataset is partitioned into more coherent latent domains.
    *   The description of "reshaping the test datasets" in Section 4.3 is difficult to follow. This procedure appears to operate in a transductive setting, where all test data must be available at once for partitioning. This assumption is not clearly stated or justified.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this paper introduces a novel and important idea for the domain adaptation community. It correctly identifies a key limitation of current practices and proposes an interesting, principled approach to address it. The main weaknesses lie in the separation of the two proposed criteria within the optimization framework and the use of outdated features in the experimental validation.

Despite these shortcomings, the conceptual contribution is significant and thought-provoking. The work opens up a new and valuable direction for research. Therefore, I recommend acceptance. The authors should, however, acknowledge the limitations of their current method and experimental setup in the final version and consider the suggestions for improvement, especially regarding the feature representation, as this would greatly strengthen their empirical claims.
