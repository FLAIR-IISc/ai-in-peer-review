This paper addresses the problem of discovering latent domains within visual datasets to improve domain adaptation performance. The core idea is to repartition one or more source datasets into K subsets, termed "latent domains," based on two principles: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. Maximum distinctiveness is achieved by maximizing the pairwise distributional differences between the discovered domains, measured by the mean embedding distance in a Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS). To prevent the method from simply clustering by class, the formulation includes a constraint that forces the class label distribution within each discovered domain to approximate the global class prior. The second principle, maximum learnability, is used to automatically determine the optimal number of domains, K. This is done by selecting the K that maximizes the average cross-validated classification accuracy on classifiers trained within each individual domain.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Formulation:** While the idea of discovering latent domains is not entirely new, the proposed formulation is original. The combination of maximizing inter-domain distinctiveness using a non-parametric kernel method while simultaneously enforcing a label prior constraint within a relaxed convex optimization framework is a significant technical contribution. This approach directly tackles the challenge of separating domains based on factors like lighting or pose, rather than just semantic class.

2.  **Principled Selection of K:** The method for automatically selecting the number of latent domains (K) is a notable and practical strength. By linking K to the "learnability" of the resulting domains (measured via domain-wise cross-validation), the authors provide a well-motivated procedure that avoids ad-hoc choices and ensures the discovered domains contain sufficient data for training effective classifiers.

3.  **Good Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that using the discovered latent domains as sources for adaptation consistently improves performance over using the original datasets. The gains are shown on both object recognition and action recognition tasks, and the method outperforms a relevant competing approach from prior work.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of Terminology:** The paper's clarity could be improved by providing precise definitions for the terms "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain." These terms are sometimes used interchangeably, particularly in the experimental setup (Section 4.2), where source datasets (e.g., S_j) are also referred to as source domains. A clear definition, perhaps with illustrative examples early in the paper, would help the reader better understand the problem setting and the method's inputs and outputs.

2.  **The Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The LPC is key to preventing the algorithm from degenerating into a simple class-based clustering. However, it is also a strong assumption that may be limiting in some real-world scenarios. For example, in an animal classification task, certain poses (latent domains) might be correlated with specific classes. We may observe both horses and cows in a standing pose, but cows might be far more likely to be pictured sitting or lying down than horses. In such a case, forcing the class distributions to be similar across all domains could hinder the discovery of meaningful, pose-based latent domains. It would be beneficial for the authors to discuss this limitation.

3.  **Clarity of Experimental Details:**
    *   The notation `r(U_k, B)` used in Equation 6 is not explicitly defined, though its meaning can be inferred from the context. A clear definition would be helpful.
    *   The rationale for the specific choices of source and target dataset combinations for the experiments is not fully explained. A more systematic evaluation, such as a leave-one-dataset-out protocol, could provide a more comprehensive and convincing validation of the method's robustness.

4.  **Framing as "Unsupervised":** The paper describes the problem of discovering latent domains as "unsupervised" (e.g., line 74). This could be misleading, as the proposed method explicitly uses class labels to enforce the Label Prior Constraint. The learning is unsupervised with respect to the domain labels, but supervised with respect to the semantic class labels. Clarifying this distinction would position the work more accurately.

**Summary:**

This paper presents a technically sound and novel method for automatically discovering latent domains in visual data. The formulation, which balances maximizing domain distinctiveness with a label distribution constraint, is elegant, and the procedure for selecting the number of domains is practical and well-justified. The main weaknesses relate to the clarity of some concepts and the potential limitations of the label prior assumption. The experimental results are positive, though the evaluation protocol could be more systematic. Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation that addresses the important and often overlooked question of what constitutes a "domain." I recommend acceptance, but I strongly encourage the authors to address the clarity issues raised above in the final version.
