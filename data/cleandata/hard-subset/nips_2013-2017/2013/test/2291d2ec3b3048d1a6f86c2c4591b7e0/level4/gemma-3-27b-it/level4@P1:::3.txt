This paper proposes a convex framework for partitioning datasets into K subsets, designed such that each subset exhibits similar semantic class distributions and is as distinct from the others as possible. This distinction, termed “maximum distinctiveness,” is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences of subsets in the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) induced by a chosen kernel. Each of these subsets is referred to as a “latent domain.” The number of latent domains, K, is identified by maximizing average “learnability” – how well a classifier can be learned – within each domain.

While enforcing a similar distribution of classes within each domain prevents clusters from being dominated by a single class, it may also be a limiting assumption for latent domain discovery in certain tasks. For instance, considering poses as latent domains for an animal classification task (e.g., horses and cows), the class distribution within each pose-based domain might not be similar. We observe both horses and cows in left/right standing poses, but horses are less frequently pictured in a sitting pose than cows. 

Although the discovery of latent domains is not entirely novel, the idea of controlling class label distributions to improve latent domain identification within a (relaxed) convex framework *is* new. Identifying the number of latent domains by evaluating classification quality within each domain is also a notable practice, ensuring each domain has sufficient samples per class to generalize and learn discriminative features.

In several places, the concepts of “dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain” are not clearly differentiated and can be easily confused. These concepts should be precisely defined, ideally with illustrative examples. Specifically, Section 4.2 needs clarification; the terms “dataset” and “domain” appear to be used interchangeably (Si is described as both), and the experimental setting and concept definitions should be made explicit. Additionally,  max<sub>k</sub> r(U<sub>k</sub>,B) is used in equation 6 without prior definition.

The experimental validation appears adequate, with results demonstrating reasonable improvements over the baselines. However, the rationale for selecting the specific source and target datasets for reporting is not fully explained. For instance, a leave-one-dataset-out adaptation scheme might provide a more comprehensive evaluation. The qualitative results are helpful for visually understanding what the algorithm achieves.

On line 74, describing the problem as an unsupervised learning problem may be misleading, as the methods utilize semantic class labels. The discovery of latent domains via encouraging similar class distributions within each domain, formulated within a (relaxed) convex framework, represents a notable technical contribution. However, certain concepts within the paper and the experimental validation require clarification.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to latent domain discovery with a principled convex formulation.
*   The integration of class distribution control during domain identification is a valuable contribution.
*   The DWCV method for determining the number of domains is a practical and well-motivated approach.
*   Experimental results demonstrate improved performance over baselines.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The assumption of similar class distributions within each domain might be restrictive.
*   Lack of clarity in terminology and experimental setup.
*   Justification for dataset selection in experiments is weak.
*   The claim of being “unsupervised” is misleading given the use of class labels.
