

This paper introduces a novel approach to domain adaptation in computer vision by automatically partitioning images across multiple datasets into meaningful domains based on underlying aspects (e.g., viewpoint, illumination, clutter, compression), rather than treating entire datasets as single domains. The core innovation lies in a partitioning algorithm driven by two complementary principles: (1) maximizing separability of domains in feature space, and (2) ensuring each domain supports high discriminative accuracy for the original classification task. This strategy aims to align domains with intrinsic visual factors rather than arbitrary dataset boundaries, potentially enabling more robust classifiers.

**Originality and Significance**  
The work addresses a significant gap in domain adaptation literature: the lack of methods for *automatically discovering* useful domains. By framing domain discovery as an optimization problem centered on both structural separability and downstream task performance, the paper offers a promising step toward more principled adaptation. The approach is novel in its dual-criteria design, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over dataset-based domains and a recent domain discovery method [19], validating its practical utility.

**Technical Critique**  
The primary concern lies in the optimization methodology (Section 2). The connection between the proposed algorithm and the formal problem (equations 2–3) remains unclear. Specifically, the maximal learnability criterion (Section 2.2) functions solely as a post-hoc validation metric to select the optimal number of domains, rather than being integrated into the partitioning objective. An integrated formulation—where both separability and learnability jointly guide the partitioning—would strengthen the theoretical foundation. As currently implemented, the partitioning is driven *only* by separability, with learnability acting as a separate validation step. This disconnect weakens the method’s conceptual coherence, though the authors acknowledge the exploratory nature of the work.

**Quality and Clarity**  
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, but would benefit from visualizations. Key concepts—such as the domain partitioning process and the distribution of aspects across domains—would be significantly clarified with illustrative figures (e.g., t-SNE plots of domain clusters, aspect distributions). The absence of such figures reduces accessibility for readers unfamiliar with the technical details.

**Experiments**  
The experimental results are compelling. The proposed domains consistently outperform dataset-based domains and [19] across multiple benchmarks (Section 4.2), demonstrating the value of aspect-aware partitioning. The inclusion of test data in the "reshaping" process (Section 4.3) is an interesting idea but requires clearer exposition. Crucially, the method assumes all test data is available simultaneously (an *imputation setting*), a point that should be explicitly stated to avoid misinterpretation in sequential or streaming scenarios.

**Critical Limitation: Image Representation**  
A major weakness is the use of an outdated image descriptor: a single bag-of-words (BoW) representation based on sparse SURF features with a 800-entry codebook. This is suboptimal by contemporary standards. Modern approaches (e.g., spatial pyramid BoW on dense SURF features [Jurie et al., CVPR 2005; Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006]) or Fisher Vectors [Perronin et al., ECCV 2010] would substantially improve representational power. The paper also fails to specify the descriptor similarity measure; Euclidean distance is inappropriate for histogram-based features, and X² or intersection kernels should be used instead. The authors should adopt these standard practices to elevate their experimental baseline. The recommended improvements (spatial pyramid, Fisher vectors, appropriate kernels) are well-established in the literature and would place the work on par with state-of-the-art vision systems.

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper presents a valuable contribution to domain adaptation by introducing a conceptually fresh approach to domain discovery. While the optimization framework requires refinement and the image representation is underdeveloped, the core idea is sound and empirically validated. The authors’ focus on aligning domains with intrinsic visual aspects—rather than dataset boundaries—addresses a critical limitation in current practice. With the suggested improvements (especially modernizing the descriptor and clarifying the test-set assumption), the work could become a significant reference in the field. Given the exploratory nature of the problem and the clear empirical gains, the paper is a meaningful step forward. It should be accepted, with the understanding that future work will refine the method’s technical execution.
