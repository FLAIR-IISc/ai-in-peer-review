## Review of NIPS 2013 Submission

This paper presents a novel approach to domain adaptation by automatically discovering latent domains within a training dataset. The core idea is to partition the data into domains such that feature vectors within each domain are maximally different from those in other domains, as measured by a specified kernel function. This is formulated as an integer program, relaxed to a continuous optimization problem with quadratic cost and linear constraints, and solved using cross-validation to determine the optimal number of domains. The method is evaluated on static image classification and a multi-view action recognition dataset, demonstrating performance improvements over a prior domain adaptation approach.

**Strengths:**

The paper is well-written and the proposed approach appears novel, though I admit I am not a domain adaptation expert. The demonstrated performance gains over the baseline method [19] are encouraging. The problem formulation is mathematically sound, and the relaxation to a continuous optimization problem is a sensible approach to handling the inherent intractability of the original integer program.

**Weaknesses & Questions:**

Currently, I lean towards rejection, but would be open to changing my assessment based on a strong rebuttal addressing the following concerns.

(i) **Motivation:** The motivation for this work is not entirely clear. The authors argue (line 77) that simple clustering based on appearance is susceptible to creating domains based on individual categories. However, this claim lacks supporting evidence. Furthermore, it is unclear how the model formulation in Section 2 explicitly prevents this per-category domain formation. A more detailed explanation, potentially with examples, would be beneficial.

(ii) **Label Prior Constraint:** The necessity of the label prior constraint (line 154) is not sufficiently justified. I am curious about the performance of the method *without* this constraint. To better understand its impact, it would be helpful to compare against several baselines: (a) partitioning the data using k-means clustering on the appearance vectors, (b) using an unsupervised object discovery method (e.g., Sivic et al. ICCV '05) on the appearance vectors, and (c) randomly partitioning the dataset into equal-sized subsets. These comparisons will help demonstrate the value of the proposed approach and the specific contribution of the label prior.

**Further Comments & Clarifications:**

*   **Line 82:** The phrase “maximally different in distribution from each other” is used repeatedly. Please clarify what is meant by “distribution” in this context. Is it the distribution of features, class labels, or something else?
*   **Line 166:** It seems that β<sub>mk</sub>, as currently formulated, already represents a value on a simplex. Could the authors confirm this?
*   **Line 182:** The constraints presented here appear to differ from those initially introduced on line 153. Please clarify the relationship between these constraint sets.
*   **Line 215:** Please provide justification or a proof for the stated bound.
*   **Line 289:** More details are needed regarding the geodesic flow kernel [4]. Was this a reimplementation, or was publicly available source code used?
*   **Lines 313/340:** While the paper shows performance gains, further insights are needed into *why* the proposed approach outperforms the baselines. A discussion of systematic failures of the baselines that the proposed method avoids would be particularly helpful in understanding the strengths of this approach.
*   **Equation (1):** There appears to be a typo; M’<sub>k</sub> should be M<sub>k’</sub>.

**Relevant Literature:**

The authors might consider including the following work in their related work section:

Torralba, A., & Efros, A. A. (2011). Unbiased look at dataset bias. *Proceedings of the IEEE Conference on Computer Vision and Pattern Recognition (CVPR)*.

**Overall:**

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. However, the lack of clarity regarding the motivation and the necessity of the label prior constraint, coupled with the absence of comparisons to relevant baselines, currently leads me to a slightly negative assessment. A strong rebuttal addressing these points could significantly improve my evaluation.
