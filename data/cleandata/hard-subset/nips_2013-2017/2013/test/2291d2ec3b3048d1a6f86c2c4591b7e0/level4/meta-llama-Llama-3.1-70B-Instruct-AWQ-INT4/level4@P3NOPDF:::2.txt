This paper presents a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision datasets, which typically exhibit multiple aspects such as viewpoint, illumination, level of clutter, compression, and more. Unlike existing methods that equate domains with datasets, thereby mixing these aspects together, the proposed technique automatically partitions the set of all images across all datasets into domains. The partitioning process is driven by two principles: creating separate domains in feature space and ensuring that a good discriminative classifier can be trained on each domain to classify the original classes, rather than separating the domains.

The originality and significance of this work lie in its contribution to the limited body of research on automatically defining domains. The paper proposes a promising approach to discovering useful domains, which can support the training of better classifiers for the original problem. However, there are some concerns regarding the proposed optimization method (Section 2), as it is unclear how closely it solves the original problem (2)+(3). Furthermore, the two driving criteria – maximal learnability and maximal separability – are not well-integrated, with the former being used as a 'wrapper' around the latter to determine the number of domains. This results in the maximal learnability criterion acting as a post-hoc validation score, rather than being directly incorporated into the partitioning process. A more integrated approach would produce a partitioning that maximizes a goal function including both criteria.

Despite these shortcomings, which may be attributed to the exploratory nature of this type of research, the paper is a step in the right direction for the community and warrants acceptance.

The paper is well-written, but lacks figures to illustrate the concepts presented, which would enhance the clarity and quality of the work.

The experiments demonstrate a significant advantage in using the domains produced by the proposed method over simply using datasets as domains and over the recent domain discovery technique [19] (Section 4.2). The idea of including the test set in the 'reshaping' process is intriguing, but its presentation is unclear (Section 4.3). Additionally, this corresponds to an imputation setting, where all test data is available simultaneously, but this is not explicitly stated.

However, the image descriptor used in the paper is overly simplistic and outdated, relying on a single bag-of-words of sparse SURF features for the entire image, with only 800 codebook entries. This is a weak representation by today's standards. It is recommended that the authors adopt a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words, computed on dense SURF features. Furthermore, it is unclear what similarity measure is used to compare image descriptors, and it is hoped that the authors employed X^2 or an intersection kernel, rather than the unsuitable Euclidean distance. As a next step, a Fisher Vector representation could further enhance the image representation and bring it in line with modern systems. The authors may find the following papers helpful: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010.

Overall, the paper presents interesting and novel ideas on an important problem and achieves good results. While there is room for improvement, particularly in terms of the image representation used, the work is a valuable contribution to the field.
