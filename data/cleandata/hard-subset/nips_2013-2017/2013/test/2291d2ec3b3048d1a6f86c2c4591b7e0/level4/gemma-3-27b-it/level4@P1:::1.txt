The goal of this work is to automatically discover latent domains within a training set, which are then leveraged in a domain adaptation framework to improve classification performance on a test set. The paper defines a kernel-based function to measure the difference between distributions of feature vectors. The objective is to partition the data into domains such that this dissimilarity measure is maximized across pairs of domains. The problem is formulated as an integer programming problem subject to two constraints: each data point belongs to exactly one domain, and the distribution of class labels within each domain matches the overall input distribution. This problem is then relaxed into a continuous optimization problem with a quadratic cost function and linear constraints. Finally, the optimal number of domains is determined using cross-validation.

The approach is evaluated on two datasets: static images from [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset [15]. A key finding is that the proposed method achieves improved performance compared to the domain adaptation approach of [19].

**Strengths:** The paper is well-written and, to the best of my knowledge, presents a novel approach within the field of domain adaptation. The reported performance gains over [19] are encouraging.

**Weaknesses:** I currently lean towards rejection, but am open to being convinced otherwise by a strong rebuttal. My primary concerns are outlined below:

(i) The motivation for this work is not entirely clear. On line 77, the paper states that “simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains.” I would like to see evidence supporting this claim. Furthermore, I would like a clearer explanation of how the model formulation in Section 2 avoids this issue, specifically how it prevents reshaping into per-category domains.

(ii) Related to the above, on line 154, the justification for the "label prior constraint" is unclear. I am curious about the performance impact of removing this constraint. Establishing a baseline comparison against simpler unsupervised partitioning methods—such as k-means clustering or unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al. ICCV '05) applied to the appearance vectors—would be valuable. Additionally, reporting the performance achieved with a completely random partitioning of the dataset would provide a useful reference point.
