This paper proposes a novel method for domain adaptation in computer vision. The core idea is to move beyond the standard practice of equating domains with datasets, which often conflates multiple underlying factors such as viewpoint, illumination, and clutter. Instead, the authors propose a technique to automatically partition the entire collection of images from all available datasets into a set of new, more meaningful domains. This partitioning is guided by two principles: 1) domains should be maximally separable in the feature space, and 2) it should be possible to train a high-performing discriminative classifier for the original task on each resulting domain. The authors argue that this approach is more likely to discover domains corresponding to the true underlying data-generating factors.

**Originality and Significance**

The problem of automatically discovering domains is underexplored, and this paper makes a valuable contribution in this direction. The proposed idea of learning domain partitions that are optimized for a downstream classification task is novel and significant.

On the negative side, I am not fully convinced of the proposed optimization method (Section 2). The connection between the optimization method and the original objective function in equations (2) and (3) is not sufficiently justified, leaving it unclear how closely the proposed algorithm approximates a solution to the stated problem. Second, the two core criteria—maximal separability and maximal learnability—are not well integrated. The learnability criterion is used only as a wrapper to select the number of domains, essentially functioning as a post-hoc validation metric. The partitioning itself is driven solely by the separability criterion. A more principled, integrated approach would directly optimize a single objective function that combines both criteria.

Despite these limitations, which may be characteristic of work in such an exploratory area, the paper represents an important step in a promising direction for the domain adaptation community. I therefore recommend acceptance.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, its clarity could be significantly improved by the inclusion of figures to illustrate the core concepts, such as the domain partitioning process.

**Experiments**

The experimental results are promising. The paper demonstrates a significant performance advantage for the proposed method when compared against baselines that use the original datasets as domains or employ the recent domain discovery technique of [19].

The idea of including the test set in the "reshaping" process (Section 4.3) is interesting, but this part of the methodology is not presented clearly. Furthermore, this setup implies a transductive or imputation setting where all test data is available at once, a crucial assumption that is not explicitly stated or discussed.

A major weakness of the experimental evaluation lies in the choice of image features. The paper relies on a single bag-of-words histogram of sparse SURF features with a small 800-word codebook. This representation is outdated and significantly weaker than contemporary methods. The results could be made much more convincing by using stronger, standard features. I strongly recommend that the authors at least adopt a spatial pyramid representation with dense local features. Furthermore, the paper does not specify the similarity measure used to compare these histograms; for histogram-based representations, metrics like the Chi-squared or intersection kernel are more appropriate than the Euclidean distance. To truly align the work with the state of the art, adopting a Fisher Vector representation would be a valuable next step. The authors may find the following references helpful for improving their feature representation: Zhang et al., IJCV 2006; Jurie & Triggs, CVPR 2005; Lazebnik et al., CVPR 2006; Perronnin et al., ECCV 2010.

**Overall**

In summary, this paper introduces novel and interesting ideas for the important problem of domain adaptation and demonstrates promising results. While the work has some methodological weaknesses and could be substantially strengthened by using a more modern image representation, its conceptual contribution is valuable.
