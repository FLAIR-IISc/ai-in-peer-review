This paper presents a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision, addressing the common issue of distribution mismatches between training and testing data. The approach moves beyond equating datasets with domains, which often conflates underlying factors like viewpoint, illumination, and clutter. Instead, it automatically partitions image datasets into latent domains guided by two principles: maximizing distinctiveness between domains and ensuring each domain supports the learning of a strong discriminative classifier. This aims to identify domains aligned with the underlying data-generating factors rather than arbitrary dataset boundaries.

**Originality and Significance:**
The work addresses an important and relatively unexplored area – the automatic discovery of meaningful domains within datasets for improved domain adaptation. Existing domain adaptation research largely focuses on *how* to adapt, while this paper tackles the fundamental question of *what* constitutes a domain in visual recognition. The proposed approach represents a valuable step towards more robust and effective domain adaptation strategies. 

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**
The core idea of leveraging both distinctiveness and learnability to define latent domains is compelling. The experimental results (Section 4.2) demonstrate a clear advantage over using original datasets as domains and outperform the recent method proposed by Hoffman et al. [20]. The concept of incorporating the test set into the domain reshaping process (Section 4.3) is also a promising direction.

However, the proposed optimization method (Section 2) could be further clarified. It’s not entirely clear how closely the relaxed continuous optimization problem approximates the original integer programming formulation. Furthermore, the two driving criteria—distinctiveness and learnability—appear somewhat decoupled. Currently, learnability is primarily used as a validation metric to determine the number of domains, rather than being integrated directly into the partitioning process. A more unified objective function incorporating both criteria could potentially yield better results.

**Quality and Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. However, the inclusion of illustrative figures visualizing the discovered domains would significantly enhance understanding.

**Experiments:**
The experimental evaluation provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The improvements over baseline approaches are noteworthy. The conditional reshaping of test datasets is an interesting extension, although its presentation could be more explicit, particularly regarding the imputation setting (assuming all test data is available simultaneously). 

A key area for improvement lies in the image representation. The reliance on a simple bag-of-words representation with sparse SURF features and a limited codebook size (800) feels outdated. Employing more modern techniques, such as spatial pyramids of bag-of-words with dense SURF features or Fisher Vectors, would likely improve performance and bring the image representation in line with current state-of-the-art methods.  Relevant references include Zhang IJCV 2006, Jurie CVPR 2005, Lazebnik CVPR 2006, and Perronin ECCV 2010.  Additionally, clarifying the similarity measure used for comparing image descriptors (e.g., Chi-squared or intersection kernel instead of Euclidean distance) would be beneficial.

**Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**
*   Novel and important research question addressing a fundamental challenge in domain adaptation.
*   Compelling core idea of leveraging distinctiveness and learnability for domain discovery.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   Exploration of a promising extension to include the test set in the reshaping process.

**Con:**
*   Optimization method could benefit from further clarification and potentially tighter integration of the two driving criteria.
*   Image representation is relatively simple and could be significantly improved with modern techniques.
*   Lack of illustrative figures hinders understanding of the discovered domains.



**Overall:**
Despite some areas for improvement, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The proposed approach offers a novel and promising direction for automatically discovering meaningful domains within datasets, ultimately leading to improved generalization performance. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the points raised regarding the image representation and potentially explore a more integrated optimization strategy.
