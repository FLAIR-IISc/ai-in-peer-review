This paper presents a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision datasets, which typically exhibit multiple aspects such as viewpoint, illumination, level of clutter, compression, etc. Instead of simply equating domains with datasets, which can lead to mixing these aspects together, the proposed technique automatically partitions the set of all images across all datasets into domains. The partitioning is driven by two principles: making separate domains in feature space, and making them so that a good discriminative classifier can be trained on each of them (to classify the original classes, not to separate the domains). This technique is more likely to partition according to the underlying aspects rather than datasets.

Originality and Significance:
The paper proposes a valuable idea towards automatically discovering useful domains, which can support training better classifiers for the original problem. There is limited work on automatically defining domains, and this contribution is a step in the right direction. The approach has the potential to improve the performance of domain adaptation algorithms by identifying more meaningful domains.

However, there are some concerns regarding the proposed optimization method (section 2). It is not entirely clear how closely it solves the original problem (2)+(3). Moreover, the two proposed driving criteria (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) are not well-integrated yet. The maximal learnability criterion is only used as a 'wrapper around' the maximal separability criterion to determine the number of domains. Essentially, it acts as a post-hoc validation score deciding how good the domain partitioning learned for a given number of domains is, but the partitioning itself is made based on the separability criterion alone. An integrated process would instead produce the partitioning that maximizes some goal function including both criteria directly.

Quality and Clarity:
The paper is well-written, but lacks figures to illustrate the concepts presented. Adding visual aids would significantly enhance the clarity and readability of the paper.

Experiments:
The experiments demonstrate a significant advantage in using the domains produced by the proposed method over just using datasets as domains and over the very recent domain discovery technique [19] (sec. 4.2). However, there are some concerns regarding the experimental setup. The image descriptor used is very simple and outdated: just one bag-of-words of sparse SURF features for the entire image, and with just 800 codebook entries. This is not competitive with current state-of-the-art methods. It is recommended that the authors use a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words, computed on dense SURF features. Additionally, it is not clear what similarity measure is used to compare image descriptors; hopefully, it is X^2 or an intersection kernel, rather than the Euclidean distance, which is not suitable for comparing histograms.

The idea of including the test set in the 'reshaping' process is interesting but not clearly presented (sec. 4.3). This corresponds to an imputation setting, where all test data is available at the same time, but this is not stated clearly.

Overall, the paper presents interesting novel ideas on an important problem and achieves good results. The paper can be improved in several ways, especially in terms of the image representation used. With some revisions to address the mentioned concerns, the paper has the potential to make a more significant impact in the field.

Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:

Pro:
- The paper proposes a novel and valuable idea towards automatically discovering useful domains for domain adaptation.
- The approach has the potential to improve the performance of domain adaptation algorithms by identifying more meaningful domains.
- The experiments demonstrate a significant advantage in using the domains produced by the proposed method over just using datasets as domains and over the very recent domain discovery technique [19].

Con:
- The proposed optimization method is not entirely clear, and it is not obvious how closely it solves the original problem.
- The two proposed driving criteria are not well-integrated yet, which may limit the effectiveness of the approach.
- The image descriptor used is very simple and outdated, which may not be competitive with current state-of-the-art methods.
- The presentation of the idea of including the test set in the 'reshaping' process is not clear, and it is not stated whether this corresponds to an imputation setting.

Recommendation:
The paper is a step in the right direction, but it requires some revisions to address the mentioned concerns. With improvements to the optimization method, image representation, and presentation of the test set 'reshaping' process, the paper has the potential to make a more significant impact in the field.
