- Paper tackles discovering latent domains in visual datasets to improve domain adaptation by repartitioning sources into K subsets (latent domains).
- Core objectives: maximize inter-domain distinctiveness (via RKHS mean embedding distance) and maximize learnability to choose K.
- Includes a Label Prior Constraint (LPC) to prevent trivial class-based clustering by forcing per-domain class distributions to match the global class prior.
- K is selected by maximizing average cross-validated classification accuracy of domain-wise classifiers (principled selection).
- Strength: novel formulation combining non-parametric kernel distinctiveness with a relaxed convex LPC—targets non-semantic factors like lighting/pose.
- Strength: practical, well-motivated automatic procedure for selecting K based on learnability rather than ad-hoc choice.
- Strength: empirical gains shown on object and action recognition tasks, outperforming a relevant prior method.
- Weakness: terminology is unclear—“dataset,” “domain,” and “latent domain” are used interchangeably and need precise definitions/illustrative examples.
- Weakness: LPC is a strong assumption that can hinder discovery when class distributions genuinely vary across latent factors (e.g., pose correlated with class).
- Weakness: experimental clarity issues—notation r(U_k,B) in Eq.6 undefined and choice of source/target combinations not fully justified; a more systematic protocol (e.g., leave-one-dataset-out) is suggested.
- Weakness: calling the task “unsupervised” is misleading since semantic class labels are used (unsupervised w.r.t. domain labels but supervised w.r.t. classes).
- Overall: technically sound, novel, and empirically effective method; recommend acceptance with revisions to clarify terminology, LPC limitations, and experimental details.
