This paper presents a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision datasets, which often exhibit multiple aspects such as viewpoint, illumination, and level of clutter. Instead of equating domains with datasets, the proposed method automatically partitions the set of all images across all datasets into domains. The partitioning process is driven by two principles: maximizing the distinctiveness of separate domains in feature space and ensuring that each domain allows for the training of a good discriminative classifier to classify the original classes.

Originality and significance:
The paper proposes a valuable idea for automatically discovering useful domains that can support the training of better classifiers for the original problem. There is limited work on automatically defining domains, making this contribution significant. However, the proposed optimization method in section 2 is not entirely convincing, as it is unclear how closely it solves the original problem. Furthermore, the two proposed driving criteria are not well-integrated, with the maximal learnability criterion being used primarily as a post-hoc validation score to determine the number of domains. A more integrated process would be beneficial, where the partitioning is made to maximize a goal function that includes both criteria directly.

Despite these shortcomings, the paper is a step in the right direction for the community and should be accepted.

Quality and clarity:
The paper is well-written, but it would benefit from additional figures to illustrate the concepts presented.

Experiments:
The experiments demonstrate a significant advantage in using the domains produced by the proposed method over using datasets as domains and over the recent domain discovery technique in section 4.2. The idea of including the test set in the 'reshaping' process is interesting, but its presentation is unclear. This corresponds to an imputation setting, where all test data is available simultaneously, but this is not explicitly stated.

However, the image descriptor used is outdated and simplistic, consisting of a single bag-of-words of sparse SURF features for the entire image with only 800 codebook entries. It is recommended that the authors use a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words, computed on dense SURF features. Additionally, it is unclear what similarity measure is used to compare image descriptors; hopefully, it is X^2 or an intersection kernel, as the Euclidean distance is not suitable for comparing histograms. A Fisher Vector representation could further improve the image representation and bring it up to the level of modern systems. The authors may find the following papers helpful: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010.

Overall, the paper presents interesting novel ideas on an important problem and achieves good results. However, it can be improved in several ways, particularly in terms of the image representation used.
