- Novel convex framework that enforces similar class-label distributions within discovered latent domains to avoid class collapse.  
- Selecting K by maximizing intra-domain learnability is practical and helps ensure sufficient per-class samples for training.  
- Empirical results show gains over baselines and qualitative visualizations aid interpretation.  

- Requiring similar class distributions across domains can be overly restrictive and may mask true class-dependent domain structure.  
- Terms dataset, domain, and latent domain are conflated in the paper and need clear, formal definitions and examples.  
- Equation (7) uses max_k r(U_k, B) without defining r(·), U_k, or B—these must be defined and their role explained.  
- Experimental protocol (choice of source–target pairs) is not well motivated; a leave-one-dataset-out protocol would be more systematic and reduce selection bias.  

- Precisely define dataset vs. (source/target) domain vs. latent domain and provide a toy schematic illustrating the partitioning.  
- Specify the kernel and RKHS used for the distinctiveness term and analyze sensitivity to kernel choice.  
- Describe how K and other hyperparameters are chosen in practice, including validation strategy and stability across runs.  
- Revisit the claim of being “unsupervised”—using class labels to enforce distribution similarity makes the method weakly- or semi-supervised.  

- Consider making the class-distribution similarity constraint a tunable regularizer to allow legitimate class-imbalances when appropriate.  
- Report per-class domain composition diagnostics to check whether the constraint overly homogenizes domains.  
- Provide ablations isolating the effects of label-distribution control versus the RKHS distinctiveness term.  

- Overall: an interesting, technically sound formulation combining label-distribution control, RKHS distinctiveness, and learnability-driven K selection, with promising empirical gains but needing clearer terminology, missing equation components, a more transparent evaluation protocol, and a nuanced discussion of supervision assumptions.
