The paper proposes an approach to automatically discover latent domains in a training set, which is then used in a domain adaptation framework to improve classification performance on a test set. The authors define a function that measures the difference between two feature vectors over a specified kernel and aim to partition the data points into domains such that this function is maximized across each pair of domains. This problem is formulated as an integer programming problem with two constraints: each point is assigned to exactly one domain, and the distribution over class labels in each domain must match the input distribution over the entire point set. The problem is then relaxed to a continuous optimization over a quadratic cost with linear constraints, and the number of domains is determined via cross-validation.

The approach is evaluated on two datasets: static images of [2] and the IXMAS multi-view action dataset of [15]. Notably, the paper demonstrates improved performance over the domain adaptation approach of [19].

Positives: The paper is well-written, and the approach appears to be novel, although I am not an expert in domain adaptation. The performance gains over [19] are also appreciated.

Negatives: Initially, I leaned towards rejecting the paper due to two main concerns that I would like to see addressed in the rebuttal, which may convince me to change my score:

(i) The motivation behind this paper is unclear. On line 77, the authors argue that "simply clustering images by their appearance is prone to reshaping datasets into per-category domains." However, I would like to see evidence supporting this claim and an explanation of how the model formulation in Section 2 overcomes this issue, specifically how it prevents reshaping into per-category domains.

(ii) Related to the first concern, the necessity of the second constraint ("label prior constraint") on line 154 is unclear. I am curious about the performance without this constraint. Additionally, a baseline comparison with k-means clustering or unsupervised object discovery (e.g., Sivic et al. ICCV '05) over appearance vectors would be beneficial. Furthermore, it would be interesting to see the performance when the dataset is randomly partitioned into equal sets.

Some additional comments:

+ Line 82: The phrase "maximally different in distribution from each other" is used throughout the paper. It would be helpful to clarify what this means, specifically the distribution over what variables.

+ Line 166: It appears that β_{mk} already lives on a simplex as formulated. Could the authors confirm this?

+ Line 182: These constraints seem different from those formulated earlier (starting on line 153). Could the authors clarify this discrepancy?

+ Line 215: What is the justification or proof for this bound?

+ Line 289: Please provide more details on the use of the geodesic flow kernel [4]. Was this a reimplementation or publicly available source code used?

+ Lines 313/340: It would be helpful to provide insights into the differences in performance. I would like to better understand why the proposed approach performs better and see systematic failures of the baselines that the proposed approach overcomes.
