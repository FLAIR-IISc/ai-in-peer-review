This paper introduces a convex framework for partitioning a dataset (or datasets) into K subsets, designed to exhibit similar semantic class distributions within each subset while maximizing distinctiveness between them. This distinctiveness, termed maximum distinctiveness, is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences of the subsets in the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) induced by a chosen kernel function. These subsets are referred to as latent domains. The optimal number of latent domains, K, is determined by maximizing the average learnability – the performance of a classifier trained – within each domain.

While enforcing similar class distributions within each domain prevents any single class from dominating a cluster, this constraint may limit latent domain discovery in certain applications. For example, consider an animal classification task with poses as latent domains (e.g., horses and cows). The class distributions within each pose may not be similar; horses and cows are both commonly seen standing, but horses are rarely depicted sitting, whereas cows frequently are. 

Although latent domain discovery is not novel, the approach of controlling class label distributions to improve latent domain identification within a (relaxed) convex framework is a new contribution. Furthermore, determining the number of latent domains by evaluating classification quality within each domain is a notable practice, ensuring sufficient samples per class for effective generalization and discrimination.

The paper suffers from a lack of clarity regarding the concepts of dataset, domain, and latent domain, leading to potential confusion. These terms should be precisely defined, ideally with illustrative examples. Section 4.2, in particular, requires clarification. As currently presented, “dataset” and “domain” appear to be used interchangeably (Si being referred to as both a dataset and a source domain). The experimental setup and associated definitions should be clarified in this section. Additionally, the notation ‘maxk r(U_k,B)’ is used in equation 7 without prior definition.

The experimental validation appears adequate, demonstrating reasonable improvements over the baselines. However, the rationale for selecting the specific source and target datasets used in the evaluation is unclear. A more comprehensive evaluation, such as a leave-one-dataset-out adaptation scheme, might be more informative. The qualitative results are helpful in visualizing the algorithm’s behavior. 

The framing of the problem on line 74 as an unsupervised learning task may be misleading, as the method leverages semantic class labels. The formulation of latent domain discovery through encouraging similar class distributions within each domain, within a (relaxed) convex framework, represents a notable technical contribution. However, several concepts within the paper, and the experimental validation, require further clarification.
