This paper proposes a novel technique for domain adaptation in computer vision datasets, which often exhibit multiple aspects such as viewpoint, illumination, level of clutter, compression, etc. Unlike existing approaches that equate domains with datasets, thereby mixing these aspects, the proposed method automatically partitions the set of all images across datasets into domains. This partitioning is driven by two key principles: separating domains in feature space and ensuring that a good discriminative classifier can be trained on each domain to classify the original classes, not to separate the domains. This approach is more likely to partition according to the underlying aspects rather than datasets.

Originality and significance:
The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field by proposing a method for automatically defining domains, a topic that has received limited attention. The idea of discovering useful domains to train better classifiers for the original problem is well-motivated and timely.

However, there are some limitations to the proposed approach. The optimization method presented in Section 2 is not entirely convincing, as it is unclear how closely it solves the original problem (2)+(3). Furthermore, the two driving criteria - maximal learnability and maximal separability - are not well-integrated. The maximal learnability criterion is used as a post-hoc validation score to determine the number of domains, whereas the partitioning itself is based solely on the separability criterion. A more integrated approach would involve producing a partitioning that maximizes a goal function incorporating both criteria directly.

Despite these limitations, which may be due to the exploratory nature of this work, the paper represents a step in the right direction for the community and should be accepted.

Quality and clarity:
The paper is well-written, but lacks figures to illustrate the concepts presented, which would enhance the reader's understanding.

Experiments:
The experiments demonstrate a significant advantage in using the domains produced by the proposed method over simply using datasets as domains and the recent domain discovery technique [19] (Section 4.2). The idea of including the test set in the 'reshaping' process is interesting, but its presentation is unclear (Section 4.3). Additionally, this corresponds to an imputation setting, where all test data is available simultaneously, but this is not explicitly stated.

On the negative side, the image descriptor used is overly simplistic and outdated, relying on a single bag-of-words of sparse SURF features for the entire image with only 800 codebook entries. This is no longer competitive in modern systems. The authors are recommended to use a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words, computed on dense SURF features. Furthermore, it is unclear what similarity measure is used to compare image descriptors; hopefully, it is X^2 or an intersection kernel, as the Euclidean distance is not suitable for comparing histograms. Consideration of a Fisher Vector representation could further improve the image representation and bring it in line with modern systems. The following papers may provide useful guidance: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010.

Overall, the paper presents interesting and novel ideas on an important problem and achieves good results. While there is room for improvement, particularly in terms of the image representation used, the paper is a valuable contribution to the field.
