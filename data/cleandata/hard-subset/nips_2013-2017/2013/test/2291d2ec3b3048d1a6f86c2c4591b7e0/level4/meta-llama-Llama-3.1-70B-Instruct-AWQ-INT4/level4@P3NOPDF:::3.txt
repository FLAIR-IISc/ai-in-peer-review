This paper proposes a convex framework for partitioning datasets into K subsets, referred to as latent domains, such that each subset has a similar semantic class distribution and is as distinct from others as possible. The distinctiveness of these subsets, termed maximum distinctiveness, is achieved by maximizing the pairwise mean differences of subsets in the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) induced by a selected kernel. The identification of the optimal number of latent domains (K) is accomplished by maximizing the average learnability within each latent domain, which measures how well a classifier can be learned.

Although the approach ensures that the distribution of classes within each domain is similar, preventing clusters from being dominated by a single class, it may be a limiting assumption for certain tasks. For instance, in an animal classification task, considering poses as latent domains may not yield similar class distributions within each domain. Specifically, while both horses and cows may be depicted in left-right standing poses, horses are rarely pictured in a sitting pose, whereas cows are.

While the concept of latent domain discovery is not novel, the idea of controlling class label distributions to improve the identification of latent domains within a relaxed convex framework is a new contribution. The method of identifying the number of latent domains by evaluating the quality of classification within each latent domain is also noteworthy, as it ensures that each latent domain has sufficient samples for each class to facilitate better generalization and learning of class discrimination.

However, the paper could benefit from clearer definitions of key concepts, including "dataset," "domain," and "latent domain," accompanied by supporting examples. The experiments section (4.2) requires clarification, particularly with regards to the interchangeable use of "dataset" and "domain," as Si is referred to as both datasets and source domains. The experimental setting and concept definitions should be clarified to avoid confusion. Additionally, the term "maxk r(U_k,B)" is used in equation 7 without a clear definition.

The experimental validation appears to be adequate, with the results showing reasonable improvements over the baselines. However, the rationale behind the selection of source and target datasets for reporting is unclear. A more comprehensive evaluation, such as leave-one-dataset-out adaptation, may be more informative. The qualitative results provide a helpful visualization of the algorithm's performance.

It is worth noting that the problem of learning latent domains is stated as an unsupervised learning problem on line 074, which may be misleading given that the method utilizes semantic class labels. Nevertheless, the discovery of latent domains via encouraging similar class distributions in each latent domain, formulated within a relaxed convex framework, is a notable technical contribution. To further strengthen the paper, the authors should clarify some of the concepts and provide more detailed explanations of the experimental validation.
