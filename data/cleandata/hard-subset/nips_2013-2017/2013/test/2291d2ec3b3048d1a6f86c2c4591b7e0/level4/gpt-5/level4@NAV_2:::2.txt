This paper introduces a new approach to domain adaptation for vision, where datasets typically conflate multiple factors (e.g., viewpoint, illumination, clutter, compression). Rather than equating “domain” with “dataset,” which blends these factors, the method automatically partitions all images pooled across datasets into latent domains. The partitioning is driven by two criteria: enforcing that domains be well separated in feature space, and ensuring that each domain supports training a strong discriminative classifier on the original categories (not to distinguish domains). This makes it more likely that the partitions reflect underlying nuisance factors rather than dataset boundaries.

Originality and significance:
There is relatively little prior work on automatically inducing domains, and this paper presents a promising direction for uncovering useful domains that can yield better classifiers for the primary task. On the downside, I remain unconvinced by the optimization procedure in Section 2: it is unclear how closely the relaxed solver approximates the original constrained problem (2)+(3). In addition, the two driving principles are not yet tightly integrated: maximum learnability is used only as a wrapper around the maximum separability objective to select K, functioning as a post-hoc validation score for a given K, while the partition itself is determined solely by separability. A more unified formulation would optimize a single objective that incorporates both criteria directly. Despite these limitations—perhaps expected at this exploratory stage—the paper represents a constructive step for the community and merits acceptance.

Quality and clarity:
The paper is clearly written and organized, but it would benefit from additional figures to illustrate the key concepts.

Experiments:
Positively, the experiments show clear gains when using the discovered domains instead of treating datasets as domains, and they outperform the recent domain discovery approach [19] (Sec. 4.2). The idea of reshaping the test set is interesting but not presented with sufficient clarity (Sec. 4.3). Moreover, this corresponds to a batch/imputation setting in which all test data are available at once; this assumption should be explicitly stated.

On the negative side, the image representation is quite dated: a single bag-of-words of sparse SURF with an 800-word codebook is weak by current standards. I recommend using a spatial pyramid with dense SURF features. It is also unclear which similarity measure is used for comparing histograms; ideally χ^2 or intersection kernels should be employed, as Euclidean distance is ill-suited for histograms. As a next step, a Fisher Vector representation could further improve performance and bring the image features in line with modern practice. Relevant references include: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronin ECCV 2010.

Overall, the paper offers interesting, novel ideas on an important problem and demonstrates solid results. There is room for improvement, especially in the choice of image representation.
