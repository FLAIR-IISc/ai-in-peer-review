This paper proposes a domain adaptation technique for computer vision datasets that vary along multiple latent factors (e.g., viewpoint, illumination, clutter, compression). Rather than equating domains with datasets—which conflates these factors—the method automatically partitions all images across datasets into domains. The partitioner is guided by two principles: (i) encouraging separability of domains in feature space, and (ii) ensuring that a strong discriminative classifier can be trained within each domain for the original classes (not for domain separation). This approach is more likely to recover partitions aligned with underlying aspects rather than with dataset boundaries.

Originality and significance:
- There is relatively little prior work on automatically defining domains, and this paper offers a promising idea for discovering useful domains that support better classifiers for the original task.
- On the negative side, I am not fully convinced by the optimization method in Section 2; it is unclear how closely the proposed algorithm solves the original objective (Eqs. (2)+(3)). Moreover, the two driving criteria are not well integrated: the maximal learnability criterion is used only as a wrapper around the maximal separability criterion to choose the number of domains. In effect, it serves as a post-hoc validation score for a partitioning learned solely via separability. An integrated approach would directly optimize a single objective that combines both criteria and yields the partitioning accordingly.
- Despite these shortcomings—which may reflect the exploratory stage of this line of work—the paper is a step in the right direction and should be accepted.

Quality and clarity:
- The paper is generally well written, but it lacks figures that would help illustrate the key concepts.

Experiments:
- Positives: The experiments show a significant advantage to using the discovered domains over (a) treating datasets as domains and (b) the recent domain discovery technique [19] (Section 4.2).
- The idea of including the test set in the “reshaping” process is interesting but not clearly presented (Section 4.3). This corresponds to an imputation setting in which all test data are available simultaneously; this assumption should be stated explicitly.
- Negatives: The image descriptor is very simple and outdated: a single bag-of-words over sparse SURF features for the entire image with only an 800-word codebook. This is weak by current standards. I recommend using a spatial pyramid of bag-of-words computed on dense SURF features. It is also unclear which similarity measure is used to compare image histograms; chi-squared or intersection kernels would be appropriate, whereas Euclidean distance is not. As a next step, a Fisher Vector representation could further improve results and bring the image representation in line with modern systems. The following references may be helpful: Zhang IJCV 2006; Jurie CVPR 2005; Lazebnik CVPR 2006; Perronnin ECCV 2010.

Overall assessment:
The paper presents interesting, novel ideas on an important problem and achieves good results. It can be improved in several ways, particularly in the choice of image representation.
