This paper introduces a (relaxed) convex formulation to partition one or more datasets into K subsets—termed latent domains—such that each subset respects approximately the same semantic class prior and is as mutually different as possible. Distinctiveness is enforced by maximizing pairwise discrepancies between subsets via the distance between kernel mean embeddings in an RKHS (i.e., an MMD-style criterion). The number of latent domains K is selected by maximizing average within-domain learnability, measured by domain-wise cross-validated classification performance.

While matching class-prior distributions across domains helps prevent clusters from collapsing onto single classes, this constraint can be restrictive for some problems. For example, if latent domains correspond to poses in an animal recognition task (e.g., horses vs. cows), the pose distribution within each class may naturally differ: both horses and cows appear in left/right standing poses, but sitting poses are far more common for cows than for horses. Enforcing similar class-prior proportions across domains could therefore mask meaningful domain structure.

Although latent domain discovery has prior art, the use of an explicit label-prior control within a (relaxed) convex objective is novel. Selecting K by validating within-domain discriminative performance is also a useful design choice, as it guards against overly small domains that lack sufficient per-class examples to learn generalizable classifiers.

In several places, the terminology around dataset, domain, and latent domain is ambiguous and invites confusion. Clear, formal definitions—ideally accompanied by illustrative examples—would help, especially in Section 4.2. As written, Si is referred to as both a dataset and a source domain, which suggests that “dataset” and “domain” are being used interchangeably. The experimental setup and terminology in Section 4.2 should be tightened. In addition, the quantity maxk r(Uk, B) is used in Eq. 7 without being explicitly defined.

The empirical validation is generally adequate, and the improvements over baselines are reasonable. However, the rationale for the chosen source/target splits is not fully justified. A leave-one-dataset-out adaptation protocol could provide a more standard and comprehensive evaluation. The qualitative visualizations are informative and help convey what the method is capturing.

At line 074, describing the problem (latent domain discovery) as unsupervised may be misleading given that semantic class labels are used by the method. The formulation that promotes similar class distributions within each latent domain through a (relaxed) convex objective is a solid technical contribution, but the paper would benefit from clarifying key concepts and tightening the experimental protocol.
