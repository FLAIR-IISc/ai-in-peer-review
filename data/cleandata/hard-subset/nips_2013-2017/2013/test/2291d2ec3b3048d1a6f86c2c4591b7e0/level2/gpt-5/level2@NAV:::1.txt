Summary and relation to prior work: The paper tackles an important but underexplored question in visual domain adaptation: how to automatically discover latent domains within image/video datasets rather than equating datasets with domains. The authors propose two axioms—maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability—to guide domain discovery. Distinctiveness is operationalized via a kernel two-sample criterion (MMD in RKHS, à la Gretton et al.) maximized over domain assignments under a label prior constraint (LPC); learnability is enforced via domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) to select K. A relaxed quadratic program is used to optimize soft assignments. The method is compared to the Gaussian-mixture latent domain model of Hoffman et al. and is combined with GFK for adaptation, including a “conditional reshaping” of heterogeneous test sets to match discovered training domains. Experiments on Caltech-256/Amazon/DSLR/Webcam and IXMAS show improvements over original datasets and over [20].

Quality: The core idea is sound and well-motivated; using MMD to drive separation and using supervised feedback to prevent per-class clustering is sensible. Empirical support spans multiple benchmarks, with both “oracle” and practical multi-source settings; conditional test reshaping is a nice addition. However, important details are missing or deferred: optimization is a maximization of a convex quadratic (nonconvex overall), yet convergence behavior, initialization, and sensitivity to kernel bandwidth/δ are not analyzed; scalability of the O(M^2) kernel is not discussed; statistical significance of gains is not reported.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized; constraints and objectives are explicit. Some parts (conditional reshaping QP) rely on the supplement; adding complexity and solver details would aid reproducibility.

Originality: The combination of nonparametric discrepancy (MMD), LPC, and DWCV for latent domain discovery, plus conditional test reshaping, appears novel relative to Hoffman et al. and generalized clustering (Jegelka et al.). The positioning within multi-source adaptation is appropriate.

Significance: If robust, this can change practice in adaptation by replacing dataset-as-domain heuristics and improving transfer. The demonstrated improvements are promising, though bounded by relatively small benchmarks and reliance on oracle analyses.

Pros:
- Addresses an important, under-specified step in domain adaptation.
- Principled use of MMD with supervised regularization; automatic K selection.
- Demonstrates benefits on both training and test reshaping; interpretable domains.

Cons:
- Optimization nonconvexity/scalability not fully addressed.
- LPC may be unrealistic when class priors vary across true domains.
- Limited ablations and significance analysis; reliance on oracle metrics.

Suggestions: Provide runtime/complexity and scalability experiments; ablate LPC/DWCV and kernel choices; add statistical tests; compare to additional discrepancy measures (e.g., Wasserstein, CMD) and to alternative clustering baselines; report sensitivity to K and initialization; release code. Overall, a solid and promising contribution; I lean toward acceptance contingent on strengthened empirical analysis.
