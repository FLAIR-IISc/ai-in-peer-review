### Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental and often overlooked problem in visual domain adaptation: the definition of a "domain". The authors argue that the common practice of treating entire datasets as single, discrete domains is a flawed simplification, as datasets often comprise a mixture of latent domains determined by factors like lighting, pose, and background. To address this, they propose a novel unsupervised method to automatically discover these latent domains within a collection of source datasets.

The core of their approach is built on two intuitive principles for what constitutes a good set of domains:
1.  **Maximum Distinctiveness:** The discovered domains should be as different from each other as possible in terms of their underlying data distributions. This is formalized by maximizing the sum of pairwise Maximum Mean Discrepancies (MMD) between all discovered domains. The optimization is constrained to ensure each data point is assigned to a domain and that the class label distribution within each discovered domain is similar to the overall prior (Label Prior Constraint).
2.  **Maximum Learnability:** The data within each discovered domain should be sufficient to train a strong classifier. This principle is cleverly used to automatically determine the optimal number of domains, K. The authors propose a "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) procedure, selecting the K that yields the highest cross-validation accuracy on the partitioned data.

The authors evaluate their method on object and action recognition tasks, showing that performing domain adaptation from their automatically discovered domains leads to significantly better performance on target datasets than adapting from the original, monolithic datasets. They also demonstrate an extension where unlabeled test sets can be "reshaped" to match the discovered source domains for improved adaptation.

### Strengths

1.  **Important and Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper tackles a critical conceptual issue in domain adaptation. The premise that datasets are not monolithic entities but rather heterogeneous collections is highly compelling and provides a strong motivation for the work. This reframing of the problem is a significant contribution in itself.

2.  **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The proposed method is grounded in two clear and intuitive axioms (distinctiveness and learnability). The non-parametric formulation using MMD to measure distinctiveness is well-chosen, as it avoids making restrictive assumptions about the distributions of the latent domains, which is a key advantage over prior work like Hoffman et al. [20].

3.  **Creative Model Selection:** The DWCV method for choosing the number of domains K is an elegant and practical solution to a difficult problem. By directly connecting the choice of K to the ultimate goal of discriminative performance, it sidesteps the need for heuristics and provides a task-relevant criterion for model selection.

4.  **Thorough and Convincing Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is strong. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on multiple datasets and tasks (object and action recognition). The use of an "oracle" accuracy (GOURS) effectively establishes an upper bound and shows that the discovered domains have high potential. The comparison with adapting from original datasets and the baseline method from Hoffman et al. [20] clearly demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach. The extension to reshaping test sets is a valuable addition that shows the versatility of the core idea.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Optimization Details:** The paper states that the relaxed optimization problem (a maximization of a convex quadratic function over a linear constraint set) is still NP-hard but that "existing solvers are adequate". This statement is somewhat vague. For reproducibility and a clearer understanding of the method's limitations, it would be beneficial to provide more details on the specific solver used, whether the solutions are local optima, and how sensitive the final domain assignments are to initialization or solver choice.

2.  **Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The LPC is a strong assumption, positing that the class priors are independent of the domain. While intuitive for some scenarios, it might not hold in others (e.g., a "beach scene" domain is likely to have a different distribution of object classes than an "office scene" domain). A discussion of the limitations of this assumption would be welcome. An ablation study removing or relaxing this constraint further could also provide valuable insight into its impact on the quality of the discovered domains.

3.  **Scalability:** The proposed method requires the computation of an M x M kernel matrix, where M is the total number of training instances. This O(M^2) complexity could be prohibitive for very large datasets common in modern computer vision. A discussion of potential avenues for scaling up the approach, for instance by using kernel approximation techniques (e.g., Nyström method, Random Fourier Features), would significantly increase the potential impact of this work.

4.  **Clarity on Conditional Reshaping:** The details of the conditional reshaping of the test set are deferred to the supplementary material. While space is limited, including a brief formal statement of the objective function in the main paper would help the reader better grasp this part of the contribution without needing to consult external material.

### Overall Evaluation

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel and significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation. It addresses a fundamental problem with a well-principled and technically sound approach. The writing is clear, the experiments are thorough, and the results convincingly support the authors' claims. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly relate to opportunities for additional clarification and discussion of limitations, which can be addressed in a future version. The paper is original, significant, and likely to influence how researchers approach domain adaptation problems in the future. I strongly recommend acceptance.
