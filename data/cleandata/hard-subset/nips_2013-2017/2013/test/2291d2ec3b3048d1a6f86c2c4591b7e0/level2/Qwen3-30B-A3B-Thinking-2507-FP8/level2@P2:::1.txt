

This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in visual datasets for domain adaptation, addressing the common practice of equating datasets with domains. The authors introduce two axiomatic properties for domains: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing distribution differences between domains) and maximum learnability (ensuring strong discriminative models can be learned from each domain). They formulate a nonparametric optimization procedure to identify domains satisfying these properties and demonstrate improvements over existing methods on object and action recognition tasks.

Strengths: The paper identifies a significant gap in domain adaptation research - the common practice of treating datasets as domains ignores the complex, overlapping factors affecting visual data (lighting, pose, background, etc.). The two properties proposed (distinctiveness and learnability) are well-motivated and address this gap. The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering both object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and action recognition (IXMAS), with convincing results showing consistent improvements over baselines. The paper also provides insightful visualizations of the identified domains, revealing how they align with underlying factors like object pose and appearance.

Weaknesses: The theoretical justification for why maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability are the right properties for domain discovery is insufficient. While the authors argue these properties lead to better adaptation, they don't provide a formal argument showing how these properties directly improve adaptation performance. The optimization procedure involves relaxing integer constraints to continuous variables, but the authors don't adequately justify this relaxation or analyze its theoretical impact on the solution quality. The paper claims [20] is "the first and only work addressing latent domain discovery," but this appears questionable given the field's rapid development; the paper should more thoroughly compare with recent methods.

The paper's most significant contribution is recognizing that datasets often contain multiple latent domains, and that domain adaptation performance can be improved by reshaping datasets into these latent domains. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently address how the discovered domains relate to the specific adaptation algorithms used. The results show that reshaping improves performance, but the paper doesn't explain why reshaping into these particular domains is beneficial beyond the stated properties.

The paper is generally well-written and clear, though the technical details of the optimization procedure could be presented more accessibly. The experimental setup is sound, but the paper could better justify the choice of kernel bandwidth and other hyperparameters. The evaluation strategy is appropriate, using both oracle accuracy (for theoretical comparison) and practical adaptation performance.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to domain adaptation research by recognizing that datasets often contain multiple latent domains and proposing a method to discover them. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by:

1. Providing a more rigorous theoretical justification for the two proposed properties, particularly how they relate to adaptation performance.
2. Including a more thorough comparison with recent domain adaptation work, especially after 2013.
3. Exploring the limitations of the approach (e.g., scalability, sensitivity to hyperparameters).
4. Providing more detailed analysis of how the discovered domains align with specific visual factors (beyond the qualitative examples).

The paper is technically sound, addresses an important problem, and demonstrates practical improvements. While the theoretical foundation could be stronger, the experimental results are convincing and the approach has clear potential for improving domain adaptation in real-world applications. The paper represents a meaningful step toward better understanding and utilizing the latent structure within visual datasets for domain adaptation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the theoretical justification and comparison with more recent work.
