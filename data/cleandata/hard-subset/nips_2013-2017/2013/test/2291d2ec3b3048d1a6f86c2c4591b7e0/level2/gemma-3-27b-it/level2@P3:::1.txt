This paper proposes a novel approach to domain adaptation by automatically discovering latent domains within datasets, rather than equating datasets with domains. The core idea is to identify domains that maximize both distinctiveness and learnability, achieved through a nonparametric formulation and efficient optimization procedure. The method is evaluated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, demonstrating improved performance compared to standard approaches.

The motivation behind this work is well-articulated. The limitations of treating entire datasets as single domains in domain adaptation are clearly explained, and the paper convincingly argues for a more nuanced approach that identifies underlying domain structures. The two proposed properties – maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability – are intuitive and well-defined, providing a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed method. The formulation using kernel methods appears sound and allows for flexibility in modeling domain distributions. The DWCV approach for determining the optimal number of domains is a practical and reasonable strategy.

A key strength is the experimental validation on diverse tasks (object recognition, human activity recognition) and datasets. The results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in improving classification accuracy. The ablation studies, comparing performance against existing approaches and different components of the proposed method, are valuable. The visualization of identified domains (Figure 1) provides helpful qualitative insight into the discovered domain structures. The inclusion of an analysis of the dominant factors within the identified domains adds further understanding.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be strengthened. While the paper acknowledges related work in domain adaptation, a more thorough discussion of existing unsupervised domain discovery techniques beyond Hoffman et al. [20] would be beneficial. Are there clustering methods or manifold learning techniques that could be framed as related work?

The optimization procedure described in Section 2.1 relies on relaxing the integer programming problem. While this is a common approach, a more detailed discussion of the implications of this relaxation would be useful. How does the relaxation affect the optimality of the solution? Are there any guarantees on the quality of the approximation? Furthermore, the computational complexity of the proposed approach is not thoroughly discussed. A brief analysis of the scalability of the method to larger datasets would be valuable.

The presentation could be improved in certain areas. While generally well-written, some sections are dense and could benefit from further clarification. For instance, the explanation of the label prior constraint (LPC) could be more accessible. The notation used in the equations could also be standardized for consistency.  More detail about hyperparameter settings (e.g., kernel bandwidth, δ in the LPC) would enhance reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance:
* The paper addresses an important and often overlooked aspect of domain adaptation – the identification of latent domain structures.
* The proposed method is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and supported by strong experimental results.
* The two properties of maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability provide a principled framework for domain discovery.
* The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over existing approaches.

Arguments against acceptance:
* The computational complexity of the method is not fully analyzed.
* The discussion of related work could be more comprehensive.
* The impact of the integer programming relaxation could be better discussed.
* Some sections of the paper are dense and could benefit from improved clarity.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and could be addressed with further refinement. It is recommended to accept this paper, contingent upon addressing the reviewer's concerns regarding computational complexity, related work, and clarity of presentation. The suggested revisions are reasonable and should further strengthen the paper's impact.
