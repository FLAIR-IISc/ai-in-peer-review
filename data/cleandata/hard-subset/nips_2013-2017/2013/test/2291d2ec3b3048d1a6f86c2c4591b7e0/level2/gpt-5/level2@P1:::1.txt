Summary
This paper tackles the often implicit assumption in visual domain adaptation that “datasets = domains.” The authors argue that visual datasets are typically heterogeneous mixtures of latent domains induced by factors like pose, illumination, and background, and that treating them as a single domain can harm adaptation. They propose to automatically reshape datasets into latent domains by optimizing two axioms: (1) maximum distinctiveness, measured nonparametrically via RKHS mean embeddings (MMD-style distances) to find partitions whose marginal feature distributions differ most; and (2) maximum learnability, enforced by selecting the number of domains K using domain-wise cross-validation accuracy. The optimization starts with an integer program on binary assignments zmk, imposes a label prior constraint (LPC) to avoid label-driven clustering, and then relaxes to a continuous quadratic program in βmk. The approach is applied to object recognition (Office/Caltech) and cross-view action recognition (IXMAS), with additional “conditional reshaping” of test data to match discovered training domains. Compared to Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12), which uses parametric Gaussian mixtures for latent domains, this work leverages nonparametric distributional distances and a learnability-driven model selection.

Strengths
- Problem importance and clear motivation: discovering latent domains is well-motivated in vision, where nuisance factors rarely align with dataset boundaries.
- Methodological novelty: maximizing pairwise RKHS mean differences to induce domain partitions, combined with a label-aware constraint to discourage per-class clustering, is interesting and complements prior parametric work.
- Practicality: DWCV offers a transparent criterion to select K; conditional reshaping of test sets is a useful extension for heterogeneous targets.
- Empirical evidence: on Office/Caltech and IXMAS, the discovered domains outperform both original dataset partitions and the ECCV’12 latent-domain approach, and improve downstream adaptation (with GFK) under both oracle and practical source selection strategies.

Weaknesses and questions
- Use of labels and LPC assumption: Domain discovery is supervised (relies on labels), and the LPC enforces per-domain label proportions to match the global prior (with δ≈1%). This is a strong assumption; in many realistic data collection regimes, class frequencies vary across latent domains (e.g., certain poses more common for some classes). How sensitive is the method to violations of LPC or to δ? Could LPC be made a soft penalty with a tunable weight?
- Optimization details and scalability: The relaxed objective maximizes a convex quadratic form, which is NP-hard in general. The paper notes “off-the-shelf solvers are adequate,” but lacks concrete algorithmic details (solver type, initialization, runtimes, convergence behavior) and scalability analysis. The M×M kernel matrix will limit M; are Nyström or random features practical? What are typical M and K in experiments, and how does runtime scale?
- Objective structure: TDD sums all pairwise squared RKHS mean distances. There is a known identity ∑_{k<k’}||μk−μk’||^2 = K∑k||μk−μ̄||^2 that could simplify the objective and enable more efficient optimization or insights (e.g., a spectral view). It would be helpful to exploit or discuss this.
- Potential degeneracies and constraints: The constraint 1/M ≤ ∑k βmk ≤ 1/C is unusual and its derivation from “Mk≥C and Mk≤M” is not fully clear. Please clarify its role in preventing degenerate assignments and its effect on solution quality.
- Selection of K via DWCV: While intuitive, DWCV optimizes in-domain supervised accuracy on the discovered domains. Evidence that A(K) correlates with target-domain adaptation performance is limited to small plots; a more systematic analysis (e.g., multiple random splits, variance across runs) would strengthen confidence.
- Experimental clarity: Several key components (MATCHING strategy, conditional test reshaping QP) are referenced but not fully specified in the main text. Precise definitions (distance used for matching, constraints/objective for test reshaping) and ablations (distinctiveness only vs. learnability only, effect of δ and kernel bandwidth) would clarify the sources of gains.
- External validity: The datasets are relatively small. It would be useful to discuss applicability to larger-scale recognition (e.g., deeper features, modern descriptors) and whether the method interacts favorably with representation learning.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a reasonable nonparametric criterion and a pragmatic model selection strategy. However, the optimization and constraint design deserve more detail, and the LPC assumption should be stress-tested. The experimental comparisons are promising but rely partly on oracle metrics; the practical selection/matching pipeline should be more completely specified and assessed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and motivated, with clean high-level exposition. Some notational choices and constraints (e.g., the β constraints) need clarification. Including algorithmic pseudocode, solver details, and a clearer description of MATCHING and conditional reshaping would help.

Originality
The problem framing and nonparametric distinctiveness criterion are novel relative to ECCV’12 latent-domain discovery. The DWCV selection and test-set conditional reshaping add practical value.

Significance
If robust, the approach can impact how adaptation studies prepare source/target data and may improve performance in settings where nuisance factors are mixed within datasets. The idea that “domains” should be learned rather than assumed is valuable for the community.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide algorithmic details (solver, initialization, complexity) and scalability strategies (Nyström/random features).
- Relax LPC as a soft penalty; report sensitivity to δ and to class-prior shifts across domains.
- Detail MATCHING and conditional reshaping and add ablations isolating each component’s contribution.
- Consider leveraging the pairwise-distance identity for optimization efficiency.
- Report statistical variability (multiple runs) and, if possible, experiments with different feature representations.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and under-explored bottleneck in domain adaptation (discovering domains).
- Conceptually clean nonparametric formulation; careful attempt to prevent per-class clustering.
- Empirically improves over baselines and prior latent-domain discovery on two modalities.

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong assumptions (LPC) and reliance on labels limit generality; sensitivity analysis is thin.
- Optimization/scalability details are sparse; unclear performance on larger datasets.
- Some key components (matching, conditional reshaping) are underspecified in the main text.

Overall
A solid and timely contribution with promising results and a clear idea. With added clarity on optimization and assumptions, and stronger empirical analysis, it would be a valuable addition. I lean toward acceptance.
