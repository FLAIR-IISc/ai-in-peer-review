

This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in visual datasets, addressing a critical gap in domain adaptation research where datasets are often incorrectly equated with domains. The authors introduce two key axiomatic properties for ideal domains: maximum distinctiveness (ensuring domains are maximally different from each other in distribution) and maximum learnability (ensuring strong discriminative models can be learned from each domain). They develop a nonparametric formulation and efficient optimization procedure to discover these domains without requiring manual domain specification.

The paper's main contribution is well-motivated and addresses a real problem in visual domain adaptation research. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms both the standard practice of using datasets as domains and the only prior work on latent domain discovery [20] across multiple object recognition and human activity recognition tasks. Their experimental evaluation is thorough, using standard datasets like Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, and Webcam for object recognition, and IXMAS for action recognition. The DWCV (domain-wise cross-validation) method for determining the optimal number of domains is particularly clever and well-justified.

Strengths include the clear theoretical foundation, the practical utility demonstrated in the experiments, and the comprehensive comparison to existing approaches. The method's ability to reshape both training and test datasets for better adaptation is a significant practical contribution. The paper is well-written with sufficient technical detail for reproducibility.

A minor weakness is the limited discussion of the method's limitations. While the paper acknowledges that the approach may struggle when domains are too similar, a more detailed analysis of failure cases would strengthen the work. Additionally, while the paper references relevant domain adaptation literature, it could more thoroughly discuss how their approach relates to multi-source domain adaptation methods.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses an important problem in domain adaptation. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are convincing, and the method has clear practical value. The paper makes a significant advance over the prior work on latent domain discovery and should be accepted for publication. The authors have provided a valuable tool for practitioners and researchers working in domain adaptation who need to handle complex data distributions beyond simple dataset boundaries.
