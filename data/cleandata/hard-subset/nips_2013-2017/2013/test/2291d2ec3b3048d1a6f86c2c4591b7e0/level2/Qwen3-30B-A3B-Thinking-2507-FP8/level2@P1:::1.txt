

This paper addresses an important but often overlooked issue in domain adaptation: the assumption that datasets correspond to single domains, when in reality datasets may contain multiple latent domains (e.g., different lighting conditions, viewpoints, or backgrounds). The authors propose a novel approach to automatically discover these latent domains based on two key properties: maximum distinctiveness (domains should be maximally different from each other) and maximum learnability (domains should support strong discriminative models). 

The paper makes a strong case for the importance of this problem, noting that treating a heterogeneous dataset as a single domain can harm performance, as demonstrated in their motivating example of human action recognition with different camera views. The authors' solution is technically sound, using a kernel-based method to measure domain discrepancies without assuming a specific parametric form for the distributions (unlike [20], which uses Gaussian mixtures). Their DWCV procedure for determining the number of domains is particularly well-motivated and practical.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, demonstrating improvements on both object recognition (using Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, and Webcam datasets) and human activity recognition (using IXMAS dataset with multiple camera views). The results consistently show that reshaping datasets into the discovered domains leads to better adaptation performance compared to using the original datasets or domains discovered by [20]. The authors provide insightful qualitative analysis showing how the identified domains correspond to meaningful factors like object pose, appearance, and background.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is compelling, the technical approach is carefully explained, and the experimental results are convincing. The authors effectively relate their work to previous literature, particularly highlighting the differences from [20] and other domain adaptation approaches.

I have a few minor concerns. The paper states that "the standard practice of equating datasets with domains is a weak proxy for all the real conditions that alter the statistics in complex ways," but this could be strengthened with more concrete examples of how this practice has led to suboptimal results in previous work. While the authors mention that their approach can reshape both training and test data, the experimental focus is primarily on reshaping training data, and the test data reshaping is only briefly discussed in section 4.3. More details on the computational complexity of the approach would also be helpful for practitioners.

The paper makes a significant contribution to domain adaptation by addressing a fundamental issue in how we conceptualize domains. The proposed method is not only theoretically sound but also practically effective across multiple visual recognition tasks. The insights about the factors that define latent domains (e.g., object pose, appearance, background) could inform future dataset construction and evaluation practices. The paper's approach could be particularly valuable for applications where data collection conditions vary widely (e.g., mobile applications, video surveillance).

The paper is clearly within NIPS scope, addressing a fundamental problem in machine learning with a novel approach that advances the state of the art in domain adaptation. The authors have thoroughly evaluated their method and provided compelling evidence for its effectiveness.

Strengths:
- Identifies and addresses a meaningful gap in domain adaptation research
- Proposes a novel, well-motivated approach with two key axiomatic properties
- Provides thorough experimental validation across multiple tasks
- Offers practical insights about the factors defining latent domains
- Well-written with clear technical explanations

Weaknesses:
- Could strengthen the argument for the problem's importance with more specific examples from prior work
- Limited discussion of computational complexity
- Test data reshaping is less thoroughly explored than training data reshaping

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to domain adaptation. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. The insights about latent domain discovery could influence both future research and practical applications in visual recognition. I recommend acceptance.
