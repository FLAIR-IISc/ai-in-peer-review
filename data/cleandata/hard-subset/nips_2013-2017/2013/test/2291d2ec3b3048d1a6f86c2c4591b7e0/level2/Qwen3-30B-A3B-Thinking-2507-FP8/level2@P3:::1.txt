

The paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in visual datasets by enforcing two axiomatic properties: maximum distinctiveness (maximizing distributional differences between domains) and maximum learnability (ensuring domains support strong discriminative models). This addresses a critical gap in domain adaptation research where datasets are often incorrectly treated as single domains despite containing multiple underlying distributional factors. The authors formulate the problem nonparametrically using kernel-based distribution discrepancy measures and develop an efficient optimization procedure with label-prior constraints. The method is evaluated on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and human activity recognition (IXMAS) tasks.

The work demonstrates significant strengths across all four review criteria. Quality is strong: the theoretical foundation is sound, with clear justification for the two properties and proper handling of constraints (e.g., label prior consistency). Experimental validation is comprehensive, showing consistent improvements over original datasets and the only prior work on latent domain discovery [20] across multiple adaptation scenarios. The DWCV procedure for determining the number of domains is well-motivated and empirically validated. Clarity is excellent: the paper clearly articulates the problem, distinguishes from prior work, and presents experiments in a logical flow. The technical details of the optimization are sufficiently precise for reproducibility. Originality is compelling: while domain adaptation is well-studied, the specific problem of latent domain discovery—without relying on predefined domain labels or parametric assumptions—is novel, as correctly noted in the comparison to [20]. The nonparametric approach effectively avoids the distributional modeling limitations of prior methods. Significance is high: the method directly addresses a pervasive issue in visual domain adaptation (dataset heterogeneity) and demonstrates practical utility through improved adaptation performance. The findings also offer insights for future dataset construction.

Key weaknesses require attention. The claim that [20] is "the first and only work addressing latent domain discovery" needs verification against recent literature. For instance, [27] (Generalized clustering via kernel embeddings) shares conceptual similarities with the distinctiveness property, though the focus differs (unsupervised clustering vs. domain discovery). The paper should explicitly acknowledge this connection and clarify how the approach differs. The experimental section would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the factors driving domain discovery (e.g., quantifying the contribution of pose vs. lighting vs. background in Figure 1). While the paper shows qualitative examples, a quantitative breakdown of dominant factors would strengthen the interpretation. Additionally, the computational cost of DWCV for large datasets is not addressed—this could be a practical barrier for real-world deployment. The authors should include runtime analysis or discuss scalability trade-offs.

The most valuable revision would be to refine the related work to properly contextualize the distinctiveness property against [27] and clarify the methodological differences. The paper should also expand the analysis of domain-discovery factors beyond qualitative examples to include statistical measures of feature importance. These suggestions are reasonable and essential for a high-quality NIPS submission. The current results are compelling, but a more precise positioning against related work and deeper analysis of discovered domain characteristics would elevate the contribution. The approach is clearly significant for advancing domain adaptation practice and theory, and the technical execution meets NIPS standards. With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
