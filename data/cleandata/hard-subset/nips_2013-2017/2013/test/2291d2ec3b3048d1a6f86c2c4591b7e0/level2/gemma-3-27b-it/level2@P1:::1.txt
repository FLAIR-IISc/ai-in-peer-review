This paper proposes a method for automatically discovering latent domains within image and video datasets, aiming to address the limitations of equating datasets with domains in domain adaptation research. The core idea is to find domains that are both maximally distinct from each other and maximally learnable, achieved through a nonparametric optimization procedure. The approach is evaluated on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, demonstrating improved classification performance compared to traditional approaches.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant issue in domain adaptation – the weak proxy of using datasets as domains. The problem is clearly articulated, and the proposed approach is conceptually sound. The idea of maximizing both distinctiveness and learnability is a clever way to guide the domain discovery process. The use of kernel methods to measure domain discrepancy is appropriate, and the formulation of the optimization problem is reasonably well-presented. The experimental evaluation on multiple datasets and tasks is a strength, and the reported results show promising improvements over baselines.

However, several aspects could be strengthened. The connection between the theoretical motivations (maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability) and the practical implementation could be made more explicit. For example, while the kernel-based distance measure is a reasonable proxy for distinctiveness, a more intuitive explanation of *why* maximizing this distance leads to better domains would be helpful. Similarly, the DWCV approach for determining the number of domains is well-defined, but a more thorough discussion of its sensitivity to parameters (like the cross-validation folds) would be valuable.

The paper's clarity suffers somewhat from a dense writing style and a lack of visual aids explaining the optimization process. For example, a diagram illustrating the optimization steps would significantly improve understanding. The connection to Hoffman et al. [20] is mentioned, but a more detailed comparison of the advantages and disadvantages of each approach would strengthen the paper. The discussion of related work, while present, could be more expansive, particularly in the areas of clustering and subspace learning.

The originality of the work lies in the combination of the distinctiveness and learnability criteria and the specific nonparametric formulation used to optimize them. While latent domain discovery is not entirely new (as acknowledged by the authors), this formulation appears novel and tackles the problem in a different way than previous work.

The significance of the work is potentially high. If successful, automatically discovering meaningful domains can greatly simplify the domain adaptation process and improve the robustness of visual recognition systems. The results presented in the paper demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods, suggesting that this approach has the potential to be widely adopted. The extension to reshaping the test set based on the training domains is particularly insightful and adds to the significance of the work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in domain adaptation.
*   Proposes a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   Demonstrates promising results on multiple datasets and tasks.
*   The idea of reshaping the test set is insightful and practical.
*   Clear theoretical justification for the approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The paper’s writing style is dense and lacks visual aids.
*   The connection between theory and implementation could be more explicit.
*   A more detailed comparison to related work (especially Hoffman et al. [20]) is needed.
*   The sensitivity of the DWCV procedure to its parameters is not fully discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation, and the proposed approach has the potential to significantly improve the performance of visual recognition systems. Addressing the concerns regarding clarity, related work discussion, and the sensitivity of the DWCV procedure will strengthen the paper and make it even more impactful.
