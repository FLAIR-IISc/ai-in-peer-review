- Proposes automatic discovery of latent domains in visual datasets for domain adaptation, challenging the dataset=domain assumption.  
- Introduces two axiomatic properties for domains: maximum distinctiveness (maximize inter-domain distribution differences) and maximum learnability (ensure each domain yields strong discriminative models).  
- Formulates a nonparametric optimization procedure with relaxed integer-to-continuous variables to identify domains satisfying these properties.  
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines on object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and action recognition (IXMAS).  
- Provides insightful visualizations showing discovered domains align with factors like object pose and appearance.  
- Strength: Identifies an important gap—datasets contain overlapping latent factors (lighting, pose, background) that affect adaptation.  
- Strength: Well-motivated properties and thorough experimental evaluation with convincing results.  
- Weakness: Insufficient theoretical justification linking maximum distinctiveness and learnability directly to improved adaptation performance.  
- Weakness: Inadequate justification and theoretical analysis of the integer-to-continuous relaxation in the optimization.  
- Weakness: Claim that prior work [20] is the only latent domain discovery effort is questionable; needs broader comparison with recent methods.  
- Weakness: Does not sufficiently analyze how discovered domains interact with or benefit specific adaptation algorithms beyond empirical gains.  
- Weakness: Technical optimization details could be clearer, and hyperparameter choices (e.g., kernel bandwidth) lack justification and sensitivity analysis.  
- Suggested improvements: Provide stronger theoretical grounding for the two properties, compare with more recent literature (post-2013), analyze scalability and hyperparameter sensitivity, and offer deeper quantitative alignment between discovered domains and visual factors.  
- Overall: Technically sound with meaningful empirical gains; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen theory and comparisons.
