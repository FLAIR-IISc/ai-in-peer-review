This paper addresses a fundamental, yet often overlooked, problem in visual domain adaptation: the standard practice of equating entire datasets with discrete domains. The authors argue that this is a weak proxy for the complex factors (e.g., pose, lighting) that define underlying data distributions. The main contribution is a novel, non-parametric approach to automatically discover these latent domains within a given dataset. The method reshapes the data by optimizing for two key properties: "maximum distinctiveness," ensuring discovered domains are distributionally different from one another, and "maximum learnability," ensuring each domain supports the training of a strong classifier. This work is distinct from most domain adaptation literature, which assumes predefined domains, and improves upon the most related work (Hoffman et al., 2012) by avoiding restrictive parametric assumptions about the domain distributions.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper’s core idea is highly original and significant. It challenges a foundational assumption in the field and provides a principled method to move beyond it. This work could have a substantial impact on how researchers approach domain adaptation and could inform future dataset collection practices.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is technically sound and well-motivated. Using a kernel-based measure for domain distinctiveness is elegant and flexible. The introduction of "learnability" via domain-wise cross-validation to select the number of domains is a sensible and effective heuristic. The experimental evaluation is extensive, demonstrating clear benefits on both object and action recognition tasks. The additional experiment on reshaping the test set further strengthens the paper's claims.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clear, the methodology is explained logically, and the experiments are easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Optimization Details:** The core optimization problem is a non-convex maximization of a convex function, which is NP-hard. The paper states that "existing solvers are adequate" but provides no details on the specific solver, initialization strategy, or how the method handles potential local optima. Including these details would significantly improve the paper's completeness and reproducibility.

**Recommendation:**
This is a high-quality paper with a novel and important contribution to the domain adaptation community. The proposed method is elegant, well-motivated, and supported by strong empirical evidence. The weakness regarding optimization details is minor and can be addressed. I strongly recommend acceptance.
