This paper addresses the fundamental problem of defining domains for visual domain adaptation. The authors argue that the common practice of treating entire datasets as single, monolithic domains is a flawed simplification, as datasets are often agglomerations of multiple latent domains corresponding to factors like lighting, pose, and background. To overcome this, the paper proposes a novel method to automatically "reshape" visual datasets by discovering these latent domains. The core idea is to partition data based on two principles: 1) "maximum distinctiveness," where the discovered domains should have maximally different data distributions, and 2) "maximum learnability," where each discovered domain should contain enough data to train a strong classifier.

Maximum distinctiveness is formulated as maximizing the sum of pairwise distances between domain distributions, measured non-parametrically using a kernel-based two-sample test statistic (MMD). This is posed as a constrained integer programming problem, with a crucial "label prior constraint" to prevent the algorithm from simply clustering by class. This problem is then relaxed for continuous optimization. The principle of maximum learnability is used to select the optimal number of domains, K, via a proposed "domain-wise cross-validation" (DWCV) procedure that chooses the K yielding the highest average cross-validation accuracy across the discovered domains. The paper's contribution is a significant departure from most domain adaptation literature, which typically assumes predefined source and target domains. It is most closely related to the work of Hoffman et al. (2012), but uses a non-parametric approach to model domain distributions, which is arguably more flexible.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a highly significant and foundational problem in domain adaptation. The critique of the "dataset-as-domain" paradigm is well-founded, and the proposed solution is a novel and principled step towards a more nuanced understanding of domains. If successful, this approach could change how researchers collect data and evaluate domain adaptation algorithms. The idea of automatically discovering an optimal data partition for adaptation is a strong contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The formulation based on maximizing distinctiveness and learnability is elegant and intuitive. Using a non-parametric kernel method (MMD) to measure distribution difference is appropriate and avoids restrictive distributional assumptions. The domain-wise cross-validation procedure for model selection (choosing K) is also a reasonable and well-justified heuristic.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The motivation is compelling, the proposed method is explained logically, and the connection to related work is well-articulated.

4.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincingly support the paper's claims.
    *   The use of both object and action recognition datasets demonstrates the method's general applicability.
    *   The "oracle" evaluation (Table 1) effectively shows that the discovered domains are inherently better for adaptation than the original dataset partitions.
    *   The experiments with practical multi-source adaptation methods (Table 2) demonstrate that these benefits translate to real-world scenarios where target labels are unavailable.
    *   The extension to reshaping the test set is a powerful idea and shows impressive gains on the IXMAS dataset, a very suitable application.
    *   The qualitative analysis in Figure 1 provides valuable insight into the kinds of visual factors the method is able to discover.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Scalability:** The proposed optimization involves computing and manipulating an M x M kernel matrix, where M is the total number of data instances. This scales quadratically in both memory and computation, which could make the method prohibitive for large-scale datasets that are common today. The paper does not discuss this limitation or potential solutions (e.g., using approximations like the Nystrom method or random Fourier features).

2.  **The Label Prior Constraint (LPC):** The assumption that class priors are constant across all latent domains is a strong one. While it is a clever way to prevent the trivial solution of clustering by class label, it might not hold in all real-world scenarios. For instance, a domain of "office scenes" might naturally have a different distribution of object classes than a domain of "street scenes." A discussion of the limitations imposed by this constraint would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Details of Optimization:** The paper notes that the relaxed optimization problem is still NP-hard and is solved using "off-the-shelf optimization packages." This is somewhat vague. Given that this is a non-concave maximization problem, it would be helpful to know which solver was used, whether the solution is a local or global optimum of the relaxed problem, and how sensitive the results are to initialization.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Arguments for acceptance:** The paper makes a strong conceptual and methodological contribution by questioning a fundamental assumption in domain adaptation and providing a principled algorithm to address it. The formulation is elegant, and the extensive experiments provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The work has high potential to be influential in the field.
*   **Arguments against acceptance:** The primary concerns are practical: the scalability of the method to large datasets is not addressed, and some algorithmic details regarding the optimization are not fully specified.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty and significance, far outweigh its weaknesses. It introduces a new and important way of thinking about the domain adaptation problem.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you please comment on the computational complexity of the proposed method and its scalability to larger datasets? Have you considered any kernel approximation techniques to mitigate the O(M^2) complexity?
2.  Could you elaborate on the `MATCHING` strategy used in the practical multi-source adaptation experiments? How is the best source domain selected to match a target domain without using target labels?
3.  Regarding the optimization of Eq. (4), could you specify the solver used? Since this is a non-convex problem, were there any issues with local optima, and how was initialization handled?
