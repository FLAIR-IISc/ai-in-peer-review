This paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discover latent domains in image or video datasets. The authors argue that the standard practice of equating datasets with domains in visual domain adaptation research is a weak proxy for the real conditions that alter the statistics in complex ways. They propose two key properties that latent domains should possess: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. The authors devise a nonparametric formulation and efficient optimization procedure that can successfully discover domains among both training and test data.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. The use of kernel-based methods to measure domain discrepancies and the introduction of the total domain distinctiveness (TDD) metric are particularly noteworthy. The authors also provide an extensive evaluation of their approach on object recognition and human activity recognition tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness in improving adaptation results.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to identify latent domains that are not necessarily aligned with the dataset boundaries. The authors show that their approach can discover domains that are determined by factors such as object pose, appearance, and background, which are not always controlled by the dataset collectors. This is a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation, as it allows for more accurate modeling of the underlying data distributions.

The paper also provides a thorough discussion of related work, including previous approaches to domain adaptation and clustering. The authors highlight the limitations of these approaches and demonstrate how their own approach overcomes these limitations.

One potential weakness of the paper is the reliance on the domain-wise cross-validation (DWCV) procedure for determining the number of domains. While the authors provide some justification for this approach, it is not entirely clear why this procedure is optimal. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed approach, particularly in comparison to other domain adaptation methods.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of domain adaptation. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness in improving adaptation results.
