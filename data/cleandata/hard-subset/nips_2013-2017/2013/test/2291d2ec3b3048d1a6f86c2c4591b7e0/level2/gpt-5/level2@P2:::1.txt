Summary
This paper addresses a practical gap in visual domain adaptation: datasets are commonly treated as domains, even though real “domains” arise from latent factors (pose, illumination, background, etc.). The authors propose to automatically reshape datasets into latent domains by optimizing two desiderata: maximum distinctiveness (maximize pairwise distributional discrepancy between domains via RKHS mean embeddings/MMD) and maximum learnability (choose the number of domains K via domain-wise cross-validation). The optimization enforces a label prior constraint (LPC) so that label proportions in each discovered domain match the global prior, preventing trivial per-class partitions. The approach is nonparametric and avoids explicit mixture models. Experiments on Office-like object recognition (Caltech-256, Amazon, DSLR, Webcam) and IXMAS multi-view action recognition show that the discovered domains improve downstream adaptation (using GFK) and that conditionally reshaping test sets to match training domains further helps in cross-view action recognition. Comparisons to Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12) suggest superior performance.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on kernel two-sample tests and MMD (Gretton et al., NIPS’07), and multi-source adaptation (e.g., Mansour et al., NIPS’09).
- Closest to Hoffman et al. (ECCV’12) on latent domain discovery via generative mixtures; authors’ nonparametric, discriminability-driven alternative is a clear contrast.
- Related to generalized clustering with discriminability criteria (Jegelka et al.), but here label information and an adaptation use case drive the formulation.
- Uses GFK (Gong et al., CVPR’12) for adaptation, making the gains attributable primarily to the domain discovery step.

Strengths
- Well-motivated problem with clear impact: replacing ad hoc dataset-as-domain practice with learned latent domains is important and broadly useful.
- Principled use of RKHS mean embeddings to quantify domain discrepancy; the TDD objective aligns with the goal of separating heterogeneous nuisance factors.
- Practical and intuitive model selection via domain-wise cross-validation (maximum learnability).
- Conditional reshaping of test data to align with discovered training domains is novel and practically compelling (especially for multi-view actions).
- Empirical results, as described, consistently favor the proposed method over original datasets and the Hoffman et al. baseline, with sensible ablations (UNION/ENSEMBLE/MATCHING).

Weaknesses and concerns
- LPC assumption: Enforcing identical label priors per discovered domain is strong and may not hold in realistic settings (e.g., certain views or lighting conditions correlate with specific categories). This constraint may bias the partitions and exclude valid domain shifts with label skew. A soft regularization or class-conditional formulation could be more appropriate.
- Optimization details and scalability: The relaxed problem maximizes a convex quadratic form with linear constraints, which is NP-hard in general. The paper states off-the-shelf solvers work well, but lacks details on the solver, initialization, runtime, and scaling to larger M (full kernel matrix O(M^2) memory/time). Without these, reproducibility and practicality are unclear.
- Objective design: TDD favors pushing domains apart in terms of P(x), not P(x|y). While LPC blocks per-class clustering, the method may still induce partitions along idiosyncratic appearance statistics that don’t best support category discrimination after adaptation. A discrepancy defined over class-conditional embeddings (e.g., sum_c MMD(P_k(x|y=c), P_k’(x|y=c))) could better align with discrimination while obviating LPC.
- Model selection via DWCV: Choosing K by within-domain cross-validation optimizes within-source predictability, not necessarily target adaptation. Although Figure 2 suggests correlation, this remains heuristic; a target-agnostic adaptation proxy (e.g., HΔH bounds or source-target discrepancy measures) could be more principled.
- Sensitivity: No analysis of sensitivity to kernel bandwidth, δ in LPC, or to feature representations. Since MMD is bandwidth-sensitive, robustness should be shown.
- Comparison breadth: Results hinge on GFK; it would help to demonstrate benefits across multiple adaptation backbones (e.g., TCA, CORAL/SA, subspace alignment, or earlier feature-transform methods) to show generality.
- Test-time reshaping: It appears to fix the number of test domains to K and match to training domains by minimizing P(x) discrepancy. This may be restrictive when test mixtures differ in cardinality or contain novel modes; discussion or experiments on mismatch would strengthen the case.

Quality
- Technically sound core idea and sensible optimization, but theoretical properties (e.g., identifiability, convergence behavior of the relaxation, robustness to LPC mis-specification) are not analyzed.
- Empirical validation is promising but would benefit from fuller details (solver, hyperparameters, runtime) and broader baselines.

Clarity
- Overall clear, with intuitive motivations. However, the translation from integer to relaxed variables (β), the rationale behind the bound 1/M ≤ Σ_k β_mk ≤ 1/C, and how it enforces Mk ∈ [C, M] should be clarified. Presenting the full relaxed program, solver, and algorithmic steps (including initialization) would help reproducibility.

Originality
- The framing via maximum distinctiveness + maximum learnability, the nonparametric MMD-based objective with LPC, and conditional reshaping of test data constitute a novel combination beyond prior latent domain discovery work.

Significance
- If robust and scalable, the method could alter how practitioners prepare source pools for adaptation and inspire test-time domain disentangling. The proposed analysis also informs dataset construction.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to kernel bandwidth and δ in LPC? Any data-driven bandwidth selection tried?
- What solver and initialization do you use for the QP? Complexity and runtimes for Office/IXMAS?
- How does performance change if LPC is replaced by a soft penalty, or if class-conditional MMD is used?
- Can you report results with another adaptation method to show robustness (e.g., TCA, SA/CORAL)?
- What happens when the test set contains a different number of latent domains than K?

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace hard LPC with a soft regularizer, or directly match class-conditional embeddings to prevent per-class clustering while allowing label skew.
- Provide algorithmic details, complexity, and scaling tricks (Nyström or random features for K, block-coordinate updates for β).
- Add sensitivity analyses and ablations (no LPC, varying δ; kernel bandwidth choices).
- Broaden comparisons to additional adaptation baselines; include runtime and memory usage.
- Explore a target-aware but label-free criterion for K (e.g., minimizing source-target discrepancy after domain discovery).
- For test-time reshaping, allow K_test ≠ K_train and include a model selection heuristic.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and underexplored problem with a principled, novel approach.
- Empirical gains are consistent and practically meaningful.
- Test-time conditional reshaping is a compelling and useful addition.

Cons against acceptance
- Strong LPC assumption and lack of sensitivity analysis.
- Limited optimization and scalability details; unclear reproducibility.
- Heuristic model selection and limited breadth of adaptation baselines.

Overall
A thoughtful and potentially impactful paper with a solid core idea and promising results. Addressing the LPC rigidity, adding optimization/scalability details, and expanding empirical analyses would strengthen the contribution.
