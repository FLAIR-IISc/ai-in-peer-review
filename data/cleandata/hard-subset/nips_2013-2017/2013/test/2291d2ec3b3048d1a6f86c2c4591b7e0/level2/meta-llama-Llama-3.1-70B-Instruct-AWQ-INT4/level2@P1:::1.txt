This paper proposes an approach to automatically discover latent domains in image or video datasets for domain adaptation in visual recognition problems. The authors introduce two desirable properties for the identified domains: maximum distinctiveness and maximum learnability. They develop a nonparametric formulation and efficient optimization procedure to discover domains that satisfy these properties.

The main idea of the paper is to reshape the datasets into domains that are maximally distinctive and learnable, rather than relying on the standard practice of equating datasets with domains. The authors argue that this approach can lead to better adaptation results, especially when the test datasets are also composed of heterogeneous domains.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of the problem, the proposed approach, and the experimental results. The experiments are extensive and cover both object recognition and human activity recognition tasks.

The strengths of the paper are:

* The authors propose a novel approach to domain adaptation that can handle heterogeneous datasets.
* The approach is nonparametric and does not rely on any specific distribution or model.
* The experiments are extensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The paper provides a clear explanation of the problem and the proposed approach.

The weaknesses of the paper are:

* The optimization problem is NP-hard, and the authors rely on a relaxation to make it tractable. However, the relaxation may not always lead to the optimal solution.
* The approach requires a good choice of kernel and kernel bandwidth, which can be problem-dependent.
* The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of the time complexity of the approach.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a new approach to domain adaptation that is different from existing methods. The idea of reshaping datasets into domains that are maximally distinctive and learnable is novel and has the potential to lead to better adaptation results.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in visual recognition, namely, domain adaptation. The approach has the potential to be applied to a wide range of applications, including object recognition, human activity recognition, and image classification.

In terms of quality, the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The experiments are extensive and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach. However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough analysis of the time complexity of the approach.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper for publication. The approach is novel, and the experiments demonstrate its effectiveness. However, the authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above, including the optimization problem and the choice of kernel and kernel bandwidth.
