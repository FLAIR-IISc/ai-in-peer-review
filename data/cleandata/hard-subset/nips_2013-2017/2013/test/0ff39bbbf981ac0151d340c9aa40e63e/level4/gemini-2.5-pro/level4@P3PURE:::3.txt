This paper proposes a two-step method for translingual representation learning, which first uses matrix completion to generate multilingual vectors and then applies Cross-Lingual Latent Semantic Indexing (CL-LSI) to these vectors.

**Quality and Significance**
The core idea is promising, as matrix completion is a well-controlled non-linear inference technique. If the results are shown to be robust, the contribution would be significant.

However, the robustness of the experimental results is a major concern. The reported gains may not stem from the superiority of the learned representations but rather from an artifact of the evaluation setup. Specifically, the linear SVM classifier is sensitive to feature scaling. The improved performance could simply be due to LSI producing a scaling that is more favorable to the SVM than the OPCA or CCA baselines.

To substantiate the central claims of the paper, I strongly urge the authors to validate their method using a classification model that is insensitive to monotonic rescaling of individual features, such as boosted decision trees. If the proposed method maintains its superior performance with such a classifier, the findings would be far more convincing and the conclusion that the representation itself is better would be justified.

**Novelty**
The proposed combination of matrix completion and CL-LSI for this task appears to be novel.

**Clarity**
The paper has some clarity issues that should be addressed.
First, the description on line 113 regarding the construction of a "unified document-term matrix" is ambiguous. It is unclear whether this matrix uses a single canonical term for all surface forms of an entity (e.g., "Merkel") or treats surface forms from different languages as distinct terms (e.g., "Merkelen" vs. "Merkelde"). This point requires clarification.

Second, the description of the experimental setup is fragmented, with key details distributed within the text and Table 1. This organization makes it difficult for the reader to fully grasp the experimental conditions.

**Conclusion**
In summary, the paper presents a novel and interesting idea. However, the central claim is not convincingly supported due to the potential for the results to be an artifact of the interaction between feature scaling and the choice of a linear SVM classifier. The paper would be significantly strengthened by re-evaluating the method with a classifier that is insensitive to such scaling effects to demonstrate the robustness of the approach.
