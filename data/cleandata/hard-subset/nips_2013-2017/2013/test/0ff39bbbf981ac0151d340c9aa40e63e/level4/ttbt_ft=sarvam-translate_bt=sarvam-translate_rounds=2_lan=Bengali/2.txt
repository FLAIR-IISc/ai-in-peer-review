Summary: A two-step approach for learning cross-lingual topic representation using matrix completion and latent semantic analysis techniques is presented. A projected gradient descent algorithm is used to optimize the matrix completion problem. Test results show that the proposed method performs better than the mono-lingual baseline and cross-lingual baselines in the sentiment analysis task of the parallel Amazon review dataset. Learning curves with unlabeled data of various sizes illustrate that the proposed algorithm learns highly accurate cross-lingual topic representation with a relatively small amount of parallel data.

Quality: The tests supporting the claim were conducted thoroughly. The proposed two-step learning algorithm was carefully compared with two baselines (plain bag-of-words, cross-lingual LSA) and two state-of-the-art cross-lingual dimensionality reduction algorithms.

Clarity: This paper is well-organized. All parameters (including how to select them) were written on the paper; the results are easy to reproduce. Just a small remark: I would suggest that the motivation for using matrix completion should be explained in the introduction (although it is described in section 3).

Originality: Although the task of learning cross-lingual representation is not new, the application of matrix completion to this task is novel, and it is clear that the proposed algorithm achieves better performance than previous methods in the literature.

Significance: The results of the two-step approach are consistently higher than those of other baselines. We can learn from this work that matrix completion can be used as a pre-processing for cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks, and such formulations may be effective in other tasks as well. This paper proposes a two-step approach for learning cross-lingual topic representation that performs matrix completion before latent semantic analysis in the cross-lingual sentiment classification task. Test results show that the proposed method performs better than the mono-lingual baseline and other cross-lingual baselines, including one of the state-of-the-art methods, and is stable even when trained on unlabeled parallel data of various sizes.
