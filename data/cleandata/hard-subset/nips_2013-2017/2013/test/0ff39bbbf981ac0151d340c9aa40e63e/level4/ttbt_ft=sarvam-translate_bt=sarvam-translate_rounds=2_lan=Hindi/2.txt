Summary: This paper presents a two-step method for learning multilingual topic representation using matrix completion and latent semantic analysis techniques. It utilizes an approximate gradient descent algorithm to optimize the matrix completion problem. Experimental results show that the proposed method performs better than the mono-lingual baseline and cross-lingual baseline on sentiment analysis tasks on the parallel Amazon review dataset. Learning curves with unlabeled data of various sizes demonstrate that the proposed algorithm learns highly accurate cross-lingual topic representation with a relatively small amount of parallel data.

Quality: The experiments to support the claim were well-conducted. The proposed two-step learning algorithm was carefully compared with two baselines (simple bag-of-words, cross-lingual LSA) and two state-of-the-art cross-lingual dimensionality reduction algorithms.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized. All parameters (including how to select them) were written in the paper; the results are easy to reproduce. Just a small note: I would suggest explaining the motivation for using matrix completion in the introduction (although it is described in Section 3).

Novelty: Although the task of learning cross-lingual representation is not new, the application of matrix completion to this task is novel, and it is clear that the proposed algorithm achieves better performance than previous methods in the literature.

Significance: The result of the two-step approach consistently exceeds that of other baselines. We can learn from this work that matrix completion can be used as a preprocessing for cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks, and such construction can be effective in other tasks as well. This paper proposes a two-step method for learning a cross-lingual topic representation that performs matrix completion before latent semantic analysis on a cross-lingual sentiment classification task. Experimental results show that the proposed method performs better than the mono-lingual baseline and other cross-lingual baselines, including one of the state-of-the-art methods, and remains stable even when trained on different sizes of unlabeled parallel data.
