This paper proposes a two-step method for translingual representation learning, which first uses matrix completion to create multilingual vectors and then applies Cross-Lingual Latent Semantic Indexing (CL-LSI) to the result.

**Quality:** While the proposed approach is intuitive, I have concerns about the robustness of the experimental results. The positive outcomes may be an artifact of the classifier choice; a linear SVM is sensitive to feature scaling, and it is possible that the superior performance reported stems from the scaling properties of LSI being more favorable than those of OPCA or CCA, rather than from a fundamentally better representation.

To validate the method's effectiveness, I strongly urge the authors to conduct experiments using a machine learning system that is insensitive to the rescaling of individual features, such as boosted decision trees. If the proposed two-step system still outperforms other techniques with such a classifier, the claims of the paper would be substantially strengthened.

**Significance:** If the results are proven to be robust, the contribution would be significant. The use of matrix completion, a well-controlled non-linear inference technique, in this context is a valuable approach.

**Novelty:** The proposed combination of matrix completion and CL-LSI for this task appears to be novel.

**Clarity:** The paper has some clarity issues that should be addressed.
-   Line 113 mentions the construction of "a unified document-term matrix for all documents." This phrase is ambiguous. Please clarify whether this means using a single canonical term for all surface forms (e.g., mapping "Merkelen" and "Merkelde" to "Merkel") or treating each surface form from each language as a distinct feature.
-   The description of the experimental setup is split between the main text and Table 1, which made it difficult to fully understand the methodology. Consolidating these details would improve the paper's readability.

In summary, the paper presents a neat idea. However, the current evaluation is insufficient to determine whether the result is robust or simply a scaling artifact. The conclusion would be far more convincing if supported by results from classifiers that are not sensitive to feature scaling.
