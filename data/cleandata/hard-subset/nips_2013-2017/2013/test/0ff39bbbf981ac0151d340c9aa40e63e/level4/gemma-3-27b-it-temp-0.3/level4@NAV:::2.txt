## Review:

**Summary:** This paper introduces a two-stage approach for learning cross-lingual topic representations, leveraging matrix completion followed by latent semantic analysis. The matrix completion problem is addressed using a projected gradient descent algorithm. Evaluation on a parallel Amazon review dataset for sentiment analysis demonstrates that the proposed method surpasses both monolingual and existing cross-lingual baselines. Furthermore, learning curves demonstrate the algorithm’s ability to learn accurate cross-lingual topic representations even with limited parallel data.

**Quality:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and effectively supports the authors’ claims. The proposed two-step learning algorithm undergoes careful comparison with two baselines – a standard bag-of-words approach and cross-lingual LSA – as well as two state-of-the-art cross-lingual dimensionality reduction techniques.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly presented. All parameters and their selection are thoroughly documented, facilitating reproducibility. A minor suggestion would be to expand on the motivation for employing matrix completion within the Introduction (although it is adequately described in Section 3).

**Originality:** While cross-lingual representation learning is an established field, the application of matrix completion to this problem is a novel contribution. The results clearly demonstrate that the proposed algorithm achieves superior performance compared to existing methods.

**Significance:** The consistently higher performance of the two-step approach compared to the baselines is noteworthy. This work highlights the potential of matrix completion as a valuable preprocessing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification and suggests that this formulation may be applicable to other tasks as well. In essence, the paper presents a two-step method – matrix completion followed by latent semantic analysis – for cross-lingual sentiment classification. Experimental results confirm its superior performance over monolingual and other cross-lingual baselines, including a state-of-the-art method, and its robustness across varying amounts of unlabeled parallel data.
