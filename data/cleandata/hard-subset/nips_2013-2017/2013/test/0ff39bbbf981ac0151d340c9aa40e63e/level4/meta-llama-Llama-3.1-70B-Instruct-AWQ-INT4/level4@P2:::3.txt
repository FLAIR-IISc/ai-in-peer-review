The paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross language representation learning, which is a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing. The approach involves first formulating a matrix completion problem to produce a complete parallel document-term matrix for all documents in two languages, and then inducing a low dimensional cross-lingual document representation by applying latent semantic indexing on the obtained matrix.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed method through extensive experiments on cross language sentiment classification tasks on multilingual Amazon product reviews. The results show that the proposed method significantly outperforms several other cross language representation learning methods, especially when the number of parallel bilingual documents is small.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and detailed description of their approach. However, I would like to see more robust evaluation of the method, including its sensitivity to different classification models and feature scaling. The use of linear SVM as the classification model may not be the most robust choice, as it is sensitive to feature scaling. I would recommend using a classification model that is insensitive to rescalings of the individual feature, such as boosted decision trees.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to improve cross language text classification tasks, which is an important problem in natural language processing. The use of matrix completion as a non-linear inference technique is a well-controlled approach that can provide robust results.

The novelty of the paper is high, as the authors propose a new approach to cross language representation learning that combines matrix completion and latent semantic indexing.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but there are some issues that need to be addressed. For example, the description of the experimental setup is split across Table 1, which makes it difficult to follow. Additionally, some of the technical terms, such as "unified document-term matrix," need to be clarified.

Overall, I believe that the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing, but it needs to be revised to address some of the issues mentioned above.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to cross language representation learning that combines matrix completion and latent semantic indexing.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their proposed method through extensive experiments on cross language sentiment classification tasks.
* The use of matrix completion as a non-linear inference technique is a well-controlled approach that can provide robust results.

Arguments for rejection:

* The paper needs more robust evaluation of the method, including its sensitivity to different classification models and feature scaling.
* The use of linear SVM as the classification model may not be the most robust choice.
* Some of the technical terms need to be clarified, and the description of the experimental setup needs to be improved.
