- Paper proposes a two-step cross-domain classification: low-rank + sparse matrix completion via projected gradient descent, then LSI for dimensionality reduction and classifier training, with convergence guarantee and positive experiments.  
- Clarify why LSI is used to reduce M from d to k — is it for computational convenience, and why prefer LSI over alternatives like PCA?  
- Explain whether M* is expected to be dense despite sparsity enforcement and how that motivates dimensionality reduction.  
- Separate and quantify the contributions of labeled source data versus unlabeled parallel data (provide separate experimental results).  
- Clarify experimental setup: training/test splits (4,000 source labeled, 100 target labeled, 2,000 unlabeled, 1,900 test), the varying unlabeled sizes (200–2,000), and reconcile differences in CL-KCCA and TSL results between Figure 1 and Table 1.  
- Justify the low-rank assumption for a fully observed document-term matrix despite sparsity, since sparsity does not imply low-rank and documents are not generally linear combinations of others.  
- Provide intuition for why TSL outperforms other methods (e.g., CCA) and how the low-rank constraint on M* yields advantages compared to alternative techniques.  
- Report algorithm running time and computational cost comparisons of projected gradient descent versus CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA.  
- Minor edits: correct notation (ij) → (i,j) before Eq. (2) and remove "such as" before Eqs. (4) and (5).  
- Overall: method is simple and effective but requires technical clarifications to improve clarity and impact.
