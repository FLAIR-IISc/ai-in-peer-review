The paper proposes a method for cross-lingual representation learning by first using matrix completion to create multilingual vectors for each document, and then performing latent semantic indexing (LSI) on these representations. 

Regarding quality, while the approach appears promising, the robustness of the results warrants further investigation. The observed performance gains could potentially be attributed to the scaling properties of LSI being particularly well-suited for use with a linear support vector machine (SVM), rather than inherent improvements derived from the method itself. To strengthen the claims, I recommend that the authors evaluate their method using a machine learning system that is invariant to feature rescaling, such as boosted decision trees (readily available in Weka, for example). Demonstrating superior performance with boosted trees would lend greater confidence to the reported results.

Concerning significance, a robust and consistently positive outcome would be valuable, as matrix completion offers a controlled and non-linear approach to inference.

The method presents a novel combination of techniques. The concept of leveraging matrix completion as a preprocessing step for cross-lingual representation learning is not commonly found in the literature.

However, the clarity of the presentation could be improved. For instance, the description on line 113 regarding the construction of the "unified document-term matrix" is ambiguous. It is unclear whether this involves utilizing a single term for each surface form or explicitly labeling each surface form per language (e.g., differentiating "Merkel" in English versus its equivalents in other languages). Clarification on this point is needed. Additionally, the experimental setup is dispersed across Table 1, making it slightly challenging to fully grasp the methodology at first read. 

Overall, the work presents an interesting idea. However, further validation with alternative classifiers – beyond the linear SVM – is crucial to ascertain the robustness of the results and mitigate concerns regarding potential scaling artifacts.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The approach is novel, combining matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning.
*   Experimental results show promising performance gains over baseline methods.
*   The method appears effective even with a limited number of parallel bilingual documents.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The robustness of the results is uncertain; performance gains may be due to the specific choice of classifier (linear SVM).
*   Clarity issues exist in the description of the method and experimental setup.
