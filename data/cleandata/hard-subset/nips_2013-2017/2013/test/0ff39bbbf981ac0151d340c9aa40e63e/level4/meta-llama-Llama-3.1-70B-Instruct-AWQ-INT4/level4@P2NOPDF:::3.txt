The paper proposes a novel method for translingual representation learning, combining matrix completion and CL-LSI to create multilingual vectors. 

Quality: The results appear promising, but their robustness is uncertain. The observed improvements could be attributed to the scaling properties of LSI, which might be more suitable for linear SVM than OPCA or CCA. To alleviate these concerns, the authors are encouraged to experiment with machine learning systems that are invariant to individual feature rescalings, such as boosted decision trees (available in Weka). If the proposed two-step system outperforms other techniques using boosted decision trees, the results would be more convincing.

Significance: If the results are indeed robust, they would be noteworthy, as matrix completion is a well-controlled non-linear inference technique. The potential impact of this approach on translingual representation learning would be substantial.

Novelty: The proposed method appears to be novel, offering a fresh perspective on translingual representation learning.

Clarity: While the paper presents an interesting idea, there are some clarity issues that need to be addressed. Specifically, the description of the unified document-term matrix construction process in line 113 is unclear. It would be beneficial to clarify whether the authors used a single term for every surface form (e.g., "Merkel") or labeled each surface form from each language (e.g., "Merkelen" and "Merkelde"). Additionally, the experimental setup description is fragmented across Table 1, making it challenging to follow. The authors are encouraged to reorganize and clarify these sections to improve the paper's overall readability.

Overall, the idea presented in the paper is intriguing, and the authors are encouraged to further investigate its robustness by experimenting with different classifiers, such as boosted decision trees, to confirm the results are not merely a scaling artifact.
