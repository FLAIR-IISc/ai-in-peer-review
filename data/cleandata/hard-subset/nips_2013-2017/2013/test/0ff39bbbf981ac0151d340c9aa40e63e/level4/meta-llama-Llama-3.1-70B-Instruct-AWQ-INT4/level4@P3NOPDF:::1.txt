The paper proposes a novel matrix completion approach to tackle the cross-domain classification task, effectively leveraging labeled data from an auxiliary domain and unlabeled parallel data. This approach involves a two-step process. First, it constructs a single incomplete matrix encompassing all documents and domains, which is then completed by enforcing low-rank and sparsity conditions using the projected gradient descent algorithm. Second, the feature dimension of the completed matrix is reduced using Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI), and a standard classifier is trained on this new representation. The paper also provides a convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm and presents favorable experimental results.

The authors have presented a clear and effective method, although some technical points require clarification. One such point is the reduction of feature dimension from d to k using LSI after estimating the completed matrix M. It would be beneficial to elaborate on the reasoning behind this step, specifically why the completed matrix M lacks sufficient capacity to handle feature sparseness. Is this reduction primarily driven by computational convenience, given that M* will be relatively dense even with sparsity enforcement? Furthermore, the choice of LSI over other dimensionality reduction techniques, such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA), warrants explanation.

The paper appears to tackle two distinct aspects of exploiting external data: (1) leveraging labeled source language data to aid in learning labeled target language data, and (2) leveraging unlabeled parallel data. It would be helpful to clearly distinguish between these two aspects and report results separately to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the approach's strengths.

The experiment settings in Section 5.2 are somewhat unclear. To confirm, the training data consisted of (a) 4k labeled source language documents, (b) 100 labeled target language documents, and (c) 2k unlabeled parallel documents. The classification results were then reported on the remaining 1900 labeled target language documents. In Section 5.3, the only difference appears to be the variation in the number of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2k. However, it is unclear why the performance of CL-KCCA is better than TSL in Figure 1 for EFM and EGM at 2k, but not in Table 1.

The authors should provide a brief explanation for why a fully observed document-term matrix would be low-rank. While the matrix is sparse, it is likely that no document is a linear combination of other documents in the vocabulary, resulting in a rank equal to the minimum of d and k.

It would be beneficial to gain insight into why TSL generally outperforms other methods, particularly CCA. A multiview approach like CCA seems like a natural fit for this cross-language representation learning task. In contrast, the matrix completion approach is less intuitive, with the primary driving force being the enforcement of the low-rank condition on M*. A comparison with other techniques would be helpful in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of this approach.

Additionally, information on the running time of the algorithm would be valuable. Specifically, how does the projected gradient descent algorithm compare to other methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA in terms of computational time?

Minor corrections are suggested: fixing "(ij)" to "(i,j)" two sentences before equation (2), and removing "such as" in the sentences before equations (4) and (5). Overall, the paper presents a simple, effective, and well-presented method, although some technical points require clarification.
