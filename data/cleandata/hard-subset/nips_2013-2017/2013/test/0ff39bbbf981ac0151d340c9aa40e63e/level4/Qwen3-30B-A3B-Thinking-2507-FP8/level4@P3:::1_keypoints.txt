- Proposes a two-step cross-language method: complete a partially observed bilingual document-term matrix via low-rank sparse recovery (projected gradient descent) then apply LSI for cross-lingual representations.
- Novelty: differs from prior CL-LSI/CL-KCCA and translation-based methods by performing matrix completion before dimensionality reduction.
- Technical strength: method is sound, with rigorous convergence analysis for the optimizer.
- Empirical strength: thorough experiments (18 tasks, 4 languages × 3 categories) showing consistent improvements, especially with limited parallel data (200 documents).
- Practical significance: substantial gains over baseline (8.59–14.61%) and value for low-resource language scenarios.
- Clarity: paper is well-organized and reproducible with clear presentation of technical details and experiments.
- Weakness — LSI justification: insufficient explanation why LSI is used (noise reduction vs. computational efficiency) and why chosen over alternatives like PCA.
- Weakness — low-rank assumption: the claim that the full document-term matrix is naturally low-rank lacks theoretical or empirical support.
- Weakness — experimental clarity: ambiguity about number of labeled documents used per language and apparent discrepancies between figures and tables need clarification.
- Weakness — comparison to multiview methods: limited intuition/explanation for why matrix completion outperforms multiview approaches such as CL-KCCA beyond invoking low-rankness.
- Overall: technically solid and significant contribution; minor revisions to clarify assumptions and experiments recommended; reviewer recommends accept.
- Pros summarized: novel two-step approach, strong empirical results, convergence guarantees, effective with minimal parallel data.
- Cons summarized: need stronger LSI/low-rank justification, clearer experimental description, and better comparison to multiview methods.
