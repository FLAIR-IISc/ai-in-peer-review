Summary: This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that combines matrix completion with latent semantic indexing (LSI). The key idea is to build a unified document–term matrix over the concatenated vocabularies of two languages, where entries for non-parallel documents are missing in the other language’s features. The authors formulate a convex matrix completion problem with a trace-norm penalty (to encourage low rank), an ℓ1 term (to encourage sparsity), and nonnegativity constraints, along with squared-loss penalties for both observed entries and (regularized) missing entries. They solve this problem with a projected gradient descent algorithm that alternates a gradient step, singular value shrinkage, and projection, and provide convergence guarantees. After completion, they apply LSI (via SVD) to obtain a low-dimensional, shared cross-lingual representation on which standard monolingual classifiers can be trained. Experiments on cross-lingual sentiment classification using multilingual Amazon reviews show consistent improvements over a monolingual target baseline and strong cross-lingual baselines, including CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA. Learning curves demonstrate that the proposed method is particularly effective when only a small number of unlabeled parallel documents are available, addressing a common constraint in practice. This approach is clearly positioned relative to prior work that either translates data or directly applies LSI/KCCA/OPCA to concatenated parallel data; here, matrix completion is used first to recover a more informative bilingual matrix before dimensionality reduction.

Quality: The experiments are thorough and support the main claims. The method is compared against a monolingual bag-of-words baseline and three established cross-lingual representation learning methods (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA). The evaluation spans 18 language/category transfer tasks with repeated trials and reports averages and standard deviations. Hyperparameter selection is described, and the same settings are used across tasks, lending credibility to the reported robustness. The optimization procedure is clearly specified, and the convergence analysis is provided.

Clarity: The paper is well organized and, in general, clearly written. The algorithmic details (objective, gradients, shrinkage operator, projection, step sizes) and parameter-selection procedure are explicit, which aids reproducibility. Minor suggestion: the Introduction would benefit from a more explicit motivation for using matrix completion before LSI—e.g., why completing the bilingual document–term matrix should better capture cross-lingual co-occurrence structure than applying LSI directly to the sparse, partially observed matrix (this rationale appears in Section 3 but could be highlighted earlier).

Originality: While cross-lingual representation learning is well studied, applying matrix completion to a unified bilingual document–term matrix prior to LSI is a novel and well-justified contribution. The approach is distinct from prior methods that rely solely on translation, bilingual lexicons, or directly applying LSI/KCCA/OPCA to concatenated parallel data. The projected gradient descent formulation with trace-norm and ℓ1 regularization under nonnegativity constraints is also carefully adapted to this setting.

Significance: The two-step method yields consistent and often substantial gains over strong baselines across many transfer directions and domains, and it remains effective with limited parallel data. This suggests matrix completion can serve as a powerful preprocessing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification and potentially for other cross-lingual NLP tasks where parallel data are scarce. The demonstrated robustness and practical setup (small parallel sets, standard classifiers) make the contribution likely to be useful to both researchers and practitioners.
