This paper proposes a matrix completion approach to cross-domain classification that exploits both labeled data in an auxiliary domain and unlabeled parallel data. The method has two stages. First, it constructs a single incomplete matrix spanning all documents and domains, and completes it by enforcing low-rank and sparsity constraints via a projected gradient descent algorithm. Second, it applies LSI to reduce the dimensionality of the completed matrix and trains a standard classifier in the reduced space. The paper also provides a convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent procedure and reports favorable experimental results.

Comments and questions:
- After estimating the completed matrix M, you reduce the feature dimension from d to k using LSI because M “lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness.” Could you elaborate? Is the main motivation computational—i.e., M* is relatively dense despite sparsity regularization, so you prefer a compact representation for efficiency and generalization? If so, why choose LSI specifically over other dimensionality reduction methods (e.g., PCA), and how sensitive are results to this choice?
- The paper appears to leverage two distinct sources of external data: (1) labeled source-language data to help learn a classifier for the target language, and (2) unlabeled parallel data. If this reading is correct, it would help to clearly separate these two contributions in the exposition and to report their effects independently (e.g., ablations that isolate the benefit of labeled source data vs. unlabeled parallel data).
- The experimental setup in Section 5.2 is somewhat unclear. Am I correct that training uses (a) 4k labeled source-language documents, (b) 100 labeled target-language documents, and (c) 2k unlabeled parallel documents, and that test performance is reported on the remaining 1,900 labeled target-language documents? Then, in Section 5.3, the only change is that you vary the number of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2k. If so, how do you explain the discrepancy where CL-KCCA outperforms TSL in Figure 1 for EFM and EGM at 2k, but not in Table 1?
- Can you clarify why a fully observed document–term matrix would be low rank? Such matrices are sparse, but in general documents are not linear combinations of other documents in the vocabulary basis, so the rank would typically be min(d, k). What structural assumptions (e.g., latent topic models) justify a low-rank prior in this setting, and how does that assumption interact with sparsity?
- Please provide intuition for why TSL tends to outperform other methods, especially CCA-based approaches. CCA/multiview methods seem a natural fit for cross-lingual representation learning, while matrix completion is less obvious. Is the main advantage the low-rank constraint on M*, and if so, why is that constraint particularly effective here compared with, say, CCA’s correlation maximization or other cross-lingual projections?
- What is the running time of the algorithm in practice? How does the cost of projected gradient descent compare to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA in terms of time per iteration, number of iterations to convergence, and overall wall-clock time? Please include complexity in terms of the number of documents, vocabulary size, and number of nonzeros, and report actual runtimes on your datasets.

Suggested edits:
- Replace (ij) with (i, j) two sentences before Equation (2).
- Remove “such as” in the sentences before Equations (4) and (5).

Overall, the paper presents a simple and effective method for cross-domain classification via matrix completion. While several technical points need clarification—especially the rationale for post-completion dimensionality reduction, the precise experimental setup, and the justification for the low-rank assumption—the approach is generally well presented and the empirical results are promising.
