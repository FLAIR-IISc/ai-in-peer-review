This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, addressing the challenge of disjoint feature spaces in cross-lingual text classification. The core idea is to leverage both labeled data in a source language and unlabeled parallel documents to bridge the language gap. The method first constructs an incomplete document-term matrix and completes it via a matrix completion technique with low-rank and sparsity constraints, solved using a projected gradient descent algorithm with a proven convergence guarantee. Subsequently, latent semantic indexing (LSI) is applied to reduce dimensionality and generate cross-lingual document representations for classification. The authors demonstrate strong performance on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks with Amazon product reviews, outperforming several baseline methods, particularly when parallel data is limited.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly presented, and tackles a relevant problem in natural language processing. The proposed approach is conceptually simple yet effective, and the inclusion of a convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm is a positive aspect. The experimental results are convincing, showing consistent improvements over baseline methods. The paper builds upon existing work in cross-lingual learning, such as machine translation-based methods [18, 6, 23], bilingual lexicon approaches [16], and cross-lingual representation learning techniques like CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], and CL-OPCA [15], and clearly positions itself within this landscape.

However, several points require further clarification. The rationale for applying LSI *after* matrix completion to address feature sparseness could be more thoroughly explained. While the authors mention it, elaborating on *why* LSI is preferred over other dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., PCA) would strengthen the argument. The paper appears to combine two distinct aspects of leveraging external data – utilizing labeled source language data and leveraging unlabeled parallel data – and it would be beneficial to explicitly disentangle these and present results for each aspect separately. 

The experimental setup (Section 5.2) requires more detailed clarification. Specifically, confirming the precise number of labeled and unlabeled documents used for training and testing would improve reproducibility. The discrepancy in performance between Table 1 and Figure 1 for CL-KCCA on the EFM and EGM tasks at 2k parallel documents should be addressed. A brief explanation as to why a low-rank assumption is reasonable for a completed document-term matrix would be helpful, considering its inherent sparsity. The intuition behind the superior performance of TSL compared to methods like CCA, a seemingly natural approach for cross-lingual representation learning, needs further elaboration. Specifically, how does enforcing the low-rank constraint on the completed matrix contribute to performance gains compared to the projection-based methods? Finally, providing information about the runtime of the algorithm, and comparing it to the computational cost of competing methods, would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results demonstrating superior performance compared to several baselines.
*   Clear and concise presentation of the method.
*   Theoretical guarantee of convergence for the optimization algorithm.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in cross-lingual learning.
*   Novel combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of detailed explanation for the choice of LSI over alternative dimensionality reduction techniques.
*   Ambiguity in the experimental setup and some inconsistencies in the reported results.
*   Insufficient intuition regarding the performance advantage of the proposed method over existing approaches like CCA.
*   Missing runtime analysis.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some areas requiring clarification, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. The proposed method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. The strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on the rationale behind using LSI, clarify the experimental setup, provide a more intuitive explanation for the method’s performance, and include runtime information.
