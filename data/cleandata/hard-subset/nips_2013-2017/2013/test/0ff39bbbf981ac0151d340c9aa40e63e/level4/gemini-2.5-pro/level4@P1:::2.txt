### **Summary**

This paper addresses the problem of cross-language text classification, where labeled data is scarce in a target language but abundant in a source language. The authors propose a novel two-step method for learning a shared cross-lingual document representation by leveraging unlabeled parallel documents. First, they construct a unified document-term matrix for all documents in both languages. For non-parallel documents, the term entries for the other language are considered missing. The core idea is to treat this as a matrix completion problem, recovering a complete, low-rank matrix by filling in these missing entries. This is formulated as a convex optimization problem with trace norm and L1 regularization, which is solved using a projected gradient descent algorithm with proven convergence guarantees. In the second step, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to the completed matrix to induce a dense, low-dimensional cross-lingual representation. This shared representation can then be used to train a standard monolingual classifier. The method is evaluated on 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks using the multilingual Amazon product reviews dataset, demonstrating superior performance over several existing methods, especially when the amount of parallel data is limited.

### **Evaluation**

#### **Quality**

The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The proposed two-step learning algorithm is carefully evaluated against appropriate baselines, including a monolingual classifier trained on target data (TBOW), Cross-Lingual LSI (CL-LSI), and two other strong methods, CL-OPCA and CL-KCCA. The convergence of the proposed optimization algorithm is formally proven. The experimental results consistently show the superiority of the proposed method across numerous language pairs and data domains. Furthermore, the authors include an important ablation study on the impact of the amount of parallel data, which provides strong evidence for the claim that their method is particularly effective in low-resource settings.

#### **Clarity**

The paper is well-written and organized. The proposed method is described clearly, from the initial problem formulation through the optimization algorithm and the final LSI step. The authors provide sufficient detail about their experimental setup, including the dataset, evaluation tasks, and hyperparameter selection process, which should make it straightforward for others to reproduce their results. 

One minor suggestion for improvement would be to more explicitly state the motivation for using matrix completion in the introduction. While this is well-explained in Section 3, highlighting the intuition earlier—that matrix completion can leverage co-occurrence patterns both within and across languages to effectively "translate" documents into a shared vocabulary space—could further strengthen the paper's opening.

#### **Originality**

While the problem of learning cross-lingual representations is well-established, the specific approach presented here is novel. The key innovation lies in applying matrix completion to the unified document-term matrix *before* applying a dimensionality reduction technique like LSI. Previous methods such as CL-LSI apply LSI directly to the concatenated matrix (often treating missing values as zeros), which can lose significant information. By first recovering a complete, low-rank matrix, the proposed method is able to better capture the underlying semantic relationships. This framing of the problem is a novel and effective combination of existing techniques. The paper also clearly situates its contribution within the context of related work.

#### **Significance**

The results presented in this paper are significant. The proposed two-step approach consistently and substantially outperforms the baseline and state-of-the-art comparison methods on a standard benchmark dataset. A particularly important finding is the method's robustness to the amount of available parallel data. It achieves strong performance even with a very small number of parallel documents, a scenario where other methods degrade significantly. This makes the approach highly valuable for practical applications where large parallel corpora are not available. This work effectively demonstrates that matrix completion can serve as a powerful preprocessing step for cross-lingual learning tasks, and this formulation is promising for extension to other cross-lingual problems beyond sentiment classification.
