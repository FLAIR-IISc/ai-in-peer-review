

Summary: This paper introduces a novel two-step approach for cross-lingual topic representation learning, combining matrix completion with latent semantic analysis (LSA). The method employs projected gradient descent to solve the matrix completion problem and demonstrates superior performance over monolingual baselines and cross-lingual baselines on sentiment analysis using the parallel Amazon review dataset. Notably, learning curves across varying amounts of unlabeled parallel data show the algorithm achieves high accuracy with minimal parallel data requirements.  

Quality: Experiments are comprehensive and well-designed, providing strong empirical support for the claims. The proposed two-step framework is rigorously compared against two monolingual baselines (bag-of-words), two cross-lingual baselines (including cross-lingual LSA), and two state-of-the-art cross-lingual dimensionality reduction methods. Results consistently demonstrate performance gains across all evaluation metrics.  

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and logically organized. All critical parameters and their selection criteria are explicitly described, ensuring reproducibility. A minor suggestion: the motivation for using matrix completion should be expanded in the Introduction (currently detailed only in Section 3) to better contextualize the methodological choice.  

Originality: While cross-lingual representation learning is established, the application of matrix completion to this domain is novel. The work clearly establishes that the proposed method outperforms all prior approaches in the literature for cross-lingual sentiment classification.  

Significance: The two-step framework yields consistently higher accuracy than all baselines, including a state-of-the-art method. This demonstrates that matrix completion can effectively preprocess data for cross-lingual sentiment tasks, with potential applicability to other cross-lingual NLP problems. The key insight—that matrix completion followed by LSA enhances cross-lingual representation learning—is both practical and broadly relevant.  

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and clearly articulated contribution to cross-lingual representation learning. The method’s efficiency (requiring limited parallel data) and strong empirical results make it a valuable addition to the field. The minor clarity improvement suggested would further strengthen the manuscript.
