- Task and method: Proposes TSL — a two-step cross-language method: matrix completion to impute a unified document-term matrix, then LSI to derive a shared low-dimensional representation for classification.  
- Originality: Novel formulation of cross-lingual representation learning as a matrix completion problem.  
- Empirical significance: Strong improvements over baselines across 18 tasks, especially robust with few unlabeled parallel documents (low-resource scenarios).  
- Technical quality: Uses a projected gradient descent algorithm with a formal convergence guarantee.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally well-written and the method is clearly explained.  
- Low-rank assumption concern: The claim that a fully observed document-term matrix is "naturally low-rank" needs stronger justification.  
- Comparison to multi-view methods: The paper should better explain why matrix completion (low-rank enforcement) is preferable to CCA-style multi-view alignment and what inductive bias it introduces.  
- Role of LSI: Need clearer motivation for the second LSI step — whether it’s primarily for noise reduction, dimensionality reduction, or other semantic benefits, and why LSI is chosen over alternatives.  
- Experimental setup clarity: Clarify exact training/test splits and data used (e.g., numbers of labeled source, labeled target, and unlabeled parallel documents).  
- Result inconsistency: Resolve discrepancy where Table 1 shows TSL beating CL-KCCA on EFM/EGM but Figure 1 shows CL-KCCA slightly better at 2000 parallel documents.  
- Ablation needed: Include ablation studies isolating the effects of labeled source data versus unlabeled parallel data.  
- Computational cost: Provide analysis of runtime and scaling of the projected gradient algorithm versus baselines (documents × vocabulary size).  
- Minor edits: Fix index notation "(ij)" → "(i, j)" and rephrase the sentence in Section 4.1 for clarity.  
- Overall verdict: Novel, simple, and effective approach with significant low-resource gains, but strengthened by addressing low-rank justification, experimental details, and computational costs.
