The paper proposes a two-step approach to translingual representation learning: first applying matrix completion to obtain multilingual vectors for each row of the document–term matrix, and then performing CL-LSI on these vectors.

Quality: The idea is promising, but I am not convinced the empirical gains are robust. The positive results might simply reflect that LSI’s scaling is a better match than OPCA or CCA for a linear SVM. To rule out this confound, please evaluate with a classifier that is insensitive to per-feature rescaling, such as boosted decision trees (available in Weka). If boosted trees on top of the two-step pipeline outperform the alternatives, I would find the conclusions convincing.

Significance: If the improvements hold under such tests, the result would be valuable, since matrix completion is a well-controlled non-linear inference method.

Novelty: The approach appears novel.

Clarity: A few points were unclear.
- Line 113 states that you “construct a unified document–term matrix for all documents.” Does this mean you use a single term for each surface form across languages (e.g., treating “Merkel” as one feature), or do you keep language-specific variants as distinct features (e.g., “Merkelen,” “Merkelde”)? Please clarify precisely how the vocabulary is constructed and shared across languages.
- The description of the experimental setup is fragmented across Table 1; it took me some time to piece it together. Consider consolidating the setup details in the text and using the table only for summary statistics.

Overall: This is a neat idea. However, I would have liked to see results with classifiers other than a linear SVM. As it stands, I cannot tell whether the gains reflect a genuine advantage of the representation or a scaling artifact.
