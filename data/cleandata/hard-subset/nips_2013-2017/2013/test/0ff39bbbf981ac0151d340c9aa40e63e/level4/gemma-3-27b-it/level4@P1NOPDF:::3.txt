The paper proposes a method for translingual representation learning, first employing matrix completion to generate multilingual vectors for each document, and then applying Canonical Latent Semantic Indexing (CL-LSI) to these vectors.

Quality: While the approach appears promising, I am not entirely convinced by the robustness of the results. The observed improvements could potentially be attributed to the scaling properties of LSI being more favorable than those of Orthogonal Procrustes Canonical Correlation Analysis (OPCA) or Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) when used with a linear SVM. 

I recommend the authors evaluate their system using a machine learning model that is insensitive to feature scaling, such as boosted decision trees (readily available in Weka, for example). If the two-step system, when coupled with boosted decision trees, outperforms other techniques, I would be more confident in the validity of the results. 

Significance: If the results prove to be robust, this would be a noteworthy contribution, as matrix completion provides a well-defined, non-linear inference technique.

Novelty: The core idea appears to be novel.

Clarity: There are some points where the paper’s clarity could be improved. For instance, line 113 discusses “constructing a unified document-term matrix for all documents.” It is unclear whether this refers to using a single term to represent all surface forms (e.g., “Merkel”), or to treating each surface form from each language as a distinct term (e.g., “Merkelen” and “Merkelde”). Clarification on this point would be helpful. Additionally, the experimental setup is described somewhat fragmentedly across Table 1, which made it initially difficult to grasp the full details. 

Overall, this seems like a promising idea. I would have liked to see the authors explore classifiers beyond the linear SVM – my main concern is determining whether the observed performance gains are genuinely robust or simply an artifact of feature scaling.
