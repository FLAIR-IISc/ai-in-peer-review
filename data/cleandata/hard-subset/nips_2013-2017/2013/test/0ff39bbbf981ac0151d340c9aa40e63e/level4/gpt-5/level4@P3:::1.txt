Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles cross-language text classification by learning a shared representation from a small set of parallel bilingual documents. The key idea is a two-step procedure: (1) construct a unified bilingual document–term matrix in the concatenated vocabulary; treat parallel pairs as fully observed rows and non-parallel documents as partially observed; then complete this matrix by solving a convex, nonnegative matrix completion problem with nuclear-norm and sparsity regularization, optimized via a projected gradient method with singular value shrinkage and convergence guarantees; (2) apply latent semantic indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation on which a standard monolingual classifier (linear SVM) is trained.

The work is positioned against two main lines of prior cross-lingual classification: (a) translation-based pipelines (machine translation or lexicons) followed by monolingual learning or domain adaptation; and (b) methods that directly learn shared representations, such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, SCL, and multilingual topic models. Unlike CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, or CL-OPCA that operate on paired documents or concatenated matrices directly, this paper first imputes missing cross-language features via matrix completion, then performs LSI. Empirical evaluation on multilingual Amazon reviews (English, French, German, Japanese; three domains) shows consistent gains over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, particularly when the number of parallel documents is small.

Strengths
- Simple and generally applicable approach: the two-step pipeline is easy to reproduce and to plug into existing monolingual classifiers.
- Sound optimization: convex formulation with nonnegativity, nuclear norm, and sparsity; clear algorithmic description and convergence proof building on established results.
- Strong empirical performance: consistent improvements over competitive baselines across 18 transfer settings, with especially good robustness when parallel data is scarce.
- Sensible experimental protocol: model selection done once and reused across tasks; comparisons against widely used baselines.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Rationale for the LSI step: After completion, the paper states that using M* “lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness,” motivating LSI. Please elaborate. Is the primary goal noise reduction and improved generalization, or computational efficiency given that M* may still be high-dimensional/dense? Why LSI over alternatives (e.g., PCA on TF-IDF, NMF, or directly learning a low-rank factorization during completion)? An ablation reporting performance with and without LSI, and with PCA/NMF, would clarify its contribution.
- Low-rank assumption: The paper states a “fully observed document–term matrix is naturally low-rank.” While topic structure often implies approximate low rank, a document–term matrix can be full rank in principle. Please rephrase and/or justify the assumption empirically (e.g., singular value decay plots), and clarify how the nuclear norm translates to better cross-lingual alignment than, say, purely co-variance-based methods.
- Role of labeled and unlabeled data: The method leverages (1) labeled source data and (2) unlabeled parallel data. It would help to disentangle their contributions, e.g., via ablations using (a) only unlabeled parallel data for representation plus target labels; (b) only source/target labeled data without parallel data; and (c) varying both.
- Experimental setup clarity (Sec. 5.2/5.3): Please confirm that training uses (a) 4k labeled source docs, (b) 100 labeled target docs, and (c) up to 2k unlabeled parallel docs; testing is on the remaining target documents. In Sec. 5.3, where np is varied from 200 to 2000, Table 1 suggests TSL ≥ CL-KCCA across tasks, yet Fig. 1 shows CL-KCCA ≥ TSL for EFM/EGM at np=2000. What explains this discrepancy (different seeds, parameter settings, or folds)?
- Why does TSL beat CCA-style methods? CCA (and KCCA) are natural for multi-view alignment but rely heavily on paired data. Please add intuition for TSL’s advantage: e.g., (i) completion exploits both within-language and cross-language co-occurrences across all documents (not just paired rows), (ii) nonnegativity and sparsity reduce overfitting with few pairs, and (iii) the nuclear norm encourages a shared latent space aligned by parallel rows while being constrained by monolingual structure.
- Complexity and scalability: Please report wall-clock time and memory usage versus CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA. The shrinkage step requires repeated SVDs; do you use partial/randomized SVD? What are typical iterations to convergence, stopping criteria, and sensitivity to τ, γ, ρ, µ? Practical guidelines would strengthen the contribution.

Quality
- The formulation is technically sound and builds on well-understood convex optimization. The convergence analysis is clear. Empirical comparisons are broad and positive. Additional ablations (LSI necessity, parameter sensitivity, impact of nonnegativity) and runtime would further improve rigor.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable. Please clarify the experimental protocol, reconcile Table/Figure differences, and expand the motivation for LSI and the low-rank assumption. Minor typos listed below.

Originality
- Combining matrix completion with cross-lingual representation learning is a novel angle relative to CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA and SCL. The projected gradient framework with nonnegativity is a clean instantiation of known tools in this new context.

Significance
- Results suggest meaningful improvements, particularly in low-resource parallel settings, a common and important regime. The approach is likely to be useful to practitioners.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, broadly applicable method with a solid convex formulation and convergence guarantees.
- Consistent empirical gains across many transfer pairs, especially with limited parallel data.
- Clear positioning relative to prior cross-lingual methods.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient analysis of the LSI step and low-rank assumption; limited ablations.
- Experimental setup details and a minor inconsistency between table/figure need clarification.
- No runtime/scalability analysis, which is pertinent given repeated SVDs.

Minor comments and typos
- Fix “(ij)” to “(i, j)” two sentences before Eq. (2).
- Remove “such as” in the sentences before Eqs. (4) and (5).
- Typos: “parellel” → “parallel”; “documentterm” → “document-term”; ensure consistent notation for matrix dimensions and for k vs. d.
- Briefly motivate nonnegativity constraints and discuss their empirical effect, if any.

Overall, this is a simple and effective approach with promising results. Addressing the clarifications and adding a few ablations and runtime metrics would substantially strengthen the paper.
