Summary: The authors propose a novel two-step approach for learning cross-lingual topic representations by leveraging matrix completion and latent semantic analysis techniques. This method employs a projected gradient descent algorithm to solve the matrix completion problem. The experimental results demonstrate the proposed method's superiority over both mono-lingual and cross-lingual baselines in sentiment analysis tasks using the parallel Amazon review dataset. Furthermore, the learning curves illustrate the algorithm's ability to learn accurate cross-lingual topic representations even with a relatively small number of parallel data points.

Quality: The experiments conducted in this study are comprehensive and effectively support the authors' claims. The proposed two-step learning algorithm is rigorously compared to two baselines (plain bag-of-words and cross-lingual LSA) and two state-of-the-art cross-lingual dimensionality reduction algorithms, providing a thorough evaluation of its performance.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it easy to follow and reproduce the results. All parameters, including their selection process, are explicitly stated in the paper. However, a minor suggestion would be to provide additional motivation for using matrix completion in the Introduction, as its explanation is currently confined to Section 3.

Originality: Although the task of cross-lingual representation learning is not novel, the application of matrix completion to this task is innovative. The proposed algorithm demonstrates improved performance compared to existing methods in the literature, showcasing its potential.

Significance: The results of the two-step approach consistently outperform other baselines, indicating the effectiveness of matrix completion as a preprocessing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks. This formulation may also be applicable to other tasks, making this work a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance in cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks, and its stability across different sizes of unlabeled parallel data is a notable advantage.
