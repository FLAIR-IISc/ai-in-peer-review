Summary: This paper presents a two-step method for learning cross-lingual topic representations, leveraging matrix completion and latent semantic analysis. A projected gradient descent algorithm is used to optimize the matrix completion problem. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms both monolingual and existing cross-lingual baselines on a sentiment analysis task using a parallel Amazon review dataset. Learning curves, generated with varying amounts of unlabeled data, illustrate that the algorithm effectively learns accurate cross-lingual topic representations even with a limited number of parallel data points.

Quality: The experiments are thorough and strongly support the paper’s claims. The proposed two-step learning algorithm is carefully compared to two baselines – a plain bag-of-words model and cross-lingual LSA – and two state-of-the-art cross-lingual dimensionality reduction algorithms. 

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and easy to follow. All parameters, including guidance on their selection, are clearly defined, facilitating reproducibility of the results. A minor suggestion: elaborating on the motivation for using matrix completion in the Introduction (although it is described in Section 3) would be beneficial.

Originality: While cross-lingual representation learning is a well-studied area, the application of matrix completion to this task is novel. The proposed algorithm demonstrably achieves superior performance compared to existing methods in the literature.

Significance: The two-step approach consistently achieves higher performance than the baselines. This work demonstrates the potential of using matrix completion as a pre-processing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification, and this formulation could prove effective in other applications as well. In summary, this paper proposes a two-step method to learn cross-lingual topic representations, employing matrix completion as a precursor to latent semantic analysis for cross-lingual sentiment classification. Experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms monolingual and other cross-lingual baselines, including a state-of-the-art approach, and exhibits robustness when trained with different sizes of unlabeled parallel data.
