This paper introduces a matrix completion framework for cross-domain classification that leverages both labeled data from an auxiliary (source) domain and unlabeled parallel data. The proposed approach consists of two main steps. First, documents from all domains are organized into a single, large, and incomplete matrix. This matrix is then completed using a projected gradient descent algorithm that enforces both low-rank and sparsity constraints. Second, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is used to reduce the dimensionality of the completed matrix, and a standard classifier is subsequently trained on this low-dimensional representation. The authors provide a convergence guarantee for their optimization algorithm and report strong experimental results.

Here are some comments and questions:

1.  The paper states that after matrix completion, LSI is used to reduce the feature dimension because the completed matrix M* "lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness." Could you please elaborate on this point? Is the primary motivation for this dimensionality reduction step to improve computational efficiency, given that M* will be relatively dense despite the sparsity constraint? Furthermore, could you justify the choice of LSI over other common dimensionality reduction techniques, such as PCA?

2.  The paper appears to address two distinct settings for exploiting external data: (1) leveraging labeled source-domain data to improve classification in the target domain, and (2) leveraging unlabeled parallel data. If this understanding is correct, the paper would benefit from explicitly distinguishing these two settings and presenting their results separately to better isolate the contribution of each data source.

3.  The experimental setup in Section 5.2 could be described more clearly. To confirm my understanding: was the training data composed of (a) 4,000 labeled source documents, (b) 100 labeled target documents, and (c) 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents? Were the results reported on the remaining 1,900 labeled target documents? Similarly, is the only difference in the setup for Section 5.3 that the number of unlabeled parallel documents is varied from 200 to 2,000? If this is correct, there appears to be an inconsistency in the results. In Figure 1 (for EFM and EGM), CL-KCCA outperforms TSL when 2,000 parallel documents are used, but the results in Table 1 show the opposite. Could you please clarify this discrepancy?

4.  Could you provide more intuition for the low-rank assumption on the document-term matrix? While such a matrix is typically very sparse, it is not immediately obvious why it should be low-rank. One might expect it to be nearly full-rank, as it is unlikely that any given document can be expressed as a linear combination of a small number of other documents.

5.  Could you provide some intuition as to why the proposed TSL method generally outperforms other approaches, particularly multiview methods like CCA? CCA seems like a very natural fit for this cross-language representation learning task. In contrast, the matrix completion approach feels less direct. It appears the main driver of performance is the enforcement of a low-rank structure on the combined matrix M*. How does this conceptually compare to the objective of maximizing correlation between views, as in CCA?

6.  Could you comment on the computational complexity and running time of the proposed algorithm? Specifically, how does the projected gradient descent step compare in terms of efficiency to baseline methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA?

Suggested minor changes:
*   Two sentences before equation (2), please change `(ij)` to `(i,j)`.
*   In the sentences preceding equations (4) and (5), the phrase "such as" could be removed for conciseness.

Overall, this paper presents a simple and effective matrix completion approach for cross-domain classification. While the core idea is appealing and the method is generally well-presented, several technical points and experimental details, as noted above, would benefit from further clarification.
