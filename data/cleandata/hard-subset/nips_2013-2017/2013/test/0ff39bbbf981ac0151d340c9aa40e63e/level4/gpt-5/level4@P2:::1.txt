Summary
This paper tackles cross-language text classification by learning a shared representation from bilingual data via a two-step pipeline. First, the authors build a unified document–term matrix spanning the concatenated vocabularies of two languages; rows for parallel document pairs are fully observed, while non-parallel documents have missing entries in the other language’s vocabulary. They pose a convex matrix completion problem that combines trace-norm regularization (to encourage low rank), an ℓ1 penalty (to promote sparsity), nonnegativity constraints, and squared-loss terms on both observed and missing entries (with different weights). They solve this with a projected gradient descent scheme that alternates a gradient step, singular value shrinkage, and projection onto the nonnegative orthant, and they prove convergence. Second, they apply LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation, on which a standard linear SVM is trained. Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews show consistent gains over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, particularly when few parallel documents are available.

Relation to prior work
The method builds on ideas from CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA that exploit parallel/comparable corpora to induce shared spaces, but differs by first imputing missing cross-language features via matrix completion and only then applying LSI. The optimization uses well-known trace-norm minimization techniques with convergence properties.

Strengths
- Clear, modular approach: impute cross-language features, then compress with LSI.
- Solid optimization setup with a simple, scalable algorithm and a convergence guarantee.
- Strong empirical performance across 18 tasks; particularly robust in the low-parallel-data regime.
- Minimal reliance on large-scale translation; leverages a relatively small set of unlabeled parallel documents.

Weaknesses and questions
- Motivation for LSI after completion: The paper says the completed matrix “lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness.” Please clarify. Is the main motivation dimensionality reduction for robustness and computational efficiency (since M* may still be high-dimensional and relatively dense)? Why LSI rather than PCA or other reductions, and does the choice matter empirically?
- Separating sources of gain: The paper appears to benefit from (1) labeled source-language data and (2) unlabeled parallel data. It would be helpful to disentangle these contributions, e.g., by ablations that remove parallel data or restrict labeled source data.
- Experimental setup clarity (Sec. 5.2/5.3): Please confirm the training/test splits: (a) ~4000 labeled source docs, (b) 100 labeled target docs, (c) 2000 unlabeled parallel docs; test on the remaining ~1900 labeled target docs. In Sec. 5.3, you vary only the number of parallel docs from 200 to 2000. If so, how do you reconcile the apparent discrepancy that CL-KCCA slightly outperforms TSL in Figure 1 for EFM/EGM at 2000 parallels, whereas in Table 1 TSL is best?
- Low-rank justification: The paper states that a fully observed document–term matrix is “naturally low-rank.” While sparsity is clear, low-rank is less obvious since documents need not be linear combinations of a small number of basis documents. Please provide intuition or empirical evidence (e.g., singular value decay) supporting the low-rank assumption in this setting.
- Why TSL over CCA-based multiview methods? CL-KCCA is a natural choice for aligning views. What characteristics of the proposed completion-plus-LSI pipeline make it superior (e.g., leveraging within-language co-occurrence and cross-language structure jointly, nonnegativity, sparsity)? A high-level comparison would strengthen the narrative.
- Runtime and scalability: Please report training time and memory usage relative to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA. How do costs scale with vocabulary size, number of documents, and number of parallels? What are the stopping criteria for the projected gradient method?
- Hyperparameters: Briefly discuss sensitivity to γ, ρ, τ, and k. Is τ chosen from theory or tuned? How many iterations are typically required?

Quality
- The optimization formulation is sound and the convergence argument is clear. Additional experimental details (ablation, runtime, sensitivity) would further support the claims.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written. Clarifying the experimental protocol and the rationale for the LSI stage would improve readability.

Originality
- Using matrix completion to impute cross-language features prior to LSI is a novel and interesting twist compared to applying CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA directly.

Significance
- Results are strong and the approach is practical, especially when parallel data are scarce. The method should be of interest to researchers in cross-lingual representation learning and text classification.

Pros
- Simple, effective pipeline with a convergence guarantee.
- Consistent empirical gains, especially with limited parallel data.
- Minimal reliance on large-scale translation.

Cons
- Some claims (low-rank assumption; LSI choice) need clearer motivation.
- Experimental setup requires clarification; limited ablation and runtime analysis.

Suggestions and minor edits
- Fix (ij) to (i, j) two sentences before Eq. (2).
- Remove “such as” in the sentences before Eqs. (4) and (5).
- Correct typos (e.g., “parellel” → “parallel”).
- Define E, Y, and Ŷ at first use and keep notation consistent.
- Briefly justify the nonnegativity constraint and the equivalence turning the ℓ1 term into a linear term under M ≥ 0.

Overall assessment
Despite a few points needing clarification, this is a clear, well-motivated paper with a simple method and strong results. I lean positive, contingent on addressing the questions above, especially experimental clarity, the rationale for LSI, and runtime/scalability.
