The paper introduces a two-stage method for cross-lingual text classification that uses labeled data in a source language together with unlabeled parallel documents. The pipeline is: (1) build a unified bilingual document–term matrix covering both languages, where non-parallel documents have missing entries; recover the matrix by solving a convex matrix completion problem that enforces low rank (via a trace norm) and sparsity (via an ℓ1 term) under nonnegativity, using a projected gradient descent algorithm with a convergence guarantee; (2) apply LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation, then train a standard classifier in this space. Experiments on Amazon sentiment classification show consistent gains over baselines and representative cross-lingual representation learners (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA), particularly when only a small number of parallel documents are available.

Here are some comments and questions:
- After completing M, you reduce the dimensionality from d to k with LSI because M “lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness.” Could you clarify this motivation? Is the primary goal to denoise and control dimensionality (since M* will likely become relatively dense despite sparsity regularization)? If so, why prefer LSI over alternatives such as PCA or other dimensionality reduction methods?
- The paper seems to leverage two types of external information: (1) labeled source-language data to help with target labeling, and (2) unlabeled parallel data. If this reading is correct, it would be helpful to disentangle these contributions and report effects separately.
- The experimental setup in Section 5.2 could be made crisper. My understanding is that training uses (a) 4k labeled source documents, (b) 100 labeled target documents, and (c) 2k unlabeled parallel documents; evaluation is on the remaining 1,900 labeled target documents. In Section 5.3, you vary only the number of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2k. Under this setup, why does CL-KCCA slightly outperform TSL for EFM and EGM at np = 2k in Figure 1, whereas Table 1 does not show that pattern?
- Please briefly justify the claim that a fully observed document–term matrix is “naturally low rank.” While it is sparse, its rank need not be low (the rank can be as high as min(t, d)); a short intuition or a refined statement would help.
- Could you provide intuition for why TSL outperforms other methods, especially CCA-based approaches? Multiview methods like CCA are a natural fit for cross-lingual representation learning. In contrast, matrix completion is less obvious; if the low-rank prior on M* is the main driver, how does its inductive bias compare to CCA/OPCA/LSI?
- What is the runtime profile of your solver? How does the projected gradient descent with singular-value shrinkage compare in practice to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA in terms of computational cost?

- Remove “such as” in the sentences immediately preceding Equations (4) and (5).

Overall, the paper presents a matrix completion-based approach to cross-domain classification. While a few technical points need clarification, the method is simple, effective, and, on the whole, clearly described.
