

The paper introduces a novel approach to translingual representation learning, combining matrix completion for multilingual vector construction with Constrained Latent Semantic Indexing (CL-LSI) on the resulting vectors. While the core idea is promising, the evaluation methodology raises significant concerns about the robustness of the reported results, which must be addressed before the contribution can be fully validated.

The primary issue lies in the experimental design. The authors report superior performance using linear SVMs on the proposed two-step pipeline (matrix completion followed by CL-LSI) compared to alternative methods (OPCA, CCA). However, linear SVMs are highly sensitive to feature scaling, and the observed gains may stem from LSI's inherent scaling properties rather than the novelty of the framework. For instance, LSI's dimensionality reduction might preserve scaling patterns that benefit linear SVMs, while OPCA and CCA could inadvertently disrupt these patterns. To conclusively demonstrate the method's efficacy, the authors must replace the linear SVM with a classifier invariant to feature rescaling—such as gradient-boosted decision trees (e.g., XGBoost or LightGBM)—which are widely available and standard in robust ML evaluation. If the proposed pipeline outperforms other methods under such a classifier, the results would be more credible. Without this, the current evidence remains ambiguous.

The significance of the work hinges entirely on resolving this robustness question. If validated, the approach would represent a meaningful contribution by leveraging matrix completion as a controlled non-linear inference technique for cross-lingual tasks—a departure from typical linear or neural methods. However, as presented, the significance is speculative due to the unresolved scaling artifact concern. The authors should explicitly address this in the revision.

Regarding novelty, the integration of matrix completion with CL-LSI for translingual representation appears original within the scope of the literature. The authors do not overtly claim novelty for the individual components, but the specific combination and application to cross-lingual tasks seem unexplored.

Clarity issues further undermine the paper's impact. Line 113 states the authors "construct[ing] a unified document-term matrix for all documents" but fails to clarify whether this involves: (a) normalizing surface forms (e.g., treating "Merkel" and "Merkelen" as identical terms), or (b) retaining language-specific surface forms (e.g., treating "Merkel" and "Merkelen" as distinct terms). This ambiguity is critical, as it directly affects how multilingual alignment is achieved. The authors must specify their approach and justify it. Additionally, the experimental setup is fragmented across Table 1 and the main text, forcing readers to cross-reference multiple sections to understand the evaluation protocol. A consolidated description of the dataset, language pairs, and classifier configurations in one coherent subsection would greatly improve reproducibility.

The paper’s overall contribution is interesting but currently lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims. The suggested classifier comparison (tree-based methods) is essential to distinguish genuine methodological innovation from scaling artifacts. Without it, the results remain vulnerable to the criticism that they reflect SVM-specific behavior rather than the pipeline's inherent strength. The authors should also streamline the experimental description and resolve the term construction ambiguity to strengthen the paper’s foundation.

In summary, the method is conceptually novel and potentially impactful, but the evaluation falls short of establishing robustness. The authors are strongly encouraged to: (1) replace linear SVMs with rescaling-invariant classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees) in their experiments; (2) clarify the unified document-term matrix construction; and (3) consolidate the experimental setup description. If these revisions are implemented, this work could make a valuable contribution to translingual representation learning. As currently presented, however, the evidence does not sufficiently justify the proposed method's superiority.
