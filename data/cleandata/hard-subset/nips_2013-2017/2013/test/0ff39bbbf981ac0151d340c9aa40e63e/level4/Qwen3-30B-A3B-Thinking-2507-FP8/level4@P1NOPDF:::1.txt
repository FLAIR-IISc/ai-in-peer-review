

The paper proposes a matrix completion approach for cross-domain classification, effectively leveraging labeled data from an auxiliary domain and unlabeled parallel data. The method involves two key steps: first, constructing a single incomplete matrix encompassing all documents and domains, then completing it under low-rank and sparsity constraints via projected gradient descent. Second, it reduces the feature dimension of the completed matrix using Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) before training a standard classifier. The paper provides a convergence guarantee for the optimization algorithm and demonstrates favorable empirical results.  

Below are specific comments and questions:  

- After completing the matrix $M$, dimensionality is reduced from $d$ to $k$ via LSI, as $M$ "lacks sufficient capacity to handle feature sparseness." Could you clarify this rationale? Is the primary motivation computational efficiency (since $M^*$ becomes dense despite sparsity enforcement), or is there a theoretical justification? Additionally, why was LSI chosen over alternatives like PCA?  

- The work appears to exploit external data through two distinct mechanisms: (1) leveraging labeled source-domain data to improve target-domain classification, and (2) utilizing unlabeled parallel data. These aspects seem disjointed. To enhance clarity, please explicitly distinguish these two contributions and report their individual impacts in experiments.  

- The experimental setup in Section 5.2 requires clarification. For training, did you use: (a) 4,000 labeled source documents, (b) 100 labeled target documents, and (c) 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents? And for evaluation, were results reported on the remaining 1,900 labeled target documents? If so, Section 5.3 varies only the unlabeled parallel data size (200–2,000 documents). Given this, why does CL-KCCA outperform TSL in Figure 1 (EFM/EGM at 2,000 unlabeled documents) but not in Table 1?  

- The paper asserts that a fully observed document-term matrix would be low-rank. However, documents typically do not form linear combinations of one another in the vocabulary space (e.g., rank would be $\min(d, n)$, where $n$ is document count). Could you provide intuition for why this matrix is low-rank in practice?  

- TSL consistently outperforms methods like CCA (e.g., Figure 1). Given that multiview approaches like CCA are natural for cross-language representation learning, why does matrix completion (via low-rank enforcement) yield better results? What specific advantage does the low-rank constraint offer over alternatives like CCA?  

- What is the computational cost of the projected gradient descent algorithm? How does its runtime compare to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA?  

**Suggested revisions:**  
- Correct "(ij)" to "(i,j)" two sentences before Equation (2).  
- Remove "such as" in the sentences preceding Equations (4) and (5).  

Overall, the method is simple, effective, and clearly presented. Addressing the points above would strengthen the paper’s technical contribution and reproducibility.
