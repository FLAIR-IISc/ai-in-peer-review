The paper proposes a novel method for translingual representation learning, combining matrix completion with CL-LSI (Cross-Lingual Latent Semantic Indexing) to create multilingual vectors. This approach is intriguing, but its robustness is uncertain.

In terms of quality, the results presented in the paper appear promising, but it is unclear whether they are solely due to the superior scaling of LSI compared to OPCA (Orthogonal Projection on the Conditioned Attributes) or CCA (Canonical Correlation Analysis) for a linear SVM. To address this concern, I strongly recommend that the authors employ a machine learning system that is invariant to rescalings of individual features, such as boosted decision trees, which are available in Weka. If the two-step system, paired with boosted decision trees, outperforms other techniques, it would significantly strengthen the results.

If the findings are indeed robust, the significance of this work would be substantial, as matrix completion is a well-controlled, non-linear inference technique. The novelty of the proposed method is also a notable aspect, as it appears to be a fresh approach to translingual representation learning.

However, there are some issues with clarity in the paper. For instance, line 113 mentions constructing a "unified document-term matrix for all documents," but it is unclear whether this refers to using a single term for every surface form (e.g., "Merkel") or labeling each surface form from each language (e.g., "Merkelen" and "Merkelde"). A clarification on this point would be beneficial.

Additionally, the experimental setup is described across Table 1, which made it somewhat challenging to follow. It would be helpful to consolidate this information in a single location or provide a clearer explanation of the setup.

In conclusion, the idea presented in the paper is intriguing, and I appreciate the authors' effort to explore a new approach to translingual representation learning. Nevertheless, I believe that further experimentation with different classifiers, such as boosted decision trees, is necessary to confirm the robustness of the results and rule out potential scaling artifacts.
