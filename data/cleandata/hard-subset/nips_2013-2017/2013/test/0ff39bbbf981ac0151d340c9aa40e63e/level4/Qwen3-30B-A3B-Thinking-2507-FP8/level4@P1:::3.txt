

The paper proposes a novel two-step approach for cross-lingual representation learning, leveraging matrix completion to fill missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) for low-dimensional cross-lingual embeddings. The method exploits unlabeled parallel documents to bridge disjoint language feature spaces, avoiding the need for full document translation required by prior work. The authors evaluate their approach on Amazon product reviews across English, French, German, and Japanese, demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, particularly with limited parallel data.

**Strengths:**  
The core idea of using matrix completion to recover a low-rank bilingual document-term matrix before applying LSI is novel and well-motivated. The method effectively addresses the challenge of sparse bilingual feature spaces without relying on translation tools, aligning with recent trends in resource-efficient cross-lingual learning. The theoretical foundation is sound, with a convergence analysis for the projected gradient descent algorithm. The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering 18 cross-lingual sentiment tasks and systematically testing the impact of parallel data size (200–2000 documents). The results are compelling, showing TSL outperforming all baselines by 8.59–14.61% average accuracy, with robust performance even at small parallel data scales.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**  
While the paper’s technical contribution is strong, two methodological concerns require clarification. First, the evaluation exclusively uses linear SVMs as the classifier. Given that LSI produces a linear transformation of the feature space, the results may be sensitive to SVM’s inherent sensitivity to feature scaling. For instance, the observed superiority of TSL over CL-LSI/CL-OPCA could stem from LSI’s dimensionality reduction interacting favorably with SVM’s linearity, rather than a fundamental advantage of the representation. To rigorously validate the method’s efficacy, the authors should test with a classifier less sensitive to feature scaling, such as boosted decision trees (e.g., in Weka), which would better isolate the quality of the learned representations from the classifier’s idiosyncrasies. Without this, the significance claim remains partially unverified.

Second, the paper’s description of the unified vocabulary construction lacks clarity. On line 113, it states the method constructs a "unified document-term matrix for all documents," but it is ambiguous whether this uses language-specific surface forms (e.g., "Merkel" for English and "Merkelen" for German) or a shared semantic representation (e.g., a single term for "Chancellor"). This distinction critically impacts the interpretation of the matrix completion step. The authors should explicitly clarify whether the vocabulary is language-agnostic or language-specific, as this affects how cross-lingual co-occurrence patterns are captured.

Additionally, the experimental setup description is fragmented across Table 1 and the main text, requiring readers to cross-reference to understand the data partitioning (e.g., 100 labeled target documents, 2000 unlabeled parallel documents). A concise summary of the setup in the methods section would improve reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper presents a novel, well-executed method for cross-lingual representation learning with strong empirical results. The core innovation—using matrix completion to recover missing bilingual features before LSI—addresses a genuine gap in the literature and shows promise for low-resource scenarios. However, the exclusive use of linear SVMs limits confidence in the robustness of the performance gains. If the authors validate their approach with an invariant classifier (e.g., boosted trees), the significance would be substantially strengthened. The clarity issues regarding vocabulary construction are minor but should be resolved for precision. Given the method’s novelty, technical soundness, and empirical strength, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to cross-lingual NLP. I recommend acceptance with the above suggestions addressed in revision.
