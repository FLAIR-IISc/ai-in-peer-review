

Summary: This paper addresses cross-lingual text classification by proposing a novel two-step method to learn shared document representations across languages using unlabeled parallel documents. The authors first construct a bilingual document-term matrix with missing entries (representing unobserved language features) and apply matrix completion via projected gradient descent to recover a low-rank matrix, followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) to derive dense cross-lingual representations. The approach builds upon prior NIPS work on cross-lingual representation learning (e.g., CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], CL-OPCA [15]), but differs fundamentally by using matrix completion to fill missing features before LSI, rather than applying dimensionality reduction directly on the incomplete matrix. The method is evaluated on Amazon product reviews across English, French, German, and Japanese, demonstrating consistent superiority over monolingual baselines and state-of-the-art cross-lingual techniques.

Quality: The experimental design is robust and well-justified. The authors conduct thorough comparisons across 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks (e.g., English→French, English→German) against four baselines: monolingual bag-of-words (TBOW), CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA. Key strengths include: (1) systematic parameter selection via 3-fold validation on one task, (2) comprehensive analysis of unlabeled data size impact (200–2000 parallel documents), and (3) 10-fold repetition to report standard deviations. Results show TSL significantly outperforms all baselines (e.g., +8.59% to +14.61% accuracy over TBOW), with consistent gains across languages and categories. The convergence proof for the projected gradient descent algorithm (Section 4.2) adds theoretical rigor, though the optimization details could be slightly clearer for reproducibility.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and technically precise. The two-step framework (matrix completion → LSI) is clearly motivated and logically presented. Key notations (e.g., Ω, Ω̂ for observed/missing entries) are consistently defined. The experimental section explicitly states dataset preprocessing (TF-IDF unigrams), SVM implementation (libSVM defaults), and parameter choices (γ=0.1, ρ=10⁻⁴, k=50), enabling full reproducibility. A minor suggestion: the motivation for matrix completion (why not use standard imputation?) could be strengthened in Section 1 by explicitly contrasting with prior methods that treat missing values as zeros (e.g., CL-LSI [10] does not address missing entries in the document-term matrix).

Originality: While cross-lingual representation learning is well-studied at NIPS (e.g., [10,15,20]), the application of matrix completion to fill missing entries in bilingual document-term matrices is novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work: unlike CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA that operate directly on incomplete matrices, TSL first completes the matrix to capture cross-lingual co-occurrence patterns. This leads to measurable gains—especially with scarce parallel data (e.g., TSL achieves strong results with only 200 parallel documents, while competitors degrade sharply). The novelty is sufficiently distinct from recent NIPS proceedings (2013–2017) based on the cited related work.

Significance: The work demonstrates a practically valuable advance: TSL reduces the parallel data requirement for effective cross-lingual learning, a critical bottleneck in low-resource language scenarios. The consistent performance gains across 18 tasks (outperforming CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA on all 18, CL-LSI on 17/18) suggest broad applicability. Crucially, the method’s efficacy with minimal parallel data (200 documents) makes it highly relevant for real-world deployment where bilingual corpora are scarce. This could inspire similar matrix completion approaches in other cross-lingual NLP tasks (e.g., translation, retrieval).

Strengths:  
✓ Rigorous experiments with 18 tasks, 10 repetitions, and statistical reporting  
✓ Clear theoretical foundation (matrix completion + LSI) with convergence guarantees  
✓ Strong empirical results, especially with small parallel data (200 docs)  
✓ Well-motivated comparison to 4 relevant baselines (including 3 recent NIPS methods)  

Weaknesses:  
✗ Minor ambiguity in Section 1: Why matrix completion is necessary (vs. other imputation) merits deeper discussion  
✗ Limited analysis of failure cases (e.g., why CL-LSI underperforms on EJB task)  

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances cross-lingual learning with a novel, theoretically sound, and empirically effective method. The results are compelling, the approach is well-justified, and the work aligns strongly with NIPS’ focus on principled, impactful machine learning. I recommend acceptance.
