This paper addresses the task of cross-domain classification by proposing a novel matrix completion approach. The method is designed to leverage both labeled data from an auxiliary (or source) domain and unlabeled parallel data. The proposed framework operates in two stages. First, documents from all domains are consolidated into a single, large, and incomplete feature matrix. This matrix is then completed using a projected gradient descent algorithm that enforces both low-rank and sparsity constraints. In the second stage, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to the completed matrix for dimensionality reduction, after which a standard classifier is trained on the resulting low-dimensional representations. The authors provide a convergence guarantee for their optimization algorithm and present experimental results that show the proposed method outperforming several baseline techniques.

While the method is simple, effective, and generally well-presented, several points require clarification to strengthen the paper.

**Major Comments and Questions:**

1.  **Motivation for Dimensionality Reduction:** The motivation for applying LSI after the matrix completion step needs further clarification. The paper states this is because the completed matrix M* "lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness." Could the authors elaborate on this? Is the primary motivation to reduce the dimensionality of the now-dense matrix M* for computational efficiency? Furthermore, a justification for choosing LSI over other standard techniques, such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA), would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Experimental Clarity and Ablation:** The experimental evaluation could be improved by more clearly delineating the contributions of the different data sources. The proposed method leverages two distinct types of external information: labeled source-domain data and unlabeled parallel data. An ablation study that separately evaluates the impact of each of these components would provide valuable insight into the model's behavior.

3.  **Experimental Settings and Discrepancies:** The experimental settings in Section 5.2 require clarification. Could the authors explicitly state the training and testing splits? For instance, is the model trained on 4,000 labeled source documents, 100 labeled target documents, and 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents, with evaluation performed on the remaining 1,900 labeled target documents? Clarifying this would help in interpreting the results. On that note, there appears to be a discrepancy between Table 1 and Figure 1. In Figure 1, at the 2,000 parallel document level, CL-KCCA seems to outperform the proposed TSL method for the EFM and EGM tasks, which contradicts the results presented in Table 1. Please clarify this.

4.  **Justification of the Low-Rank Assumption:** The low-rank assumption is central to the proposed matrix completion framework. While this assumption is well-motivated for recommender systems, its application to a document-term matrix is less intuitive. Could the authors provide a justification for why a fully-observed document-term matrix would be inherently low-rank? In a standard bag-of-words representation, documents are often linearly independent, which would imply a full-rank matrix (rank = min(#docs, #features)). An explanation of how this assumption holds in the context of text data would be beneficial.

5.  **Intuition Behind Performance:** A deeper intuitive explanation for the model's strong performance would be valuable. Multi-view learning methods like Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) seem like a very natural fit for this cross-domain task. In contrast, the matrix completion formulation is less conventional. What is the key mechanism that allows the proposed TSL method to outperform these more traditional approaches? Is the enforcement of a global low-rank structure across all documents and domains the primary driver of its success? A discussion comparing the implicit assumptions of the low-rank model versus those of multi-view models would significantly enhance the paper's contribution.

6.  **Computational Complexity:** For completeness, a discussion of the computational complexity and empirical running time of the proposed algorithm would be a useful addition. How does the training time of the projected gradient descent algorithm compare to the baseline methods, such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA?

**Minor Suggested Changes:**

*   In the text two sentences before Equation (2), please change `(ij)` to `(i,j)` for consistency.
*   In the sentences preceding Equations (4) and (5), consider removing "such as" for conciseness (e.g., "enforcing the sparsity of M by using the l1 norm" can be changed to "enforcing the sparsity of M using the l1 norm").

**Conclusion:**

The paper proposes an interesting and effective matrix completion approach for cross-domain classification. The method is straightforward and shows strong empirical performance. Addressing the questions above, particularly regarding the motivation for certain design choices and clarifying the experimental setup, would significantly improve the paper's clarity and impact.
