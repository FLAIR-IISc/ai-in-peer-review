- Paper proposes a novel two-step matrix completion approach for cross-domain classification: complete an incomplete document matrix via low-rank + sparsity using projected gradient descent, then apply LSI and train a classifier; convergence guarantee and favorable experiments reported.

- Clarify why feature dimension is reduced from d to k using LSI after estimating M: why M alone cannot handle feature sparsity and whether the reduction is mainly for computational reasons given M*’s expected density.

- Explain the choice of LSI over other dimensionality reduction methods (e.g., PCA) and justify its suitability for the completed matrix.

- Clearly distinguish the two ways external data are exploited—(1) labeled source-language data and (2) unlabeled parallel data—and report results for each aspect separately.

- Clarify experiment settings in Section 5.2 (confirm training data: 4k labeled source, 100 labeled target, 2k unlabeled parallel; test on remaining 1900 labeled target) and the variation in Section 5.3 (unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2k).

- Resolve the discrepancy where CL-KCCA outperforms TSL in Figure 1 for EFM and EGM at 2k but not in Table 1, and explain differences between these result presentations.

- Provide a brief justification or discussion for why a fully observed document-term matrix would be low-rank, since documents are sparse and may not be linear combinations of others.

- Offer insight into why TSL generally outperforms other methods (especially CCA) and explain when matrix completion’s low-rank enforcement is preferable to multiview approaches.

- Compare the proposed matrix completion approach with other relevant techniques to clarify strengths and weaknesses.

- Report running time and computational complexity of the projected gradient descent algorithm and compare runtime with CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA.

- Fix minor typos: change “(ij)” to “(i,j)” before equation (2), and remove “such as” in the sentences before equations (4) and (5).

- Overall assessment: method is simple, effective, and well-presented, but several technical points and experiment/reporting details need clarification.
