Summary:
The paper proposes a two-stage approach to translingual representation learning: (1) apply matrix completion to construct multilingual vectors, and (2) run cross-lingual LSI (CL-LSI) on these vectors. The goal is to improve cross-lingual document classification performance.

Quality:
The idea is appealing, but I am not yet convinced the empirical gains are robust. A key concern is that the improvements might stem from feature scaling effects that particularly favor linear SVMs when combined with LSI, rather than from genuinely better cross-lingual representations. To strengthen the evidence:

- Use classifiers that are less sensitive to feature scaling (e.g., boosted decision trees as implemented in Weka, or other tree ensembles). If the two-step system outperforms OPCA/CCA with such models, it would mitigate the scaling-artifact concern.
- Report results with standardized/normalized features (e.g., per-feature z-score, or L2-normalized document vectors) across all methods so that scaling impacts are controlled. Clarify whether such normalization was already applied.
- Provide ablations: (a) matrix completion alone without CL-LSI; (b) CL-LSI without matrix completion; (c) vary rank/latent dimension; and (d) swap CL-LSI with OPCA/CCA on the same completed matrix to isolate where the gains arise.
- Include statistical significance tests or confidence intervals over multiple runs/splits.

Significance:
If the gains persist under scale-insensitive models and proper normalization, the contribution would be meaningful. Using matrix completion as a principled inference step before cross-lingual projection is attractive and could generalize to low-resource or partially aligned settings.

Novelty:
The combination of matrix completion with CL-LSI for translingual representation learning appears novel, and the framing is interesting.

Clarity:
Some parts of the paper need clarification to ensure reproducibility and interpretability.

- Construction of the unified document–term matrix (line 113): Please specify whether tokens are shared across languages (e.g., a single feature for “Merkel” regardless of source language) or whether they are language-tagged or otherwise disambiguated (e.g., separate features for language-specific surface forms like “Merkelen” vs. “Merkelde”). If any normalization, transliteration, stemming, or dictionary-based mapping is used to unify terms, describe it precisely. Also clarify how OOV words and language-specific morphology are handled.
- Experimental setup: The description seems fragmented (split across Table 1). Provide a concise narrative of datasets, languages, train/dev/test splits, label spaces, preprocessing (tokenization, stopwording, TF-IDF or other weighting), and evaluation metrics in one place. Clearly state whether features are normalized before training the classifier.
- Matrix completion details: Specify the objective, solver, and hyperparameters (e.g., nuclear-norm regularization, target rank, stopping criteria), handling of sparsity, missingness pattern across languages, and computational complexity. Indicate whether completion is performed per row or globally, and whether language blocks are constrained or coupled during completion.
- CL-LSI details: Report the weighting scheme (TF, TF-IDF, or BM25), centering/whitening steps, choice of latent dimension, and how cross-lingual alignment is enforced (e.g., via aligned documents, dictionary constraints, or co-occurrence). Explain how the multilingual vectors from completion feed into CL-LSI.
- Baselines: Ensure fair comparisons with OPCA and CCA by matching dimensionality, normalization, and training data. If applicable, include more recent cross-lingual representation methods or at least strong bilingual dictionary/embedding baselines.
- Robustness: Explore sensitivity to hyperparameters (rank, latent dimension, regularization strength), the proportion of missing cross-lingual alignments, and the choice of languages (typologically close vs. distant).

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean pipeline with a well-motivated inference step (matrix completion) before cross-lingual dimensionality reduction.
- Potential to benefit scenarios with partial alignments or missing translations.
- The idea appears novel and has practical relevance for cross-lingual classification.

Weaknesses:
- Potential confound due to feature scaling with linear SVMs.
- Incomplete clarity on the construction of the unified vocabulary and experimental setup.
- Limited exploration of alternative classifiers and ablations.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add experiments with boosted decision trees (Weka) or other tree-based models and compare against OPCA/CCA under identical preprocessing.
- Normalize features consistently across all methods; report with and without normalization.
- Provide thorough ablations and significance testing.
- Clarify the unified document–term matrix and the full pipeline with enough detail for reproduction.
- Discuss runtime and scalability, and, if possible, release code or scripts to facilitate verification.

Overall assessment:
This is a neat and promising idea with potentially meaningful impact, but the current evidence leaves open the possibility that the observed gains are artifacts of scaling or model choice. With clearer methodology, scale-insensitive baselines, and stronger robustness checks, I would be much more confident in the contribution. At present, I view the paper as borderline: novel and interesting, but in need of additional validation and clarity.
