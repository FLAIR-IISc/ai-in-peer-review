Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning. Step 1 formulates matrix completion on a unified bilingual document–term matrix: rows are documents, columns are the concatenated vocabularies from two languages, parallel pairs are fully observed rows, and non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language. The objective combines a trace norm (for low rank), an L1 penalty (for sparsity), squared loss on observed entries, a small penalty on missing entries to avoid overfitting, and nonnegativity constraints. A projected gradient descent algorithm with shrinkage is used, and convergence is established via non-expansiveness arguments. Step 2 applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to obtain a shared low-dimensional space, on which a linear SVM is trained.

The approach is compared against standard cross-lingual representation methods that also use parallel data: CL-LSI [Littman et al.], CL-KCCA [Vinokourov et al.], and CL-OPCA [Platt et al.]. Experiments on Amazon multilingual reviews (English, French, German, Japanese) across 18 source–target–category tasks show that the proposed method generally outperforms these baselines, with especially strong gains when only a small number of parallel documents is available.

Quality
Technical soundness: The optimization problem is a reasonable convex relaxation of low-rank, sparse, nonnegative recovery with squared losses. The projected gradient method with nuclear-norm shrinkage is standard, and the convergence result is appropriate for the chosen operator splitting. The design choice to regularize missing entries (ρ) is sensible to prevent overfitting to observed entries.

Empirical support: The experimental setup is appropriate for cross-lingual sentiment with limited target labels. The method is compared against strong representation-learning baselines that also exploit parallel data. However, two issues limit the strength of the conclusions:

- Potential scaling confound: Because the final classifier is a linear SVM, performance can be sensitive to feature scaling and normalization. Since the method includes matrix completion followed by LSI, the resulting scaling of features may differ from CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA in ways that favor SVM. Please clarify whether documents were L2-normalized before/after LSI across all methods, and whether per-feature rescaling was matched.

- Missing ablations: To isolate where the gains come from, please report:
  (a) Using M* (completed matrix) directly with SVM (no LSI), and
  (b) Using LSI on the original (partially observed) matrix with missing entries set to zero, which is essentially CL-LSI, but under exactly the same preprocessing and normalization as your method.
These ablations would make clear whether the improvement is due to better imputation, to LSI, or to their interaction.

Robustness: To test for classifier dependence and scaling sensitivity, I recommend adding a classifier less sensitive to feature scaling, such as gradient boosted decision trees (e.g., in Weka) or a tree ensemble. If the two-step representation also improves performance for trees, the claims of robust cross-lingual representation would be more convincing.

Efficiency and reproducibility: Please provide runtime, number of iterations to convergence, and any use of partial SVDs in the shrinkage step, compared to baselines. Reporting sensitivity to γ, ρ, k, and τ would help practitioners.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with clear motivation and method description, and a useful convergence proof. Two points need clarification:

- Unified document–term matrix (line ~113): Please specify precisely how the unified vocabulary is constructed. Do identical surface forms across languages share a single column or are they treated as distinct (e.g., “Merkel” in German vs. English mentions)? An explicit example row for a parallel pair and a non-parallel document would remove ambiguity. Also clarify tokenization, stop-word handling, and whether TF–IDF is computed per language or over the concatenated corpus.

- Experimental description: Some details appear split between the text and the table/figures. Please consolidate the setup: number of labeled target examples (100), whether all labeled source examples are used, how model selection was performed (tuned on EFB and reused for all tasks), and whether the same k is used across methods. A brief summary of the 18 tasks in-text would help readers navigate without repeatedly consulting tables/figures.

Originality
The core idea—performing matrix completion to impute missing cross-language features in a concatenated bilingual matrix, followed by LSI—is, to my knowledge, novel relative to prior cross-lingual LSI, OPCA, and KCCA methods that operate directly on observed concatenations or paired projections. Combining nuclear norm minimization with nonnegativity and sparsity constraints for this problem is a meaningful methodological contribution.

Significance
If the gains persist after controlling for scaling and classifier choice, this would be a useful advance: (i) it leverages relatively small parallel corpora effectively; (ii) it yields a conceptually simple representation pipeline usable with standard classifiers; and (iii) it offers a theoretically grounded imputation approach. The empirical improvements, especially in the low-parallel-data regime, suggest practical impact.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated two-step formulation with a provably convergent algorithm.
- Broad comparison against representative cross-lingual baselines.
- Strong empirical performance, particularly with few parallel documents.
- Method does not require translating all training data; uses only a small parallel set.

Cons
- Possible sensitivity to feature scaling with linear SVM; normalization details unclear.
- Missing ablations to disentangle contributions of matrix completion vs. LSI.
- Some ambiguity in construction of the unified vocabulary and preprocessing.
- Limited discussion of computational cost and parameter sensitivity.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations (M* only; standardized CL-LSI) and a scale-insensitive classifier (e.g., boosted trees).
- Clarify vocabulary construction, handling of shared forms, and TF–IDF computation.
- Report normalization steps consistently across methods and their impact.
- Provide runtime/iteration counts and sensitivity analyses for γ, ρ, k, τ.
- Consider releasing code/scripts to facilitate reproducibility.

Overall, this is a neat and promising idea with solid theoretical grounding. Addressing the above concerns—especially the potential scaling confound and ablation studies—would substantially strengthen the paper.
