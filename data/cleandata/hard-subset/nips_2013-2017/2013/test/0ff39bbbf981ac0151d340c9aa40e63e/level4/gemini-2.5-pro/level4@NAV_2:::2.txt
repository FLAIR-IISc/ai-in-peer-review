**Summary**
This paper proposes a two-step method for learning cross-lingual document representations, which is particularly useful for cross-language text classification when labeled data in the target language is scarce. The core idea is to first construct a unified document-term matrix for both source and target languages and then treat the problem as one of matrix completion to infer unobserved cross-lingual term features. This completed matrix is then used as input to Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to induce a dense, low-dimensional shared representation. The authors formulate the matrix completion step as a convex optimization problem, which is solved using a projected gradient descent algorithm with proven convergence guarantees. The method is evaluated on a cross-lingual sentiment classification task using the Amazon product reviews dataset, where it is shown to outperform several existing methods, especially in scenarios with a limited number of parallel documents.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The claims are well-supported by extensive experiments on 18 different cross-lingual classification tasks. The proposed Two-Step Learning (TSL) method is rigorously compared against a monolingual baseline (TBOW) as well as established cross-lingual representation learning methods, including CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA. The inclusion of an analysis on the impact of varying the amount of parallel data effectively demonstrates the robustness of the proposed approach.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow. The authors have done a commendable job of detailing their methodology, optimization algorithm, and experimental setup. The inclusion of parameter selection details enhances the reproducibility of the work. One minor suggestion for improvement: while the motivation for using matrix completion is explained in Section 3, the introduction could be strengthened by briefly foreshadowing this key idea to better frame the paper's contribution from the outset.

**Originality:** While the task of cross-lingual representation learning is not new, the application of matrix completion as a preceding step to dimensionality reduction in this context is novel. Unlike prior work such as CL-LSI or CL-OPCA that operates directly on a sparse, concatenated document-term matrix, this paper's primary contribution is to first infer the missing feature entries. This conceptual shift is clearly articulated, and the work is appropriately contextualized within the existing literature.

**Significance:** The experimental results are significant, showing that the proposed method consistently and often substantially outperforms the compared methods. The finding that matrix completion can serve as an effective preprocessing step for cross-lingual tasks is an important contribution. This formulation may prove effective for other cross-domain NLP problems characterized by sparse or incomplete feature overlaps. Furthermore, the method's strong performance even with a small number of parallel documents makes it particularly valuable for practical applications where such resources are limited.
