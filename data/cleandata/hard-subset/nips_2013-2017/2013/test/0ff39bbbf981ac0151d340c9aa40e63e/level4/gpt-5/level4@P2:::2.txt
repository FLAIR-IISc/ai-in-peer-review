Summary: This paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-lingual document representation that combines matrix completion with latent semantic indexing (LSI). The authors construct a unified bilingual document-term matrix in which non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language’s vocabulary. They formulate recovery of these entries as a convex matrix completion problem with trace-norm and sparsity regularization under non-negativity constraints, optimized via a projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence guarantees. LSI is then applied to the completed matrix to produce a shared low-dimensional representation on which standard monolingual classifiers can be trained. Experiments on multilingual Amazon product reviews (English, French, German, Japanese) for cross-lingual sentiment classification show consistent gains over a target-language bag-of-words baseline and over cross-lingual representation methods such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA. Learning curves w.r.t. the number of unlabeled parallel documents indicate strong performance even with limited parallel data.

Quality: The method is technically sound. The optimization is clearly specified, convex after relaxation, and accompanied by a convergence analysis of the projected gradient procedure. The experimental setup is thorough: 18 transfer tasks, a fixed low-resource target setting (100 labeled target examples), repeated trials, and reasonable baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) with shared classifiers (linear SVM) and transparent hyperparameter selection. Results consistently support the claims of improved accuracy and robustness to reduced parallel data.

Clarity: The paper is well organized and readable. Problem formulation, algorithmic details (including step sizes and constraints), and parameter-selection procedures are clearly described, aiding reproducibility. Minor suggestion: motivate the matrix completion step more explicitly in the Introduction to foreground why completing the bilingual document-term matrix before LSI better leverages cross-lingual co-occurrence structure than applying LSI directly.

Originality: While cross-lingual representation learning is well studied, using matrix completion to impute missing cross-language features prior to LSI is a novel and well-justified contribution. The distinction from prior approaches that directly apply LSI, KCCA, or OPCA to concatenated bilingual matrices is clear.

Significance: The two-step method yields consistent and sometimes substantial gains over strong baselines across languages and domains, and remains effective with few parallel documents. This demonstrates that matrix completion is a powerful preprocessing strategy for inducing cross-lingual semantic spaces and may generalize to other multilingual tasks beyond sentiment classification.
