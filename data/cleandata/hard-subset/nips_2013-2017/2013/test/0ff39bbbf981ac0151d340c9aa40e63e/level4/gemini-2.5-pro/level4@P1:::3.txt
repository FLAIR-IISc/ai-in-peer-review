This paper presents a novel two-step method for learning cross-lingual document representations, aimed at improving cross-language text classification, particularly when parallel data is scarce. The core idea is to first construct a unified document-term matrix for documents from both a source and target language. This matrix has many missing entries, as monolingual documents only have features for their own language. The first step of the proposed method uses matrix completion to infer these missing values, effectively creating a complete, low-rank bilingual representation for every document. The second step applies Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to this completed matrix to produce a dense, low-dimensional semantic space where a standard classifier can be trained. The method is evaluated on a cross-lingual sentiment classification task using Amazon product reviews, where it shows superior performance compared to several baselines, especially with a small number of parallel documents.

**Strengths:**
*   The proposed combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning is a novel and interesting idea. Using matrix completion to infer a complete document-term matrix is a well-principled approach to leveraging co-occurrence patterns both within and across languages.
*   The experimental results, as presented, are strong. The method consistently outperforms several relevant baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) across numerous language pairs and domains.
*   The method's robustness with very limited parallel data is a significant practical advantage, as large parallel corpora are often unavailable.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

My primary concern revolves around the robustness of the experimental results and whether the observed gains are an artifact of the chosen classifier. The paper relies exclusively on a linear Support Vector Machine (SVM) for evaluation. The performance of a linear SVM is known to be sensitive to the scaling and geometric properties of the input features. The different representation learning methods compared (LSI, OPCA, KCCA) produce feature spaces with distinct characteristics. It is plausible that the representation generated by the proposed TSL method is simply better scaled or more linearly separable for an SVM than the representations produced by the baseline methods. Consequently, the reported performance improvements may not be solely attributable to the superior quality of the semantic representation itself, but rather to a favorable interaction with the specific classifier used.

To strengthen the claims, I would strongly encourage the authors to re-evaluate their method using a classifier that is insensitive to monotonic feature transformations and scaling, such as boosted decision trees or random forests. If the proposed TSL method still outperforms the baselines when using such a classifier, it would provide much more compelling evidence that the learned representation is intrinsically better.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Quality:** The technical approach is sound, and the convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm is provided. However, as noted above, the experimental validation could be made more robust. The central claim—that this two-step process produces a better representation—is not fully convincing without testing it with classifiers that are not sensitive to feature scaling artifacts.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but a few points could be clarified:
    *   In Section 3 (around line 113), the paper describes the construction of a "unified document-term matrix". It would be helpful to clarify how the vocabulary is unified. For instance, are words from different languages treated as distinct features (e.g., 'cat_en' and 'chat_fr'), or are identical surface forms (e.g., proper nouns) collapsed into a single feature? This detail is important for understanding the structure of the matrix being completed.
    *   The description of the experimental setup is somewhat distributed. While the core information is present, integrating the details from the caption of Table 1 into the main text of Section 5.2 would improve readability.

*   **Originality:** The idea of applying matrix completion to a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix before performing dimensionality reduction appears to be novel. It is a creative combination of existing, well-understood techniques to address a challenging problem.

*   **Significance:** If the results are shown to be robust beyond the specific classifier used, the contribution would be significant. Matrix completion is a powerful and well-controlled technique, and its successful application here could open up a new direction for cross-lingual learning. The method's effectiveness with small amounts of parallel data makes it particularly valuable for low-resource scenarios.

**Summary of Recommendation:**
This paper introduces a neat and novel idea for cross-lingual representation learning. However, I have reservations about whether the strong results are truly due to the quality of the representation or are an artifact of its favorable scaling for the linear SVM classifier. A simple additional experiment with a scale-invariant classifier (e.g., boosted trees) would greatly strengthen the paper and make me more confident in the results.
