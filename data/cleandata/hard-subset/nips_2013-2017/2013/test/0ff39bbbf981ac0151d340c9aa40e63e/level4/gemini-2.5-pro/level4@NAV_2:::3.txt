This paper proposes a novel two-step method for learning cross-lingual document representations. The core idea is to leverage a small set of parallel documents to bridge the vocabulary gap between a source and a target language. The first step involves constructing a unified document-term matrix for all documents in both languages. This matrix is inherently sparse, as monolingual documents have missing entries for the vocabulary of the other language. The authors formulate a matrix completion problem, relaxed as a convex optimization problem with trace norm and l1 regularization, to infer these missing entries. The second step applies Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to the resulting completed matrix to produce a low-dimensional, dense representation shared across both languages. This approach is similar in spirit to methods like CL-LSI, but it introduces the matrix completion step as a novel, principled way to densify the cross-lingual document-term matrix before dimensionality reduction. The method is evaluated on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using the Amazon product reviews dataset, showing superior performance over several baselines.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion with LSI for this task is a novel and interesting idea. It directly addresses the problem of missing cross-lingual information in a unified document matrix, which is often handled by simply treating missing values as zeros.
*   **Significance:** If the results are robust, the method could be a significant contribution. Applying a well-understood and powerful technique like matrix completion offers a new perspective on cross-lingual representation learning. The demonstrated effectiveness with a small number of parallel documents is particularly promising for low-resource scenarios.
*   **Technical Quality:** The optimization problem is well-formulated, and the paper provides a projected gradient descent algorithm with a formal convergence analysis, which adds to the technical soundness of the work.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Robustness of Experimental Validation:** The primary weakness of the paper lies in the experimental evaluation. The performance gains are demonstrated using only a linear SVM as the downstream classifier. Since linear SVMs are sensitive to the scaling and geometry of the feature space, it is unclear whether the observed improvements stem from the inherent quality of the learned representations or from the fact that the proposed two-step process produces representations that are coincidentally better scaled for a linear SVM compared to the baselines (e.g., CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA). This makes it difficult to definitively attribute the performance gains to the representation learning method itself.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**
1.  **Classifier Choice:** To substantially strengthen the paper's claims, this reviewer urges the authors to supplement their experiments with a classifier that is insensitive to monotonic transformations and scaling of individual features. For example, tree-based ensemble methods like Gradient Boosted Decision Trees or Random Forests would be excellent choices. If the proposed TSL method maintains its superior performance with such classifiers, it would provide much more convincing evidence for the robustness and general utility of the learned representations.

2.  **Clarity on Matrix Construction:** The paper mentions constructing "a unified document-term matrix for all documents". Could the authors please clarify how terms are unified across languages? For example, are orthographically identical words in two different languages (e.g., proper nouns, cognates) treated as a single feature in the unified vocabulary, or are they kept distinct (e.g., "word_en" and "word_fr")? This is a crucial detail for understanding the model and for reproducibility.

3.  **Clarity on Experimental Setup:** The description of the experimental methodology is somewhat fragmented, with details spread between the main text and the caption of Table 1. It would improve the paper's clarity if the experimental setup (e.g., train/test splits, use of parallel data, parameter selection protocol) was described in a single, self-contained section.

In summary, the paper presents a neat and well-motivated idea. However, the experimental validation needs to be more thorough to rule out the possibility that the results are an artifact of the chosen classifier.
