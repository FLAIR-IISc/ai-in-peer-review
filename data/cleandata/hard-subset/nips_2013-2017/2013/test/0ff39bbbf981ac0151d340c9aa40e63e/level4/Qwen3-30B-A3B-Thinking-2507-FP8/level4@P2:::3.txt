

The paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that first uses matrix completion to fill missing entries in a document-term matrix constructed from parallel bilingual documents, then applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to induce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. This approach differs from previous methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, which directly apply dimensionality reduction techniques to the original concatenated document-term matrix without addressing its sparsity.

The paper's main contributions are: 1) Formulating the problem as a matrix completion task to recover missing features from bilingual documents; 2) Providing a projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence guarantees; and 3) Demonstrating superior performance on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks, particularly with limited parallel data.

Quality: The technical approach is sound, with a well-formulated optimization problem and rigorous convergence analysis. However, the experimental evaluation has a notable limitation. The authors exclusively use linear SVMs for classification, which are highly sensitive to feature scaling. The observed performance gains could potentially stem from the scaling properties of LSI rather than the matrix completion step itself. To strengthen the evaluation, the authors should test their method with classifiers less sensitive to feature scaling, such as boosted decision trees or random forests. Without this validation, it remains unclear whether the improvements are due to the proposed two-step method or merely the scaling behavior of LSI with linear SVMs.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-structured but contains several clarity issues. The construction of the unified document-term matrix (Section 3) requires clarification: it's unclear whether the matrix uses a single term for each surface form across languages (e.g., "Merkel" for both English and German) or distinct terms for each language (e.g., "Merkel" and "Merkelen"). This distinction is critical for understanding how the method bridges language gaps. Additionally, the experimental setup description is fragmented across multiple sections and tables, making it difficult to follow. A more cohesive presentation of the experimental design would significantly improve clarity.

Originality: The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to cross-lingual representation learning. While matrix completion has been used in related contexts, the specific two-step method proposed here—first completing the matrix and then applying LSI—appears to be new. The authors properly situate their work relative to previous approaches, demonstrating a thorough understanding of the field. The distinction between their method and existing techniques like CL-LSI (which applies LSI directly to the original matrix) is clear and well-justified.

Significance: If the results are robust, this work would be significant because it demonstrates that effective cross-lingual representation learning can be achieved with minimal parallel data—a key practical challenge in cross-lingual learning where large parallel corpora are often unavailable. The experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms existing approaches, particularly when the number of parallel documents is small (as demonstrated in Section 5.3). This addresses an important practical limitation of current methods.

Strengths: The technical formulation is elegant and well-supported with theoretical guarantees. The experimental results are compelling, particularly the robustness to small parallel datasets. The paper properly situates its work within the literature, and the two-step approach is conceptually straightforward yet effective.

Weaknesses: The limited classifier evaluation (only linear SVMs) is the most significant concern. The paper would benefit from additional experiments with classifiers that are insensitive to feature scaling. The clarity issues regarding matrix construction and experimental setup also need addressing.

Overall, this is a promising contribution to cross-lingual learning that addresses an important problem with a novel technical approach. The results are suggestive but require additional validation to fully establish their robustness. I recommend acceptance with the caveat that the authors address the classifier dependency in the camera-ready version. The significance of the work would be substantially enhanced with this additional validation, making it a strong contribution to the field of cross-lingual representation learning.
