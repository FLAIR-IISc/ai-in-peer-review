- Proposes a novel two-step method: matrix completion to impute missing cross-lingual term information, followed by LSI to obtain shared low-dimensional document representations.  
- Builds a unified document-term matrix for source and target languages where monolingual docs have structurally missing entries, framing imputation as matrix completion.  
- Formulates a convex optimization problem enforcing low-rank, sparsity, and non-negativity, solved via projected gradient descent with a convergence guarantee.  
- Differs from prior CL-LSI approaches by explicitly imputing missing entries instead of implicitly treating them as zeros.  
- Empirically outperforms strong baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA, monolingual) on Amazon product review sentiment classification across 18 cross-lingual tasks.  
- Demonstrates strong robustness in low-resource settings, maintaining good performance with as few as ~200 parallel documents.  
- Technical formulation is well-motivated and justified (trace norm for low-rank recovery, L1 for sparsity, loss terms for observed/unobserved entries).  
- Inclusion of convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm adds significant technical rigor.  
- Experimental methodology and parameter selection are clearly described, facilitating reproducibility.  
- No major technical flaws identified; overall work is high quality and complete.  
- Minor clarity suggestion: Introduction should better motivate matrix completion by highlighting the structurally missing data in the combined vocabulary matrix.  
- Originality lies in the novel combination and application of matrix completion + LSI for cross-lingual representation learning.  
- Significance: results are likely impactful for researchers and practitioners, especially where parallel corpora are scarce.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel formulation, strong empirical results, practical relevance, and technical soundness.  
- Argument against acceptance: contribution is primarily a novel combination of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new algorithm or theory, though empirical gains are compelling.
