The paper introduces a two-stage approach to translingual representation learning: first, use matrix completion to construct multilingual vectors for each row, and then apply CL-LSI to these vectors.

Quality: The idea is promising, but I am not convinced the results are robust. The observed gains may simply reflect LSI’s favorable scaling behavior relative to OPCA or CCA when combined with a linear SVM. I recommend evaluating a classifier that is insensitive to per-feature rescaling, such as boosted decision trees (e.g., in Weka). If boosted trees on top of the proposed two-step representation outperform competing techniques, I would find the evidence much more compelling.

Significance: If the improvements hold under scale-invariant evaluation, the contribution would be noteworthy, particularly because matrix completion is a well-understood nonlinear inference tool.

Novelty: The approach appears novel.

Clarity: Several points need clarification. Line 113 states that you “construct a unified document–term matrix for all documents.” Does this imply a single, shared vocabulary where identical surface forms across languages map to the same term (e.g., “Merkel”), or do you maintain language-specific surface forms (e.g., “Merkelen,” “Merkelde”) as distinct terms? Please clarify the tokenization and vocabulary construction across languages. In addition, the experimental setup is fragmented around Table 1, which made it difficult to reconstruct; consolidating the description and clearly enumerating datasets, preprocessing, features, and evaluation protocols would improve readability.

Overall: This is a neat idea. However, without experiments using classifiers beyond a linear SVM—especially ones robust to feature scaling—it is hard to determine whether the reported gains reflect genuine representational improvements or a scaling artifact.
