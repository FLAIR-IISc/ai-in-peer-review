This paper addresses the task of cross-language text classification, where labeled data in a target language is scarce. The authors propose a novel two-step method called TSL (Two-Step Learning) that leverages labeled data from a source language and a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. The core idea is to first create a unified document-term matrix containing all documents from both languages, represented over a concatenated vocabulary. This matrix has many missing entries, as a monolingual document has no observed features for words from the other language. In the first step, the authors frame the problem as one of matrix completion, recovering a complete, low-rank, and sparse document-term matrix using a projected gradient descent algorithm. In the second step, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to this completed matrix to derive a low-dimensional, dense cross-lingual representation. A standard monolingual classifier can then be trained on this shared representation. This approach differs from prior work, such as CL-LSI or CL-KCCA, which typically apply dimensionality reduction directly to a concatenated matrix of parallel documents without an explicit completion step. The paper provides a convergence guarantee for its optimization algorithm and presents strong empirical results on a cross-language sentiment classification task.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Originality:** The primary contribution is the novel formulation of cross-lingual representation learning as a matrix completion problem. This is an elegant and principled way to infer missing cross-lingual feature values and create a shared representation space, moving beyond simple concatenation or translation-based approaches.

2.  **Significance and Empirical Performance:** The method demonstrates significant improvements over several strong baselines, including CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, across 18 cross-language classification tasks. The most compelling result is the method's robustness when the number of unlabeled parallel documents is small. This is a highly significant finding, as it suggests the approach is particularly well-suited for low-resource scenarios where parallel corpora are limited.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed projected gradient descent algorithm is theoretically grounded with a formal convergence proof, which adds to the technical soundness of the paper.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The proposed method is explained clearly, making the core ideas easy to follow.

**Weaknesses / Questions for Clarification:**

The paper is promising, but several points could be clarified to strengthen the contribution.

1.  **Conceptual Justification:**
    *   The claim that "a fully observed document-term matrix is naturally low-rank" (Section 3.1) warrants more discussion. While document-term matrices are sparse, it is not immediately obvious that they should be low-rank, as this would imply that document vectors are linear combinations of a small number of basis documents. A more detailed justification for this core assumption would be beneficial.
    *   The paper could provide more intuition on why the matrix completion formulation (TSL) is fundamentally better than multi-view methods like CCA. CCA-based approaches seem like a natural fit for aligning two feature spaces using parallel data. What specific inductive bias is introduced by enforcing a low-rank structure on the combined matrix that makes it more effective than explicitly maximizing the correlation between language-specific projections?

2.  **Methodological Details:**
    *   The motivation for the second LSI step could be expanded. The paper states it is to handle "feature noise and sparseness," but the completed matrix M* should already be dense and have imputed values. Is the primary motivation for LSI to reduce dimensionality for computational efficiency, or does it offer other semantic benefits over the completed matrix? A brief justification for choosing LSI over other dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., PCA) would also be helpful.

3.  **Experimental Clarity and Design:**
    *   The experimental setup described in Section 5.2 could be made more explicit. To confirm, was the training set for the classifier composed of (a) all 4000 labeled source documents, (b) 100 randomly sampled labeled target documents, and (c) representations learned using these documents plus 2000 unlabeled parallel documents? Is the model then tested on the remaining 1900 documents from the target language?
    *   There appears to be a minor inconsistency in the reported results. In Table 1, TSL outperforms CL-KCCA on the EFM and EGM tasks. However, in Figure 1, CL-KCCA appears to have a slight performance advantage at the 2000 parallel document mark for these same tasks. Could the authors clarify this discrepancy?
    *   The experiments combine the effects of two different data sources: labeled source-language data and unlabeled parallel data. An ablation study that isolates the impact of each (e.g., performance with only parallel data, or with varying amounts of labeled source data) would provide deeper insight into the method's behavior.

4.  **Practical Considerations:**
    *   A discussion of the computational cost of the proposed algorithm would be valuable. How does the running time of the iterative projected gradient descent algorithm scale with the number of documents and vocabulary size, and how does it compare empirically to the baselines (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, etc.)? This information is important for assessing the practical applicability of the method.

**Minor Suggestions:**

*   In the paragraph before Equation (2), the index notation "(ij)" should be changed to "(i, j)" for consistency.
*   In the first sentence of Section 4.1, the phrase "such as f(M) = ..." could be rephrased for clarity, e.g., "This algorithm decomposes the objective function f(M) in (2) into a non-smooth term and a smooth term: f(M) = ...".

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents a novel, simple, and effective method for cross-language representation learning. The core idea of using matrix completion is original and the empirical results, especially in low-resource settings, are significant. While the paper is strong, it would be improved by addressing the points of clarification raised above, particularly regarding the low-rank assumption, experimental details, and computational cost. Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field.
