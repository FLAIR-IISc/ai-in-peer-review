Summary
The paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-lingual text classification that learns a shared document representation across languages using only a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 formulates matrix completion on a unified bilingual document-term matrix (constructed by concatenating vocabularies) to infer missing entries for non-parallel documents. The objective combines a trace norm (low-rank), an L1 term (sparsity), squared loss on observed entries, and a small quadratic penalty on missing entries, with a nonnegativity constraint. The authors solve this convex problem via a projected gradient descent method with singular value shrinkage and provide a convergence proof under standard step-size conditions. Step 2 applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation on which a monolingual linear SVM is trained.

The work relates to prior cross-lingual representation methods that use translation or bilingual resources (e.g., CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], CL-OPCA [15]) and multi-view/co-training approaches [2, 22]. Unlike methods that directly apply LSI/KCCA/OPCA to concatenated representations, this paper first imputes missing features via matrix completion to better exploit cross-language co-occurrence structure.

On multilingual Amazon reviews (Books, DVD, Music; English, French, German, Japanese), the method consistently outperforms a target-only bag-of-words baseline and three cross-lingual baselines (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) across 18 transfer tasks, and remains robust when the number of parallel documents is small.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The optimization objective is a standard convex surrogate for low-rank completion with additional sparsity and nonnegativity. The projected gradient descent with singular value shrinkage is appropriate, and the convergence argument (via non-expansive mappings) is clear. The design choices (trace norm + L1 + nonnegativity) are reasonable, though more justification would help (see below).
- Empirical evaluation: Experiments are well structured, spanning 18 language/category pairs. Hyperparameters are selected once and reused across tasks, reducing overfitting risk. Results are averaged over multiple random draws, with standard deviations reported. The analysis of performance as a function of the number of parallel documents is particularly informative, showing strong performance even with 200 parallel pairs.
- Missing elements: Runtime and scalability are not discussed, despite repeated SVDs in the inner loop. Ablations (e.g., removing the L1 term, omitting the nonnegativity constraint, using the completed matrix without LSI) would clarify the contribution of each component. Statistical significance testing beyond means/standard deviations would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The problem setup, notation (Ω, Ω̂), and algorithmic steps are described precisely, and all hyperparameters and selection procedures are provided, aiding reproducibility. Minor suggestions:
- Strengthen the motivation in the introduction for matrix completion in this setting (why completion before LSI is preferable to direct CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA on sparse, partially observed bilingual matrices).
- Clarify whether the parallel documents are disjoint from labeled data to avoid leakage.
- Provide details on vocabulary sizes, sparsity levels, and computational costs (iterations, wall-clock times, hardware).

Originality
Applying convex matrix completion (with trace norm, L1, and nonnegativity) to impute a bilingual document-term matrix prior to LSI is a novel and well-motivated combination. While both matrix completion and CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA are established individually, the two-step pipeline is new in this cross-lingual classification context and empirically effective. One omission is a comparison to structural correspondence learning (SCL) [16], which is a strong and widely used cross-lingual baseline of the era.

Significance
The method achieves consistent gains over strong baselines and is robust to scarce parallel data, a practically important regime. The approach is simple to implement and potentially generalizes to other cross-lingual tasks beyond sentiment classification. Broader significance would be strengthened by:
- Demonstrating scalability on larger vocabularies/corpora.
- Evaluating on non-English pivot pairs if available (e.g., FR–DE) to assess dependence on English as a hub.
- Testing sensitivity to the amount of labeled target data (beyond 100 instances).

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, principled formulation with convergence guarantees.
- Consistent and substantial empirical improvements across 18 tasks.
- Robust performance with very limited parallel data.
- Reproducible experimental setup; sensible hyperparameter protocol.
- Method is broadly applicable and leverages standard tools (completion + LSI).

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Lacks runtime/scalability analysis; repeated SVDs may limit applicability at scale.
- Missing ablations to isolate the benefits of L1 and nonnegativity, and of the LSI step after completion.
- No comparison to SCL [16] or co-training approaches [22], which are relevant and strong baselines of the period.
- Evaluation limited to sentiment on Amazon reviews and primarily English-centric pairs.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add runtime, memory, and convergence diagnostics, and discuss potential accelerations (e.g., randomized or truncated SVD).
- Include ablations: (i) remove L1; (ii) remove nonnegativity; (iii) classify directly on the completed matrix without LSI; (iv) vary ρ to show its effect.
- Compare to SCL and (if feasible) multilingual topic models [19] or co-training [22].
- Explore transfer with varying target-labeled sizes and, if data permits, non-English pairs.
- Expand the introduction’s motivation for matrix completion in cross-lingual settings and provide an intuition for why completion helps relative to direct CL-LSI.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and well-executed paper with a simple, elegant idea that delivers robust empirical gains. With added comparisons and scalability analysis, it would be even stronger. I lean toward acceptance.
