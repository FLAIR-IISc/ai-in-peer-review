### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel two-stage approach for translingual representation learning. The core idea is to first leverage matrix completion to generate dense, multilingual vector representations for terms from sparse, parallel data. Subsequently, Cross-Lingual Latent Semantic Indexing (CL-LSI) is applied to these completed vectors to derive the final representations. The method is evaluated on a cross-lingual document classification task, where it is shown to outperform other techniques like Orthogonal Procrustes Correspondence Analysis (OPCA) and Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) when a linear SVM is used as the classifier.

**Strengths**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed combination of matrix completion and CL-LSI appears to be novel in this domain. It presents an interesting way to address the data sparsity issue inherent in creating cross-lingual embeddings.

*   **Significance:** If the empirical gains are shown to be robust, the method offers an elegant way to incorporate a well-controlled non-linear inference technique (matrix completion) into the pipeline for cross-lingual representation learning. This is an interesting and promising direction that could inspire further work.

**Weaknesses and Major Concerns**

The primary concern with this paper lies in the experimental validation, which may not be sufficient to demonstrate the robustness of the proposed method's benefits. The reported performance improvements are based on a linear SVM classifier. It is well-known that the performance of linear SVMs can be sensitive to the scaling of input features.

The gains observed for the proposed CL-LSI-based method might stem from the particular scaling properties of LSI, which could be more amenable to a linear SVM than the scaling produced by the baseline methods (OPCA and CCA). This raises the question of whether the observed advantage is a genuine modeling improvement or a coincidental artifact of the classifier choice. Without further experiments, it is difficult to be certain that the proposed method is truly superior.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

To substantiate the paper's claims, I strongly urge the authors to re-evaluate their method using a classification model that is insensitive to monotonic transformations and scaling of individual features. A strong candidate would be a gradient-boosted decision tree ensemble (implementations are readily available in toolkits like Weka or scikit-learn). If the proposed two-step system maintains its superior performance with such a classifier, the evidence for its effectiveness would be significantly more compelling.

**Clarity Issues**

The paper requires clarification on several points to improve readability and reproducibility:

1.  On line 113, the authors mention constructing a "unified document-term matrix for all documents." It is unclear how terms are unified across languages. For instance, are different surface forms of the same entity (e.g., "Merkel" in English and its various declensions in German) treated as a single, unified term, or are they maintained as distinct, language-specific terms? This detail is crucial for understanding the model's construction.

2.  The description of the experimental setup could be improved. Key details are currently distributed throughout the text and within Table 1, which required some effort to piece together. Consolidating the description of the datasets, preprocessing steps, and evaluation protocol into a single, cohesive section would greatly enhance the paper's readability.

**Overall Assessment**

In summary, the paper presents an interesting and novel idea for translingual representation learning. However, the current experimental evidence is not fully convincing due to its reliance on a classifier sensitive to feature scaling. If the authors can demonstrate the method's robustness by employing a different class of classifiers and can clarify the methodological and experimental details mentioned above, this paper could represent a valuable contribution to the field. As it stands, the central claim is not sufficiently supported.
