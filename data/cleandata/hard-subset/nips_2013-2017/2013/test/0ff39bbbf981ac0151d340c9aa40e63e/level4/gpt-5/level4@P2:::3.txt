Summary
This paper addresses cross-language text classification by learning a shared document representation from bilingual data. The key idea is a two-step procedure: (1) construct a unified bilingual document-term matrix that concatenates vocabularies from two languages, treat unobserved entries as missing, and recover the full matrix via convex matrix completion (trace norm plus sparsity with nonnegativity) using a projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence guarantees; (2) apply LSI to the recovered matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation and train a monolingual classifier. Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews (18 English–French/German/Japanese sentiment transfer tasks) compare against TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, showing consistent gains, especially when few parallel documents are available.

Relation to prior work
The approach is closest to CL-LSI (Littman et al.), CL-KCCA (Vinokourov et al.), and CL-OPCA (Platt et al.), which use parallel/comparable corpora to induce shared spaces, as well as multi-view/co-training methods. The main distinction is performing convex matrix completion over a unified bilingual document-term matrix before dimensionality reduction, rather than applying LSI/CCA/OPCA directly to the original concatenated matrix. Using small amounts of parallel data is a practical strength.

Quality
Strengths
- Clear formulation: convex relaxation with trace norm and L1, nonnegativity, and a simple projected gradient scheme; the convergence argument is standard and appropriate.
- Empirical coverage: 18 transfer tasks across three language pairs and three domains, plus an analysis of performance versus number of parallel documents.
- Performance: consistent improvements over CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, with especially strong results when parallel data is scarce.

Concerns and questions
- Potential scaling/normalization confound: Since LSI and linear SVM are both sensitive to feature scaling, improvements might partially reflect a favorable rescaling rather than a more informative representation. Please report results with classifiers less sensitive to feature scaling (e.g., boosted decision trees or random forests; boosted decision trees are readily available in Weka). If the two-step representation improves these as well, that would strengthen the claim of robustness.
- Ablations: It is important to quantify the contribution of matrix completion versus LSI. Please include:
  • LSI on the original concatenated matrix (no completion).
  • Using the completed high-dimensional matrix with a classifier (no LSI).
  • Effects of nonnegativity and the ℓ1 term.
  • Sensitivity to γ, ρ, and the regularization on missing entries.
- Inductive vs transductive setting: The representation is learned using “all documents from the two languages.” If this includes target test documents, the setting is transductive and can inflate performance. Please report an inductive variant that uses only source documents, labeled target training documents, and the selected parallel subset for representation learning.
- Statistical significance: Add significance tests across runs for per-task comparisons.
- Efficiency: Provide runtime, number of SVDs/iterations, memory footprint, and a comparison of computational cost to CL-KCCA and CL-OPCA.

Clarity
- Unified vocabulary: Please clarify whether the unified vocabulary keeps language-specific surface forms as distinct features (recommended), i.e., “Merkel_en” vs “Merkel_de,” and how identical strings shared across languages are handled. This will help readers understand how cross-lingual co-occurrence is mediated through the parallel rows and completion step.
- Experimental setup: Consolidate the setup in the text (numbers of labeled target examples, whether all unlabeled target documents are used in representation learning, parameter selection protocol) rather than requiring readers to infer details from tables/figures.
- Algorithm details: Specify the stopping criterion, average number of iterations, whether any adaptive stepsize or warm-starts are used, and how τ is chosen in practice.

Originality
Combining convex matrix completion on a bilingual document-term matrix with subsequent LSI for cross-lingual representation appears novel relative to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA. The idea of imputing missing cross-lingual features to exploit low-rank structure is a fresh angle.

Significance
If the gains persist across scale-invariant classifiers and in an inductive setting, the method would offer a practical, robust approach for scenarios with limited parallel data. The optimization is principled and broadly applicable, potentially influencing cross-lingual transfer beyond sentiment analysis.

Additional suggestions
- Include SCL (Prettenhofer & Stein) as a baseline if possible, since it is closely related and appears in the references.
- Evaluate on additional domains or language pairs if available, and consider non-English↔non-English transfer via English as a pivot.
- Provide qualitative examples of cross-lingual associations the completion step recovers (e.g., term alignments inferred without a lexicon).

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually simple, well-motivated, and (likely) novel combination.
- Strong empirical results, especially with few parallel documents.
- Solid optimization with convergence guarantees.

Cons against acceptance
- Possible dependence on scaling and classifier choice; missing scale-invariant evaluations.
- Transductive training risk if test documents are used in representation learning.
- Missing ablations and efficiency analysis; some clarity issues.

Overall
A neat and promising idea with encouraging results. With additional classifier-agnostic evaluations, ablations, and clarity on the inductive setting and experimental details, this would be a strong submission. As it stands, I am cautiously positive but would like to see the suggested analyses to confirm robustness.
