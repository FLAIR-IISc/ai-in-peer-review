

The paper introduces a translingual representation learning method that first employs matrix completion to generate multilingual vectors for each row, followed by CL-LSI applied to these vectors.  
Quality: While the approach is conceptually promising, the robustness of the results is questionable. The observed positive outcomes may stem solely from the scaling properties of LSI being more favorable than OPCA or CCA for a linear SVM, rather than from the proposed method itself. To establish validity, the authors should evaluate an ML classifier insensitive to individual feature rescaling—such as boosted decision trees (available in Weka). If the two-step system yields superior results with such classifiers, the claim would be compelling.  
Significance: A robust finding would be valuable, as matrix completion represents a well-controlled non-linear inference technique.  
Novelty: The core idea appears novel.  
Clarity: Several points require clarification. Line 113 states the paper constructs "a unified document-term matrix for all documents." It is unclear whether this entails using a single term per surface form (e.g., "Merkel" across languages) or labeling each language-specific surface form (e.g., "Merkelen" and "Merkelde"). The experimental setup description is fragmented across Table 1, complicating comprehension.  
Overall, the idea is promising, but the results' robustness hinges on addressing the scaling concern. The authors should evaluate alternative classifiers beyond linear SVM to determine whether the performance gain is methodological or an artifact of feature scaling.
