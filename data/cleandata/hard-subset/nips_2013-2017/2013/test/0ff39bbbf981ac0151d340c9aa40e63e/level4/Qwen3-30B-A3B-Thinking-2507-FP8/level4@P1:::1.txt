

This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that addresses the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages by leveraging unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The authors first construct a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix where parallel documents are fully observed while non-parallel documents contain missing values for the other language's vocabulary. They then formulate a matrix completion problem to recover the missing entries while enforcing low-rank and sparsity constraints, followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The approach is evaluated on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, demonstrating significant improvements over existing methods, particularly with limited parallel data.

The paper makes a clear contribution by addressing an important challenge in cross-lingual learning through matrix completion, which differs from previous approaches that directly apply techniques like LSI, KCCA, or OPCA on the original concatenated document-term matrix. This approach builds on related work such as CL-LSI [10] and CL-OPCA [15], but distinguishes itself by first completing the matrix before performing dimensionality reduction.

The paper's strengths include a well-motivated problem formulation, clear technical presentation, and strong experimental results showing consistent improvements over baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) across 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks. The authors provide a convergence proof for their projected gradient descent algorithm, which adds theoretical rigor to their method. The experimental design is thorough, with ablation studies on the impact of unlabeled parallel document size (200-2000 documents), demonstrating the method's robustness when parallel data is scarce.

However, several aspects require clarification to strengthen the paper's contribution and reproducibility:

1. The authors state that LSI is applied to the completed matrix "to address feature sparseness," but it's unclear why LSI is specifically chosen over other dimensionality reduction techniques. Could the authors elaborate on whether the choice is primarily computational (smaller k for efficiency) or whether LSI provides specific benefits for cross-lingual representations? The paper should clarify why LSI is preferred over PCA or other methods.

2. The paper combines two distinct data exploitation strategies: (a) using labeled source language data to aid target language classification, and (b) using unlabeled parallel data for representation learning. The experimental design mixes these, making it difficult to isolate their individual contributions. It would be valuable to present results that separately quantify the impact of each component.

3. The experimental setup (Section 5.2) requires clarification. The paper states they use "all documents from the two languages and the additional 2000 unlabeled parallel documents for representation learning," followed by "100 labeled target documents" for training. However, the exact composition of training data (e.g., 4,000 labeled source documents, 100 labeled target documents, 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents) and test data (1,900 labeled target documents) is not explicitly stated. Additionally, Figure 1 shows CL-KCCA outperforming TSL on EFM and EGM at 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents, while Table 1 shows TSL consistently outperforming all methods. The authors should explain this discrepancy.

4. The paper claims a fully observed document-term matrix would be "low-rank" but provides no justification. While the matrix is sparse, it's not immediately clear why the rank would be low. The authors should clarify their assumption about the rank structure of document-term matrices.

5. The paper's main advantage over multiview methods like CCA (which would naturally handle the two language views) is not fully explained. The authors should provide intuition on why enforcing low-rank on the completed matrix is more effective than directly modeling the two language views.

6. The authors should include runtime comparisons with baseline methods to assess practical scalability, especially given the large matrix size in cross-lingual learning.

The paper contains minor technical issues that should be addressed: (i) "parellel" should be corrected to "parallel" in the draft; (ii) "ij" should be "i,j" in the text before Equation (2); (iii) "such as" should be removed before Equations (4) and (5).

Overall, this is a well-motivated paper with a simple yet effective approach that demonstrates significant empirical improvements. The technical content is clear, and the method is well-presented. The minor clarifications requested would strengthen the paper's contribution and make it more valuable to the NIPS community.

Strengths:
- Novel two-step approach that first completes the document-term matrix before dimensionality reduction
- Strong empirical results showing consistent improvements over baselines
- Convergence proof for the optimization algorithm
- Demonstrated robustness with limited parallel data

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient explanation of why LSI is chosen over other dimensionality reduction techniques
- Ambiguous experimental design that combines labeled source data and unlabeled parallel data
- Unjustified claim about the low-rank nature of document-term matrices
- Lack of runtime comparison with baselines

This paper presents a valuable contribution to cross-lingual learning that would be appropriate for NIPS. With the minor clarifications addressed, it would make a strong addition to the conference.
