

The paper introduces a two-step methodology for cross-lingual representation learning that effectively leverages both labeled auxiliary language data and unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The approach first constructs a unified but incomplete document-term matrix spanning multiple languages, then employs matrix completion with low-rank and sparsity constraints to fill missing entries using projected gradient descent. Subsequently, latent semantic indexing is applied to the completed matrix to derive low-dimensional cross-lingual representations for classification. The authors provide convergence guarantees for their optimization algorithm and demonstrate superior performance through comprehensive experiments on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks.

Key technical questions:
- The paper states that LSI is applied to the completed matrix "to handle feature sparseness," but it's unclear whether this dimensionality reduction primarily serves computational efficiency (since M* becomes relatively dense) or addresses fundamental representation issues. Could the authors clarify the rationale for choosing LSI specifically over other dimensionality reduction techniques like PCA?
- The methodology appears to utilize two distinct components of external data: labeled source language documents and unlabeled parallel documents. It would be valuable to explicitly distinguish these contributions and report performance metrics separately for each component to better understand their individual impacts.
- The experimental setup requires clarification (Section 5.2). It appears the authors used 4,000 labeled source language documents, 100 labeled target language documents, and 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents for training, with classification evaluated on the remaining 1,900 target language documents. In Section 5.3, the number of unlabeled parallel documents is varied from 200 to 2,000. However, the performance differences between TSL and CL-KCCA in Figure 1 (EFM and EGM at 2,000 unlabeled documents) contradict the results in Table 1 where TSL outperforms CL-KCCA across all tasks. Could the authors explain this discrepancy?
- The claim that a fully observed document-term matrix would be low-rank requires further justification. While the matrix is sparse, it's not clear why the rank would be low, as documents are unlikely to be linear combinations of other documents in the vocabulary space, which would typically result in a rank of min(d, t) where d is vocabulary size and t is number of documents.
- The paper demonstrates that TSL outperforms methods like CL-KCCA, which seems to be a more natural approach for cross-lingual representation learning. What is the specific mechanism by which enforcing low-rank structure on M* provides advantages over CCA-based approaches? How does this compare to other low-rank methods in the literature?
- The running time of the proposed algorithm would be valuable to report. How does the computational complexity of the projected gradient descent algorithm compare to other methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA?
