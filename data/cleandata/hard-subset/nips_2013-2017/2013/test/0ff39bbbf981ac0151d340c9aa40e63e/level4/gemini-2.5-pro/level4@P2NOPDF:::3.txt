This paper presents a two-step method for translingual representation learning. The core approach involves first applying matrix completion to generate dense, multilingual vectors from sparse, monolingual document-term matrices, and then performing Cross-Lingual Latent Semantic Indexing (CL-LSI) on the resulting completed vectors.

**Quality and Significance**

The proposed approach is intriguing, as it combines matrix completion—a well-controlled non-linear inference technique—with established methods for cross-lingual learning. If the results are shown to be robust, this contribution would be a significant and novel application of this technique.

However, the central weakness of the current evaluation lies in the potential lack of robustness of the experimental results. The paper relies exclusively on a linear SVM for evaluation. The observed performance gains over baselines like OPCA and CCA might not stem from a superior representation but rather from an artifact of feature scaling. Specifically, the scaling produced by LSI may be incidentally more compatible with a linear SVM than the scaling produced by the baseline methods. This leaves open the question of whether the proposed representation itself is superior, or if the gains are merely a side effect of the classifier choice.

To properly validate the method, I strongly urge the authors to supplement their experiments with a machine learning model that is insensitive to monotonic rescaling of individual features. Boosted decision trees, for example (readily available in toolkits like Weka), would be an excellent candidate. If the proposed two-step method maintains its superior performance over the baselines when using such a classifier, it would substantially strengthen the paper's claims and demonstrate that the learned representations are indeed more effective. Without this additional validation, the central claim remains inconclusive.

**Novelty**

The core idea of using matrix completion as a precursor to CL-LSI for translingual representation learning appears to be novel.

**Clarity**

The paper is mostly clear, but two points require clarification:

1.  On line 113, the paper mentions constructing a "unified document-term matrix for all documents." This description is ambiguous. It is unclear whether this matrix treats a given surface form (e.g., "Merkel") as a single feature regardless of its language of origin, or if language-specific features are created (e.g., "Merkel_en" and "Merkel_de"). Clarifying how the vocabulary is unified is crucial for understanding the method and ensuring reproducibility.

2.  The description of the experimental setup could be improved. Key details are currently distributed between the main text and the caption of Table 1, which makes it difficult for the reader to form a complete picture of the evaluation procedure. Consolidating this information would enhance the paper's readability.

**Summary**

In summary, the paper introduces a clever and promising idea. However, the current evaluation is inconclusive because its reliance on a single, scale-sensitive classifier (linear SVM) makes it impossible to disentangle the quality of the representation from artifacts of feature scaling. Verifying the results with a classifier that is insensitive to feature scaling, such as boosted decision trees, is essential to substantiate the paper's claims.
