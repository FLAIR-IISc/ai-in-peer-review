### **Summary**

This paper introduces a two-step method for learning cross-lingual topic representations. The proposed approach first applies matrix completion to a cross-lingual term-document matrix and then uses latent semantic analysis to derive the final representation. The matrix completion problem is optimized using a projected gradient descent algorithm. In experiments conducted on a parallel Amazon review dataset for sentiment analysis, the proposed method is shown to outperform both a mono-lingual baseline and several cross-lingual baselines. Furthermore, learning curves demonstrate that the algorithm can learn highly accurate representations even with a relatively small amount of parallel data.

### **Quality**

The claims made in the paper are well-supported by thorough experiments. The proposed two-step learning algorithm is rigorously evaluated and compared against a comprehensive set of baselines, including a standard bag-of-words model, cross-lingual LSA, and two state-of-the-art cross-lingual dimensionality reduction algorithms. This careful comparison effectively showcases the advantages of the proposed method.

### **Clarity**

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. All parameters and the methods for their selection are described in sufficient detail, which should make the results easily reproducible. As a minor suggestion for improvement, I would recommend that the authors elaborate on the motivation for using matrix completion in the Introduction. While this is explained in Section 3, introducing the core intuition earlier would help frame the paper's contributions more effectively from the outset.

### **Originality**

While cross-lingual representation learning is an established research area, the application of matrix completion to this task is novel. The experimental results clearly demonstrate that this novel approach leads to superior performance compared to previous methods in the literature, validating the originality of the contribution.

### **Significance**

The performance of the proposed two-step method is consistently and significantly better than that of the evaluated baselines. This work effectively demonstrates that matrix completion can serve as a powerful pre-processing step for tasks like cross-lingual sentiment classification. The stability of the method across different sizes of unlabeled parallel data further underscores its practical value. The formulation presented here may also prove effective for a variety of other cross-lingual NLP tasks.
