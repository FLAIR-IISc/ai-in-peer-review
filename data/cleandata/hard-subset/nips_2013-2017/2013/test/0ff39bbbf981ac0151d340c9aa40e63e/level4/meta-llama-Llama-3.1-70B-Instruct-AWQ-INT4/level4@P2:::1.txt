The paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-language representation learning, which addresses the challenge of cross-language text classification by exploiting unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The approach involves two steps: first, it constructs a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix and completes this matrix using a projected gradient descent algorithm to enforce low-rank and sparsity conditions; second, it reduces the feature dimension of this completed matrix using latent semantic indexing (LSI) and then trains a standard classifier on this new representation.

The paper provides a clear motivation for the approach, discussing the limitations of existing methods and the benefits of using unlabeled parallel data. The technical details of the method are well-explained, including the matrix completion problem, the projected gradient descent algorithm, and the convergence guarantee. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing that it outperforms other cross-lingual representation learning methods, especially when the number of parallel bilingual documents is small.

However, there are some points that need clarification. For example, it would be helpful to elaborate on why the completed matrix M* lacks sufficient capacity to handle feature sparseness, and why LSI is chosen over other dimensionality reduction techniques. Additionally, the experiment settings could be clarified, including the number of labeled source language documents, labeled target language documents, and unlabeled parallel documents used for training, as well as the classification results reported on the remaining target language documents.

Some minor corrections are also suggested, such as fixing the notation (ij) to (i,j) in equation (2), and removing unnecessary phrases like "such as" in the sentences before equations (4) and (5).

Overall, the paper presents a simple, effective, and well-presented method for cross-language representation learning. With some clarifications and minor corrections, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
