

**Review**

**Summary and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that bridges the feature space gap between languages by exploiting unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The approach first constructs a partially observed document-term matrix where parallel documents are fully observed while non-parallel documents have missing values for the other language's vocabulary. It then solves a matrix completion problem to recover a low-rank, sparse matrix using projected gradient descent, followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The method is compared against CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA, and a target-bag-of-words baseline.

The work builds on prior approaches that use bilingual resources for cross-language learning, particularly CL-LSI [10] and CL-KCCA [20]. While these methods apply dimensionality reduction directly on the concatenated matrix, the authors' key innovation is to first complete the matrix using low-rank and sparse recovery before applying LSI. This distinguishes their work from translation-based methods [18, 6] and other representation learning approaches [16, 15].

**Strengths**

*Quality*: The technical approach is sound with rigorous convergence analysis for the projected gradient descent algorithm. The experimental design is thorough, with 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks across three languages (English, French, German, Japanese) and three product categories. The results consistently show that the proposed method (TSL) outperforms baselines, especially with limited parallel data (200 documents), demonstrating strong empirical validity.

*Originality*: The two-step procedure (matrix completion followed by LSI) represents a novel combination of techniques for cross-language learning. The paper clearly differentiates this from existing methods that either translate documents or directly apply dimensionality reduction on the incomplete matrix.

*Significance*: Cross-language text classification is a practical problem with significant real-world applications. The paper demonstrates that TSL achieves substantial performance gains (8.59-14.61% over the baseline) with minimal parallel data, making it particularly valuable for low-resource language scenarios where manual annotation is costly.

*Clarity*: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. Technical details of the matrix completion formulation and optimization algorithm are logically presented. The experimental setup is sufficiently described for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses and Concerns**

1. *Justification for LSI Step*: The paper states that LSI is applied "to handle feature noise and sparseness" in the completed matrix, but this is not sufficiently explained. It would be helpful to clarify whether dimensionality reduction is primarily for computational efficiency (as the completed matrix will be dense) or for noise reduction. Additionally, the choice of LSI over other dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., PCA) should be justified.

2. *Low-Rank Assumption*: The paper claims "a fully observed document-term matrix is naturally low-rank," but this requires stronger justification. Document-term matrices are typically sparse, but the low-rank assumption (critical to the matrix completion approach) needs more theoretical or empirical support. Why should the matrix be low-rank? Is this a standard assumption in NLP?

3. *Experimental Clarity*: The experimental setup (Section 5.2) is somewhat ambiguous. It states that "all documents from the two languages and the additional 2000 unlabeled parallel documents" are used for representation learning, but it's unclear how many labeled documents are used from each language. The discrepancy between Figure 1/2 (showing CL-KCCA outperforming TSL on EFM/EGM at 2000 parallel documents) and Table 1 (showing TSL outperforming all methods) also needs clarification.

4. *Comparison to Multiview Methods*: While the paper acknowledges that multiview approaches like CL-KCCA are natural for cross-language learning, it doesn't provide sufficient intuition for why the matrix completion approach outperforms these methods. The low-rank constraint appears to be the key differentiator, but the paper should better explain how this compares to other techniques.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to cross-language representation learning. The method is well-motivated, theoretically supported, and empirically validated. The main concerns are with clarifying certain technical choices (particularly regarding the LSI step and low-rank assumption) and improving the experimental description. With minor revisions addressing these points, the paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

**Recommendation**: Accept

**Arguments Pro and Con**

*Pro*:
- Novel two-step approach combining matrix completion and LSI
- Strong empirical results showing significant performance gains
- Convergence guarantees for the optimization algorithm
- Demonstrates effectiveness with minimal parallel data (200 documents)

*Con*:
- Justification for LSI step and low-rank assumption needs strengthening
- Experimental setup description could be clearer
- Better explanation needed for why matrix completion outperforms multiview approaches

The paper addresses an important problem with a technically sound solution. The contribution is significant, and the results are compelling. The concerns raised are relatively minor and could be addressed with brief clarifications in the final version.
