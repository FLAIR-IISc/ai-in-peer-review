The paper proposes a method for translingual representation learning that first applies matrix completion to construct multilingual vectors for each row, followed by CL-LSI on these multilingual vectors.

Quality: The approach is appealing, but I am not convinced the results are robust. The observed gains may stem from the scaling properties of LSI being better matched to a linear SVM than OPCA or CCA, rather than from the proposed two-step pipeline itself. To test robustness, I strongly recommend using a learning algorithm that is insensitive to rescaling of individual features—e.g., boosted decision trees (as available in Weka). If boosted decision trees on top of the two-step system outperform competing techniques, I would find the conclusions much more convincing.

Significance: If the improvements hold under such scale-insensitive evaluation, the result would be quite interesting, since matrix completion is a well-controlled non-linear inference technique.

Novelty: The idea appears to be new.

Clarity: Some aspects of the exposition need clarification.
- Line 113: “construct a unified document–term matrix for all documents.” Does this mean you use a single term for every surface form across languages (e.g., “Merkel”), or do you keep language-specific surface forms distinct (e.g., “Merkelen,” “Merkelde”)? Please clarify.
- The description of the experimental setup is fragmented across Table 1, which made it difficult to reconstruct. Consolidating this description in the main text would improve readability.

Overall: This is a neat idea, but I would have liked to see results with classifiers beyond a linear SVM. As it stands, I cannot tell whether the gains are robust or primarily a scaling artifact.
