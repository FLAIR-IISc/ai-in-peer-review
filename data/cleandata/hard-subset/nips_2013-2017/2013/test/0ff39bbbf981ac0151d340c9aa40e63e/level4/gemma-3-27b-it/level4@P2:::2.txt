This paper introduces a novel two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, combining matrix completion with latent semantic analysis (LSA). The core idea is to bridge the feature space gap between languages using a small set of parallel bilingual documents, first completing a partially observed document-term matrix and then inducing a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation. A projected gradient descent algorithm is employed for solving the matrix completion problem, with convergence guarantees provided. The method is evaluated on cross-lingual sentiment classification using Amazon product reviews, demonstrating superior performance compared to several baselines, particularly when parallel data is limited.

The paper is technically sound, presenting a clear approach and supporting claims with thorough experimentation. The authors carefully compare their method against a monolingual baseline (bag-of-words), a classic cross-lingual method (cross-lingual LSI), and two state-of-the-art dimensionality reduction techniques designed for cross-lingual settings. The inclusion of experiments varying the amount of parallel data is a strong point, highlighting the method’s robustness. 

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The experimental setup is well-defined, including details on parameter selection, which enhances reproducibility. While the motivation for using matrix completion is explained in Section 3, briefly expanding on this rationale within the Introduction could further improve the paper’s flow and accessibility. 

The task of cross-lingual representation learning is well-established, but the application of matrix completion in this context represents a novel contribution. The authors clearly differentiate their work from related approaches, providing relevant citations (e.g., [10, 15, 20]) and demonstrating a performance gain over existing methods. The method’s ability to achieve strong results with limited parallel data is particularly noteworthy.

The results presented are significant. The consistent improvement over baseline methods suggests that matrix completion is a valuable preprocessing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification. This formulation could potentially be extended to other cross-lingual learning tasks. The demonstrated ability to learn effective representations with relatively small amounts of parallel data is also a significant contribution, addressing a key challenge in cross-lingual NLP.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning.
*   Strong empirical results consistently outperform several baselines.
*   Demonstrated robustness with limited parallel data.
*   Clear writing and well-defined experiments promote reproducibility.
*   Potential for broader impact beyond sentiment classification.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The improvement, while consistent, is not always dramatic. Further analysis of *why* the method works so well could strengthen the paper.
*   The theoretical contribution, while present in the convergence analysis, could be expanded upon.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual NLP. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and empirically validated. While further theoretical investigation or more substantial performance gains could elevate the work further, the current version represents a significant step forward and is worthy of acceptance to NIPS.
