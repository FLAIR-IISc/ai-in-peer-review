Summary: This paper proposes a two-step method for learning cross-lingual topic representations by first performing matrix completion and then applying latent semantic analysis. The matrix completion problem is solved via a projected gradient descent algorithm. Experiments on a parallel Amazon review dataset for sentiment analysis show that the proposed approach outperforms both monolingual and cross-lingual baselines. Learning curves across varying amounts of unlabeled parallel data further indicate that the method achieves highly accurate cross-lingual topic representations with relatively little parallel data.

Quality: The experiments are thorough and well-aligned with the paper’s claims. The proposed two-step algorithm is carefully compared against strong baselines, including a plain bag-of-words model, cross-lingual LSA, and two state-of-the-art cross-lingual dimensionality reduction methods. The empirical results consistently favor the proposed approach, and the analysis with different sizes of unlabeled data is informative and supports the method’s data efficiency.

Clarity: The paper is well organized. All parameters and selection procedures are clearly specified, making the results easy to reproduce. One minor suggestion: provide more motivation in the Introduction for using matrix completion in this context (even though the details are explained in Section 3). A brief intuitive rationale early on would improve accessibility.

Originality: While cross-lingual representation learning is a well-studied problem, the application of matrix completion to this task is novel. The paper convincingly demonstrates that this formulation, combined with LSA, can outperform existing methods in the literature.

Significance: The two-step approach consistently outperforms the baselines, indicating that matrix completion is an effective preprocessing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification. The method’s robustness across different amounts of unlabeled parallel data is noteworthy, and the learning curves suggest that strong performance can be achieved with relatively small parallel corpora. This formulation is promising and may transfer to other cross-lingual tasks beyond sentiment analysis. Overall, the work offers a clear, well-executed contribution with practical significance.
