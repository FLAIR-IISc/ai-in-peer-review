This paper presents an approach for translingual representation learning that follows a two-stage process. First, it employs matrix completion to generate multilingual vectors for each row. Subsequently, it applies Cross-Lingual Latent Semantic Indexing (CL-LSI) to the resulting vectors.

**Quality:** While the approach is conceptually sound, I have reservations about the robustness of the reported results. The positive outcomes could potentially be an artifact of the interaction between the model and the classifier; specifically, the scaling properties of LSI may simply be more compatible with a linear SVM than those of the OPCA or CCA baselines.

To strengthen the paper's claims, I strongly recommend that the authors re-evaluate their method using a machine learning system that is invariant to the rescaling of individual features. An excellent choice would be boosted decision trees (as implemented in toolkits like Weka). If the proposed two-step system still outperforms other techniques when paired with such a classifier, the findings would be far more convincing.

**Significance:** If the results are indeed robust, this work would be a noteworthy contribution, as it leverages matrix completion, which is a principled and well-understood non-linear inference technique.

**Novelty:** The proposed combination of techniques appears to be novel.

**Clarity:** The manuscript could be improved in terms of clarity. For instance, the statement on line 113 regarding the construction of "a unified document-term matrix for all documents" is ambiguous. It is unclear whether this means using a single term for all surface forms (e.g., "Merkel") or labeling each language-specific surface form distinctly (e.g., "Merkelen," "Merkelde"). Furthermore, the description of the experimental setup is somewhat fragmented and split across Table 1, which made it difficult to fully grasp the configuration at first.

In summary, the paper puts forth an interesting idea. However, my main criticism is the choice to use only a linear SVM, which leaves open the question of whether the reported success is a robust finding or a scaling artifact. I would have more confidence in the results had the authors validated them with other types of classifiers.
