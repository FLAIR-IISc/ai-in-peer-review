This paper presents a novel matrix completion approach for cross-domain classification tasks. The proposed method leverages labeled data from an auxiliary domain and unlabeled parallel data. It involves a two-step process: first, constructing a single incomplete matrix encompassing all documents and domains, and then completing this matrix by enforcing low-rank and sparsity conditions using the projected gradient descent algorithm. Subsequently, it reduces the feature dimension of the completed matrix using Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) and trains a standard classifier on this new representation. The paper also provides a convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm and presents favorable experimental results.

Some comments and questions:

- After estimating the completed matrix M, the authors reduce the feature dimension from d to k using LSI, citing that M "lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness." Could the authors elaborate on this statement? Is the primary motivation for dimensionality reduction computational convenience, given that the completed matrix M* will be relatively dense despite sparsity enforcement? Furthermore, what led the authors to choose LSI over other dimensionality reduction techniques, such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA)?

- The paper appears to explore two distinct aspects of exploiting external data: (1) utilizing labeled source language data to aid in learning labeled target language data, and (2) leveraging unlabeled parallel data. If this interpretation is correct, it would be beneficial to clearly differentiate between these two aspects and report results separately.

- The experiment settings in Section 5.2 are somewhat unclear. To confirm, the authors used (a) 4,000 labeled source language documents, (b) 100 labeled target language documents, and (c) 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents for training data. The classification results were then reported on the remaining 1,900 labeled target language documents. In Section 5.3, the only variation was in the number of unlabeled parallel documents, ranging from 200 to 2,000. Could the authors explain why the performance of CL-KCCA surpasses that of TSL in Figure 1 for EFM and EGM at 2,000, yet this is not the case in Table 1?

- Could the authors briefly clarify why a fully observed document-term matrix would be low-rank? While it is sparse, it is unlikely that any document is a linear combination of other documents in the vocabulary, suggesting that the rank would be min(d, k).

- The authors may want to provide insight into why TSL generally outperforms other methods, particularly CCA. A multiview approach like CCA seems like a natural fit for this cross-language representation learning task. In contrast, the matrix completion approach is less intuitive. Is the primary driving force behind TSL's performance the enforcement of the low-rank condition on M*? How does this compare to other techniques?

- What is the running time of the algorithm? Does the projected gradient descent algorithm require more, less, or approximately the same time as other methods, such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA?

Suggested changes:

- Replace "(ij)" with "(i,j)" two sentences before equation (2).
- Remove "such as" in the sentences preceding equations (4) and (5).

Overall, the paper proposes a matrix completion approach to the cross-domain classification task. Although some technical points require clarification, the method is simple, effective, and generally well-presented.
