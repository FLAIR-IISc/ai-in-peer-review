The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning, addressing the challenge of disjoint feature spaces by leveraging both labeled source language data and unlabeled parallel documents. The approach involves formulating a matrix completion problem to create a complete document-term matrix and then applying latent semantic indexing (LSI) to induce a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation. The authors provide a convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm used in the matrix completion step and demonstrate strong empirical performance on cross-language sentiment classification tasks.

This work builds upon existing approaches to cross-language learning, which generally fall into two categories: translation-based methods [18, 6, 23] and methods constructing cross-lingual representations [16, 10, 20, 15]. The proposed method aligns more closely with the latter, utilizing parallel data similarly to CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], and CL-OPCA [15], but differs by first completing the document-term matrix before applying dimensionality reduction. The contribution lies in the novel application of matrix completion to this problem and the demonstrated performance gains, particularly with limited parallel data.

The strengths of the paper include a clear problem definition, a well-motivated approach, theoretical justification (convergence guarantee), and comprehensive experimental evaluation. The authors thoroughly compare their method against several strong baselines, and the results consistently demonstrate superior performance. The analysis of the impact of parallel data size is also insightful. The writing is generally clear and the paper is well-organized.

However, there are areas for improvement. The justification for using LSI after matrix completion could be strengthened. While the authors state it’s due to handling feature sparseness, a more detailed explanation of why LSI is preferred over alternatives like PCA would be helpful. The experimental setup requires further clarification – specifically, the interplay between labeled source data, labeled target data, and unlabeled parallel data. Discrepancies in results between Table 1 and Figure 1 (specifically the performance of CL-KCCA) require investigation. Furthermore, the intuition behind why a fully observed document-term matrix is expected to be low-rank could be more clearly articulated. Finally, a discussion of the computational complexity and running time of the proposed method, compared to the baselines, would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning.
*   Strong empirical results consistently outperforming several baselines.
*   Theoretical guarantee of convergence for the optimization algorithm.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation, including analysis of parallel data size.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Justification for choosing LSI over other dimensionality reduction techniques is weak.
*   Experimental setup is somewhat unclear and requires more detailed explanation.
*   The intuition behind the low-rank assumption of the document-term matrix is not fully explained.
*   Lack of discussion regarding computational complexity and running time.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. Addressing the points raised above would further strengthen the work and make it an even more valuable addition to the conference.
