

The paper proposes a matrix completion approach for cross-domain classification, leveraging labeled data from an auxiliary domain and unlabeled parallel data. The method involves two key steps: first, constructing a single incomplete matrix encompassing all documents and domains, then completing it via projected gradient descent under low-rank and sparsity constraints; second, reducing the feature dimension of the completed matrix using Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) before training a standard classifier on the resulting representation. The paper provides a convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm and reports favorable experimental results.

The following points require clarification:

1.  After completing the matrix $\mathbf{M}$, the authors reduce the feature dimension from $d$ to $k$ using LSI, citing "insufficient capacity to handle feature sparseness." Could you elaborate on this rationale? Is the primary motivation computational convenience (since $\mathbf{M}^*$ becomes relatively dense even with sparsity enforcement), or is there a specific theoretical advantage? Additionally, why was LSI chosen over alternatives like PCA for dimensionality reduction?

2.  The approach exploits external data through two distinct mechanisms: (i) leveraging labeled source-domain data to aid target-domain learning, and (ii) leveraging unlabeled parallel data. It would strengthen the paper to explicitly distinguish these two aspects and report results separately for each.

3.  The experimental setup (Section 5.2) requires clarification. Specifically: (a) Training data comprises 4,000 labeled source documents, 100 labeled target documents, and 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents; (b) Results are reported on the remaining 1,900 labeled target documents. Section 5.3 varies the unlabeled parallel data size from 200 to 2,000. However, Figure 1 shows CL-KCCA outperforming TSL for EFM and EGM at 2,000 unlabeled documents, while Table 1 reports the opposite. Please reconcile this discrepancy.

4.  The paper assumes a fully observed document-term matrix is low-rank. Could you provide intuition for this? While document-term matrices are sparse, it is unlikely that documents are linear combinations of others in the vocabulary (implying rank $\min(d, k)$), so the low-rank assumption requires justification.

5.  TSL consistently outperforms methods like CCA (e.g., CL-KCCA). Given that CCA is a natural multiview approach for cross-language representation learning, why does TSL (a matrix completion method) perform better? What is the primary mechanism driving this improvement (e.g., the low-rank enforcement on $\mathbf{M}^*$), and how does this compare to other techniques?

6.  What is the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm? How does the runtime of the projected gradient descent compare to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA?

Suggested minor revisions:
- Correct "(ij)" to "(i,j)" two sentences before Equation (2).
- Remove "such as" from the sentences preceding Equations (4) and (5).

Overall, the method is simple, effective, and well-presented, though clarifying the points above would significantly strengthen the paper.
