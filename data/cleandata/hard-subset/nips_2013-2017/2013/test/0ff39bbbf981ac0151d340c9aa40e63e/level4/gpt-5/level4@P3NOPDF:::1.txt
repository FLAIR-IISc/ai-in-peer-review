This paper proposes a matrix-completion-based approach to cross-domain (cross-language) text classification that exploits both labeled data in an auxiliary domain and unlabeled parallel data. The method proceeds in two stages: (1) construct a single incomplete matrix spanning all documents and domains, and complete it under joint low-rank and sparsity constraints via projected gradient descent (PGD); (2) reduce the dimensionality of the completed matrix using LSI and train a standard classifier on the reduced representation. The paper also provides a convergence guarantee for the PGD procedure and reports favorable empirical results.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple pipeline that unifies multiple sources of supervision (labeled auxiliary data and unlabeled parallel data) through a single matrix completion step.
- The optimization is explicit and accompanied by a convergence guarantee.
- Empirical results are strong relative to several cross-lingual baselines.
- The presentation is generally clear.

Main questions and requests for clarification
- Motivation for the LSI step after completion: The paper states that the completed matrix M “lacks sufficient capacity for handling feature sparseness,” motivating an additional LSI projection from d to k dimensions. Please elaborate. Is the concern primarily computational (M* becomes relatively dense despite sparsity regularization), statistical (e.g., noise reduction/denoising), or both? Why is LSI the preferred choice over alternatives such as PCA, RP, or autoencoders? If LSI is used because M* is intended to be low-rank, would PCA on M* be equivalent or preferable? An ablation comparing LSI vs PCA (and perhaps no reduction) would help.
- Disentangling the two forms of external information: The paper appears to leverage (1) labeled source-language data to aid target-language classification and (2) unlabeled parallel data. Please separate and report the contribution of each signal (e.g., via ablations that remove labeled source data or remove parallel data), and clarify how each is incorporated in the construction and completion of the matrix.
- Experimental setup clarity (Section 5.2): Please confirm the training/test splits and counts:
  - 4k labeled source-language documents,
  - 100 labeled target-language documents,
  - 2k unlabeled parallel documents used for training,
  - remaining 1,900 labeled target-language documents used for testing.
  If this is not accurate, please specify the exact counts per setting. In Section 5.3, you vary the number of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2k; given this, why does CL-KCCA outperform TSL for EFM and EGM at 2k in Figure 1, yet not in Table 1? Are the splits, preprocessing, or evaluation metrics different across these results, or is there a statistical variance explanation? Confidence intervals or standard deviations would be useful.
- Low-rank assumption: Please clarify why a fully observed document–term matrix would be low-rank. While such matrices are sparse, in general their algebraic rank can be min(d, n). Are you positing a latent low-dimensional topic or concept structure across languages that makes the completed matrix approximately low-rank? If so, it would help to articulate this modeling assumption explicitly and relate it to the cross-lingual alignment provided by parallel data and shared labels.
- Why TSL outperforms other methods, especially CCA: A multiview method like CCA seems natural for cross-lingual representation learning. Provide intuition for why matrix completion with low-rank and sparsity constraints yields a better representation than CCA/LSI/OPCA. For example, is the gain driven by jointly coupling labeled and unlabeled signals across domains within a single optimization, sparsity promoting attribute selection, or better handling of missing entries than CCA? A targeted comparison or visualization (e.g., alignment quality, class separability) would strengthen the case.
- Algorithmic complexity and runtime: Please report training time, number of PGD iterations, and wall-clock comparisons against CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA on the same hardware. A brief complexity analysis in terms of matrix sizes, sparsity, and the cost per PGD step (including projections) would help readers assess scalability.

Minor comments and edits
- Two sentences before Equation (2), change (ij) to (i,j).
- Before Equations (4) and (5), remove “such as.”
- Define notation consistently (e.g., M vs M* for the completed matrix) and ensure all symbols are introduced before use.
- A brief pseudocode or schematic of the two-step pipeline would aid reproducibility.
- If space allows, include details on hyperparameter selection (rank proxy, sparsity weight, k for LSI) and their sensitivity.

Overall assessment
This is a well-motivated and clearly written paper that introduces a simple yet effective approach to cross-domain classification by unifying labeled auxiliary data and unlabeled parallel data via matrix completion. The convergence analysis and empirical gains are compelling. The paper would be further strengthened by (1) a clearer justification of the low-rank assumption and the post-completion LSI step, (2) disentangling and quantifying the contributions of labeled auxiliary data versus unlabeled parallel data, (3) clarifying experimental splits and resolving the discrepancy between Figure 1 and Table 1, and (4) providing runtime and complexity comparisons. With these clarifications, the work would make a solid and useful contribution.
