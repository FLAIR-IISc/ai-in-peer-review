The paper proposes a matrix completion approach to the cross-domain classification task, capable of exploiting labeled data in an auxiliary domain and also unlabeled parallel data. The approach involves two steps: first, constructing a single incomplete matrix encompassing all documents and domains, and then completing this matrix while enforcing low-rank and sparsity conditions using the projected gradient descent algorithm; second, reducing the feature dimension of this completed matrix using Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI), and subsequently training a standard classifier on the resulting representation. The paper also presents a convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm and favorable experimental results.

Here are some comments and questions:

- After estimating the completed matrix M, you reduce the feature dimension from *d* to *k* with LSI because M “lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness.” Can you elaborate on this? Is the primary motivation for dimensionality reduction computational convenience, given that M* will be relatively dense even with sparsity enforcement? Furthermore, if so, is there a reason you chose LSI over other dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., PCA)?
- There appear to be two distinct aspects to leveraging external data in this paper: 1) leveraging labeled source language data to aid learning for the labeled target language data, and 2) leveraging the unlabeled parallel data. If this is correct, it would be helpful to clearly distinguish between these two and report results separately.
- On a related note, the experimental settings are somewhat unclear (section 5.2). Were you using (a) 4k labeled source language documents, (b) 100 labeled target language documents, and (c) 2k unlabeled parallel documents for training? And are you reporting classification results on the remaining 1900 labeled target language documents? Then, in section 5.3, the only variation is the number of unlabeled parallel documents, ranging from 200 to 2k? In that case, why does the performance of CL-KCCA surpass TSL in Figure 1 for EFM and EGM at 2k, but not in Table 1?
- Can you briefly clarify why a fully observed document-term matrix would be expected to be low-rank? While it may be sparse, it is unlikely that any document is a linear combination of others in the vocabulary, implying a rank of min(*d*, *k*).
- Can you provide intuition as to why TSL generally outperforms other methods, especially CCA? A multiview approach like CCA seems naturally suited to this cross-language representation learning task. In contrast, the matrix completion approach feels less intuitive. It appears the main driving force is enforcing the low-rank condition on M*. How does this compare to other techniques?
- What is the running time of the algorithm? Does the projected gradient descent algorithm take more, less, or approximately the same time as other methods such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA?

Suggested changes:

- Fix “(ij)” to “(i,j)” in the two sentences before equation (2).
- Remove “such as” in the sentences before equations (4) and (5). 

The paper proposes a matrix completion approach to the cross-domain classification task. Although some technical points require clarification, the method is simple, effective, and generally clearly presented.
