This paper introduces a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, leveraging matrix completion to construct a unified document-term matrix followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) to derive cross-lingual document representations. The core idea is to bridge the feature space gap between languages using a low-rank approximation learned via matrix completion, particularly beneficial when parallel data is limited.

This work builds upon existing approaches to cross-lingual learning, which often rely on machine translation [18, 6, 23], bilingual lexicons, or shared resource utilization [16, 10, 20]. It differentiates itself by framing the problem as a matrix completion task, aiming to infer missing feature co-occurrences in a concatenated document-term matrix derived from parallel documents. This connects to the broader area of representation learning and utilizes matrix completion, a technique previously applied in other contexts [4, 11]. Related methods like CL-LSI [10], CL-OPCA [15], and CL-KCCA [20] also attempt to induce cross-lingual representations, but this work proposes a distinct two-step process with matrix completion as a key component.

The paper demonstrates promising results on cross-lingual sentiment classification using Amazon product reviews, showing performance gains over several baseline methods, particularly when parallel data is scarce. The strength of the work lies in its novel combination of matrix completion and LSI to address the cross-lingual representation problem, offering a potentially robust approach when parallel resources are limited. However, a key weakness is the reliance on a linear SVM for evaluation. The observed performance improvements might be attributable to the scaling properties of LSI being particularly well-suited for linear classifiers, rather than a fundamental improvement in representation quality. The results’ robustness isn’t definitively established.

To address this concern, I strongly recommend the authors evaluate their method using classifiers less sensitive to feature scaling, such as boosted decision trees (available in Weka or similar packages). If the two-step method consistently outperforms other techniques even with these classifiers, it would significantly strengthen the claim of genuine representation learning improvement. The paper's clarity could also be improved. Specifically, the description of the unified document-term matrix construction on line 113 is ambiguous. It needs clarification: Does it use a single term for all surface forms, or does it maintain distinct entries for each language’s surface forms? The experimental setup description is somewhat fragmented, scattered between the main text and Table 1, making it less accessible.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach combining matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning.
*   Demonstrated performance improvements over several baselines on a cross-lingual sentiment classification task.
*   Potentially robust method, particularly when parallel data is limited.
*   Clear and well-defined problem statement.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Evaluation relies heavily on a linear SVM, raising concerns about the robustness of the results.
*   Lack of evaluation with classifiers insensitive to feature scaling.
*   Minor clarity issues in the description of the unified document-term matrix and the experimental setup.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising and novel approach to cross-lingual representation learning. While the current results are encouraging, the reliance on a linear SVM raises concerns about their robustness. Addressing this limitation through evaluation with alternative classifiers would significantly strengthen the paper. Addressing the clarity issues described above would also enhance the paper’s accessibility. I currently lean towards **weak accept**, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding classifier choice.
