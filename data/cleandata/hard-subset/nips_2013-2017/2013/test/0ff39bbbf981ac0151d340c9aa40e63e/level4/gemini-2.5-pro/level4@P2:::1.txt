This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-language representation learning, which is applied to the task of cross-language text classification. The core idea is to leverage a small set of unlabeled parallel documents to bridge the vocabulary gap between a source and a target language. The method first constructs a unified document-term matrix for all documents from both languages. This matrix is inherently sparse and incomplete, as monolingual documents have missing entries for the vocabulary of the other language. The first step of the proposed method is to recover a complete, low-rank version of this matrix by formulating and solving a matrix completion problem. The second step involves applying Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to produce a low-dimensional, dense representation for all documents. A standard classifier can then be trained on this shared representation. The paper supports its approach with a convergence proof for its optimization algorithm and strong empirical results on a cross-language sentiment classification task.

The proposed approach is an interesting and novel application of matrix completion to the problem of cross-lingual learning. It differs from prior work like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, which typically apply dimensionality reduction techniques directly to the original sparse, concatenated document-term matrix. By first filling in the missing entries under a low-rank assumption, the proposed method aims to capture richer cross-lingual co-occurrence patterns before creating the final semantic representation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality:** The formulation of the cross-lingual representation learning problem as a matrix completion task is novel and provides a new perspective compared to more traditional projection-based methods like CCA.
*   **Significance and Performance:** The experimental results are impressive. The proposed Two-Step Learning (TSL) method consistently outperforms several strong baselines across multiple language pairs and domains. Crucially, it demonstrates remarkable robustness when the number of available parallel documents is small, which is a significant practical advantage for many real-world scenarios.
*   **Technical Quality:** The optimization problem is well-defined, and the authors provide a convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm used to solve it, adding to the technical soundness of the work.
*   **Clarity:** For the most part, the paper is well-written and the proposed method is presented clearly.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**

Despite the strengths, several aspects of the paper could be clarified to strengthen the contribution.

1.  **Motivation for the LSI Step:** After completing the matrix M to get M*, the paper states that this matrix "lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness" and thus applies LSI. This motivation is slightly confusing. One would expect the completed matrix M* to be relatively dense due to the nature of matrix completion, even with the L1 sparsity regularizer. Could the authors elaborate on this reasoning? Is the primary goal of the LSI step simply dimensionality reduction for computational efficiency, or does it serve another purpose, such as noise reduction? Additionally, it would be helpful to know why LSI was chosen over other dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., PCA).

2.  **Low-Rank Assumption:** A central premise of the method is that a fully observed document-term matrix is "naturally low-rank". While such a matrix is certainly sparse, the low-rank assumption is less obvious, as it implies that document vectors are linear combinations of a small number of basis documents. Could the authors provide a more detailed justification or intuition for this core assumption? This would help motivate the matrix completion formulation more strongly.

3.  **Intuition for Superior Performance:** The TSL method outperforms strong multiview baselines like CL-KCCA, which feel like a very natural fit for this problem. It would be highly beneficial if the authors could provide more intuition about *why* their method works so well. How does enforcing a low-rank structure on the unified matrix capture cross-lingual relationships more effectively than methods that explicitly maximize the correlation between projections of the two languages?

4.  **Experimental Clarity:**
    *   The experimental setup in Section 5.2 could be described more explicitly. My understanding is that for training, the method uses: (a) all labeled source language documents (e.g., 4000 English reviews), (b) 100 randomly chosen labeled target language documents, and (c) 2000 unlabeled parallel documents. The evaluation is then performed on the remaining 1900 labeled target documents. Is this interpretation correct?
    *   There appears to be a minor inconsistency in the results. In Table 1, TSL outperforms CL-KCCA on the EFM and EGM tasks. However, in Figure 1, the plot for np=2000 (which should correspond to the setting in Table 1) shows CL-KCCA performing slightly better than or comparably to TSL on these same tasks. Please clarify this discrepancy.

5.  **Disentangling Data Contributions:** The method leverages two distinct types of information: labeled source-language data and unlabeled parallel data. The current experiments evaluate their combined effect. To better understand the method's properties, it would be interesting to disentangle these contributions. For example, an ablation study that shows performance without the labeled source data could provide valuable insights.

6.  **Computational Cost:** The paper lacks a discussion of the computational complexity or empirical running time. How does the projected gradient descent algorithm scale with the number of documents and the size of the vocabulary? A comparison of its training time against the baseline methods would be a valuable addition for practitioners.

**Minor Suggested Changes:**

*   In Section 3.1, two sentences before equation (2), please correct "(ij)" to "(i, j)" for consistency.
*   In Section 4.1, the phrase "such as" before equations (3) and (5) seems redundant and could be removed.

In summary, this paper introduces a simple, effective, and novel method for cross-language representation learning. The approach is well-motivated and supported by strong experimental results, particularly in low-resource settings. While the core ideas are clear, the paper would be significantly improved by addressing the points of clarification raised above, especially regarding the motivation for certain design choices and the details of the experimental evaluation.
