This paper introduces a novel two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, designed for text classification tasks where labeled data is scarce in the target language. The core idea is to leverage both labeled source-language data and unlabeled parallel documents. The first step involves constructing a unified document-term matrix for both languages, which is inherently sparse and incomplete. The authors formulate this as a matrix completion problem, recovering a full, low-rank, and sparse matrix using a projected gradient descent algorithm. In the second step, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to the completed matrix to generate a low-dimensional shared representation for all documents. A standard classifier is then trained on this representation. The authors provide a convergence proof for their optimization algorithm and demonstrate strong empirical performance on a cross-lingual sentiment classification task, outperforming several baseline methods.

The proposed method is simple, effective, and generally well-presented. The empirical results are strong and the approach is novel for this task. However, the paper would benefit from additional clarification on several key points, including the motivation for certain design choices and the experimental setup, as detailed below.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Motivation for LSI:** The paper states that LSI is applied to the completed matrix M* because it "lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature noise and sparseness." This justification is slightly confusing. While the original matrix is sparse, the completed matrix M*, after filling in the missing values, should be dense (even with an l1 penalty). Could you clarify the motivation for this second step? Is the primary reason to reduce dimensionality for computational efficiency, or is there another benefit related to noise reduction? Furthermore, could you elaborate on the choice of LSI over other standard dimensionality reduction techniques, such as PCA?

2.  **Disentangling Data Contributions:** The proposed method leverages two distinct types of data: labeled source-language documents and unlabeled parallel documents. It would significantly strengthen the paper to disentangle the contributions of these two data sources. For instance, an ablation study showing performance with only labeled source data (and no parallel data) would help clarify the specific role and importance of the parallel corpus in the matrix completion step.

3.  **Experimental Clarity and Inconsistency:** The experimental setup in Section 5.2 could be described more clearly. To confirm my understanding: for representation learning, you use all documents from both languages (e.g., 4000 source, 2000 target) plus 2000 unlabeled parallel documents. For classifier training, you use all 4000 labeled source documents and 100 labeled target documents. Testing is then performed on the remaining 1900 target documents. Is this interpretation correct?
    If so, there appears to be a small inconsistency in the results. In Table 1, TSL outperforms CL-KCCA on the EFM and EGM tasks. However, in Figure 1, the plots for EFM and EGM show CL-KCCA performing slightly better than TSL when the number of parallel documents is 2000. Could you please clarify this discrepancy?

4.  **Low-Rank Assumption:** The matrix completion step is motivated by the assumption that a "fully observed document-term matrix is naturally low-rank." Could you provide more intuition for this assumption? While document-term matrices are sparse, it is not immediately obvious that they should have low rank. One might expect the rank to be high (close to min(#docs, #vocab)) since individual documents are typically not simple linear combinations of each other. A deeper justification for this core assumption would be beneficial.

5.  **Intuition vs. Alternative Methods:** The proposed method outperforms several strong baselines, including multiview learning methods like CL-KCCA. Intuitively, methods like CCA seem like a natural fit for this problem, as they are designed to find shared correlations between two views (languages). The matrix completion approach is less conventional for this task. Could you provide some intuition as to why filling in a unified matrix and enforcing a global low-rank structure is more effective than directly learning projections that maximize correlation, as in CCA-based methods?

6.  **Computational Cost:** Could you comment on the computational complexity and practical running time of the proposed algorithm? A comparison of the training time against the baseline methods (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) would be helpful for understanding the practical trade-offs.

**Minor Comments:**

*   Two sentences before Equation (2), "(ij) ∈ Ω" should probably be "(i, j) ∈ Ω" for consistency with the surrounding text.
*   In the first sentence of Section 4.1, the description of the objective function decomposition seems incorrect: "This algorithm takes the objective function f(M) in (2) as a composition of a non-smooth term and a convex smooth term such as f(M) = γ‖M‖∗ + g(M)". Here, f(M) is the entire objective function, not an example of the smooth term. Please rephrase for clarity.
