- Proposal: two-step cross-lingual method — matrix completion on a unified bilingual document–term matrix (nuclear norm + L1 + nonnegativity + small penalty on missing entries) followed by LSI and a linear SVM.  
- Empirical claim: Outperforms CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA on Amazon multilingual reviews, especially with few parallel documents.  
- Technical soundness: Convex relaxation and projected gradient with nuclear-norm shrinkage are reasonable; convergence proof is appropriate.  
- Regularization choice: Penalizing missing entries (ρ) is sensible to avoid overfitting to observed entries.  
- Experimental setup: Appropriate for limited-label cross-lingual sentiment tasks and compared against strong baselines.  
- Scaling confound: Potential sensitivity to feature scaling and normalization with linear SVM; normalization procedures across methods need clarification.  
- Missing ablations: Must report (a) SVM on completed matrix M* without LSI and (b) LSI on original matrix with zeros (standardized CL-LSI) to isolate sources of gains.  
- Robustness recommendation: Evaluate with a scale-insensitive classifier (e.g., gradient boosted trees) to test classifier dependence.  
- Efficiency/reproducibility: Report runtime, iterations to convergence, use of partial SVDs, and sensitivity to γ, ρ, k, τ.  
- Vocabulary clarity: Specify how unified vocabulary is built, whether identical surface forms are merged, tokenization, stop-word handling, and TF–IDF computation per language vs concatenated corpus.  
- Experimental clarity: Consolidate details (100 labeled target examples, use of source labels, model selection protocol, consistent k across methods) and summarize the 18 tasks in-text.  
- Originality: Novel use of matrix completion for bilingual feature imputation and meaningful combination of nuclear norm with nonnegativity and sparsity.  
- Significance: Potentially valuable advance if scaling and classifier issues are resolved; practical for small parallel corpora and simple downstream use.  
- Pros: Clear, motivated two-step formulation with convergence proof; broad, representative baselines; strong low-parallel-data performance; no need to translate all training data.  
- Cons: Unclear normalization leading to scaling sensitivity; missing ablation studies; ambiguity in vocabulary/preprocessing; limited discussion of computational cost and parameter sensitivity.  
- Suggested fixes: Add ablations and tree-based classifier experiments, clarify vocabulary/TF–IDF and normalization, report runtime/iterations and parameter sensitivity, and release code/scripts.  
- Overall assessment: Promising and well-grounded idea that would be substantially strengthened by resolving the scaling confound, adding ablations, and improving reproducibility details.
