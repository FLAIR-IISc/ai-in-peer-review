The paper introduces a matrix completion technique for cross-domain classification, effectively leveraging labeled data from an auxiliary domain alongside unlabeled parallel data. The method proceeds in two stages: first, constructing and completing an incomplete matrix encompassing documents from all domains via projected gradient descent with low-rank and sparsity constraints; and second, applying Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) for feature dimension reduction, followed by training a standard classifier. A convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm is provided, alongside promising experimental results.

However, several points require further clarification. The reduction of feature dimensionality from *d* to *k* using LSI, motivated by the claim that the completed matrix *M* “lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness,” warrants elaboration. Is this primarily a computational consideration, given that *M* will likely remain relatively dense despite sparsity enforcement? If so, what motivated the choice of LSI over alternative dimensionality reduction techniques like PCA?

The paper appears to address two distinct aspects of utilizing external data: leveraging labeled source language data to improve target language classification, and utilizing unlabeled parallel data. Clearly delineating these two contributions and presenting results separately would enhance understanding.

Regarding the experimental setup (Section 5.2), clarification is needed. Was the training data comprised of (a) 4k labeled source language documents, (b) 100 labeled target language documents, and (c) 2k unlabeled parallel documents, with classification performance evaluated on the remaining 1900 labeled target language documents? Furthermore, does Section 5.3 solely vary the number of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2k? If so, the discrepancy between the performance of CL-KCCA versus TSL in Figure 1 (for EFM and EGM at 2k) and Table 1 is unclear.

The rationale behind expecting a low-rank structure in a fully observed document-term matrix would benefit from explanation. While sparse, it’s not immediately obvious that documents are linear combinations of each other, suggesting a rank of min(*d*, *k*).

An intuitive explanation of why TSL consistently outperforms other methods, particularly CCA, would be valuable. While CCA represents a natural approach to cross-lingual representation learning, the matrix completion approach feels less direct. The effectiveness appears driven by the low-rank constraint on *M* – a comparison to other techniques enforcing similar constraints would be insightful.

Finally, providing information on the algorithm’s running time, and comparing it to methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, would be beneficial.

Minor revisions are suggested: correct “(ij)” to “(i,j)” in the two sentences preceding equation (2), and remove “such as” from the sentences preceding equations (4) and (5). 

Overall, the paper presents a simple, effective, and generally well-written method, though some technical details require further clarification.
