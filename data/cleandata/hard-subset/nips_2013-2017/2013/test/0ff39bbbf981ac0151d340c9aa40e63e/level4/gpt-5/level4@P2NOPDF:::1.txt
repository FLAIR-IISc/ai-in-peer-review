This paper proposes a matrix-completion approach to cross-domain classification that leverages labeled data from an auxiliary domain and unlabeled parallel data. The method has two stages: (1) construct a single incomplete matrix spanning all documents and domains, then complete it under joint low-rank and sparsity constraints using projected gradient descent (with a convergence guarantee); and (2) apply LSI to the completed matrix for dimensionality reduction before training a standard classifier. The empirical results are favorable.

Comments and questions:
- Post-completion LSI: You state that the completed matrix M “lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness,” motivating an LSI step that reduces dimensionality from d to k. Please clarify this rationale. Is the primary motivation computational (since M* can be relatively dense even with sparsity regularization)? If so, why LSI specifically, as opposed to PCA or other dimensionality reduction methods?
- Distinguishing data sources: The paper appears to exploit two distinct external signals: (1) labeled source-language data to assist labeled target-language learning, and (2) unlabeled parallel data. If this reading is correct, it would help to separate these effects and report ablations that isolate the contribution of each.
- Experimental setup clarity (Sec. 5.2): Please confirm whether training uses (a) 4k labeled source-language documents, (b) 100 labeled target-language documents, and (c) 2k unlabeled parallel documents, with testing on the remaining 1,900 labeled target-language documents. In Sec. 5.3, do you vary only the number of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2k? If so, why does CL-KCCA outperform TSL at 2k for EFM and EGM in Figure 1, but not in Table 1?
- Low-rank assumption: Could you justify why a fully observed document–term matrix should be low rank? Sparsity alone does not imply low rank, and documents are generally not strict linear combinations of others in the vocabulary; naively, the rank could be min(d, k).
- Intuition for TSL’s performance: Please provide intuition for why TSL generally outperforms other methods, especially CCA, which is a natural multiview approach for cross-language representation learning. If the primary benefit stems from enforcing a low-rank prior on M*, how does this compare—conceptually and empirically—to alternative regularization or representation-learning techniques?
- Runtime: Please report wall-clock time and scaling behavior. Does projected gradient descent run faster, slower, or comparably to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA?

Suggested edits:
- Replace “(ij)” with “(i, j)” two sentences before Equation (2).
- Remove “such as” in the sentences before Equations (4) and (5).

Overall, the paper presents a simple and effective method with a clear exposition. While several technical points would benefit from clarification, the approach is promising and the results are encouraging.
