## Review of NIPS 2013 Submission: [Paper Title]

This paper proposes a novel matrix completion approach for cross-domain classification, effectively leveraging labeled data from an auxiliary domain and unlabeled parallel data. The method constructs a single incomplete matrix representing documents across all domains, then completes this matrix using projected gradient descent with low-rank and sparsity constraints. Subsequently, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied for dimensionality reduction, followed by training a standard classifier on the resulting representation. The paper provides a convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm and demonstrates promising experimental results. Overall, the approach is conceptually simple, effective, and generally well-presented. However, several technical aspects require clarification.

My primary concern revolves around the motivation for dimensionality reduction via LSI. The paper states that LSI is employed because the completed matrix M “lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness.” Could the authors elaborate on this point? Is the primary driver for reducing the dimensionality from *d* to *k* computational efficiency, given that M* will likely remain relatively dense even with sparsity enforcement? If so, why was LSI chosen over alternative dimensionality reduction techniques such as PCA? A justification for this specific choice would be beneficial.

The paper appears to address two distinct ways of incorporating external data: (1) leveraging labeled source language data to improve classification in the target language, and (2) utilizing unlabeled parallel data. If this interpretation is correct, clearly delineating these two contributions and presenting results for each independently would significantly enhance clarity.

The experimental setup (Section 5.2) is somewhat unclear. To clarify, the training data consists of (a) 4,000 labeled source language documents, (b) 100 labeled target language documents, and (c) 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents? The classification performance is then evaluated on the remaining 1,900 labeled target language documents. Subsequently, Section 5.3 explores variations by altering the number of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2,000. Given this, there’s an inconsistency between Figure 1 (showing CL-KCCA outperforming TSL for EFM and EGM at 2,000 unlabeled documents) and Table 1, where this trend is not observed. Resolving this discrepancy is crucial.

Furthermore, the rationale behind assuming a low-rank structure in a fully observed document-term matrix warrants further explanation. While the matrix is sparse, it’s unlikely that documents are simple linear combinations of each other within the vocabulary space, suggesting a rank of min(*d*, *k*). A clearer justification for the low-rank assumption would strengthen the paper.

The paper demonstrates that TSL generally outperforms other methods, particularly CCA. Given that CCA is a natural approach for cross-language representation learning as a multi-view method, I would appreciate an intuition as to why TSL proves superior. Is the effectiveness driven primarily by the imposed low-rank constraint on M*? If so, how does this constraint compare to techniques employed in other related approaches?

Finally, information regarding the computational cost of the algorithm is missing. How does the running time of the projected gradient descent algorithm compare to that of alternative methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA? Providing this information will allow for a more complete assessment of the practicality of the proposed approach.

I also noted a minor typographical error: please correct “(ij)” to “(i,j)” in the two sentences preceding equation (2). Additionally, I recommend removing "such as" before equations (4) and (5) for improved conciseness. 

Despite these questions and suggestions, this paper presents a compelling and innovative approach to cross-domain classification. Addressing the points raised above would significantly strengthen the manuscript.
