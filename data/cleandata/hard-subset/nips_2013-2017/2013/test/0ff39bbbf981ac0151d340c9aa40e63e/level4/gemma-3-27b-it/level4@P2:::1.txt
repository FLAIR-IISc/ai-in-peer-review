This paper proposes a novel matrix completion approach for cross-lingual text classification, leveraging both labeled source language data and unlabeled parallel corpora. The method consists of two key steps: first, constructing and completing a partially observed document-term matrix using a projected gradient descent algorithm with low-rank and sparsity constraints; and second, applying latent semantic indexing (LSI) to reduce dimensionality and obtain cross-lingual document representations for classification. The paper includes a convergence guarantee for the optimization algorithm and demonstrates promising experimental results.

The approach builds upon prior work in cross-lingual learning, which often relies on machine translation, bilingual lexicons [18, 6, 23], or cross-lingual representations built from resources like bilingual word pairs [16] or parallel corpora [10, 20, 15]. This paper distinguishes itself by directly completing a combined document-term matrix to bridge the language gap, rather than relying on translation or existing bilingual resources as a direct input to representation learning. Related work in low-rank matrix completion [4, 11] provides the theoretical foundation for the matrix completion step.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The two-step approach of matrix completion followed by LSI is a unique combination for cross-lingual representation learning.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The paper provides a convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm used for matrix completion.
*   **Empirical performance:** The experimental results on Amazon product reviews demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms several baseline approaches, particularly when the amount of parallel data is limited.
*   **Clarity of presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Motivation for LSI:** The rationale for applying LSI after matrix completion – specifically addressing “feature sparseness” – is not fully elaborated. A clearer explanation of why LSI is preferred over other dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., PCA) would be beneficial. The connection to computational convenience should be acknowledged if relevant.
*   **Disentangling data sources:** The paper leverages both labeled source language data and unlabeled parallel data. It would be valuable to clearly delineate the contribution of each source and report results separately to better understand the method’s behavior.
*   **Experimental clarity:** The description of the experimental setup (Section 5.2) could be more precise. Clarifying the number of documents used for training and testing, and the specific roles of labeled and unlabeled data, would improve reproducibility. The discrepancy between Table 1 and Figure 1 regarding CL-KCCA’s performance (EFM and EGM tasks at 2k parallel documents) requires investigation.
*   **Low-rank assumption justification:** The claim that a fully observed document-term matrix would be low-rank needs further justification. While sparse, the relationships between documents in vocabulary space may not naturally lead to low rank.
*   **Intuition for performance:** A more in-depth discussion of *why* the proposed method outperforms other techniques, particularly canonical correlation analysis (CCA) – a seemingly natural approach for cross-lingual representation learning – would be helpful. Emphasis on the role of the low-rank constraint in the matrix completion step, and how it differs from CCA, would be valuable.
*   **Computational complexity:** The paper lacks a discussion of the algorithm's running time and its comparison to other methods (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The proposed method presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to cross-lingual representation learning.
*   The experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness, particularly in low-resource scenarios.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The rationale for certain design choices (e.g., LSI) is not fully explained.
*   The experimental setup could be more precise, and some inconsistencies need addressing.
*   The lack of discussion regarding computational complexity is a limitation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. The proposed method is innovative and demonstrates strong empirical performance. While some clarifications and additional details would strengthen the paper, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

*   Elaborate on the motivation for using LSI.
*   Clarify the experimental setup and address the discrepancy in results between Table 1 and Figure 1.
*   Provide a more detailed explanation of why the proposed method outperforms CCA.
*   Discuss the computational complexity of the algorithm.
*   Correct the minor typo "(ij)" to "(i,j)" before equation (2).
*   Remove “such as” before equations (4) and (5).
