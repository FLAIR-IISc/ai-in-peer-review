

This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that addresses the fundamental challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages. The authors recognize that existing approaches typically either translate documents between languages (requiring full translation of all documents) or apply dimensionality reduction techniques (like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, or CL-OPCA) directly to the concatenated document-term matrix. Their key insight is that a small set of unlabeled parallel documents can bridge language gaps by enabling matrix completion to fill missing feature entries before applying latent semantic indexing (LSI). This approach differs significantly from previous work that either assumes full translation of all documents [18, 6, 23] or applies LSI directly to the incomplete matrix [10, 15, 20].

The paper makes a technically sound contribution that is well-supported by both theoretical analysis and comprehensive experiments. The authors formulate the problem as a matrix completion task with convergence guarantees, providing a projected gradient descent algorithm that handles the non-smooth optimization problem. This is a significant methodological contribution that addresses a practical limitation of previous approaches. The experimental evaluation on multilingual Amazon product reviews demonstrates consistent improvements over strong baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) across 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks. Notably, the proposed method achieves substantial gains (8.59-14.61% average accuracy improvements over the baseline) even with minimal parallel data (as few as 200 unlabeled parallel documents), which is a significant practical advantage over competing methods that require larger parallel datasets.

The paper excels in technical quality and experimental rigor. The authors carefully compare their method against both a monolingual baseline (TBOW) and three state-of-the-art cross-lingual representation learning approaches. They conduct thorough parameter selection experiments and provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, including specific parameter values (γ=0.1, ρ=10^-4, k=50) and implementation details. The convergence analysis of their optimization algorithm is mathematically sound, providing theoretical guarantees for their approach. The experimental results are well-presented with clear tables and figures demonstrating consistent performance improvements across different language pairs and classification tasks.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and methodology. The introduction clearly establishes the problem context and limitations of previous work, while the technical sections provide sufficient detail for experts to understand the contributions. The only minor improvement would be to strengthen the motivation for matrix completion in the introduction, as the draft review suggests, by more explicitly contrasting it with the direct application of LSI to incomplete matrices that characterizes previous approaches.

The originality of the work is substantial. While cross-lingual representation learning is a well-studied problem, the authors' application of matrix completion as a preprocessing step before LSI represents a novel approach that effectively leverages the limited parallel data available. The paper clearly articulates how their method differs from related work, particularly noting that they only require a small set of unlabeled parallel documents rather than translating all training data. The comparison with CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA in Section 2 provides a thorough context for their contribution.

The significance of this work is evident from both the experimental results and the practical implications. The method's ability to achieve strong performance with minimal parallel data addresses a critical practical limitation in cross-lingual learning, where obtaining large parallel corpora is often prohibitively expensive. The consistent performance improvements across multiple language pairs (English-French, English-German, English-Japanese) and categories (Books, DVD, Music) demonstrate the method's general applicability. The authors' observation that their approach outperforms competitors even with just 200 unlabeled parallel documents is particularly noteworthy, as it suggests the method could be valuable in scenarios where parallel data is extremely scarce.

The paper's main weakness is a lack of discussion regarding potential limitations or failure cases. While the authors demonstrate strong performance across the tested tasks, it would be valuable to understand under what conditions the method might not perform as well (e.g., with languages that have very different grammatical structures or vocabulary sizes). Additionally, the paper could benefit from a brief discussion of computational complexity compared to the baselines, particularly regarding the scalability of the matrix completion approach to larger datasets.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in cross-lingual representation learning. The method is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing approaches. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing, and the paper is well-written and organized. The authors have made a significant contribution that will likely be valuable to researchers and practitioners working on multilingual natural language processing tasks.

Pro: Novel application of matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning, strong experimental results with consistent improvements over baselines, theoretical convergence guarantees, effective performance with minimal parallel data, well-written and organized.

Con: Limited discussion of potential limitations/failure cases, could provide more detailed comparison of computational complexity with baselines.
