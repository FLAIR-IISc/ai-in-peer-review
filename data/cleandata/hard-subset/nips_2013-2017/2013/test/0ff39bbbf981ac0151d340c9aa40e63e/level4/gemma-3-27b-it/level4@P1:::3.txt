The paper proposes a method for cross-lingual representation learning by first using matrix completion to create multilingual vectors for each document, and then performing Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) on these vectors.

**Quality:** While this seems like a promising approach, the robustness of the results is somewhat unclear. The observed improvements could potentially be attributed to the scaling properties of LSI being more favorable for a linear SVM compared to methods like OPCA or CCA. To strengthen the claims, I would encourage the authors to evaluate the proposed method using a machine learning system that is insensitive to feature scaling – for example, boosted decision trees (readily available in packages like Weka). If the two-step method consistently outperforms other techniques when coupled with boosted decision trees, this would provide stronger evidence of its effectiveness. The convergence analysis of the projected gradient descent algorithm is a positive aspect.

**Significance:** If the results prove to be robust across different classifiers, this work could be a valuable contribution. Matrix completion is a well-established, controlled non-linear inference technique, and successfully leveraging it for cross-lingual representation learning would be noteworthy.

**Originality:** The proposed approach appears novel, combining matrix completion with LSI in this specific context.

**Clarity:** There were some areas where the paper could benefit from increased clarity. For instance, line 113 mentions “constructing a unified document-term matrix for all documents.” It would be helpful to clarify whether this involves using a single term to represent all surface forms (e.g., treating variations of "Merkel" as the same term) or labeling each surface form from each language separately (e.g., distinguishing between “Merkelen” and “Merkelde”). Additionally, the description of the experimental setup is somewhat fragmented across the text and Table 1, which made it initially challenging to grasp the details. A more consolidated presentation of the experimental setup would enhance readability.

Overall, this paper presents an interesting idea. While the results are promising, further investigation with alternative classifiers is needed to confirm their robustness and rule out potential scaling artifacts. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The approach is novel and combines established techniques (matrix completion, LSI) in a creative way.
*   The experimental results demonstrate performance improvements over baseline methods.
*   The authors provide a convergence analysis for their optimization algorithm.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The reliance on a linear SVM as the classifier raises concerns about whether the observed improvements are robust or due to scaling effects.
*   Some sections of the paper could benefit from improved clarity and organization.
