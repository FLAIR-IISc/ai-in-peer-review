Summary: This paper introduces a two-step method for learning cross-lingual topic representations, leveraging matrix completion and latent semantic analysis. A projected gradient descent algorithm is employed to optimize the matrix completion problem. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method surpasses both monolingual and existing cross-lingual baselines in a sentiment analysis task utilizing a parallel Amazon review dataset. Learning curves, generated using varying amounts of unlabeled data, illustrate the algorithm’s ability to learn highly accurate cross-lingual topic representations even with limited parallel data.

Quality: The experimental evaluation is thorough and effectively supports the paper’s claims. The proposed two-step learning algorithm is carefully compared against two baselines (a plain bag-of-words model and cross-lingual LSA) and two state-of-the-art cross-lingual dimensionality reduction techniques.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and facilitates reproducibility; all parameters, including their selection criteria, are clearly documented. A minor suggestion would be to elaborate on the motivation for employing matrix completion in the Introduction (although it is currently described in Section 3). 

Originality: While cross-lingual representation learning is an established area, the application of matrix completion to this problem is novel. The proposed algorithm demonstrably achieves superior performance compared to existing methods in the literature.

Significance: The consistent performance gains over other baselines highlight the value of this two-step approach. This work suggests that matrix completion can serve as an effective preprocessing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification, and this formulation may extend to other tasks as well. In essence, this paper proposes a two-step method – matrix completion followed by latent semantic analysis – for learning cross-lingual topic representations in the context of sentiment classification. Experimental results show that the proposed method outperforms monolingual and several cross-lingual baselines, including a state-of-the-art approach, and exhibits robustness across varying sizes of unlabeled parallel data.
