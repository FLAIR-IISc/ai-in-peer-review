This paper introduces a method for translingual representation learning, combining matrix completion to generate multilingual vectors with Cross-Lingual Latent Semantic Indexing (CL-LSI). The approach aims to create a shared embedding space for documents across languages.

Regarding the quality of the results, while the proposed method demonstrates promise, I have concerns about the robustness of the observed improvements. The reported gains may be attributable to the inherent scaling properties of LSI, which might be more favorable for a linear SVM classifier than those of Orthogonal Projections to Canonical Spaces (OPCA) or Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA). To convincingly demonstrate the method’s effectiveness, I recommend the authors evaluate it using a machine learning system less sensitive to feature scaling. Specifically, employing a boosted decision tree algorithm (readily available in tools like Weka) would provide a more reliable assessment. If the two-step system, when coupled with boosted decision trees, consistently outperforms alternative techniques, I would be significantly more confident in the findings.

The potential significance of this work lies in the use of matrix completion, a controlled non-linear inference technique, to build cross-lingual representations. A robust and validated outcome would represent a valuable contribution to the field.

The core idea presented appears novel.

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues. For example, line 113 refers to “construct[ing] a unified document-term matrix for all documents,” and it is unclear whether this entails consolidating all surface forms under a single term (e.g., treating "Merkel," "Merkelen," and "Merkelde" as a single entity) or explicitly labeling each language-specific surface form. Clarification on this point is crucial for understanding the methodology. Furthermore, the presentation of the experimental setup is fragmented across Table 1, which made it somewhat difficult to fully grasp the details without careful cross-referencing. A more consolidated description would improve readability. 

Overall, the concept is appealing, but I believe further experimentation with alternative classifiers – beyond the linear SVM – is necessary to establish the robustness of the results and rule out the possibility of scaling artifacts influencing the observed performance differences.
