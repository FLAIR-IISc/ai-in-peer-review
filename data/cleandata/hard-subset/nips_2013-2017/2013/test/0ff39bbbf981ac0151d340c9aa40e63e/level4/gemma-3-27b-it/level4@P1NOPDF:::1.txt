The paper proposes a matrix completion approach to cross-domain classification, capable of leveraging labeled data from an auxiliary domain and unlabeled parallel data. The approach consists of two steps. First, it constructs a single incomplete matrix encompassing all documents and domains, then completes this matrix using projected gradient descent with low-rank and sparsity constraints. Second, it reduces the feature dimension of the completed matrix using Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI), and subsequently trains a standard classifier on this new representation. The paper also provides a convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm, alongside promising experimental results.

Here are some comments and questions:

- After estimating the completed matrix M, you reduce the feature dimension from *d* to *k* with LSI because M “lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness.” Could you elaborate on this? Is the primary motivation to reduce dimensionality for computational efficiency, given that M* will likely be relatively dense even with sparsity enforcement? If so, is there a specific reason LSI was chosen over other dimensionality reduction techniques, such as PCA?

- It seems there are two distinct aspects to how external data is utilized in this paper: 1) leveraging labeled source language data to improve learning for labeled target language data, and 2) leveraging unlabeled parallel data. If this is accurate, it would be beneficial to clearly differentiate these two approaches and present results for each separately.

- Relatedly, the experimental setup in section 5.2 is somewhat unclear. Were you using (a) 4,000 labeled source language documents, (b) 100 labeled target language documents, and (c) 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents for training? And are you reporting classification results on the remaining 1,900 labeled target language documents? If so, the sole difference in section 5.3 appears to be varying the number of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2,000. In that case, why does CL-KCCA outperform TSL in Figure 1 for EFM and EGM at 2,000 documents, while this isn’t reflected in Table 1?

- Could you briefly clarify the rationale for expecting a low-rank structure in a fully observed document-term matrix? While it is sparse, it’s unlikely that any document is a linear combination of others in the vocabulary, which would suggest a rank of min(*d*, *k*).

- What is the intuition behind TSL's superior performance compared to other methods, particularly CCA? A multiview approach like CCA seems like a natural fit for this cross-language representation learning task. In contrast, the matrix completion approach feels less direct. It appears the key driver of performance is the low-rank constraint imposed on M*. How does this compare to other techniques?

- What is the running time of the algorithm? Does the projected gradient descent algorithm require more, less, or a comparable amount of time compared to other methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA?
