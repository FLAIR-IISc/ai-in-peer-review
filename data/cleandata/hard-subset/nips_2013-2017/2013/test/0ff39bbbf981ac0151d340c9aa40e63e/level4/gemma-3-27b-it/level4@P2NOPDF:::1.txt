This paper introduces a novel matrix completion approach to cross-domain classification, effectively leveraging labeled data from an auxiliary domain alongside unlabeled parallel data. The method proceeds in two stages: first, a unified, incomplete matrix representing documents across all domains is constructed and completed using projected gradient descent with low-rank and sparsity constraints. Second, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to reduce the dimensionality of the completed matrix, creating a new feature representation upon which a standard classifier is trained. The authors also provide a convergence guarantee for their projected gradient descent implementation and demonstrate promising experimental results.

However, several points require further clarification. The paper states that LSI is employed to reduce the feature dimension (from *d* to *k*) because the completed matrix *M* “lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness.” This rationale is unclear. Is the primary motivation for dimensionality reduction computational efficiency, stemming from the expected density of *M* even with sparsity enforcement? If so, why was LSI specifically chosen over alternative dimensionality reduction techniques like PCA? A justification for this choice would be beneficial.

The paper appears to leverage external data in two distinct ways: using labeled source language data to improve performance on labeled target language data, and utilizing unlabeled parallel data. If this interpretation is correct, clearly delineating these two contributions and presenting experimental results separately would significantly enhance clarity.

Regarding the experimental setup (Section 5.2), it would be helpful to confirm my understanding. The training data consists of (a) 4,000 labeled source language documents, (b) 100 labeled target language documents, and (c) 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents. The classification performance is then evaluated on the remaining 1,900 labeled target language documents. Furthermore, Section 5.3 appears to only vary the number of unlabeled parallel documents, ranging from 200 to 2,000. Given this, the discrepancy between Figure 1 (where CL-KCCA outperforms TSL for EFM and EGM at 2,000 unlabeled documents) and Table 1 (where this trend is not observed) requires explanation.

The assumption of low-rank structure in a fully observed document-term matrix warrants further justification. While the matrix is sparse, it’s not immediately obvious why documents would be linear combinations of each other, suggesting the rank would likely be min(*d*, *k*). A clearer explanation of why low-rank is expected would strengthen the argument.

Intuitively, why does TSL generally outperform other methods, especially CCA? CCA seems like a natural approach to cross-language representation learning, as it directly addresses the problem as a multiview learning task. The proposed matrix completion approach feels less direct. The performance gain appears to be driven by the low-rank constraint imposed on *M*. A discussion comparing this constraint to other regularization or dimensionality reduction techniques used in cross-lingual learning would be valuable.

Finally, providing information regarding the computational cost of the algorithm is crucial. How does the running time of the projected gradient descent algorithm compare to that of other methods such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA?

Minor points: Please correct “(ij)” to “(i,j)” in the two sentences preceding Equation (2). Additionally, remove “such as” from the sentences before Equations (4) and (5).

Despite these points, this is a well-written and clearly presented paper. The proposed method is both simple and effective, and the results are promising. Addressing the above questions and clarifications would further strengthen the contribution.
