The paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-language representation learning, which is capable of exploiting a set of parallel bilingual documents to bridge the feature spaces of different languages. The approach involves two steps: first, it constructs a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix and then learns the unobserved feature entries of this sparse matrix by formulating a matrix completion problem and solving it using a projected gradient descent optimization algorithm; second, it performs latent semantic indexing over the recovered document-term matrix and induces a low-dimensional dense cross-lingual representation of the documents.

The proposed method is related to previous work on cross-language text classification, which can be broadly categorized into two groups: methods that transform the training or test data from one language domain into the other language domain, and methods that construct cross-lingual representations by exploiting bilingual word pairs, parallel corpora, and other multilingual resources. The proposed approach shares similarity with the latter group of methods but differs in that it first fills the missing entries of the document-term matrix using matrix completion and then performs latent semantic indexing over the recovered low-rank matrix.

The paper presents a convergence guarantee on the projected gradient descent algorithm and favorable experimental results on cross-language sentiment classification tasks on multilingual Amazon product reviews. The proposed method outperforms several other cross-language representation learning methods, including CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, especially when the number of parallel bilingual documents is small.

The paper is well-written, and the proposed method is clearly presented. However, some technical points need to be clarified. For instance, it would be helpful to elaborate on why the completed matrix M lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness and why LSI is chosen over other dimensionality reduction techniques. Additionally, it would be beneficial to clearly distinguish the two disjoint aspects of exploiting external data in this paper: leveraging the labeled source language data to help learn the labeled target language data, and leveraging the unlabeled parallel data.
