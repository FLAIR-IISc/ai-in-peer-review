

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that addresses the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages by leveraging parallel bilingual documents. The approach first constructs a partially observed document-term matrix where parallel documents provide complete rows while non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language's vocabulary. The authors then formulate a matrix completion problem to fill these missing entries while enforcing low-rank and sparsity constraints, followed by latent semantic indexing to produce a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation. This work builds on prior NIPS contributions like CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], and CL-OPCA [15], which directly operate on the concatenated document-term matrix without first completing it. Unlike these methods, the proposed approach captures cross-lingual co-occurrence patterns through matrix completion before dimensionality reduction, offering a novel perspective on cross-lingual representation learning.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a rigorous convergence analysis of the projected gradient descent algorithm used for matrix completion. The experimental evaluation on Amazon product reviews across multiple language pairs (English-French, English-German, English-Japanese) shows consistent and significant improvements over baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA), with the proposed method outperforming others by up to 14.61% accuracy. Notably, the approach maintains strong performance even with minimal parallel data (as few as 200 documents), demonstrating practical utility where other methods degrade significantly.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with clear mathematical formulations and well-structured exposition. The method is logically presented in a two-step process, and the experimental setup is detailed. However, several aspects would benefit from clarification to enhance reproducibility and understanding:

1. The rationale for using LSI specifically after matrix completion needs elaboration. The authors state that the completed matrix "lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness," but do not clearly explain why LSI is preferable to other dimensionality reduction techniques like PCA or random projections. The paper should clarify whether this choice is driven by empirical performance, theoretical properties, or computational considerations.

2. The paper conflates two distinct aspects of using external data: leveraging labeled source language data and leveraging unlabeled parallel documents. The experimental section (5.2) does not clearly separate these components, making it difficult to assess the specific contribution of the matrix completion step versus the use of labeled source data. A controlled experiment isolating the impact of parallel data would strengthen the claims.

3. The claim that "a fully observed document-term matrix is naturally low-rank" requires more justification. While document-term matrices are sparse, it's not obvious why they would be low-rank. The paper should clarify whether this property arises from the structure of the data (e.g., semantic relationships between words) or is an assumption made for the matrix completion problem.

4. The experimental results in Figure 1 and Table 1 present a discrepancy for tasks EFM and EGM at 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents. The paper should clarify why CL-KCCA outperforms TSL in Figure 1 but not in Table 1, which reports results with the full 2,000 parallel documents.

The originality of this work is strong, as the two-step approach (matrix completion followed by LSI) represents a novel strategy for cross-lingual representation learning that differs from existing methods. The significance is also high, as the method demonstrates practical advantages in scenarios with limited parallel data, a common constraint in real-world applications.

The paper's main weakness is the insufficient explanation of key technical choices and the experimental design. While the method is simple and effective, the paper would be stronger with more detailed justification for its design decisions and clearer presentation of the experimental setup.

In terms of the NIPS criteria:
- Quality: High (sound technical approach, rigorous convergence proof, strong empirical results)
- Clarity: Good (well-structured, but could improve explanations of key choices)
- Originality: High (novel two-step approach to cross-lingual representation learning)
- Significance: High (practical improvements with limited parallel data)

Pros for acceptance: Novel approach, strong experimental results, clear convergence guarantee, practical utility with minimal parallel data.

Cons for acceptance: Insufficient explanation for key technical choices (LSI selection, low-rank assumption), experimental design could better isolate contributions of parallel data versus labeled source data.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to cross-lingual representation learning with clear advantages over existing methods, particularly in data-scarce scenarios. With minor clarifications addressing the points above, this paper would make a strong addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
