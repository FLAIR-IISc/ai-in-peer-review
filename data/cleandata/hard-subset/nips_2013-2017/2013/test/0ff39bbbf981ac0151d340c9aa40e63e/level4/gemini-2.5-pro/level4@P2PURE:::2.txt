### Summary
This paper proposes a two-step method for learning cross-lingual topic representations, which first applies matrix completion and then latent semantic analysis. The matrix completion problem is optimized using a projected gradient descent algorithm. On a parallel Amazon review dataset for sentiment analysis, the proposed method is shown to outperform both monolingual and cross-lingual baselines. Furthermore, learning curves demonstrate that the algorithm can learn a highly accurate cross-lingual topic representation even with a relatively small amount of parallel data.

### Quality
The claims are supported by thorough experiments. The proposed two-step algorithm is rigorously benchmarked against several baselines, including a plain bag-of-words model and cross-lingual LSA, as well as two state-of-the-art cross-lingual dimensionality reduction algorithms.

### Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. It details all parameters and their selection criteria, which should make the results easy to reproduce. A minor suggestion would be to elaborate on the motivation for using matrix completion in the Introduction, as this is currently deferred to Section 3.

### Originality
While cross-lingual representation learning is an established research area, the application of matrix completion to this problem is novel. The proposed algorithm demonstrably outperforms previous methods in the literature, highlighting the contribution's originality.

### Significance
The proposed two-step method consistently outperforms all baselines, demonstrating its significance. The key insight from this work is that matrix completion can serve as an effective preprocessing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification. This formulation may also prove valuable for other cross-lingual tasks. The experimental results convincingly show that performing matrix completion before latent semantic analysis leads to a superior cross-lingual representation that outperforms even state-of-the-art methods and remains robust with varying amounts of unlabeled parallel data.
