The paper proposes a matrix completion approach to cross-domain (cross-language) classification that exploits both labeled data in an auxiliary/source domain and unlabeled parallel data. The method proceeds in two stages: (1) it constructs a single incomplete matrix spanning all documents and domains and then completes this matrix under low-rank and sparsity constraints via a projected gradient descent (PGD) algorithm; (2) it applies LSI to reduce the dimensionality of the completed matrix and then trains a standard classifier on this representation. The paper also provides a convergence guarantee for PGD and reports favorable experimental results.

Comments and questions:
- Dimensionality reduction after completion. After estimating the completed matrix M, you reduce the feature dimension from d to k using LSI because M “lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness.” Could you elaborate on this rationale? Is the main reason computational convenience, given that M* will be relatively dense even with sparsity enforcement? If so, why choose LSI specifically over other dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., PCA)?
- Disentangling the roles of external data. The paper appears to exploit external data in two distinct ways: (1) leveraging labeled source-language data to help learn in the target language, and (2) leveraging unlabeled parallel data. If this interpretation is correct, it would help to clearly distinguish these two aspects and report ablation results for each one separately.
- Experimental setup clarity (Section 5.2) and consistency (Section 5.3). Please confirm: are you using (a) 4k labeled source-language documents, (b) 100 labeled target-language documents, and (c) 2k unlabeled parallel documents for training, and then reporting classification accuracy on the remaining 1,900 labeled target-language documents? In Section 5.3, is the only change that you vary the number of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2k? If so, why is CL-KCCA better than TSL in Figure 1 for EFM and EGM at 2k, but not in Table 1?
- Low-rank assumption on document–term matrices. Could you clarify why a fully observed document–term matrix would be low-rank? Such matrices are sparse, but typically no document is a linear combination of others in the vocabulary space, so the rank would be min(d, k) absent additional structure. What modeling assumptions or empirical regularities justify the low-rank prior here?
- Intuition for TSL’s performance relative to CCA and other baselines. Can you provide more intuition for why TSL generally outperforms other methods, especially CCA-based approaches? A multiview method like CCA seems natural for cross-language representation learning, whereas matrix completion is less obvious. Is the primary driver of TSL’s gains the low-rank constraint on M*? How does this mechanism compare to what CCA (or other techniques) captures or fails to capture in this setting?
- Running time. What is the algorithm’s wall-clock time and computational complexity in practice? How does the cost of the PGD-based completion compare with CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA (e.g., more, less, or about the same), for the dataset sizes considered?

Suggested edits:
- Fix “(ij)” to “(i, j)” two sentences before Equation (2).
- Remove “such as” in the sentences before Equations (4) and (5).

The paper proposes a matrix completion approach to the cross-domain classification task. Although several technical points require clarification, the method is simple, effective, and, in general, clearly presented.
