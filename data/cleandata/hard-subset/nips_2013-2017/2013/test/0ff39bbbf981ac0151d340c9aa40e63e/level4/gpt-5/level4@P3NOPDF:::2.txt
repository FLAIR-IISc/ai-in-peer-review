Summary:
This paper proposes a two-step approach for learning cross-lingual topic representations by first performing matrix completion and then applying latent semantic analysis (LSA). The matrix completion step is optimized via a projected gradient descent algorithm. The method is evaluated on cross-lingual sentiment classification using a parallel Amazon reviews dataset, where it consistently outperforms monolingual and cross-lingual baselines, including state-of-the-art dimensionality reduction techniques. Learning curves show strong performance even with relatively small amounts of parallel unlabeled data.

Quality:
The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-aligned with the paper’s claims. The proposed method is compared against appropriate baselines: plain bag-of-words, cross-lingual LSA, and two state-of-the-art cross-lingual dimensionality reduction methods. The empirical results clearly demonstrate improvements in accuracy and robustness across different amounts of parallel data. The use of projected gradient descent for matrix completion appears effective, and the study provides informative learning curves that highlight data efficiency.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. Parameter settings and selection procedures are clearly described, which enhances reproducibility. One area for improvement is the motivation for using matrix completion: while Section 3 provides details, a brief, high-level rationale in the Introduction would help readers quickly understand why completion is critical in this cross-lingual setting (e.g., addressing sparsity and missing correspondences in bilingual term-document matrices).

Originality:
Applying matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning is a novel and well-justified idea. Although cross-lingual representation learning itself is an established topic, the proposed combination of completion followed by LSA is distinctive and practically impactful. The paper also contributes by demonstrating that this approach can serve as an effective preprocessing step for downstream sentiment classification.

Significance:
The performance gains over strong baselines indicate that the approach is both useful and competitive. The results suggest broader applicability beyond sentiment analysis—any task that benefits from cross-lingual low-dimensional representations could potentially profit from the same two-step pipeline. The demonstrated stability across different sizes of unlabeled parallel data is particularly encouraging for realistic scenarios where parallel resources are limited.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled two-step formulation that addresses a real pain point (missing data/sparsity) in cross-lingual settings.
- Solid empirical evaluation with strong baselines and compelling learning curves.
- Good attention to reproducibility: parameters and selection strategies are documented.
- Consistent gains that are maintained across varying amounts of unlabeled data.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Motivation and intuition: The introduction could better explain why matrix completion is especially suitable for cross-lingual term-document matrices (e.g., implicitly aligning lexical spaces, mitigating sparsity, and propagating information across languages).
- Ablations: It would strengthen the paper to include ablation studies explicitly isolating the contribution of the completion step beyond cross-lingual LSA (e.g., LSA alone with identical rank; alternative completion methods or loss functions; effect of removing the projected gradient step).
- Optimization details: More information on convergence criteria, step-size scheduling, stopping conditions, and initialization would improve clarity. A brief discussion of computational complexity and memory requirements would help readers assess scalability to larger vocabularies, more languages, or non-parallel corpora.
- Sensitivity analysis: Results on sensitivity to key hyperparameters (rank, regularization strengths, step size) and to the quality/quantity of parallel data would provide a clearer picture of robustness.
- Statistical significance: If not already included, report significance tests for key comparisons to support the claim of consistent superiority.
- Generalization: The evaluation is based on Amazon reviews. It would be helpful to comment on generalization to other domains or language pairs, especially those with higher typological differences or less aligned vocabularies.
- Baselines: Depending on the timeframe and availability, consider discussing comparisons to contemporaneous cross-lingual embedding approaches or structural correspondence learning variants, or clarify why they are not directly comparable.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a concise paragraph in the Introduction explaining the role of matrix completion in addressing sparsity and alignment gaps in bilingual matrices, and how it complements LSA.
- Provide an ablation that compares: (1) LSA without completion; (2) completion with different ranks; and (3) completion using alternative regularization (e.g., nuclear norm vs. low-rank factorization, if applicable).
- Include convergence plots or time-to-solution metrics, and a short complexity analysis.
- Report statistical significance and confidence intervals where appropriate.
- Offer a brief discussion on extending the approach to multi-lingual (>2) settings and to non-parallel (weakly aligned) corpora, if feasible.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-executed paper with a clear methodological contribution and strong empirical results. The idea of preceding LSA with matrix completion is novel in this context and demonstrably effective, especially in low-resource parallel scenarios. With minor clarifications—most notably strengthening the motivation in the Introduction, adding targeted ablations, and elaborating on optimization and scalability—the paper would be even more compelling. I recommend acceptance.
