This paper introduces a matrix completion method for cross-domain classification, designed to leverage both labeled data from an auxiliary domain and unlabeled parallel data. The proposed method consists of two main stages. First, it constructs a unified, incomplete matrix encompassing documents from all domains. This matrix is then completed under low-rank and sparsity constraints, optimized via a projected gradient descent algorithm. Second, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to the completed matrix for dimensionality reduction, and a standard classifier is subsequently trained on the resulting low-dimensional representations. The authors provide a convergence guarantee for their optimization algorithm and report strong experimental performance.

Here are some comments and questions:

-   The paper states that LSI is used for dimensionality reduction on the completed matrix M because it "lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness." Could the authors provide more detail on this point? Is the primary motivation for dimensionality reduction computational efficiency, given that the completed matrix M* is expected to be relatively dense despite the sparsity constraint? Furthermore, was there a specific reason for choosing LSI over alternative techniques like Principal Component Analysis (PCA)?

-   The method appears to leverage external data in two distinct ways: (1) using labeled source-domain data to aid classification in the target domain, and (2) incorporating unlabeled parallel data. If this understanding is accurate, the paper's clarity would be enhanced by explicitly distinguishing these two contributions and perhaps presenting their effects in separate ablation studies or results.

-   Relatedly, the experimental setup in Section 5.2 could be clarified. To confirm, is the training data composed of (a) 4,000 labeled source documents, (b) 100 labeled target documents, and (c) 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents, with evaluation performed on the remaining 1,900 labeled target documents? Following this, is the only variable changed in Section 5.3 the quantity of unlabeled parallel documents (from 200 to 2,000)? If so, please explain the apparent discrepancy where CL-KCCA outperforms TSL in Figure 1 (for EFM and EGM at 2k documents), while the opposite is shown in Table 1.

-   Could the authors provide a brief justification for the low-rank assumption on a fully observed document-term matrix? While such a matrix is certainly sparse, it is not immediately obvious that it would be low-rank, as individual documents are unlikely to be linear combinations of others. This would imply a rank closer to min(d, k).

-   What is the intuition behind the proposed TSL method's superior performance, particularly when compared to Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA)? Approaches like CCA, which treat the problem from a multiview perspective, seem like a very natural fit for cross-language learning. The proposed matrix completion framework is less conventional in this context. It appears the low-rank constraint on the completed matrix M* is the key to the method's success. How does this mechanism conceptually compare to the learning objectives of competing methods?

-   Could you comment on the computational complexity and empirical running time of the proposed algorithm? Specifically, how does the convergence time of the projected gradient descent optimization compare to that of the baseline methods, such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA?

-   Consider removing "such as" from the sentences before Equations (4) and (5) for conciseness.

This paper introduces a novel matrix completion framework for cross-domain classification. While several technical aspects require further clarification as noted above, the proposed method is commended for its simplicity, effectiveness, and overall clarity of presentation.
