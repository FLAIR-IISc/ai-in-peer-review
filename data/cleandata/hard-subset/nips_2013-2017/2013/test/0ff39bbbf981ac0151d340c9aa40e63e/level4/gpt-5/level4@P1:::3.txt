Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a two-step approach to cross-lingual representation learning. Step 1 formulates a matrix completion problem on a unified bilingual document–term matrix: parallel document pairs are fully observed across both vocabularies, while non-parallel documents are partially observed with missing entries in the other language. The completion objective combines a trace norm (low rank), an ℓ1 term (sparsity), and squared loss with nonnegativity, solved by a projected gradient descent algorithm with a convergence guarantee. Step 2 applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to obtain a shared low-dimensional representation, on which a linear SVM is trained. Experiments on cross-lingual sentiment classification for Amazon reviews compare against TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, reporting consistent gains, especially with few parallel documents.

This positions the work alongside CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA approaches that learn multilingual embeddings from parallel/comparable data, but differs by first imputing the bilingual matrix before applying LSI.

Quality:
- The optimization formulation is sound: the trace-norm relaxation of rank plus sparsity and nonnegativity is standard, and the projected gradient/shrinkage scheme with a convergence argument is appropriate.
- My main concern is robustness of the empirical advantage. Because the final classifier is a linear SVM, improvements may be partly due to feature scaling effects introduced by LSI that differ from OPCA or CCA. In other words, the gains might reflect a favorable scaling rather than a fundamentally better representation.
- To address this, please evaluate with a learning algorithm that is insensitive to per-feature rescaling, such as boosted decision trees (readily available in Weka). If the two-step method still outperforms alternatives under a scale-insensitive classifier, the claim becomes much more convincing.

Significance:
- If the improvement holds under scale-insensitive classifiers, this is a neat and potentially impactful result: matrix completion offers a well-controlled, non-linear inference mechanism to bridge languages and seems to reduce reliance on large parallel corpora.

Originality:
- The idea of first completing the bilingual document–term matrix and then applying LSI appears novel relative to prior cross-lingual representation methods (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA), which operate directly on the original concatenated matrices.

Clarity:
- Please clarify the construction of the “unified document–term matrix for all documents.” Do columns correspond to a single shared surface form across languages (e.g., “Merkel”), or are they language-tagged (e.g., “Merkelen,” “Merkelde”), effectively duplicating cross-lingual variants? The examples in the text leave this ambiguous.
- The experimental setup is hard to follow because key details are split across text and Table 1. It would help to consolidate in one place: which documents are labeled/unlabeled, how many parallel pairs are used, training/test splits, and how k, γ, ρ, and other hyperparameters are selected.

Pros:
- Conceptually simple and well-motivated two-step pipeline.
- Solid optimization with a convergence guarantee.
- Strong empirical results, particularly with few parallel documents.
- Potentially broad applicability to cross-lingual tasks.

Cons:
- Possible scaling confound due to reliance on a linear SVM.
- Only one classifier evaluated; robustness across learners not demonstrated.
- Ambiguity in the definition of the unified vocabulary/columns.
- Experimental description could be more streamlined.

Recommendation and suggestions:
- Add experiments with boosted decision trees to assess robustness to feature scaling. If the gains persist, it will substantially strengthen the paper.
- Clarify the unified matrix construction (shared vs language-specific columns) and provide a small illustrative example.
- Consolidate the experimental protocol and hyperparameter selection into a single subsection for readability.

Overall, this is a neat idea with promising results. Demonstrating that the improvements are not a scaling artifact of LSI with a linear SVM—and clarifying the matrix construction and experimental description—would make the contribution much stronger.
