This paper introduces a novel approach for cross-domain classification based on matrix completion. The method is designed to leverage both labeled data from an auxiliary (source) domain and unlabeled parallel data to improve classification performance in a target domain with limited labeled data. The approach consists of two main steps. First, it constructs a single, large, and incomplete matrix that encapsulates documents from all domains. This matrix is then completed using a projected gradient descent algorithm, subject to low-rank and sparsity constraints. Second, following completion, the feature dimensionality of the now-dense matrix is reduced using Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI). A standard classifier is then trained on this reduced, low-dimensional representation. The authors provide a convergence guarantee for their optimization algorithm and present experimental results that demonstrate the method's effectiveness compared to several baselines.

The proposed method is elegant in its simplicity, providing a unified framework for integrating labeled source data, labeled target data, and unlabeled parallel data. The experimental results are promising, and the paper is generally well-written. However, several aspects of the method and experiments require further clarification.

**Major Comments and Questions:**

1.  **Motivation for Dimensionality Reduction:** The motivation for the LSI step post-completion requires further clarification. The paper states this is because the completed matrix M* "lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness." This is somewhat counter-intuitive, as one would expect the completed matrix to be dense after the completion step. Could the authors elaborate on this reasoning? Is the primary motivation for dimensionality reduction computational efficiency, given the dense nature of M*? Furthermore, the choice of LSI over other techniques like PCA should be justified.

2.  **Clarity of Experimental Setup and Results:**
    *   The framework capably leverages two distinct sources of external information: labeled source-domain data and unlabeled parallel data. However, their individual contributions are not disentangled in the experiments. It would strengthen the paper to include an ablation study that evaluates the performance when using only the source-domain data, and then adding the parallel data, to clearly show the benefit of each component.
    *   The description of the experimental setting in Section 5.2 is ambiguous. To confirm: is the training data composed of (a) 4,000 labeled source documents, (b) 100 labeled target documents, and (c) 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents? Is the model then evaluated on the remaining 1,900 labeled target documents? Clarifying these details is crucial for reproducibility.
    *   There appears to be an inconsistency in the results. In Figure 1, for the EFM and EGM tasks with 2k parallel documents, CL-KCCA seems to outperform TSL, yet in Table 1, TSL is reported as superior. Could the authors please clarify this discrepancy?

3.  **Justification for the Low-Rank Assumption:** The central assumption of the model is that a fully observed document-term matrix would be low-rank. While these matrices are typically sparse, the low-rank assumption is less obvious. Intuitively, it is unlikely that any document is a perfect linear combination of a small set of other documents, which would imply that the rank of the full matrix is high (min(d, n), where d is vocabulary size and n is the number of documents). A more detailed intuition or justification for why this low-rank assumption is appropriate and effective in this context would be beneficial.

4.  **Intuitive Comparison with Baselines:** The paper would benefit from a deeper intuitive discussion on why the proposed matrix completion approach (TSL) generally outperforms methods like CCA, which seem like a more canonical choice for learning shared representations across domains. What is the key advantage conferred by enforcing a low-rank structure on the combined document-term matrix compared to, for instance, maximizing correlation between projected views as in CCA?

5.  **Computational Complexity:** The practical applicability of the method depends on its computational cost. The paper does not include a discussion of the algorithm's running time. A comparison of the training time for the projected gradient descent algorithm against the baseline methods (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, etc.) would be a valuable addition.

**Minor Suggested Changes:**

*   Two sentences before Equation (2), "(ij)" should be changed to "(i, j)" for clarity and consistency.
*   In the sentences preceding Equations (4) and (5), the phrase "such as" could be removed for improved conciseness.

In summary, this paper presents a simple and effective matrix completion method for cross-domain classification. While the core idea is appealing and the results are strong, the paper would be significantly improved by addressing the points above, particularly regarding the motivation for certain design choices and the clarity of the experimental setup.
