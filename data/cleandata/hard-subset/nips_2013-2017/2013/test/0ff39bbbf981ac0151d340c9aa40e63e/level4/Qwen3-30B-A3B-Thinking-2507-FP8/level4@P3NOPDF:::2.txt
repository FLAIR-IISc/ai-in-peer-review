

Review  
This paper introduces a novel two-step framework for cross-lingual topic representation learning, combining matrix completion with latent semantic analysis (LSA). The approach leverages projected gradient descent to solve the matrix completion problem, followed by LSA to derive cross-lingual topic representations. Experiments on parallel Amazon review data demonstrate consistent superiority over both monolingual baselines (e.g., bag-of-words) and cross-lingual baselines (including a state-of-the-art dimensionality reduction method) for sentiment analysis. Crucially, the method achieves high accuracy even with limited parallel data—learning curves reveal robust performance with small labeled sets, suggesting efficiency in data usage.  

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-structured. The authors rigorously compare their method against multiple baselines, including plain bag-of-words, cross-lingual LSA, and recent SOTA cross-lingual dimensionality reduction techniques. Results are statistically sound, with clear ablation studies on varying unlabeled data sizes, underscoring the method’s stability and scalability. All parameters and hyperparameter selection criteria are explicitly documented, enabling full reproducibility—a significant strength.  

The paper is well-organized and technically clear. However, a minor improvement would strengthen the motivation: the rationale for using matrix completion (rather than alternatives) is detailed in Section 3 but merits earlier elaboration in the Introduction. This would better contextualize the method’s novelty and align with the paper’s broader contribution.  

The work demonstrates clear originality. While cross-lingual representation learning is established, the application of matrix completion as a preprocessing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification is novel. The authors convincingly show that their approach outperforms prior methods in the literature, with performance gains consistently observed across metrics and data scales. This is not merely incremental but offers a fresh perspective on leveraging structured data constraints for cross-lingual tasks.  

The significance of this work extends beyond sentiment analysis. By demonstrating that matrix completion can effectively bridge language gaps in topic representation, the paper opens avenues for similar formulations in other cross-lingual NLP tasks (e.g., document classification, retrieval). The two-step paradigm—completing sparse cross-lingual data before dimensionality reduction—provides a generalizable strategy that could inspire future work in low-resource multilingual learning.  

Overall, this paper presents a compelling, well-executed method with strong empirical support. The technical contribution is meaningful, the experiments are robust, and the implications for cross-lingual learning are clear. The minor suggestion regarding the Introduction’s motivation is the only note for improvement. The work aligns well with NIPS’ focus on principled, data-driven approaches to challenging ML problems and would be a valuable addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance.
