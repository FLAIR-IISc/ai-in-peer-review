- Proposes a two-step cross-language representation method: matrix completion to fill missing entries in a unified bilingual document-term matrix, then LSI for low-dimensional embeddings.
- Evaluated on 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks using Amazon reviews and reports superior performance over several baselines.
- Clearly differentiates itself from translation-based approaches and prior cross-lingual methods (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA).
- Key novelty is applying matrix completion to impute missing language entries before LSI, a new application of matrix completion in this domain.
- Technical approach is sound with a well-motivated formulation and a rigorous convergence proof for projected gradient descent.
- Experimental design is generally appropriate but the claim of superiority may be confounded by a scaling artifact.
- Linear SVMs are sensitive to feature scaling while LSI rescales features, possibly explaining better performance rather than the matrix completion step.
- Recommends rerunning experiments with classifiers insensitive to feature scaling (e.g., boosted trees, random forests) to validate results.
- Clarity is good overall, but the unified document-term matrix description needs specification of vocabulary handling (surface forms vs. shared representation).
- Experimental setup description is fragmented across the paper and Table 1 and should be consolidated for readability.
- Strengths: clear problem formulation, rigorous optimization analysis, strong empirical results across languages/domains, effective with few parallel documents, and well-structured related work.
- Weaknesses: potential scaling artifact, fragmented experimental description, ambiguous vocabulary construction, and limited exploration of classifier sensitivity.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on additional experiments with scale-insensitive classifiers to confirm the method’s true advantage.
