## Review of NIPS 2013 Submission: Translingual Representation Learning via Matrix Completion and CL-LSI

This paper proposes a novel method for translingual representation learning, combining matrix completion to generate multilingual document vectors with Canonical Latent Semantic Indexing (CL-LSI) for dimensionality reduction. The core idea of leveraging matrix completion as a non-linear inference technique to build a shared representation across languages is interesting and potentially impactful.

**Quality:** While the proposed approach demonstrates promising results, I have concerns regarding the robustness of these findings. The observed performance gains could potentially be attributed to the inherent scaling properties of LSI, which may be more favorable than those of Orthogonal Procrustes Analysis (OPCA) or Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) when paired with a linear Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifier. Consequently, the observed improvements may not directly reflect the benefits of the proposed two-step process—matrix completion followed by CL-LSI—but rather a fortunate interaction with the chosen classifier. 

To address this concern, I strongly recommend the authors evaluate their system using machine learning models that are less sensitive to feature scaling. Specifically, employing a boosted decision tree ensemble (readily available in tools such as Weka) would provide a more reliable assessment of the true effectiveness of their method. If the proposed system consistently outperforms competing approaches when evaluated with boosted trees, I would be significantly more confident in the validity and significance of the results.

**Significance:** Should the results prove robust to classifier choice, this work would represent a meaningful contribution. The use of matrix completion—a well-defined and controlled non-linear inference technique—to build translingual representations is a potentially powerful approach, offering a principled alternative to existing methods. Demonstrating the efficacy of this approach would be a valuable addition to the field.

**Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion and CL-LSI for translingual representation learning appears to be a novel contribution. The authors effectively integrate these techniques to address the challenge of building shared representations across languages.

**Clarity:** The paper could benefit from improved clarity in several areas. Specifically, line 113’s discussion of “construct[ing] a unified document-term matrix for all documents” is ambiguous. It is unclear whether this refers to using a single term to represent all surface forms of a named entity (e.g., "Merkel" for "Merkelen" and "Merkelde"), or alternatively, treating each surface form as a distinct term labeled by language. Explicitly clarifying this point is crucial for understanding the methodology. 

Furthermore, the description of the experimental setup is fragmented and dispersed across Table 1. This made it challenging to quickly grasp the details of the experimental design. Consolidating this information into a dedicated section within the main text would greatly enhance readability and comprehension.

**Overall:** This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable approach to translingual representation learning. However, the reliance on a linear SVM classifier raises concerns regarding the robustness of the reported results. Addressing these concerns through experiments with alternative, scaling-insensitive classifiers—such as boosted decision trees—is essential. Improving the clarity of the paper, particularly concerning the document-term matrix construction and the experimental setup, would further strengthen the contribution. I encourage the authors to address these points in a revised version.
