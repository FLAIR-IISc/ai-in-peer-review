

This paper presents a matrix completion approach for cross-domain classification, leveraging labeled data from an auxiliary domain and unlabeled parallel data. The method constructs a single incomplete matrix spanning all documents and domains, completes it via projected gradient descent under low-rank and sparsity constraints, then applies LSI for dimensionality reduction before standard classification. The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and supported by convergence guarantees and competitive empirical results. The core idea is simple yet effective, and the presentation is generally clear. Below are specific comments to strengthen the paper.

**Key Clarifications Needed:**
1.  **LSI Justification and Alternatives:** The paper states LSI is used "because M lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness." This requires elaboration. Is the primary motivation computational efficiency (since the completed matrix M* becomes dense despite sparsity enforcement), or is there a specific theoretical advantage of LSI over alternatives like PCA for this task? Please clarify the rationale for choosing LSI over other dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., PCA, which is also linear and common for document data). A brief comparison of why LSI is preferable in this context would be valuable.

2.  **Disentangling Data Exploitation Mechanisms:** The method leverages two distinct data sources: (a) *labeled auxiliary domain data* to aid learning on the *labeled target domain*, and (b) *unlabeled parallel documents* to bridge domains. These are conceptually separate contributions. The paper should explicitly distinguish these two aspects in the methodology and results. Reporting results separately for each mechanism (e.g., "with only labeled source data," "with only unlabeled parallel data," "with both") would significantly enhance the clarity and impact of the experimental evaluation.

3.  **Experimental Setup Consistency:** The description in Section 5.2 and Figure 1/Table 1 requires careful reconciliation. The setup appears to be: (a) 4k labeled source documents, (b) 100 labeled target documents used for *training*, (c) 2k unlabeled parallel documents used for *training*, with classification reported on the remaining 1900 labeled target documents. Section 5.3 varies the unlabeled parallel data size (200-2k). However, Figure 1 shows CL-KCCA outperforming TSL for EFM/EGM at 2k unlabeled data, while Table 1 (which uses the full 2k) shows TSL as better. Please clarify this discrepancy. Is Table 1 using a different experimental configuration or a different metric than Figure 1? Consistency in reporting is essential.

**Technical Questions:**
4.  **Low-Rank Justification for Document-Term Matrices:** The paper assumes a fully observed document-term matrix is low-rank. While document-term matrices are often *sparse*, they are typically *not* low-rank (e.g., rank ≈ min(d, n) for d terms, n documents). Please provide intuition or evidence for why the *completed* matrix M* (or the underlying true representation) is expected to be low-rank, as this is central to the approach. How does this align with the typical high-rank nature of raw document-term matrices?

5.  **TSL's Superiority Over CCA-Based Methods:** TSL consistently outperforms methods like CL-KCCA and CL-OPCA. Given that CCA is a natural multiview approach for cross-domain learning, its relative underperformance is notable. What is the key insight driving TSL's success? Is it primarily the low-rank enforcement on M*, or are there other factors (e.g., the specific optimization, the use of LSI)? A brief discussion comparing the core mechanisms of TSL and CCA-based methods would help readers understand this empirical finding.

6.  **Computational Efficiency:** The paper does not report running times. Given the iterative nature of the projected gradient descent algorithm, how does its runtime compare to other methods (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA)? Is the overhead of matrix completion justified by the performance gains, particularly for larger datasets? A brief discussion or table of runtime comparisons would be useful for practical adoption.

**Minor Corrections:**
*   Correct "parellel" to "parallel" (line 2).
*   Replace "(ij)" with "(i,j)" two sentences before Equation (2).
*   Remove "such as" from the sentences preceding Equations (4) and (5).

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a promising work that effectively applies matrix completion to cross-domain classification. The method is novel in its integration of labeled auxiliary data and unlabeled parallel data within a unified matrix completion framework, and the results are compelling. Addressing the points above—particularly clarifying the LSI rationale, disentangling the two data exploitation mechanisms, resolving the experimental setup discrepancy, and justifying the low-rank assumption—would significantly strengthen the paper's technical contribution and readability. The core idea is sound and well-executed, and the paper is suitable for acceptance pending these clarifications. The suggested changes are minor but crucial for precision.
