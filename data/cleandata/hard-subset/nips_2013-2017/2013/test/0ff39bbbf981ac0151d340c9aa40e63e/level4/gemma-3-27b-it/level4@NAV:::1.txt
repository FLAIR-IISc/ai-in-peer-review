The paper introduces a matrix completion technique for cross-domain classification, effectively utilizing labeled data from an auxiliary domain alongside unlabeled parallel data. The method proceeds in two stages: first, constructing and completing a unified incomplete matrix representing documents from all domains, leveraging projected gradient descent with low-rank and sparsity constraints; and second, applying Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) for dimensionality reduction followed by training a standard classifier. A convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm is provided, accompanied by promising experimental results. 

However, several points warrant further clarification. The motivation for reducing the feature dimension from *d* to *k* using LSI, stated as addressing insufficient capacity to handle feature sparseness in the completed matrix *M*, could benefit from elaboration. Is the primary driver for this dimensionality reduction computational efficiency, given that *M* will likely be relatively dense even with sparsity enforcement? If so, what motivated the choice of LSI over alternative dimensionality reduction methods like PCA?

The paper appears to address two distinct aspects of leveraging external data: utilizing labeled source language data to enhance target language classification, and incorporating unlabeled parallel data. Explicitly differentiating these two contributions and presenting corresponding results separately would improve clarity. 

Relatedly, the experimental setup (section 5.2) requires further detailing. Specifically, was the training data comprised of (a) 4k labeled source language documents, (b) 100 labeled target language documents, and (c) 2k unlabeled parallel documents, with classification performance evaluated on the remaining 1900 labeled target language documents? And does section 5.3 solely vary the quantity of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2k? If so, the discrepancy in performance between CL-KCCA and TSL in Figure 1 (EFM, EGM at 2k) versus Table 1 is unclear.

The justification for assuming low-rank structure in a fully observed document-term matrix would be helpful. While sparse, it is unlikely that documents are linear combinations of each other, suggesting a rank of min(*d*, *k*). 

Furthermore, an intuition regarding TSL’s superior performance compared to methods like CCA would be valuable. Given that CCA is a naturally suited multiview approach for cross-lingual representation learning, the matrix completion strategy feels less intuitive.  The low-rank constraint imposed on *M* appears to be central to the method’s success; a discussion of how this constraint compares to other relevant techniques would be insightful.

Finally, reporting the algorithm’s running time, and comparing it to methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, would provide a more complete evaluation.

Minor corrections are suggested: please revise "(ij)" to "(i,j)" in the two instances preceding equation (2), and remove “such as” before equations (4) and (5). Overall, despite these areas for clarification, the paper presents a simple, effective, and generally well-articulated approach.
