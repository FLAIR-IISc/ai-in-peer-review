This paper proposes a matrix completion approach for cross-domain classification that leverages both labeled data from an auxiliary domain and unlabeled parallel data. The two-step approach first constructs a single, incomplete matrix containing documents from all domains. This matrix is then completed by enforcing low-rank and sparsity constraints via a projected gradient descent algorithm. In the second step, the dimensionality of the completed matrix is reduced using Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI), and a standard classifier is trained on the resulting low-dimensional representations. The authors provide a convergence guarantee for their algorithm and demonstrate favorable experimental results.

The method is simple, effective, and generally well-presented. However, several technical points require clarification.

**Comments and Questions:**

*   The authors state that after completing the matrix M, its dimensionality is reduced using LSI because M "lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness." Could the authors elaborate on this point? Is the primary motivation for dimensionality reduction to improve computational efficiency, given that the completed matrix M* will be relatively dense despite the sparsity constraint? Additionally, what was the rationale for choosing LSI over other dimensionality reduction techniques, such as PCA?
*   The proposed method appears to leverage two distinct types of external data: 1) labeled source-domain data to assist with learning in the target domain, and 2) unlabeled parallel data. If this understanding is correct, the paper would benefit from a clearer distinction between these two contributions, perhaps by including an ablation study or reporting experimental results that isolate the impact of each data type.
*   The experimental settings in Section 5.2 are somewhat unclear. To clarify: was the training data composed of (a) 4k labeled source-domain documents, (b) 100 labeled target-domain documents, and (c) 2k unlabeled parallel documents, with results reported on a test set of the remaining 1900 labeled target-domain documents? Furthermore, is the only difference in Section 5.3 the variation in the number of unlabeled parallel documents (from 200 to 2k)? If so, there appears to be an inconsistency: in Figure 1, CL-KCCA outperforms TSL on the EFM and EGM datasets with 2k parallel documents, whereas in Table 1, TSL outperforms CL-KCCA under what seems to be the same setting. Could the authors clarify this discrepancy?
*   Could the authors provide more intuition for the low-rank assumption on a fully observed document-term matrix? While such a matrix is typically sparse, it seems unlikely that documents would be exact linear combinations of one another. This would imply that the matrix is full-rank, i.e., its rank is min(#documents, #vocabulary size). Clarification on why the low-rank assumption is justified in this context would be helpful.
*   A multi-view method like Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) seems like a very natural fit for this cross-language representation learning task, whereas the matrix completion formulation is less conventional. Could the authors provide some intuition as to why their proposed method (TSL) generally outperforms alternatives, particularly CCA-based approaches? It appears the low-rank constraint is the primary driver of performance; how does imposing this structural assumption compare to the objectives of other techniques?
*   Could the authors comment on the computational complexity and empirical running time of the proposed algorithm? Specifically, how does the convergence time of the projected gradient descent algorithm compare to that of the baseline methods, such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA?

**Suggested Minor Changes:**

*   In the text two sentences before equation (2), please change "(ij)" to "(i,j)".
*   In the sentences preceding equations (4) and (5), the phrase "such as" should be removed for grammatical correctness.
