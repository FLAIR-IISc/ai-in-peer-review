- Proposes a novel two-step method (matrix completion to fill cross-lingual gaps, then LSI) to learn shared cross-lingual document representations.  
- Uses a small set of unlabeled parallel documents to bridge vocabularies and evaluates on Amazon cross-lingual sentiment classification.  
- Reports improved performance over baselines, especially with very limited parallel data.  
- Main experimental weakness: evaluation uses only a linear SVM, which is sensitive to feature scaling and may bias results.  
- Concern that observed gains could be due to better scaling for a linear SVM rather than superior semantic representations.  
- Recommends evaluating with classifiers insensitive to feature scaling (e.g., gradient-boosted trees or random forests).  
- Technical formulation is sound, with a clear optimization objective and projected gradient descent plus convergence analysis.  
- Clarity issue: unclear how the "unified document-term matrix" vocabulary is constructed — needs explicit definition.  
- Clarity issue: experimental setup details are fragmented across text and table captions and should be consolidated.  
- Originality: novel use of matrix completion preceding LSI to create a shared representation from an incomplete joint matrix.  
- Significance: high potential for low-resource cross-lingual learning if empirical robustness is established.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel elegant approach, solid technical backing, promising low-data results.  
- Argument for rejection: reliance on a single, scale-sensitive classifier undermines the robustness of the central claims.  
- Suggestions for improvement: add scale-invariant classifier evaluations, explicitly define unified matrix construction, and reorganize experimental description.  
- Overall recommendation: interesting and promising but not yet convincingly validated; reviewer would reconsider with additional classifier-robust results.
