The paper proposes a novel matrix completion approach to tackle the cross-domain classification task. This approach effectively leverages labeled data from an auxiliary domain and unlabeled parallel data. The method consists of two primary steps. Firstly, it constructs a single incomplete matrix encompassing all documents and domains, and then completes this matrix by enforcing low-rank and sparsity conditions using the projected gradient descent algorithm. Secondly, it reduces the feature dimension of the completed matrix using Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI), and subsequently trains a standard classifier on this new representation. The paper also provides a convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm and presents favorable experimental results.

This review aims to provide constructive feedback to enhance the paper's clarity and overall quality. Several comments and questions are raised to facilitate further discussion and improvement.

One aspect that requires clarification is the motivation behind reducing the feature dimension from d to k using LSI after estimating the completed matrix M. The authors mention that M "lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness." It would be beneficial to elaborate on this statement. Is the primary reason for dimensionality reduction the desire for smaller dimensions due to computational convenience, as M* will be relatively dense even with sparsity enforcement? Additionally, the choice of LSI over other dimensionality reduction techniques, such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA), warrants explanation.

The paper appears to exploit external data in two distinct ways: (1) leveraging labeled source language data to aid in learning labeled target language data, and (2) leveraging unlabeled parallel data. If this interpretation is correct, it would be helpful to clearly differentiate between these two aspects and report results separately to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the approach's strengths and limitations.

The experiment settings in Section 5.2 are somewhat unclear. To confirm, the training data consisted of (a) 4k labeled source language documents, (b) 100 labeled target language documents, and (c) 2k unlabeled parallel documents. The classification results were then reported on the remaining 1900 labeled target language documents. Furthermore, the primary difference in Section 5.3 lies in varying the number of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2k. In this context, it is puzzling that the performance of CL-KCCA surpasses that of TSL in Figure 1 for EFM and EGM at 2k, yet this is not reflected in Table 1. A clarification on this discrepancy would be appreciated.

The authors assume that a fully observed document-term matrix would be low-rank. While it is acknowledged that the matrix is sparse, it is unclear why it would be low-rank, as documents are unlikely to be linear combinations of other documents in the vocabulary, resulting in a rank of min(d, k).

The paper's results suggest that TSL generally outperforms other methods, including CCA. However, CCA, being a multiview approach, seems like a more natural fit for this cross-language representation learning task. In contrast, the matrix completion approach is less intuitive. The primary driving force behind TSL's performance appears to be the enforcement of the low-rank condition on M*. A comparison with other techniques would provide valuable insight into the relative strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

Lastly, it would be beneficial to report the running time of the algorithm. Specifically, how does the projected gradient descent algorithm's computational time compare to that of other methods, such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA?

Several minor suggestions are also proposed to enhance the paper's clarity and readability. Firstly, "(ij)" should be corrected to "(i,j)" two sentences before Equation (2). Secondly, the phrases "such as" should be removed from the sentences preceding Equations (4) and (5).

In conclusion, although some technical points in the paper require clarification, the proposed method is simple, effective, and generally well-presented. Addressing the raised questions and concerns will further strengthen the paper and provide a clearer understanding of the approach's contributions and limitations.
