Summary: This paper presents a two-step method for learning a cross-lingual topic representation, leveraging matrix completion and latent semantic analysis. A projected gradient descent algorithm is used to optimize the matrix completion problem. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms both monolingual and cross-lingual baselines on a sentiment analysis task using a parallel Amazon review dataset. Learning curves illustrating performance with varying amounts of unlabeled data show that the algorithm learns highly accurate cross-lingual topic representations with a relatively small amount of parallel data.

Quality: The experiments are thorough and effectively support the authors’ claims. The proposed two-step learning algorithm is carefully compared with two baselines – a plain bag-of-words model and cross-lingual LSA – as well as two state-of-the-art cross-lingual dimensionality reduction algorithms. 

Clarity: This paper is well-organized and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including a discussion of parameter selection. A minor suggestion would be to elaborate on the motivation for using matrix completion within the Introduction, although it is currently described in Section 3.

Originality: While cross-lingual representation learning is a well-studied task, the application of matrix completion to this problem is novel. The proposed algorithm clearly achieves improved performance compared to existing methods in the literature. 

Significance: The consistently superior results of the two-step approach are noteworthy. This work demonstrates the potential of matrix completion as a preprocessing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification, and this formulation may prove effective in other tasks as well. In summary, this paper proposes a two-step method—matrix completion followed by latent semantic analysis—to learn a cross-lingual topic representation for sentiment classification. Experimental results show the proposed method outperforms monolingual and other cross-lingual baselines, including a state-of-the-art method, and remains stable even with varying amounts of unlabeled parallel training data.
