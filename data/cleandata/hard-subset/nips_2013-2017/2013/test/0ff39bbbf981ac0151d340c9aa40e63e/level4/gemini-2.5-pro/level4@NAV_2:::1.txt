This paper introduces a novel two-step learning method for cross-language text classification. The core problem it addresses is the disjoint feature spaces between languages, which hinders the training of classifiers for a target language with scarce labeled data. The proposed approach first constructs a unified, partially-observed document-term matrix for both source and target language documents. It then uses a matrix completion algorithm, formulated as a convex optimization problem with trace norm and L1 regularization, to fill in the missing entries. This step is intended to recover a low-rank matrix that captures cross-lingual word co-occurrence patterns from a small set of parallel documents. In the second step, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to this completed matrix to derive a low-dimensional, dense cross-lingual representation. A standard monolingual classifier can then be trained on this representation. The method is evaluated on cross-language sentiment classification tasks using Amazon reviews, where it demonstrates superior performance compared to several related methods, particularly when the amount of available parallel data is limited. This work is related to previous approaches that use parallel or comparable corpora to learn shared representations, such as CL-LSI and CL-KCCA, but distinguishes itself by explicitly modeling the problem via matrix completion before dimensionality reduction.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality:** The formulation of the cross-lingual representation learning problem as a matrix completion task is novel. While matrix completion and LSI are established techniques, their two-step combination for this specific problem is a creative and interesting approach.
*   **Significance and Performance:** The method shows strong empirical results, significantly outperforming a number of competitive baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) across 18 different cross-language tasks. A particularly significant finding is the method's robustness when only a small number of unlabeled parallel documents are available, which is a common real-world scenario.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a theoretical convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm used to solve the optimization problem, which strengthens the technical soundness of the work.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The proposed approach is presented in a logical, step-by-step manner that is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Clarification:**
While the approach is promising, the paper could be strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Motivation and Intuition:**
    *   The paper states that a fully observed document-term matrix is "naturally low-rank." While these matrices are certainly sparse, the low-rank assumption is less intuitive, as one might not expect individual documents to be linear combinations of others. A more detailed justification for this key assumption would strengthen the paper's foundation.
    *   The motivation for the second LSI step could be clarified. The paper suggests M* "lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness," but M* should be relatively dense after completion. Is the primary goal of LSI to improve computational efficiency for the downstream classifier, or does it offer other semantic benefits over using the completed matrix directly?
    *   The paper would benefit from a more in-depth discussion providing intuition for why the proposed matrix completion approach is more effective than methods like CL-KCCA, which seem a very natural fit for learning shared representations from paired data. What specific properties of enforcing a low-rank structure on the unified matrix give it an edge?

2.  **Experimental Clarity and Evaluation:**
    *   The experimental setup in Section 5.2 should be stated more explicitly to ensure reproducibility. To confirm: was the training set for the proposed method composed of (a) all 4000 labeled source documents, (b) 100 randomly sampled labeled target documents for training the final SVM, and (c) the 2000 unlabeled parallel documents for representation learning? Is the model then evaluated on the remaining 1900 target documents?
    *   There appears to be a minor inconsistency in the reported results. In Table 1, TSL outperforms CL-KCCA on the EFM and EGM tasks. However, in Figure 1 (at np=2000), CL-KCCA appears to have slightly better or equal performance on these same tasks. This discrepancy should be clarified or corrected.
    *   The proposed method leverages two distinct types of external data: labeled source-language documents and unlabeled parallel documents. An ablation study to disentangle their effects would be valuable. For instance, an experiment using only labeled source/target data (without the parallel corpus) would help quantify the specific contribution of the matrix completion step in integrating the parallel data.
    *   A discussion of the computational complexity and empirical running time of the proposed algorithm compared to the baselines would be a valuable addition for assessing its practical utility.

**Minor Suggested Changes:**
*   Two sentences before equation (2), "(ij)" should be "(i, j)" for consistency.
*   In Section 4.1, the phrase "such as f(M) = ..." before equation (3) could be rephrased, as f(M) is defined here, not given as an example.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and effective method for cross-language representation learning. The empirical results are strong, and the approach is well-motivated. Addressing the points of clarification regarding the underlying assumptions and experimental details would further strengthen this solid contribution.
