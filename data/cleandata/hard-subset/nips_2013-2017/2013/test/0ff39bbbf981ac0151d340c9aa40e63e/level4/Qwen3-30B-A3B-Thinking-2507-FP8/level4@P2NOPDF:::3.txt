

The paper introduces a translingual representation learning approach combining matrix completion for multilingual vector construction with CL-LSA (not LSI) on the resulting vectors. While the method presents an interesting direction, the evaluation's robustness requires clarification.  

**Quality:** The reported performance gains over OPCA and CCA appear potentially attributable to the linear SVM's sensitivity to feature scaling rather than the core method's merits. Specifically, LSI-based scaling may inherently favor linear SVMs over other techniques. To validate the approach's true efficacy, the authors should replace the linear SVM with a scaling-insensitive model—such as gradient-boosted decision trees (e.g., in Weka)—as a more reliable benchmark. If the two-step system (matrix completion + CL-LSA) consistently outperforms alternatives with such a classifier, the results would be more convincing. Without this test, the observed gains remain ambiguous.  

**Significance:** If the results hold under scaling-insensitive evaluation, this work would demonstrate matrix completion as a well-controlled non-linear inference technique for cross-lingual tasks—a meaningful contribution to the field.  

**Novelty:** The core idea appears novel within the translingual representation learning context.  

**Clarity:** Several points require clarification:  
1. Line 113 describes a "unified document-term matrix for all documents." It is unclear whether this implies language-agnostic term normalization (e.g., treating "Merkel" and "Merkelen" as the same term via lemmatization) or language-specific surface forms (e.g., retaining distinct tokens per language). The authors should explicitly specify the term treatment strategy.  
2. The experimental setup description is fragmented across Table 1 and surrounding text, causing confusion. Consolidating this into a single, clear section would greatly improve readability.  

**Overall:** The proposal is promising but hinges on addressing the scaling artifact concern. The current evaluation does not sufficiently isolate the method's contribution from SVM-specific scaling effects. A revised evaluation with a scaling-insensitive classifier would strengthen the paper significantly. While the approach merits attention, the present results do not conclusively demonstrate robustness. The authors should prioritize this validation before publication.
