This paper proposes a novel two-step method to learn cross-lingual semantic data representations for cross language text classification by exploiting unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The authors first formulate a matrix completion problem to infer unobserved feature values of the concatenated document-term matrix in the space of unified vocabulary set from the source and target languages. Then, they perform latent semantic indexing over the completed low-rank document-term matrix to produce a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation of the documents. Monolingual classifiers are then used to conduct cross language text classification based on the learned document representation.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning. The proposed approach is thoroughly evaluated through extensive experiments with tasks of cross language sentiment classification on Amazon product reviews. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed two-step learning method significantly outperforms the other four comparison methods. Moreover, the proposed approach needs much less parallel documents to produce a good cross language text classification system.

The quality of the paper is high, with experiments thoroughly carried out to support the claim. The proposed two-step learning algorithm is carefully compared with two baselines (plain bag-of-words, cross lingual LSA) and two state-of-the-art cross-lingual dimensionality reduction algorithms. The authors provide a clear description of the parameter selection process, making it easy to reproduce the results.

The originality of the paper lies in the application of matrix completion to the task of cross-lingual representation learning. Although the task of cross-lingual representation learning is not new, the proposed algorithm achieves better performance than previous methods in the literature. The significance of the paper is also high, as the result of the two-step approach is consistently higher than other baselines. The authors demonstrate that matrix completion can be used as a preprocessing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks, and this kind of formulation may be effective in other tasks as well.

One minor comment is that the motivation for using matrix completion could be explained more clearly in the introduction, although it is described in Section 3.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to cross-lingual representation learning that achieves better performance than previous methods.
* The experimental results are thorough and well-presented, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The paper may benefit from a clearer explanation of the motivation for using matrix completion in the introduction.
* Some readers may find the technical details of the matrix completion algorithm and the latent semantic indexing method challenging to follow.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed approach is thoroughly evaluated through extensive experiments. The originality and significance of the paper are high, making it a valuable contribution to the field of natural language processing.
