

**Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"**

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning that leverages parallel bilingual documents to bridge the feature spaces of different languages. The authors first construct a unified document-term matrix where parallel documents are fully observed and non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language. They then solve a matrix completion problem to fill these missing entries, followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The method is evaluated on cross-language sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, with results showing superior performance compared to several baselines.

The paper situates its work well within the NIPS literature. It properly distinguishes itself from translation-based approaches (e.g., [18, 6, 23]) that translate documents between languages, and from other cross-lingual representation methods (e.g., CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], CL-OPCA [15]). The key innovation is using matrix completion to fill missing entries in the document-term matrix before applying LSI, rather than applying these techniques directly to the incomplete matrix. This approach aligns with the growing interest in using matrix completion for representation learning, as seen in recent NIPS papers like Candès and Recht [4].

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound, with a well-motivated matrix completion formulation and a rigorous convergence proof for the projected gradient descent algorithm. The experimental design is generally appropriate, with 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks across three languages. However, the claim that the proposed method significantly outperforms baselines may be overstated due to a potential scaling artifact. The authors use linear SVMs, which are sensitive to feature scaling, while LSI inherently rescales features. This could explain the superior results rather than the matrix completion component being the key contribution. I recommend repeating experiments with classifiers insensitive to feature scaling (e.g., boosted decision trees or random forests) to verify if the results hold. Without this, the evidence for the method's superiority remains somewhat tenuous.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, though some aspects need clarification. The description of the unified document-term matrix (Line 113) requires specification of whether the vocabulary combines surface forms (e.g., "Merkel" and "Merkelen" as separate terms) or uses a common representation for words across languages. The experimental setup description is fragmented across the paper and Table 1, making it somewhat difficult to follow. A more consolidated presentation of the experimental design would improve readability. The algorithm description is clear and well-organized.

**Originality:** The core idea of using matrix completion to fill missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix before applying LSI appears novel. While matrix completion has been used for related tasks (e.g., [4, 11]), this specific application to cross-language representation learning represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors have adequately distinguished their work from previous approaches in the related work section, particularly highlighting how their method differs from CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA.

**Significance:** If the results hold with classifiers insensitive to feature scaling, this work would be significant as it provides a principled way to leverage small amounts of parallel data for cross-language representation learning. The paper's claim that the method performs well even with a small number of parallel documents (Figures 1-2) is particularly promising for real-world applications where parallel data is scarce. However, the significance is currently limited by the potential scaling artifact identified above.

**Strengths:**
- Clear problem formulation with well-motivated matrix completion approach
- Rigorous convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm
- Strong empirical results across multiple language pairs and domains
- Demonstrated effectiveness with small amounts of parallel data
- Well-structured related work that properly contextualizes the contribution

**Weaknesses:**
- Potential scaling artifact in results due to linear SVM sensitivity
- Fragmented presentation of experimental setup
- Ambiguity in describing the unified vocabulary construction
- Limited exploration of classifier sensitivity to feature scaling

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should repeat their experiments with classifiers insensitive to feature scaling (e.g., boosted decision trees or random forests) to confirm whether the TSL method's superiority is due to the matrix completion component or simply a feature scaling effect. This would significantly strengthen the paper's claims and provide more convincing evidence for the method's value. The paper addresses an important problem in cross-language learning with a novel approach, and with these revisions, it would make a valuable contribution to the field.
