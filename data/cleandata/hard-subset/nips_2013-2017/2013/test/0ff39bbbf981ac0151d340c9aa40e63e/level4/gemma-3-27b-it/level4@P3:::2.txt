This paper presents a novel two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, combining matrix completion with latent semantic analysis (LSA). The approach aims to bridge the feature space gap between languages by leveraging parallel bilingual documents, and employs a projected gradient descent algorithm for optimization. The method is evaluated on cross-lingual sentiment classification using Amazon product reviews, demonstrating improved performance compared to several baselines, particularly when the amount of parallel data is limited.

This work addresses the important problem of cross-lingual text classification, a task frequently encountered in multilingual NLP applications. Previous NIPS work has explored related techniques, including cross-lingual embeddings (e.g., [16]), topic modeling ([19]), and adaptation methods utilizing machine translation [18, 23]. This paper builds on this foundation by introducing a matrix completion step to refine cross-lingual representations, a strategy not previously prominent in this line of research.

The paper is technically sound and the experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The authors compare their method against a strong set of baselines, including a bag-of-words approach, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA. The inclusion of results with varying amounts of parallel data provides valuable insight into the method’s robustness. The convergence analysis of the projected gradient descent algorithm, though detailed, adds to the paper’s rigor. The authors are careful to report results across multiple tasks, offering a more holistic assessment of the approach’s performance. 

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The notation is consistent, and the algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner. The description of parameter selection is particularly helpful for reproducibility. While the motivation for matrix completion is stated in Section 3, briefly elaborating on its benefit – namely, handling sparsity and capturing co-occurrence patterns – in the introduction would further enhance clarity. 

The originality of this work lies in the application of matrix completion as a preprocessing step for cross-lingual representation learning. While both matrix completion and LSA are established techniques, their combination within this framework appears novel. The authors effectively demonstrate that this approach offers performance improvements over existing methods, suggesting a unique contribution to the field. The thorough referencing of related work indicates awareness of the existing literature.

The significance of this work is substantial. The consistent outperformance of the proposed method, particularly in low-resource scenarios (i.e., limited parallel data), is a valuable contribution. The findings suggest that matrix completion can be a beneficial preprocessing step for cross-lingual tasks beyond sentiment classification.  The method provides a practical approach for leveraging limited parallel data, which is often a constraint in real-world applications. This research advances the state of the art by offering a new and effective technique for cross-lingual representation learning.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical results demonstrating superior performance over existing methods.
*   Novel application of matrix completion to the cross-lingual representation learning problem.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation, including analysis with varying amounts of parallel data.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the method and results.
*   Theoretical justification for the optimization algorithm.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The improvement over existing methods, while consistent, is not always dramatic. (Though the advantage is more pronounced in low-resource settings).
*   The theoretical analysis of the matrix completion step could be further developed.



**Overall Assessment:**  I recommend acceptance. This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual NLP. The proposed method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates promising results. The clarity of the presentation and the thoroughness of the evaluation make this a strong submission.
