The paper proposes a method for translingual representation learning, first employing matrix completion to generate multilingual vectors for each row, and then applying Canonical-Latent Semantic Indexing (CL-LSI) to these vectors.

Quality: While the proposed approach appears promising, I am not convinced that the reported results are robust. The observed improvements could be attributable to the scaling properties of LSI being more favorable than those of Orthogonal Procrustes Canonical Correlation Analysis (OPCA) or Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) when used in conjunction with a linear SVM. 

I recommend the authors evaluate their system using a machine learning system less sensitive to feature scaling, such as boosted decision trees (readily available in Weka, for example). If the two-step system consistently outperforms other techniques when coupled with boosted decision trees, I would be more confident in the validity of the results. 

Significance: A robust demonstration of the method’s effectiveness would be a valuable contribution, as matrix completion offers a well-defined, non-linear inference technique.

Novelty: The core idea presented appears novel.

Clarity: The paper suffers from some clarity issues. Specifically, line 113 references “construct[ing] a unified document-term matrix for all documents,” but it is unclear whether this involves using a single term to represent all surface forms (e.g., “Merkel”) or maintaining separate labels for each surface form in each language (e.g., “Merkelen” and “Merkelde”). Clarification on this point is needed. Furthermore, the description of the experimental setup is fragmented across Table 1, making it somewhat difficult to fully grasp.

Overall, this appears to be a neat idea. I encourage the authors to explore classifiers beyond the linear SVM – my primary concern is whether the observed performance gains are robust or simply an artifact of the feature scaling characteristics of LSI.
