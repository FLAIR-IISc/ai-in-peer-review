This paper presents a novel two-step method for learning cross-lingual topic representations, leveraging matrix completion and latent semantic analysis. It employs a projected gradient descent algorithm to optimize the matrix completion problem. Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms monolingual baselines and existing cross-lingual approaches on a sentiment analysis task utilizing a parallel Amazon review dataset. Furthermore, learning curves with varying amounts of unlabeled data illustrate the algorithm’s ability to learn highly accurate cross-lingual topic representations even with a limited number of parallel examples.

**Quality:** The experiments are thorough and convincingly support the authors’ claims. The proposed two-step learning algorithm is carefully compared to a straightforward bag-of-words baseline, cross-lingual LSA, and two state-of-the-art cross-lingual dimensionality reduction algorithms. The convergence analysis of the optimization algorithm is also a strong point.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. All parameters and the method for selecting them are detailed, facilitating reproducibility. A minor suggestion: the motivation for employing matrix completion could be elaborated upon further in the Introduction, though it is adequately described in Section 3. The mathematical notation is generally clear, though occasionally dense.

**Originality:** While cross-lingual representation learning is a well-established field, the application of matrix completion to this task is novel. The authors clearly demonstrate that the proposed algorithm achieves superior performance compared to existing methods in the literature. The combination of matrix completion as a pre-processing step followed by LSI appears to be a unique contribution.

**Significance:** The proposed two-step approach consistently yields higher results than the established baselines. This work suggests that matrix completion can be effectively utilized as a pre-processing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification, and this formulation may prove beneficial in other related tasks as well. The robustness of the method even with limited parallel data is particularly noteworthy.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning.
*   Strong empirical results consistently outperform existing methods.
*   Demonstrated robustness with limited parallel data.
*   Clear and well-supported technical arguments, including convergence analysis.
*   Reproducibility is facilitated by detailed parameter settings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The performance gains, while consistent, are not always dramatic. Further analysis of failure cases or ablation studies could strengthen the paper.
*   The paper primarily focuses on sentiment analysis; exploring other downstream tasks could broaden the impact.



In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to cross-lingual representation learning. The authors have conducted thorough experiments and provided a clear and well-written description of their method. I recommend acceptance.
