- Paper proposes cross-language text classification via a two-step pipeline: convex nonnegative matrix completion on a concatenated bilingual doc-term matrix, then LSI on the completed matrix for monolingual SVM classification.
- Method leverages small sets of parallel bilingual documents as fully observed rows and completes partially observed non-parallel rows with nuclear-norm and sparsity regularization.
- Optimization is convex with nonnegativity, nuclear norm, and sparsity terms, solved by projected gradient with singular value shrinkage and convergence guarantees.
- Empirical evaluation on multilingual Amazon reviews (EN, FR, DE, JP; three domains) shows consistent gains over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, especially with scarce parallel data.
- Strength: simple, generally applicable two-step pipeline that integrates easily with existing monolingual classifiers.
- Strength: sound convex formulation and clear convergence analysis built on established optimization results.
- Strength: strong empirical performance across 18 transfer settings, robust when parallel data is limited.
- Strength: sensible experimental protocol with model selection reused across tasks and comparisons to common baselines.
- Weakness: unclear rationale for the LSI step — need ablation vs. no LSI and comparisons to PCA/NMF or joint low-rank factorization.
- Weakness: low-rank assumption is stated without empirical justification; need singular value decay plots and clearer wording.
- Weakness: unclear contributions of labeled source data vs. unlabeled parallel data; recommend ablations isolating each source.
- Weakness: experimental details need clarification (training/test splits, number of labeled/unlabeled docs) and reconcile discrepancies between Table 1 and Fig. 1.
- Weakness: insufficient intuition for why matrix-completion-based TSL outperforms CCA-style methods; suggest explanations about using all co-occurrences, sparsity, and nuclear-norm alignment.
- Weakness: scalability concerns from repeated SVDs — need wall-clock time, memory usage, SVD strategy (partial/randomized), iteration counts, stopping criteria, and parameter sensitivity.
- Quality: technically sound formulation and clear convergence analysis, but additional ablations and runtime metrics would improve rigor.
- Clarity: generally well organized and readable, but experimental protocol, LSI motivation, and low-rank justification need clarification.
- Originality: novel combination of matrix completion with cross-lingual representation learning and a clean projected-gradient nonnegative instantiation.
- Significance: meaningful improvements in low-resource parallel settings with likely practical utility.
- Arguments for acceptance: simple, broadly applicable method with convex guarantees and consistent empirical gains, particularly under limited parallel data.
- Arguments against acceptance: missing analyses for LSI and low-rank assumptions, experimental inconsistencies, and no runtime/scalability evaluation.
- Minor comments/typos: fix “(ij)”→“(i, j)”, remove “such as” before Eqs. (4)/(5), correct “parellel”→“parallel”, “documentterm”→“document-term”, and motivate/discuss nonnegativity’s empirical effect.
