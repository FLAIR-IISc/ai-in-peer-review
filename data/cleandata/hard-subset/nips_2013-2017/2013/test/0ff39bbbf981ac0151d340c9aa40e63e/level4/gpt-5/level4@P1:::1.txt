This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that exploits both labeled data in an auxiliary (source) language and unlabeled parallel documents. The approach first constructs a unified, partially observed document–term matrix spanning both languages, then completes this matrix via a convex relaxation of a low-rank, sparse matrix completion objective optimized with a projected gradient descent algorithm. In the second step, it applies LSI to the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation on which a standard classifier is trained. The authors provide a convergence guarantee for the optimization procedure and report favorable results on multilingual Amazon sentiment classification, comparing against CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple pipeline: matrix completion to bridge vocabularies, followed by LSI for dimensionality reduction.
- Uses a scalable projected gradient method with a convergence argument grounded in non-expansive operators.
- Competitive empirical performance on Amazon sentiment benchmarks with a standard SVM classifier.
- The formulation directly leverages observed cross-lingual co-occurrences in parallel data while regularizing missing entries.

Questions and points needing clarification:
- On the rationale for the LSI step: After estimating M*, you reduce the dimensionality from d to k because M* “lacks sufficient capacity of handling feature sparseness.” Please elaborate. Is the main motivation computational and statistical—i.e., that M* will be relatively dense despite ℓ1 regularization, and a lower-dimensional semantic space improves generalization? If so, why choose LSI specifically over alternatives such as PCA or other low-rank factorization methods?
- The paper appears to leverage two distinct sources of external information: (1) labeled source-language data and (2) unlabeled parallel data. It would be helpful to clearly distinguish these contributions experimentally (e.g., ablations showing the effect of each).
- Experimental setup (Section 5.2) is a bit hard to follow. Please confirm: did you use (a) 4k labeled source-language documents, (b) 100 labeled target-language documents, and (c) 2k unlabeled parallel documents for training, and evaluate on the remaining 1900 labeled target documents? In Section 5.3, are you varying only the number of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2k? If so, why does CL-KCCA outperform TSL for EFM and EGM at 2k in Figure 1, whereas it does not in Table 1?
- You state that a fully observed document–term matrix is “naturally low-rank and sparse.” Sparsity is clear, but low rank is not obvious: in general, documents need not be linear combinations of others, so the rank can be as high as min(t, d). Please clarify the conditions under which low-rank structure is expected (e.g., latent topic structure), and how the trace norm promotes this in practice.
- Intuition for why TSL outperforms multiview methods like CCA: CCA/KCCA are natural choices for bilingual representation learning. In contrast, matrix completion may seem less direct. Is the key advantage the reconstruction of missing cross-lingual features under a low-rank prior that captures within- and cross-language co-occurrence patterns before LSI, thereby reducing noise and mismatch? A brief conceptual comparison to CL-LSI/CL-OPCA/CL-KCCA would help.
- Runtime: How does the projected gradient descent for (2) compare computationally with CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA on your datasets (both in theory—per iteration complexity—and in wall-clock time)?

Minor editorial suggestions:
- Fix “(ij)” to “(i, j)” two sentences before Equation (2).
- Remove “such as” in the sentences immediately before Equations (4) and (5).

Assessment by review criteria:
- Quality: The optimization is sound (convex relaxation with convergence guarantee), and the empirical evaluation is convincing on the chosen benchmark. Some theoretical claims (e.g., low-rank justification) would benefit from clearer explanation.
- Clarity: Generally well organized and readable. The experimental protocol should be described more precisely, and the motivation for the LSI step should be elaborated.
- Originality: The combination of matrix completion with LSI for cross-lingual representation is a neat and, to my knowledge, novel twist compared to applying LSI/OPCA/KCCA directly to concatenated features.
- Significance: The method is simple to implement, seems effective, and compares favorably to standard baselines. Clarifying the contributions of labeled source vs. unlabeled parallel data would strengthen the case for broader impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, principled method with a clear optimization framework and convergence guarantee.
- Strong empirical performance against established baselines.
- Practical relevance for cross-lingual classification with limited target labels.

Cons/concerns:
- Need for clearer justification of the low-rank assumption and the LSI step choice.
- Ambiguities in the experimental setup and in the discrepancy between Table 1 and Figure 1.
- No runtime or scalability comparison to competing methods.

Overall: Although a few technical points need clarification, the approach is simple, effective, and, in general, clearly presented.
