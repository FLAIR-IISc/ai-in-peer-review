Summary
The paper addresses cross-language text classification by proposing a two-step representation learning method that leverages a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 completes a unified bilingual document-term matrix in which non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language; completion is formulated as a convex optimization problem combining a trace-norm penalty (low rank), an ℓ1 penalty (sparsity; linear under nonnegativity), squared losses on observed entries, a small quadratic penalty on missing entries, and nonnegativity constraints. A projected gradient scheme with a gradient step on the smooth part, singular-value shrinkage, and projection to the nonnegative orthant is used; a convergence argument is provided. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain a shared low-dimensional representation, after which a linear SVM is trained. Experiments on the Amazon multilingual sentiment dataset (EN–FR/DE/JA; 18 directions and categories) show consistent improvements over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, particularly when the number of parallel documents is small.

Relation to previous work
The paper is situated within cross-lingual representation learning using parallel or comparable data (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, bilingual topic models) and methods translating data into a common language followed by monolingual learning (including multi-view/co-training). The key distinction is performing matrix completion prior to representation learning, rather than applying LSI/KCCA/OPCA directly to the concatenated matrix or parallel pairs. This is a simple but appealing idea that uses bilingual signal from parallel pairs to propagate cross-lingual structure to non-parallel documents.

Quality
- Method: The optimization objective is reasonable and well-motivated: trace norm for low-rank structure, tiny ℓ1 penalty under nonnegativity, squared loss on observed entries, and a small penalty on unobserved entries to discourage overfitting. The algorithmic choice—gradient step on the smooth part, singular value shrinkage, and projection—is computationally plausible for medium-scale problems. However, the convergence analysis appears to conflate proximal splitting for a sum of two nonsmooth terms. The update uses prox of the nuclear norm followed by projection; the proximal of the sum (nuclear norm + indicator of the nonnegative orthant) is not, in general, the composition of the two proximals. The cited fixed-point non-expansiveness result (Ma et al., 2011) covers a different problem without the extra projection and Ω̂ penalty. As written, the proof does not establish convergence to the minimizer of the stated objective. This is the main technical concern.
- Experiments: The evaluation on 18 tasks is comprehensive, with sensible protocol and parameter selection, reporting means and standard deviations, and a study of performance vs. number of parallel documents. Using all unlabeled documents (including test) for representation learning is transductive; competing methods also exploit unlabeled data, but the baseline TBOW does not, which should be noted in discussing fairness.
- Missing baselines: Structural Correspondence Learning (SCL; Prettenhofer & Stein, 2010) is a strong and widely used baseline on this dataset and is cited but not compared. Bilingual topic models (e.g., Smet et al., 2011) are also notable references; a comparison to at least one representative topic-model baseline would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The construction of the unified matrix, the role of Ω and Ω̂, and the algorithm steps are clear. Important implementation details are missing: how SVDs are computed per iteration (full vs. partial/randomized), stopping criteria, runtime/memory, and the scale of t and d. The statement that fully observed document-term matrices are “naturally low-rank” overreaches; an approximate low-rank assumption should be justified empirically or rephrased.

Originality
The idea of combining matrix completion with cross-lingual representation learning is, to the best of this reviewer’s knowledge, novel relative to CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA. The specific convex formulation with nonnegativity and a small quadratic penalty on missing entries is a reasonable variation.

Significance
Empirically, the method yields consistent gains, especially in low-parallel-data regimes, which is valuable for practical cross-lingual scenarios. If the optimization/convergence issues are addressed, the approach could be adopted by practitioners working on low-resource transfer. As it stands, the technical contribution would benefit from a corrected or alternative solver with a sound guarantee.

Pros
- Simple, conceptually clean two-step pipeline with strong empirical results across 18 tasks.
- Robustness to small numbers of parallel documents is convincingly demonstrated.
- Broad comparisons to classical cross-lingual representation methods.

Cons
- Convergence proof does not match the proposed composite nonsmooth objective; correctness of the algorithm as a solver for the stated problem is not established.
- Missing strong baselines (SCL, a multilingual topic model), and no machine-translation-based baseline.
- Limited discussion of computational cost and scalability; repeated SVDs can be expensive at realistic vocabularies.
- Transductive use of all unlabeled documents is not explicitly contrasted with inductive settings.

Suggestions to improve the paper
Essential
- Fix the optimization section. Options include: (a) reformulate using a proximal gradient method where the nonsmooth part is the sum of nuclear norm and nonnegativity indicator, with a proper proximal operator (e.g., via Dykstra’s method or Douglas–Rachford splitting), and provide a correct convergence guarantee; or (b) adopt ADMM/PDHG with variable splitting and prove convergence for the given objective. Empirically show the corrected solver matches or improves current results.
- Add at least one strong additional baseline: SCL on this dataset. If feasible, include a bilingual topic model baseline. Clarify whether MT-based baselines are out of scope; if so, explain.
- Report computational complexity, implementation details (partial/randomized SVD, stopping tolerance), and wall-clock/runtime on representative tasks.

Optional but valuable
- Provide an ablation: (i) LSI on the raw concatenated matrix (CL-LSI; already present), (ii) matrix completion without LSI (direct classification on M*), (iii) effect of removing the ℓ1 term and nonnegativity, (iv) sensitivity to ρ and γ.
- Evaluate an inductive setting in which representation learning does not use the test set’s unlabeled documents; or clarify that all methods are transductive and discuss implications.
- Analyze how many parallel pairs are required to propagate cross-lingual structure (e.g., plot performance vs. number of fully observed rows only, holding total unlabeled size fixed).

Overall assessment
The paper proposes a sensible and empirically effective idea with promising results and clear practical relevance. However, the current optimization analysis is not sound for the stated objective, and important baselines are missing. With a corrected solver and stronger comparisons, this work would make a solid contribution. As submitted, it is borderline due to the methodological gap.
