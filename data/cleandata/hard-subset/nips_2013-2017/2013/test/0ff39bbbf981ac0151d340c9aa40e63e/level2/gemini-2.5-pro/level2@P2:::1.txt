This paper addresses the problem of cross-language text classification, where labeled data in a source language is used to classify documents in a target language with scarce labels. The core challenge is bridging the disjoint vocabularies. The authors propose a novel two-step learning method (TSL) that leverages a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. First, all documents are placed in a unified document-term matrix spanning the vocabularies of both languages. This results in a matrix with many missing entries, as monolingual documents only have values for their own language's terms. The authors' key insight is to formulate this as a matrix completion problem, aiming to recover a low-rank, sparse, and non-negative matrix that fills in these missing cross-lingual term values. This optimization problem is solved using a projected gradient descent algorithm for which a convergence proof is provided. In the second step, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to this completed matrix to learn a low-dimensional, shared semantic representation. Classifiers can then be trained in this common space.

The proposed method is a novel extension of prior work on learning cross-lingual representations, such as Cross-Lingual LSI (CL-LSI), CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA. While these methods also operate on a concatenated document-term matrix from parallel corpora, they typically treat missing entries as zeros. This paper's main contribution is the explicit and principled step of "filling in the blanks" using matrix completion, based on the well-founded assumption that the true, complete matrix is low-rank.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The idea of applying matrix completion as a precursor to dimensionality reduction for cross-lingual representation learning is novel and well-motivated. It provides a more principled way to handle the unified document-term matrix structure than simply assuming missing entries are zero. The results suggest this is a significant contribution, as the method shows strong performance, particularly in the low-resource setting where only a small number of parallel documents are available. This robustness makes the approach highly relevant for practical applications where large parallel corpora are not always available.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The optimization problem for matrix completion is well-formulated, using standard convex relaxations (trace norm for rank, L1 for sparsity) and incorporating non-negativity constraints. The authors provide a projected gradient descent algorithm for solving the problem and include a convergence analysis, which adds mathematical rigor to their contribution.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The proposed TSL method is compared against several relevant and strong baselines on a standard dataset across 18 different cross-lingual tasks. The results consistently demonstrate the superiority of TSL. The analysis of the method's performance with a varying number of parallel documents is particularly insightful and effectively highlights the key advantage of the proposed approach: its ability to perform well even with limited parallel data.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is generally clear, but some details could be elaborated for better reproducibility.
    *   The initialization of matrix `M` in Algorithm 1 is described as "the nonnegative projection of the rank-1 approximation of M0". It is not immediately clear how this rank-1 approximation is computed from the partially observed matrix `M0`. A more explicit statement (e.g., is SVD performed on `M0` with zeros substituting missing values?) would be helpful.
    *   The convergence proof for Theorem 1 heavily references a theorem from a previous paper [11]. While this is common practice, a brief sketch of the proof's logic would make the paper more self-contained.

2.  **Depth of Analysis:**
    *   The hyperparameter selection was performed on a single task (EFB) and then fixed for all 18 tasks. While this demonstrates some level of robustness, it would be beneficial to include a brief sensitivity analysis. How much does performance vary with different choices of γ, ρ, and the latent dimension `k` on other tasks? This would provide a better understanding of the method's stability.
    *   The core claim is that matrix completion effectively imputes meaningful cross-lingual term information. This could be strengthened with a qualitative analysis. For example, it would be interesting to inspect the completed matrix `M*` and see if the imputed values for a French document in the English vocabulary space are semantically plausible. This would provide more direct evidence for why the completion step is so effective.

3.  **Related Work:** The related work section is adequate, but could be slightly expanded. The proposed method combines matrix completion (a form of matrix factorization) with LSI (SVD). The discussion could benefit from a comparison to other joint matrix factorization techniques for cross-lingual learning, such as those based on Non-negative Matrix Factorization (e.g., [13]), to better position the work within the broader context of factorization-based methods.

In summary, this is a strong paper presenting a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound method for cross-language representation learning. The experimental results are comprehensive and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, especially in low-resource scenarios. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed with additional clarification and analysis. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is a good fit for NIPS.
