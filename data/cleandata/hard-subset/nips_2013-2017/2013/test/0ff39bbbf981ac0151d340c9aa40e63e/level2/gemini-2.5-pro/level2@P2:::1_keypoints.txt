- Problem: Cross-language text classification using labeled source-language data and scarce target-language labels; main challenge is disjoint vocabularies.
- Method: Proposes two-step learning (TSL): matrix completion to impute missing cross-lingual term values, then LSI to learn a shared low-dimensional semantic space.
- Key insight: Treat unified document-term matrix with missing entries as a matrix completion problem assuming the true matrix is low-rank, sparse, and non-negative.
- Optimization: Formulates convex relaxations (trace norm, L1, non-negativity) and solves via projected gradient descent with a provided convergence proof.
- Novelty: Novel application of matrix completion as a principled precursor to dimensionality reduction for cross-lingual representation learning.
- Empirical strength: Thorough experiments on 18 cross-lingual tasks show consistent superiority, especially in low-resource settings with few parallel documents.
- Reproducibility issue: Initialization of matrix M (rank-1 nonnegative projection of M0) is not clearly specified for partially observed M0.
- Theoretical clarity: Convergence proof heavily cites prior work; a brief proof sketch would improve self-containment.
- Analysis gap: Hyperparameters were tuned on a single task and fixed; a sensitivity analysis across tasks would better characterize robustness.
- Qualitative validation missing: Inspecting imputed entries in the completed matrix M* would strengthen the claim that completion yields semantically meaningful cross-lingual information.
- Related work: Related-work discussion could better compare to other joint factorization approaches (e.g., NMF-based cross-lingual methods) to position the contribution.
- Overall assessment: Strong, well-motivated, technically sound contribution with convincing empirical results; minor clarifications and additional analyses would improve the paper.
