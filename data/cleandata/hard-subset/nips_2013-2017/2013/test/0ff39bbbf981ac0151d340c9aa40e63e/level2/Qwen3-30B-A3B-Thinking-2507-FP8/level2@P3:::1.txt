

The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that addresses the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages. The approach first constructs a unified document-term matrix from bilingual documents and fills missing entries through matrix completion, then applies latent semantic indexing to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The method is evaluated on Amazon product reviews for cross-lingual sentiment classification, with results showing consistent improvements over existing approaches, particularly when parallel bilingual documents are scarce.

The work makes a technically sound contribution to the field of cross-lingual text classification. The matrix completion formulation (Equation 2) is well-motivated by the observation that document-term matrices are naturally low-rank, and the projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence guarantees (Theorem 1) provides a scalable solution to this optimization problem. The experimental setup is thorough, with 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks across three languages (English, French, German, Japanese) and three product categories. The results (Table 1 and Figures 1-2) demonstrate that the proposed method (TSL) consistently outperforms four baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA), with improvements of 8.59-14.61% over the target baseline. The analysis of varying amounts of parallel data (Figure 1-2) further highlights the method's robustness with limited parallel resources, a significant practical advantage.

The paper's strengths include a clear problem formulation, rigorous theoretical analysis of the optimization algorithm, and comprehensive empirical evaluation. The comparison with related work in Section 2 effectively positions the contribution, noting that prior methods (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) apply operations directly on the incomplete document-term matrix, whereas the proposed approach first completes the matrix. This distinction is well-justified and represents a meaningful technical contribution.

However, the paper could be strengthened in several ways. The discussion of the matrix completion approach would benefit from a clearer explanation of why this intermediate step is necessary compared to simply using the available parallel documents for training. While the authors note that existing methods require translating all training documents, the paper does not explicitly address how the proposed method avoids the need for translation while still leveraging the semantic relationships between languages. A brief analysis of the computational complexity compared to the baselines would also be valuable, particularly regarding scalability to larger datasets.

The experimental section would be more compelling if it included a comparison with more recent approaches from 2013 NIPS (e.g., neural network-based methods that were emerging at the time), though this is not strictly necessary for the contribution. The paper's focus on the matrix completion approach is appropriate for the time period, but a brief acknowledgment of the growing interest in neural methods would provide better context.

The writing is clear and well-organized, with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithm is presented with appropriate pseudocode. The paper adequately addresses the limitations of the approach by noting that it requires a small set of unlabeled parallel documents, which aligns with the practical constraints of cross-lingual learning.

The significance of the work is evident from the consistent performance improvements across diverse language pairs and product categories. The ability to achieve strong results with minimal parallel data (as demonstrated in Section 5.3) addresses a key practical challenge in cross-lingual learning, making the approach valuable for real-world applications where parallel data is scarce.

The paper meets all four criteria for quality at NIPS: it is technically sound with appropriate theoretical support, clearly written with sufficient detail, makes a meaningful contribution by introducing a novel two-step approach that differs from prior work, and demonstrates significant improvements over baselines with practical implications.

The main opportunity for improvement would be to strengthen the discussion of why the matrix completion step is necessary and how it specifically addresses the limitations of existing methods. A more detailed comparison of computational requirements would also enhance the paper's contribution. These are reasonable additions that would require minimal additional work.

Overall, this paper presents a solid technical contribution to cross-lingual representation learning with clear experimental validation. The method is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and demonstrates significant practical advantages over existing approaches, particularly when parallel data is limited. The work is a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and would be of interest to researchers working on multilingual NLP systems.
