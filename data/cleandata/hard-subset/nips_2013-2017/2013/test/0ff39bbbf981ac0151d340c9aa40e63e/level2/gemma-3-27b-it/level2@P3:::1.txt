This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning. The core idea is to first complete a partially observed document-term matrix constructed from parallel bilingual documents, and then apply Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to obtain cross-lingual document representations. The paper clearly identifies the challenge of disjoint feature spaces in cross-language text classification and proposes a sensible approach to bridge this gap. The experimental evaluation on Amazon product reviews demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly when the amount of parallel data is limited.

The paper is well-structured and the writing is generally clear. The motivation for the work is clearly presented, and the approach is explained in a logical manner. The related work section provides a good overview of existing techniques for cross-language text classification, and the paper appropriately positions its contribution within the existing literature. The optimization algorithm is described with sufficient detail, and the convergence analysis provides some theoretical justification for the method. The experimental setup is also well-defined, and the results are presented clearly in tables and figures.

One strength of the paper is its focus on the scenario where only a small amount of parallel data is available. This is a practical consideration, as obtaining large amounts of parallel data can be expensive and time-consuming. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms other methods in this setting, which is a significant contribution. Another strength is the careful consideration of the optimization problem and the use of a projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence guarantees. The ablation study on the amount of parallel data is also helpful in understanding the behavior of the method.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be improved. First, the matrix completion step relies on the assumption that the document-term matrix is low-rank. While this assumption is reasonable, it is not explicitly justified in the paper. It would be helpful to provide some evidence or intuition to support this assumption, perhaps by analyzing the rank of real-world document-term matrices. Second, the choice of the LSI dimension, k, seems somewhat arbitrary. The paper mentions selecting k from a set of values {20, 50, 100, 200, 500}, but does not provide a clear rationale for these values. A more systematic approach to selecting k, such as using cross-validation, might be beneficial. Third, the experiments are limited to sentiment classification on Amazon product reviews. While this is a good starting point, it would be helpful to evaluate the method on other datasets and tasks to assess its generality. Including results from topic classification or document categorization would strengthen the findings. Finally, the description of Algorithm 1 lacks details that would allow for easy reproduction of the work. Specifically, the initialization of `M` with “the nonnegative projection of the rank-1 approximation of M0” needs further explanation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The proposed method is novel and addresses a challenging problem in cross-language text classification.
*   The experimental results demonstrate that the method outperforms existing techniques, especially when the amount of parallel data is limited.
*   The theoretical analysis provides some guarantees about the convergence of the optimization algorithm.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The assumption of low-rank document-term matrices is not fully justified.
*   The selection of the LSI dimension, k, is somewhat arbitrary.
*   The experiments are limited to a single dataset and task.
*   Lack of implementation details hindering reproducibility.



**Overall assessment:**

This is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of cross-language text classification. The proposed method is novel, effective, and well-motivated. While there are some areas where the paper could be improved, these are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. A revision addressing the points above would make this paper a strong addition to NIPS.
