- Proposes a two-step cross-lingual representation method: matrix completion on a unified document-term matrix followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI).
- Evaluated on Amazon product reviews for cross-lingual sentiment classification across English, French, German, and Japanese.
- Experimental setup is thorough: 18 cross-lingual tasks across three product categories.
- Results show consistent improvements over four baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA), with 8.59–14.61% gains over the target baseline.
- Method is especially robust when parallel bilingual documents are scarce, demonstrating practical value.
- Matrix completion formulation is well-motivated by the low-rank nature of document-term matrices.
- Optimization uses projected gradient descent with convergence guarantees (Theorem 1), yielding a scalable solution.
- Key technical novelty: completing the document-term matrix before applying cross-lingual operations, unlike prior work that operates on incomplete matrices.
- Strengths: clear problem formulation, rigorous theoretical analysis, comprehensive empirical evaluation, and reproducible presentation.
- Weakness: unclear explanation of why matrix completion is necessary versus simply using available parallel documents for training.
- Weakness: paper doesn’t explicitly explain how it avoids translation while leveraging cross-lingual semantics.
- Weakness: lacks computational complexity analysis and scalability comparison to baselines.
- Weakness: missing comparison to contemporary neural network–based methods (circa 2013 NIPS), though acknowledged as not strictly necessary.
- Overall significance: technically sound, practically relevant, meets NIPS quality criteria; main improvements would be clarifying the necessity of matrix completion and adding computational/modern-method comparisons.
