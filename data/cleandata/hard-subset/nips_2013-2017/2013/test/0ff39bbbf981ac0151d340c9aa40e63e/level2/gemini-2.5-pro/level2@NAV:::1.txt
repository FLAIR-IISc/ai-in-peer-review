This paper proposes a novel two-step learning method (TSL) for cross-lingual text classification. The core contribution is a representation learning technique that leverages a small set of parallel documents. First, the method constructs a unified document-term matrix for both source and target languages. For documents that are not part of a parallel pair, entries corresponding to the other language's vocabulary are treated as missing. A matrix completion algorithm, solved via projected gradient descent, is then used to infer these missing entries, creating a complete, low-rank matrix. Second, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to this completed matrix to generate a low-dimensional, shared semantic space. This approach is positioned as an improvement over methods like Cross-Lingual LSI (CL-LSI), which implicitly treat missing cross-lingual features as zeros rather than explicitly inferring them.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Quality:** The idea of applying matrix completion to the cross-lingual document-term matrix before dimensionality reduction is novel and intuitive. It provides a principled way to leverage word co-occurrence patterns both within and across languages. The technical contribution is solid, including a convergence proof for the proposed optimization algorithm.
*   **Significance and Empirical Support:** The paper's claims are well-supported by extensive experiments on 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks. TSL consistently and significantly outperforms relevant baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA). The most compelling result is the method's strong performance even with a very small number of parallel documents (as few as 200), demonstrating its practical utility in low-resource scenarios.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation, methodology, and experimental setup are all clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Scalability Discussion:** The paper lacks a discussion on the computational complexity and scalability of the matrix completion step. Given that document-term matrices can be very large, an analysis of the runtime in comparison to the baselines would strengthen the work.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The method involves several hyperparameters (γ, ρ, µ, k). While a selection process is described, a more thorough sensitivity analysis would be beneficial to understand how robust the performance is to different parameter settings across various tasks.
*   **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The baselines are appropriate for the class of methods being compared. However, a brief discussion acknowledging more recent neural approaches (e.g., using multilingual pre-trained language models) would help situate the work within the broader, contemporary state of the art.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel contribution, thorough empirical validation, and significant practical implications for low-resource cross-lingual learning. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed in a final version.
