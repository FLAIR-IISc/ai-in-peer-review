

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning using matrix completion on bilingual document-term matrices, addressing the core challenge of disjoint language feature spaces. The key claim is that filling missing entries via matrix completion followed by LSI yields superior cross-lingual representations compared to prior methods, particularly with minimal parallel data. The authors position this work against established approaches like CL-LSI [10] and CL-KCCA [20], emphasizing their two-step process (matrix completion → LSI) as distinct from direct application of LSI/KCCA on incomplete matrices.

The paper's strengths are substantial. It provides rigorous theoretical support via convergence proofs for the projected gradient descent algorithm (Lemmas 1-3), which is rare for applied NLP work. The empirical evaluation on 18 Amazon sentiment tasks is comprehensive: TSL consistently outperforms all baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) by 8.59–14.61% average accuracy, with statistically significant gains (10 repetitions per task). Crucially, TSL demonstrates robustness with as few as 200 unlabeled parallel documents—a major practical advantage over competitors. The methodology aligns well with NIPS' focus on sound technical contributions, and the literature review correctly contextualizes the work against seminal NIPS papers like CL-LSI.

Weaknesses are minor but noteworthy. While the paper acknowledges the small-data advantage, it omits analysis of failure modes (e.g., performance on highly dissimilar language pairs like English-Japanese). A brief ablation study comparing matrix completion vs. direct LSI on the same matrix would further strengthen the novelty claim. Additionally, the experimental scope is limited to sentiment classification; extension to other NLP tasks (e.g., named entity recognition) would enhance significance.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution meeting all NIPS criteria. The method is technically sound (quality), clearly presented (clarity), offers a meaningful innovation (originality), and solves a practical problem (significance). The minor limitations do not undermine the strong results. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility within conference scope. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing failure analysis.
