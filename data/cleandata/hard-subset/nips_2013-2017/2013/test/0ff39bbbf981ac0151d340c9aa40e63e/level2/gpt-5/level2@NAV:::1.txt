Summary and relation to prior work: The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning: (1) complete a unified bilingual document–term matrix via convex matrix completion with trace-norm and sparsity regularization under nonnegativity constraints, solved by a projected gradient descent with shrinkage and a convergence proof; (2) apply LSI to the completed matrix to obtain a shared low-dimensional representation for downstream monolingual classification. The approach is evaluated on 18 English–French/German/Japanese Amazon sentiment tasks. The work is well-situated relative to CL-LSI (Littman et al.), CL-KCCA (Vinokourov et al.), and CL-OPCA (Platt et al.), as well as NIPS work on translated learning and multilingual co-/multi-view learning, but differs by explicitly imputing missing cross-lingual features before dimensionality reduction.

Quality: The optimization formulation is sound, and the convergence argument follows established non-expansive operator results. Empirical support is reasonably strong: multiple tasks, multiple runs, and comparisons to three relevant baselines show consistent gains, particularly when parallel data is scarce. However, statistical significance is not reported, and computational efficiency (per-iteration SVD cost, memory footprint) is not discussed. The choice to regularize missing entries toward zero (ρ term) is intuitive but could bias imputations; an ablation on ρ/µ is missing.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written and organized; the algorithm and notations are explicit. Reproducibility is fair (hyperparameter ranges provided), though implementation details (runtime, hardware, stopping criteria, handling of TF-IDF scaling) are sparse.

Originality: Using convex matrix completion to bridge disjoint vocabularies and then applying LSI is a novel and plausible combination; the optimization itself is standard. The idea of imputing cross-lingual co-occurrences from few parallel documents is a fresh angle relative to applying LSI/CCA directly.

Significance: The method advances cross-lingual classification performance on a standard benchmark and appears particularly useful in low-parallel-data regimes. Broader impact would be stronger with evaluations beyond sentiment and beyond Amazon reviews.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled formulation with convergence guarantees.
- Consistent empirical improvements over strong baselines, especially with limited parallel data.
- Simple pipeline compatible with standard classifiers.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing statistical tests and runtime/scalability analysis.
- Limited dataset diversity; no comparison to word- or sentence-level cross-lingual embeddings.
- No ablations isolating the effect of matrix completion vs. LSI.

Suggestions: Report significance tests; add ablations on ρ/µ and “LSI-only” vs. full two-step; include computational complexity and wall-clock results; compare to bilingual embedding approaches; analyze learned structure (e.g., cross-lingual nearest neighbors, effective rank). Overall, a solid, promising contribution; addressing the empirical and scalability gaps would strengthen it further.
