The paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-language representation learning, which aims to bridge the feature spaces of different languages by exploiting a set of parallel bilingual documents. The method first formulates a matrix completion problem to produce a complete parallel document-term matrix for all documents in two languages, and then induces a low-dimensional cross-lingual document representation by applying latent semantic indexing on the obtained matrix.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its components. The authors provide a thorough review of related work in the field, highlighting the differences between their approach and existing methods. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with a large-scale dataset and multiple baselines for comparison.

The proposed method is technically sound, with a clear and well-motivated formulation of the matrix completion problem. The use of a projected gradient descent algorithm to solve the optimization problem is also well-justified. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, showing significant improvements over the baseline methods.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to bridge the gap between different languages using a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. This is a challenging problem, and the authors' approach shows promise in addressing it. The method is also robust to the size of the unlabeled parallel data, as demonstrated by the experimental results.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main limitations is the assumption that the parallel bilingual documents are available. In practice, obtaining such documents can be challenging, especially for low-resource languages. The authors could have discussed this limitation and potential ways to address it.

Another limitation is the use of a simple linear support vector machine (SVM) as the monolingual classification model. While SVM is a popular choice, it may not be the best choice for all scenarios. The authors could have explored other classification models, such as deep neural networks, to see if they can improve the performance of the proposed method.

In terms of originality, the paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-language representation learning. While the individual components of the method, such as matrix completion and latent semantic indexing, are not new, the way they are combined and applied to cross-language representation learning is innovative.

The significance of the paper lies in its ability to address the challenging problem of cross-language text classification. The proposed method shows promise in improving the performance of cross-language text classification systems, especially when the number of parallel bilingual documents is small. The paper also contributes to the development of more robust and effective methods for cross-language representation learning.

In conclusion, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing. The proposed method shows promise in addressing the challenging problem of cross-language text classification, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness.
