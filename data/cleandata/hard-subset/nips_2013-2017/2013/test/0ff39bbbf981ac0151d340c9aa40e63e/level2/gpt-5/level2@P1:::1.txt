Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a two-step approach to cross-lingual representation learning for text classification. Step 1 completes a partially observed, concatenated bilingual document-term matrix by solving a convex matrix completion problem with a nuclear-norm penalty, an ℓ1 penalty (which reduces to a linear term under nonnegativity), squared loss on observed entries, squared shrinkage on unobserved entries (weighted by ρ), and a nonnegativity constraint. The optimization is carried out via a projected gradient descent scheme with singular-value shrinkage and nonnegative projection; a convergence argument based on non-expansiveness is provided. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix and trains linear SVMs in the shared low-dimensional space. Experiments on Amazon multilingual reviews (EN, FR, DE, JA) for sentiment classification show consistent gains over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, with particularly strong performance when only a small number of parallel documents are available.

The work connects to established cross-lingual representation learning methods, notably CL-LSI (SVD on bilingual concatenations), CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, as well as earlier approaches using bilingual lexicons and topic models. The main novelty relative to CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA is to explicitly impute missing cross-lingual features via convex matrix completion before performing LSI, rather than operating directly on an incomplete/zero-filled matrix or only on paired documents.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple idea that bridges disjoint vocabularies without heavy reliance on large parallel corpora or translation of all data.
- Convex formulation with a scalable proximal/projection algorithm and a clear convergence argument (under specified step size).
- Thorough experimental protocol on a standard benchmark with many language/category pairs; gains are consistent and larger when parallel data are scarce.
- Sensible hyperparameter selection strategy and an informative analysis of performance vs. number of parallel pairs.

Weaknesses and detailed comments:

Quality (technical soundness and evaluation):
- Objective design: The loss includes a penalty ρ∑(i,j)∈Ω̂ 1/2‖Mij‖2, which biases missing entries toward zero. This is reasonable for unobserved within-language words but may oppose cross-lingual imputation for non-parallel documents. Please analyze sensitivity to ρ more extensively and discuss its interpretation: for which regimes of ρ do cross-lingual signals survive versus being shrunk away?
- Algorithmic details: The convergence proof establishes non-expansiveness of each operator and cites prior work for convergence, but a precise stopping criterion, convergence rate, and complexity per-iteration are not reported. Since each iteration requires an SVD (or truncated SVD), practical scalability depends on matrix sizes (t and d), rank, and choice of SVD routine. Please report wall-clock time, memory footprint, average rank across settings, and whether randomized/truncated SVD is used.
- Baselines and ablations: CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA are appropriate baselines. However:
  - An ablation with “no completion” (set γ=0, ρ=0) followed by LSI would isolate the benefit of the completion step beyond standard CL-LSI.
  - An ablation with “completion only” (use M* directly without LSI) would test whether the second step is necessary.
  - Include multilingual topic models (e.g., bilingual LDA) or probabilistic/weighted matrix factorization with missing data as additional baselines, as they also address incomplete observations.
- Statistical testing: Means and standard deviations are reported; please add significance testing across runs for the main comparisons.
- Reproducibility: Clarify TF-IDF computation (per-language vs. joint corpus), feature normalization, and any preprocessing (stopwording, document length normalization). Also specify initialization (“rank-1 approximation” details) and stopping tolerance.

Clarity:
- The term “complete parallel document-term matrix” is potentially confusing; only paired documents are fully observed, others are completed by optimization. Clarify this early to avoid implying fully parallel corpora are required.
- Provide more intuition for the two regularizers: nuclear norm vs. LSI. Since both encourage low-rank structure, why is the second SVD step needed after nuclear-norm minimization? A short discussion or an empirical comparison (e.g., varying k and γ) would help.
- Describe how the “parallel bilingual documents” in the dataset are constructed (human- or machine-translated, one-to-one alignment, domain match).

Originality:
- The combination of convex matrix completion (with nonnegativity) applied to a bilingual document-term matrix followed by LSI is, to my knowledge, novel in cross-lingual text classification. It is related to weighted/regularized matrix factorization with missing entries, but the two-step decomposition (impute then LSI) is a distinctive modeling choice.

Significance:
- The empirical gains, especially with few parallel documents, are promising and practically relevant. The approach has potential beyond sentiment classification. Including at least one additional domain (e.g., news or Wikipedia) would strengthen generality claims.
- The work could influence follow-on research in cross-lingual representation learning where parallel data are limited.

Specific questions for the authors:
- How sensitive is performance to γ, ρ, and τ beyond the selected ranges? Can you provide a brief sensitivity plot?
- Do you use term reweighting or rescaling across languages to mitigate frequency/IDF mismatches?
- How large are the learned ranks prior to the final LSI step? Does the nuclear norm alone already produce a compact representation?
- For CL-LSI and CL-OPCA, what data are used to construct pseudo “dual-language documents” when documents are not paired? Please clarify to ensure fairness.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add ablation studies and runtime/memory reports.
- Provide qualitative analysis (e.g., cross-lingual nearest neighbors or aligned topics) to illustrate the learned shared structure.
- Consider alternative proximal formulations that integrate the nonnegativity constraint directly in the nuclear-norm proximal step (or factorized nonnegative matrix models) and compare empirically.
- Discuss applicability to more than two languages at once (extend to block-structured M0) and whether the method scales.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Conceptually simple, well-motivated approach with a convex formulation and convergence guarantee.
- Consistent and often substantial empirical improvements, particularly in the low-parallel-data regime.
- Addresses an important and timely problem for the NIPS community.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited baselines/ablations; unclear how much each component contributes.
- Missing runtime/scalability evidence for large vocabularies/corpora.
- Evaluation confined to one dataset/domain.

Overall: A solid and promising contribution. With additional ablations and computational analysis, this would be a strong paper. I lean toward acceptance.
