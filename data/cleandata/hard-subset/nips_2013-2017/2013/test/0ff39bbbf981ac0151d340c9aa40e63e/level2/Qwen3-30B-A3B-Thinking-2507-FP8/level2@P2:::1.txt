

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification by first using matrix completion to fill missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix (leveraging unlabeled parallel documents), then applying latent semantic indexing (LSI) to obtain low-dimensional representations. The authors claim their method outperforms existing approaches, particularly with limited parallel data.

**Strengths:**  
The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks across three languages (French, German, Japanese) using Amazon reviews. The results consistently show the proposed method (TSL) outperforming baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) by 8.59–14.61% in accuracy. The ablation study on varying parallel data sizes (200–2000 documents) demonstrates TSL's robustness, especially with scarce resources. The convergence proof for the projected gradient descent algorithm is technically sound and well-justified. The paper also provides clear implementation details for the optimization procedure.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Critical technical error:** The claim that "the non-smooth ℓ1 norm regularizer [...] is equivalent to a smooth linear function ‖M‖₁ = ∑ᵢⱼ Mᵢⱼ" under nonnegativity constraints is incorrect. The ℓ₁ norm remains non-smooth (though convex) even with M ≥ 0. This mischaracterization undermines the mathematical foundation of the optimization approach. The paper should correct this and clarify the role of the ℓ₁ term (e.g., as a sparsity-inducing regularizer, not a smooth approximation).

2. **Insufficient novelty justification:** The core idea—using matrix completion to fill missing entries before LSI—is not sufficiently differentiated from prior work. The related work cites CL-LSI (which applies LSI directly to the concatenated matrix), but the authors fail to compare with methods that *also* use matrix completion for cross-lingual learning. For instance, [11] (cited in the paper) uses matrix completion for similar tasks, yet the paper does not discuss how this work differs from or improves upon it. The novelty is overstated; the method is a straightforward adaptation of matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning, not a novel technique.

3. **Ambiguous experimental setup:** The paper states that "all four methods exploit unlabeled parallel reviews," but does not clarify how the baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) use these documents. For example, CL-LSI is described as "learning cross-lingual representations with all data from both language domains," but it is unclear whether the unlabeled parallel data is used in the same way as TSL. Without this detail, the comparison is unfair and the claimed superiority of TSL is difficult to validate.

4. **Lack of statistical significance:** The results report average accuracy and standard deviations but omit statistical tests (e.g., t-tests) to confirm that performance differences are significant. The 8.59–14.61% gains over baselines need stronger evidence to be convincing.

**Constructive Suggestions:**  
- Correct the ℓ₁ norm error and clarify its role in the optimization. If the ℓ₁ term is intended for sparsity, explicitly state that it is not smoothed and discuss potential alternatives (e.g., using ℓ₂ for smoothness if desired).  
- Strengthen the novelty claim by comparing with matrix completion-based approaches (e.g., [11]) and explaining why their method is more suitable for cross-lingual tasks.  
- Detail how baselines utilize unlabeled parallel data to ensure a fair comparison.  
- Include statistical significance tests for the accuracy results.  
- Discuss computational scalability: The matrix completion step could be expensive for large vocabularies, yet the paper does not address runtime or memory requirements.

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper addresses an important problem with a well-motivated approach and strong empirical results. However, the critical technical error, insufficient novelty justification, and ambiguous experimental setup significantly weaken the contribution. With corrections to these issues, the work could be a solid addition to the literature. As written, the technical flaws prevent confident acceptance. The significance is promising but not demonstrably superior to existing methods due to the unresolved comparisons. The clarity of the method is good, but the technical errors reduce the paper's quality.  

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper requires major revisions to address the technical inaccuracies and clarify its novelty before it can be considered for publication.
