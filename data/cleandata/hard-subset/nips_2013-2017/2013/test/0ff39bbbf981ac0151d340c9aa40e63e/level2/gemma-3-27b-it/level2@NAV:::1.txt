## Review of “A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning”

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, addressing the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages. The core idea is to first complete a partially observed bilingual document-term matrix using a matrix completion technique, then apply Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to derive cross-lingual document representations. The authors motivate this approach by highlighting the benefits of capturing co-occurrence patterns across languages, especially with limited parallel data. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the proposed method, including the mathematical formulation and optimization algorithm (projected gradient descent). The authors appropriately connect their work to related research in machine translation, domain adaptation, and cross-lingual representation learning (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA), positioning their contribution as requiring less parallel data than many existing approaches. The experimental setup using the Amazon product reviews dataset is reasonable, and the comparison with several baselines provides a good evaluation framework.  The results demonstrably show the proposed method outperforms the baselines, particularly with small amounts of parallel data. The inclusion of an ablation study (impact of parallel data size) strengthens the findings.

However, the paper lacks some depth in discussing the computational complexity of the proposed approach, especially the projected gradient descent algorithm. While convergence is mentioned, a more detailed analysis or empirical assessment of runtime would be valuable. Additionally, the parameter selection process (gamma, rho, k) is briefly mentioned but could benefit from further elaboration. It's unclear how sensitive the performance is to these parameters. The presentation of results, while clear, is primarily tabular. Visualizations of the learned representations (e.g., via t-SNE) might offer more intuitive insight. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach combining matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation.
*   Strong empirical results, consistently outperforming baselines, particularly with limited parallel data.
*   Clear and well-written paper, effectively contextualizing the work within the existing literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of computational complexity.
*   Parameter selection details are sparse.
*   Lack of visualization to aid understanding of the learned representations.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual representation learning. While some areas could benefit from further elaboration, the novelty, strong empirical results, and clear presentation make it a worthwhile addition to the conference. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the computational complexity and parameter sensitivity concerns. Encouraging the authors to include a visualization of the learned representations would further enhance the paper.
