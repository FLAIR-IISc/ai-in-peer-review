## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning, addressing the disjoint feature space problem in cross-lingual text classification. The core idea is to first complete a partially observed document-term matrix (constructed from parallel bilingual documents) using matrix completion, and then apply Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to induce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors provide a projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence guarantees for solving the matrix completion problem. The method is evaluated on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews.

The paper is well-motivated, tackling a relevant problem in NLP. The proposed approach is conceptually sound – leveraging matrix completion to bridge the language gap before dimensionality reduction with LSI is a reasonable strategy. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of related work, positioning their method within the existing landscape of machine translation-based, bilingual resource-based, and representation learning approaches. The experimental results show consistent improvements over several baselines, particularly when the amount of parallel data is limited, which is a significant advantage. The inclusion of convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm strengthens the technical contribution.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. While the experimental setup is described, the details regarding hyperparameter tuning (beyond mentioning a parameter selection process for one task) are limited. A more thorough ablation study exploring the contribution of each step (matrix completion vs. LSI) would be beneficial. The presentation could be improved; the mathematical notation is dense, and some sections (e.g., the optimization algorithm description) are difficult to follow without careful reading.  The impact of the choice of the ℓ1 regularization parameter (µ) isn't discussed in detail.  Furthermore, the paper doesn't address potential scalability issues with very large datasets, beyond mentioning the need for a scalable optimization algorithm.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to cross-lingual representation learning.
*   Strong empirical results demonstrating performance gains over baselines.
*   Theoretical analysis of the optimization algorithm.
*   Addresses a practical problem with a clear benefit (performance with limited parallel data).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited detail in hyperparameter tuning and ablation studies.
*   Dense mathematical notation and potentially difficult-to-follow explanations.
*   Lack of discussion on scalability to very large datasets.

**Overall:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. While there are areas for improvement in terms of clarity and experimental detail, the novelty of the approach and the strong empirical results suggest it has merit. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, specifically requesting a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning, an ablation study, and improved clarity in the mathematical descriptions.
