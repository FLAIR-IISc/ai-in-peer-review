Summary:
The paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-lingual text classification. Step 1 formulates completion of a unified bilingual document-term matrix (rows are documents, columns are the concatenated vocabularies) as a convex optimization problem: minimize a sum of trace norm, ℓ1 norm, squared error on observed entries, and squared deviation from zero on missing entries, subject to nonnegativity. The optimization is solved by a projected gradient descent/proximal method that alternates a gradient step on the smooth part, a nuclear-norm shrinkage, and a projection onto the nonnegative orthant, with a convergence argument based on non-expansiveness. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation, on which a linear SVM is trained. Experiments on Amazon multilingual reviews (EN, FR, DE, JA; 18 transfer tasks across 3 categories) show consistent improvements over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, particularly when only a small number of parallel documents are available.

Relation to prior work:
The method is conceptually related to CL-LSI (Littman et al.), CL-KCCA (Vinokourov et al., NIPS 2002), CL-OPCA (Platt et al.), and multi-view/co-training approaches (Amini et al., NIPS). The novelty is to first impute cross-lingual feature co-occurrences via convex matrix completion before applying LSI, leveraging limited parallel data without translating the whole corpus.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple pipeline with a convex formulation that leverages both within- and cross-language co-occurrence structure via a unified matrix.
- Empirical gains are consistent and meaningful across 18 tasks, with careful protocol (parameters chosen on one task and fixed for all; 10 random splits).
- Robustness to small parallel corpora is well demonstrated by varying the number of parallel pairs.
- The optimization procedure is straightforward to implement (proximal gradient with singular value thresholding and nonnegativity projection), and the objective decomposes nicely with interpretable terms (low-rank for semantics, ℓ1 for sparsity, ridge-like penalty on missing entries).

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Theoretical gap: The problem includes two nonsmooth terms (trace norm and indicator of nonnegativity). The algorithm applies shrinkage (prox of the trace norm) followed by a separate projection (prox of the indicator). In general, prox_{f+g} ≠ proj_C ∘ prox_f unless special conditions hold. The convergence argument based on non-expansiveness of the composition shows convergence to a fixed point of the operator but does not establish that the fixed point minimizes the original convex objective with both nonsmooth terms. A rigorous proof (e.g., via Dykstra’s algorithm, ADMM, or forward–backward–forward splitting for composite proximals) is needed to justify optimality claims, or the problem should be reformulated to match the applied algorithm.
- Low-rank claim: The paper asserts “a fully observed document-term matrix is naturally low-rank and sparse.” Sparsity is true, but low-rank is not generally satisfied by document-term matrices; instead, low-rank is an inductive bias (as in LSI). This should be motivated as an approximation rather than a property.
- Ablation/method analysis is limited. It would be valuable to separate the contribution of the completion step from LSI: (i) M* without LSI (direct SVM), (ii) LSI on the original sparse matrix (CL-LSI) vs. on M*, (iii) remove ℓ1, remove nonnegativity, or remove the missing-entry regularization (ρ) to understand which terms matter. The current choice µ=1e-6 seems arbitrary without justification.
- Baselines: Missing strong and widely used baselines such as translate-train/translate-test using MT or CL-SCL (Prettenhofer & Stein, cited). Including at least one translation-based baseline would contextualize the practical utility. Also, reporting tuned SVM C (rather than libsvm defaults) would strengthen fairness for TBOW.
- Scalability: No runtime or memory analysis, despite repeated SVDs in both the shrinkage step and final LSI. Even approximate SVD can be costly; reporting time vs. problem size and iterations would help assess practicality.
- Experimental details: It is not entirely clear how non-parallel documents are utilized by baseline methods that rely on bilingual alignment (e.g., CL-LSI/OPCA) when the number of parallel pairs shrinks. Clarifying exactly what data each baseline uses in the np experiments is important for fairness.

Quality:
- The empirical section is solid in breadth and shows strong results; however, the optimization theory is incomplete relative to the stated objective, and ablations are missing. This weakens the causal link between the proposed modeling choices and the performance gains.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized. The optimization section is concise, but it should be explicit that I(·) denotes the identity operator, and clarify the link between the algorithm and solving (2). Some minor typos/citation formatting issues exist.

Originality:
- Using nuclear-norm-based completion on a unified bilingual document-term matrix as a precursor to LSI is a neat idea and, to my knowledge, new in this setting (distinct from CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA). The components are standard, but their combination for cross-lingual representation with limited parallel data is interesting.

Significance:
- The empirical gains are promising and likely of interest to practitioners dealing with scarce parallel data. If the theoretical concerns are addressed and the method scales, this could be a useful addition to cross-lingual representation learning.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a correct convergence/optimality guarantee for the constrained problem (e.g., via proximal methods for sums of nonsmooth functions, Dykstra, or ADMM), or adjust the algorithm to match a provable framework and update Theorem 1 accordingly.
- Add ablations to isolate the contribution of matrix completion vs. LSI and the roles of µ, ρ, γ, and the nonnegativity constraint.
- Include at least one translation-based baseline and CL-SCL; tune SVM hyperparameters for all methods.
- Report computational complexity, number of iterations, wall-clock time, and memory footprint; consider randomized SVD and discuss scalability to larger corpora/vocabularies.
- Temper the low-rank claim and motivate it as a modeling assumption; discuss when completion may hurt (e.g., noisy or domain-mismatched parallel data).
- Release code and settings for reproducibility; clarify preprocessing (tokenization, stopwording, normalization).

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, intuitive method with strong empirical results across many tasks, especially in low-parallel regimes.
- Novel use of matrix completion in cross-lingual representation learning.
- Clear exposition and broad relevance.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incomplete/possibly incorrect convergence argument for the stated optimization problem.
- Missing strong baselines and ablations; limited analysis of where the gains come from.
- No scalability analysis.
