- Proposes TSL: a two-step method for cross-language text classification using matrix completion on a unified document-term matrix followed by LSI for shared representations.  
- Addresses the core challenge of disjoint vocabularies across languages by treating missing cross-language entries explicitly.  
- Constructs a unified document-term matrix where monolingual documents have missing entries for the other language’s vocabulary.  
- Frames recovery of missing entries as a convex optimization balancing low rank (trace norm), sparsity (L1 norm), and reconstruction error.  
- Uses a projected gradient descent algorithm with per-iteration SVD shrinkage and provides a formal convergence proof.  
- Applies LSI to the completed dense matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual embedding.  
- Key novelty: explicitly inferring missing entries via matrix completion rather than implicitly treating them as zeros like prior CL-LSI/CL-OPCA/CL-KCCA methods.  
- Empirically outperforms several strong baselines across 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks.  
- Particularly effective in low-resource settings with few parallel documents, demonstrating data efficiency.  
- Paper is technically sound, clearly written, and well-organized with detailed experimental setup and clear results.  
- Reproducibility concern: unclear how the rank-1 approximation of M0 (with missing values) is computed for initialization in Algorithm 1.  
- Scalability concern: computational cost not discussed — per-iteration SVD may be expensive versus single-SVD baselines like CL-LSI; training-time comparisons are missing.  
- Experimental suggestions: include parameter sensitivity analysis (γ, ρ, k) instead of single-task tuning and add an MT-based baseline (translate-then-classify) for comparison.  
- Acceptance arguments: novel principled approach, solid optimization and algorithmic treatment, strong and comprehensive empirical gains, and practical advantage in low-parallel-data regimes.  
- Revision arguments: need discussion of computational complexity, clearer implementation details (initialization), and expanded experimental analyses.
