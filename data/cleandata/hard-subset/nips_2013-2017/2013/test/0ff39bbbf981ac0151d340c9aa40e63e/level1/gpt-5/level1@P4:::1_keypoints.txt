- Proposes a two-step cross-lingual method: convex low-rank + sparse nonnegative matrix completion on a bilingual doc-term matrix, then LSI + linear SVM for classification.  
- Novelty: imputing missing cross-language features via nuclear-norm + ℓ1 + nonnegativity before LSI is a meaningful, moderately novel combination of known components.  
- Relates to prior work (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, SCL) but differs by explicitly imputing missing language features rather than operating directly on concatenated/paired data.  
- Formulation: convex relaxation with trace norm, ℓ1 sparsity, squared losses (observed and weighted missing), and nonnegativity is reasonable and interpretable.  
- Algorithm: projected gradient / SVT / nonnegativity projection sequence is natural and operators are shown non-expansive, with step size tied to Lipschitz constant.  
- Theoretical gap: convergence proof is incomplete—composition of SVT and projection is not guaranteed to be the proximal operator of the sum, so optimality for the constrained problem is not established.  
- Practical concerns: per-iteration full SVDs and no complexity/runtime or stopping-criterion details raise scalability questions for large NLP matrices.  
- Experimental setup: unsupervised/transductive use of all documents is consistent across baselines, but hyperparameters chosen on one task and transferred may bias results.  
- Empirical results: shows consistent gains across 18 sentiment tasks and robustness with few parallel documents, indicating practical promise.  
- Evaluation limitations: single dataset/family of tasks, limited baselines (no bilingual topic models or newer embeddings), and insufficient ablations to isolate component contributions.  
- Clarity: generally well-written with clear construction and optimization details; minor issues include reference typos and missing pseudocode stopping/SVD details.  
- Strengths: clear motivation, convex formulation leveraging cross-language co-occurrence, simple intuitive algorithm, and strong empirical gains in low-parallel-data regimes.  
- Weaknesses: incomplete convergence justification, lack of scalability/implementation details, limited evaluation scope, missing ablations (ℓ1/nonnegativity effects, zero-filled baseline), and single-task hyperparameter tuning.  
- Suggested improvements: provide rigorous optimization treatment (e.g., ADMM or correct proximal splitting) or explicitly frame algorithm as heuristic; add truncated/randomized SVD, runtime, and stopping criteria; run ablations and broaden baselines/datasets.  
- Argument for acceptance: relevant problem, simple general approach, consistent empirical improvements, and reasonable conceptual novelty.  
- Argument against acceptance: theoretical shortcomings on convergence, scalability/implementation omissions, and limited experimental breadth reduce confidence in impact.  
- Overall: promising and empirically effective idea but hindered by a significant theoretical gap and insufficient scalability/experimental analysis.
