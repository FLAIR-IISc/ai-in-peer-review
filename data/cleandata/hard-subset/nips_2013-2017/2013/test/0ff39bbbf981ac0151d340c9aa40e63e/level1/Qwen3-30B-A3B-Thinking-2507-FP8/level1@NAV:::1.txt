

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning that addresses the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages. The authors first construct a bilingual document-term matrix with missing entries for non-parallel documents, then solve a matrix completion problem to fill these entries using projected gradient descent with convergence guarantees. Finally, they apply latent semantic indexing to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The method is evaluated on Amazon product reviews for cross-language sentiment classification, demonstrating superior performance compared to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, particularly with limited parallel data.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution by applying matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning, a novel approach distinct from previous methods that directly operate on partially observed matrices. The theoretical analysis provides convergence guarantees for their optimization algorithm, and the experimental results are well-designed with thorough comparisons across 18 language pairs and categories. The method's effectiveness with minimal parallel data is particularly significant for practical applications.

Strengths include the clear mathematical formulation, strong empirical validation showing 8.59-14.61% accuracy improvements over baselines, and practical relevance to low-resource language scenarios. The paper is well-organized with appropriate citations to related work, including key NIPS contributions like CL-LSI [10] and CL-KCCA [20].

Weaknesses include limited discussion of the method's limitations (e.g., performance with languages having vastly different grammatical structures) and no comparison with more recent neural approaches to cross-lingual representation learning. While the matrix completion approach is novel in this context, it's worth noting that similar techniques have been applied in other domains like collaborative filtering.

Arguments for acceptance: Novel application of matrix completion to cross-lingual learning with strong empirical results, significant performance gains especially with limited parallel data, and solid theoretical grounding.

Arguments against acceptance: The core matrix completion technique isn't entirely novel, and the paper could better address recent neural methods in the field.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances cross-lingual representation learning with a practical and theoretically grounded approach. The method's ability to achieve strong results with minimal parallel data is particularly valuable for real-world applications. I recommend acceptance.
