- Proposes TSL, a novel two-step method for cross-language text classification using a small set of unlabeled parallel documents.  
- Step 1: formulates filling cross-lingual missing entries in a unified document-term matrix as a convex matrix completion problem with trace-norm (low-rank), L1 (sparsity), and non-negativity regularizers.  
- Solves matrix completion with a projected gradient descent algorithm and provides a formal convergence proof.  
- Step 2: applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain a shared low-dimensional semantic space for training a monolingual classifier.  
- Key novelty: explicitly completing missing cross-lingual term relationships before dimensionality reduction, distinguishing it from CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA.  
- Experiments: extensive evaluation on 18 cross-lingual sentiment tasks (Amazon reviews) comparing against four strong baselines.  
- Empirical results: TSL consistently and significantly outperforms baselines and remains robust with very few parallel documents (as few as 200).  
- Technical soundness: well-justified formulation, appropriate regularizers, and rigorous algorithmic analysis.  
- Clarity and reproducibility: paper is well-written with sufficient methodological and experimental detail.  
- Originality: novel combination of matrix completion and LSI applied to cross-language representation learning.  
- Significance: practical advantage for low-resource settings and potential applicability to other multi-view/transfer problems.  
- Weakness: parameter tuning was done on a single task and fixed across all experiments, which may not be optimal for all language pairs.  
- Weakness: scalability and computational cost (iterative SVDs in shrinkage) are not analyzed and may limit large-scale applicability.  
- Weakness: evaluation is limited to one dataset and one task (Amazon sentiment), reducing evidence of generalizability.
