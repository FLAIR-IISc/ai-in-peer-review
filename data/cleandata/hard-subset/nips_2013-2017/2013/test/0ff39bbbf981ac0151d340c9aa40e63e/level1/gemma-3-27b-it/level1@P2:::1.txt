This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification, addressing the disjoint feature space problem. The approach first completes a document-term matrix using matrix completion, then applies LSI for dimensionality reduction and representation learning. The method is evaluated on Amazon product reviews for sentiment classification, demonstrating improvements over several baselines, particularly when parallel data is limited.

**Quality:** The paper is generally technically sound. The matrix completion step and subsequent LSI are standard techniques, and their application to this cross-lingual setting is reasonable. The convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm, while referencing existing work, adds to the rigor. However, the justification for choosing the specific loss function and regularization parameters (µ, ρ, γ) in the matrix completion step feels somewhat arbitrary and could benefit from more thorough ablation or theoretical grounding.  While the experiments show performance gains, a more detailed analysis of *why* the method works – e.g., examining the learned matrix completion, investigating the impact of different vocabulary sizes – would strengthen the claim of understanding. The experimental setup, with 100 labeled target language examples, is quite limited and may not fully reflect real-world scenarios.  It's also unclear whether the parameter settings were determined through cross-validation or a separate validation set, which is important for reproducibility. The formulation of the optimization problem and the algorithm are clearly presented.

**Clarity:** The paper is reasonably well-written and organized. The two-step process is easy to follow. However, the notation could be slightly more consistent (e.g., defining variable usages earlier). Algorithm 1 is helpful. The related work section is comprehensive, but quite dense and could be more focused on the most relevant contributions. The motivation for choosing certain parameters could be explained better. The explanation of the convergence analysis could be made more accessible to a broader audience.

**Originality:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI isn't entirely novel, as both techniques have been used in cross-lingual settings individually (as acknowledged in the related work). However, applying matrix completion *before* LSI in this specific manner to bridge the language gap appears to be a novel contribution. The focus on scenarios with limited parallel data is also a valuable point, as this is a common challenge in real-world applications. The paper does a good job of relating its approach to existing work, highlighting its differences and potential advantages. It correctly cites relevant literature, including prior NIPS publications.

**Significance:**  The problem of cross-lingual text classification is important, particularly with the increasing availability of data in multiple languages. The demonstrated performance improvements, especially with limited parallel data, suggest the method could be valuable. The potential to reduce the reliance on large parallel corpora is a significant advantage. While the gains are not enormous, the consistent improvement over strong baselines and the focus on a practical scenario (limited parallel data) makes this work relevant.  The method is likely to be of interest to researchers working on cross-lingual NLP, transfer learning, and representation learning. The uniqueness of this work lies in the combination of techniques and its performance in a low-resource scenario.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of matrix completion as a preprocessing step for cross-lingual representation learning.
*   Demonstrated improvements over strong baselines on a standard benchmark dataset.
*   Focus on a practical scenario (limited parallel data).
*   Technically sound with a convergence guarantee for the optimization algorithm.
*   Clear writing and organization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental analysis – more ablation studies and sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
*   Small size of the labeled target language dataset (100 examples).
*   Justification for parameter selection is weak.
*   While novel, the combination of existing techniques doesn't represent a massive paradigm shift.



**Overall Assessment:** I believe this paper is a solid contribution and warrants acceptance. While there are areas for improvement, the novelty, technical soundness, and promising experimental results justify its inclusion in NIPS. I would rate it as a strong accept.  The authors should be encouraged to address the concerns regarding experimental analysis and parameter justification in future work.
