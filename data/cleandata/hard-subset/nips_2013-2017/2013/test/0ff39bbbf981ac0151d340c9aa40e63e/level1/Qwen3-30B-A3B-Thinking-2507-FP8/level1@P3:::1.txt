

**Peer Review**

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning that addresses the challenge of disjoint feature spaces in different languages. The authors first construct a bilingual document-term matrix with missing entries for non-parallel documents, then solve a matrix completion problem to fill these missing values using a convex optimization approach with trace norm and ℓ1 regularization. Finally, they apply latent semantic indexing to the completed matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The method is evaluated on Amazon product reviews across English and three other languages for sentiment classification tasks.

The work builds on established approaches like CL-LSI [10] and CL-OPCA [15], which directly operate on the concatenated document-term matrix without addressing the missing data problem. The authors correctly identify that treating missing entries as zeros discards valuable information, making their matrix completion step a meaningful contribution. While related to prior work on matrix completion [4, 11], the application to cross-lingual representation learning is novel.

**Strengths**:
1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The convex optimization formulation with trace norm regularization is well-justified, and the convergence analysis is rigorous and properly grounded in established matrix completion literature. The projected gradient descent algorithm (Algorithm 1) is well-motivated and the convergence proof is complete.

2. **Practical Significance**: The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, especially with limited parallel data (as low as 200 documents). This addresses a critical practical constraint in cross-lingual learning where parallel data is often scarce.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experiments cover 18 cross-lingual tasks across three product categories and three language pairs, with thorough analysis of parameter sensitivity and the impact of parallel data size. The results consistently show TSL outperforming baselines by 8.59-14.61% on average.

**Weaknesses**:
1. **Outdated Baselines**: The comparison is limited to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, and TBOW. The paper misses a critical opportunity to compare with contemporary neural approaches like multilingual BERT [Devlin et al., 2018] or MUSE [Conneau et al., 2018], which have become standard for cross-lingual tasks. This significantly weakens the significance claim.

2. **Methodological Limitations**: The low-rank assumption for the document-term matrix isn't thoroughly justified. For languages with very different structures (e.g., English vs. Japanese), this assumption may not hold, but the paper doesn't analyze such failure cases or discuss when the method might underperform.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity**: While the paper reports parameter selection on one task, it doesn't analyze how sensitive the method is to these parameters across different language pairs or categories. The claim that "the same parameters work for all tasks" needs stronger justification.

4. **Missing Baseline**: The paper doesn't compare with a simple baseline of using bilingual dictionaries to map words between languages (e.g., [23]), which is a common approach in cross-lingual learning and would provide a more complete benchmark.

**Overall Assessment**:
The paper presents a technically sound and well-executed approach to cross-lingual representation learning with clear empirical advantages. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the results demonstrate practical value, particularly in low-resource settings. However, the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art neural methods and insufficient discussion of method limitations significantly reduce the paper's significance as a NIPS contribution.

**Recommendation**: Accept with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to cross-lingual learning, but must be strengthened by:
1. Adding comparisons with recent neural methods (e.g., multilingual BERT, MUSE)
2. Including a bilingual dictionary baseline for fair comparison
3. Discussing the limitations of the low-rank assumption with specific failure cases
4. Providing more thorough parameter sensitivity analysis

**Pro/Con Summary**:
*Pros*: Novel two-step approach, strong theoretical foundation, compelling results with limited parallel data, comprehensive evaluation design.
*Cons*: Outdated baselines, insufficient discussion of limitations, missing comparison with dictionary-based approaches, limited analysis of parameter sensitivity.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, making it easy to follow the methodology and results. With the suggested revisions, this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS, addressing a practical challenge in cross-lingual learning with both theoretical and empirical rigor. The work advances the state of the art in cross-lingual representation learning, particularly for low-resource scenarios where parallel data is limited.
