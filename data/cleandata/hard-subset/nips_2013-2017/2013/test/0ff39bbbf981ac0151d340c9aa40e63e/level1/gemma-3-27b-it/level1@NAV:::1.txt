## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning, addressing the challenge of disjoint feature spaces in multilingual text classification. The core idea is to first complete a partially observed document-term matrix built from parallel bilingual documents via a matrix completion technique, then apply Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to induce cross-lingual document representations. The authors provide a projected gradient descent algorithm for solving the matrix completion problem with convergence guarantees, and demonstrate superior performance on cross-lingual sentiment classification using Amazon product reviews compared to several baselines, particularly when parallel data is limited.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in cross-lingual learning. The authors accurately position their method within the existing landscape, referencing techniques like machine translation [18, 6, 23], bilingual lexicons [16], and prior cross-lingual representation learning approaches such as CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], and CL-OPCA [15].  The explicit acknowledgement of related work, and careful citation, is a strength.  The matrix completion approach leverages ideas from collaborative filtering and signal processing, linking the problem to a well-studied area. 

**Strengths:** The method appears technically sound, with a clear formulation of the matrix completion problem and a proposed algorithm with convergence analysis. The experimental results consistently show improvements over baselines, suggesting a meaningful contribution. The finding that the method performs well with limited parallel data is particularly valuable. The detailed description of the algorithm and experimental setup enhances reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:** The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed approach, especially the projected gradient descent algorithm.  Given the size of document-term matrices, scalability could be a concern. While the convergence analysis is provided, practical considerations for parameter tuning (γ, ρ, τ) are not deeply explored.  The experimental evaluation focuses solely on sentiment classification and a single dataset. Investigating performance on other tasks (e.g., document categorization) and datasets would strengthen the generalizability claims. The impact of the initial matrix approximation in the algorithm is not discussed. Finally, while the ablation study on the size of parallel data is good, a brief discussion on the quality of parallel data could be added. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The proposed method demonstrates improved performance on a challenging cross-lingual learning task.
*   The technical contribution – the combination of matrix completion and LSI with a convergence-guaranteed algorithm – is novel and well-motivated.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and thoroughly references related work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Scalability concerns regarding the computational complexity of the algorithm need to be addressed.
*   The evaluation is limited to a single task and dataset.



**Overall:** I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Addressing the scalability concerns and expanding the experimental evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
