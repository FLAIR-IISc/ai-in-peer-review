Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles cross-language text classification by learning shared representations from a mix of parallel and non-parallel documents. The key idea is a two-step pipeline: (1) form a unified document-term matrix over the concatenated vocabularies, treat entries corresponding to the “other” language as missing for non-parallel documents, and perform convex matrix completion with a nuclear norm (for low rank), an ℓ1 term (for sparsity), squared losses on observed entries, and a small quadratic penalty on the missing entries; (2) apply LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation on which a monolingual classifier is trained.

The method is compared to CL-LSI [Littman et al.], CL-KCCA [Vinokourov et al.], and CL-OPCA [Platt et al.], as well as a target-only bag-of-words baseline. On Amazon Reviews (EN–FR/DE/JA, three domains), the proposed approach outperforms baselines, especially with few parallel documents.

Conceptually, the paper occupies a space between multi-view/translation-based approaches (e.g., EM self-training, bi-weighting, co-training, co-classification) and cross-lingual representation methods (CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA; multilingual topic models). The novelty is to impute the bilingual document-term matrix via convex matrix completion before dimensionality reduction.

Strengths
- Simple, generally applicable framework that uses only a small set of parallel documents and otherwise unlabeled monolingual corpora.
- Convex formulation with a clear proximal-style algorithm; basic convergence argument provided; nonnegativity is handled neatly.
- Strong and consistent empirical gains over well-known cross-lingual representation baselines across 18 tasks; robustness when parallel data is scarce is a notable practical advantage.
- Careful experimental protocol (multiple random splits; cross-task parameter reuse to limit overfitting of hyperparameters).

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Optimization correctness: The algorithm applies gradient descent on g(M), then singular value shrinkage (prox for nuclear norm), then a projection onto the nonnegative orthant (prox for indicator). The prox of a sum is generally not equal to the composition of individual prox operators. The proof leverages non-expansiveness to show convergence of the fixed-point iterations, but does not establish that the fixed point is an optimizer of the stated objective with both nuclear norm and nonnegativity. Referencing [Ma et al.] is not directly sufficient because their convergence analysis concerns a different composite without an additional projection. A more rigorous treatment (e.g., forward–backward splitting with the prox of the sum via Dykstra/ADMM, or showing that the composite is the proximal map of γ||·||* + I{·≥0}) is needed to justify the optimality claim.
- Modeling assumptions: The paper repeatedly asserts that a fully observed document-term matrix is “naturally low-rank,” which is not generally true for TF-IDF; it is better to argue “approximately low-rank” in the sense of rapid singular value decay, and to support this empirically. The two-step pipeline imposes low-rank twice (via nuclear norm, then LSI), which may be redundant; an ablation that removes the LSI step (or removes the completion step) would clarify where the gains come from.
- Scalability: Each iteration requires an SVD for shrinkage, which is expensive for large t×d. The paper does not discuss using partial SVD, randomized SVD, or stopping criteria/empirical runtimes, nor does it compare wall-clock time vs. CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA. A complexity analysis and practical runtime measurements would strengthen the claim of scalability.
- Baselines and related work coverage (for the 2013–2017 timeframe): Missing comparisons to cross-lingual word embedding transfer (e.g., Klementiev et al., 2012; Mikolov et al., 2013 bilingual mapping; Zou et al., 2013), which quickly became standard for cross-lingual representation. While document-level KCCA is included, word-level bilingual embeddings plus averaging or retrofitting were competitive and sometimes superior on sentiment. Also, multi-task/shared-representation neural methods were emerging; even if out of scope computationally, a discussion is warranted.
- Methodological details: CL-KCCA settings (kernel choice, regularization) are not specified, which can materially impact results. Sensitivity analyses for γ, ρ, τ, and k are limited (selected on EFB then reused), and µ is fixed without justification. It would be helpful to report whether results are robust to these choices.
- Evaluation protocol: Representation learning uses all documents (including test) in an unsupervised/transductive fashion; this is common, but should be stated explicitly and aligned with how baselines are trained. Statistical significance testing would help interpret the magnitude of gains.

Quality
The empirical section is solid and the method appears to work well, particularly with limited parallel data. However, the optimization analysis is not fully convincing as stated; the algorithm likely converges to a useful solution, but the proof that the limit minimizes the stated objective is incomplete.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Notation is mostly clear, with small issues: (i) the transition from constraints in (1) to the penalized form in (2) deserves a brief justification; (ii) clarify that nonnegativity is enforced on all entries (the text around (1) could be read as only on missing entries). Provide more details on SVD computation and parameter selection. Figures/tables are referenced but not shown here; assuming they are legible, the narrative is informative.

Originality
Combining convex matrix completion with nonnegativity and a small quadratic bias on unobserved entries, followed by LSI for cross-lingual representation, is a novel combination in this context. Nonetheless, both ingredients are established; the main contribution is the insight that completing the bilingual doc-term matrix before LSI improves robustness with few parallel pairs.

Significance
The improvements over strong, classical baselines across many language/domain pairs, and robustness to small numbers of parallel documents, make this a potentially useful technique. Broader impact would be clearer with comparisons to bilingual embedding-based methods and a scalability demonstration.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a correct optimization framework for nuclear norm plus nonnegativity (e.g., using ADMM or Dykstra’s algorithm for the prox of a sum), or tighten the convergence proof to show optimality for the current operator splitting.
- Add ablations: (a) no LSI after completion; (b) LSI without completion (already approximated by CL-LSI but using identical preprocessing); (c) remove ℓ1 term; (d) remove nonnegativity.
- Include runtime/complexity and truncated/randomized SVD details.
- Compare to cross-lingual word embedding baselines and discuss their relation.
- Clarify CL-KCCA configuration and perform sensitivity analysis for key hyperparameters.

Arguments for acceptance
- Consistent, sizable gains over established cross-lingual representation baselines on 18 tasks.
- Practical value when parallel data is scarce.
- Conceptually simple and broadly applicable.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete/possibly incorrect convergence analysis for the proposed proximal splitting with projection.
- Missing comparisons to contemporary cross-lingual embedding methods limit the assessment of significance.
- Scalability and computational costs not evaluated.

Overall: Promising and practically useful idea with strong empirical results; acceptance would be strengthened by fixing the theoretical gap and broadening baselines.
