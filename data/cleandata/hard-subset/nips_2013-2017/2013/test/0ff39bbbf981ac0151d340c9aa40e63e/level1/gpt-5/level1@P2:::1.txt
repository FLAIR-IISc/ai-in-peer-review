Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a two-step approach to cross-lingual representation learning for text classification. Step 1 formulates matrix completion on a concatenated bilingual document–term matrix: each document is represented in the union vocabulary of two languages; non-parallel documents have missing entries for words from the other language, and parallel document pairs are represented as fully observed “dual-language” rows. The completion objective is convex: a trace norm (for low rank) plus an ℓ1 norm (for sparsity) with nonnegativity constraints, and squared loss both on observed entries (fitting M0) and on missing entries (regularizing them toward 0 with weight ρ). The authors solve it by a projected gradient descent scheme with singular value shrinkage and nonnegativity projection, and prove convergence via non-expansiveness. Step 2 runs LSI (SVD) on the completed matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual document representations and trains a linear SVM.

Relative to prior approaches that induce cross-lingual representations from parallel/comparable data—CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], CL-OPCA [15], multilingual topic models [19]—the novelty is to first “impute” missing cross-lingual features via a convex matrix completion, and only then learn the low-dimensional space. Compared to multi-view/co-training and tri-factorization approaches [1,2,13], this is a different mechanism for coupling the views. The optimization builds on matrix completion literature (e.g., [4,11]).

Experiments use Amazon multilingual sentiment (EN, FR, DE, JA; Books/DVD/Music). The method outperforms TBOW and three cross-lingual baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) across 18 transfer settings, with stronger gains when the number of parallel documents is small.

Strengths:
- Clear, convex formulation with a simple, scalable iterative solver and a convergence guarantee based on standard non-expansive operator arguments.
- Conceptually appealing use of completion to exploit both within- and cross-language co-occurrences, followed by a robust LSI step.
- Thorough evaluation on 18 tasks with systematic study of the effect of parallel data size; consistent improvements over strong families of baselines.
- Practical setup: needs only a modest number of unlabeled parallel pairs and few labeled target examples.

Weaknesses:
- Missing key baselines: structural correspondence learning (CL-SCL) [16] is a strong, widely used method on this dataset, as are translate-train/translate-test pipelines (even with off-the-shelf MT).
- Limited ablation/diagnostics: no comparison to (i) doing LSI directly with missing data (weighted low-rank approximation), (ii) using only trace norm or only ℓ1, or (iii) skipping Step 1 and applying CL-LSI to the same partially observed matrix.
- Scalability details are sparse: no runtime, memory, or rank/truncation strategy per iteration; SVD in each iteration can be costly for large t×d matrices.
- Some modeling choices are under-justified: squared loss on TF-IDF, the role/sensitivity of ρ (penalizing missing entries toward zero), and the equivalence of ℓ1 to a linear term under nonnegativity.
- Clarity issues: the construction of “fully observed row” for a parallel pair is ambiguous (are the two translations concatenated into one row or handled as two rows?); stopping criteria and step-size selection are not detailed; no statistical tests.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: Technically sound convex program and correct algorithmic components; the convergence proof leverages standard results. Empirical evaluation is broad within one benchmark and shows consistent gains, especially at low parallel data. However, absence of stronger baselines (CL-SCL, translation-based) and ablations limits the strength of the empirical evidence, and scalability claims are not substantiated experimentally.
- Clarity: Generally well organized with explicit formulations and algorithm description. A few ambiguities (parallel row construction), missing implementation details (partial vs full SVD, stopping rules), and small inconsistencies (nonnegativity only on Ω̂ in (1) vs M ≥ 0 later).
- Originality: The two-step combination—trace-norm-based completion of a bilingual document–term matrix followed by LSI—is, to my knowledge, novel in cross-lingual text classification. Related work typically applies LSI/CCA/OPCA directly; tri-factorization and co-regularization differ in mechanism. The optimization itself is not novel but appropriate.
- Significance: If confirmed against stronger baselines and at larger scales, the approach could be a useful addition for scenarios with limited parallel data. As presented, significance is moderate: gains over CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA are encouraging, but the lack of CL-SCL and MT comparisons makes it hard to judge impact.

Questions for the authors:
- How exactly are parallel pairs represented—concatenated into a single dual-language row or two rows? If concatenated, how do you avoid doubling the number of tokens relative to non-parallel rows?
- What SVD routine and rank truncation did you use per iteration (full vs partial), and what are the time/memory footprints and convergence criteria?
- How sensitive is performance to ρ, γ, and μ? What happens if μ=0 or if the ℓ1 term is dropped?
- Why not directly solve a weighted low-rank approximation (LSI with missing entries) and compare to it?
- Could you report comparisons to CL-SCL [16] and to translate-train/test baselines?

Suggestions:
- Add CL-SCL and translation-based baselines; include ablations (no completion; trace norm only; ℓ1 only; no nonnegativity); and report statistical tests.
- Provide runtime/scaling plots and implementation details (truncated SVD, stopping rules).
- Clarify the data construction for parallel rows and the rationale for squared loss on TF-IDF.
- Discuss when completion helps relative to directly applying CL-LSI.

Reasons to accept:
- Conceptually simple, convex, and well-motivated two-step method with convergence guarantees.
- Strong empirical gains over several standard cross-lingual representation learners, especially with few parallel pairs.
- Addresses an important problem and uses only unlabeled parallel data plus limited target labels.

Reasons to reject:
- Missing strongest baselines (CL-SCL, translation) leaves the empirical case incomplete.
- No ablation or complexity analysis; unclear scalability for large vocabularies/corpora.
- Some clarity issues in model/data construction and limited justification of hyperparameters.

Overall: A promising and likely useful idea with solid optimization and encouraging results, but the empirical section needs stronger baselines and diagnostics to fully establish significance. I lean toward borderline accept if the missing comparisons and clarifications can be addressed; otherwise borderline reject.
