- Paper proposes a two-step cross-language pipeline: convex matrix completion on a unified bilingual doc-term matrix (treating missing language entries as missing) followed by LSI/SVD for cross-lingual representations.
- Evaluated against CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA and a target-only baseline on Amazon reviews (EN–FR/DE/JA) and outperforms baselines, especially with few parallel documents.
- Strength: simple, broadly applicable framework using few parallel documents and mostly unlabeled monolingual corpora.
- Strength: convex formulation with a proximal-style algorithm, basic convergence argument, and neat nonnegativity handling.
- Strength: strong and consistent empirical gains across 18 tasks and robust performance when parallel data is scarce.
- Strength: careful experimental protocol with multiple random splits and cross-task parameter reuse to limit hyperparameter overfitting.
- Weakness: optimization correctness is questionable — composition of prox operators and projection doesn’t guarantee minimization of the stated objective; convergence proof is incomplete.
- Weakness: modeling claim that doc-term matrices are “naturally low-rank” is too strong; should argue “approximately low-rank” and provide empirical support.
- Weakness: redundancy risk from imposing low-rank twice (nuclear norm then LSI); lacks ablation to attribute gains to each step.
- Weakness: scalability concerns — SVD per iteration is expensive; no discussion of partial/randomized SVD, runtimes, or complexity comparison to baselines.
- Weakness: related work/baselines omit important contemporaneous cross-lingual word embedding methods and emerging neural shared-representation approaches.
- Weakness: methodological details missing for CL-KCCA settings and limited sensitivity analysis for key hyperparameters (γ, ρ, τ, k, µ).
- Weakness: evaluation is transductive (uses all documents including test) but this isn’t explicitly stated; lacks statistical significance testing.
- Quality: empirical results are solid and promising, but the theoretical optimization analysis is not fully convincing.
- Clarity: generally well written and organized, though some notation and formulation transitions need brief clarifications and more SVD/parameter details.
- Originality: novel combination of convex matrix completion with nonnegativity and a quadratic bias followed by LSI; components are established but combination is new in this context.
- Significance: practically useful given improvements and robustness with few parallel pairs, but impact limited by missing embedding baselines and scalability evaluation.
- Suggested improvements: provide a correct optimization framework (e.g., ADMM/Dykstra), add ablations (no-LSI, no-completion, remove ℓ1, remove nonnegativity), report runtimes/use truncated SVD, compare to cross-lingual word embeddings, and clarify CL-KCCA and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Arguments for acceptance: consistent sizable gains over classical baselines, practical value with scarce parallel data, and conceptual simplicity.
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete/possibly incorrect convergence analysis, missing comparisons to modern cross-lingual embedding methods, and unaddressed scalability/computational cost concerns.
- Overall: promising, practically useful approach with strong empirical results that would be strengthened by fixing the theoretical gap and broadening baselines and scalability analysis.
