This paper proposes a novel two-step learning method (TSL) for cross-language text classification. The core challenge in this task is bridging the disjoint feature spaces of different languages. The authors address this by first constructing a unified document-term matrix for both source and target languages. For documents existing in only one language, the features from the other language are treated as missing values. The first step of their method is to "fill in" these missing entries by formulating and solving a matrix completion problem, aiming to recover a low-rank matrix. The second step applies Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to this completed matrix to induce a low-dimensional, shared semantic representation. This approach is conceptually related to prior work like CL-LSI and CL-OPCA, which also learn a joint representation from parallel corpora. However, the key novelty here is the explicit matrix completion step, which aims to densify the representation before dimensionality reduction, rather than applying LSI directly to the sparse concatenated matrix.

The paper has several significant strengths:
*   **Originality:** The formulation of cross-lingual representation learning as a matrix completion problem is a novel and intuitive contribution. It directly addresses the missing data issue inherent in combining monolingual documents into a shared space.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The optimization problem is well-defined, and the authors provide a projected gradient descent algorithm with a formal convergence proof, which adds mathematical rigor to their approach.
*   **Significance:** The experimental results on the Amazon product review dataset are strong. The proposed TSL method consistently outperforms several established baselines, including CL-LSI and CL-KCCA. Critically, the method demonstrates particular robustness when the number of parallel documents is small, which is a highly practical and important advantage.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is presented logically, from motivation to the final classification step.

Despite these strengths, there are a few areas for improvement:
*   **Evaluation:** The hyperparameter tuning appears to be minimal, with parameters selected based on a single task and then applied universally. A more rigorous cross-validation strategy would strengthen the empirical claims. Additionally, the comparison is primarily against methods from a decade ago. Including comparisons to more recent neural cross-lingual embedding approaches would better contextualize the work's significance in the current NLP landscape.
*   **Clarity:** The use of TF-IDF as input to a matrix completion formulation that assumes non-negativity could be further clarified, as the statistical properties of TF-IDF differ from raw counts, for which such models are often designed.

In summary, this paper presents a well-motivated and technically solid method for cross-language representation learning. The novelty of the matrix completion step combined with strong empirical results, especially in low-resource settings, makes this a valuable contribution to the field.
