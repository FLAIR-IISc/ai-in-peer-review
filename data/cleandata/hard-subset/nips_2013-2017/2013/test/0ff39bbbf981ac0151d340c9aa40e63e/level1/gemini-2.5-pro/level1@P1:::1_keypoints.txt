- Problem: Cross-language text classification with disjoint vocabularies, using labeled source-language data and scarce target-language labels.  
- Method: Proposes a two-step learning (TSL) that completes a unified document-term matrix via convex matrix completion, then applies LSI (SVD) to get shared low-dimensional representations.  
- Novelty: Uses trace-norm and L1-regularized matrix completion to infer missing cross-lingual term co-occurrences instead of treating them as zeros.  
- Optimization: Solves the convex problem with a projected gradient descent algorithm and provides a convergence proof.  
- Empirical results: Evaluated on 18 cross-language tasks; TSL consistently and significantly outperforms CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA and other baselines.  
- Low-resource robustness: Maintains strong performance even with very small parallel corpora (e.g., 200 documents).  
- Technical soundness: Well-defined convex formulation and formal convergence analysis add rigor.  
- Scalability concern: Per-iteration full SVD on the t×d matrix may be computationally expensive; no complexity/runtime analysis provided.  
- Lack of qualitative analysis: No examples showing which cross-lingual term weights are learned, leaving the central intuition unvalidated qualitatively.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Multiple hyperparameters tuned on a single task without sensitivity analysis or justification (e.g., µ = 10^-6).  
- Convergence clarity: Relation between step size τ and parameter ρ is unclear; algorithm inputs and theoretical conditions need better alignment.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, well-motivated approach with strong empirical gains and practical value in low-resource settings.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Unaddressed computational scalability and missing qualitative validation of the matrix completion step.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
