Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses cross-language text classification by proposing a two-step representation learning method that exploits a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 formulates a convex matrix completion problem over a unified bilingual document-term matrix, whose rows contain both languages for parallel pairs and are partially observed for non-parallel documents. The objective combines a trace-norm (low-rank) penalty and an L1 penalty under nonnegativity, with squared loss on observed entries and a regularization (with weight ρ) on missing entries toward zero. A projected gradient descent (PGD) algorithm with singular value shrinkage and nonnegativity projection is used, with a convergence guarantee based on non-expansive operators. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual document representations for downstream monolingual SVMs.

This builds on prior cross-lingual representation learning methods such as CL-LSI (Littman et al.), CL-KCCA (Vinokourov et al.), and CL-OPCA (Platt et al.), as well as SCL-based feature induction (Prettenhofer & Stein). The novelty is to first fill cross-language missing entries via convex matrix completion before dimensionality reduction, rather than operating directly on the original concatenated matrix. The optimization relates to nuclear-norm matrix completion (Candès & Recht; Ma et al.), adapted to nonnegative TF-IDF data and cross-lingual structure.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The formulation is coherent and convex; the PGD algorithm with soft-thresholding (singular value shrinkage) is standard and the convergence proof is reasonable (non-expansiveness of gradient step, shrinkage, and projection). The analysis correctly notes that constant terms in the gradient do not affect non-expansiveness. However, some details are missing: stopping criteria, use of partial SVDs for scalability, iteration counts, and time/memory complexity for large vocabularies.
- Empirical support: Experiments on 18 cross-language sentiment tasks (Amazon reviews) compare against TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA. Reported average accuracies show consistent gains, particularly with very few parallel documents. The study also varies the number of parallel pairs and shows robustness. While convincing, the paper would benefit from statistical significance tests, reporting actual numbers (Table/Figures are referenced but not visible here), and ablations (e.g., using M* without LSI; removing the L1 or nonnegativity).
- Assumptions/limitations: Penalizing missing entries toward zero (with ρ) can bias imputation; justification and sensitivity to ρ are partially explored but a deeper analysis (e.g., why small ρ is best) would help. The approach is unsupervised in the representation stage; leveraging labels in the completion might further help.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The construction of the bilingual matrix and the algorithm steps are easy to follow. Notation is consistent. Minor issues: define I(·) explicitly as identity, explain the choice τ < min(2, 2/ρ) via Lipschitz constant reasoning, and state computational tricks (e.g., randomized/partial SVD). A brief schematic of the two-step pipeline would aid readability.

Originality:
The combination of convex matrix completion with subsequent LSI for cross-lingual representation appears novel relative to CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA and SCL. The optimization itself uses known tools, but the application and modeling choices (nonnegativity, L1 under nonnegativity, explicit penalization of missing entries) are a fresh take. That said, the conceptual step—from imputing missing bilingual features to LSI—is incremental rather than a fundamental algorithmic advance.

Significance:
Results suggest meaningful improvements in a widely studied setting, especially when parallel data are scarce—an important regime. The method is general and could apply to other multilingual tasks. However, the empirical scope is limited to sentiment on a single dataset; including a second dataset (e.g., news/topic classification) would strengthen claims. Comparisons to emerging cross-lingual word-embedding methods (circa 2013) or MT-based translation baselines would help establish breadth.

Strengths:
- Simple, principled, convex formulation with a clear optimization algorithm and convergence guarantee.
- Strong, consistent empirical gains over solid baselines, particularly in low-parallel-data regimes.
- Does not require translating all training data; uses modest unlabeled parallel corpora.
- Well-written and easy to reproduce at a high level (parameters reported).

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical breadth (single dataset/domain) and missing runtime/scalability analysis.
- No ablation to isolate the contribution of completion vs. LSI; limited sensitivity analysis beyond a few hyperparameters.
- Lack of statistical tests and limited reporting of exact numbers in the text.
- Theoretical component is limited to convergence; no recovery guarantees or error bounds under cross-lingual structure.
- Baselines miss some contemporaneous alternatives (e.g., translation-based classifiers; cross-lingual embeddings).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide runtime, iteration counts, and memory usage; discuss use of truncated SVD and wall-clock comparisons to CL-LSI/CL-KCCA.
- Add ablations: (i) M* without LSI, (ii) remove L1 or nonnegativity to assess their impact, (iii) vary ρ more finely and discuss effect.
- Include an additional dataset and a translation-based baseline; if feasible, compare against a cross-lingual embedding approach.
- Report statistical significance and include per-task numerical results in the main paper or appendix.
- Offer qualitative analysis of recovered cross-lingual associations (e.g., nearest neighbors across languages) to interpret what the completion learns.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important problem with a practical, well-grounded method.
- Empirically strong, especially with scarce parallel data; likely to be useful to practitioners.
- Clear exposition and a convex optimization with convergence guarantees.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Novelty is moderate; method is a combination of known components.
- Experimental scope and baselines could be broader; lacks runtime/scalability evidence.
- Limited theoretical novelty beyond convergence.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The method is solid and empirically effective, and the paper is clearly written. Strengthening the experimental breadth and analysis would make it a firm accept.
