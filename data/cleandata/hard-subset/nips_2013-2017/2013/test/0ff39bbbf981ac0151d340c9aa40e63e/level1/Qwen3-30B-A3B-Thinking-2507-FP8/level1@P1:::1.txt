

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that addresses the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages. The authors first construct a bilingual document-term matrix where parallel documents have complete observations while non-parallel documents have missing values for the other language. They then formulate a matrix completion problem to fill these missing entries, followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The approach differs from prior work that either applies LSI/KCCA/OPCA directly to the incomplete matrix or relies on translation of all documents.

The paper relates to NIPS work such as CL-LSI (by [10]), CL-KCCA (by [20]), and CL-OPCA (by [15]), which directly apply dimensionality reduction techniques to the concatenated document-term matrix. Unlike these methods, the authors' key innovation is to first complete the matrix using a convex optimization approach before applying LSI. The work also relates to translation-based approaches like those in [17, 21, 22], but avoids the need to translate all documents.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with a well-formulated matrix completion problem. The authors provide a convex relaxation of the rank minimization problem, develop a projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence guarantees (Theorem 1), and conduct thorough experiments on 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks. The experimental design properly evaluates the impact of varying numbers of parallel documents (Figure 1-2), demonstrating the method's robustness with limited parallel data.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The two-step approach is clearly explained, the mathematical formulation is precise, and the algorithm is described with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The experimental setup is thoroughly documented, including parameter selection procedures.

**Originality:** The core idea of using matrix completion to fill missing entries before applying LSI is novel compared to existing cross-lingual representation learning methods. The authors properly distinguish their work from related approaches in the literature, particularly noting that previous methods (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) apply dimensionality reduction directly to the incomplete matrix, while their method first completes the matrix. This represents a meaningful technical contribution.

**Significance:** The results demonstrate significant improvements over baselines (8.59-14.61% average accuracy gains over TBOW) and outperforms other cross-lingual methods across 16 of 18 tasks. Crucially, the method achieves strong performance even with very limited parallel data (200 documents), which is a practical advantage for low-resource language scenarios. Cross-language text classification has clear applications in multilingual information retrieval and sentiment analysis.

## Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of their approach. For instance, it would be valuable to understand how the method performs with languages that have very different word order or grammatical structures. The matrix completion formulation assumes non-negative features and uses a simple ℓ1 norm for sparsity, but the authors don't discuss how this might affect results with different feature representations.

**Clarity:** While the paper is well-written overall, the connection between the matrix completion step and the subsequent LSI step could be more clearly motivated. The authors state that "exploiting such a matrix representation directly for cross language text classification lacks sufficient capacity," but don't provide evidence or intuition for why the completed matrix is better suited for LSI than the original incomplete matrix.

**Originality:** The paper cites related work well, but doesn't sufficiently distinguish their approach from the matrix completion methods in [4, 11]. While the application to cross-language learning is novel, the matrix completion technique itself is not new. The authors should more clearly articulate why standard matrix completion methods don't directly apply to this domain.

**Significance:** The paper demonstrates strong results on Amazon product reviews, but doesn't discuss how these findings might generalize to other domains or more challenging language pairs. It would strengthen the significance to include examples of specific cross-lingual tasks where the method could have practical impact.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel two-step approach that significantly outperforms existing methods, especially with limited parallel data
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees
- Thorough experimental evaluation across multiple language pairs and domains
- Clear, well-written presentation that would be valuable to the NIPS community
- Practical relevance to cross-lingual applications with limited parallel data

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of the method's limitations and failure cases
- The matrix completion technique itself is not novel, though its application is
- Could better distinguish the approach from existing matrix completion work
- Limited discussion of how the method might scale to larger language pairs or more languages

## Conclusion
This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to cross-lingual representation learning. The two-step approach of first completing the bilingual document-term matrix via matrix completion and then applying LSI represents a meaningful technical advancement over existing methods. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate clear advantages, particularly in low-resource settings. The paper is well-written and technically sound.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limitations discussion and better distinguish the matrix completion component from existing techniques. The work is significant, original, and of high quality, making it a strong candidate for NIPS.
