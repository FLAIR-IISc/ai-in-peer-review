## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, addressing the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages. The core idea is to first complete a partially observed document-term matrix constructed from parallel bilingual documents using a matrix completion technique, and then apply Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to induce a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation. The authors provide a projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence guarantees for solving the matrix completion problem. The method is evaluated on cross-lingual sentiment classification using Amazon product reviews, demonstrating performance improvements over several baselines, particularly with limited parallel data.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in cross-lingual learning. It correctly positions itself within the landscape of methods utilizing machine translation (e.g., [18, 6, 23]) and those leveraging parallel corpora (e.g., [10, 20, 15]). The proposed approach distinguishes itself by focusing on matrix completion as a means of bridging the vocabulary gap *before* applying dimensionality reduction, a nuance not commonly found in related work like CL-LSI [10] or CL-OPCA [15]. The inclusion of a convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm is a definite strength, demonstrating a rigorous approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The two-step approach combining matrix completion and LSI is a reasonable and potentially effective strategy.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The convergence analysis of the projected gradient descent algorithm adds credibility.
*   **Empirical results:** The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over several baselines, particularly when parallel data is scarce.
*   **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited baselines:** While several baselines are included, the comparison could be strengthened by including more recent and sophisticated cross-lingual embedding techniques (e.g., those based on pre-trained multilingual language models like mBERT or XLM-RoBERTa).
*   **Parameter sensitivity:** The paper mentions parameter selection using a single task (EFB). A more systematic analysis of parameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
*   **Scalability discussion:** While the algorithm is presented, a more detailed discussion of its computational complexity and scalability to very large datasets would be valuable.
*   **Lack of error analysis:** The paper would benefit from a qualitative error analysis to understand the types of errors the proposed method makes and potential areas for improvement.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to cross-lingual representation learning.
*   The theoretical analysis and empirical results support the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in NLP.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The comparison to existing methods is not exhaustive, and more recent baselines should be considered.
*   The lack of detailed scalability analysis and error analysis weakens the overall contribution.

**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While there are areas for improvement, the novelty of the approach, the theoretical grounding, and the positive empirical results make it worthy of acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
