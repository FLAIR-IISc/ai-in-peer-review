Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning aimed at cross-language text classification. Step 1 formulates a matrix completion problem on a unified bilingual document-term matrix in which parallel document pairs have observed features in both languages, while non-parallel documents have missing features in the other language. The objective is convex after relaxing rank to the trace norm, and includes ℓ1 (sparsity) regularization, squared loss on observed entries, squared loss toward zero on missing entries (weighted by ρ), and a nonnegativity constraint. A projected gradient descent scheme is used: a gradient step on the smooth part, singular value thresholding (SVT) to induce low rank, and projection onto the nonnegative orthant. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual document representations, after which a linear SVM is trained.

The approach relates to prior cross-lingual representation methods such as CL-LSI (Littman et al., 1998), CL-KCCA (Vinokourov et al., NIPS 2003), and CL-OPCA (Platt et al., EMNLP), as well as structural correspondence learning (Prettenhofer & Stein, ACL 2010). Unlike these, which operate directly on concatenated/paired data via LSI/CCA/OPCA, the present work first imputes missing entries via a low-rank+sparse matrix completion with nonnegativity, then performs LSI. The optimization builds on ideas from SVT/fixed-point methods for nuclear norm minimization (e.g., Ma et al., 2011).

On cross-lingual sentiment classification with multilingual Amazon reviews (English–French/German/Japanese), the method reportedly outperforms TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA across 18 tasks, especially when few parallel documents are available.

Quality (technical soundness):
- Formulation: The relaxed objective is convex and reasonable: trace norm promotes low rank; ℓ1 and nonnegativity encourage sparse, interpretable counts; squared losses handle observed and missing entries (the latter shrinking toward zero with weight ρ).
- Algorithm: The update sequence (gradient on smooth g, SVT with τγ, then projection onto nonnegativity) is natural, but the theoretical justification is not fully rigorous for the stated constrained problem. The non-expansiveness of each operator is correctly shown, and the step size condition matches the Lipschitz constant of ∇g (max(1, ρ)), but concluding convergence to an optimal solution of the constrained problem (trace norm + indicator of nonnegativity + smooth term) requires that the composite of SVT and projection equals the proximal operator of the sum of two nonsmooth terms (nuclear norm + indicator), which is generally not true. The proof appeals to fixed-point non-expansiveness (Ma et al., 2011) but those results do not directly cover the added projection. As written, the argument establishes convergence to a fixed point of the composed map, not necessarily optimality of (2). This is a significant theoretical gap.
- Practical aspects: Computational complexity and scalability are not discussed. Each iteration requires an SVD; for large matrices typical in NLP, this can be prohibitive unless partial or randomized SVDs are used. Stopping criteria are not specified.
- Experimental methodology: Representation learning uses all documents (including test) in an unsupervised/transductive fashion; this is consistent across baselines. Hyperparameters are chosen on one task (EFB) and transferred to others; this is pragmatic but could bias results if that task is not representative.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The construction of the unified matrix and the role of parallel/non-parallel documents are explained. The optimization section is readable with explicit gradients and operators. Minor issues: typos in references/venues; algorithm pseudocode could state stopping criteria and whether truncated SVD is used.

Originality:
The idea of first imputing missing entries via convex matrix completion on a bilingual document-term matrix, then applying LSI, appears novel relative to CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA and SCL-based methods. The individual components are known, but their combination to propagate cross-lingual co-occurrence to non-parallel documents is a meaningful twist. The nonnegativity + ℓ1 within nuclear-norm matrix completion in this context is also a reasonable design choice. The novelty is moderate.

Significance:
Empirically, the method shows consistent gains across 18 tasks and robustness with small parallel corpora, an important practical regime. However, significance is limited by evaluation on a single dataset/few domains and by the absence of comparisons to alternative contemporary approaches (e.g., bilingual topic models, more recent multilingual embeddings if contemporaneous). The technique could be adopted in other cross-lingual settings, but scalability concerns and the theoretical gap may hinder impact.

Strengths:
- Clear problem setup and practical motivation.
- Convex formulation leveraging both within- and cross-language co-occurrence.
- Simple algorithm with intuitively sound steps; non-expansiveness and step-size conditions are discussed.
- Strong empirical results, especially with few parallel pairs, and consistent comparisons to established baselines.
- Ablation with varying amounts of parallel data is informative.

Weaknesses:
- Convergence proof does not rigorously establish convergence to the optimum of the constrained objective; composition of SVT and nonnegativity projection is not the proximal of the sum.
- No complexity/runtime analysis or implementation details (e.g., truncated SVD), which is crucial for large-scale text.
- Limited evaluation breadth: one dataset, one task family, and baselines restricted to classical CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA; no ablations on the contribution of ℓ1/nonnegativity vs. matrix completion alone, nor TSL vs. direct LSI on zero-filled matrices.
- Hyperparameter selection on a single task; potential sensitivity not fully explored.
- Clarity on how CL-LSI/OPCA are instantiated with non-parallel documents could be improved to ensure fairness.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a rigorous optimization argument (e.g., treat the indicator of nonnegativity within the proximal step or use ADMM/forward–backward–forward splitting that handles multiple nonsmooth terms), or clearly state the algorithm as a heuristic with empirical convergence.
- Include computational details: use of truncated/randomized SVD, per-iteration cost, convergence criteria, and wall-clock times.
- Add ablations: (i) LSI on zero-filled matrix; (ii) matrix completion without ℓ1 or without nonnegativity; (iii) effect of ρ and γ. Report sensitivity beyond one task.
- Broaden baselines (if feasible) and clarify fairness in constructing dual-language documents for CL-LSI/OPCA when documents are not parallel.
- Consider evaluation on additional datasets or tasks to strengthen significance claims.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a relevant cross-lingual problem with a simple, general approach.
- Empirical gains are consistent and pronounced, particularly in low-parallel-data regimes.
- Conceptual novelty in combining matrix completion with LSI to propagate cross-lingual structure.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Theoretical justification is insufficient for the claimed convergence to the optimum under the constraint; algorithm may be heuristic.
- Missing scalability analysis and key implementation details for practical adoption.
- Limited experimental scope and ablations reduce confidence in generality and in identifying which components drive improvements.

Overall assessment:
Promising empirical results and a reasonable, potentially impactful idea, but the current theoretical and experimental limitations are nontrivial.
