- Proposes a two-step cross-language classification: convex matrix completion on a bilingual document-term matrix, then LSI-based dimensionality reduction.
- Objective combines trace-norm (low-rank) and L1 penalties under nonnegativity, with squared loss on observed entries and a ρ-weighted penalty on missing entries.
- Uses projected gradient descent with singular value shrinkage and nonnegativity projection; convergence argued via non-expansive operator properties.
- Final representations obtained by SVD on the completed matrix and used by monolingual SVMs.
- Novelty: imputing cross-lingual missing entries via convex matrix completion before applying LSI, differing from prior CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA/SCL approaches.
- Builds on nuclear-norm matrix completion literature, adapted to nonnegative TF-IDF and bilingual structure.
- Formulation is coherent and convex; convergence proof is reasonable but some optimization details are omitted.
- Missing practical optimization details: stopping criteria, use of partial/randomized SVD, iteration counts, and time/memory complexity for large vocabularies.
- Empirically strong: consistent accuracy gains on 18 Amazon sentiment cross-language tasks, especially with very few parallel documents.
- Experimental reporting lacks statistical significance tests, per-task numeric results in-text, and key ablations.
- Ablation gaps: no evaluation of M* without LSI, removal of L1 or nonnegativity, or fuller sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters.
- Penalizing missing entries toward zero (ρ) can bias imputations; justification and deeper sensitivity analysis for ρ are needed.
- The representation learning is unsupervised; leveraging labels during completion could potentially improve results.
- Paper is generally clear and well organized; minor clarifications (e.g., notation I(·), τ choice, schematic pipeline) would help.
- The approach is original in application and modeling choices but is an incremental combination of known tools rather than a fundamental algorithmic advance.
- Significance: meaningful improvements in low-parallel-data regimes and general applicability, but evaluation limited to a single dataset/domain.
- Comparisons miss contemporaneous alternatives (translation-based classifiers, cross-lingual embeddings) which would broaden empirical claims.
- Strengths: principled convex formulation, convergence guarantee, strong empirical gains in scarce-parallel-data settings, and reproducibility-level reporting.
- Weaknesses: limited empirical breadth and scalability analysis, no ablations, absence of statistical tests, and limited theoretical recovery guarantees.
- Suggested improvements: report runtime/memory and iteration counts, use truncated SVD, add ablations and finer ρ sweeps, include another dataset and translation/embedding baselines, add statistical tests and qualitative analyses.
- Acceptance pros: addresses an important problem with a practical, well-grounded, and empirically effective method.
- Acceptance cons: moderate novelty, insufficient experimental breadth and baseline comparisons, and missing scalability evidence.
- Overall recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on strengthening experimental analyses and broader evaluations.
