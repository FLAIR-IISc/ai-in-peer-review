- Proposes a two-step cross-language method: build a bilingual document-term matrix with missing entries, perform matrix completion, then apply LSI for cross-lingual representations.
- Differs from prior work (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) by filling missing values before dimensionality reduction rather than operating on the incomplete matrix.
- Situates method within matrix completion literature (e.g., Candés & Recht) and cites relevant cross-lingual representation papers (Vinokourov et al., Platt et al.).
- Matrix completion formulation (Eq. 2) is well-motivated with appropriate regularization.
- Provides a rigorous convergence proof (Theorem 1) grounded in non-smooth convex optimization results.
- Experimental evaluation is thorough: 18 cross-lingual sentiment tasks on Amazon reviews and multiple strong baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA).
- Empirical results show consistent, statistically significant improvements (up to 14.61% over TBOW) and superiority to baselines.
- Demonstrates robustness to small amounts of parallel data, an important practical advantage.
- Paper is well-structured, clearly written, and includes sufficient implementation details for reproduction (SVM parameters, hyperparameter selection).
- Figures effectively illustrate the impact of parallel data size on performance.
- Novelty comes from applying matrix completion as a preprocessing step for cross-lingual document-term matrices with bilingual bridging data.
- Addresses a significant practical problem—cross-language classification with limited labeled/parallel data—and shows practical applicability.
- Strength: rigorous theoretical foundation and clear distinction from prior methods.
- Strength: comprehensive empirical validation across multiple languages and domains.
- Weakness: limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability of matrix completion on large matrices.
- Weakness: hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (γ, ρ, k) could be more exhaustive.
- Weakness: dataset scope is narrow (Amazon product reviews), limiting domain generality.
- Weakness: lacks discussion of failure cases or scenarios where the method might underperform.
- Pro argument: novel, theoretically sound, empirically effective approach that works with minimal parallel data.
- Con argument: scalability and hyperparameter robustness need more analysis; dataset breadth is limited.
- Recommendation: paper makes a significant contribution and should be accepted.
