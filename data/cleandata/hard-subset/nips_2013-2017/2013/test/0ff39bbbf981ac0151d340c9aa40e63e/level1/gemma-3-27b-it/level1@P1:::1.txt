This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning, addressing the disjoint feature space problem in cross-lingual text classification. The method first performs matrix completion on a concatenated document-term matrix (with missing values for non-parallel documents) and then applies Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to induce a low-dimensional cross-lingual document representation. The authors provide a convergence analysis of their projected gradient descent algorithm and demonstrate strong performance on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, particularly when parallel data is limited.

This work builds on a substantial body of research in cross-lingual learning. Approaches like machine translation [18, 6, 23] and utilizing bilingual resources [16, 10, 20, 15] are well-established. The proposed method shares similarities with techniques that exploit parallel data, like CL-LSI [10] and CL-OPCA [15]. However, it distinguishes itself by first addressing the missing data problem through matrix completion before applying dimensionality reduction, a strategy not commonly found in prior work. The cited work by Dai et al. [22] utilizes co-training with parallel corpora, differing in approach from the proposed matrix completion and LSI combination.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The two-step approach of matrix completion followed by LSI is a fresh take on cross-lingual representation learning. Addressing the missing data challenge directly via matrix completion seems like a sensible approach, particularly given the frequent scarcity of parallel data.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The inclusion of a convergence analysis for the projected gradient descent algorithm adds rigor to the work. This is a significant plus, as many machine learning papers lack such analysis.
* **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate consistent outperformance over several baselines, including CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA. The improvement is particularly noticeable when the amount of parallel data is small, highlighting a key advantage of the proposed method.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is clearly presented, and the experimental setup is well-defined.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors perform a comprehensive evaluation, varying the amount of parallel data to assess the robustness of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Matrix Completion Justification:** While the authors argue that the document-term matrix is “naturally low-rank,” this assumption could be explored further. A more detailed justification for *why* matrix completion is expected to be effective in this specific cross-lingual setting would strengthen the paper.  For example, examining the rank of empirically observed matrices or connecting it to theoretical bounds could be valuable.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper reports parameter selection through experimentation on a single task (EFB).  While necessary, it would be beneficial to analyze the sensitivity of performance to the key parameters (γ, ρ, k) across a wider range of tasks. This would provide more insights into the practical usability of the method.
* **Computational Complexity:**  The authors mention the need for a scalable algorithm, but don’t provide details on the computational cost of the matrix completion step.  Given the large size of document-term matrices, this could be a practical limitation.  A discussion of the algorithm’s scalability and perhaps a comparison of running times with other methods would be valuable.
* **Limited Baselines:** While the chosen baselines are relevant, exploring more recent cross-lingual representation learning methods would further solidify the paper’s contribution.  For instance, methods utilizing pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-RoBERTa) are now commonly used and would serve as strong benchmarks.
* **Discussion of Negative Results:** The paper could benefit from a deeper discussion of scenarios where the proposed method doesn’t perform well. Are there specific language pairs or datasets where it struggles? Understanding the limitations is crucial.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to cross-lingual representation learning.
* It provides a theoretical justification for the proposed algorithm.
* The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods, particularly in low-resource scenarios.
* The writing is clear and the presentation is thorough.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The justification for the matrix completion step could be strengthened.
* A more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity and computational complexity is needed.
* The comparison with state-of-the-art methods (including those based on pre-trained language models) is limited.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. The proposed method is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically effective. While some aspects could be further strengthened (parameter sensitivity, computational complexity, comparison with more recent methods), the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should elaborate on the justification for matrix completion, analyze parameter sensitivity more thoroughly, and include a discussion of the computational cost of the algorithm.  A brief comparison with a strong baseline utilizing pre-trained multilingual language models would also be beneficial.
