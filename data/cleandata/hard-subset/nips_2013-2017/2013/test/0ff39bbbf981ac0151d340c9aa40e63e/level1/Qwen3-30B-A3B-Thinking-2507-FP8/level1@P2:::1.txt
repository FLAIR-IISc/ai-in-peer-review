

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning that addresses the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages. The core idea is to first construct a bilingual document-term matrix with missing entries (for non-parallel documents), then use matrix completion to fill these missing values, and finally apply latent semantic indexing (LSI) to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors formulate the matrix completion as a convex optimization problem with trace norm and ℓ1 regularization, provide a projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence guarantees, and evaluate their approach on cross-language sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews.

The paper relates to previous work on cross-language text classification, particularly CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], and CL-OPCA [15], which also aim to construct cross-lingual representations by operating on concatenated bilingual document-term matrices. However, the authors claim their method differs by first completing the matrix before applying LSI, rather than applying LSI directly to the incomplete matrix. While the authors cite relevant literature, they could better articulate how their matrix completion approach specifically differs from these previous methods in terms of assumptions, computational requirements, and underlying mathematical formulations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness and Quality:** The paper presents a well-motivated approach to a well-known problem in cross-language learning. The matrix completion formulation (equation 2) is theoretically grounded, and the convergence analysis (Theorem 1) provides strong theoretical guarantees. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering 18 different language pairs and categories, with proper statistical significance (10 repetitions).

2. **Practical Significance:** The paper demonstrates a meaningful practical contribution by showing that the proposed method achieves significant performance gains (8.59-14.61% average improvement) over baselines, especially when the number of parallel documents is small. This addresses a key practical challenge in cross-language learning where obtaining large amounts of parallel data is often expensive.

3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-structured, with clear sections explaining the problem, approach, optimization algorithm, and experimental setup. The two-step methodology is easy to follow, and the experimental results are presented clearly in Table 1 and Figures 1-2.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient Differentiation from Related Work:** While the authors mention CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, they don't adequately explain how their matrix completion approach fundamentally differs in terms of mathematical formulation, assumptions, or computational requirements. For instance, CL-LSI also uses LSI on a bilingual document-term matrix, but the authors don't explain why their matrix completion step is necessary or how it improves upon CL-LSI's approach. This weakens the originality claim.

2. **Limited Theoretical Analysis of Matrix Completion Approach:** The paper formulates the matrix completion problem with both trace norm (for rank minimization) and ℓ1 norm (for sparsity), but doesn't provide theoretical guarantees about the recovery error or how the choice of γ and ρ affects the quality of the recovered matrix. This is particularly important since the paper claims their method works well with small amounts of parallel data.

3. **Lack of Scalability Discussion:** While the authors mention the matrix M is "typically very large," they don't discuss how their projected gradient descent algorithm scales to larger datasets or how it compares computationally to the other methods (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA). For NIPS, which values scalable methods, this is a notable omission.

4. **Superficial Parameter Selection Justification:** The paper mentions they selected parameters (γ, ρ, k) using the EFB task, but doesn't explain why they chose this specific task for parameter tuning or how sensitive the results are to parameter choices. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness of their method.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that makes a practical contribution to cross-language text classification. The two-step approach is well-motivated and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages, particularly with limited parallel data. However, the paper would benefit from stronger differentiation from previous work, more detailed theoretical analysis of the matrix completion approach, and a discussion of scalability.

The paper meets the quality criteria with sound technical approach and thorough evaluation, though the theoretical contribution is somewhat incremental. The clarity is good, though some aspects of the methodology could be explained more thoroughly. The originality is moderate - the core idea of using matrix completion for cross-language learning is novel, but the connection to previous work needs strengthening. The significance is strong for practical cross-language applications, though the theoretical contribution is somewhat limited.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro Acceptance:*
- The paper provides a theoretically grounded solution to a well-known practical challenge in cross-language learning.
- The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines.
- The method's ability to work well with small amounts of parallel data is a significant practical advantage.

*Con Acceptance:*
- The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from previous methods that also use bilingual document-term matrices.
- The theoretical analysis of the matrix completion step is limited, particularly regarding recovery guarantees and parameter sensitivity.
- The scalability of the proposed approach is not adequately addressed.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to cross-language text classification with strong empirical results and theoretical grounding. While the novelty could be better articulated, the practical significance and technical soundness outweigh these concerns. The paper would benefit from minor revisions to better distinguish it from related work and provide more detailed discussion of the matrix completion properties, but these are not critical flaws. The work is relevant to NIPS as it addresses an important problem in NLP with a novel technical approach that could inspire further research.
