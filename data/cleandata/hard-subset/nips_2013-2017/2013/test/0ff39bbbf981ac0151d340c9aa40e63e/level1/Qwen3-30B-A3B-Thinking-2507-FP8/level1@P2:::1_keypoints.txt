- Proposes a two-step method: fill missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix via matrix completion, then apply LSI for cross-lingual representations.
- Relates to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA but does not clearly distinguish the new matrix-completion formulation from these prior methods.
- Strength — technically sound: convex matrix completion with trace-norm and ℓ1 regularization and convergence-guaranteed projected gradient descent.
- Strength — practical impact: shows large empirical gains (8.6–14.6% avg.) on sentiment transfer, especially with few parallel documents.
- Strength — clear presentation and well-structured experiments across 18 language pairs with statistical significance.
- Weakness — insufficient differentiation: lacks detailed comparison of assumptions, math, and computational trade-offs vs. prior bilingual-matrix methods.
- Weakness — limited theoretical analysis: no recovery-error guarantees or guidance on how γ and ρ affect matrix recovery quality.
- Weakness — scalability concerns: no discussion of algorithmic scaling or runtime comparison for very large matrices.
- Weakness — parameter tuning justification is superficial and sensitivity to γ, ρ, k is not analyzed.
- Overall assessment — solid, practically significant contribution with moderate novelty but some theoretical and comparative gaps.
- Pro-accept: theoretically grounded, strong empirical improvements, useful for low-parallel-data settings.
- Con-accept: insufficient differentiation from prior work, limited matrix-completion theory, unclear scalability.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to strengthen comparisons, theoretical analysis, and scalability discussion.
