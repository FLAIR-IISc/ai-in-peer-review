- Paper proposes a novel two-step method (matrix completion then LSI) to learn cross-lingual document representations using a small set of parallel documents.
- Method formulates recovery of a unified document-term matrix with missing cross-lingual entries as a low-rank matrix completion problem using trace-norm relaxation.
- Authors optimize with a projected gradient descent algorithm and provide a convergence proof (Theorem 1).
- Experimental evaluation is thorough: 18 cross-lingual tasks, multiple strong baselines, and consistent superiority of the proposed method.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and methods and experiments are easy to follow.
- Combining matrix completion with LSI to densify cross-lingual matrices is an original and insightful contribution.
- Approach is particularly effective in low-resource settings, performing well with as few as ~200 parallel documents.
- Results show substantial gains (e.g., up to 14.6% accuracy improvement over target-only baseline), indicating practical significance.
- Strength: directly addresses the flaw of treating missing cross-lingual entries as zeros in prior methods.
- Strength: technically sound formulation and provably convergent optimization.
- Weakness: Scalability concerns due to SVD in each iteration; no complexity analysis or large-scale experiments provided.
- Weakness: Lack of justification for L1 sparsity regularization on the completed matrix, which seems at odds with completion goals.
- Weakness: Missing qualitative analysis of learned representations and inferred cross-lingual term associations.
- Weakness: No sensitivity analysis for key hyperparameters (γ, ρ, and LSI dimensionality k).
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-motivated idea with strong empirical support and clear writing.
- Arguments against acceptance: unresolved scalability issues and insufficient justification for some modeling choices.
- Recommendation: Accept, as weaknesses are limitations for future work rather than fatal flaws.
