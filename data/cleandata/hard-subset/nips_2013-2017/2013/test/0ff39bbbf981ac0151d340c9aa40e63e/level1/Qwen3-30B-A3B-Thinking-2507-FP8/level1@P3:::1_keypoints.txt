- Proposes a two-step cross-language representation method: matrix completion to fill missing bilingual document-term entries, then LSI for low-dimensional cross-lingual embeddings.  
- Novel application of convex trace-norm + ℓ1-regularized matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning, addressing missing-data ignored by prior CL-LSI/CL-OPCA approaches.  
- Theoretical soundness: convex formulation and projected gradient descent (Algorithm 1) have rigorous convergence analysis grounded in matrix completion literature.  
- Practical significance: shows clear empirical gains, especially with limited parallel data (as few as 200 documents).  
- Comprehensive evaluation: 18 cross-lingual tasks across three product categories and three language pairs, with parameter sensitivity and parallel-data-size analyses; TSL outperforms baselines by 8.59–14.61% on average.  
- Weakness — outdated baselines: lacks comparison to modern neural methods (e.g., multilingual BERT, MUSE), weakening the significance claim.  
- Weakness — methodological limitation: low-rank assumption for document-term matrices is not well-justified or analyzed for failure cases (e.g., typologically distant languages).  
- Weakness — parameter sensitivity: insufficient analysis across different language pairs/categories to support the claim that one set of parameters works for all tasks.  
- Weakness — missing simple baseline: no comparison to bilingual-dictionary based word mapping approaches, which are common and relevant.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound and empirically promising for low-resource scenarios, but reduced impact due to missing SOTA comparisons and limited limitation analysis.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Required revisions: add comparisons with recent neural methods (multilingual BERT, MUSE); include a bilingual-dictionary baseline; discuss limitations of the low-rank assumption with failure cases; provide more thorough parameter sensitivity analysis.  
- Pro summary: novel two-step approach, strong theory, good empirical performance with limited parallel data, comprehensive evaluation design.  
- Con summary: outdated baselines, insufficient discussion of limitations, missing dictionary-based comparison, limited parameter-sensitivity analysis.
