- Proposes a two-step cross-lingual representation method: matrix completion on a concatenated document-term matrix followed by LSI to produce cross-language document vectors.  
- Introduces a projected gradient descent algorithm to solve the matrix completion problem with stated convergence guarantees.  
- Novelty lies in performing matrix completion as a pre-processing step before LSI, distinguishing it from prior CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA approaches.  
- Empirical results show improved performance on Amazon product sentiment classification, especially when parallel data is scarce.  
- Provides a clear, step-by-step Algorithm 1 for the projected gradient descent method.  
- Includes theoretical grounding (convergence analysis) that adds rigor to the approach.  
- Conducts an ablation study analyzing the impact of parallel data size (Figures 1 and 2).  
- Correctly positions the work relative to machine translation, domain adaptation, and bilingual-resource-based representation methods.  
- Clarity issues: notation and exposition in the matrix completion section are dense; the regularization loss c(Mij, M0ij) needs more explanation and intuition for algorithm choice.  
- Experimental scope is narrow, limited to Amazon sentiment classification, reducing evidence of broader applicability.  
- Baseline selection is incomplete: lacks comparisons to more recent deep-learning cross-lingual state-of-the-art methods.  
- Parameter sensitivity is underreported: grid search ranges and rationale for chosen values are not fully disclosed.  
- Computational cost and scalability are not discussed despite potential expense of matrix completion on large datasets.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel combination, solid theoretical basis, and strong empirical gains in low-resource settings.  
- Arguments against acceptance: clarity shortcomings, limited experimental breadth and baselines, and missing computational-cost analysis.  
- Overall assessment and recommendation: valuable contribution warranting acceptance with minor revisions to address clarity, experimental scope, and scalability concerns.
