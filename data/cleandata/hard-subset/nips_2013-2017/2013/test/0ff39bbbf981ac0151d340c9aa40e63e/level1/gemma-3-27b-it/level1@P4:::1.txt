This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning, addressing the disjoint feature space problem in cross-lingual text classification. The method first performs matrix completion on a concatenated document-term matrix containing both languages, filling in missing values, and then applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to generate cross-lingual document representations. The authors provide a projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence guarantees for solving the matrix completion problem and demonstrate improved performance on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks with Amazon product reviews, particularly when parallel data is scarce.

The paper builds on a substantial body of work in cross-lingual learning. It correctly positions itself relative to methods employing machine translation ([18, 6, 23]), domain adaptation ([17, 9, 21]), and other representation learning techniques utilizing bilingual resources ([16, 10, 20, 15]). The approach shares similarities with CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA but differentiates itself by first performing matrix completion before applying LSI.  This pre-processing step aimed at recovering low-rank information is a novel aspect within this specific combination of techniques.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The two-step approach of matrix completion followed by LSI is a reasonable and potentially effective combination, differing from direct application of dimensionality reduction techniques on the raw concatenated matrix.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The authors provide a projected gradient descent algorithm for matrix completion with stated convergence guarantees.  This adds rigor to the method.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results on Amazon reviews demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms several baseline techniques, especially when the amount of parallel data is limited. This addresses a practical concern in cross-lingual learning.
*   **Detailed Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 provides a clear, step-by-step description of the projected gradient descent method.
*   **Ablation Study:** The inclusion of a study on the impact of the size of parallel data (Figure 1 and Figure 2) strengthens the findings and provides insight into the method's behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity – Notation & Detailed Explanation:** The notation, particularly concerning the matrix completion section (Section 3.1), can be dense and difficult to follow without careful reading. The explanation of the regularization loss function `c(Mij, M0ij)` is brief and could benefit from expansion. While the algorithm is presented, a more intuitive explanation of *why* this specific algorithm is well-suited to the problem would be helpful.
*   **Experimental Scope:**  The experiments are limited to sentiment classification on Amazon reviews.  While this is a reasonable starting point, demonstrating generalization to other datasets or tasks (e.g., topic classification, news categorization) would strengthen the claims of broader applicability.
*   **Baseline Selection:** While several baselines are included, a more thorough comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods in cross-lingual representation learning would be beneficial. More recent models leveraging deep learning techniques, for example, are absent.
*    **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions parameter selection via a grid search on the EFB task but doesn't fully disclose the range of parameters explored or the rationale for the specific chosen values. A more detailed discussion of parameter sensitivity could enhance reproducibility.
*   **Computational Cost:** Although convergence guarantees are provided, a discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm, particularly regarding scalability to large datasets, would be valuable. Matrix completion can be computationally expensive.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The proposed method represents a novel combination of techniques with a solid theoretical foundation.
*   The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over several established baselines, particularly in low-resource scenarios.
*   The inclusion of a convergence analysis and ablation study adds to the credibility of the work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The clarity of certain sections, particularly related to the optimization algorithm, could be improved.
*   The experimental scope is limited, and a comparison to more recent and potentially stronger baselines is lacking.
*   The computational cost of the method is not discussed.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. The proposed two-step method is innovative and supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results.  However, improving clarity, expanding the experimental evaluation, and addressing the computational cost concerns would significantly strengthen the paper.  Despite these weaknesses, the novelty and positive results justify acceptance, with the expectation that the authors will address the reviewer's comments in a revised version.



**Recommendation:** Accept (with minor revisions)
