- Proposes a novel two-step cross-language method: matrix completion of a bilingual document-term matrix followed by LSI for cross-lingual representations.  
- Constructs a bilingual matrix with missing entries for non-parallel documents and formulates a convex matrix completion problem to fill them.  
- Provides a convex relaxation of rank minimization and a projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence guarantees (Theorem 1).  
- Evaluates on 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks and studies impact of varying numbers of parallel documents.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical gains (8.59–14.61% average accuracy over TBOW) and outperforms baselines on 16/18 tasks.  
- Shows robustness with very limited parallel data (good performance with ~200 parallel documents), useful for low-resource languages.  
- Clear, well-written, and reproducible presentation with detailed algorithm and experimental setup.  
- Novelty: first to apply matrix completion prior to LSI in this cross-lingual context, distinct from CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA and translation-based methods.  
- Lacks a thorough discussion of limitations and failure cases, e.g., performance with languages having very different syntax.  
- Does not fully motivate or provide intuition/evidence for why completing the matrix improves subsequent LSI over using the incomplete matrix directly.  
- Insufficient differentiation from prior matrix completion methods ([4,11]); the matrix completion technique itself is not new.  
- Limited discussion on generalization to other domains, more challenging language pairs, multi-language scaling, or large-scale scenarios.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel two-step pipeline, solid theory, strong empirical results, practical relevance, and clear writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete limitations analysis, limited novelty of underlying matrix completion, and scalability/generalization concerns.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to discuss limitations more, better distinguish the matrix completion component from prior work, and address scalability/generalization.
