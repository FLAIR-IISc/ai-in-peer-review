This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning. Step 1 formulates matrix completion on a unified bilingual document–term matrix constructed from parallel and non-parallel documents: observed entries come from each document’s native vocabulary, missing entries correspond to the other language. The authors optimize a convex surrogate combining nuclear norm (for low rank), an L1 term that reduces to a linear penalty under nonnegativity, and squared losses on observed and (down-weighted) unobserved entries, with nonnegativity constraints. A projected gradient method with singular-value shrinkage is used, with a convergence argument via non-expansive operators (in the spirit of Ma et al., 2011). Step 2 applies LSI to the completed matrix to obtain a shared low-dimensional space for downstream monolingual classification. Experiments on cross-lingual sentiment classification with the Amazon reviews corpus show consistent gains over CL-LSI (Littman et al., 1998), CL-KCCA (Vinokourov et al., NIPS 2002), CL-OPCA (Platt et al., 2010), and a target-only baseline, especially when few parallel documents are available. The approach relates to prior NIPS work on translated learning and multi-view learning (e.g., Dai et al., NIPS; Amini et al., NIPS) but differs by imputing missing bilingual features before learning the shared space.

Quality: The optimization is sound and clearly derived; the convergence proof is appropriate. The empirical evaluation is thorough across 18 transfer directions, with a useful analysis of sensitivity to the number of parallel pairs. However, there is no runtime/complexity analysis for repeated SVDs, no stopping criteria, and no ablation to disentangle the benefits of completion vs. LSI. The claim that the solution is sparse is not fully justified given an L1 term that is a global sum under nonnegativity.

Clarity: Generally well written and organized, with clear notation and algorithmic description. A few details could be expanded (computational cost, memory footprint, parameter selection rationale, and statistical significance reporting).

Originality: Moderate. Using convex matrix completion to bridge disjoint vocabularies before LSI is a neat, novel combination compared to CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA and SCL (Prettenhofer & Stein, 2010).

Significance: The improvements, particularly in low-parallel regimes, are compelling and likely of practical interest. The approach should transfer to other cross-lingual tasks.

Pros for acceptance:
- Simple, principled two-step formulation with convergence guarantees.
- Strong, consistent empirical gains; robust with few parallel pairs.
- Clear relation to and improvement over established baselines.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited scalability analysis; repeated SVDs may be costly.
- Missing ablations and significance tests.
- Comparisons omit modern bilingual embeddings; only English-centric transfers.

Overall: Weak accept. The method is technically sound, clear, and empirically useful; addressing scalability and ablations would strengthen it further.
