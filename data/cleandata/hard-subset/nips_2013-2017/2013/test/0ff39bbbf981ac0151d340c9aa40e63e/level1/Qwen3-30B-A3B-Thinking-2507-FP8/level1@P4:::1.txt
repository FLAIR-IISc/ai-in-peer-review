

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that addresses the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages. The authors first construct a bilingual document-term matrix where parallel documents have complete observations and non-parallel documents have missing values for the other language's vocabulary. They then solve a matrix completion problem to fill missing entries, followed by latent semantic indexing to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The approach differs from prior work like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA that apply dimensionality reduction directly on the incomplete matrix without filling missing values.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work: it builds upon matrix completion techniques (e.g., Candés & Recht, 2008) while addressing a specific application in cross-lingual learning. The authors correctly cite relevant NIPS papers such as those by Vinokourov et al. (2006) and Platt et al. (2008) on cross-lingual representation learning. They clearly distinguish their approach from translation-based methods (e.g., Dai et al., 2007) that require full document translation, and from other representation learning methods that operate directly on incomplete matrices.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The matrix completion formulation (Equation 2) is well-motivated and includes appropriate regularization terms. The convergence proof (Theorem 1) is rigorous and correctly builds upon established results from non-smooth convex optimization. The experimental design is thorough, with 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks on Amazon product reviews and multiple baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA). Results consistently show the proposed method (TSL) outperforms baselines, with statistically significant improvements (up to 14.61% over TBOW). The authors appropriately acknowledge the method's dependency on parallel data size and provide evidence of robustness with small parallel datasets.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The two-step methodology is logically presented, with sufficient mathematical detail for reproduction. The experimental section provides all necessary implementation details (e.g., SVM parameters, hyperparameter selection procedure). The figures (Figure 1-2) effectively illustrate the impact of parallel data size. The writing is concise and avoids unnecessary jargon.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by introducing matrix completion as a preprocessing step before LSI for cross-lingual representation learning. While matrix completion has been used in other contexts (Candés & Recht, 2008), applying it specifically to cross-lingual document-term matrices with bilingual bridging data is novel. The authors successfully demonstrate how their approach differs from prior work in Section 2, particularly noting that previous methods (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) operate directly on incomplete matrices rather than filling missing entries first. This is a meaningful advancement over previous approaches.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important practical problem in natural language processing: cross-language text classification with limited labeled data in the target language. The method's effectiveness with small amounts of parallel data (as shown in Section 5.3) is particularly valuable for real-world applications where parallel data is scarce. The results demonstrate clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods, suggesting the approach could be adopted by practitioners. The paper advances the field by providing a more robust solution for cross-lingual representation learning that requires fewer parallel documents than existing methods.

**Strengths:**
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with convergence proof
- Clear distinction from prior work with well-motivated approach
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple language pairs and domains
- Demonstrated effectiveness with minimal parallel data (a significant practical advantage)
- Well-written and well-organized presentation

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of computational complexity compared to baselines (matrix completion on large document-term matrices could be expensive)
- Hyperparameter sensitivity analysis could be more thorough (e.g., sensitivity to γ, ρ, k beyond the selected values)
- Dataset scope is somewhat limited to Amazon product reviews (though this is common for cross-lingual sentiment tasks)
- No discussion of failure cases or scenarios where the method might not perform well

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

**Pro:**
- Presents a novel two-step approach that significantly improves upon existing cross-lingual representation learning methods
- Provides rigorous theoretical foundation with convergence proof
- Demonstrates clear empirical advantages, especially with small parallel data sets
- Well-organized paper with thorough experimental evaluation
- Addresses a meaningful practical problem in natural language processing

**Con:**
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability
- Some aspects of hyperparameter selection could be more thoroughly explored
- Dataset used is somewhat narrow (Amazon product reviews)

**Recommendation:**
The paper makes a significant contribution to cross-lingual representation learning with a novel method that is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. The approach addresses a practical limitation of existing methods (requiring large amounts of parallel data) and demonstrates clear improvements across multiple tasks. The quality of the work meets the high standards of NIPS, with strong technical content, clear presentation, and significant empirical validation. While the computational complexity discussion could be strengthened, this does not detract from the paper's overall contribution. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and should be accepted.
