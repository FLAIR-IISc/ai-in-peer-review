

# Peer Review: "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that first constructs a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix with missing entries for non-parallel documents, then uses matrix completion to fill these missing values, and finally applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors claim their method significantly outperforms existing approaches (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews.

The paper builds upon previous work on cross-lingual representation learning, particularly CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], and CL-OPCA [15]. While the authors correctly identify that these methods apply LSI/KCCA/OPCA directly on the partially observed document-term matrix, they don't sufficiently justify why matrix completion would be more effective for this specific task. The paper's core contribution is the two-step learning procedure: matrix completion followed by LSI.

## Quality Assessment

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-defined optimization problem for matrix completion with a convergence proof for the proposed projected gradient descent algorithm. The experimental setup is standard and well-executed.

2. **Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines across 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks. The results are statistically significant, with TSL outperforming the next best method (CL-KCCA) by 2.7-3.3% on average.

3. **Data Efficiency:** The paper correctly highlights that the proposed method works well with very few unlabeled parallel documents (as few as 200), which is a significant practical advantage.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical Justification Gap:** The paper lacks a strong theoretical foundation for why the document-term matrix should be low-rank or why matrix completion is appropriate for cross-lingual representation learning. Previous work (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA) already assumes a low-rank structure in their representations, but the paper doesn't explain why their matrix completion approach captures better semantic relationships than direct application of LSI/KCCA on the partially observed matrix.

2. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison:** The paper compares only with older methods (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) but fails to compare with more recent neural approaches. For instance, multilingual BERT (mBERT) has been shown to achieve state-of-the-art results on cross-lingual tasks [1]. Without this comparison, it's unclear whether the proposed method remains competitive with modern approaches.

3. **Parameter Selection Flaw:** The authors select parameters using only the EFB task and apply them to all 18 tasks. This is a methodological weakness that could lead to suboptimal performance on some tasks. A more robust approach would involve cross-validation across multiple tasks.

4. **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the matrix completion step, which is critical for real-world applications. For large-scale cross-lingual tasks with millions of documents, the proposed method might be prohibitively expensive compared to simpler approaches.

5. **Missing Analysis:** The paper lacks qualitative analysis of the learned representations. A visualization of the top words for the top semantic dimensions would help demonstrate how the method captures cross-lingual semantic relationships.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to experiments. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the convergence analysis is appropriately included.

However, the paper could be improved by:
- Providing a clearer explanation of why matrix completion is necessary before LSI (why not apply LSI directly to the partially observed matrix?).
- Adding a simple example to illustrate how the matrix completion step fills missing entries with meaningful values.
- Including a figure showing the relationship between the size of unlabeled parallel data and performance for each method.

## Originality Assessment

The paper's core idea of using matrix completion to fill missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix is somewhat novel, but the distinction from existing methods (particularly CL-LSI) is not sufficiently clear. CL-LSI [10] already uses a dual-language document-term matrix, but applies LSI directly without filling missing values. The authors don't provide evidence that their matrix completion step actually improves the quality of the representation compared to directly applying LSI on the partially observed matrix.

The paper could have strengthened its originality claim by:
- Conducting an ablation study showing the impact of the matrix completion step (e.g., comparing performance with and without matrix completion).
- Providing a theoretical argument for why the low-rank assumption holds for cross-lingual document-term matrices.

## Significance Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The problem addressed (cross-lingual text classification with limited labeled data) is important and practical.
- The method's ability to work with very few unlabeled parallel documents is a significant practical advantage.
- The consistent improvements over baselines across multiple language pairs and domains demonstrate the method's robustness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't discuss how the method scales to larger datasets or more languages.
- The approach is limited to bag-of-words representations, whereas modern cross-lingual methods use neural representations (e.g., BERT).
- The paper doesn't provide any discussion of the method's limitations or scenarios where it would fail.

## Comparison with Recent Work

The paper fails to adequately compare with recent neural approaches to cross-lingual representation learning, such as mBERT [1] or XLM-R [2]. These models have achieved state-of-the-art results on cross-lingual tasks and would serve as much stronger baselines than the older CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA methods. The authors should at least acknowledge these approaches and explain why their method is still relevant in the era of neural cross-lingual representation learning.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a technically sound method for cross-lingual text classification with promising empirical results. However, it has significant weaknesses in theoretical justification, baseline comparison, and practical scalability that limit its contribution to the field.

The method is interesting but not sufficiently novel compared to the state-of-the-art, and the lack of comparison with neural approaches weakens the significance claim. The paper would be much stronger if it included a comparison with modern neural methods and provided a more rigorous theoretical foundation for the matrix completion step.

## Recommendations

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- The method is simple and effective for cross-lingual representation learning with limited parallel data.
- The empirical results are consistent and statistically significant.
- The approach is particularly valuable for resource-constrained scenarios where parallel data is scarce.

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- The theoretical justification for the matrix completion step is weak.
- The method is compared only with older approaches, not with current state-of-the-art neural methods.
- The paper lacks analysis of the computational complexity and scalability.
- The parameter selection procedure is not robust.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a comparison with at least one modern neural approach (e.g., mBERT) to demonstrate the relative merits of the proposed method.

2. Conduct an ablation study showing the impact of the matrix completion step (comparing performance with and without matrix completion).

3. Provide a more robust parameter selection procedure (e.g., cross-validation across multiple tasks).

4. Discuss the computational complexity and scalability of the method for large-scale applications.

5. Include qualitative analysis of the learned representations (e.g., visualization of top words for key semantic dimensions).

6. Strengthen the theoretical justification for why the document-term matrix is low-rank and why matrix completion is appropriate for this task.

## Final Recommendation

I recommend **weak rejection**. The paper presents an interesting method with promising empirical results, but it lacks sufficient novelty and theoretical foundation to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The most critical issue is the lack of comparison with modern neural approaches, which are now the standard for cross-lingual representation learning. Without addressing this gap, the paper's contribution is limited to a minor improvement over older methods that are no longer state-of-the-art.

If the authors can substantially revise the paper to include comparisons with recent neural approaches, strengthen the theoretical justification, and provide a more robust evaluation, this could become a strong paper for NIPS.
