This paper presents a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, leveraging unlabeled parallel bilingual documents via matrix completion and latent semantic indexing (LSI). The core idea – using matrix completion to bridge the vocabulary gap and then applying LSI – is a compelling and novel approach to cross-lingual transfer. It smartly addresses the challenge of disparate feature spaces without relying heavily on translation, a common bottleneck in this field. The authors appropriately situate their work within existing literature, highlighting connections to translation-based methods and other cross-lingual representation learning techniques like CL-LSI and CL-KCCA.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion *before* LSI is a distinct contribution. While matrix completion has been used in related areas, its application here to explicitly address the vocabulary mismatch in cross-lingual scenarios is novel. This is the paper’s strongest aspect.
* **Potential for Efficiency:** The method promises to reduce reliance on extensive machine translation, a significant advantage. The claim that it performs well even with limited parallel data is particularly promising.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the Amazon review dataset demonstrate significant improvements over several baselines, which suggests the method is effective in practice. The ablation study (examining the impact of parallel data size) further strengthens the results.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is reasonably well-written and logically organized, particularly the description of the method and the optimization algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Optimization Details:** The projected gradient descent algorithm is presented, but the practical considerations around parameter tuning (γ, ρ, τ) could be expanded. While the paper mentions parameter selection via a development set, more detail on the search process would be helpful.
* **Theoretical Justification:** While the rationale for matrix completion (low-rank structure, capturing co-occurrence) is intuitive, a more formal theoretical analysis of why this approach is expected to work well would strengthen the paper. Why is low-rank assumption appropriate here?
* **Limited Dataset:** The experiments are limited to the Amazon review dataset. While this is a standard benchmark, evaluating the method on other datasets (e.g., news articles, scientific abstracts) would increase confidence in its generalizability.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The comparison lacks some recent, stronger cross-lingual methods, potentially understating the relative improvement. Specifically, methods leveraging pre-trained multilingual language models (like mBERT or XLM-R) should be considered.
* **Missing Error Analysis:** A deeper dive into *why* the method succeeds (or fails) in certain cases is missing. Examining specific examples where the method performs well and poorly could reveal valuable insights.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea is novel and addresses a challenging problem in cross-lingual learning.
* The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating significant improvements over established baselines.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex method.
* The potential to reduce reliance on machine translation is a significant benefit.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The theoretical justification for the method is somewhat weak.
* The experiments are limited to a single dataset and lack comparison to state-of-the-art methods leveraging pre-trained multilingual models.
* The practical considerations of the optimization algorithm are not fully addressed.




**Overall Assessment:**

I lean strongly towards acceptance. While the paper is not perfect, the novelty of the approach and the promising empirical results outweigh the weaknesses. The paper introduces a potentially impactful direction for cross-lingual representation learning. The weaknesses, while present, are not critical and could be addressed in future work. I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. I would recommend the authors address the limitations mentioned above if given the opportunity to revise. Specifically, adding a comparison to a baseline using a pre-trained multilingual language model and providing more details on the parameter tuning process would significantly strengthen the paper.



**Score:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)
