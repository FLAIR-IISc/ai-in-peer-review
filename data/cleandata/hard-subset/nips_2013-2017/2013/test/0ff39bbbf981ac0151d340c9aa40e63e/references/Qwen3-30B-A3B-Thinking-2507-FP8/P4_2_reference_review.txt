

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that addresses the challenge of cross-language text classification where labeled data in the target language is scarce. The authors construct a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix where parallel documents have fully observed entries and non-parallel documents have missing values for the other language's vocabulary. They then formulate a matrix completion problem to fill in the missing entries, followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The method is evaluated on 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using the multilingual Amazon product reviews dataset.

The approach relates to previous work in cross-lingual learning (e.g., CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) but differs by filling missing entries before applying dimensionality reduction, rather than applying LSI directly to the incomplete matrix. The authors position their work as requiring fewer parallel documents than methods that translate all training data, and as leveraging word co-occurrence patterns across languages through matrix completion.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach with a rigorous mathematical formulation. The matrix completion problem is well-posed as a convex optimization problem with nonnegativity constraints, and the authors provide a detailed projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence analysis (Theorem 1). The experimental design is comprehensive, with 18 cross-lingual tasks across three target languages (French, German, Japanese) and three product categories (Books, DVD, Music). The results demonstrate significant performance improvements over four baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA), with average accuracy gains of 8.59-14.61% across tasks.

The paper is careful in evaluating both strengths (performance with minimal parallel data) and weaknesses (performance degradation with very small parallel data sets). The convergence analysis provides theoretical guarantees for the optimization algorithm. However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the theoretical justification for why matrix completion followed by LSI outperforms direct LSI on the incomplete matrix.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical formulations are presented with appropriate notation and the algorithm is described step-by-step. The experimental setup is thoroughly explained, including dataset details, parameter selection procedures, and evaluation metrics. The results are presented clearly in tables and figures, with appropriate statistical measures (average accuracy and standard deviations).

Some technical details could be clarified further. For instance, the parameter selection process (using the EFB task to select parameters for all 18 tasks) lacks justification for why this approach is valid across different language pairs and product categories. The paper also doesn't provide sufficient details on the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to alternatives, which would be valuable for practitioners.

## Originality

The core contribution of using matrix completion to fill missing entries before applying LSI represents a novel approach to cross-lingual representation learning. The authors clearly differentiate their method from previous work (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) in Section 2, noting that prior methods apply dimensionality reduction directly to the incomplete matrix, while their method first completes the matrix. This distinction is significant, as the authors demonstrate that filling missing values before dimensionality reduction leads to better representations.

The paper adequately references relevant work at NIPS (e.g., [10, 15, 20] for CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) and other venues. However, the authors do not compare with more recent neural approaches to cross-lingual representation learning (e.g., multilingual BERT, mBERT) that have become prominent in the field since the cited works. This omission is a notable gap, as the paper claims to advance the state of the art in cross-lingual learning.

## Significance

The problem of cross-lingual text classification is important for practical applications where labeled data in target languages is scarce. The paper demonstrates that the proposed method significantly outperforms existing approaches, with improvements of up to 14.61% in accuracy. Crucially, the method requires substantially fewer parallel documents to achieve good performance (as few as 200 unlabeled parallel documents), which is a significant practical advantage.

The empirical results are convincing, with consistent improvements across all 18 cross-lingual tasks. The ablation study on the amount of parallel data (Figure 1 and 2) further demonstrates the method's robustness. The paper's contribution is significant for both theoretical and practical aspects of cross-lingual learning.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel two-step approach (matrix completion followed by LSI) that outperforms existing methods
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across 18 cross-lingual tasks
- Demonstrates robust performance even with very limited parallel data (200 documents)
- Provides theoretical guarantees for the optimization algorithm
- Clear mathematical formulation and algorithm description

**Weaknesses:**
- Does not compare with more recent neural network-based cross-lingual methods
- Parameter selection procedure lacks justification for generalizability across tasks
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and scalability
- Theoretical justification for why matrix completion improves over direct LSI on incomplete data is not sufficiently detailed
- Does not discuss limitations of the approach (e.g., performance with very different language pairs)

## Recommendation

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to cross-lingual representation learning with a novel two-step approach. The experimental results are convincing, and the method demonstrates significant improvements over existing approaches. The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

While the omission of comparison with recent neural approaches is a notable weakness, the paper's contribution is still valuable for the field of cross-lingual learning. The method's ability to work with very limited parallel data represents a meaningful practical advantage.

Given the technical soundness, clear presentation, and significant empirical improvements demonstrated, I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in cross-lingual text classification.
