

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning that first fills missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix using matrix completion, then applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors evaluate their method on 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, demonstrating consistent improvements over several baselines including CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA. The work builds on previous cross-lingual representation learning approaches (particularly CL-LSI [10]) but differs by incorporating matrix completion before LSI.

## Strengths
1. **Problem relevance and motivation**: The paper addresses an important NLP challenge (cross-language text classification) with clear practical motivation for reducing annotation effort in target languages. The matrix completion approach to handle missing features in bilingual document-term matrices is well-motivated by the low-rank structure of such matrices.

2. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The authors conduct comprehensive experiments across 18 different language-category combinations, providing strong empirical evidence of the method's effectiveness. The ablation study on the size of parallel data demonstrates the method's robustness, performing well even with as few as 200 unlabeled parallel documents.

3. **Technical soundness**: The optimization algorithm is well-described with a convergence analysis. The paper presents a clear two-step process (matrix completion followed by LSI) that differs meaningfully from previous approaches.

4. **Practical contribution**: The method's ability to work with minimal parallel data (200 documents) is a significant practical advantage for real-world applications where parallel data is scarce.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art neural methods**: The paper does not compare with recent neural approaches to cross-lingual representation learning (e.g., multilingual BERT, XLM-R, or mT5), which have become the standard for most NLP tasks. This significantly limits the perceived significance of the reported improvements, as the baselines are relatively old methods.

2. **Insufficient justification for matrix completion**: The paper claims that "treating missing features as zeros will lose a lot of information" but fails to provide empirical evidence or a direct comparison with this approach. This is a critical gap in the methodology justification.

3. **Limited discussion of computational complexity**: The paper does not address the computational complexity of the matrix completion step, which could be a significant limitation for large-scale applications, especially given the size of document-term matrices in practice.

4. **Parameter selection is ad-hoc**: The parameter selection process (using a single task to select parameters for all 18 tasks) is not well-justified, raising questions about the generalizability of the selected parameters.

5. **Lack of qualitative analysis**: The paper provides no qualitative analysis of the learned representations (e.g., word embeddings or topic distributions), which would help explain why the method works.

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection, but with the following suggestions for improvement:

1. Compare the proposed method with recent neural cross-lingual approaches (e.g., XLM-R) to demonstrate its standing relative to current state-of-the-art.

2. Provide empirical evidence for the necessity of matrix completion by comparing to a baseline that treats missing features as zeros.

3. Include a discussion of computational complexity and scalability, particularly for large-scale applications.

4. Conduct a more rigorous parameter selection process, potentially using cross-validation across multiple tasks.

5. Add qualitative analysis of the learned representations to provide insights into the method's mechanism.

The paper presents a technically sound approach with promising results, but the lack of comparison with current neural methods and insufficient justification for the matrix completion step significantly limit its impact for NeurIPS. The method's practical advantages are valuable, but without demonstrating how it compares to modern neural approaches, its significance is diminished. With the suggested improvements, particularly adding comparisons with recent neural methods and providing stronger justification for the matrix completion step, the paper could become a strong candidate for acceptance.
