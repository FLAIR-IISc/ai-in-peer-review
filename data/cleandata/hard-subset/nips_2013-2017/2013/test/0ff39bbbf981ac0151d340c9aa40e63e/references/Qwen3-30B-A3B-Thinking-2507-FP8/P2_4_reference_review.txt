

# Review: "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual text classification that first constructs a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix with missing values for non-parallel documents, then fills these missing entries via matrix completion, and finally applies latent semantic indexing to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors compare their approach (TSL) against four baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) on 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews. The results show TSL outperforms all baselines, with improvements of 8.59-14.61% over the target baseline.

The work relates to previous cross-lingual representation learning methods like CL-LSI [10], CL-OPCA [15], and CL-KCCA [20], which also use bilingual document-term matrices. The key distinction is that TSL first completes the missing entries before applying LSI, whereas previous methods apply dimensionality reduction directly to the partially observed matrix.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a rigorous formulation of the matrix completion problem as a convex optimization problem with non-negativity constraints. The convergence proof for the projected gradient descent algorithm (Theorem 1) is well-justified and adds theoretical credibility to the method.

2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The authors conduct extensive experiments across 18 cross-lingual classification tasks (English to French, German, Japanese and vice versa) with multiple language categories. The evaluation of the impact of unlabeled parallel data size (Figure 1-2) is particularly valuable for understanding practical applicability.

3. **Practical Significance**: The paper demonstrates that the proposed method achieves significant improvements even with very few parallel documents (as few as 200), which addresses a key practical limitation in cross-lingual learning where parallel data is often scarce.

4. **Clear Methodology**: The two-step approach is conceptually straightforward and well-motivated, with clear descriptions of both the matrix completion and LSI steps.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty**: The core idea of using matrix completion to fill missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix is not sufficiently distinct from existing approaches. CL-LSI [10] already uses a concatenated document-term matrix (though without matrix completion), and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why their matrix completion step is necessary or provides significant advantage over simply applying LSI to the observed entries.

2. **Incomplete Baseline Comparison**: The comparison is limited to four methods, all from 2010-2015. The authors should have compared against more recent approaches, such as multilingual BERT or other transformer-based cross-lingual models, which have become the state-of-the-art in cross-lingual representation learning. This omission significantly undermines the significance claim.

3. **Lack of Discussion on Limitations**: The paper doesn't discuss potential failure cases or limitations of their approach. For example, how does the method perform when the language pair has very different syntactic structures? How sensitive is it to the quality of the initial parallel documents?

4. **Missing Computational Complexity Analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the matrix completion step, which could be prohibitive for large-scale applications. For a conference like NeurIPS, this is a significant omission.

5. **Limited Dataset Diversity**: The experiments are conducted solely on the Amazon product reviews dataset. Cross-lingual representation learning is a broad field, and results on a single dataset don't adequately demonstrate generalizability.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound approach to cross-lingual representation learning with a clear two-step methodology. The experimental results are convincing within the limited scope of the comparison, and the method's ability to work with minimal parallel data is a practical advantage.

However, the paper's novelty is questionable given that similar approaches (CL-LSI) have been previously proposed, and the method doesn't fundamentally differ from existing work in a way that would justify a NeurIPS publication. The omission of more recent baselines (like multilingual transformers) is particularly problematic for a NeurIPS submission, as it fails to demonstrate the method's standing against current state-of-the-art.

The paper would benefit from:
1. A more thorough comparison with recent cross-lingual representation methods
2. A detailed discussion of limitations and failure cases
3. Analysis of computational complexity
4. Experiments on additional datasets

## Recommendation

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is technically sound and the experimental results are promising within the limited scope, the novelty is insufficient for NeurIPS, and the omission of recent baselines significantly weakens the contribution. The paper represents an incremental improvement over existing work rather than a significant advancement in the field.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments**: The method is well-motivated, the optimization algorithm is theoretically sound, and the results demonstrate practical utility with minimal parallel data.

**Con Acceptance Arguments**: The novelty is questionable, the comparison is outdated, the method doesn't significantly differ from prior work, and the paper fails to demonstrate how it advances the state of the art beyond existing methods.

The paper would be a better fit for a more application-oriented venue like EMNLP or ACL, where the focus is on practical cross-lingual text classification rather than theoretical or methodological advancement. For NeurIPS, it falls short of the expected novelty and significance criteria.
