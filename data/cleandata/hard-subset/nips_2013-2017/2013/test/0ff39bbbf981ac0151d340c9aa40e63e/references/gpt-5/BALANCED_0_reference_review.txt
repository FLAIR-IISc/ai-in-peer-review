Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses cross-language text classification when labeled data in the target language are scarce. The key idea is a two-step representation learning approach: (1) construct a unified bilingual document-term matrix over the concatenated vocabularies; treat non-parallel documents as partially observed and perform matrix completion with a convex objective combining nuclear norm (low-rank), ℓ1 regularization (sparsity), squared losses on observed and missing entries (with weight ρ), and nonnegativity constraints; (2) apply LSI (truncated SVD) to the completed matrix to derive a shared low-dimensional cross-lingual representation, on which a linear SVM is trained. Optimization uses an iterative scheme with a gradient step on the smooth part, singular value shrinkage (prox for nuclear norm), and projection onto the nonnegative orthant, with a convergence argument based on non-expansiveness. Experiments on the Amazon multilingual reviews dataset compare against TBOW (target-only), CL-LSI [Littman et al.], CL-OPCA [Platt et al.], and CL-KCCA [Vinokourov et al.]. The proposed method generally outperforms baselines and shows robustness to small amounts of unlabeled parallel data.

This builds on a well-studied line of cross-lingual representation learning: CL-LSI, KCCA/OPCA, SCL [Prettenhofer & Stein], co-/multi-view learning [Amini et al.], and multilingual topic models. The novel ingredient is framing the bilingual concatenated matrix with missing entries and recovering it via nuclear-norm matrix completion before LSI, rather than applying LSI/KCCA directly to the sparse original.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and potentially broad applicability: the missing-entry view of cross-lingual term co-occurrence is appealing, and the combination of low-rank and sparsity with nonnegativity is well-motivated for TF-IDF data.
- Solid empirical performance across 18 tasks, with consistent gains over classical cross-lingual baselines and robustness when few parallel documents are available.
- The approach uses only unlabeled parallel data (no bilingual dictionaries), which is attractive in low-resource settings.
- The optimization objective is convex, and the algorithm is computationally simple to implement (gradient + shrinkage + projection).

Weaknesses
Quality
- Algorithmic correctness is questionable. The update sequence “gradient step → singular value shrinkage → nonnegativity projection” is not, in general, the proximal operator of the sum γ||M||* + I{M≥0}. Prox of a sum is not the composition of proxes unless special conditions hold. The provided convergence proof only shows that the composed mapping is non-expansive; it does not establish that fixed points are minimizers of the stated convex problem. This is a major theoretical gap. Standard approaches (e.g., ADMM, Dykstra’s algorithm, projected proximal gradient with proper handling of composite nonsmooth terms) would be more appropriate; please either fix the algorithm or provide a rigorous proof that your operator indeed yields an optimal solution of (2).
- Evaluation lacks key ablations that would isolate where the gains come from: (a) using the completed matrix directly vs. after LSI; (b) LSI on the original sparse concatenated matrix with missing treated as zero (to quantify the matrix completion benefit); (c) sensitivity to ρ and µ; (d) effect of removing the ℓ1 term.
- Missing details for reproducibility and scalability: stopping criterion, per-iteration complexity and SVD strategy (full vs. truncated), wall-clock time and memory footprint, behavior with larger vocabularies, and how τ is selected beyond the bound depending on ρ.
- Representation learning uses “all documents from the two languages” including test instances. While the baselines also use unlabeled target data, please clarify the transductive setup and ensure all methods use exactly the same unlabeled data for representation learning. Also include a setting where only training (unlabeled) data are used.
- Baselines are dated. Given the current state of the field, comparisons to strong modern baselines (e.g., mBERT/XLM-R fine-tuned with 100 target labels, translate-train/translate-test pipelines, bilingual/multilingual sentence embeddings such as LASER/LaBSE) are needed to assess significance.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written. However, several points need clarification: (i) why the specific step-size bound τ < min(2, 2/ρ)? Provide a Lipschitz constant derivation for ∇g; (ii) justify the loss on missing entries (ρ-weighted squared penalty toward zero) and discuss the bias it induces; (iii) clarify initialization and its effect; (iv) fix minor typos (“if only if”).
- Report the actual numbers in Table 1 and include significance tests, not just “outperforms on 17/18 tasks.”

Originality
- The idea of applying matrix completion to bilingual document-term matrices is interesting and, to my knowledge, not standard in cross-lingual classification. However, both components (nuclear-norm completion and LSI) are well-known; the novelty lies in their combination and the missing-data formulation. The optimization scheme itself follows known shrinkage/projection heuristics.

Significance
- On the Amazon dataset and classical baselines, the method appears effective. However, without ablations and stronger baselines, it is hard to assess the broader impact. The scalability and practicality for larger corpora/vocabularies are also unclear.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually neat formulation connecting cross-lingual co-occurrence with matrix completion.
- Consistent empirical gains on a standard benchmark; good performance with few parallel pairs.
- Uses only unlabeled parallel data; simple pipeline.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical flaw/gap: the proposed solver does not match the stated composite objective; convergence to the true minimizer is not established.
- Missing ablations and incomplete experimental details.
- Lack of comparisons to contemporary cross-lingual models and translation-based baselines.
- Transductive use of test instances for representation learning not fully discussed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a correct optimization algorithm with proof (e.g., ADMM handling both nuclear norm and nonnegativity; or show that your composed operator equals the prox of γ||M||* + I{M≥0}).
- Add ablations: LSI vs. no LSI, zero-filled LSI baseline, sensitivity to ρ/µ/γ/τ, and effect of initialization.
- Include runtime/memory and truncated SVD details; report convergence curves.
- Clarify the transductive setup and add a setting limited to training-only unlabeled data.
- Compare against modern baselines (mBERT/XLM-R, LASER/LaBSE, translate-train/test) under the same label budget.
- Release code or detailed pseudo-code with stopping criteria.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is promising and empirically encouraging, but the optimization issue and missing modern baselines/ablations prevent acceptance at NeurIPS standards. Confidence: high.
