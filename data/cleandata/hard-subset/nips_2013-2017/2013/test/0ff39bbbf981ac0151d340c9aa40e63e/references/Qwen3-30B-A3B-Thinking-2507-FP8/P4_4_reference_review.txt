

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that leverages unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to learn cross-lingual representations. The approach first constructs a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix where parallel documents are fully observed while non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language's vocabulary. It then fills missing entries through matrix completion (using a convex optimization formulation) before applying latent semantic indexing to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The method differs from prior approaches like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA that directly apply dimensionality reduction on the original concatenated matrix. The authors evaluate their method on 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews across English, French, German, and Japanese.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness (Quality):** The paper presents a theoretically grounded approach with a well-formulated optimization problem for matrix completion. The authors provide a convergence proof for their projected gradient descent algorithm (Theorem 1), demonstrating technical rigor. The experimental design is comprehensive, including 18 cross-language tasks, multiple language pairs, and a thorough analysis of the impact of unlabeled parallel data size. The comparison with four strong baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) is appropriate and well-executed.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The methodology section (Section 3) provides sufficient mathematical details for understanding the approach, including the matrix completion formulation and the LSI step. Algorithm 1 is clearly presented, and the experimental setup (Section 5.1) provides adequate details for reproducibility. The presentation of results in Table 1 and Figures 1-2 is clear and effectively supports the claims.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear distinction from prior work by using matrix completion to fill missing entries before applying LSI, rather than applying dimensionality reduction directly on the incomplete matrix as in CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA. The related work section (Section 2) properly contextualizes this contribution and correctly identifies the differences from previous methods. The approach appears novel in the context of cross-lingual representation learning.

**Significance:** The empirical results are compelling, showing consistent improvements over baselines across all 18 tasks. The method achieves up to 14.61% higher accuracy than the baseline TBOW and outperforms all other methods in 17 out of 18 tasks. The finding that the method works effectively even with as few as 200 unlabeled parallel documents (compared to the 2000 used in other methods) represents a significant practical advantage, as obtaining large parallel corpora is often difficult.

## Weaknesses

**Theoretical Novelty:** While the matrix completion approach is novel for this specific application, the paper does not sufficiently justify why matrix completion is particularly suitable for this problem beyond the general low-rank assumption. The connection to existing work on matrix completion (e.g., Candès & Recht, 2008) could be strengthened, and the paper does not discuss why the specific optimization formulation (with the ℓ1 norm and trace norm) is better suited for this problem than other matrix completion approaches.

**Experimental Scope:** The evaluation is limited to sentiment classification on Amazon product reviews. While this is a standard benchmark, the paper would benefit from additional experiments on different tasks or datasets to demonstrate broader applicability. The paper does not explore the method's performance with more than two languages or with languages that have significant linguistic differences.

**Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper uses a fixed set of parameters for all experiments after initial tuning on one task (EFB). It would be valuable to see how sensitive the results are to parameter choices across different language pairs and tasks, particularly regarding the choice of γ, ρ, and k.

**Comparison with Recent Work:** The related work section does not adequately reference more recent NIPS papers on cross-lingual representation learning, such as those using neural networks for cross-lingual transfer (e.g., "Cross-Lingual Transfer Learning for Text Classification" from NIPS 2017). This is a notable omission given the rapid advances in neural approaches to this problem.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Clear novelty in the two-step approach (matrix completion before LSI)
- Strong empirical results demonstrating significant improvements over multiple baselines
- Theoretical contribution with convergence proof for the optimization algorithm
- Practical significance with demonstrated effectiveness even with very limited parallel data
- Well-structured and clearly written paper

**Con Acceptance:**
- Limited to sentiment classification task on Amazon product reviews
- Does not compare with recent neural approaches to cross-lingual representation learning
- Theoretical novelty could be better justified in relation to matrix completion literature
- No analysis of computational complexity or scalability to larger datasets
- The paper does not address the potential for the method to be extended to other representation learning tasks

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a technically sound and well-executed approach to cross-lingual representation learning with clear empirical advantages over existing methods. The method's ability to achieve strong performance with minimal parallel data is particularly noteworthy. While the theoretical novelty could be better contextualized, the practical contributions are significant. The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, and significance, though it could benefit from broader experimental validation and comparison with more recent neural approaches. The method represents a meaningful advancement in cross-lingual representation learning that could be valuable to practitioners and researchers. I recommend acceptance.
