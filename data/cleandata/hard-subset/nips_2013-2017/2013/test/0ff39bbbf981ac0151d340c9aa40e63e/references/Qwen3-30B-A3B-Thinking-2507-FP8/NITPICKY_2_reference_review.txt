

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that first constructs a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix with missing values for non-parallel documents, then fills these missing entries using matrix completion, and finally applies LSI to the completed matrix for classification. The authors evaluate their method on Amazon product reviews across multiple language pairs.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a clear and logical approach to cross-lingual text classification, and the experimental results show consistent improvements over baselines across multiple language pairs. The method is well-motivated by the need to reduce reliance on large amounts of parallel data, and the empirical results demonstrate that their approach can achieve good performance with very limited parallel documents.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical Errors and Inconsistencies:** The paper contains several technical errors that undermine the credibility of the method. Equation (1) has a formatting error: "Mij = M 0 ij" should be "Mij = M0ij" (missing space). More critically, the claim that "the non-smooth ℓ1 norm regularizer in the objective function of (2) is equivalent to a smooth linear function ‖M‖1 = ∑ ij Mij" is factually incorrect. The ℓ1 norm is not equivalent to a linear function; it's the sum of absolute values. With non-negativity constraints, it does become a linear function, but the phrasing is misleading and suggests a fundamental misunderstanding of the optimization problem.

2. **Citation Mismatches:** The paper contains multiple citation mismatches between the text and references. For example, [1] in the text refers to "multi-view co-classification method for multilingual document categorization," but the reference entry is "A co-classification approach to learning from multilingual corpora." Similarly, [2] in the text refers to "multilingual document categorization," but the reference is "Learning from multiple partially observed views - an application to multilingual text categorization." These inconsistencies suggest the authors did not carefully verify their references against the text.

3. **Algorithmic Implementation Details:** The paper claims to use "the nonnegative projection of the rank-1 approximation of M0" for initialization, but fails to specify how this approximation is computed. Without this detail, the method cannot be reproduced, which violates NIPS's standard for reproducibility. Additionally, the paper states "0 < τ < min(2, 2 ρ )" in multiple places, but in the proof of Lemma 1, it should be "0 < τ < min(2, 2/ρ)" (the division symbol is missing).

4. **Experimental Design Flaws:** The paper uses "default parameter setting" for SVM without any justification or explanation of how these parameters were selected. This is a significant omission in experimental design, as SVM parameters substantially affect classification performance. The paper also fails to report statistical significance tests for the performance improvements, making it difficult to assess whether the observed improvements are meaningful.

5. **Inconsistent Terminology:** The paper inconsistently uses "cross-language" and "cross-lingual" throughout the text, which creates confusion for readers. For example, in the introduction, it says "cross language text classification" but later uses "cross-lingual representation learning" without explanation.

6. **Parameter Selection Justification:** The paper states "we used the first task EFB to perform model parameter selection" but fails to explain why EFB was chosen over other tasks. This is not a standard practice in cross-lingual evaluation, as different language pairs may have different characteristics that could affect parameter selection.

7. **Reference Formatting:** The references contain inconsistent formatting, with some entries using full journal names and others using conference abbreviations. This is unprofessional for a NIPS submission.

**Conclusion:** While the paper presents a potentially interesting approach to cross-lingual representation learning, the numerous technical errors, citation mismatches, and methodological inconsistencies significantly undermine its credibility. The paper fails to meet NIPS's standards for technical soundness and reproducibility. The authors should carefully revise the mathematical formulations, verify all citations, provide complete implementation details, and address the statistical significance of their results before resubmission. Given the severity of these issues, I recommend rejection.
