Summary
The paper addresses cross-language text classification by proposing a two-step method (TSL) to learn cross-lingual document representations using a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 fills the missing entries of a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix via convex matrix completion with trace norm, L1 sparsity, squared losses on observed entries, a small penalty on unobserved entries, and nonnegativity constraints, solved by a projected gradient descent with singular value thresholding and nonnegativity projection. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) on the recovered matrix and trains a linear SVM in the induced low-dimensional space. Experiments on the multilingual Amazon reviews dataset across 18 English↔{French,German,Japanese} sentiment classification tasks show consistent gains over CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, and substantial improvements over a target-only BOW baseline. The method is reported to be robust to small numbers of parallel documents.

Relation to prior work
The method is closest to CL-LSI [Littman et al.] and subsequent representation learning approaches (CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) that perform dimension reduction on concatenated bilingual matrices or paired corpora. The novelty here is to first treat the concatenated bilingual document-term matrix with missing entries for non-parallel documents as a matrix completion problem before applying LSI. Compared with SCL [Prettenhofer & Stein], bilingual lexicon/parallel-topic approaches, and multi-view/co-training methods, TSL leverages only small parallel corpora without translation of all training documents. The optimization uses standard proximal methods for trace norm minimization (e.g., fixed-point/Bregman iterative ideas from Ma et al.) with an added nonnegativity projection.

Strengths
- Simple, clear formulation that integrates sparsity, low-rank structure, and nonnegativity, with a convergence proof for the projected proximal scheme.
- Conceptually appealing: completion can propagate cross-lingual co-occurrence structure from a small set of parallel pairs into the missing blocks, then LSI denoises further.
- Empirical coverage: 18 cross-lingual tasks in both directions over three domains; consistent improvements over established cross-lingual representation baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA).
- Robustness to small parallel sets: performance degrades less than competitors when the number of parallel pairs is reduced to 200.

Weaknesses
- Missing strong modern baselines. No comparisons to MT-based pipelines (translate-then-train), cross-lingual word/document embeddings (e.g., mapping-based methods), or multilingual pretrained models (e.g., mBERT/XLM-R fine-tuning with source+100 target labels), which are now standard and often strong in low-resource cross-lingual transfer.
- Limited ablations. While CL-LSI serves as a “no-completion” comparator, there is no “completion-only” ablation (training directly on the completed matrix), nor exploration of the importance of the nonnegativity, L1 term, or the penalty ρ on missing entries.
- Scalability and efficiency not addressed. The algorithm performs an SVD-based shrinkage at each iteration over a potentially large t×d matrix. There is no reporting of runtime, memory footprint, number of iterations, or use of partial SVD—raising concerns about scalability to larger corpora or vocabularies.
- Theory is standard and limited to convergence. No problem-specific insights (e.g., identifiability/conditions under which cross-lingual information is recoverable via completion, sensitivity to parallel coverage).
- Experimental design details lacking. Table/Figure values are not shown here; significance tests are not reported. Hyperparameters are tuned on a single task and reused for all, but sensitivity analyses (γ, ρ, k, μ) are minimal; μ is fixed arbitrarily. No error analysis or per-language vocabulary sizes are provided.
- Narrow application scope: only sentiment classification on Amazon reviews; unclear generality to other text classification tasks or less-related language pairs.

Quality
- Technically sound convex formulation; the proximal gradient scheme with singular value thresholding and nonnegativity projection is appropriate and the non-expansiveness-based convergence argument follows prior work.
- Claims of empirical gains over CL baselines are plausible given the design; however, without comparisons to modern cross-lingual transfer baselines and without runtime/scalability analysis, it is hard to assess practical impact.
- The role and optimal setting of ρ (penalizing deviation from zero on unobserved entries) is not well justified; large ρ could bias against completion, small ρ makes completion dominant—this deserves analysis.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. The construction of M0 and the handling of parallel vs. non-parallel rows is clear. Algorithm 1 is concise.
- Some references are incomplete; a few typos and inconsistent citation formats. More detail on stopping criteria, SVD implementation (full vs. truncated), and preprocessing (vocabulary sizes, tokenization per language) would help reproducibility.

Originality
- The two-step idea (completion before LSI) is a novel combination in this context, though both components are standard. Compared to CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA, the main novelty is imputing the missing bilingual blocks in a convex low-rank+sparse manner before dimension reduction.

Significance
- On the chosen dataset and baselines, the improvements appear consistent. However, significance is limited without comparisons to stronger modern baselines and without demonstrating scalability. The approach could be useful in low-parallel-data settings if it scales and still beats multilingual transformers/MT pipelines.

Questions for the authors
- How does TSL compare to MT-based transfer (translate target to source or vice versa, then train SVM) and to multilingual PLMs (e.g., fine-tuned mBERT/XLM-R with source + 100 target labels)? Please include these.
- What are the runtime, number of iterations, and memory usage per task? Do you use truncated SVD? How does performance vary with the rank k in SVD?
- Ablations: (a) completion-only (no LSI), (b) no L1 term, (c) no nonnegativity, (d) varying ρ across orders of magnitude. How sensitive are results?
- How many parallel pairs are actually needed to propagate cross-lingual structure when languages have disjoint vocabularies? Any failure cases with non-parallel domains?
- Can you report results on another dataset/task (topic classification, news categorization) to demonstrate generality?

Pros
- Clear, convex formulation with a simple algorithm and convergence guarantee.
- Consistent empirical gains over classical cross-lingual representation baselines.
- Robust to small parallel corpora.

Cons
- Lacks comparisons to modern, strong baselines (MT and multilingual PLMs).
- No scalability/runtime analysis; algorithm may be expensive at scale.
- Limited ablations and analysis of hyperparameters; theory limited to standard convergence.

Minor comments
- Clarify initialization choice (rank-1 approximation vs. using M0 with zeros); justify empirically.
- Provide precise stopping criteria and tolerance.
- Clean up citations (years/venues missing in many).

Reproducibility
- Dataset is public; SVM and SVD are standard; most hyperparameters are given. Code release and more training details (stopping, SVD variant) would substantially help.

Recommendation
- Lean reject. The idea is sound and results versus classical baselines are promising, but for NeurIPS standards the paper needs (i) comparisons against modern cross-lingual transfer baselines, (ii) ablations and sensitivity analyses, and (iii) scalability/runtime evidence. With these additions, the contribution could be compelling, especially for low-parallel settings. Confidence: medium.
