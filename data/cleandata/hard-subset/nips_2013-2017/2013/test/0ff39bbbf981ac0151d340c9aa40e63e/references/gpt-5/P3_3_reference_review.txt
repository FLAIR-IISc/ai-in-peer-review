Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-lingual text classification that exploits a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 performs matrix completion on a unified document-term matrix formed by concatenating vocabularies across two languages. Parallel pairs yield fully observed rows; non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language. The completion problem is formulated as a convex optimization with a nuclear norm (low-rank), an ℓ1 penalty (sparsity), squared loss on observed entries, and a small quadratic penalty on missing entries, under non-negativity constraints. The authors solve it with a projected gradient descent procedure with singular value shrinkage and non-negativity projection, and provide a convergence argument. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to derive a low-dimensional shared representation, on which a linear SVM is trained. On Amazon multilingual reviews (EN/FR/DE/JA), the method (TSL) outperforms TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, and is reported to be robust even with as few as 200 parallel documents.

Relation to prior work
The approach is most closely related to CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA and multilingual topic models that induce shared representations, and to methods that translate data to align feature spaces. The novelty claimed over CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA is to first impute the cross-lingual co-occurrence structure via matrix completion before performing LSI, rather than running LSI/CCA directly on concatenated or translated matrices. The paper cites SCL [Prettenhofer & Stein], co-training, and domain adaptation work, but does not compare to SCL empirically. It also does not discuss more recent cross-lingual representations (e.g., bilingual word embeddings or multilingual transformers), though the core setting differs (uses small parallel corpora, not bilingual lexicons or MT).

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and intuitive: cast cross-lingual alignment as imputing missing cross-language features, then compress via LSI.
- Uses only a small number of unlabeled parallel documents; shows robustness to very small parallel sizes in most settings.
- Formulation is convex (after relaxation), with reasonable regularization choices (nuclear norm, small ℓ1, non-negativity, and a shrinkage toward zero for missing entries).
- Empirical results across 18 tasks indicate consistent gains over three mainstream cross-lingual baselines, with a simple downstream classifier.
- Clear articulation of the data construction (unified vocabulary, partial observations) and the loss terms.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical correctness of the optimization algorithm: the objective comprises a smooth term, a nuclear norm, and a non-negativity constraint (indicator). The proposed iteration performs gradient step → nuclear shrinkage → projection onto non-negativity. In general, the proximal operator of a sum of two non-smooth terms does not equal the composition of their proximal operators; the non-expansiveness arguments provided are insufficient to conclude convergence to the true minimizer of the constrained problem. Standard treatments would use a single proximal for γ||·||* + I(M≥0), Dykstra’s algorithm, or ADMM/Primal-Dual methods. The current proof sketch (via [11]) appears to rely on results that do not cover the additional non-negativity projection after shrinkage.
- Claims about low-rank nature of document-term matrices are overstated. While sparse, such matrices are typically high-rank; nuclear norm here acts as a denoising/structure-inducing prior rather than exploiting an inherent low rank. The paper should more carefully motivate why low-rank structure is expected and when the relaxation is helpful.
- Methodological details and ablations are sparse: no analysis of the influence of µ (ℓ1), non-negativity, ρ on missing entries, or whether step 2 (LSI) is needed after nuclear-norm completion (e.g., compare SVM on M* vs on Z). Since µ is set to 1e-6, ℓ1 likely has negligible effect—why include it?
- Computational scalability is not addressed. Each iteration requires an SVD; with large vocabularies, this is expensive. There is no discussion of truncated/randomized SVD, ranks obtained, per-iteration runtime, or convergence speed.
- Evaluation protocol appears transductive: “all documents from the two languages” (including test) are used for representation learning. This is common in representation learning baselines (CL-LSI/OPCA), but should be clearly stated and discussed. Results with only training + unlabeled parallel data would strengthen the case.
- Baselines: Missing strong and relevant baselines such as SCL [16], “translate-train/test” with a modern MT system, or bilingual word-embedding projection (even if the paper’s goal is small parallel data). This makes it hard to assess practical significance.
- Reporting: Table/Figures are referenced but not included; no statistical significance tests; no per-task effect sizes presented here.

Quality
The empirical work suggests the method is competitive in the intended regime, but the theoretical convergence proof is incomplete for the composite non-smooth case. Important practical details (runtime, rank, convergence diagnostics) and ablations are missing. Claims are generally plausible but somewhat stronger than the evidence presented.

Clarity
Writing is mostly clear and well organized. Some notation/typos should be fixed (e.g., define c(x,y)=1/2||x−y||^2 explicitly; minor formatting issues). Provide algorithmic complexity and practical implementation choices (e.g., truncated SVD).

Originality
Using matrix completion with nuclear norm plus non-negativity on a bilingual concatenated matrix, followed by LSI, is an interesting combination not standard in prior CL-LSI/KCCA work. However, each component is known; the novelty is the particular two-step pipeline and the treatment of missing entries.

Significance
If robust and efficient, the approach is useful for low-resource cross-lingual classification with small parallel corpora. However, without comparisons to stronger modern baselines and without runtime/scalability analysis, the broader impact is uncertain.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a correct convergence guarantee for the presence of both nuclear norm and non-negativity constraint (or switch to ADMM/primal-dual with proof)?
- How critical are non-negativity and ℓ1? Please include ablations.
- What are the per-iteration costs, number of iterations, and total runtimes vs. baselines? Do you use truncated/randomized SVD?
- Does performance degrade if you restrict representation learning to training + unlabeled parallel data (no test documents)?
- Compare SVM on M* vs. on Z to justify the second LSI step.
- Include SCL, translate-train/test, and a bilingual embedding baseline if possible.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important cross-lingual setting with limited parallel data.
- Simple, generic method with strong empirical gains over classical baselines.
- Potentially useful idea (impute cross-lingual co-occurrence before dimensionality reduction).

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical convergence claim appears insufficient for the constrained composite objective.
- Missing key baselines and ablations; no scalability analysis.
- Transductive evaluation and incomplete reporting limit the strength of the evidence.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The idea is promising and empirically encouraging, but the optimization theory and experimental rigor need strengthening before publication at NeurIPS.
