

# Review for "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that first fills missing entries in a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix using matrix completion, then applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors claim this approach outperforms existing methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA on cross-language sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews. The work relates to prior NIPS contributions such as CL-LSI (Gao et al., 2007) and CL-KCCA (Platt et al., 2007), which also use bilingual document-term matrices but apply LSI or KCCA directly without filling missing entries. The paper also connects to more recent work on cross-lingual learning, though it misses some key contemporary developments.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-structured matrix completion formulation with theoretical convergence analysis for their projected gradient descent algorithm. The optimization problem is well-motivated and the convergence proof is technically sound.

2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The authors conduct comprehensive experiments across 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks, comparing against multiple baselines. They also investigate the impact of varying amounts of unlabeled parallel data, providing valuable insights into data efficiency.

3. **Clear Methodology**: The two-step approach (matrix completion followed by LSI) is logically presented, and the experimental setup is well-documented with appropriate parameter selection procedures.

4. **Practical Contribution**: The demonstration that the method performs well even with very small amounts of parallel data (200 documents) is a significant practical contribution for resource-constrained settings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper fails to compare with modern cross-lingual representation learning approaches, particularly multilingual pre-trained language models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R), which have become the new standard for cross-lingual tasks. This is a major oversight in the field, as these models have consistently outperformed traditional methods on cross-lingual benchmarks. The authors should compare against at least one such model to properly contextualize their contribution.

2. **Insufficient Justification for Approach**: The paper claims that "treating missing features as zeros certainly will lose a lot of information" but doesn't provide empirical evidence or theoretical analysis to support this claim. A comparison of the proposed method against a simple baseline that treats missing entries as zeros would strengthen their argument.

3. **Limited Analysis of Matrix Completion**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of why matrix completion is particularly well-suited for this task compared to other approaches (e.g., imputation with average values, or using only observed entries). The authors should clarify the specific advantages of their matrix completion approach.

4. **Outdated Dataset**: The use of the Amazon product reviews dataset (from 2010s) is problematic, as more recent cross-lingual benchmarks like XTREME or XNLI have become standard. The paper should either compare on these benchmarks or justify why the Amazon dataset remains relevant for this contribution.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a comparison against at least one modern multilingual pre-trained model (e.g., XLM-R) to properly position the contribution within current research.

2. Conduct an ablation study comparing the performance of the proposed method with and without the matrix completion step (i.e., treating missing entries as zeros).

3. Provide more detailed analysis of the matrix completion component, explaining why this particular formulation (with trace norm and ℓ1 regularization) is advantageous for this specific task.

4. Update the experimental evaluation to use more recent cross-lingual benchmarks (e.g., XTREME) to demonstrate broader relevance.

5. Clarify the relationship between the proposed method and recent work on cross-lingual representation learning, particularly addressing how their approach differs from or complements modern neural approaches.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a technically sound approach to cross-lingual representation learning with a clear two-step methodology and thorough experimental evaluation. The paper demonstrates good performance on a standard dataset and shows that the method works well with limited parallel data. However, the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods (particularly multilingual pre-trained models) significantly undermines the paper's contribution and relevance to current research in the field. The significance of the work is limited because it doesn't address the dominant paradigm in cross-lingual learning today.

I recommend **rejection** with the understanding that the paper could be significantly strengthened with the suggested revisions. The core idea of using matrix completion for cross-lingual representation learning has merit, but the paper fails to properly position it within the current landscape of cross-lingual learning research. With the suggested revisions—particularly adding comparisons to modern baselines—the paper could become a strong contribution to the field.
