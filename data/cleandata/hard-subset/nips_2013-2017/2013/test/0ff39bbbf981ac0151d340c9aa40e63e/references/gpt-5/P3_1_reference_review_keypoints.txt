- Paper proposes TSL: convex matrix-completion (nuclear norm + ℓ1 + observation loss + small missing-entry penalty + nonnegativity) followed by LSI and linear SVM for cross-lingual classification.
- Algorithm: iterative gradient step for smooth part, singular-value shrinkage for nuclear norm, and projection onto nonnegative orthant.
- Empirically, TSL outperforms CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA and a target-only baseline on 18 Amazon cross-lingual sentiment tasks, especially in low-parallel regimes.
- Strength: realistic problem setting with few parallel pairs and many non-parallel documents.
- Strength: principled convex objective and a simple prox-like iterative pipeline.
- Strength: extensive empirical evaluation across 18 tasks with ablations on amount of parallel data.
- Strength: hyperparameters chosen on one task and reused, reducing per-task overfitting risk.
- Strength: robust empirical performance in low-parallel-data scenarios.
- Major concern: optimization correctness—two nonsmooth terms (nuclear norm and nonnegativity) make the proposed operator composition not necessarily a valid proximal update; convergence to minimizer is not established.
- Requested fix: provide a correct convergence proof using three-operator splitting (Condat–Vu/Davis–Yin) or Dykstra’s method, or absorb nonnegativity into the prox.
- Concern: ℓ1 penalty under nonnegativity is linear (sum of entries) and does not induce sparsity in the usual sense; claim of sparsity is misleading—ablate µ including µ=0.
- Concern: penalizing missing entries toward zero biases completion; need theoretical/empirical justification and sensitivity analysis for ρ.
- Concern: redundancy of two-step pipeline—completion already enforces low rank; ablate/use direct top-k factors from completion versus separate LSI.
- Concern: transductive setup includes target test documents in representation learning; provide inductive experiments excluding test documents.
- Missing baselines: include SCL and translate-train/translate-test (MT or dictionary) baselines, and optionally bilingual embeddings.
- Scalability/practicality concern: each iteration requires (truncated) SVD on a large sparse matrix; no complexity analysis, runtimes, iteration counts, stopping criterion, or memory usage reported.
- Reproducibility gaps: unclear TF-IDF computation (joint vs per-language), document normalization, tokenization for non-Latin scripts, stopping/step-size choices.
- Quality assessment: technically promising but optimization analysis is incomplete; empirical breadth is good but missing key baselines and ablations.
- Clarity assessment: generally well written, but convergence claim and some experimental details need clarification.
- Originality: novel combination (bilingual completion + LSI) but largely an incremental mix of known components.
- Significance: potentially useful in low-parallel-data regimes if empirical gains hold under stronger baselines and inductive settings.
- Suggestions: add correct convergence analysis or use standard splitting; include SCL and translation baselines; report runtimes, SVD ranks, memory; run inductive experiments; ablate µ, ρ, and the LSI step; clarify preprocessing/TF-IDF; analyze when cross-lingual correspondences emerge.
- Pros for acceptance: addresses an important problem, consistent empirical gains, convex formulation with simple algorithmic steps.
- Cons for acceptance: incorrect/incomplete convergence claim, missing baselines/ablations, transductive evaluation bias, and no scalability evidence.
- Recommendation: borderline reject; revise optimization analysis and expand experiments/ablations to become a solid contribution.
