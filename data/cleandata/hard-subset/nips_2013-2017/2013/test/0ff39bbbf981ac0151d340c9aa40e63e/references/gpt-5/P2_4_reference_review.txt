Summary
The paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-lingual text classification using a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 constructs a unified document-term matrix over the concatenated vocabularies of two languages and completes missing entries via a convex matrix completion objective with a trace norm (for low-rank), an ℓ1 term (for sparsity), squared losses on observed and missing entries (the latter with a weight ρ), and a nonnegativity constraint. The authors solve this with a projected gradient descent algorithm that alternates a gradient step, singular value thresholding (shrinkage), and projection onto the nonnegative orthant, and provide a convergence argument. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) on the completed matrix to produce a low-dimensional shared representation, on which a linear SVM is trained. Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews (EN–FR/DE/JA) across 18 transfer tasks show the method generally outperforms TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, and is robust when the number of parallel pairs is small.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against two lines: (i) translation-based or multi-view methods that translate or co-train across views; and (ii) cross-lingual representation learning using parallel/comparable corpora (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, multilingual topic models). The novelty here is to impute the unified doc-term matrix via matrix completion before running LSI, rather than applying LSI/KCCA/OPCA directly to the (sparse, partially aligned) concatenated matrix. The literature review is reasonably comprehensive for these families.

Quality
- Modeling: The objective is sensible and interpretable: nuclear norm encourages low-rank structure in co-occurrence; the ℓ1 term with nonnegativity encourages sparsity; losses on observed/missing entries control fit and shrinkage, respectively. Using parallel pairs to couple the vocabularies is principled.
- Optimization: The algorithm is a proximal-gradient-like scheme, but there is a technical concern. The problem has two nonsmooth terms: γ‖M‖∗ and the indicator of the nonnegative orthant. The proposed update applies the proximal map of the nuclear norm (shrinkage) and then the projection (prox of indicator) sequentially. In general, the prox of a sum is not equal to the composition of the individual prox operators unless special conditions hold. The provided convergence proof argues non-expansiveness of each operator and of their composition, and cites [11], but it does not show that the fixed point of this composed operator is the minimizer of the stated composite objective with both nonsmooth parts. A more standard route would use a splitting method (e.g., Douglas–Rachford/ADMM) or the proximal of the sum if available. As written, the theoretical guarantee appears incomplete for the constrained problem; this should be clarified or corrected.
- Theory details: The loss c(x, y) is written ambiguously as “12‖x − y‖.” I assume 1/2 (x−y)^2; please correct notation. The step-size condition τ < min(2, 2/ρ) emerges from a non-expansiveness proof; that’s fine, but a convergence rate (e.g., O(1/k)) for the smooth+nonsmooth case would be useful.
- Empirics: The experimental setup is standard and thorough across 18 tasks, and the gains are consistent. However:
  - No ablation isolates the effect of matrix completion vs. simply running LSI on the raw concatenated matrix (beyond CL-LSI, which may not be exactly the same setting). A direct comparison “LSI on raw vs. LSI on completed M” with identical preprocessing would strengthen the case that step 1 is the key driver.
  - No runtime/scalability analysis is given, despite repeated SVDs per iteration; practical complexity vs. CL-LSI/CL-KCCA would be helpful.
  - The representation is learned on all documents (including target test data), i.e., transductive. This is common in representation learning but should be clearly acknowledged, and a setting using only training+parallel data would be informative.
  - Missing stronger baselines: a simple MT-based translate-test or translate-train baseline (using available MT) and modern bilingual embeddings are absent; the former is a common point of comparison in this literature.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-structured. Algorithmic details are mostly present. Minor issues:
- Clarify c(x, y) and all norms on scalars vs. matrices.
- Explain initialization and stopping criteria; provide pseudo-code with convergence checks.
- Justify the choice of τ, γ, ρ; provide sensitivity plots (beyond a single selection on EFB).
- Expand the description of how CL-LSI and CL-OPCA baselines are instantiated in this dataset (especially how parallel pairs are used, and whether non-parallel docs are treated as missing or zero).

Originality
Moderate. Applying convex matrix completion with nonnegativity and sparsity to a concatenated bilingual doc-term matrix, followed by LSI, is a novel combination in this specific cross-lingual setting. The components themselves (nuclear-norm completion, LSI) are well-known. The contribution is more architectural than algorithmic.

Significance
Empirically, the method improves over several established cross-lingual representation approaches across many tasks and is robust with few parallel pairs, which is practically meaningful. The significance would be strengthened by:
- Clearer theoretical guarantees for the constrained optimization.
- Stronger and more modern baselines (MT, bilingual embeddings) and ablations.
- Runtime/scalability evidence.

Pros
- Simple, intuitive two-step pipeline; easy to reuse monolingual classifiers.
- Consistent gains across 18 tasks; robustness with limited parallel data.
- Uses weak supervision (unlabeled parallel pairs), avoiding full translation.

Cons
- The convergence proof for the constrained problem seems incomplete; sequential prox steps do not generally solve the sum of nonsmooth terms.
- Transductive evaluation; no analysis with only training unlabeled data.
- Missing ablations and stronger baselines; no computational cost analysis.
- Some notational imprecision.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide a correct convergence guarantee (or adopt a splitting method) for the objective with both nuclear norm and nonnegativity constraint?
- How much of the gain comes from matrix completion vs. LSI alone? Please add a direct ablation.
- What are the wall-clock times and memory vs. CL-LSI/CL-KCCA on your largest tasks?
- How sensitive is performance to γ, ρ, μ, and k? Any principled way to set ρ?
- Can the method work in an inductive setting without access to test documents during representation learning?

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important problem and presents a conceptually appealing approach with promising empirical results. However, I have reservations about the rigor of the optimization/convergence treatment and the lack of ablations and stronger baselines. With clarifications and additional experiments, this could be a solid contribution. As is, I view it as borderline; I lean toward acceptance if the theoretical gap is addressed, otherwise weak reject.
