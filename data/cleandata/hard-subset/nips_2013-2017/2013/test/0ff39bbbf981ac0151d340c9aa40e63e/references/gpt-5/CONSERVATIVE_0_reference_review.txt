Summary
The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual document representation. Step 1 performs matrix completion on a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix where unpaired documents have entire blocks of missing entries for the other language. The objective combines a trace norm (low rank), an ℓ1 term (sparsity, which reduces to a linear term under nonnegativity), squared losses on observed entries and on missing entries (weighted by ρ), and a nonnegativity constraint. It is optimized via a projected gradient descent scheme that alternates a gradient step, singular value shrinkage, and projection to the nonnegative orthant. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) on the recovered matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations, on which a linear SVM is trained. Experiments on Amazon Reviews (EN, FR, DE, JA; Books/DVD/Music) compare against TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, claiming superior performance and robustness to limited parallel data.

Relation to prior work
The approach sits among representation-learning methods that leverage parallel/comparable data: CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL/Kernel CCA, multilingual topic models, and SCL. The claimed novelty is to first “complete” the concatenated matrix before LSI, rather than directly applying LSI/CCA/OPCA. However, matrix completion/low-rank modeling has been widely studied, and the paper does not provide theoretical justification for recovery under the highly structured missingness pattern induced by cross-lingual concatenation.

Strengths
- Clear high-level motivation: filling structured missing entries could better exploit cross-lingual co-occurrence than zero-filling.
- Simple algorithmic pipeline (PGD + shrinkage + nonnegativity) that is easy to implement.
- Uses a standard benchmark and multiple language pairs/categories.
- Some care in discussing the optimization procedure and a convergence argument for the iterative scheme (non-expansiveness lemmas).

Weaknesses
Technical
- The recovery assumptions are not addressed. Classical matrix completion guarantees require incoherence and (approximately) random missingness. Here, missingness is block-structured (entire halves of rows are unobserved for non-parallel docs), with only a small set of parallel rows fully observed. The paper gives no conditions under which the problem is identifiable or when the method should work.
- The convergence proof is not fully convincing for the stated objective. The algorithm alternates gradient, nuclear-norm shrinkage, and projection to the nonnegative orthant. The proximal of γ||M||* + I{M≥0} is generally not equal to sequentially applying shrinkage then projection. Citing non-expansiveness of the components and Theorem 4 of Ma et al. does not establish convergence to the optimum of the constrained composite problem. At best, the method is a heuristic with a fixed-point iteration; the optimality claim needs a proper operator splitting argument (or remove the claim).
- The ℓ1 term under nonnegativity reduces to a linear penalty on the sum of entries, while the squared loss on missing entries penalizes deviation from zero; together these strongly bias toward small/zero entries. There is no ablation on µ and ρ to show this does not induce trivial/over-regularized solutions.

Empirical
- Baseline tuning appears unfair/incomplete. For CL-KCCA, kernel choices and hyperparameters (kernel type/bandwidth/regularization) matter critically, but only the dimensionality k is tuned. Similarly for CL-OPCA/CL-LSI, k alone is tuned. This likely disadvantages the baselines.
- Missing strong baselines: structural correspondence learning (SCL; Prettenhofer & Stein) is cited but not compared; translation-based baselines (e.g., MT to a pivot language then monolingual classification) are common and often strong; more recent cross-lingual embedding methods are not considered. The claim that other methods require translating all training documents is not accurate for CL-KCCA/SCL.
- No ablation to isolate where gains come from: LSI on the raw concatenated matrix with zero fill; LSI on only parallel rows; effect of the completion step alone without LSI; sensitivity to µ, ρ, τ; effect of removing the nonnegativity constraint.
- No complexity/runtime/memory analysis. Each iteration requires an SVD on t×d matrices, with d the union vocabulary across two languages. The method may be impractical for realistic vocabularies; no wall-clock or scalability evidence is provided.
- Statistical rigor is limited. While averages and std devs are reported, there are no significance tests, and the figures/tables (not shown in the submission text) are not fully discussed. Hyperparameters are selected on one task (EFB) and fixed everywhere, which may advantage the proposed method or disadvantage baselines; per-task tuning would be more standard.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but leaves several important implementation details unspecified: vocabulary pruning/stopword handling, matrix sizes, SVD computation (full vs. partial), iteration counts/criteria, and how CL-LSI/OPCA were instantiated given only a limited set of parallel documents.
- Many references are incomplete (missing years/venues).

Originality
- The combination “matrix completion then LSI” is an incremental variation on established low-rank approaches. Without theory addressing the structured missingness and without broad, fair empirical comparisons, the novelty feels limited.

Significance
- The task is important. However, given the above issues, the contribution does not convincingly advance the state of the art. The lack of scalability evidence further limits practical impact.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide theoretical justification or at least empirical diagnostics for recovery under your structured missingness? E.g., conditions on the amount/diversity of parallel rows needed.
- How exactly were CL-LSI/CL-OPCA constructed with limited parallel documents? Did you augment documents with translations? If so, how for non-parallel documents?
- What kernels and regularization were used for CL-KCCA? Please tune them per task for fairness.
- What are the dimensions t and d in your experiments? What SVD algorithm and rank did you use per iteration? Wall-clock times and memory?
- Ablations on µ, ρ, and the effect of removing the ℓ1 term and the nonnegativity constraint?

Pros
- Addresses a relevant problem with a clean idea.
- Empirical results suggest potential improvements across many setups.
- Optimization is simple to implement.

Cons
- Insufficient theoretical grounding for the key modeling assumption and convergence claim.
- Baseline comparisons/tuning incomplete; missing strong baselines.
- No ablations or scalability analysis.
- Incremental novelty.

Recommendation: Reject

Confidence: High

Suggestions for improvement
- Either provide a correct proximal-splitting analysis handling the nuclear norm plus nonnegativity (or adjust the algorithm accordingly), or present the method as a heuristic without optimality claims.
- Add SCL and MT-based baselines; fairly tune all baselines (including kernel/regularization).
- Provide ablations and sensitivity analyses; include runtime/memory and approximate SVD details.
- Discuss identifiability and provide empirical stress tests showing when completion helps/hurts under varying amounts of parallel data and vocabulary overlap.
- Fix references and clarify implementation details.
