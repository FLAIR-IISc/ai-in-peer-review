Summary
This paper tackles cross-language text classification by proposing a two-step method (TSL) to learn cross-lingual document representations from a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 formulates a matrix completion problem on a unified bilingual document-term matrix: each document is represented in the concatenated vocabulary; non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language. The authors propose a convex objective combining a nuclear norm (low-rank), an ℓ1 norm (sparsity), squared loss on observed entries, a shrinkage-to-zero penalty on missing entries, and nonnegativity. They solve it with a projected proximal gradient descent algorithm with singular value thresholding, prove convergence via non-expansive operator composition, and obtain a completed, low-rank, nonnegative matrix. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the recovered matrix to produce a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation on which a standard linear SVM is trained. Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews (EN–FR/DE/JA, sentiment) show consistent improvements over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, and robustness with as few as 200 parallel documents.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated with respect to translation-based adaptation, multi-view methods, and cross-lingual representation learning via CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, as well as topic models. The novelty here is to treat the concatenated bilingual document-term matrix with missing blocks as a matrix completion problem with nonnegativity and sparsity, then apply LSI afterward. Optimization follows standard proximal gradient/shrinkage ideas (e.g., Ma et al., 2011), and the cross-lingual setup echoes earlier dual-language matrices (Littman et al., 1998) but differs by first imputing the missing cross-language entries via a convex low-rank recovery.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Casting cross-lingual BoW fusion as convex matrix completion with explicit treatment of missing cross-language entries is a clean and appealing idea. The two-step pipeline is simple, modular, and potentially broadly applicable beyond sentiment.
- Robustness with little parallel data: The method appears to leverage parallel supervision efficiently; gains with as few as 200 parallel pairs are compelling.
- Solid, if standard, optimization: The algorithm is clear, with a simple convergence argument, and the proximal/shrinkage structure is appropriate for the objective.
- Empirical gains across 18 tasks, and parameter-tied evaluation: Selecting hyperparameters on one task and holding them fixed for others strengthens the case for robustness.

Weaknesses
- Incremental on the optimization side: The solver and convergence proof rely on well-known proximal gradient and singular value thresholding techniques; the technical contribution is primarily the formulation, not new algorithms.
- Two-step potential suboptimality: After nuclear-norm completion, a second SVD for LSI effectively applies two separate low-rank operations. It is unclear why the latent factors from completion (e.g., V) are not used directly, or why a joint objective (e.g., supervised or joint factorization) wouldn’t be better.
- Scalability details are thin: The shrinkage step generally requires an SVD each iteration; there is no complexity analysis, practical approximations (e.g., truncated randomized SVD), vocabulary pruning, or runtime/memory numbers. For large vocabularies, this could be a bottleneck.
- Missing modern baselines: Translation-based baselines (MT train/test) and cross-lingual word/sentence embeddings (e.g., bilingual word embeddings, CLWE; multilingual sentence embeddings) are not compared. Even if not the main focus, these are strong, commonly used baselines in cross-lingual transfer.
- Limited ablations: The roles of the ℓ1 term and nonnegativity are asserted but not dissected. The effect of ρ (penalizing unobserved entries toward zero) versus relying on the nuclear norm alone would be informative.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The convex relaxation and proximal algorithm are standard and appropriate. The convergence proof is correct (non-expansiveness of gradient step, shrinkage, and projection). Claims are mostly supported empirically, though the absence of runtime analysis and ablations makes it harder to assess practicality and the necessity of each design choice. The experimental setup is reasonable, but additional baselines and deeper analysis would strengthen the case.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The construction of the unified matrix and the role of Ω/Ω̂ are well explained. Algorithm 1 is easy to follow. Minor notes:
- Provide stopping criteria and iteration counts.
- Clarify how the initial rank-1 approximation is computed at scale.
- Report vocabulary sizes and whether feature pruning is used.
- Briefly justify the choice of nonnegativity plus ℓ1 (given ℓ1 reduces to a linear term under nonnegativity) and discuss its interaction with the ρ penalty.

Originality
Moderate to good. The framing of cross-lingual representation learning as matrix completion over a concatenated bilingual BoW with missing blocks is, to my knowledge, not standard and constitutes a fresh angle relative to CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA and multi-view factorization. The optimization pieces are standard. The two-step “complete then LSI” pipeline is a simple but nontrivial design choice.

Significance
Promising. If robust, this idea could be adopted wherever limited parallel data exists and extensive monolingual data is available, beyond sentiment (e.g., topic classification, genre). Demonstrated improvements across many language pairs/categories, especially in low-parallel regimes, suggest practical utility. Broader impact would be stronger with comparisons to strong contemporary baselines and scalability evidence.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can you report training time, iterations, and peak memory for the largest setting? Do you use truncated/randomized SVD per iteration?
- Why not use the factors from the completion step directly for representation, instead of a second SVD? Any empirical comparison?
- Ablate µ and ρ, and the nonnegativity constraint, to show their contributions.
- Compare to at least one translation-based baseline (e.g., translate train/test) and one cross-lingual embedding baseline.
- Test on another dataset or include robustness to vocabulary size and feature pruning.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, simple, and novel formulation with a compelling conceptual shift.
- Consistent and sizable empirical gains; strong performance with very few parallel pairs.
- Solid, reproducible optimization and theory for convergence.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing strong modern baselines; limited ablations and no runtime analysis.
- Algorithmic contributions are standard; two-step design may be suboptimal.
