

# Peer Review: "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that leverages unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The approach first constructs a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix with missing entries for non-parallel documents, then fills in these missing entries using matrix completion, and finally applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors claim their method significantly outperforms existing approaches, especially with very limited parallel data.

The paper makes a reasonable attempt to position itself against prior work in cross-language representation learning, particularly contrasting with methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA that directly apply dimensionality reduction techniques on the partially observed document-term matrix. The authors provide a convergence analysis for their projected gradient descent algorithm and conduct extensive experiments on 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews across English, French, German, and Japanese.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a well-structured methodology with a clear two-step approach.
- The convergence analysis of the optimization algorithm is thorough and correctly applied.
- The experimental design is comprehensive, with 18 different cross-language tasks and multiple parameter sensitivity analyses.
- The claim that the method works well with very few parallel documents (as few as 200) is potentially valuable for practical applications where parallel data is scarce.

**Weaknesses:**
- The core contribution (matrix completion + LSI) is not sufficiently differentiated from prior work. CL-LSI [10] already uses a similar concatenated document-term matrix approach, and the paper doesn't convincingly explain why matrix completion is necessary or superior to directly applying LSI on the incomplete matrix.
- There is no ablation study to isolate the contribution of the matrix completion step versus the LSI step. It's unclear whether the matrix completion is the key innovation or if the two-step approach is simply better than using LSI directly on the incomplete matrix.
- The paper fails to compare against modern neural approaches to cross-lingual representation learning (e.g., multilingual BERT, XLM-R, mBERT), which have become the state-of-the-art in this field. This is a significant omission as these methods have largely superseded the traditional matrix-based approaches.
- The theoretical justification for why the document-term matrix would be low-rank (and why matrix completion would be particularly effective) is weak. The paper states that "a fully observed document-term matrix is naturally low-rank and sparse," but doesn't provide evidence or analysis to support this claim in the cross-lingual context.
- The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity or scalability, which is important for practical adoption, especially given the matrix size in real-world applications.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written.
- The methodology section provides sufficient detail for the core algorithm.
- The experimental setup is well-described and consistent across all tasks.

**Weaknesses:**
- The motivation for using matrix completion isn't clearly articulated. The paper states that "learning from this partially observed matrix directly by treating missing features as zeros certainly will lose a lot of information," but doesn't explain why matrix completion is better than other approaches for handling missing data (e.g., mean imputation, nearest neighbor imputation).
- The difference between their approach and CL-LSI is somewhat obscured. CL-LSI also constructs a concatenated document-term matrix, so it's unclear why their matrix completion step is necessary.
- The paper doesn't adequately explain why the ℓ₁ norm and trace norm regularization terms are appropriate for this problem. The authors mention "to enforce sparsity" but don't justify why sparsity is particularly important for cross-lingual representation learning.
- The parameter selection process is described but not thoroughly analyzed. The paper states they selected parameters based on one task (EFB), but doesn't discuss how sensitive the performance is to these parameters across different language pairs.

## Originality

The paper's approach is a combination of two well-known techniques: matrix completion and LSI. While the specific application to cross-lingual representation learning is novel, the combination itself is not particularly innovative. 

The key issue is that the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from prior work. CL-LSI [10] already uses a similar concatenated document-term matrix approach, and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why their matrix completion step is necessary or superior. The authors claim "different from these methods which apply operations such as LSI, KCCA, and OPCA directly on the original concatenated document-term matrix," but they don't provide evidence that applying LSI directly on the incomplete matrix would perform significantly worse than their approach.

Furthermore, the paper doesn't adequately acknowledge that neural approaches to cross-lingual representation learning have largely surpassed the traditional matrix-based methods they're comparing against. Recent papers like XLM-R [1] and mBERT [2] have demonstrated state-of-the-art results on cross-lingual tasks with much less reliance on parallel data.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The claim that the method works well with very few parallel documents (200 instead of 2000) is potentially significant for practical applications where parallel data is scarce.
- The consistent outperformance across 18 different language pairs and categories demonstrates robustness.

**Weaknesses:**
- The significance is limited by the fact that the problem of cross-lingual representation learning has largely been addressed by neural approaches. The paper's contribution doesn't advance the field significantly beyond existing techniques.
- The improvements reported (8.59% to 14.61% over baseline) are substantial but don't represent a paradigm shift in the field.
- The paper doesn't discuss how their method would scale to more languages or larger datasets, which is important for practical applications.
- The paper doesn't address how their approach would compare to simple imputation methods (e.g., mean imputation) followed by LSI, which might be simpler and just as effective.

## Comparative Analysis with Recent Work

The paper's approach is positioned against older methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, but fails to acknowledge that these methods have been largely superseded by neural approaches. For example:

- XLM-R [1] uses a multilingual transformer with 100 languages and achieves state-of-the-art results on cross-lingual transfer tasks with minimal parallel data.
- mBERT [2] has been shown to outperform traditional cross-lingual methods on multiple benchmarks.
- The paper by Conneau et al. (2019) [1] demonstrates that multilingual BERT can achieve near-fully supervised performance on cross-lingual tasks with only 100 parallel examples.

The paper's claim that "the proposed method produces good performance even with a very small number of unlabeled parallel bilingual documents" is interesting, but it's not clear how this compares to modern neural approaches that also work well with small amounts of parallel data.

## Recommendation

**Pro Accept:**
- Well-structured and clearly written
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple language pairs
- Demonstrates consistent improvements over baseline methods
- Provides convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm

**Con Reject:**
- Fails to compare against state-of-the-art neural approaches
- Core contribution (matrix completion + LSI) is not sufficiently novel
- Lacks ablation studies to isolate the contribution of key components
- Weak theoretical justification for why matrix completion is suitable for this problem
- Limited discussion of limitations and failure cases

## Final Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that demonstrates a practical approach to cross-lingual representation learning with limited parallel data. However, the contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking, and the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work or compare against modern neural approaches that have become the standard in the field.

The paper would be significantly stronger with:
1. Comparisons to recent neural methods (XLM-R, mBERT, etc.)
2. Ablation studies to isolate the contribution of matrix completion
3. Better theoretical justification for the choice of matrix completion
4. Discussion of limitations and failure cases

Without these improvements, the paper represents a modest incremental contribution that doesn't advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. Given the current state of the field, where neural approaches have largely superseded traditional matrix-based methods, the paper's contribution is less significant than the authors claim.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a comparison to at least one recent neural approach (e.g., XLM-R) to establish the relative significance of the contribution.
2. Include ablation studies showing the performance of the two-step approach versus just using LSI on the incomplete matrix.
3. Provide a more thorough theoretical justification for why matrix completion is particularly suitable for this problem.
4. Discuss the limitations of the approach and potential failure cases (e.g., when the languages have very different structures or when the vocabulary overlap is minimal).
5. Compare against simpler imputation methods (e.g., mean imputation) to demonstrate that matrix completion is necessary for the performance gain.
