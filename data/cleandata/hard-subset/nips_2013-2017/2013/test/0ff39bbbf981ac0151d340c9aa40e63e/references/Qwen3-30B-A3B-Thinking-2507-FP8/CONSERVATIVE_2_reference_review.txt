

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that first uses matrix completion to fill missing entries in a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix, then applies latent semantic indexing to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors claim this approach outperforms existing methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA on Amazon product review sentiment classification tasks.

**Summary and Context:** The paper builds on established work in cross-lingual representation learning, particularly the CL-LSI approach by Littman et al. (2000) and subsequent extensions. The authors' main innovation appears to be inserting a matrix completion step before LSI, arguing that this better captures cross-lingual word co-occurrence patterns. The paper compares against four baselines but fails to include more recent state-of-the-art approaches in the field, such as those using transformer-based multilingual models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R).

**Strengths:** The paper is clearly written with a logical structure. The methodology is well-explained, and the convergence proof for the optimization algorithm is technically sound. The experimental section provides adequate results on a standard benchmark dataset, demonstrating consistent improvements over the baselines tested.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty and Justification:** The core idea of using matrix completion for cross-lingual representation learning is not sufficiently novel. Matrix completion has been widely applied in NLP and recommendation systems for decades. The authors fail to convincingly justify why their specific formulation is better than existing methods that directly address the missing values problem. For instance, CL-LSI (Littman et al., 2000) and CL-KCCA (Platt et al., 2007) already handle the bilingual document-term matrix with missing entries without requiring matrix completion. The paper doesn't explain why a low-rank matrix completion step is necessary when the authors' own approach (CL-LSI) achieves comparable results without it.

2. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison:** The paper compares against methods from 2007-2015 (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA), while the field has advanced significantly since then. Recent state-of-the-art methods like XLM-R and mBERT have demonstrated superior performance on cross-lingual tasks. The absence of these modern baselines makes it difficult to assess the true significance of the proposed approach. Without these comparisons, the claimed "significant outperformance" lacks context.

3. **Insufficient Theoretical Justification:** The paper states that the matrix completion step "automatically captures important and robust low-rank information," but provides no theoretical analysis to support this claim. Why is the matrix low-rank? Why would matrix completion specifically improve the cross-lingual representation compared to other methods of handling missing values? The paper relies on empirical results alone without addressing these fundamental questions.

4. **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims the method "produces good performance even with a very small number of unlabeled parallel bilingual documents," yet the experiments only test with 200-2000 documents. In practice, 200 parallel documents is not "very small" for cross-lingual learning, and the paper doesn't show how the method scales to truly limited-resource scenarios.

5. **Computational Considerations:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the matrix completion step, which could be prohibitive for large-scale applications. This is particularly concerning given that the approach requires solving a convex optimization problem on a large document-term matrix.

**Overall Assessment:** While the paper presents a technically sound implementation of a matrix completion approach for cross-lingual representation learning, it fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty or provide adequate justification for why this approach is superior to existing methods. The claimed improvements over older baselines are not compelling in the context of current state-of-the-art approaches. The paper would benefit from more thorough comparisons against recent methods, clearer theoretical justification for the matrix completion step, and a more nuanced discussion of the method's limitations.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's contribution appears to be a minor extension of existing techniques rather than a significant advance. The lack of comparison against modern baselines and the insufficient justification for the matrix completion step undermine the paper's scientific contribution. The field has moved beyond this level of incremental improvement, and the paper does not provide enough evidence to suggest it represents a meaningful step forward.
