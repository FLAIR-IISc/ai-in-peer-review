## Peer Review for NIPS: A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification. The core idea is to leverage unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to construct a concatenated document-term matrix, fill in missing values using matrix completion, and then apply Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to create cross-lingual representations for classification. The authors demonstrate improved performance on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews compared to several baselines, including methods relying on machine translation and existing cross-lingual representation techniques. A key claim is the method’s robustness, even with a small amount of parallel data.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in cross-lingual learning. It acknowledges and correctly positions itself relative to both translation-based approaches (e.g., [17, 23]) and those focused on creating cross-lingual representations (e.g., [16, 10, 20]). The authors appropriately differentiate their approach from CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA by highlighting the matrix completion step *before* dimensionality reduction. This is a key novelty. The references provided are relevant and up-to-date, demonstrating awareness of the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion followed by LSI for cross-lingual representation learning is a novel contribution. The matrix completion step is a smart way to leverage limited parallel data to 'fill in' the cross-lingual gaps in the document-term matrix.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements over several strong baselines across a range of tasks. The performance gains are statistically significant and not merely incremental. The ablation study (Section 5.3) showing the impact of parallel data size is particularly valuable.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the matrix completion approach, including a convex relaxation and a projected gradient descent optimization algorithm. The convergence analysis (Section 4.2) is a positive aspect, adding rigor to the proposed method.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The two-step approach is clearly explained, and the mathematical formulations are presented in a readable manner.
* **Practical Significance:** The method’s ability to achieve good performance with limited parallel data is highly practical, as obtaining large parallel corpora is often expensive and time-consuming.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability Concerns:** While the authors mention a “scalable projected gradient descent algorithm,” the details are limited to an algorithm outline.  The computational complexity of matrix completion, particularly on large document-term matrices, is significant. A more detailed discussion of the algorithm’s scalability, including empirical runtime results on larger datasets, would strengthen the paper.  Specifically, memory requirements could be prohibitive.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The process of parameter selection (γ, ρ, k) appears to be somewhat ad-hoc, relying on experimentation on a single task (EFB).  A more systematic analysis of parameter sensitivity and potentially a method for automatic parameter tuning would be beneficial.
* **Limited Dataset:** The Amazon review dataset, while commonly used, is relatively specific.  The performance on this dataset does not guarantee generalization to other domains or types of text.  Testing on more diverse datasets would increase confidence in the method's robustness.
* **Optimization Algorithm Details:** While the algorithm is outlined, more information on its implementation would be useful. What libraries were used? What specific optimizations were employed? Were there any challenges encountered during implementation?
* **Comparison to Recent Neural Methods:** The related work section focuses primarily on non-neural methods. Recent advances in cross-lingual representation learning have been heavily driven by pre-trained language models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R). A comparison to these state-of-the-art neural methods is crucial to fully understand the contributions of this work.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to cross-lingual representation learning.
* The empirical results demonstrate significant performance improvements over strong baselines.
* The theoretical analysis and convergence proof add rigor to the work.
* The method addresses a practically relevant problem with a solution that is robust to limited parallel data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Concerns about the scalability of the matrix completion step need to be addressed.
* A more thorough analysis of parameter sensitivity and generalization to different datasets is needed.
* A comparison to state-of-the-art neural cross-lingual models is essential.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and supported by compelling empirical results. While there are some weaknesses related to scalability, parameter sensitivity, and comparison to recent neural methods, these do not fundamentally undermine the value of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the scalability of the projected gradient descent algorithm, including empirical runtime results.
2.  Investigate parameter sensitivity and consider a method for automatic parameter tuning.
3.  Include a comparison to state-of-the-art neural cross-lingual models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R).
4.  Consider testing the method on more diverse datasets to assess its generalization ability.
