

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that uses matrix completion to fill missing entries in a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix before applying latent semantic indexing. The key insight is that by first completing the matrix to recover low-rank information based on word co-occurrence patterns across languages, the resulting representations capture cross-lingual relationships more effectively than previous approaches that apply LSI directly to incomplete matrices.

The paper makes a clear distinction from prior work: while methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA apply dimensionality reduction directly to the incomplete matrix, the proposed approach first fills missing entries via matrix completion. This is a novel application of matrix completion to cross-lingual learning, which has not been explored in previous NIPS papers. The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation with a convex optimization problem and convergence proof for their projected gradient descent algorithm. The experiments on Amazon product reviews show consistent and significant improvements over multiple baselines (8.59-14.61% average accuracy gains), and the method works effectively with minimal parallel data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Core Idea:** The application of matrix completion to fill missing entries in cross-lingual document-term matrices represents a genuinely novel direction in cross-lingual learning. This is not just an incremental improvement over existing methods but a conceptual shift in how cross-lingual representation learning should be approached.

2. **Significant Performance Gains:** The empirical results demonstrate substantial improvements over state-of-the-art methods, particularly in scenarios with limited parallel data. The method achieves strong performance with only 200 unlabeled parallel documents, which is a practical advantage for real-world applications.

3. **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous convex optimization formulation with convergence proof, which adds credibility to the approach. The algorithm is well-designed and scalable, addressing potential concerns about computational complexity.

4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured, with clear problem formulation, methodological details, and experimental results. The connection between the matrix completion approach and cross-lingual representation learning is logically developed.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Experimental Scope:** While the results on Amazon product reviews are promising, the experiments are confined to sentiment classification on a single dataset. Broader evaluation across multiple tasks, domains, and languages would strengthen the claims about generalizability.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Matrix Completion Choice:** The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why matrix completion is particularly well-suited for this problem compared to other imputation techniques (e.g., simple mean imputation, KNN imputation). A brief discussion of why low-rank structure is appropriate for cross-lingual document-term matrices would be valuable.

3. **Comparison to Recent Work:** While the related work section covers foundational methods, it could better contextualize the paper against more recent NIPS papers on cross-lingual learning, particularly those using deep learning approaches (which have become increasingly prominent).

4. **Scalability Analysis:** The paper doesn't address how the method scales to larger vocabularies or datasets, which could be a concern for real-world applications with massive text corpora.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a bold and novel approach to cross-lingual representation learning that significantly advances the state of the art. The core idea of using matrix completion to fill missing entries before applying LSI is conceptually fresh and well-executed. While the experimental scope could be expanded, the performance gains are substantial and consistent across 18 different cross-lingual tasks. The method's effectiveness with minimal parallel data is particularly noteworthy for practical applications.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper's central contribution to be genuinely innovative. The method represents a new direction in cross-lingual learning rather than a minor refinement of existing techniques. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate the approach's superiority.

The minor weaknesses (limited experimental scope, lack of theoretical justification for matrix completion choice) are outweighed by the paper's strong novelty and significant performance improvements. These could be addressed in future work, but don't detract from the current contribution's value.

**Recommendation:** Accept

This paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning that aligns well with NIPS's emphasis on novel and impactful approaches. The method's novelty and strong empirical results justify acceptance, and it opens up promising new directions for future research in cross-lingual representation learning.
