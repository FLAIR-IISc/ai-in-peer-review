This paper presents a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, leveraging unlabeled parallel data via matrix completion and LSI. The core idea—using matrix completion to “fill in” a concatenated document-term matrix representing both languages—is genuinely novel and a potentially fruitful direction. The authors correctly identify the challenges of cross-lingual transfer and position their work within the existing literature. While the experimental setup isn’t groundbreaking, the results on Amazon reviews demonstrate a consistent and significant improvement over several baselines, including established methods like CL-LSI and CL-KCCA.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation is a fresh approach. Existing methods often rely on translation or complex projections. This work proposes a more direct way to bridge the vocabulary gap.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent improvements across a range of tasks and languages. The gains, while not massive, are statistically significant and suggest the method is doing something right. The ablation study regarding parallel data size further strengthens the claims.
*   **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The introduction clearly articulates the problem and motivates the approach.
*   **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The formulation of the matrix completion problem and the outlined optimization algorithm, while standard techniques, are presented with sufficient detail.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Algorithm Complexity:** The optimization algorithm, while described, is likely computationally expensive, especially for large datasets. The paper doesn’t delve deeply into the scalability challenges or provide practical timings.
*   **Limited Dataset:** The reliance solely on the Amazon reviews dataset is a limitation. While it’s a common benchmark, generalizing the findings to other domains is questionable.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The extensive parameter tuning (γ, ρ, k) suggests the method might be sensitive to hyperparameter settings. A more thorough analysis of parameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
*   **Missing Error Analysis:** The paper lacks any detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the method fails could reveal important insights and avenues for improvement.
*   **Related Work Depth:** While the related work is mentioned, it lacks depth in discussing recent advancements in neural cross-lingual representations (e.g., using pre-trained multilingual models like mBERT). A discussion of how this method complements or contrasts with these approaches is missing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is truly novel and represents a worthwhile exploration of matrix completion in a cross-lingual context.
*   The empirical results are compelling and consistently outperform strong baselines.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.
*   Even with its limitations, the work opens up new avenues for research in cross-lingual representation learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The computational complexity and scalability issues are concerning.
*   The reliance on a single dataset limits the generalizability of the findings.
*   The lack of a deeper discussion of recent advancements in neural cross-lingual representations is a missed opportunity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a novel and promising approach to cross-lingual representation learning. While the method has potential limitations in terms of scalability and dataset diversity, the strong empirical results and the originality of the idea outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance. I would encourage the authors to address the scalability concerns and explore the application of their method to other datasets in future work. The paper prioritizes a new idea and direction which is very appealing.



**Rating:** Accept.
