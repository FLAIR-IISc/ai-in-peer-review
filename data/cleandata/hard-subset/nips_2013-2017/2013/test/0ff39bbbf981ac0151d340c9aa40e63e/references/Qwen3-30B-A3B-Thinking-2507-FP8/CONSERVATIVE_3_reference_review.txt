

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that first uses matrix completion to fill missing entries in a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix, followed by latent semantic indexing to generate cross-lingual representations. The authors claim their approach significantly outperforms several established baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) on Amazon product review sentiment classification tasks.

The paper's main contribution appears to be the application of matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning. However, this is not a novel idea in isolation. Matrix completion has been widely used in recommendation systems and other domains, and LSI is a standard technique in NLP. The paper's approach combines these established techniques without clearly articulating why this specific combination is necessary or novel compared to existing cross-lingual methods.

The authors compare their method (TSL) against four baselines, reporting that TSL achieves 8.59-14.61% higher accuracy than the target baseline (TBOW) across 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks. While the results appear promising, the validation is insufficient for a NIPS submission. The authors test on only one dataset (Amazon product reviews) with limited language combinations (English, French, German, Japanese), and only 18 tasks total. Crucially, they do not compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods in cross-lingual representation learning, such as multilingual BERT or other transformer-based approaches that have become dominant in NLP.

The paper lacks several elements expected in a high-quality NIPS submission. There is no ablation study to determine whether the matrix completion step or the LSI step contributes more to the performance gains. The authors claim their method works well with "a very small number of unlabeled parallel documents," but they only test with np ∈ {200, 500, 1000, 2000}, which is not truly small (200 parallel documents is still substantial for a machine learning task). They provide no evidence that their method would work with fewer than 200 documents.

The theoretical analysis is limited to proving convergence of their optimization algorithm, which is a standard result for projected gradient descent methods. The paper does not provide theoretical guarantees about the performance of the overall method or why their approach should outperform existing methods.

The paper's claims of "significant" improvements are not substantiated with statistical significance tests. The reported accuracy improvements (8.59-14.61%) are presented without p-values or confidence intervals, making it impossible to determine if these differences are statistically meaningful.

The related work section adequately covers existing cross-lingual methods but fails to clearly distinguish the novelty of the proposed approach. The paper mentions that their method differs from CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA because they fill missing entries before applying LSI, but this distinction is not sufficiently emphasized or justified.

The paper also lacks important details for reproducibility. The authors state they used specific parameter values (γ = 0.1, ρ = 10^-4, k = 50), but it's unclear how these were determined beyond a single task (EFB). They don't explain how these parameters generalize across all 18 tasks.

As a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes methodological rigor and thorough validation, I find the paper's claims overstated given the limited scope of their experiments. While the approach may have merit, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that this is a significant advance over existing methods. The validation is too narrow (single dataset, limited language combinations), and the comparison is not against the most relevant contemporary baselines.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Comparing against more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., multilingual BERT)
2. Providing statistical significance tests for the performance improvements
3. Conducting ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component
4. Testing with truly small numbers of parallel documents (e.g., 20-50)
5. Including more diverse datasets and language pairs

Without these improvements, the paper presents an incremental combination of established techniques without sufficient evidence of its superiority. The claim of "significantly outperforming" other methods is not adequately supported by the limited experimental validation provided.

Overall, while the paper is well-written and the methodology is technically sound, it lacks the depth of validation and comparison required for a NIPS submission. The claimed advances are not sufficiently demonstrated, and the paper would benefit from more thorough evaluation before being accepted at NIPS.
