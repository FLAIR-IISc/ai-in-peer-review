## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification. The method leverages unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to construct a concatenated document-term matrix, addresses missing values via matrix completion, and then applies Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to derive cross-lingual representations. The authors demonstrate improved performance on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, outperforming several baselines including TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA. A key finding is the method’s robustness even with a limited number of parallel documents.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly situates itself within the landscape of cross-lingual learning, referencing related work that utilizes machine translation, bilingual lexicons, and shared representation spaces. It appropriately contrasts its approach with existing methods, highlighting its reliance on *matrix completion* as a novel step before applying LSI – a detail that distinguishes it from directly applying LSI or similar dimensionality reduction techniques (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA) to the concatenated matrix. The comparison to methods relying on translation (e.g., [17, 21]) is also relevant, noting the benefit of requiring fewer parallel documents.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning appears novel.  The matrix completion step is a thoughtful approach to handling the inherent sparsity and missing data in the concatenated document-term matrix, attempting to learn relationships *across* languages before dimensionality reduction.
* **Empirical Results:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation on a standard dataset (Amazon reviews) with clear comparisons to relevant baselines. The results consistently show improvements over existing methods, and the ablation study regarding the size of parallel data is particularly insightful.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the two-step process clearly, including the mathematical formulation of the matrix completion problem and the optimization algorithm. Algorithm 1 is a helpful addition.
* **Theoretical Justification:**  The authors provide a brief convergence analysis for their optimization algorithm, demonstrating a level of mathematical rigor.
* **Practical Impact:** The demonstrated robustness to small amounts of parallel data is significant. This is a key limitation of many cross-lingual methods, and addressing it is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Matrix Completion Justification:** While the idea of using matrix completion is interesting, the paper lacks a deeper discussion of *why* a low-rank assumption is appropriate for this particular problem. Is it solely based on the observation that document-term matrices are generally low-rank, or is there a more specific argument related to cross-lingual similarity?  A more nuanced explanation would strengthen the motivation.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions parameter selection but lacks detail about the sensitivity of the results to different parameter settings. Beyond simply stating the chosen parameters, an analysis of how performance changes with different values of γ, ρ, and k would be valuable.
* **Limited Dataset:**  Experiments are confined to Amazon product reviews. While a standard benchmark, expanding to other datasets or text genres would bolster the generalizability claims.
* **Algorithm Complexity:**  While Algorithm 1 is presented, a discussion of its computational complexity (especially in relation to the dataset size and matrix dimensions) is missing. This would be useful for assessing the scalability of the method.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the chosen baselines are reasonable, the paper doesn't compare to more recent advances in cross-lingual representation learning using pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-RoBERTa). A comparison to these would better contextualize the performance gains.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to cross-lingual representation learning.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed method.
* The theoretical analysis, while concise, adds to the paper's rigor.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The justification for the matrix completion step could be stronger.
* The lack of a detailed parameter sensitivity analysis is a concern.
* The evaluation is limited to a single dataset.
* A comparison to more recent, state-of-the-art multilingual language models is missing.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. While there are some weaknesses, the novelty of the approach, the strong empirical results, and the clear presentation outweigh these concerns. The paper meets the NIPS review criteria reasonably well.

**Rating:**  **Accept (Borderline)**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Elaborate on the justification for using matrix completion, providing a more theoretical grounding for the low-rank assumption.
2.  Include a brief analysis of parameter sensitivity, showing how performance varies with different settings of γ, ρ, and k.
3.  Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm.
4.  If possible, add a brief comparison to more recent multilingual language models as a baseline.
