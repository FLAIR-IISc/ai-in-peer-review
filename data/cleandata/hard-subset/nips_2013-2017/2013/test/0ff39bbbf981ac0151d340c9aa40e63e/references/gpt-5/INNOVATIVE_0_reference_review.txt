Summary
The paper proposes a two-step method (TSL) for cross-lingual text classification. Step 1 performs matrix completion on a unified bilingual document-term matrix, where non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language’s vocabulary. The authors formulate a convex objective combining a trace norm (for low-rank), an L1 term (sparsity; linear under nonnegativity), squared loss on observed entries, and a weighted squared penalty on missing entries, with nonnegativity constraints. They solve it via a projected proximal-gradient scheme with singular value thresholding and prove convergence via non-expansiveness arguments. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) on the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation, on which a linear SVM is trained. Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews across 18 language/category tasks show consistent gains over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, with robustness to fewer parallel documents.

Relation to prior work
The approach is closest to CL-LSI/CL-OPCA/CL-KCCA that build shared spaces from parallel/comparable data, but differs by first imputing the unobserved bilingual features via convex matrix completion before dimensionality reduction. It also differs from translation-based and multi-view/co-training methods that require translating large training sets. The use of matrix completion on a bilingual document-term matrix with nonnegativity and then applying LSI appears to be a novel combination.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Casting the cross-lingual feature gap as matrix completion on a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix is elegant and bold. The two-step “impute then compress” pipeline is simple, general, and potentially useful beyond sentiment classification.
- Technical soundness: The optimization is a standard convex surrogate (trace norm + linear term + squared losses) with a reasonable proximal-gradient algorithm and a convergence argument referencing established results.
- Empirical results: Strong and consistent improvements over CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA on 17/18 tasks; large gains over TBOW with only 100 target labels. Particularly compelling robustness when the number of parallel reviews is reduced to 200.
- Practicality: Requires only a small set of unlabeled parallel documents; no full translation or heavy external resources.

Weaknesses
- Missing baselines/ablations: No comparison to SCL (cited), simple zero-fill + LSI, or a “TSL without LSI” ablation to isolate the contribution of matrix completion versus the subsequent LSI. Also absent are recent bilingual word embeddings or MT-based features (depending on the timeframe, though the authors cite comparable-era methods).
- Scalability/computation: The method requires repeated SVDs for both matrix completion (shrinkage at each iteration) and the final LSI. There is no complexity analysis, iteration counts, or wall-clock times. Practicality on larger corpora/vocabularies is unclear.
- Theoretical presentation: The convergence proof leverages non-expansiveness, but a standard proximal gradient framework with Lipschitz constants and step-size derivation would be clearer. The constraint τ < min(2, 2/ρ) is unusual; a discussion of the gradient Lipschitz constant and how it depends on Y, Ŷ, and ρ would improve rigor.
- Minor clarity issues: The loss is written as c(x, y) = 1/2||x − y|| (seems to miss the square). Details on TF-IDF normalization across languages, row/column normalization prior to completion, stopping criteria, and sensitivity to µ, γ, ρ are sparse. Some phrasing suggests using labeled data “from both languages” post-representation; please clarify to ensure fair use of supervision across methods.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a reasonable convex formulation and a standard algorithm. Claims are broadly supported by experiments, though additional baselines and ablations would strengthen the case. Some theoretical and empirical details could be tightened.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The optimization and algorithm are presented cleanly. A few notation typos and missing experimental details (normalization, complexity, stopping criteria) should be fixed.

Originality
Good. The idea of completing a bilingual document-term matrix (with explicit treatment of missing cross-language entries) before LSI is a novel and interesting direction compared to prior CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA pipelines that operate directly on concatenated or paired data.

Significance
Promising. The method appears to advance state-of-the-art within the compared set and is robust under limited parallel data. The approach is generic and may be adopted in other cross-lingual settings or extended (e.g., to multilingual >2 languages or topic models).

Questions and suggestions
- Provide an ablation: (a) zero-fill + LSI; (b) TSL without LSI (use M* directly); (c) effect of removing the penalty on missing entries (ρ = 0); (d) effect of removing nonnegativity.
- Report runtime, number of iterations, SVD ranks used (use of truncated/randomized SVD?), and memory footprint.
- Clarify supervision usage across methods to ensure fairness.
- Analyze sensitivity to µ, γ, ρ and k; include per-task variability.
- Qualitatively inspect cross-lingual nearest neighbors after completion to show the induced alignment.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and simple idea with clear intuition
- Strong, consistent empirical gains; robust with few parallel docs
- Convex formulation with a practical solver

Cons against acceptance
- Missing key baselines/ablations
- Limited scalability discussion and runtime evidence
- Minor theoretical and notational rough edges

Recommendation
Accept (leaning). The conceptual novelty and empirical gains outweigh the relatively minor omissions. I expect this line to stimulate follow-up work on completion-based cross-lingual representations.

Confidence: High.
