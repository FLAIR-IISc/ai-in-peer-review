This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification, addressing the challenge of differing feature spaces across languages. The method first constructs a concatenated document-term matrix, performs matrix completion to impute missing values (representing words absent in a document’s original language), and then applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to derive cross-lingual representations. The paper is well-motivated, building upon existing work in cross-lingual learning, translation-based methods, and cross-lingual representation learning via parallel corpora. It positions itself effectively within the existing literature, referencing relevant work (e.g., [18, 6, 23, 16, 10, 20, 15]).

**Strengths:**

*   **Technically Sound:** The approach of combining matrix completion and LSI is reasonable and well-defined. The use of projected gradient descent for matrix completion, while standard, is appropriately chosen and the convergence analysis (albeit brief) provides some justification. The algorithmic details are sufficiently clear.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on the Amazon product review dataset provide a comprehensive evaluation, comparing against several strong baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA). The results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   **Analysis of Parallel Data Size:** The investigation into the impact of the amount of unlabeled parallel data is a valuable addition, further supporting the method's robustness. The claim of good performance even with small amounts of parallel data is substantiated by the experimental results.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is consistent and the explanations are relatively easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of matrix completion and LSI is presented as a novel approach, the individual components are well-established techniques. The novelty lies primarily in the specific application to this cross-lingual problem, rather than in fundamental algorithmic breakthroughs.
*   **Matrix Completion Justification:** The justification for matrix completion focuses on the low-rank and sparse nature of the document-term matrix, but lacks deeper insight into *why* this is a particularly suitable approach for bridging the cross-lingual gap. A stronger argument linking matrix completion to capturing underlying semantic relationships across languages would be beneficial.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The parameter selection process (γ, ρ, k) relies on a single development task (EFB). The sensitivity of performance to these parameters across different language pairs and categories should be investigated more thoroughly. A sensitivity analysis would bolster the claim of robustness.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper mentions scalability but doesn’t provide concrete computational analysis or runtime comparisons with the baseline methods. This is particularly important for matrix completion, which can be computationally expensive.
*   **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates that the proposed method outperforms the baselines, it doesn't offer much insight into *where* it succeeds or fails. An error analysis identifying specific types of cross-lingual challenges where the method excels (or struggles) would be insightful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and challenging problem in NLP.
*   The proposed method is technically sound and empirically validated.
*   The experimental results are compelling and consistently demonstrate the superiority of the approach.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The novelty of the approach is incremental rather than groundbreaking.
*   The justification for using matrix completion is somewhat weak.
*   The lack of a detailed computational analysis and sensitivity analysis raises concerns about practical applicability and robustness.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. While the novelty is limited, the method is technically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated. The experimental results are convincing and demonstrate the potential of the approach. However, the lack of deeper theoretical justification, computational analysis, and parameter sensitivity analysis are weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** The authors should address the weaknesses by providing a more in-depth justification for the use of matrix completion, including a discussion of its relationship to semantic similarity across languages. A sensitivity analysis of the key parameters is also necessary. Adding runtime comparisons would strengthen the argument for practicality. A brief error analysis would offer valuable insights into the method's strengths and limitations.
