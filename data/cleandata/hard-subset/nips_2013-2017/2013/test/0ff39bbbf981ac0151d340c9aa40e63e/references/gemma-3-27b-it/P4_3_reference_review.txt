This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification. The core idea is to leverage unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to construct a unified document-term matrix, fill in missing entries via matrix completion, and then apply latent semantic indexing (LSI) to derive cross-lingual representations. The resulting representations are used with a standard monolingual classifier. The work is positioned within the established field of cross-lingual learning, relating to approaches employing machine translation, domain adaptation, and shared representation learning.

**Quality:** The technical approach appears sound, building upon well-established techniques – matrix completion and LSI – but combining them in a novel way for cross-lingual representation learning. The formulation of the matrix completion problem is standard, and the use of projected gradient descent is a reasonable optimization strategy. The convergence analysis, referencing existing work on projected gradient descent, adds credibility. However, the implementation details of the projected gradient descent algorithm (specifically, the choice of step size τ and regularization parameters γ and ρ) seem largely empirically determined via a grid search on a single task (EFB) and then applied to all tasks. Justification for these specific parameter choices, or sensitivity analysis, would strengthen the paper. The experiments are reasonably comprehensive, using a standard dataset and several baseline methods. The reported results consistently demonstrate improvements over the baselines, which is a strong indicator of effectiveness. However, statistical significance tests are not reported; this omission weakens the conclusions. The paper is careful to note the limitations of using only 100 labeled examples in the target language.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly outlines the problem and the proposed solution. The related work section provides a good overview of existing approaches, positioning the current work effectively. The approach section is detailed, with clear mathematical notation and explanations of each step. However, the algorithm description (Algorithm 1) feels somewhat abrupt and could benefit from more intuitive explanations of each step's purpose. The experimental setup is well-described, but the justification for choosing specific parameters is lacking, as mentioned above. The use of abbreviations like "TSL" is fine, but defining them explicitly upon first use would improve readability.

**Originality:** While both matrix completion and LSI are established techniques, the specific application of matrix completion *before* LSI, motivated by the challenges of cross-lingual data with sparse parallel corpora, is a novel combination. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work that either relies heavily on translation or directly applies dimensionality reduction techniques to the original sparse matrix. The contribution isn't revolutionary, but it represents a sensible and potentially effective adaptation of existing tools to a challenging problem.

**Significance:** The results are significant, as the proposed method consistently outperforms established baselines on a standard cross-lingual sentiment classification dataset. The ability to achieve good performance with a small amount of labeled target data is particularly valuable in real-world scenarios where labeled data is expensive to obtain. The paper demonstrates that the approach is robust across different language pairs and categories. However, the limited scope of the experiments – focusing solely on sentiment classification with Amazon reviews – reduces the generalizability of the findings. Exploring the performance of the method on other cross-lingual tasks (e.g., topic classification, document retrieval) would strengthen the impact of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning.
*   Consistent and statistically plausible improvements over strong baselines.
*   Robustness across different language pairs and categories.
*   Effective use of unlabeled parallel data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of statistical significance tests for the reported results.
*   Empirical parameter tuning without sufficient justification or sensitivity analysis.
*   Limited scope of experiments (sentiment classification on a single dataset).
*   Algorithm description could be more intuitive.



Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. The proposed method is technically sound, well-motivated, and empirically validated. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above – particularly adding statistical significance tests and providing more justification for parameter choices – would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
