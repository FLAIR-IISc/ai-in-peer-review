Summary:
The paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-lingual text classification. Step 1 performs matrix completion on a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix in which non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language’s vocabulary. The completion problem is posed as convex optimization with a nuclear norm (for low rank), an ℓ1 term (sparsity), quadratic losses on observed and missing entries, and nonnegativity constraints, solved via an iterative scheme consisting of a gradient step on the smooth part, singular value thresholding, and projection to the nonnegative orthant. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) on the completed matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations, on which a linear SVM is trained. Experiments on cross-lingual sentiment classification with Amazon reviews (EN/FR/DE/JA) reportedly show gains over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, and robustness with few parallel documents.

Relation to prior work:
The approach is positioned relative to cross-lingual representation learning via CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, pivot/SCL, and multi-view/co-training methods. The main distinguishing feature claimed is imputing missing entries in the bilingual document-term matrix prior to LSI, using a small set of parallel documents. Conceptually, the method combines standard nuclear-norm matrix completion with a subsequent SVD-based dimensionality reduction. The algorithm is closely related to SVT/proximal gradient approaches for trace-norm regularization and to classical CL-LSI; it also resembles learning a low-rank shared space from parallel data as in KCCA/OPCA, but via imputation.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The optimization objective is convex (nuclear norm + linear + quadratic terms + indicator of nonnegativity). However, the algorithmic justification is not fully convincing. The update is a gradient step on the smooth part, followed by singular value shrinkage (prox for the nuclear norm), followed by a projection onto the nonnegative set. In general, the proximal operator of a sum of a nuclear norm and an indicator of a convex set is not equal to sequentially applying the two prox operators; convergence of the fixed-point iteration to the true minimizer requires additional conditions or a correct operator-splitting scheme (e.g., Dykstra, ADMM). The provided non-expansiveness lemmas and Theorem 1 are insufficient to establish convergence to the optimizer of the stated problem (as opposed to a fixed point of the composed mapping). This needs clarification or a corrected algorithmic framework.
- Assumptions: The low-rank assumption on the unified bilingual document-term matrix with massive structured missingness is strong. Adding an ℓ1 penalty while constraining nonnegativity makes the objective bias toward sparsity and small magnitudes; it is unclear why this helps when the subsequent LSI further compresses the matrix. The quadratic penalty on missing entries toward zero (controlled by ρ) further biases imputation toward zeros, potentially counteracting cross-lingual signal unless carefully tuned; ablations disentangling these terms are missing.
- Empirics: Results on Amazon reviews are extensive across language/category pairs and include sensitivity to the number of parallel pairs. However, the experimental setting is transductive (using all documents, including test documents, to learn the representation), which can inflate performance. No runtime/memory or iteration counts are reported; repeated SVDs at each iteration may be costly for realistic d,t. Hyperparameters are tuned on one task and reused, but SVM regularization is set to defaults, which may disadvantage baselines. Statistical tests are not reported.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable and the method is described with equations and pseudocode. However:
- The convergence argument is terse and, as noted, likely insufficient for the stated composite objective.
- The choice and role of µ, ρ, γ are not well motivated; no ablation shows their effects.
- Important implementation details are missing (stop criteria, SVD truncation strategy, pre-processing specifics, vocabulary sizes).
- References are incomplete (missing years/pages) and there are minor typos.

Originality:
The core novelty is the two-step combination: nuclear-norm matrix completion on a bilingual document-term matrix with structured missingness, followed by LSI. Both ingredients are well-established; CL-LSI already applies SVD to a bilingual matrix, and many works project into shared low-rank spaces using parallel data (CCA/OPCA). The contribution is a relatively simple imputation-before-LSI pipeline. Without ablations, it is unclear whether gains come from imputation, from nonnegativity/sparsity, or simply from another SVD. Theoretical contribution beyond standard SVT/proximal gradient is limited.

Significance:
Cross-lingual representation learning is important, but the empirical evaluation focuses on an older dataset and omits stronger modern baselines (e.g., cross-lingual word embeddings, bilingual autoencoders, or multilingual pretrained transformers such as mBERT/XLM-R used in zero-/few-shot settings; MT-based translate-train/test baselines). Absent these, it is hard to assess impact. The scalability of the approach to larger vocabularies/corpora is not demonstrated. As presented, the work offers an incremental idea with modest theoretical depth and limited contemporary significance.

Pros:
- Clear problem setting; uses small parallel corpora.
- Simple, generally applicable pipeline; convex formulation.
- Empirical gains over several classical baselines on multiple language/category pairs.
- Sensitivity to number of parallel documents is studied.

Cons:
- Questionable/insufficient convergence proof for the stated composite objective (nuclear norm + nonnegativity).
- Limited novelty; essentially imputation plus LSI, both standard.
- Transductive evaluation; lack of strong modern baselines (mBERT/XLM-R, cross-lingual embeddings, MT baselines).
- No ablations (e.g., without ℓ1, without nonnegativity, without the second LSI step, or LSI on the original matrix with zeros).
- No runtime/complexity or large-scale experiments; repeated SVDs per iteration may be impractical.
- Incomplete references and missing implementation details hinder reproducibility.

Questions for the authors:
1. Does your algorithm optimize the exact convex problem with both nuclear norm and nonnegativity constraints? If so, please provide a correct proximal splitting argument (or use ADMM/Dykstra) and a rigorous convergence proof to the global minimizer.
2. Why include both ℓ1 and nonnegativity (and a quadratic penalty toward zero on missing entries)? Provide ablations quantifying each term’s contribution.
3. How large are t and d in your experiments? What are iteration counts, wall-clock times, and peak memory? Do you use truncated SVD?
4. Can you evaluate in an inductive setting (representations learned without access to test documents) and add strong baselines (translate-train/test, cross-lingual embeddings, mBERT/XLM-R few-shot)?
5. Why is a second SVD (LSI) necessary after nuclear-norm completion, which already enforces low rank?

Recommendation: 4 (reject). While the empirical results vs. classical baselines are promising, the novelty is limited, the theoretical justification appears incomplete for the stated problem, and the evaluation lacks modern baselines and scalability analysis. With a corrected optimization algorithm/proof, thorough ablations, stronger baselines (including inductive settings), and runtime studies, this could become a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
