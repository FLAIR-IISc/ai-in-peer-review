

# Peer Review: "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that first constructs a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix with missing values for non-parallel documents, then fills missing entries using matrix completion, and finally applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors claim their approach outperforms existing methods (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) on 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews. The paper builds on existing work in cross-lingual representation learning but introduces a novel matrix completion step before LSI, which the authors argue better captures cross-lingual co-occurrence patterns.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-structured optimization problem (equation 2) with a detailed projected gradient descent algorithm (Algorithm 1) and a solid convergence analysis (Theorem 1). The authors carefully address the non-smoothness of the problem and provide theoretical guarantees.

2. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The evaluation on 18 different cross-language tasks (from English to French, German, and Japanese across three product categories) is thorough. The results consistently show that the proposed method (TSL) outperforms all baselines, with improvements of 8.59-14.61% over the target baseline (TBOW) and consistent advantages over other methods.

3. **Practical Utility**: The paper demonstrates that TSL performs well even with very small numbers of unlabeled parallel documents (as few as 200), which is a significant practical advantage for real-world applications where parallel data is scarce.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear mathematical formulations and a logical flow from problem statement to method to evaluation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The most significant weakness is the omission of recent neural approaches to cross-lingual representation learning (e.g., multilingual BERT, XLM-R, mT5). Given that these methods have become the dominant paradigm for cross-lingual tasks in recent years, the comparison is incomplete and undermines the significance claim. The authors should compare with at least one or two recent neural baselines.

2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: While the matrix completion approach is well-motivated, the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why the low-rank assumption is valid for cross-lingual representation learning. The authors claim the matrix is naturally low-rank but provide no empirical evidence or theoretical argument specific to the cross-lingual setting.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The parameter selection process is not thoroughly justified. The authors used the EFB task to select parameters, but don't discuss how sensitive the results are to these choices or how to select parameters in new domains.

4. **Practical Scalability**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the matrix completion step compared to the baselines. Given that the matrix can be very large (with d being the size of the unified vocabulary), this is an important consideration for real-world applications.

5. **Limited Context of the Problem**: The paper claims to be "novel" but doesn't clearly articulate how it differs from CL-LSI (which also uses a concatenated document-term matrix). The distinction between "applying LSI directly on the original concatenated matrix" versus "first filling missing entries" needs to be more sharply defined.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written and technically sound paper that presents a novel two-step approach to cross-language representation learning. The method is theoretically grounded, the optimization algorithm is carefully developed, and the empirical results are strong. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of how to effectively leverage parallel data in cross-lingual tasks.

However, the omission of recent neural approaches in the comparison is a critical weakness. As the field has largely moved toward neural methods for cross-lingual representation learning, the paper's significance is diminished without showing how it compares to these state-of-the-art techniques. The authors should address this in a revision.

The paper is of moderate significance. While it provides a useful alternative approach to cross-lingual representation learning, the field has largely shifted toward neural methods, and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why a non-neural approach would be preferable in most scenarios. The method is most relevant for settings where neural approaches are not feasible due to computational constraints or limited resources.

## Recommendation

**Weak Accept** (with major revisions)

The paper has several strengths in methodology and evaluation, but the omission of recent neural baselines significantly weakens its contribution. I recommend acceptance if the authors address these concerns in a revision, particularly by adding comparisons with recent neural methods and providing a clearer justification for why their approach is valuable despite the dominance of neural methods in the field.

## Pro and Con for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Novel two-step approach that fills missing values before applying LSI
- Strong empirical results across 18 different cross-language tasks
- Demonstrates effectiveness with very small amounts of unlabeled parallel data
- Well-structured theoretical foundation with convergence analysis

**Con:**
- Missing comparison with recent neural methods (e.g., XLM-R, mT5)
- Insufficient justification for why matrix completion is better than existing methods
- Parameter selection process lacks thoroughness
- Doesn't clearly articulate how the approach differs from CL-LSI in practice

The paper has solid technical content and promising results, but the lack of comparison with the current state-of-the-art neural approaches makes it difficult to assess its true significance in the context of modern cross-lingual representation learning.
