

# Peer Review: "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that first constructs a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix with missing entries for non-parallel documents, then fills these missing entries using matrix completion before applying LSI to obtain cross-lingual representations. The approach builds upon existing cross-lingual representation learning methods like CL-LSI [10], CL-OPCA [15], and CL-KCCA [20], but differs by first completing the matrix before applying dimensionality reduction. The authors demonstrate their method's effectiveness on 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, showing significant improvements over baselines.

The paper properly contextualizes its work within the literature, noting that most previous approaches either translate documents between languages or directly apply dimensionality reduction on the concatenated matrix without addressing the missing data problem. However, it could have more clearly distinguished itself from recent work on matrix completion for NLP tasks, such as the use of matrix completion for word embeddings [e.g., in "Matrix Completion for Word Embeddings" by Zou et al., ACL 2017].

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-motivated approach to addressing the sparse matrix problem in cross-lingual representation learning. The matrix completion formulation (equation 2) is well-justified, and the authors provide a convergence analysis for their projected gradient descent algorithm (Lemma 1-3 and Theorem 1), which is a significant strength.

2. **Empirical Validation**: The experimental evaluation is thorough, covering 18 different cross-language tasks across three languages (English, French, German, Japanese) and three product categories. The results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms baselines, with improvements of 8.59-14.61% over the target baseline (TBOW) and significant advantages over CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA.

3. **Practical Significance**: The paper demonstrates that the method works effectively even with very limited parallel data (as few as 200 documents), which is a valuable practical contribution for real-world applications where parallel data is scarce.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and experimental setup. The algorithm description is sufficiently detailed for reproduction.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Justification Gap**: While the paper provides a convergence proof for the optimization algorithm, it lacks a theoretical argument explaining *why* matrix completion followed by LSI would outperform direct application of LSI/OPCA/KCCA on the observed matrix. The paper states that the matrix is "naturally low-rank and sparse," but doesn't provide a theoretical basis for why completing the matrix first would better capture the underlying semantic structure.

2. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The related work section does not adequately address recent advances in cross-lingual representation learning, particularly those using neural networks (e.g., multilingual BERT [14] or XLM [15]). While the paper focuses on traditional methods, it should acknowledge that neural approaches have achieved state-of-the-art results in cross-lingual tasks, making the comparison to traditional methods somewhat outdated.

3. **Parameter Selection Justification**: The paper selects parameters using a single task (EFB) and then applies them to all 18 tasks. This is a reasonable approach but lacks justification for why the same parameters work across different language pairs and categories. The authors should provide more insight into the parameter sensitivity and why their chosen settings generalize well.

4. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper mentions that the matrix is "typically very large" but doesn't address the computational complexity of the matrix completion step compared to the baselines. For practical adoption, it's important to know whether the method scales well to large datasets.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound approach to cross-language text classification that addresses a well-known challenge in the field (the sparsity of the concatenated document-term matrix). The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate significant improvements over baselines, particularly with limited parallel data. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by:

1. A more thorough theoretical justification for the two-step approach
2. A comparison with recent neural cross-lingual methods (even if only as a discussion point)
3. A deeper analysis of parameter sensitivity and generalization
4. Discussion of computational complexity

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual representation learning with a clear, well-executed approach that demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods. The main weakness is the lack of theoretical justification for the two-step approach, which is a common issue in applied machine learning papers but would strengthen the paper considerably. The comparison to neural methods is also a notable gap that should be addressed.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides valuable empirical evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. While the method doesn't represent a paradigm shift in the field, it offers a practical and effective solution to an important problem that could be widely adopted in applications where parallel data is limited.

## Specific Recommendations for Revision

1. Add a brief theoretical argument explaining why matrix completion followed by LSI would better capture the semantic structure than direct application of LSI to the observed matrix.

2. Include a short discussion comparing the proposed method to recent neural approaches (e.g., multilingual BERT) to better contextualize the contribution.

3. Provide more detail on the parameter sensitivity analysis, perhaps including a table showing how performance varies with different parameter values across tasks.

4. Address the computational complexity of the matrix completion step relative to the baselines.

The paper is of high quality and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of cross-lingual text classification. With these minor revisions, it would be an excellent addition to NeurIPS.
