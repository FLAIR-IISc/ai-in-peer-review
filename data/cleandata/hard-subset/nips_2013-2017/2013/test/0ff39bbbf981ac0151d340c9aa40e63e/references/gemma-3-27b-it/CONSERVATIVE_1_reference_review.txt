## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification, leveraging unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to learn cross-lingual representations. The approach first performs matrix completion on a concatenated document-term matrix with missing values, followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) to obtain a low-dimensional embedding. The motivation, bridging the language gap in cross-lingual classification, is well-established and addresses a relevant problem within the NLP community. 

While the problem setting is important and the authors correctly identify existing approaches (translation, bilingual resources, cross-lingual representations), the proposed solution feels incremental and lacks sufficient justification for its complexity. The core idea – matrix completion followed by LSI – isn't groundbreaking. Matrix completion has been used in recommender systems and other contexts for a while, and its application here, while sensible, doesn’t represent a substantial theoretical leap. Similarly, LSI is a standard dimensionality reduction technique. The novelty appears to lie in *combining* these, specifically applying matrix completion to address missing data in a cross-lingual document-term matrix before LSI. However, the authors don't provide a compelling argument why this specific combination is superior to directly applying LSI (or other dimensionality reduction methods) to the sparse matrix, perhaps with appropriate weighting schemes to handle missing values. 

The experimental setup utilizes the Amazon review dataset, a common benchmark. However, the results, while demonstrating improvement over a simple baseline (TBOW) and competitive performance with existing methods (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA), are not overwhelmingly convincing. The gains, while statistically significant (as implicitly claimed by reporting standard deviations), appear modest, and the performance improvement over strong baselines like CL-KCCA is not dramatic.  The ablation study showing the impact of parallel data size is reasonable, but doesn’t fundamentally change my assessment. The algorithmic details (projected gradient descent) are presented with appropriate mathematical formulation, but the justification for specific parameter choices (γ, ρ, τ) feels ad-hoc; a more rigorous parameter sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. The convergence analysis, while included, is largely boilerplate and doesn't address practical convergence issues that might arise with large-scale datasets.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a relevant and important problem in cross-lingual NLP.
*   Clear problem statement and motivation.
*   Technically sound methodology (matrix completion and LSI are well-established techniques).
*   Empirical results demonstrate improvement over a baseline and competitiveness with existing methods.
*   Detailed algorithmic description and convergence analysis (though theoretical).

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty; the combination of matrix completion and LSI lacks strong justification.
*   Modest performance gains over competitive baselines.
*   Ad-hoc parameter selection with insufficient sensitivity analysis.
*   The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach. For instance, the computational cost of matrix completion on large datasets isn't addressed.
*   Insufficient comparison with simpler approaches that could achieve similar results (e.g., weighted LSI).



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound method that addresses a relevant problem.
*   The empirical results show a consistent improvement over a strong baseline.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the approach is limited, and the justification for its complexity is weak.
*   The performance gains are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance, especially given the computational cost potentially associated with matrix completion.
*   The paper doesn't sufficiently explore alternative approaches or address potential limitations.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is technically sound and addresses a valid research problem, it lacks sufficient novelty and impact to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The performance gains are modest, the justification for the chosen approach is weak, and the paper doesn’t fully address the limitations of the proposed method. The work feels more like an engineering effort applying existing techniques to a new problem than a fundamental research contribution. I would encourage the authors to explore alternative approaches or provide a more compelling justification for the proposed two-step method before resubmitting.
