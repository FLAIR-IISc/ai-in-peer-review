Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles cross-language text classification by inducing shared document representations from limited parallel data. It proposes a two-step procedure: (1) build a unified document-term matrix over the concatenated vocabularies of two languages, where non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language; recover missing entries by solving a convex matrix completion problem with nuclear norm regularization, sparsity (ℓ1), squared losses on observed entries, weak quadratic regularization on missing entries, and nonnegativity constraints; (2) apply LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix and train standard monolingual classifiers in the reduced space. Optimization is performed via a projected gradient descent scheme that alternates a gradient step on the smooth part, singular value shrinkage, and nonnegativity projection; non-expansivity lemmas are provided and a convergence claim is made.

The approach relates to translate-train/test methods and multi-view learning (e.g., co-training, majority voting), cross-lingual representation learning via CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, and topic models, and pivot-based SCL. Unlike CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA, which operate directly on concatenated or paired documents, the paper first imputes missing features by matrix completion to better exploit within- and cross-language co-occurrence before dimensionality reduction. Compared to SCL-style methods using pivot word pairs, this method leverages a small set of unlabeled parallel documents.

On multilingual Amazon reviews (English, French, German, Japanese; three domains), the method outperforms TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA across 18 source-target-domain combinations, and shows robustness with as few as 200 parallel pairs.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The matrix completion objective is convex and well-motivated: nuclear norm to promote low-rank structure, ℓ1 to encourage sparsity, squared loss on observed entries, and a weak quadratic penalty on unobserved entries to avoid overfitting, all under nonnegativity constraints. The projected gradient/shrinkage scheme is a reasonable heuristic and the non-expansivity of each operator is correctly argued. However, the convergence proof appears incomplete: composing shrinkage (prox for nuclear norm) with a separate nonnegativity projection does not in general yield the proximal operator of the sum γ‖·‖* + I{·≥0}. Convergence of the resulting fixed-point iterations to a global minimizer of the constrained problem requires additional arguments (e.g., using proximal gradient with the indicator included in the non-smooth part, Dykstra-like schemes, or ADMM), and showing that fixed points correspond to optima. Absent this, the guarantee is weaker than stated.
- Empirical methodology: The evaluation is thorough across many language/domain pairs, with multiple runs and standard deviations. Hyperparameters are selected on one task and held fixed elsewhere, which reduces selection bias. The robustness to the number of parallel pairs is carefully examined.
- Missing controls: Important baselines are absent. In particular, structural correspondence learning (SCL) on this dataset is a standard baseline, as are translate-train or translate-test baselines using MT or bilingual lexicons. An ablation that applies LSI directly to the partially observed matrix (i.e., CL-LSI under the same data protocol) would isolate the gain from matrix completion.
- Efficiency: The method requires repeated SVDs (for shrinkage and for the LSI step). No runtime, memory, or scalability analysis is provided, and stopping criteria are unspecified. For large vocabularies and corpora, this may be a bottleneck.
- Experimental protocol: Representation learning uses all documents from both languages (including target test documents) plus unlabeled parallel data. This is a transductive setting; while applied consistently across methods, an inductive setting (learn representations from training only) should be reported, or at least discussed.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a precise problem statement, objective, and algorithm. Notation is consistent, and the optimization steps are explicit. Minor issues: the loss definition c(x, y) appears with a missing square; the conditions on ρ and τ could be better motivated from Lipschitz constants; initialization and stopping criteria should be specified; and a brief discussion of computational complexity would improve readability.

Originality
The main novelty is the use of convex matrix completion with nonnegativity and sparsity to impute the bilingual document-term matrix prior to LSI. While nuclear-norm matrix completion is well-studied, its application to cross-lingual feature bridging in this form appears novel, and the combination with a subsequent LSI step is a distinctive design choice compared to CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA or pivot-based SCL.

Significance
Empirically, the approach yields consistent gains across many cross-lingual sentiment tasks and is effective with limited parallel data, which is practically valuable. If the optimization claims are strengthened and broader baselines included, the method could influence subsequent cross-lingual representation learning work. The current scope is limited to one dataset and task; demonstrating generality (e.g., topic classification, other language pairs, or low-resource languages) would further increase impact.

Pros
- Clear, simple two-step pipeline with a principled convex objective for imputation.
- Strong, consistent improvements over CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA across 18 tasks.
- Robust performance with very few parallel documents.
- Some theoretical grounding (non-expansivity) and clear algorithmic description.

Cons
- Convergence proof likely insufficient for the constrained composite objective; algorithm may be heuristic.
- No runtime/scalability analysis; repeated SVDs may be costly at scale.
- Missing key baselines (SCL, translate-train/test); no ablations to disentangle contributions (matrix completion vs. LSI).
- Transductive evaluation without an inductive counterpart; potential optimistic bias.
- Limited task/dataset diversity; no statistical significance testing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a correct optimization scheme (e.g., proximal gradient with the indicator handled as part of the non-smooth term, or ADMM) and a proof that iterations converge to an optimal solution of the constrained problem. Alternatively, clearly label the current approach as heuristic.
- Report computational complexity, wall-clock times, number of iterations, and use truncated/partial SVD where applicable.
- Add baselines: SCL on the Amazon dataset and translate-train/test (using MT or bilingual lexicons). Include an ablation that runs LSI directly on the partially observed matrix to isolate the value of completion.
- Include an inductive setting where representations are learned from training-only data, or justify the transductive protocol.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for γ, ρ, µ, and k; discuss the role of nonnegativity and sparsity; add statistical significance tests.
- Expand to additional datasets or tasks to demonstrate generality.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a plausible and potentially useful approach with encouraging empirical results and reasonable clarity. Addressing the optimization gap and strengthening the experimental baselines and analysis would substantially improve confidence in the contribution and its impact.
