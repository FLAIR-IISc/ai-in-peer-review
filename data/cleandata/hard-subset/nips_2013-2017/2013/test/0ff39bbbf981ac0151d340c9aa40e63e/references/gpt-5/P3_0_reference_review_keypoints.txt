- Summary: Two-step pipeline—convex matrix completion (trace norm + l1 + shrinkage + nonnegativity) on a concatenated bilingual document–term matrix with missing entries, followed by LSI (SVD) and SVM; shows gains on EN–FR/DE/JA Amazon reviews.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA, SCL, multilingual topic models, and convex matrix completion methods; novelty is imputing missing cross-lingual features before LSI.  
- Strength: Simple, modular method that plugs into standard classifiers and needs only modest parallel data.  
- Strength: Empirically strong and consistent improvements across 18 cross-lingual sentiment tasks, robust to few parallel documents.  
- Strength: Motivation for completion is clear—exploits cross-lingual co-occurrences missing from naive concatenation.  
- Strength: Nonnegativity and sparsity align with TF-IDF data and aid interpretability.  
- Theoretical concern: Convergence proof is insufficient—composition of two proximal operators (nuclear shrinkage and nonnegativity projection) does not generally equal the proximal of the sum; forward–backward splitting arguments are incomplete.  
- Suggested remedy (theory): Use Dykstra’s algorithm, ADMM, or primal–dual schemes with established convergence guarantees, or justify conditions for current operator composition.  
- Scalability concern: Each iteration requires an SVD; no complexity analysis, truncated SVD mention, iteration counts, or runtimes are provided—critical for large vocabularies.  
- Missing ablations: No baseline isolating completion vs. LSI (e.g., zero-filled + LSI), nor ablations for ρ (missing-entry penalty), µ (l1), nonnegativity, or “completion only.”  
- Baselines missing/weak: Omits translate-train/test, pivot-based SCL implementation, and bilingual/multilingual embeddings; SVM hyperparameters not tuned, potentially underrepresenting baselines.  
- Assumption/identifiability issue: Claim that document–term matrices are “naturally low-rank” is debatable; extreme missingness may make nuclear-norm completion ill-posed and the zero-shrinkage bias needs discussion.  
- Reproducibility issues: Missing details on stopping criteria, tolerances, initialization, normalization, SVD implementations, computational resources; references have formatting problems.  
- Quality assessment: Formulation is sensible and algorithm plausible but has an incomplete convergence argument; empirical evaluation is broad but lacks key ablations and stronger baselines.  
- Clarity: Paper is generally well organized and readable, but optimization section needs rigorous connection to proximal frameworks; experimental implementation details are lacking.  
- Originality: Novel application of matrix completion before LSI in cross-lingual setting, but mainly a straightforward composition of known tools.  
- Significance: Potentially useful for low-resource cross-lingual classification if stronger baselines, ablations, and scalability/theoretical fixes are provided.  
- Suggestions for authors: Provide principled optimization method with convergence proof, report complexity and runtimes (use truncated SVD), add ablations and stronger baselines, tune SVM C, clarify CL-LSI setup, and fix references.  
- Pros for acceptance: Simple, broadly applicable idea with strong empirical results and robustness to few parallel documents.  
- Cons for acceptance: Incomplete/possibly incorrect convergence justification, missing ablations and stronger baselines, scalability and reproducibility concerns.  
- Overall recommendation: Weak reject—promising idea but needs stronger optimization treatment, additional experiments, and scalability/reproducibility details to meet standards.
