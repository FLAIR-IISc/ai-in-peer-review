## Peer Review: A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification leveraging unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The method first constructs a concatenated document-term matrix with missing values for non-parallel documents and then employs matrix completion to fill these gaps, capturing cross-lingual co-occurrence patterns. Finally, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to the completed matrix to derive low-dimensional cross-lingual representations, enabling monolingual classifiers to be used. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, showing significant improvements over several baselines, and demonstrating performance even with limited parallel data.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper acknowledges and positions itself within the existing landscape of cross-lingual learning methods. It correctly identifies two main branches: those relying on machine translation and those building cross-lingual representations.  The authors highlight similarities to methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, all utilizing parallel data. However, they differentiate their work by employing matrix completion *before* dimensionality reduction, arguing this captures low-rank information more effectively.  The related work section is well-structured and provides relevant citations.  The authors appropriately frame their contribution as building upon existing methods rather than a radical departure.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI in this specific context is a reasonable and potentially effective approach. Applying matrix completion to address the challenge of incomplete cross-lingual document-term matrices is a good idea.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate consistently strong performance across a range of tasks. The significant improvements over baselines (especially TBOW) are compelling.  The ablation study (analyzing the impact of parallel data size) further strengthens the claims.
* **Theoretical Justification:** While not deeply complex, the authors provide a reasonable theoretical grounding for the matrix completion step, including a convex relaxation and a projected gradient descent algorithm. The convergence analysis for the algorithm is a positive addition.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The two-step process is clearly explained, and the mathematical formulations are presented reasonably concisely.
* **Practicality:** The method demonstrates good performance with a relatively small amount of parallel data, which is a key advantage in real-world scenarios where such resources can be scarce.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Depth:**  While the optimization problem is stated and analyzed, the rationale *why* a low-rank matrix completion is a good assumption for this problem isn’t fully explored.  A more in-depth discussion of the theoretical properties of the recovered matrix would be beneficial.
* **Algorithm Complexity:** The paper mentions the scalability of the projected gradient descent algorithm but lacks concrete details on computational cost and memory requirements. A discussion of these practical limitations, perhaps with results on larger datasets, would be valuable.  The convergence analysis doesn't provide empirical evidence of how quickly the algorithm converges in practice.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions parameter selection (γ, ρ, k) and the method used (using EFB for initial tuning), but provides limited insights into the sensitivity of performance to these parameters. Some discussion of parameter robustness would be useful.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to existing methods is solid, incorporating more recent and sophisticated cross-lingual learning techniques (e.g., those leveraging pre-trained multilingual language models like mBERT or XLM-R) would further strengthen the evaluation.
* **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on overall accuracy. An error analysis identifying the types of errors the method makes (e.g., misclassifying specific sentiment expressions) could provide valuable insights.



**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong empirical results demonstrating significant improvements over established baselines.
* Novel and well-motivated approach addressing a challenging problem in cross-lingual learning.
* Clear and well-organized presentation.
* Inclusion of theoretical analysis (optimization, convergence) adds credibility.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited theoretical justification for the core assumption of low-rank matrix completion.
* Lack of discussion regarding computational complexity and scalability.
* Parameter sensitivity not thoroughly explored.
* Could benefit from comparison to more modern baselines (e.g., methods based on pre-trained multilingual models).




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. While the theoretical depth and discussion of practical considerations could be expanded, the strong empirical results and novel approach justify acceptance.  The method appears practical and offers a tangible improvement over existing techniques, particularly in low-resource settings.

**Recommendation:**  **Accept** with minor revisions.  I recommend the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly by:

*  Elaborating on the theoretical rationale for the matrix completion step.
*  Providing more details on the computational complexity and scalability of the algorithm.
*  Discussing the sensitivity of performance to the key hyperparameters.
*  Considering including a comparison to more recent baselines leveraging pre-trained multilingual models.
