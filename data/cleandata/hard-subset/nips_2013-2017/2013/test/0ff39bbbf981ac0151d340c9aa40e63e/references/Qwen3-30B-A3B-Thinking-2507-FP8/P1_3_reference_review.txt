

# Peer Review: "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that leverages unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to learn cross-lingual representations. The authors first construct a concatenated document-term matrix where parallel documents are fully observed and non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language. They then use matrix completion to fill these missing entries, followed by latent semantic indexing to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. This approach differs from prior work like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, which apply LSI or similar techniques directly to the partially observed matrix rather than first completing the missing values.

The paper builds upon recent work in cross-lingual representation learning [10, 15, 20] but introduces a novel two-step procedure that addresses the language barrier through matrix completion. The authors evaluate their method on 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using the multilingual Amazon product reviews dataset, demonstrating consistent improvements over several baselines.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Approach**: The two-step method (matrix completion followed by LSI) is conceptually novel and addresses a key limitation of previous methods that treat missing entries as zeros. The paper clearly articulates how this differs from CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA.

2. **Theoretical Foundation**: The authors provide a well-justified convex optimization formulation for the matrix completion problem and include a rigorous convergence analysis for their projected gradient descent algorithm (Theorem 1). This theoretical grounding strengthens the paper's contribution.

3. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experimental setup is thorough, with 18 different cross-lingual tasks across three languages (English, French, German, Japanese) and three product categories. The authors also investigate the impact of varying amounts of unlabeled parallel data (from 200 to 2000 documents), demonstrating the robustness of their method.

4. **Strong Results**: The TSL method outperforms all baselines consistently across all 18 tasks, with improvements of 8.59-14.61% over the target baseline (TBOW). The method is particularly effective with small amounts of parallel data (only 200 documents), which is a significant practical advantage.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with Recent Methods**: The paper does not compare with more recent cross-lingual representation learning methods, such as those based on multilingual BERT or other transformer-based approaches. Given the dominance of neural methods in NLP, this omission weakens the significance of the comparison.

2. **Limited Analysis of Matrix Completion**: While the matrix completion approach is theoretically sound, the paper lacks deeper analysis of why their method works better than simply using a small amount of parallel data for supervised fine-tuning. It would be valuable to see how the matrix completion captures cross-lingual relationships that are not captured by direct alignment methods.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper reports optimal parameters for TSL (γ=0.1, ρ=10^-4, k=50) but doesn't thoroughly analyze how sensitive the results are to these parameters. A more detailed sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims.

4. **Ambiguity in "Unlabeled Parallel Documents"**: The paper doesn't clearly explain how the unlabeled parallel documents are obtained. In practice, parallel documents typically require manual translation or machine translation, which could introduce noise. The authors should clarify whether these documents are truly "unlabeled" or if they contain some form of translation.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound approach to cross-lingual representation learning. The two-step method is novel and the theoretical analysis is strong. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, particularly in the low-resource setting where only small amounts of parallel data are available.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing with more recent neural approaches to cross-lingual representation learning and by providing more detailed analysis of the matrix completion process. The omission of these comparisons is notable given the current state of the field.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning with a novel and well-justified approach. The results are compelling and the method has clear practical advantages. The weaknesses identified are not fundamental to the contribution but would strengthen the paper if addressed.

## Specific Suggestions for Revision

1. Add a comparison with at least one recent neural cross-lingual method (e.g., multilingual BERT) to better position the contribution in the current NLP landscape.

2. Expand the discussion on how the unlabeled parallel documents are obtained, addressing potential concerns about their quality and the effort required to collect them.

3. Include a brief analysis of how the matrix completion process captures cross-lingual relationships, perhaps with a small case study showing example words that are successfully aligned.

4. Add a sentence or two discussing the computational complexity of the method compared to the baselines, especially for large-scale applications.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Novel two-step approach to cross-lingual representation learning
- Strong theoretical foundation with convergence analysis
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple languages and tasks
- Demonstrates significant improvements over baselines (8.59-14.61%)
- Works effectively with very small amounts of parallel data (only 200 documents)

## Arguments Against Acceptance (Minor)

- Limited comparison with recent neural methods
- Insufficient analysis of matrix completion's role in capturing cross-lingual relationships
- Ambiguity in the nature of "unlabeled parallel documents"
