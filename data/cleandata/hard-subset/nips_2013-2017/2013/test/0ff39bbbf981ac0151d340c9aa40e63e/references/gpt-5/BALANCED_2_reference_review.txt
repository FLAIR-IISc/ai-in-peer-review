Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-step approach (TSL) for cross-lingual text classification using unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 constructs a unified document-term matrix over the concatenated vocabularies of two languages. Parallel document pairs occupy a single row with both halves observed; non-parallel documents have entries missing on the other-language half. The missing entries are imputed by solving a convex matrix completion problem with nuclear norm (low-rank) and L1 (sparsity) regularization, squared losses on observed entries, a small quadratic penalty on missing entries to avoid overfitting, and nonnegativity constraints. The optimization is performed via a projected gradient descent scheme: a gradient step on the smooth part, singular value shrinkage for the nuclear norm, and projection onto the nonnegative orthant, with a convergence argument based on non-expansive mappings. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix and trains a linear SVM on the induced low-dimensional representation.

The approach is compared to TBOW (target-only bag-of-words) and three cross-lingual representation methods: CL-LSI (Dumais et al.; Littman et al.), CL-KCCA (Vinokourov et al.), and CL-OPCA (Platt et al.). Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews (18 language/category transfer tasks) report consistent gains for TSL, including under reduced amounts of parallel data.

Relative to prior work, TSL differs from MT/lexicon translation pipelines and from CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA that operate directly on concatenated or paired data by first performing matrix completion to denoise and couple the bilingual feature space. The optimization builds on nuclear-norm matrix completion (Candès & Recht) and shrinkage-based solvers (Ma et al.).

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation that directly models the bilingual concatenated document-term matrix with principled low-rank plus sparsity priors and nonnegativity.
- Simple, modular pipeline: matrix completion followed by LSI and standard classifiers.
- Theoretical convergence argument for the proposed iterative scheme (under stated step-size conditions).
- Empirically strong and robust results across 18 transfer settings and under varying numbers of parallel documents, with especially promising performance when few parallel pairs are available.
- Sensible hyperparameter selection protocol (fixed across tasks after tuning on one).

Weaknesses
- Potential transductive evaluation: the paper states that “all documents from the two languages” are used to learn the representation, which appears to include test documents. This can inflate performance for all methods that learn representations from unlabeled data and should be controlled (train/dev-only representation learning).
- Baseline comparability is unclear. For CL-LSI/CL-OPCA, the original formulations typically require fully dual-language documents (via translation) or strictly parallel corpora; here, it seems missing entries are treated as zeros. That can disadvantage these baselines relative to TSL, whose core contribution is precisely to avoid treating missing entries as zeros. A stronger comparison would either (i) translate training documents (as in the prior works), or (ii) adapt those methods to the same partially observed setting with principled handling of missing data.
- Missing strong cross-lingual baselines cited in the related work, notably Structural Correspondence Learning (SCL; Prettenhofer & Stein, 2010), which is a standard and competitive method on this dataset.
- Limited ablations. It is unclear how much each component contributes. For example, (i) using the completed matrix directly vs. after LSI, (ii) effect of the ℓ1 term, (iii) effect of the nonnegativity constraint, (iv) sensitivity to ρ that penalizes missing entries toward zero.
- Optimization practicality not discussed: per-iteration SVD on large, high-dimensional matrices is expensive. No runtime, iteration counts, or use of truncated/randomized SVD are reported. Scalability to larger corpora/vocabularies is not demonstrated.
- Convergence proof uses composition of shrinkage and projection; while non-expansiveness is shown, convergence to the solution of the exact composite objective with nuclear norm plus nonnegativity indicator typically requires proximal splitting arguments. The current argument is terse and would benefit from clarification or appropriate citations to algorithms that alternate two proximal operators (e.g., projected proximal gradient or Dykstra-like splitting).
- References are incomplete (missing years/pages) and there are minor presentation issues.

Evaluation against NeurIPS criteria
- Quality: The method is technically plausible and well-motivated; the optimization is reasonable, though the proof/algorithmic justification should be tightened. The empirical gains are strong but undermined by potential test-time leakage and possibly disadvantaged baselines.
- Clarity: The high-level idea and notation are clear. More detail is needed on how baselines are instantiated in the partially observed setting, exact data splits for representation learning, and computational costs.
- Originality: Moderate. It combines known ideas (nuclear-norm matrix completion, LSI) in a novel way for cross-lingual representation learning with partially observed bilingual matrices.
- Significance: Potentially useful if validated under stricter, fair comparisons and scaling evidence. Current empirical setup limits confidence.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually neat reframing of cross-lingual representation as matrix completion plus LSI.
- Strong and robust empirical results on a standard benchmark.
- Requires few parallel documents, addressing a practical constraint.

Cons for acceptance
- Possible transductive use of test data in representation learning.
- Baseline implementations may not reflect best-known setups; key baseline (SCL) missing.
- Lack of ablation/scalability analysis; optimization practicality not demonstrated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Re-run experiments ensuring representation learning uses only training (and dev) data; exclude test documents in all methods.
- Include SCL and a translation-based baseline (e.g., translate-train or translate-test) to strengthen comparisons; clarify and, if needed, strengthen CL-LSI/CL-OPCA setups (e.g., by translating training documents to form dual-language inputs).
- Provide ablations: no-L1; no-LSI; varying ρ, γ; effect of nonnegativity; effect of the number of shrinkage iterations.
- Report training time, number of iterations, and SVD strategy (full vs. truncated/randomized), and discuss scalability.
- Clarify the theoretical justification for applying shrinkage then nonnegativity projection; cite appropriate proximal splitting literature or adjust the algorithm accordingly.
- Improve references and fix minor typos.

Overall recommendation
Reject in current form. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the evaluation protocol and baseline choices prevent a confident assessment of the contribution. With corrected experimental setup, stronger baselines, and additional analysis, this work could be competitive.
