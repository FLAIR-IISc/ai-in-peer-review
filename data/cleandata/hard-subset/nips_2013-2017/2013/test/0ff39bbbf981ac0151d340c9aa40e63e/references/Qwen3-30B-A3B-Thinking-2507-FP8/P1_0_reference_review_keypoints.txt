- Proposes a two-step cross-lingual method: matrix completion to fill missing bilingual document-term entries, then LSI for low-dimensional cross-lingual representations.  
- Evaluated on 18 cross-lingual sentiment tasks showing consistent improvements over CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA.  
- Technically sound with a clear convex formulation and convergence analysis for the projected gradient descent algorithm.  
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive (3 languages × 3 categories × multiple repetitions) but omits recent neural cross-lingual baselines.  
- Parameter selection is documented but appears ad-hoc and justified only on a single task before being applied broadly.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written; mathematical formulation and experimental setup are precise.  
- Lacks a stronger theoretical or empirical justification for preferring matrix completion over other missing-data techniques.  
- The two-step use of matrix completion then LSI is novel in this context, though matrix completion itself is not new.  
- Addresses an important problem (limited labeled data in target languages) and shows sizable accuracy gains (8.59–14.61% avg).  
- Demonstrates robustness with relatively few parallel documents, offering practical advantages for resource-limited scenarios.  
- Strength: novel two-step approach compared to applying LSI directly to partial matrices.  
- Strength: thorough empirical validation across many tasks with statistical significance checks.  
- Strength: method performs well with limited unlabeled parallel data and includes convergence analysis.  
- Weakness: comparisons exclude modern neural transfer methods, limiting contemporary relevance.  
- Weakness: insufficient explanation of why matrix completion is especially suitable.  
- Weakness: parameter tuning process is somewhat arbitrary and not validated across tasks.  
- Weakness: claim of “very small” parallel data is questionable given experiments use 2000 unlabeled documents.  
- Weakness: lacks discussion of computational complexity and scalability relative to baselines.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: add comparisons to neural cross-lingual methods; justify matrix completion choice; discuss computational complexity/scalability; clarify the “very small” parallel data claim with context or comparisons.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, significant empirical gains, comprehensive experiments, theoretical convergence, and practical utility.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing modern baselines, weak parameter justification, unclear data-claim, and no scalability analysis.
