This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification. The core idea is to construct a concatenated document-term matrix from source and target languages, perform matrix completion to fill missing values, and then apply latent semantic indexing (LSI) for dimensionality reduction and classification. The approach leverages unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to bridge the language gap. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivation and proposed method. It appropriately positions its work within the existing literature on cross-language text classification, contrasting it with methods relying on machine translation, domain adaptation, and other cross-lingual representation learning techniques. The related work section is comprehensive and cites relevant publications, including several from recent NIPS proceedings. The mathematical formulation of the matrix completion problem is clearly presented, and the optimization algorithm (projected gradient descent) is described in detail. The experimental setup and results are also well-documented, with comparisons to several baseline methods and an analysis of the impact of parallel data size.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** While matrix completion and LSI are established techniques, their combination within a two-step framework specifically designed for cross-lingual representation learning, particularly leveraging unlabeled parallel data in this manner, presents a novel approach. The use of matrix completion to directly address the missing data problem in the concatenated document-term matrix is a key contribution.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise and informative. The algorithm is described in a step-by-step manner, which facilitates implementation and reproducibility.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is based on solid theoretical foundations. The optimization algorithm is well-justified, and the convergence analysis provides assurance of its effectiveness. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach compared to existing methods.
*   **Significance:** The results consistently show improvements over several strong baselines, suggesting the proposed method offers a practical and effective solution for cross-language text classification. The finding that the method performs well even with limited parallel data is particularly valuable, as obtaining large parallel corpora is often a significant bottleneck.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions parameter selection using a separate task (EFB), but doesn’t thoroughly discuss the sensitivity of the results to these parameters. A more comprehensive analysis of parameter sensitivity and potential robustness to parameter variations would strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Dataset:** The experiments are conducted solely on the Amazon product reviews dataset. While this dataset is commonly used in cross-lingual learning, evaluating the method on more diverse datasets, covering different domains and languages, would increase confidence in its generalizability.
*   **Algorithm 1 Detail:** While Algorithm 1 is detailed, the stepsize τ is simply stated as τ=1. Justification for this selection, or an explanation of how τ is tuned, would be helpful.
*   **Computational Complexity:** A discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly the matrix completion step, would be beneficial. While the authors mention scalability in the optimization section, a more detailed analysis of runtime performance on larger datasets would be valuable.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound method for cross-language text classification.
*   The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over strong baselines.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.
*   The method is particularly valuable due to its ability to perform well with limited parallel data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The evaluation is limited to a single dataset.
*   A more thorough analysis of parameter sensitivity is needed.
*   The computational complexity of the method is not fully discussed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of cross-language text classification. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and demonstrates promising results. While there are a few minor weaknesses, they do not significantly detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above (parameter sensitivity, dataset diversity, and computational complexity).
