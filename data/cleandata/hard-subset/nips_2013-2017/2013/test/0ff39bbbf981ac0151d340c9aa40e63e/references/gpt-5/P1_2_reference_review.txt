Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles cross-language text classification by inducing a shared representation from a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. The key idea is a two-step pipeline: (1) construct a unified document–term matrix whose columns are the concatenated vocabularies of two languages; rows corresponding to non-parallel documents are partially observed (missing the other-language half). Recover the missing entries by convex matrix completion with a trace norm (to encourage low-rank) and an ℓ1 term (to encourage sparsity), plus nonnegativity constraints and a small quadratic penalty on missing entries. This is solved by an iterative projected gradient method with singular value shrinkage. (2) Apply LSI (SVD) on the completed matrix to obtain dense, low-dimensional cross-lingual document representations; train a standard linear SVM. Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews show consistent improvements over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, with good performance even with few parallel pairs.

This connects to prior work that either translates documents and then applies monolingual learning or learns interlingual representations via CL-LSI, CCA/KCCA, OPCA, or topic models. The proposed method differs by explicitly completing the unified bilingual matrix before dimensionality reduction, rather than operating directly on the original (sparse, partially observed) matrix. The optimization is motivated by SVT-based nuclear norm minimization (Ma et al.), while the downstream LSI step follows Littman et al. and later CL-LSI variants.

Strengths
- Simple, intuitive formulation that exploits unlabeled parallel data without requiring translating all documents.
- Convex objective for matrix completion with a scalable proximal-gradient-like algorithm; clear derivations for gradients and non-expansiveness lemmas.
- Empirically strong across 18 cross-lingual sentiment tasks; gains are consistent and robust to the amount of parallel data, with good performance even at 200 pairs.
- Sensible protocol: fixed hyperparameters across tasks after tuning on a single task; repeated trials with mean and std reported.

Weaknesses
- Optimization and convergence: the algorithm applies shrinkage (prox for nuclear norm) followed by projection onto nonnegativity after a gradient step on the smooth part. For a sum of two nonsmooth terms (nuclear norm + indicator of nonnegativity), sequential application of prox operators is not, in general, the exact prox of the sum. The convergence proof via composition of non-expansive maps shows non-expansiveness, but does not establish that fixed points of the iteration are minimizers of the stated objective. A more standard approach (e.g., ADMM, projected proximal gradient with Dykstra, or primal-dual/forward–backward–forward splitting) and an accompanying optimality proof would strengthen the technical soundness.
- Computational details are sparse. Each iteration requires an SVD; no discussion of complexity, use of partial SVD, warm starts, number of iterations, or wall-clock time. Claims of scalability are not substantiated.
- Missing or weak baselines/ablations. In particular:
  - “LSI on M0 with zeros for missing entries” is a crucial baseline to isolate the value of completion vs. direct LSI.
  - An ablation showing “MC only” (classify in the completed high-dimensional space) and “LSI only” would clarify the contribution of each step.
  - CL-SCL (Prettenhofer & Stein) is cited but not compared experimentally.
- Assumptions and modeling choices are insufficiently discussed. The low-rank assumption for term–document matrices is arguable; demonstrating singular value decay and the effect of γ, ρ would help. Penalizing missing entries toward zero (ρ term) biases the completion; a sensitivity plot and rationale would be useful.
- Evaluation scope is narrow (single dataset family and task). No qualitative analysis (e.g., cross-lingual alignments recovered, example completions) to build intuition.

Quality
- The empirical study is solid within its scope and suggests the method works well.
- The main technical concern is the convergence/optimality argument for the composite nonsmooth objective; the current proof is not fully convincing. Additional algorithmic detail and a more standard optimization framework would address this.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and easy to follow. The construction of M0, the objective, and gradients are clearly presented.
- Missing implementation details (stopping criteria, SVD computation, complexity) reduce reproducibility.
- A figure illustrating the unified matrix with observed/missing blocks would help.

Originality
- The combination “matrix completion of a bilingual unified doc–term matrix” followed by LSI appears novel relative to CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA. Conceptually simple but nontrivial.
- Related work is broadly covered, though more recent cross-lingual embedding approaches are not discussed; if the paper targets a current venue, comparison to bilingual/multilingual embeddings and mapping-based methods would be expected.

Significance
- If the gains generalize beyond sentiment and Amazon reviews, the method could be a practical alternative when only small parallel corpora are available.
- The simplicity of the pipeline is attractive. However, missing baselines and limited analysis temper claims of broad impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Replace the current solver with a method that provably optimizes the stated objective (e.g., ADMM handling nuclear norm and nonnegativity, or primal–dual splitting), and provide a correct convergence proof to optimality. Alternatively, justify that the current fixed-point iteration solves the problem (e.g., via monotone operator theory) and specify conditions.
- Report runtime, number of iterations, SVD strategy (truncated, randomized), and memory use.
- Add baseline “LSI on M0 with zeros,” ablations (MC-only, LSI-only), and include SCL in experiments. Provide sensitivity analyses for γ, ρ, k and singular value spectra of M*.
- Extend to another dataset/task and include qualitative analyses of cross-lingual alignments recovered by completion.
- Release code.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear idea with a simple pipeline; promising and consistent empirical gains.
- Low-resource setting performance with few parallel pairs is practically meaningful.
- Convex formulation (if solved properly) is appealing.

Arguments against acceptance
- Optimization/convergence claims are not fully sound for the composite nonsmooth objective as implemented.
- Missing key baselines/ablations and limited analysis undermine the strength of the empirical claims.
- Limited scope of evaluation and lack of computational detail.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The approach is interesting and empirically promising, but the optimization rigor and experimental breadth need strengthening for a top-tier venue. With the suggested fixes, this could become a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
