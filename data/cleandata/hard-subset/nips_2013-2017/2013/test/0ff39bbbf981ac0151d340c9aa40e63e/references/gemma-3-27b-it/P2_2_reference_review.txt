## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification. The method addresses the language barrier by first constructing a concatenated document-term matrix (with missing values for non-parallel documents) and then completing this matrix using a matrix completion technique. Finally, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to the completed matrix to derive cross-lingual document representations. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, showing improvements over several baselines.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon existing work in cross-lingual learning, acknowledging approaches based on machine translation, bilingual lexicons, and the construction of cross-lingual representations using resources like bilingual word pairs and parallel corpora. It positions itself within the latter category, specifically comparing to methods like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, highlighting its novel approach of *first* completing the matrix before applying dimensionality reduction via LSI.  This distinction is crucial, as it attempts to leverage the limited parallel data more effectively.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The two-step approach of matrix completion *followed by* LSI is a reasonable and interesting idea.  Filling in missing values in the concatenated matrix before dimensionality reduction appears intuitively helpful, as it potentially allows the LSI to operate on a more complete representation of the relationships between terms.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate superior performance compared to several baselines across a range of cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks. The ablation study concerning the size of parallel data is valuable, showing robustness even with limited parallel resources.
* **Theoretical Justification (to a degree):**  The authors attempt to ground their method in theoretical considerations by framing matrix completion as an optimization problem and citing relevant work on projected gradient descent. The inclusion of a convergence analysis (though concise) adds to the technical rigor.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The approach is explained in a clear and understandable manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Depth:** While the authors mention the theoretical basis of matrix completion and optimization, the discussion remains relatively superficial. A more in-depth analysis of the properties of the completed matrix and its impact on the quality of the LSI representation would be beneficial. Why does filling in the missing values *specifically* help LSI?  A more convincing argument is needed.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions parameter selection for the method. However, it is not entirely clear how representative the parameter settings are for tasks beyond EFB. The selection process is not fully explained and could be a potential limitation.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparisons to CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA are useful, the paper doesn’t compare against more recent and sophisticated cross-lingual transfer learning techniques, such as those leveraging pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-RoBERTa). These have become state-of-the-art in many cross-lingual tasks and should be included for a comprehensive evaluation.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper doesn't adequately discuss the computational cost of the matrix completion step, especially for large datasets. Scalability is an important consideration, and some discussion of this aspect is needed.
* **Lack of Error Analysis:**  The paper lacks an analysis of *why* the method succeeds.  What types of errors does it still make? Examining the failed cases could offer valuable insights.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to cross-lingual representation learning.
* The empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over several existing methods.
* The paper is clearly written and logically organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical justification is somewhat superficial.
* The comparison to state-of-the-art baselines is incomplete.
* The computational cost and scalability concerns are not addressed.
* Limited error analysis.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. The proposed method is novel, empirically successful, and well-presented. However, the limitations regarding theoretical depth, comparison to stronger baselines, and computational considerations prevent me from giving it a strong recommendation.  

**Rating:**  **Weak Accept**.

I recommend acceptance, but with the caveat that the authors address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly regarding a comparison to more recent state-of-the-art techniques and a discussion of computational scalability.  A stronger theoretical justification would also be a welcome addition.
