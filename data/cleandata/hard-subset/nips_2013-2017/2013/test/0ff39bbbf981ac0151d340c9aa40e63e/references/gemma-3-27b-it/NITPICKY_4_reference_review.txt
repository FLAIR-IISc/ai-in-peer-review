## Review of “A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning”

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning, leveraging unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The approach involves matrix completion to handle missing values in a concatenated document-term matrix, followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) to derive low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The method is evaluated on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, and demonstrates improvements over several baselines.

While the core idea of leveraging matrix completion for cross-lingual representation learning is interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of shortcomings, ranging from insufficient novelty to a lack of rigorous experimentation and overly detailed, yet ultimately unconvincing, optimization analysis. 

**Strengths:**

*   The problem addressed – cross-lingual text classification – is a relevant and important area within NLP.
*   The empirical results show some improvement over baseline methods.
*   The authors attempt a detailed optimization analysis, including convergence proof.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Novelty:** The paper’s core components—matrix completion and LSI—are well-established techniques. The combination, while reasonable, doesn't constitute a significant conceptual leap. The connection to existing work is acknowledged, but the incremental nature of the contribution is downplayed. The authors state their method differs from CL-LSI, CL-KCCA and CL-OPCA by *first* filling missing entries; this is a minor implementation detail, not a fundamentally new approach.
*   **Insufficient Experimental Rigor:** The experimental setup is questionable. Using only 100 labeled target language documents is a severely limited evaluation scenario. The sensitivity of results to this small sample size is not adequately discussed. The parameter selection process (using EFB for tuning and then applying those parameters to all tasks) is concerning and may lead to overfitting to the specific characteristics of EFB. Reporting standard deviations is good, but the lack of statistical significance tests prevents a reliable assessment of the reported improvements. Furthermore, the choice of baseline methods seems somewhat arbitrary; more recent and competitive approaches should be included.
*   **Overly Detailed Optimization Analysis:** Sections 4.1 and 4.2 dedicate a considerable amount of space to the optimization algorithm and its convergence analysis. While mathematically rigorous, this level of detail feels unnecessary and detracts from the discussion of the core contributions. The convergence proof relies on established lemmas, offering limited new insight. Furthermore, the algorithm itself (projected gradient descent) is standard, lacking any novel acceleration or adaptation strategies.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The writing style is often verbose and repetitive. For example, the authors repeatedly emphasize the limited amount of parallel data. While it’s a motivation, it is overdone. The notation could be more consistent and the paper would benefit from clearer visual aids (e.g., diagrams illustrating the two-step process). The use of “M0” is confusing as it is introduced without clear explanation.
*   **Missing Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks ablation studies to isolate the impact of each step (matrix completion vs. LSI). Is the matrix completion step truly essential, or does LSI on the original sparse matrix provide comparable performance?
*    **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on successes. A discussion of scenarios where the proposed method fails or performs poorly would provide a more balanced and insightful evaluation.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   Incremental novelty.
*   Questionable experimental setup and limited statistical analysis.
*   Overly detailed and irrelevant optimization analysis.
*   Lack of ablation studies and discussion of failure cases.
*   Verbose and repetitive writing.

**For:**

*   Addresses a relevant research problem.
*   Demonstrates some empirical improvement over baselines.
*   Attempts a rigorous mathematical analysis (though arguably excessive).

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound but ultimately unremarkable contribution to the field of cross-lingual representation learning. While the experimental results suggest some improvement over baselines, the limitations in experimental design and the lack of significant novelty raise serious concerns. The extensive optimization analysis feels misplaced and distracts from the core ideas.

**Recommendation:** **Reject.**

The paper, in its current form, does not meet the standards of originality and significance required for acceptance at NIPS. Substantial revisions, including a more rigorous experimental evaluation, a more focused discussion of the key contributions, and a streamlined presentation, would be necessary for reconsideration.
