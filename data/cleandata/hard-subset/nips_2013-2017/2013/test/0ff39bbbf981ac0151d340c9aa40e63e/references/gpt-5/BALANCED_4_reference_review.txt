Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-lingual text classification that uses a small set of unlabeled parallel documents to induce shared representations. Step 1 performs matrix completion on a unified bilingual document-term matrix: each row is a document represented in the concatenated vocabulary, with entries for the “other” language missing unless the document has a parallel counterpart. The completion is cast as convex optimization with nuclear norm (low-rank), an ℓ1 term (sparsity) and nonnegativity, along with squared losses on observed entries and a shrinkage toward zero on missing entries (weighted by ρ). A projected proximal gradient method with singular value thresholding is used, with a simple convergence argument based on non-expansive operators. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to produce dense low-dimensional representations on which a linear SVM is trained.

This situates among cross-lingual representation approaches that exploit parallel/comparable data: CL-LSI (Littman et al., 1998/99), KCCA (Vinokourov et al., 2003), OPCA (Platt et al., 2010), SCL (Prettenhofer & Stein, 2010), and multilingual topic models. The key difference is to first impute missing cross-language features via low-rank completion on the bilingual document-term matrix, rather than applying LSI/CCA directly. The method is evaluated on Amazon reviews for sentiment across English, French, German, and Japanese, and compared to TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA. Reported results show consistent gains, especially with few parallel documents.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity: a clear, modular pipeline (impute, then LSI), leveraging well-studied convex surrogates (nuclear norm) and standard proximal algorithms.
- Practical focus: uses only a small set of parallel documents; robust performance even with 200 parallel pairs.
- Empirical breadth within the chosen benchmark: 18 language/domain pairs; sensitivity to the number of parallel pairs; consistent improvements over several canonical cross-lingual baselines.
- Some theoretical grounding: convergence of the projected proximal scheme is sketched using non-expansive operator arguments.

Weaknesses and detailed comments (by review criteria)

Quality
- Algorithmic correctness: The optimization formulation is reasonable, but several choices deserve deeper analysis. With nonnegativity, the ℓ1 penalty becomes µ sum Mij, effectively a bias toward smaller mass; with µ fixed to 1e−6 it may be negligible—an ablation is needed to justify the term. The ρ-weighted squared loss on missing entries is equivalent to shrinkage toward zero, which can bias the completion toward overly sparse/low-magnitude solutions; a discussion of this inductive bias and sensitivity to ρ would help.
- Theoretical novelty is limited. The convergence proof leverages known properties of singular value thresholding and projections; no new complexity or rate guarantees are provided. Lipschitz constants and step-size discussion are somewhat ad hoc (τ < min(2, 2/ρ)).
- Empirical evaluation: While results appear strong, the paper lacks ablations (e.g., matrix completion alone vs. with LSI; LSI alone on the original concatenated matrix; effect of nonnegativity; effect of µ, γ, ρ; different k). Runtime/scalability claims are unsupported: the method requires repeated SVDs on large t×d matrices; complexity, number of iterations, and use of truncated/randomized SVD are not reported. Reproducibility suffers from missing details (vocabulary size/cutoffs, stopping criteria, initialization beyond “rank-1 approx,” exact data preprocessing).
- Evaluation protocol: Representation learning uses “all documents from the two languages” (including test documents) plus parallel data. This is transductive/unsupervised target access; please clarify if this is intended and ensure fairness by aligning baselines to the same protocol. Also consider an inductive setting where only training unlabeled data is used.

Clarity
- Generally readable and well organized; the approach is clearly described.
- Some notation issues (h(·) = I(·) − τ∇g(·) uses I ambiguously for identity), and typos/spacing (min(2, 2 ρ ); missing vector/matrix norms).
- Several references are incomplete (missing years/venues), which hinders contextualization and verification.
- Table/Figure details are not included in the text provided (exact accuracies not visible), limiting assessment.

Originality
- The combination—matrix completion on a bilingual document-term matrix followed by LSI—is a modest, incremental idea building on standard components (soft-impute/proximal SVT; CL-LSI). Imputing cross-lingual features via low-rank structure is an interesting twist, but close in spirit to weighted low-rank factorization or NMF variants. Positioning relative to SCL (pivot-based), bilingual embeddings, and joint topic/embedding models could be strengthened.

Significance
- Improvements on Amazon reviews are promising, especially with limited parallel data. However, significance is limited by the narrow evaluation (one dataset, one task family) and the absence of stronger/de facto modern baselines (e.g., SCL; bilingual/aligning word/sentence embeddings). It is unclear whether the approach scales to larger corpora/vocabularies or transfers to other tasks (topic classification, genre, etc.).

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, potentially practical method that uses few parallel pairs.
- Consistent empirical gains over several classical baselines across many language pairs.
- Clear algorithmic description with a convergence argument.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty; largely a composition of known techniques.
- Missing ablations, scalability analysis, and detailed reproducibility information.
- Narrow evaluation; lacks comparisons to important baselines (e.g., SCL; modern cross-lingual embeddings).
- Transductive protocol not clearly justified; potential evaluation confounds.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: (i) no completion + LSI; (ii) completion without LSI; (iii) effect of µ, γ, ρ, nonnegativity; (iv) k-sensitivity. Report wall-clock time, SVD strategy, iterations to convergence.
- Compare against SCL, and if possible, cross-lingual embedding baselines (e.g., bilingual skip-gram, mapping-based alignments, multilingual sentence embeddings) using identical unlabeled resources.
- Clarify the inductive vs. transductive setup; ensure baselines use the same unlabeled pool. Consider a setting where only training unlabeled data are used.
- Provide evidence supporting the low-rank assumption (e.g., singular value spectra before/after completion).
- Clean up notation and complete references; provide code or detailed pseudo-code and stopping criteria.
- Evaluate beyond sentiment and/or on additional datasets to demonstrate generality.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The paper is clear and the empirical gains are encouraging, but the methodological novelty is modest, and the experimental evaluation and analysis are not yet at the standard expected for NeurIPS. With stronger baselines, thorough ablations and scalability analysis, and broader validation, this work could be much more compelling.
