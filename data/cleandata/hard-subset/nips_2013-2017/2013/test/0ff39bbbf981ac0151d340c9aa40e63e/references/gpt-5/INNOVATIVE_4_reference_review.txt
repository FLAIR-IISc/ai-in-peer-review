Summary
This paper tackles cross-language text classification with a two-step representation learning approach. Given a unified document–term matrix built from two languages (columns are the concatenated vocabularies), parallel document pairs are fully observed rows, while non-parallel documents have missing entries in the other language’s columns. Step 1 performs matrix completion on this extremely sparse nonnegative matrix via a convex formulation: a least-squares loss on observed entries, a quadratic regularizer on missing entries, nuclear norm (to promote low rank), and an ℓ1 penalty which reduces to a linear term under nonnegativity; the problem is solved by a projected (proximal) gradient method with singular value shrinkage and nonnegativity projection. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain dense, low-dimensional cross-lingual representations on which a standard monolingual classifier is trained. Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews across 18 transfer tasks show improvements over CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, and a weak TBOW baseline, and demonstrate robustness when the number of parallel documents is small.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Casting cross-lingual representation induction as matrix completion over a unified bilingual document–term matrix is a fresh and appealing idea. The explicit treatment of missing “other-language” features for non-parallel documents is natural and exploits low-rank structure induced by cross-lingual co-occurrences.
- Bold, simple pipeline: The two-step “completion then LSI” is easy to understand and potentially broadly applicable to other multi-view/multilingual scenarios with missing views.
- Solid empirical gains: On 18 English↔{French, German, Japanese} sentiment tasks, the method generally outperforms CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, with especially strong performance when only 200 parallel documents are available—suggesting practical utility when parallel data is scarce.
- Optimization is convex and uses standard proximal operations; convergence arguments are provided, and the algorithm is implementable.

Weaknesses
- Missing strong baselines: Structural Correspondence Learning (SCL) [Prettenhofer & Stein, ACL 2010] is a well-known and strong baseline on this dataset and is cited but not included. Also, translation-based baselines (translate-train or translate-test with a commodity MT system) are common and would strengthen claims. More recent cross-lingual embedding approaches are not considered (though may be contemporaneous depending on the timeframe).
- Theory/proximal details: The convergence proof is sketched via non-expansiveness of components. However, the update uses a composition of singular value shrinkage and nonnegativity projection; convergence to an optimizer of the sum-regularized problem usually requires a proximal of the sum, not the composition of individual proximals. Clarifying conditions under which the fixed points of the proposed operator coincide with minimizers of the stated objective would strengthen the theory.
- Redundancy question: If the completion step already enforces low-rank via the nuclear norm, why is a subsequent LSI necessary? An ablation (completion-only vs. LSI-only vs. both) would clarify the distinct contribution of each step.
- Practicality/scalability: The method relies on repeated SVDs; runtime, memory footprint, and use of randomized/partial SVD are not discussed. Realistic vocabularies and document counts could be challenging.
- Setup clarity: It is unclear whether CL-LSI/CL-OPCA were implemented with actual translations or just parallel concatenation; the original CL-LSI assumes translated dual-language documents. This affects fairness and interpretability of the comparison.
- Limited analysis: No statistical significance testing; limited sensitivity analysis beyond k, γ, ρ; no study of the role of the µ (ℓ1) and nonnegativity constraints.

Quality
Technically reasonable overall. The optimization formulation is standard in spirit (nuclear norm + quadratic data fit) with some design choices (nonnegativity, linearized ℓ1, penalty on missing entries) that are plausible but would benefit from ablations and clearer theoretical justification. Empirical evaluation is broad across tasks, but omits key baselines and runtime analysis.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The objective and algorithm are clear; convergence section could be more rigorous. Please clarify:
- The exact construction of comparison methods (especially CL-LSI/CL-OPCA) and whether translations were used.
- Computational complexity per iteration and practical tricks (sparse ops, randomized SVD).
- The rationale and effect of the ρ penalty on missing entries; include an ablation over ρ.
- Whether LSI adds value beyond the low-rank completion.

Originality
Moderately high. While matrix completion and LSI are known, applying completion to the unified bilingual document–term matrix with missing cross-language features, followed by LSI, is a novel combination for cross-lingual representation learning. Related multi-view completion and co-regularization work is acknowledged, but this specific formulation provides a fresh angle.

Significance
Promising. If corroborated with stronger baselines and scalability evidence, the approach could influence multilingual representation learning with scarce parallel data. The robustness to very small parallel sets is practically important.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add SCL and translate-train/test baselines; if possible, include a modern cross-lingual embedding baseline.
- Provide ablations: (i) completion-only vs. LSI-only vs. both; (ii) with/without nonnegativity and ℓ1; (iii) varying ρ including 0.
- Report training time, SVD strategy, and memory usage; discuss scalability.
- Strengthen the theoretical section or reframe the convergence result as fixed-point convergence to a well-defined proximal splitting scheme, citing appropriate operator-splitting results.
- Clarify the setup for CL-LSI/CL-OPCA to ensure fair comparisons.

Reasons to accept
- Novel and appealing formulation that addresses a real pain point (few parallel docs).
- Consistent empirical gains across many tasks, notably in the low-parallel regime.
- Simple, potentially generalizable technique that others can build on.

Reasons to reject
- Missing key baselines (SCL, translate-based), making it hard to claim state-of-the-art.
- Theoretical justification for the specific proximal composition is thin.
- Scalability and practical feasibility not demonstrated.

Overall recommendation
Accept (borderline positive). The core idea is novel and promising, with encouraging empirical results, especially under scarce parallel data. With added baselines/ablations and clarified theory/efficiency, this could be a strong contribution.

Reviewer confidence
Moderate.
