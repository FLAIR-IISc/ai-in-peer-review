This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual text classification, combining matrix completion with latent semantic indexing (LSI). The core idea is to leverage unlabeled parallel data to fill in missing entries in a concatenated document-term matrix, thereby creating a cross-lingual representation. While the problem addressed – cross-lingual text classification – is well-established and relevant to the NIPS community, and the utilization of unlabeled parallel data is a reasonable direction, the presented approach feels incremental and lacks sufficient novelty to warrant strong consideration for acceptance.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors provide a reasonable overview of related work, positioning their method within the existing landscape of cross-lingual learning techniques. The mathematical formulation of the matrix completion step is reasonably presented, although the relaxation and optimization details rely heavily on established methods without significant innovation. The experimental setup is adequately described, utilizing a standard dataset and commonly employed evaluation metrics.

However, the primary weakness lies in the lack of compelling evidence demonstrating substantial improvement over existing methods. While the results show outperformance on many tasks, the gains are not dramatic and, critically, the paper doesn't thoroughly justify *why* this two-step approach outperforms alternatives like direct application of CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, or CL-KCCA. The authors claim the matrix completion step is crucial for capturing co-occurrence information, but they lack an ablation study to quantify the contribution of this step. It isn't clear if the performance improvement stems from the matrix completion itself, or simply from the specific parameter tuning used in the experiments. The claim of robustness with limited parallel data is promising but not sufficiently demonstrated – the improvements, while present, are not overwhelming with only 200 parallel documents. 

The optimization algorithm, presented with considerable detail, is largely a standard projected gradient descent method adapted to this specific problem. While correctly implemented, this doesn't represent a significant algorithmic contribution. The convergence analysis, while appreciated, feels overly verbose and doesn't introduce novel theoretical insights.

Furthermore, the comparisons to baselines feel somewhat superficial. While the baselines are reasonable, the parameter selection process for the proposed method (and to some extent the baselines) appears somewhat arbitrary and could potentially bias the results. A more rigorous parameter search and reporting would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem in NLP.
*   Clearly written and well-organized.
*   Demonstrates some improvement over baseline methods in empirical evaluation.
*   Provides a detailed algorithmic description and convergence analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty – the approach is largely a combination of established techniques.
*   Incremental improvement over existing methods – gains are not substantial.
*   Lack of ablation study to justify the two-step approach.
*   Potential for bias in parameter selection.
*   Optimization algorithm is not a significant contribution.
*   The improvements do not seem substantial enough to justify the added complexity.

**Overall Assessment:**

I assess this paper as borderline. It’s a technically sound and clearly presented piece of work, but lacks the originality and significance typically expected at NIPS. The empirical results are encouraging but not convincing enough to demonstrate a substantial advancement over existing techniques. I would recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors could significantly strengthen the paper by providing a more thorough justification for their two-step approach (through ablation studies), conducting a more rigorous parameter search, and demonstrating more substantial performance gains. The paper as it stands feels more suited for a conference with less stringent requirements for novelty and impact.
