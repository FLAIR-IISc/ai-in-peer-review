## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification leveraging unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The core idea involves matrix completion to impute missing values in a concatenated document-term matrix, followed by Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to derive cross-lingual representations. The authors evaluate their method on cross-lingual sentiment classification with Amazon product reviews, demonstrating performance improvements over several baselines.

While the problem addressed is relevant and the general approach of utilizing parallel data for cross-lingual learning is sound, the paper suffers from a multitude of shortcomings, ranging from insufficient novelty to a lack of rigor in experimental evaluation and presentation. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevance:** Cross-lingual learning is a crucial area of NLP, and reducing reliance on labeled target language data is a valuable goal.
*   **Conceptually Simple:** The two-step process is relatively easy to understand. The idea of leveraging matrix completion to bridge the language gap is intuitively appealing.
*   **Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates improved performance compared to some baselines, which suggests the approach holds promise.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Novelty:** The individual components – matrix completion and LSI – are established techniques. The combination, while potentially effective, isn’t presented as a fundamentally novel contribution. The paper doesn’t convincingly argue *why* this specific combination is superior to existing methods beyond empirical results. Many papers have used similar combinations of established methods in the past.
*   **Insufficiently Motivated Matrix Completion:** The justification for applying matrix completion feels weak. The statement that a fully observed matrix is "naturally low-rank and sparse" is a generalization and doesn’t adequately explain *why* matrix completion is expected to be more effective than, for example, directly applying LSI to the sparse matrix or exploring alternative imputation strategies. A more detailed analysis of the matrix properties and the expected benefits of completion would be crucial.
*   **Optimization Details:** The description of the projected gradient descent algorithm is excessively verbose and lacks critical details. The choice of step size (τ) is stated as “1” without justification. The parameter selection process (γ, ρ, k) is vaguely described ("used the first task EFB to perform model parameter selection").  No mention is made of cross-validation or other robust parameter tuning techniques. This lack of detail severely hinders reproducibility.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The experimental setup raises concerns. Using *only* 100 labeled target language documents for training is an extremely small sample size, which could heavily influence the results and limit their generalizability. The chosen baselines are not state-of-the-art, making it difficult to assess the true significance of the improvements. The justification for choosing k=50 for CL-LSI/OPCA and k=100 for CL-KCCA is weak ("produced" these values – how?).
*   **Figures and Tables:** Table 1 lacks clarity; standard deviations are provided, but the absolute values are difficult to interpret without context. Figure 1 and Figure 2 are similarly uninformative without detailed axis labels.
*   **Writing Quality and Redundancy:** The paper suffers from repetitive phrasing and verbose sentences. The introduction is overly long and lacks conciseness. The related work section is descriptive rather than critical, simply listing prior work without adequately positioning the current contribution.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:** The equation (1) uses the notation "M 0 ij" which isn’t consistent with the preceding text which refers to "Mij". Also, in Section 4.1, the statement that “with nonnegativity constraints M ≥ 0, the non-smooth ℓ1 norm regularizer in the objective function of (2) is equivalent to a smooth linear function ‖M‖1 = ∑ ij Mij” is incorrect.  The non-negativity constraints do not make the ℓ1 norm equivalent to the sum of elements.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   Lack of significant novelty.
*   Insufficient experimental rigor and reproducibility.
*   Weak theoretical motivation for key components.
*   Poor writing quality and organization.
*   Numerous minor technical flaws.

**For:**

*   The proposed method shows empirical improvements over some baselines.
*   The problem addressed is relevant and important.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper tackles a relevant problem, its weaknesses significantly outweigh its strengths. The lack of novelty, coupled with the shortcomings in experimental design and presentation, make it difficult to justify acceptance at NIPS. The paper feels more like a preliminary investigation than a fully developed research contribution. 

**Recommendation:** **Reject.** 

The authors would need to substantially revise the paper, addressing the issues outlined above, to be considered for future publication. Specifically, they should: (1) clearly articulate the novelty of their approach, (2) provide a more rigorous justification for using matrix completion, (3) detail the experimental setup and parameter tuning process, (4) evaluate against stronger baselines, and (5) significantly improve the clarity and conciseness of the writing.
