### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel two-step learning method (TSL) for cross-language text classification, designed for scenarios where labeled data is scarce in the target language but abundant in a source language. The method leverages a small set of unlabeled parallel documents to bridge the linguistic gap. The core idea involves two stages:
1.  **Matrix Completion:** All documents (source, target, and parallel) are represented in a single, concatenated document-term matrix. In this matrix, monolingual documents have missing values for the vocabulary of the other language. The authors formulate a matrix completion problem to fill in these missing entries. This is cast as a convex optimization problem to find a low-rank, sparse, and non-negative matrix that fits the observed data. The problem is solved using a projected gradient descent algorithm, for which a convergence proof is provided.
2.  **Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI):** After obtaining the completed matrix, LSI (via SVD) is applied to derive a low-dimensional, dense, cross-lingual representation for all documents. Standard monolingual classifiers can then be trained on this shared representation using labeled data from both domains.

The authors evaluate their method on 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks using the multilingual Amazon reviews dataset. The proposed TSL method is shown to significantly outperform several strong baselines, including CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, particularly when the number of available parallel documents is small.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Novelty:** The main contribution—using matrix completion to explicitly impute missing cross-lingual features in a unified document-term matrix—is a novel and elegant idea. It provides a principled way to leverage the low-rank structure inherent in text data to build a bridge between languages. This is a clear conceptual improvement over prior methods like CL-LSI, which implicitly treat these missing entries as zeros, potentially losing significant information.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The matrix completion problem is well-formulated using a convex relaxation (trace norm) of the rank function, combined with appropriate L1 and non-negativity constraints suitable for text data. The authors provide a projected gradient descent algorithm to solve the optimization problem and include a formal convergence analysis, which adds to the paper's technical rigor.

3.  **Significant Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The proposed method is tested across 18 different language-pair/domain tasks and consistently outperforms several relevant and strong baselines. The study on the impact of the size of the parallel corpus is particularly insightful. It demonstrates that the proposed TSL method is more robust and data-efficient than its competitors, achieving strong performance with as few as 200 parallel documents. This is a crucial practical advantage, as large parallel corpora are not always available.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation for the two-step approach is clearly articulated, and the methodology is presented in a logical and understandable manner.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, there are several areas where it could be improved.

1.  **Reproducibility and Algorithmic Details:** The description of the optimization algorithm could be more detailed to ensure reproducibility.
    *   The initialization step in Algorithm 1 is described as the "nonnegative projection of the rank-1 approximation of M0". The exact procedure for this should be specified.
    *   The convergence criterion for the `while` loop is not mentioned. Is it based on the change in the objective function, the change in `M`, or a fixed number of iterations?
    *   Clarification on the `µ‖M‖1` term: In Section 3.1, the authors state that with non-negativity, the L1 norm is equivalent to a linear function `Σij Mij`. This is reflected in the gradient `µE`. While this is a valid regularizer, it is different from a standard L1 penalty which promotes sparsity. The authors should briefly justify this specific choice and clarify that it penalizes the total term frequency mass of the completed matrix rather than enforcing sparsity in the traditional sense.

2.  **Scope of Evaluation:** The set of baselines, while good, could be strengthened with more contemporary approaches.
    *   A significant line of recent work in cross-lingual learning involves pre-trained cross-lingual word embeddings (e.g., using methods like those of Lample et al., 2018 or Conneau et al., 2017). A simple but very strong baseline would be to represent documents by averaging the embeddings of their words and training an SVM on these representations. Comparing TSL against such a baseline would provide a more complete picture of its standing in the current state of the art.

3.  **Computational Complexity:** The paper lacks any discussion of the computational cost of the proposed method. The matrix completion step, involving an iterative algorithm with an SVD at each step, can be computationally intensive, especially for large vocabularies (`d`) and large numbers of documents (`t`).
    *   A discussion of the algorithm's complexity (e.g., per-iteration cost) would be valuable.
    *   Reporting the actual runtimes for the experiments would give readers a practical sense of the method's scalability and feasibility for larger-scale problems.

4.  **Parameter Sensitivity:** The hyperparameters (`γ`, `ρ`, `k`) were tuned on a single task (EFB) and then fixed for all others. While this demonstrates a degree of robustness, it leaves open the question of how sensitive the model is to these choices. A brief sensitivity analysis, perhaps showing how performance on a few key tasks varies with the dimensionality `k` or the regularization parameter `γ`, would strengthen the empirical claims and provide better guidance for practitioners.

### Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound method for cross-lingual representation learning. The core idea of using matrix completion is clever and proves to be highly effective in the extensive experiments provided. The results, especially the method's robustness with limited parallel data, are significant and impactful. The weaknesses identified, primarily concerning reproducibility details, the scope of the evaluation, and the lack of complexity analysis, are all addressable and do not undermine the core contribution. This is a high-quality piece of work that makes a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning.

**Decision:** Accept.
