## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a conceptually sound and well-executed approach to cross-lingual representation learning. The two-step method of matrix completion followed by LSI is a reasonable strategy to address the challenges of sparse data and bridging the language gap. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating significant improvements over several baselines. While the method isn't groundbreaking in terms of introducing completely novel theoretical concepts, it represents a solid engineering contribution with clear practical benefits. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of cross-language text classification, where labeled data is scarce in the target language. It proposes a two-step approach: first, leveraging unlabeled parallel documents to complete a concatenated document-term matrix, effectively “filling in” missing information from one language into the other; and second, applying Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to derive a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation. 

The authors correctly position their work within the broader landscape of cross-lingual learning. They acknowledge prior work that uses machine translation, bilingual lexicons, and direct transfer learning. They also highlight methods focused on building cross-lingual representations using resources like bilingual word pairs, parallel corpora, and topic models.  The paper differentiates itself by focusing on a matrix completion approach combined with LSI, highlighting that it requires fewer parallel documents than translation-based methods and improves upon direct application of LSI or other dimensionality reduction techniques (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) to the raw concatenated matrix. The connection to existing matrix completion literature is also appropriately acknowledged.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Sound Methodology:** The matrix completion step is a clever way to leverage the limited parallel data to effectively enrich the document-term matrix and capture cross-lingual relationships. Combining this with LSI provides a principled way to learn a compact and meaningful representation.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on the Amazon product review dataset are strong and demonstrate consistent improvements over several strong baselines. The ablation study (Section 5.3) regarding the impact of parallel data size is particularly valuable.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner.
* **Scalability Considerations:**  The authors acknowledge the scalability challenges of matrix completion and offer a projected gradient descent algorithm (Algorithm 1), demonstrating awareness of practical implementation concerns.  While a more detailed discussion of computational complexity would be useful, the effort is appreciated.
* **Robustness:** The consistent performance gains across multiple language pairs suggest the robustness of the proposed method.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Justification:** While the intuition behind the method is strong, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical analysis of *why* matrix completion, specifically, is well-suited for this task.  A more detailed discussion of the underlying assumptions about the low-rank structure of the document-term matrix would strengthen the paper.  Is the low-rank assumption valid for these datasets?
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The parameter selection process (Section 5.2) feels somewhat ad-hoc. While they perform parameter tuning on one task, there’s limited discussion of how the optimal parameters might vary across different language pairs or datasets.  A sensitivity analysis of the key parameters (γ, ρ, k) would be beneficial.
* **Algorithm 1 Detail:**  The convergence analysis (Section 4.2) relies on lemmas that are referenced but not fully detailed within the paper.  While citing existing literature is acceptable, briefly outlining the key elements of those lemmas would make the analysis more self-contained.  Also, a discussion of the practical convergence criteria used in the implementation would be useful.
* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While the chosen baselines are relevant, the inclusion of more recent and sophisticated cross-lingual embedding techniques (e.g., those based on transformer models like multilingual BERT) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.  It's possible the gap could narrow with such comparisons.



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  Strong experimental results consistently outperform established baselines.
*  The proposed method addresses a practical problem with a well-motivated and clearly explained approach.
*  The paper is well-written and demonstrates a good understanding of the relevant literature.
*  The scalability considerations and proposed algorithm are a positive contribution.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  Lack of deeper theoretical justification for the choice of matrix completion.
*  Parameter selection process feels somewhat ad-hoc.
*  Limited comparison to state-of-the-art transformer-based cross-lingual embedding techniques.



**5. Recommendations:**

I recommend **acceptance** with the following minor revisions:

* **Expand the theoretical discussion:**  Provide more justification for the choice of matrix completion, specifically discussing the assumptions about the underlying document-term matrix structure.
* **Parameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Conduct a brief sensitivity analysis of the key parameters (γ, ρ, k) to demonstrate their impact on performance and robustness.
* **Algorithm 1 Clarity:** Briefly summarize the key results used in the convergence analysis from the cited lemmas.  Detail the practical convergence criteria employed.
* **Consider stronger baselines:**  If feasible, include a comparison to more recent cross-lingual embedding models like multilingual BERT.
* **Clarify the projection step:** In Algorithm 1, clarify if the "nonnegative projection" is simply setting negative values to zero, or a more sophisticated projection method.




In conclusion, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. While some theoretical and experimental refinements would further strengthen the work, the current version is of sufficient quality for publication in NIPS.
