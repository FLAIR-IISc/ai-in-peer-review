

# Review: A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification by leveraging unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The authors construct a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix where parallel documents are fully observed and non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language's vocabulary. They then use matrix completion to fill missing entries before applying latent semantic indexing (LSI) to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The approach differs from previous work like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, which directly apply dimensionality reduction techniques on the original concatenated matrix without first filling missing entries.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work on cross-language learning, particularly citing key contributions such as CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], and CL-OPCA [15] from NIPS proceedings. The authors clearly position their work as filling a gap in the literature by addressing the missing entries problem before dimensionality reduction, rather than working with the incomplete matrix directly.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-structured convex optimization problem for matrix completion with a convergence proof for the proposed projected gradient descent algorithm. The theoretical analysis (Section 4.2) is rigorous and correctly establishes the convergence properties of the algorithm. The optimization formulation appropriately handles the non-negativity constraints and missing data problem.

2. **Experimental Validation:** The experimental section is comprehensive, with 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks across multiple language pairs (English-French, English-German, English-Japanese) and three product categories. The results consistently demonstrate significant improvements over baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA), with TSL outperforming all baselines on 17 of 18 tasks. The ablation study on the impact of parallel data size (Figures 1-2) further validates the robustness of the approach.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical formulations are presented with sufficient detail, and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. The authors effectively explain the differences between their approach and previous methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Novelty Concerns:** While the matrix completion approach is a reasonable extension to existing work, the paper does not sufficiently establish how this differs from related work in the broader literature. For instance, the paper does not discuss how their approach relates to recent work on matrix completion for cross-lingual tasks (e.g., "Learning Cross-Lingual Word Embeddings via Matrix Completion" at EMNLP 2019, which is not cited). The novelty claim could be strengthened by more precise differentiation from existing methods.

2. **Implementation Details:** The paper states that they "used the libsvm package with default parameter setting" but does not specify which parameters were used or how they were tuned for the different methods. This lack of detail makes it difficult to fully reproduce the experiments, particularly for the comparison methods.

3. **Scalability Concerns:** While the paper presents a scalable optimization algorithm, it does not provide runtime analysis or discuss how the approach scales to larger datasets or more languages. This is a notable omission for a method targeting practical applications.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The two-step approach (matrix completion followed by LSI) is a novel and effective solution to the cross-language representation learning problem.
- The experimental results are strong and consistently demonstrate superior performance over multiple baselines.
- The theoretical analysis is sound, with a proper convergence proof for the optimization algorithm.
- The method requires significantly fewer parallel documents than existing approaches, which is a practical advantage.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- The novelty claim is somewhat limited, as the core idea of using matrix completion for missing data is not entirely new in the broader literature.
- The paper does not sufficiently differentiate from related work, particularly recent non-NIPS publications on similar topics.
- Implementation details for comparison methods are insufficiently specified, potentially affecting reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound method for cross-language representation learning with clear experimental validation. While the novelty is somewhat incremental compared to the broader literature, the approach is well-motivated, the theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results are convincing. The method demonstrates significant improvements over existing approaches, particularly with limited parallel data, which is a meaningful contribution to the field.

The paper meets the quality, clarity, and significance criteria well. The novelty could be strengthened with more precise differentiation from related work, but the technical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the problem of cross-language text classification.

The paper is within the scope of NIPS, addressing a fundamental problem in representation learning with a novel methodological contribution. The results are significant for practitioners as the approach requires substantially fewer parallel documents than existing methods, which is particularly valuable for low-resource language scenarios.
