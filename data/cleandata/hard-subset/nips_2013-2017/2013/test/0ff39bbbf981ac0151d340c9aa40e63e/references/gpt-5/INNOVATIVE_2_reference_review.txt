Summary
The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that leverages a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 treats the concatenated bilingual document-term matrix as a partially observed matrix (non-parallel docs have missing entries in the other language) and performs convex matrix completion with a nuclear-norm penalty, an L1 penalty (which becomes linear under nonnegativity), and a weighted squared loss on observed and missing entries. The optimization is solved via a projected gradient descent with singular value shrinkage and nonnegativity projection, with a convergence argument leveraging non-expansiveness. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) on the recovered matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual document embeddings, enabling training of standard monolingual classifiers. Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews (EN–FR/DE/JA, 18 transfer tasks) show consistent gains over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, especially when the number of parallel documents is small.

Relation to prior work
The approach is closest to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, which build shared representations from parallel/comparable corpora. The key difference here is to explicitly frame cross-lingual representation learning as matrix completion over a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix with missing values for non-parallel documents, followed by LSI. Compared to pivot-based SCL and co-training approaches, the method does not require translating all training instances nor bilingual lexicons; it uses a small parallel set to propagate cross-lingual co-occurrence structure to all documents via low-rank completion. The optimization method (shrinkage/PGD) and convergence analysis follow standard matrix completion literature (e.g., Ma et al., 2011), but their application with nonnegativity and the specific loss weighting on missing entries is tailored to this problem.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Recasting cross-lingual representation learning as a missing-data problem on a bilingual document-term matrix is clean and appealing. The low-rank plus sparsity with nonnegativity is a natural inductive bias for text and a plausible way to bridge languages via a small parallel seed.
- Principled yet simple: The convex surrogate, proximal/shrinkage algorithm, and non-expansiveness-based convergence are sound and easy to implement. The method is modular and uses standard components (SVD, PGD).
- Empirical signal: Strong and consistent improvements over CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA across 18 tasks, with especially notable robustness when only 200 parallel pairs are available.
- Practical: Requires only a modest set of unlabeled parallel documents; uses off-the-shelf SVM after representation learning.
- Sensitivity study: Varies the number of parallel pairs and shows graceful degradation, which strengthens the case for the approach.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Baselines miss modern methods: The comparisons are to earlier cross-lingual representation techniques. There are no comparisons to cross-lingual word/sentence embeddings (e.g., bilingual skip-gram, MUSE/VecMap), bilingual/multilingual topic models beyond those cited, or multilingual pre-trained encoders (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R). Given the problem is classic, omitting these is a substantial gap in assessing practical impact today.
- Two-step factorization feels ad hoc: You already enforce low-rank through the nuclear norm; applying a second SVD (LSI) on M* may be redundant. An ablation (LSI on the original masked matrix, or using the factors from the shrinkage step as the embedding) would clarify the necessity and contribution of each step.
- Scalability and complexity not quantified: While the algorithm is “scalable in principle,” the paper lacks runtime/memory reporting, stopping criteria, and complexity w.r.t. number of docs and vocabulary size. Iterative SVDs on large vocabularies can be expensive; practical tricks (randomized SVD, partial SVD per iteration) are not discussed.
- Objective design choices need more motivation: The role of ρ (loss on missing entries to zero) is important; beyond a hyperparameter sweep, there’s little justification for this particular regularization. Similarly, the effect of µ (L1) under nonnegativity—essentially a linear penalty—could be analyzed; does it simply bias towards sparsity or act as a baseline shift?
- Evaluation breadth: Experiments are restricted to binary sentiment on an older dataset. It’s unclear how this generalizes to multi-class topics, other domains, or languages with less lexical overlap. No qualitative analysis of cross-lingual alignment or induced features is provided.
- Reproducibility: Code/data/preprocessing details (tokenization, vocabulary construction, normalization), convergence criteria, and the exact handling of non-parallel rows would help replication.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide ablations: (a) LSI on the original masked matrix; (b) skip LSI and use the low-rank factors from completion; (c) remove the L1 penalty; (d) remove nonnegativity?
- How does the method compare to cross-lingual embeddings (word or sentence) and multilingual transformers under the same supervision budget (100 target labels; 200–2000 parallel pairs)?
- What are the computational costs (per-iteration, total time, peak memory) and stopping criteria? Did you use randomized/partial SVD?
- How sensitive are results to ρ and µ beyond the reported grid? Any guidance for setting τ given ρ?
- Can you provide qualitative evidence (e.g., nearest neighbors across languages, alignment of sentiment words) showing that completion bridges vocabularies?

Minor comments
- Clarify precisely how CL-LSI/OPCA baselines were constructed without translating all documents; the current description could be read as assuming dual-language documents for all items.
- Include details on vocabulary size, document length stats, and normalization (e.g., TF-IDF capping, length norm).
- A small discussion on when matrix completion may hallucinate spurious cross-lingual co-occurrences would be useful.

Recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The core idea—cross-lingual representation via low-rank completion of a bilingual document-term matrix—is simple, elegant, and potentially influential, particularly for low-resource scenarios with limited parallel data. While the empirical evaluation lacks modern baselines and broader tasks, the reported gains over classic strong baselines and the robustness to scarce parallel data are compelling. With stronger comparisons and ablations, this work could have substantial impact.

Confidence: moderate.
