Summary
The paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-lingual text classification. Step 1: construct a unified document–term matrix over the concatenated vocabularies of two languages, where rows for parallel pairs are fully observed and rows for non-parallel documents have missing entries; recover missing entries by solving a convex surrogate of a matrix completion problem with trace norm (low-rank), an l1 term (claimed to promote sparsity), squared loss on observed entries, and an l2 penalty toward zero on missing entries, under a nonnegativity constraint, via a projected gradient method with singular value shrinkage. Step 2: apply LSI (SVD) to the recovered matrix to induce a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation on which a linear SVM is trained. Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews claim improvements over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, and robustness to small numbers of parallel documents.

Strengths
- A simple, generic idea: impute missing features in a concatenated bilingual matrix using low-rank structure, then apply a standard dimensionality reduction. Conceptually plausible and easy to implement.
- Uses only a small set of unlabeled parallel documents; analyzes performance as the amount of parallel data varies.
- Presents an optimization framework with a clear (if simplified) algorithmic recipe and a stepsize condition; attempts a convergence argument based on non-expansiveness.
- Empirical gains are claimed across many language/category pairs.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and theory
- Conceptual error: “a fully observed document–term matrix is naturally low-rank and sparse” is false. Such matrices are typically very high rank; low-rank is an inductive bias, not an inherent property.
- Objective design: with M ≥ 0, the l1 term reduces to a linear sum of entries. This does not meaningfully promote sparsity beyond uniform shrinkage (especially given the additional ρ penalty that already drives unobserved entries toward zero). There is no justification or ablation disentangling the roles of µ and ρ. Why both?
- Algorithmic mismatch: The algorithm applies (i) a gradient step on g (which includes the l1 term treated as smooth via µE), (ii) nuclear-norm shrinkage, and (iii) a separate projection onto M ≥ 0. This is not the proximal operator of the sum of two nonsmooth functions (trace norm + indicator of nonnegativity). Sequential application of two prox operators generally does not solve the original problem unless one uses a principled splitting method (e.g., DRS/ADMM or Dykstra’s algorithm). The proof only establishes non-expansiveness of the composed operator; it does not show that its fixed points minimize the original objective. Citing [11, Theorem 4] is insufficient for this composite setting.
- Initialization is unclear: “rank-1 approximation of M0” for a partially observed matrix—are missing entries zero-filled? This choice can bias the solution.
- Computational scalability: Each iteration requires an SVD for shrinkage plus a final SVD for LSI. No complexity discussion, no use of partial/randomized SVD, no runtime/memory numbers. For realistic t, d, this is potentially prohibitive.
- Several equations have typos/incompleteness (e.g., c(x,y)=1/2||x−y|| missing the square; “if only if” instead of “iff”), and some symbols are introduced without care (I(·) used ambiguously as identity; Ω/Ω̂, Y/Ŷ defined but the narrative is imprecise).

Empirical evaluation
- Transductive setup: “we used all documents from the two languages … for representation learning,” which appears to include the target test set in representation learning. This must be clearly stated as transductive learning and compared to an inductive variant (no access to test documents during representation learning). Otherwise the results overstate generalization.
- Missing strong baselines: No comparisons to SCL (Prettenhofer & Stein, cited), bilingual autoencoders, cross-lingual word embeddings/alignment methods, or modern multilingual transformers (mBERT/XLM-R) fine-tuned with 100 target labels. Translation-based baselines (MT + monolingual) are discussed in related work but not included.
- CL-KCCA details are insufficient (kernel choice, regularization, solver). SVM C not tuned (libsvm defaults). Hyperparameter k is chosen on one task (EFB) and transferred; ρ and γ are tuned only for TSL, not analogously for all competitors. These choices confound fairness and replicability.
- Critical omission: Table 1 and Figures 1–2 are not present in the submission text provided. Without actual numbers/plots, it is impossible to verify claims, effect sizes, or variance. No statistical significance testing is reported.
- No ablations to isolate contributions (e.g., effect of removing l1, removing nonnegativity, setting ρ=0, using only parallel rows vs. all rows in completion, LSI alone vs. completion+LSI).
- No sensitivity analysis to stepsize τ, µ, or initialization; no stopping criterion; no runtime/memory.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typos, grammatical errors, and inconsistent terminology (“cross language” vs. “cross-lingual”; “if only if”). Algorithm 1 formatting is sloppy.
- References are incomplete and inconsistent (missing years/venues/pages; incorrect acronyms; e.g., “ICCL-ACL”). Some key related work is missing (SVT for nuclear norm minimization; more recent cross-lingual representation learning).
- Reproducibility details are missing (tokenization, stopwording, TF-IDF specifics, vocabulary construction, random seeds, software, hardware).

Originality and significance
- The core novelty is a straightforward combination: matrix completion followed by LSI on a concatenated bilingual matrix. Prior work has directly applied LSI/OPCA/KCCA on concatenated or paired corpora; adding a completion step is incremental. The paper does not convincingly position itself against related matrix completion/imputation approaches or nonnegative matrix factorization/bilingual autoencoders.
- Given the dated dataset and absence of modern baselines, it is hard to assess practical significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple idea that could be useful when only a small parallel set is available.
- Claimed robust gains over several classical baselines across many tasks.
- Some theoretical effort (step-size condition, non-expansiveness lemmas).
- Study of performance vs. number of parallel pairs.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical issues: objective/algorithm mismatch and insufficient convergence justification.
- Conceptual misstatements (low-rank nature of document-term matrices).
- Experimental limitations: likely transductive setup; missing strong baselines; missing figures/tables; insufficient details for replication; no ablations or runtimes.
- Scalability concerns not addressed.
- Numerous writing/formatting/reference problems.

Recommendation
Reject. While the idea is intuitive and potentially useful, the paper, as written, has substantial theoretical, experimental, and presentation shortcomings. Addressing the proximal-splitting mismatch with a correct algorithm/proof, adding strong and modern baselines (including inductive results), providing complete results with ablations and efficiency analysis, and fixing clarity/formatting issues would be necessary for a serious reconsideration.

Confidence
High.
