Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual document representation learning using a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 performs matrix completion on a unified bilingual document-term matrix: each document is represented in the concatenated vocabulary of the two languages; non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language’s vocabulary. The authors formulate a convex relaxation with trace norm (low-rank), l1 penalty (sparsity), quadratic losses on observed entries and on missing entries (to avoid overfitting to unobserved values), and a non-negativity constraint. They develop a projected gradient descent algorithm with a shrinkage step (proximal operator for the trace norm), and prove convergence using non-expansive operator arguments. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) on the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional shared representation, followed by a linear SVM. Experiments on cross-lingual sentiment classification (Amazon reviews across English, French, German, Japanese) show that the method generally outperforms TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, and degrades less when the amount of parallel data is small.

The work connects to cross-lingual representation approaches such as CL-LSI (Littman et al.), CL-KCCA (Vinokourov et al.), CL-OPCA (Platt et al.), to SCL (Prettenhofer & Stein), and to matrix completion/optimization techniques (Candès & Recht; Ma et al.). The novelty is to explicitly complete missing cross-lingual entries in the concatenated document-term matrix before doing LSI, rather than directly projecting the sparse concatenated matrix.

Strengths
- Clear formulation: a principled convex objective combining trace norm, sparsity, nonnegativity, and observed/missing entry losses.
- Algorithmic clarity: simple projected gradient with shrinkage; convergence argument leveraging non-expansive maps is correct for the given setting.
- Empirical gains: consistent improvements over strong, relevant baselines (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) on 18 transfer tasks; robustness with only 200 parallel pairs is compelling.
- Sensible experimental protocol: hyperparameters tuned on one task and reused, reducing overfitting to each scenario; reporting means and standard deviations over multiple splits.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumption/justification of low rank: the claim that a fully observed document-term matrix is “naturally low-rank” is questionable; document-term matrices are typically high-rank albeit compressible. Please clarify why trace-norm regularization is suitable here and whether performance holds without it (ablation).
- Objective design: with nonnegativity, the l1 term becomes a uniform linear shrinkage µ∑ij Mij; together with the missing-entry penalty ρ||M||F^2 over Ω̂, this introduces two forces pushing toward zero, potentially redundant. An ablation varying µ and ρ (including µ=0 and/or ρ=0) would help disentangle their roles.
- Scalability: each iteration requires an SVD for shrinkage. For large corpora/vocabularies, this is expensive. What rank truncation strategy is used? What are the time/memory costs vs. CL-LSI/KCCA? Any convergence diagnostics and number of iterations?
- Transductive setup: representation learning uses all documents from both languages, including test-set documents (without labels). While common in cross-lingual unsupervised representation learning, please make the transductive nature explicit and discuss generalization when only unlabeled target training documents are available.
- Baselines: Missing comparisons to more recent cross-lingual representation methods that do not require parallel documents, e.g., bilingual/multilingual embeddings aligned via dictionaries or comparable corpora, or topic-model-based approaches beyond those cited. Even within the parallel-data family, a machine-translation baseline (translate-train or translate-test) would contextualize gains.
- Generality: All experiments are sentiment classification on reviews. It would strengthen the case to include another task (e.g., topic categorization) or at least report whether the method’s advantages persist with different feature preprocessing (binary/TF vs. TF-IDF).
- Reproducibility details: No code/reporting of random seeds, stopping criteria, or SVD implementation; parameter sensitivity beyond the single-task tuning is not explored.

Quality
- The optimization setup is sound; the proximal gradient scheme with shrinkage and projection is standard and the convergence proof is appropriate. However, theoretical guarantees for recovery under this data model (beyond convergence) are not provided, and the empirical section lacks ablations to support specific design choices. The experimental evaluation is solid on one dataset with multiple transfer directions but could be broader and include stronger baselines.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. Suggestions:
  - Clarify the construction of M0, especially how parallel pairs are represented (concatenation vs. averaging) and whether rows for parallel pairs duplicate documents that also appear as monolingual rows.
  - State explicitly that all documents (including test) are used for unsupervised representation learning.
  - Provide algorithmic complexity per iteration and stopping criteria; indicate whether truncated SVD is used.
  - Fix minor typos and improve phrasing around the “naturally low-rank” claim.

Originality
- The combination of matrix completion on a bilingual document-term matrix with LSI is, to my knowledge, novel relative to CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA and multilingual topic models. The optimization method itself is standard. The conceptual contribution is moderate but meaningful.

Significance
- If the gains and robustness with very small parallel corpora generalize, this is practically valuable. The method’s reliance on only a small set of parallel pairs is attractive. The lack of comparison to modern embedding-based baselines limits the strength of the claim w.r.t. the current state of the art.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled method with convergence proof.
- Consistent empirical gains over established baselines.
- Robust performance with very limited parallel data.
- Simple pipeline easily adoptable by practitioners.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited baselines and domains; no comparison to translation-based or embedding-based methods.
- Scalability not thoroughly addressed; potential computational bottleneck.
- Limited ablations; some design choices insufficiently justified.
- Transductive evaluation could overstate effectiveness in strictly inductive settings.

Questions for authors
- How does performance change if µ=0 and/or ρ=0? What is the effect of removing non-negativity?
- Do you use truncated SVD in shrinkage? What ranks and runtimes do you observe?
- Can you report results when representation learning excludes the target test documents?
- How sensitive is performance to k, γ, ρ across tasks beyond the one used for tuning?

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea is simple and well-executed, with convincing improvements on a standard benchmark and an appealing low-parallel-data regime. Strengthening the experimental section with ablations, scalability analysis, and a couple of stronger baselines would make this a clear accept.
