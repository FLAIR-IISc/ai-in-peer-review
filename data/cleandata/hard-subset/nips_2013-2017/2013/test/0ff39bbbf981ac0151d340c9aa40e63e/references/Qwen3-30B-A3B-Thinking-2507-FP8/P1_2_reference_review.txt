

# Peer Review: "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that addresses the language barrier by constructing a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix and filling missing entries through matrix completion before applying latent semantic indexing (LSI). The authors claim their method significantly outperforms existing approaches (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) on cross-language sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews. The method's key innovation is filling missing entries in the bilingual document-term matrix via matrix completion before performing LSI, rather than applying LSI directly to the incomplete matrix as in previous approaches.

The paper relates to previous work in cross-lingual learning, particularly CL-LSI [10], CL-OPCA [15], and CL-KCCA [20], which also use concatenated document-term matrices. The authors position their work as distinct because they fill missing entries before LSI, rather than applying LSI directly to the incomplete matrix. The paper provides a convergence analysis for their projected gradient descent algorithm and conducts extensive experiments on 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks.

## Quality

**Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-structured method with a clear mathematical formulation. The matrix completion problem is appropriately formulated with non-negativity constraints, and the authors provide a convergence proof for their optimization algorithm. The experimental setup is thorough, covering multiple language pairs and categories.

**Supporting Evidence**: The experimental results are presented clearly across 18 different cross-language tasks, with consistent improvements over baselines. The paper also examines the impact of varying amounts of unlabeled parallel data, which is a useful analysis. However, the paper lacks statistical significance testing for the performance improvements, which would strengthen the claims.

**Completeness**: The paper is generally complete, covering all necessary components from problem formulation to experiments. However, it lacks comparison with recent deep learning approaches that have become standard for cross-lingual tasks (e.g., XLM-R, multilingual BERT), which are now state-of-the-art in the field.

**Honest Evaluation**: The paper acknowledges the requirement for some unlabeled parallel data but doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of this requirement or what happens when parallel data is scarce. The paper also doesn't clearly address when their method might fail or what its limitations are.

## Clarity

**Writing Quality**: The paper is well-written with clear technical explanations. The methodology is logically presented and easy to follow.

**Organization**: The paper is well-organized with a logical flow from problem statement to related work, approach, experiments, and conclusion. The sections are appropriately structured.

**Reproducibility**: The paper describes the experimental setup and parameters, but lacks sufficient detail about the implementation of the matrix completion algorithm (e.g., how they handle the large-scale matrix efficiently in practice). The parameter selection process is mentioned but could be more detailed.

**Information Provided**: The paper provides enough information for an expert to understand the approach, but the comparison with recent deep learning methods is missing, which would be important for readers to evaluate the method's relevance to current practice.

## Originality

The paper's core idea of using matrix completion to fill missing entries before LSI is somewhat novel, but the difference from previous methods like CL-LSI [10] and CL-OPCA [15] is subtle. The paper states: "different from these methods which apply operations such as LSI, KCCA, and OPCA directly on the original concatenated document-term matrix, our method first fills the missing entries of the document-term matrix using matrix completion, and then performs LSI over the recovered low-rank matrix." However, this distinction isn't convincingly argued, and the paper doesn't clearly demonstrate why this small change makes such a significant difference in performance.

The paper's related work section is comprehensive, but it doesn't adequately distinguish the proposed method from similar approaches, particularly in the CL-LSI and CL-OPCA methods. The novelty appears incremental rather than groundbreaking, especially when compared to recent neural approaches to cross-lingual learning.

## Significance

The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines (8.59-14.61% average accuracy gains), which is a meaningful contribution. The method's ability to work with a small amount of unlabeled parallel data (as few as 200 documents) is a practical advantage. However, the field has largely moved toward deep learning approaches for cross-lingual representation learning, and the paper doesn't compare with these state-of-the-art methods. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the significance of the contribution relative to current practice.

The paper makes a modest contribution to the field, but the significance is somewhat limited by the lack of comparison with recent neural approaches and the relatively small performance gains compared to the state-of-the-art.

## Strengths

1. Clear problem statement and well-structured methodology
2. Comprehensive experimental evaluation across 18 different cross-language tasks
3. Practical demonstration that the method works with minimal parallel data
4. Solid theoretical foundation with convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm
5. Well-written and organized paper

## Weaknesses

1. Lack of comparison with recent deep learning approaches (XLM, XLM-R, multilingual BERT) that have become standard for cross-lingual tasks
2. Subtle novelty - the difference from CL-LSI and CL-OPCA isn't convincingly argued
3. No statistical significance testing for the performance improvements
4. Limited discussion of the method's limitations and failure cases
5. Insufficient detail about implementation details for reproducibility

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following caveats:

1. The paper makes a modest contribution to the field of cross-lingual representation learning, but the novelty is limited compared to recent work in the area.
2. The lack of comparison with state-of-the-art deep learning approaches is a significant weakness that should be addressed in any revision.
3. The paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of why the matrix completion step provides such a substantial improvement over direct LSI on the incomplete matrix.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a comparison with recent deep learning approaches (XLM, XLM-R, mBERT) to establish the relative significance of the contribution.
2. Provide a more detailed discussion of the method's limitations and when it might fail.
3. Include statistical significance testing for the performance improvements.
4. Clarify the theoretical justification for why matrix completion before LSI is better than LSI directly on the incomplete matrix.
5. Provide more details about the implementation of the matrix completion algorithm to improve reproducibility.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper presents a well-structured method with clear experimental results
- The method demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines across multiple tasks
- The ability to work with minimal parallel data is a practical advantage
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized
- The convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm is solid

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The novelty is limited, as the method is very similar to existing approaches like CL-LSI
- The paper fails to compare with recent state-of-the-art deep learning approaches
- The performance gains, while consistent, may not be large enough to represent a significant advancement
- The lack of statistical significance testing weakens the empirical claims
- The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of the approach

## Final Recommendation

This paper makes a modest but meaningful contribution to cross-lingual representation learning. While the methodology is sound and the experimental results are convincing, the lack of comparison with recent deep learning approaches and the limited novelty compared to existing methods prevent it from being a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. I would recommend acceptance with major revisions that address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly by comparing with state-of-the-art deep learning methods and providing a more detailed analysis of the method's limitations.

The paper would be significantly stronger if it demonstrated how their approach compares to the current state-of-the-art in cross-lingual representation learning, rather than just comparing to older methods. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the paper's significance for the NIPS community.
