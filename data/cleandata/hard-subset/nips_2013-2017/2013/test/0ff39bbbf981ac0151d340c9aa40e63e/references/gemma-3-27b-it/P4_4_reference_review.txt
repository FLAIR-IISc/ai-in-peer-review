This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification. The core idea is to first complete a concatenated document-term matrix constructed from parallel and non-parallel bilingual documents using matrix completion, then apply Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix for dimensionality reduction and representation learning. The resulting representations are used for classification. The authors evaluate the method on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, demonstrating improvements over several baselines.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the proposed method. It relates to a significant body of prior work in cross-lingual learning, covering both translation-based approaches and methods leveraging parallel corpora and bilingual resources. The authors provide a comprehensive overview of relevant literature, referencing numerous papers from NIPS and other venues. The optimization algorithm for matrix completion is detailed, including a convergence analysis. The experimental setup is also clearly described, with appropriate baselines and evaluation metrics. The results demonstrate a consistent performance improvement over the baselines, and the ablation study regarding the amount of parallel data provides valuable insights.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear and Well-Motivated:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and the motivation behind the proposed approach.
*   **Novelty:** While building on existing techniques (matrix completion and LSI), the combination and application to the cross-lingual learning problem, particularly with the focus on leveraging *unlabeled* parallel documents for matrix completion, is a novel contribution.
*   **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup is well-designed and the results are convincing, demonstrating statistically significant improvements over several baselines. The inclusion of an ablation study on the amount of parallel data further strengthens the evaluation.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the matrix completion problem and the optimization algorithm appear sound. The convergence analysis provides a degree of theoretical guarantee.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper mentions scalability concerns in the optimization algorithm section, but doesn’t provide detailed analysis of the computational complexity or runtime for larger datasets. This is crucial for practical applicability.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the method to hyperparameter choices (γ, ρ, k) is not fully explored. While a parameter selection process is described for one task, it’s unclear how well these parameters generalize to other tasks, and how much effort is required for tuning in a new setting.
*   **Matrix Completion Justification:** While the authors mention the low-rank nature of the document-term matrix, a more detailed justification for the choice of matrix completion as the primary approach to address the missing data problem would be beneficial. Other imputation techniques or direct application of LSI to the sparse matrix were not compared.
*   **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding the types of errors made by the proposed method and the baselines could provide valuable insights for future improvements.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem in NLP with a novel and well-motivated approach.
*   The experimental results are strong and demonstrate consistent performance improvements.
*   The technical contributions (formulation of the matrix completion problem and its optimization) are sound.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The lack of detailed computational cost analysis may limit the practical applicability of the method.
*   The hyperparameter sensitivity and the absence of a comprehensive ablation study regarding the matrix completion aspect could be concerns.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong experimental results. The weaknesses are not critical, but addressing them would further strengthen the paper. Considering the novelty and empirical performance, I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding computational cost and hyperparameter sensitivity. Specifically, including runtime results for different dataset sizes and providing guidance on hyperparameter selection would be valuable additions.
