

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that combines matrix completion with latent semantic indexing. The key insight is to first construct a unified document-term matrix across two languages where parallel documents are fully observed and non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language, then use matrix completion to fill in missing values before applying LSI for cross-lingual representation learning.

The paper makes a notable contribution by positioning its approach as distinct from prior methods that apply LSI, KCCA, or OPCA directly to the incomplete matrix. Instead, the authors argue that matrix completion allows them to automatically capture low-rank information based on word co-occurrence patterns across languages. The method is evaluated on 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks from the Amazon product reviews dataset, showing significant improvements over four baselines including CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Approach:** The two-step procedure (matrix completion followed by LSI) represents a genuinely new direction in cross-language representation learning. While previous work has used LSI on concatenated bilingual matrices, none have attempted to fill missing entries via matrix completion before applying LSI. This is a bold conceptual shift that addresses a fundamental limitation of existing methods.

2. **Strong Experimental Results:** The method significantly outperforms all baselines across 18 tasks, with improvements ranging from 8.59% to 14.61% over the target baseline. The most compelling evidence is the robust performance with very small amounts of parallel data (as few as 200 documents), which is a major practical advantage.

3. **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a solid theoretical basis with a convergence analysis of the projected gradient descent algorithm, which is a valuable contribution beyond just the empirical results.

4. **Clear Motivation:** The paper effectively articulates the limitation of existing methods that treat missing features as zeros, which is a common problem in cross-language learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Novelty Clarification:** While the approach is novel, the paper could better articulate how their matrix completion formulation differs from standard matrix completion techniques. The connection to previous matrix completion work (e.g., Candès & Recht's exact matrix completion) isn't sufficiently established.

2. **Experimental Scope:** The evaluation is limited to sentiment classification on Amazon reviews. While this is a standard benchmark, it would be stronger to demonstrate the approach on other tasks or domains to better establish generalizability.

3. **Scalability Concerns:** The paper mentions the matrix M is "typically very large" but doesn't provide any discussion of computational complexity or scalability to larger datasets, which is a potential limitation for real-world applications.

4. **Parameter Selection:** The parameter selection process (choosing parameters on one task and applying to all) is somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from more systematic analysis.

**Significance and Originality:**

This work makes a significant contribution to cross-language representation learning by introducing a fundamentally different approach to handling the missing data problem in bilingual document-term matrices. The core idea of using matrix completion as a preprocessing step before LSI is both simple and powerful, and it's remarkable that this simple modification yields such significant performance gains.

Compared to previous NIPS work on cross-language learning (e.g., [10, 15, 20] referenced in the paper), this approach is conceptually distinct and represents a clear step beyond existing methods. The authors properly contextualize their work against CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, but could better emphasize why matrix completion is uniquely suited for this problem.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. While the paper has minor weaknesses in experimental scope and theoretical justification of the matrix completion approach, the core idea is sufficiently novel and the empirical results are compelling. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel concepts over methodological perfection, I find the two-step matrix completion + LSI approach to be a bold and significant contribution that advances the field in a demonstrable way.

The method's ability to work with very small amounts of parallel data is particularly noteworthy for practical applications where parallel data is scarce. The paper clearly articulates a new direction in cross-language learning that could inspire future work, and the empirical results convincingly demonstrate its superiority over existing methods.

This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, conceptually fresh approach that NIPS values. While the methodological details could be strengthened in a revision, the novelty and significance of the core idea are substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.
