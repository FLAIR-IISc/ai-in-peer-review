

# Peer Review: "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that leverages unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to learn cross-lingual representations. The key idea is to first construct a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix where parallel documents are fully observed and non-parallel documents have missing values for the other language's vocabulary. The authors then fill the missing entries via matrix completion (using a convex optimization formulation with trace norm and ℓ1 regularization) and perform latent semantic indexing on the recovered matrix to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The method is evaluated on 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using multilingual Amazon product reviews, demonstrating consistent improvements over several baselines including CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA.

This work relates to the growing body of NIPS papers on cross-lingual representation learning, including recent approaches that use neural networks for cross-lingual transfer (e.g., [20] in the references, "Translingual document representations from discriminative projections"). While the paper acknowledges these approaches, it positions itself as a method that requires fewer parallel documents than translation-based approaches that require translating all training documents.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a well-formulated optimization problem and a convergence analysis for the proposed projected gradient descent algorithm. The matrix completion approach is theoretically grounded and the paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the problem. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, covering 18 different cross-lingual classification tasks across three languages and three product categories, with multiple repetitions to account for randomness in labeled data selection.

However, the paper could have strengthened its evaluation by including more recent baselines that use neural network approaches to cross-lingual transfer learning, which have become increasingly popular in the field. The comparison is limited to traditional methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, which were developed before the rise of deep learning approaches to this problem.

The authors provide a detailed parameter selection process for their method, which is commendable. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently explain why the specific parameters they chose (γ = 0.1, ρ = 10^-4, k = 50) are optimal, and the parameter selection process seems somewhat ad-hoc since they only describe it for one task (EFB) before applying it to all 18 tasks.

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to method to experiments. The mathematical formulation is precise, and the algorithm is clearly described. The experimental setup is adequately detailed, including dataset description, parameter selection, and evaluation metrics.

One area for improvement is the explanation of why matrix completion is particularly well-suited for this problem compared to other methods for handling missing data (e.g., imputation with mean values or more sophisticated techniques). The paper could have provided a more detailed discussion of the theoretical justification for using matrix completion specifically for cross-lingual representation learning.

## Originality

The core contribution of the paper is the two-step method that first completes the missing entries in the bilingual document-term matrix via matrix completion before applying LSI. This is a novel approach compared to existing methods that apply LSI, KCCA, or OPCA directly on the partially observed matrix. The paper clearly differentiates their approach from previous work in the related work section, particularly highlighting how their method differs from CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA.

However, the paper's originality is somewhat limited by the fact that matrix completion itself is not a new technique, and the application to cross-lingual learning is a relatively straightforward extension of existing approaches. The paper would have been stronger if it had provided more detailed justification for why this specific combination of techniques is particularly effective for cross-lingual representation learning.

## Significance

The problem of cross-language text classification is important for practical applications where labeled data in the target language is scarce. The paper demonstrates that their method requires fewer parallel documents to achieve good performance compared to baseline methods, which is a significant practical advantage. The results show consistent improvements across all 18 tasks, with the proposed method outperforming baselines by 8.59-14.61% in average test accuracy.

The paper advances the state of the art in cross-lingual representation learning, particularly for scenarios with limited parallel data. This is a significant contribution given the practical constraints of many real-world applications where collecting large amounts of parallel data is difficult or expensive.

## Strengths

1. The two-step approach (matrix completion followed by LSI) is novel compared to existing methods that apply LSI directly on the partially observed matrix.
2. The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation across 18 different cross-lingual classification tasks.
3. The method demonstrates robust performance even with a small number of unlabeled parallel documents, which is a significant practical advantage.
4. The paper includes a convergence analysis for the proposed optimization algorithm, which adds theoretical rigor.
5. The parameter selection process is well-documented and the results are statistically validated through multiple repetitions.

## Weaknesses

1. The comparison is limited to traditional methods rather than including more recent neural network-based approaches to cross-lingual transfer learning.
2. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why matrix completion is particularly well-suited for this problem compared to other missing data handling techniques.
3. The parameter selection process is somewhat ad-hoc and not fully justified across all tasks.
4. The paper claims the method requires "a very small number of unlabeled parallel documents," but the experiments use 2000 unlabeled documents, which may not be considered "small" in practice.
5. The computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method compared to baselines is not discussed.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual representation learning with a novel two-step approach that demonstrates significant improvements over existing methods. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and the method has clear practical advantages in terms of requiring fewer parallel documents.

However, the authors should address the following points before publication:

1. Include a comparison with more recent neural network-based cross-lingual transfer methods to better position their work in the current landscape of the field.
2. Provide a more detailed justification for why matrix completion is particularly well-suited for this problem.
3. Discuss the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method.
4. Clarify the claim about "very small" numbers of parallel documents by providing more context or examples of how their method compares to existing approaches in terms of data requirements.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Novel two-step approach (matrix completion followed by LSI) for cross-lingual representation learning
- Significant performance improvements over multiple baselines across 18 tasks
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation with statistical validation
- Demonstrated effectiveness with limited parallel data
- Theoretical justification with convergence analysis
- Clear problem statement and well-organized presentation

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- Limited comparison with more recent neural approaches to cross-lingual transfer learning
- Parameter selection process could be more rigorously justified
- Claims about "very small" parallel data requirements need more context
- No discussion of computational complexity or scalability

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in cross-lingual representation learning with a practical method that requires fewer parallel documents than existing approaches. With the suggested revisions, this paper would be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.
