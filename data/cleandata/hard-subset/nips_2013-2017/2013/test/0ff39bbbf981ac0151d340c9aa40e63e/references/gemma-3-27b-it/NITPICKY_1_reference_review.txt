This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification based on matrix completion and latent semantic indexing (LSI). The core idea is to construct a concatenated document-term matrix, impute missing values using matrix completion to capture cross-lingual relationships, and then perform LSI for dimensionality reduction and classification. While the problem addressed – cross-lingual text classification – is relevant to NIPS, and the proposed approach has some merit, I find numerous shortcomings, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to more significant issues with the experimental validation and justification of design choices.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Cross-lingual learning is a significant area of NLP research, particularly given the scarcity of labeled data in many languages.
*   **Reasonable Approach:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI is conceptually plausible for learning cross-lingual representations. The attempt to leverage unlabeled parallel data is also a positive aspect.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate improvements over several baselines, suggesting the approach has some practical value.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigorous Justification:** The rationale behind many design choices, such as the specific choice of the ℓ1 norm in the matrix completion objective, the projected gradient descent algorithm itself, and the selection of hyperparameters (γ, ρ, k) is lacking. The authors state they chose parameters via a grid search on a single task (EFB) but don’t provide details or justification for why those parameters generalize to other tasks.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** There are numerous instances of inconsistent notation. For example, Ω is defined as the index set of *observed* features, but then used later to define the set of *missing* features (Ω̂). This is confusing and suggests a lack of careful proofreading.
*   **Incomplete Optimization Details:** The projected gradient descent algorithm is described, but key implementation details are missing. For example, the convergence criterion isn’t specified. The claim that the algorithm provably converges relies on Lemma 1’s conditions being met, but the discussion lacks clarity on whether this is guaranteed.
*   **Limited Baselines:** The comparison to baselines is somewhat limited. While TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA are included, more recent and competitive cross-lingual learning methods are not. The authors do not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method.
*   **Insufficient Ablation Study:** There's no ablation study to understand the contribution of each step (matrix completion vs. LSI). Is the performance gain primarily due to matrix completion, LSI, or the interaction of both?
*   **Ambiguous Parallel Data Usage:** The description of how the parallel data is used is vague. It’s stated that the parallel documents "bridge the vocabulary sets," but the specific impact of parallel data quantity is not thoroughly explored. The impact of parallel data size is tested, but it does not thoroughly explain why performance improves (i.e. what kind of signal is the parallel data giving to the model).
*   **Minor Typographical Errors:** Throughout the paper, minor grammatical errors and stylistic inconsistencies detract from readability.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Section 1:** The introduction lacks sufficient motivation. Why is this particular two-step approach better than existing methods *a priori*?
*   **Section 2:** The related work section is overly descriptive and lacks critical analysis. It simply lists papers without adequately discussing their limitations or how the proposed method addresses them.
*   **Section 3.1:** The rationale for adding the ℓ1 norm to the matrix completion objective isn't clear. Why sparsity? Does it improve performance? If so, show it. The definition of Ω and Ω̂ is immediately inconsistent and confusing.
*   **Section 4.1:** The explanation of the gradient descent step is unclear and the explanation for the shrinkage step is not understandable. The update procedure is described, but the convergence criterion is missing.
*   **Section 5:** The experimental setup is adequate, but the justification for the hyperparameter selection is weak. The number of experiments (10 runs) seems insufficient for strong conclusions. The results are presented in tables, but a more detailed statistical analysis (e.g., significance testing) would be beneficial.
*   **Figure 1 and 2:** The figures are difficult to interpret without error bars on each data point.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of rigorous justification for key design choices.
*   Numerous notational inconsistencies and minor errors.
*   Incomplete experimental analysis (limited baselines, lack of ablation study).
*   Overall impression of a technically sound but not particularly innovative or well-polished work.

**For Acceptance:**

*   The problem is relevant and important.
*   The proposed approach shows some empirical improvements over baselines.
*   The paper presents a reasonably complete description of the method.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite presenting a potentially useful approach, the numerous shortcomings, particularly the lack of justification and detailed experimental analysis, lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper feels incomplete and lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS publication. While the empirical results are encouraging, they are not sufficient to overcome the substantial methodological and presentation issues. I believe significant revisions, addressing the points mentioned above, are required before this work would be suitable for NIPS.
