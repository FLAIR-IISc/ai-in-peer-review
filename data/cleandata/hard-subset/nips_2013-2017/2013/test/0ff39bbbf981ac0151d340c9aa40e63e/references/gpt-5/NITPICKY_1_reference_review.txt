Summary
This paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-lingual text classification. Step 1 completes a partially observed concatenated document-term matrix (source and target vocabularies concatenated) using a convex surrogate for low-rank matrix completion with additional sparsity (l1), nonnegativity constraints, and squared losses on both observed and missing entries (the latter with weight ρ). It is optimized via a projected gradient descent scheme that alternates a gradient step on the smooth part, singular value shrinkage (nuclear norm proximal), and nonnegativity projection. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix and trains a monolingual classifier (linear SVM) in the low-dimensional space. Experiments on the multilingual Amazon reviews dataset show improvements over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, with study of varying numbers of parallel documents.

Relation to prior work
The method is positioned relative to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, bilingual lexicon/SCL methods, and multilingual topic models. The novelty claim is to fill missing entries via matrix completion before LSI, rather than applying LSI/CCA/OPCA directly to concatenated or paired data. However, the comparison set omits strong baselines like SCL (which is cited) and modern cross-lingual embedding approaches; the framing of CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA usage could be clearer to ensure apples-to-apples.

Strengths
- Interesting and simple idea: leverage a small parallel set to “complete” cross-lingual co-occurrences, then do standard LSI.
- Uses convex surrogates (nuclear norm + squared loss) with a scalable spirit (first-order, shrinkage).
- Empirical results suggest consistent gains across many language/category pairs and robustness to small numbers of parallel documents.
- Includes a convergence discussion and parameter sensitivity to the size of parallel data.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and optimization
- Prox splitting: The algorithm alternates (i) a gradient step on g(M), (ii) nuclear-norm shrinkage, and (iii) nonnegativity projection. This is not, in general, the proximal operator for the sum (nuclear norm + indicator of nonnegativity). Composition of two prox operators does not equal the prox of their sum unless additional structure holds. The convergence proof appeals to non-expansiveness of each operator and cites [11, Theorem 4], which does not cover an additional projection constraint. As written, Theorem 1 does not convincingly establish convergence to a minimizer of the stated objective with nonnegativity.
- The bound 0 < τ < min(2, 2/ρ) is derived elementwise using Y and ρŶ, but no Lipschitz constant for ∇g over the full matrix is provided, nor is the descent property established. This matters especially since the l1 term is treated as smooth via M ≥ 0; at the boundary Mij = 0 the gradient is not uniquely defined, and the analysis ignores that subtlety.
- Biasing missing entries to zero: Adding a squared-loss penalty toward 0 on all missing entries (with ρ > 0) strongly biases completion toward zero—on top of nuclear-norm and l1 regularization. This can over-shrink infrequent or cross-lingual features. No ablation of ρ is provided beyond a coarse grid; no rationale for this design is given.
- Scalability: Each iteration needs an SVD for shrinkage. There is no complexity analysis (matrix sizes t × d, rank behavior, use of partial SVD, number of iterations, wall-clock). Claims of scalability are unsubstantiated.

Experimental protocol and fairness
- Transductive use of test data: The paper states “we used all documents from the two languages … for representation learning,” which appears to include test documents in the unsupervised step. This is a different setting from inductive domain adaptation and must be clearly justified, with matching baselines. If transductive, it should be explicit and contrasted with inductive performance.
- Hyperparameter selection: Parameters (γ, ρ, k) are selected on task EFB and then reused for all tasks. It is unclear whether test labels were used in that selection. A proper validation split (or nested CV) should be used; otherwise there is risk of tuning to test performance.
- Baselines: SCL [16] is a natural and strong comparator on this very dataset but is missing. More recent cross-lingual representation learning baselines (e.g., bilingual embeddings/Projection via seed lexicons) are also absent. For CL-LSI/CL-OPCA, the paper implies using “all data from both languages” without clarifying whether machine translation or only parallel data was used; fairness depends on these specifics.
- Classifier details: The text says “linear SVM” but “libsvm with default parameter setting.” libsvm’s default kernel is RBF; unless kernel=linear is explicitly set, this contradicts the stated “linear SVM.” This must be clarified, as it can materially affect results.
- Reproducibility: Critical details are missing: vocabulary sizes, tokenization (especially for Japanese), normalization (document length, TF-IDF specifics across languages), stopping criterion and number of iterations, SVD implementation (full vs truncated), and runtime/memory. Table 1 and Figures 1–2 are not shown; numeric results cannot be verified.

Conceptual and modeling issues
- The claim that a fully observed document-term matrix is “naturally low-rank” is not accurate in general; empirical support for a low intrinsic rank assumption is needed (e.g., spectrum plots).
- Nonnegativity plus l1: Treating l1 as smooth under nonnegativity sidesteps non-differentiability at zero; the practical effect (e.g., sparsity patterns, “sticking” at zero due to projection) is not analyzed.
- No ablations: The paper lacks ablations isolating the contributions of (a) matrix completion vs direct LSI; (b) nonnegativity; (c) the l1 term; (d) the penalty on missing entries.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typographical/formatting issues impede readability, e.g., c(x,y)=12||x−y|| vs 1/2||x−y||^2; inconsistent spacing in min(2, 2/ρ); “documentterm”; inconsistent equation numbering and punctuation; Algorithm 1 has inconsistent numbering (“. gradient descent”, “2. shrink”).
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages) and contain venue typos (e.g., “ICCL-ACL”). Many entries lack sufficient bibliographic detail for readers to locate the works.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, potentially useful idea of using matrix completion to densify cross-lingual co-occurrence before LSI.
- Promising empirical gains and robustness w.r.t. number of parallel documents.

Cons for acceptance
- Convergence proof does not convincingly apply to the stated constrained objective; algorithmic correctness is questionable.
- Potential transductive leakage and unclear hyperparameter selection protocol.
- Missing strong baselines (e.g., SCL) and unclear fairness of implemented baselines.
- Incomplete experimental and implementation details; no runtime analysis; absent ablations.
- Numerous typos/formatting/reference issues.

Questions for the authors
1. Did you include unlabeled test documents in the representation learning step? If so, please report inductive (train-only) results for completeness.
2. How was “linear SVM” configured in libsvm? Was kernel=linear set? What C value?
3. Please provide complexity, iteration counts, and wall-clock times; which truncated SVD library was used?
4. Can you provide ablations for ρ=0, µ=0, and without nonnegativity? What is the sensitivity to k?
5. Why is the sequential application of shrinkage and nonnegativity projection guaranteed to minimize the sum of nuclear norm and indicator? Please provide a rigorous proof or use a splitting method with known convergence guarantees.

Recommendation
I recommend rejection in the current form. While the idea is appealing and results look promising, the optimization methodology lacks a rigorous justification for the stated objective, the evaluation protocol is unclear and potentially transductive, critical baselines are missing, and the paper has substantial clarity and formatting issues. With a corrected algorithmic treatment (or proof), a stricter and fully specified experimental protocol (including SCL and inductive results), ablations, and polished presentation, this work could become much stronger.
