Summary
The paper addresses cross-lingual text classification when labels in the target language are scarce. It proposes a two-step method: (1) construct a unified bilingual document-term matrix where parallel document pairs are fully observed across both vocabularies but non-parallel documents are only observed in their own language; recover the missing entries by convex matrix completion with a trace-norm penalty, an ℓ1 penalty, and non-negativity; (2) run LSI (SVD) on the recovered matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations on which standard monolingual classifiers are trained. Optimization uses a projected gradient descent with singular value shrinkage and non-negativity projection, and the method is evaluated on multilingual Amazon reviews against CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA, and a weak TBOW baseline. The method reports consistent improvements and robustness with few parallel documents.

Strengths
- Well-motivated problem and clear intuition: use parallel documents to bridge vocabularies, complete the bilingual matrix to exploit cross-lingual co-occurrence, then apply LSI for denoising.
- Simple, modular pipeline that uses standard building blocks (nuclear norm minimization + SVD), easy to implement and potentially domain-agnostic.
- The empirical results on 18 English–French/German/Japanese sentiment tasks show consistent gains over CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA and robustness to small parallel corpora.
- The optimization algorithm is practical (singular value thresholding plus projection), with a convergence argument sketched via non-expansive operators.
- Ablation on the number of parallel documents is useful and relevant to low-resource scenarios.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical correctness and rigor: the convergence guarantee appears to rely on results for a different proximal mapping (SVT for the trace norm) without fully accounting for the added non-negativity projection and the treatment of the ℓ1 term as smooth. Composition of shrinkage and projection is not, in general, the proximal operator of the sum, so invoking [11, Theorem 4] is insufficient to claim convergence to the exact minimizer of (2).
- Objective design questions: the model simultaneously uses a nuclear norm, an ℓ1 penalty on entries (equivalent to a linear penalty under non-negativity), and an ℓ2 loss on missing entries toward zero (ρ-term). These all bias entries toward zero and may conflict with the goal of imputing missing cross-lingual signals. No ablation shows the necessity of ℓ1 vs the ρ-regularization, or the effect of non-negativity.
- The two-step dimensionality reduction may be redundant: matrix completion already enforces low-rank, and then LSI again computes an SVD. A single weighted low-rank approximation (or directly using the left singular vectors) could suffice. An ablation “without step 2” is missing.
- Baselines and fairness: CL-SCL (Prettenhofer & Stein, 2010), a strong baseline on this exact dataset, is cited but not included empirically. Also, MT-based translation baselines (word-by-word or off-the-shelf MT) are omitted. For CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA, only k is tuned; other method-specific hyperparameters (kernels, regularization) are not reported, and SVM C is left at default, which can disadvantage baselines.
- Scalability concerns: per-iteration SVD on a large t×d matrix is expensive; no runtime, memory footprint, or use of truncated/partial SVD is reported. The approach may not scale to larger vocabularies/corpora.
- Experimental detail gaps: unclear exactly how CL-LSI and CL-OPCA were instantiated given only a small set of parallel pairs; were non-parallel documents concatenated with zeros, or was LSI restricted to parallel pairs? The dataset description and preprocessing are standard, but reproducibility (code, seeds, iteration counts) is limited. References are incomplete.
- Limited analysis: no qualitative inspection of cross-lingual alignment (e.g., nearest-neighbor word/doc alignments), no sensitivity to k, γ, ρ beyond a single-task selection, and no reporting of variance across runs for all tasks in the main table.

Quality
- Method is technically plausible and reasonably implemented, but the optimization theory is not fully sound as written. The empirical evaluation is positive but lacks some strong baselines and careful hyperparameter tuning for competitors. No ablations to validate design choices.

Clarity
- Generally clear motivation and presentation. Notation is mostly consistent. However, the algorithmic description is terse; the use of ℓ1 “as smooth” under non-negativity and the exact proximal splitting need clarification. The proof section is too sketchy to substantiate the theorem. References need complete bibliographic info.

Originality
- The combination is incremental: matrix completion with nuclear norm and non-negativity is known; CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA are established. The novelty lies in imputing the bilingual document-term matrix before LSI. This is a reasonable idea, but not conceptually groundbreaking.

Significance
- If the reported gains hold against stronger baselines and at larger scales, the approach could be a useful practical alternative when only small parallel corpora are available. As it stands, significance is moderate due to theoretical and empirical shortcomings.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Provide a correct convergence result or reframe the algorithm as a standard proximal-splitting method and cite an appropriate theorem. Alternatively, drop the claim of optimality and report empirical convergence only.
- Ablate: remove ℓ1, vary ρ (including ρ=0), remove non-negativity, and remove the LSI step to justify each component.
- Include CL-SCL and a translation-based baseline (e.g., MT source→target + BOW SVM) and tune SVM C for all methods.
- Report runtime, iteration counts, and use truncated SVD; discuss memory usage and scalability.
- Clarify construction of CL-LSI/OPCA with limited parallel data and whether non-parallel documents are included.
- Add qualitative alignment analyses and per-task standard deviations in the main table; perform significance tests.
- Fix and complete references.

Recommendation
- Score: 5 (weak reject). The idea is reasonable and results are promising, but theoretical claims are not fully justified and the experimental comparison and analysis are not yet strong enough for NeurIPS. Addressing the above issues could make this worthy of acceptance.
