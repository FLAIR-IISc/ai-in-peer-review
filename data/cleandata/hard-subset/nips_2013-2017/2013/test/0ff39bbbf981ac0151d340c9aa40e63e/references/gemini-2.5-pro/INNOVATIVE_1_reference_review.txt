### Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a novel two-step learning method (TSL) for cross-language text classification. The core problem is to leverage labeled data from a source language to train a classifier for a target language where labeled data is scarce. The authors' approach hinges on a clever reformulation of the problem. First, they construct a single, unified document-term matrix containing all documents from both languages. In this matrix, monolingual documents are represented as sparse vectors with "missing" values for the vocabulary of the other language, while the few available unlabeled parallel documents are represented as complete vectors. The key contribution is the first step: treating this partially-observed matrix as a matrix completion problem. By solving a convex optimization problem that encourages a low-rank and sparse solution, the method "fills in" the missing cross-lingual term information. In the second step, standard Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to the completed matrix to produce a low-dimensional, dense, and shared representation space. Classifiers can then be trained in this space.

This work distinguishes itself from prior art, such as CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, which also leverage parallel corpora. While those methods directly apply dimensionality reduction or projection techniques (like LSI or CCA) to a concatenated matrix (treating unobserved cross-lingual terms as zeros), this paper introduces a principled intermediate step of matrix completion to impute these values first. This conceptual shift is the paper's main novelty.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Originality and Novelty:** The primary strength of this paper is its conceptual novelty. Framing the challenge of bridging different vocabularies as a matrix completion problem is an elegant and powerful idea. It recasts the problem from one of projection or translation to one of imputation within a unified space. This is a genuinely new perspective in the context of cross-lingual representation learning and represents the kind of bold, high-level thinking that can open up new research avenues.

2.  **Significant Empirical Performance:** The proposed TSL method demonstrates substantial and consistent improvements over several strong baselines across 18 different cross-lingual classification tasks. The gains are not marginal; TSL often outperforms the next-best method by several percentage points, which is a significant achievement in this well-studied area.

3.  **Impressive Data Efficiency:** Perhaps the most compelling result is the method's robustness when using very small amounts of unlabeled parallel data. As shown in Figures 1 and 2, TSL maintains a strong performance advantage even with just 200 parallel documents, a regime where competitors' performances degrade significantly. This is a crucial practical advantage, as large, high-quality parallel corpora can be difficult or expensive to obtain. This result strongly validates the power of the matrix completion formulation to effectively leverage sparse cross-lingual signals.

4.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The methodology is explained logically, the optimization problem is well-defined, and the connection to prior work is established. The inclusion of a convergence proof, while leveraging existing results, adds to the technical soundness of the proposed optimization algorithm.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Conservative Second Step:** The choice of Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) for the second step is methodologically conservative. LSI is a classic technique, but the field has since developed more powerful methods for learning dense representations. It would have been interesting to see the potential of the completed matrix `M*` when coupled with more modern techniques, such as a deep autoencoder. While this does not invalidate the contribution of the first step, it feels like a potential area for easy improvement and leaves the reader wondering if the full potential of the completed matrix has been realized.

2.  **Scalability Questions:** The paper does not address the computational complexity or scalability of the proposed method. The core of the algorithm involves projected gradient descent, where each iteration requires computing a gradient and performing a shrinkage operation via SVD. For datasets with very large vocabularies and many documents, the SVD step can become a significant bottleneck. A discussion of runtime performance or potential approximations for the SVD would strengthen the paper's practical claims.

3.  **Limited Hyperparameter Validation:** Hyperparameters were selected based on performance on a single task (EFB) and then applied universally to all 18 tasks. While the consistently strong results suggest a degree of robustness, this is not ideal. Different language pairs or domains might benefit from different regularization strengths. This is a minor weakness but one that could be addressed to make the experimental validation more rigorous.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea of using matrix completion to create a unified cross-lingual representation space is highly novel and conceptually significant. It is a bold departure from existing paradigms.
*   The empirical results are very strong, showing clear state-of-the-art performance.
*   The method's exceptional performance with limited parallel data is a major practical contribution.
*   The paper has the potential to inspire follow-up work exploring this "imputation-then-embedding" framework with other models.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The technical components (matrix completion, LSI) are individually not new; the novelty lies in their specific application and combination.
*   The evaluation could be strengthened with a discussion of scalability and more rigorous hyperparameter tuning.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper. The conceptual novelty of the matrix completion framework is outstanding and provides a fresh and powerful way to think about cross-lingual learning. This bold idea is backed by compelling and robust empirical evidence that demonstrates a clear and significant advance over existing methods, especially in the practical, low-resource setting. While there are minor methodological weaknesses, such as the use of a simple LSI model and limited hyperparameter tuning, these are forgivable in light of the significance and novelty of the core contribution. This is precisely the type of paper that NIPS should encourage: one that introduces a big new idea that works, paving the way for future refinements and extensions.
