## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification. The method leverages unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to learn cross-lingual representations. First, it constructs a concatenated document-term matrix and uses matrix completion to fill in missing values, effectively bridging the language gap. Second, it applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual document representations, upon which a monolingual classifier can be applied. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, showing improvements over several baselines.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion *followed by* LSI for cross-lingual representation learning is a novel approach. While both matrix completion and LSI have been used in related contexts (mentioned in the related work), their sequential application in this manner for cross-lingual learning feels fresh.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate superior performance compared to several strong baselines, including CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA. The improvement, especially on tasks with limited labeled target data, is significant.
* **Robustness to Parallel Data Size:**  The paper demonstrates that the proposed method performs well even with a small number of unlabeled parallel documents, which is a practical advantage.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and relatively easy to follow. The algorithm is clearly described, and the experimental setup is explained in detail.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The authors provide a comprehensive overview of related work in cross-lingual learning, positioning their method within the existing landscape.  They acknowledge connections to and differences from various approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Justification:** While the authors mention the expected benefits of low-rank representation and capturing co-occurrence patterns, the theoretical justification for *why* this specific two-step approach is superior to, say, directly applying LSI to the sparse matrix or other matrix factorization techniques is somewhat lacking.  A more in-depth discussion of the theoretical properties of matrix completion in this context would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The parameter selection process (γ, ρ, k) is described as being performed on a single task (EFB) and then applied to all others. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the chosen parameters and potentially suboptimal performance on other tasks. A more systematic hyperparameter tuning strategy across all tasks would strengthen the results.
* **Scalability Discussion:** The optimization algorithm (projected gradient descent) is described, but a more detailed discussion of its scalability to very large datasets and vocabularies would be valuable, particularly given the computational cost of matrix completion.  Mentioning practical considerations (e.g., memory requirements, parallelization strategies) would be helpful.
* **Limited Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on sentiment classification. Expanding the experimental evaluation to other cross-lingual tasks (e.g., topic categorization) would demonstrate the broader applicability of the proposed method.



**Review Criteria Assessment:**

* **Quality (8/10):** The paper presents a technically sound approach with well-supported empirical results. The optimization algorithm is described in detail and its convergence is discussed. However, the lack of deeper theoretical justification and the somewhat ad-hoc hyperparameter tuning prevent a higher score.
* **Clarity (9/10):** The paper is generally very clearly written and well-organized. The notation is consistent, and the experimental setup is thoroughly explained.
* **Originality (7/10):**  The combination of matrix completion and LSI is novel, but both techniques are well-established in related fields. The originality lies in the specific application and the demonstrated effectiveness of this combination for cross-lingual learning.
* **Significance (8/10):** The results are significant, demonstrating a clear improvement over existing methods, particularly in low-resource scenarios.  The ability to leverage unlabeled parallel data effectively is a valuable contribution.  This approach has the potential to reduce the need for expensive labeled data in cross-lingual applications.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong empirical performance consistently outperforming several strong baselines.
* Novel combination of techniques (matrix completion + LSI) for cross-lingual representation learning.
* Robustness to the amount of available parallel data.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited theoretical justification for the chosen approach.
* Potential hyperparameter sensitivity and lack of systematic tuning.
* Scalability concerns not fully addressed.
* Limited evaluation on diverse cross-lingual tasks.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors have presented a compelling and effective approach to cross-lingual representation learning. Addressing the weaknesses related to theoretical justification, hyperparameter tuning, and scalability would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.  Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1. Provide a more detailed theoretical discussion of the benefits of matrix completion in the context of cross-lingual representation learning.
2. Perform a more systematic hyperparameter tuning strategy, potentially using cross-validation, across all tasks.
3.  Briefly discuss the scalability of the algorithm and potential strategies for handling large datasets.
4.  Consider adding experiments on at least one additional cross-lingual task.
