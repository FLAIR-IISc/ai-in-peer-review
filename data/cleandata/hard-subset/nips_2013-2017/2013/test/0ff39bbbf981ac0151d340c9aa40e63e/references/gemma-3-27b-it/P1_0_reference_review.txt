## NIPS Paper Review: A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification. The core idea is to leverage a small set of unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to learn cross-lingual representations. The first step employs matrix completion to fill missing entries in a concatenated document-term matrix, effectively bridging the vocabulary gap between languages. The second step utilizes Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) on the completed matrix to create a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation for subsequent classification. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on cross-lingual sentiment classification with Amazon product reviews, showing improvements over several existing methods.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper positions itself within the broader field of cross-lingual learning, specifically addressing the challenges posed by differing feature spaces. It acknowledges existing approaches that rely on machine translation (e.g., [18, 23]), domain adaptation ([17, 9, 21]), and multi-view learning ([22, 2, 1, 13, 12]). The authors differentiate their work by requiring only a small amount of *unlabeled* parallel data, contrasting with methods that translate entire datasets. They also connect their approach to methods leveraging bilingual resources like word pairs ([16]) and parallel corpora ([10, 20, 15]), but emphasize the novelty of using matrix completion *before* dimensionality reduction techniques like LSI.  The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, covering key approaches and accurately positioning the contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning appears novel. Applying matrix completion to a document-term matrix with missing language-specific terms is a clever way to exploit the limited parallel data to bridge the vocabulary gap.
* **Performance:** The experimental results demonstrate significant performance improvements over several baselines, indicating the efficacy of the proposed method. The improvements are consistent across various language pairs and categories.
* **Efficiency with Limited Parallel Data:**  The paper specifically highlights and empirically demonstrates the effectiveness of the method even with a small amount of unlabeled parallel data, a practical advantage in real-world scenarios where parallel corpora are often scarce. The provided results showing performance gains even with only 200 parallel documents are compelling.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of cross-lingual text classification and the challenges posed by language barriers.
* **Detailed Optimization Algorithm:** The inclusion of the projected gradient descent algorithm, complete with convergence analysis, demonstrates a thorough understanding of the underlying optimization problem.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Matrix Completion Justification:** While the matrix completion step is presented as a key innovation, the rationale for expecting a low-rank structure in this specific document-term matrix is not fully elaborated.  A more detailed discussion of *why* this assumption is reasonable would strengthen the paper.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions parameter selection (γ, ρ, k) but lacks a comprehensive discussion of the sensitivity of the results to these parameters.  More analysis of parameter tuning and its impact on performance would be valuable.
* **Scalability:** While the algorithm is presented, its practical scalability to very large datasets is not thoroughly addressed. The authors mention a scalable algorithm, but provide little detail on computational complexity or memory requirements.
* **Limited Baselines:** While the selected baselines are relevant, the comparison could be strengthened by including more recent and state-of-the-art cross-lingual learning methods, if available.
* **Presentation of Results:** While the tables are clear, the figures (especially Figure 1 and 2) could be more informative. For example, error bars representing standard deviation could be included directly on the plotted lines for easier comparison.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a well-defined optimization problem and a clear description of the algorithm. The empirical results support the claims, but further analysis of parameter sensitivity and scalability would strengthen the work.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the algorithm is presented in a structured manner. However, some sections, particularly those relating to the optimization algorithm, could benefit from further simplification for a broader audience.

**Originality:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning is a novel approach.  The paper clearly differentiates itself from existing work by focusing on the use of unlabeled parallel data and a unique two-step learning procedure.

**Significance:** The results suggest that the proposed method is a significant improvement over existing techniques, particularly in scenarios with limited parallel data. The potential to reduce the need for expensive manual annotation makes this work potentially valuable for real-world applications.


**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Novel and effective approach to cross-lingual representation learning.
* Strong empirical results demonstrating significant performance improvements.
* Practical relevance due to the ability to leverage limited parallel data.
* Thorough theoretical analysis, including the development and convergence analysis of the optimization algorithm.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of detailed justification for the low-rank assumption in matrix completion.
* Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity and scalability.
*  Could benefit from inclusion of more recent state-of-the-art baselines.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. The proposed method is novel, effective, and addresses a practical challenge. While some weaknesses remain, they are not critical enough to warrant rejection. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly focusing on a more detailed justification of the matrix completion step, analysis of parameter sensitivity, and a discussion of scalability.
