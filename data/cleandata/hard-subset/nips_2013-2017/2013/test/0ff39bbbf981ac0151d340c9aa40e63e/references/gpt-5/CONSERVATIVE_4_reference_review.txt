Summary
The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual text classification. Step 1 completes a large, partially observed bilingual document–term matrix (constructed by concatenating vocabularies and treating non-parallel regions as missing) by solving a convex optimization problem with a trace-norm (low-rank) penalty, an ℓ1 penalty (sparsity), squared losses on observed entries, a small quadratic regularizer on missing entries, and nonnegativity constraints. A projected gradient–shrinkage procedure with per-iteration SVD is used. Step 2 applies standard LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain low-dimensional representations for training a linear SVM. Experiments on the multilingual Amazon reviews dataset (EN, FR, DE, JA; 3 categories) compare against TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, claiming consistent gains and robustness with few parallel pairs.

Relation to prior work
The approach sits between classical CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA methods that operate directly on concatenated (or paired) matrices and more recent cross-lingual representation learning via bilingual lexicons, topic models, and co-training. The novelty is to first impute the missing cross-language blocks via nuclear-norm–based completion, then apply LSI. This is related to matrix completion literature (e.g., Ma et al., Candès & Recht) and older cross-lingual representation methods, but the experimental baselines are limited to pre-neural approaches.

Strengths
- Intuitive formulation: impute missing cross-language entries using low-rank structure inferred from parallel pairs and co-occurrence in both languages.
- Convex objective; the algorithmic steps (gradient, shrinkage, nonnegativity projection) are simple to implement.
- Empirical improvements over CL-LSI/CL-OPCA/CL-KCCA on Amazon reviews, with some analysis of performance vs. number of parallel pairs.
- Clear problem setup and reasonable experimental protocol (fixed 100 target labels; source labels used; multiple runs).

Weaknesses and evaluation by criteria

Quality (technical soundness)
- The optimization algorithm and convergence claim are under-justified. The proposed iteration applies shrinkage (prox of nuclear norm) followed by a projection onto the nonnegative orthant. In general, prox of a sum (nuclear norm + indicator of nonnegativity) is not equal to sequential application of individual prox operators. The non-expansiveness arguments do not establish that fixed points of the composite operator correspond to optimizers of the stated constrained problem (2). The citation to [11, Theorem 4] (Ma et al.) pertains to unconstrained nuclear-norm problems without the extra nonnegativity projection. A rigorous proof (e.g., via proximal splitting with convergence guarantees for the sum of a nonsmooth nuclear norm and an indicator) or adoption of an algorithm designed for multiple nonsmooth terms (e.g., Dykstra’s algorithm, ADMM) is needed.
- The modeling assumptions are weakly defended. The statement that a “fully observed document–term matrix is naturally low-rank” is questionable for TF-IDF document–term matrices; low-rank structure is an assumption, not a property. The paper should provide evidence (e.g., singular value spectra) and discuss when the assumption holds.
- The inclusion of both trace norm and ℓ1 penalties under nonnegativity imposes competing priors (low-rank tends to dense factors; ℓ1 encourages elementwise sparsity). There is no ablation showing whether ℓ1 materially helps, nor sensitivity to μ and ρ. The choice to penalize missing entries toward zero (ρ > 0) biases the imputation; this trade-off should be explored.
- Scalability is not addressed. Each iteration requires (at least truncated) SVD of a large t×d matrix; no timings, memory profiles, or iteration counts are reported. It is unclear whether the method scales beyond the relatively small Amazon datasets.
- Baseline tuning is limited (libsvm defaults; same k across methods). No statistical significance testing is reported beyond standard deviations.
- No ablation on the “two-step” nature: What happens if one uses only the completed matrix (no LSI)? Or only LSI with zero-filled missing blocks? This is key to substantiate the claimed benefit of Step 1.

Clarity
- Overall clear and well organized. The algorithm is specified, and derivations for the gradient are straightforward.
- However, the convergence section is misleading in its current form; it should be rewritten to accurately reflect what is proved and what is assumed. Provide precise conditions and references for composite nonsmooth proximals with constraints.
- Important practical details are missing: how SVD is computed (full vs. truncated), stopping criteria, and computational cost.

Originality
- The two-step pipeline (matrix completion on a concatenated bilingual matrix followed by LSI) is an incremental combination of established tools. The core idea—using nuclear-norm completion to impute unobserved language blocks—is somewhat novel in this specific cross-lingual setting, but close in spirit to prior CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA approaches that impose shared low-dimensional structure using parallel data. The paper would benefit from a sharper positioning and a deeper comparison (qualitative or theoretical) to CL-OPCA and KCCA.

Significance
- Improvements over the chosen baselines are promising on Amazon reviews. However, the scope is narrow (one dataset, one task).
- Missing strong contemporary baselines severely limits significance: no comparisons to cross-lingual word embeddings (e.g., bilingual mappings), multilingual contextual models (mBERT/XLM-R), or MT-based pipelines. Given the maturity of cross-lingual NLP, claims of “significantly outperforms” need to be grounded against such baselines.
- Without scalability analysis and broader evaluation, it is hard to see adoption by practitioners.

Questions for authors
- Provide a rigorous convergence proof (or correct algorithm) for the constrained objective with both nuclear norm and nonnegativity. Would ADMM or Dykstra be more appropriate?
- Ablations: remove ℓ1 term; vary ρ (including ρ=0); skip Step 2; replace Step 2 with NMF; sensitivity to k.
- Computational costs: iterations, SVD strategy, runtime vs. CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA.
- Stronger baselines: CLWE mappings, multilingual transformers, and an MT+monolingual classifier baseline.
- Justify the low-rank assumption empirically (e.g., spectra on observed matrices).

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, conceptually appealing method leveraging limited parallel data.
- Empirical gains over classical baselines; robustness with few parallel pairs.
- Clear exposition.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical gap in the optimization/convergence for the constrained problem.
- Limited novelty (combination of known components) and narrow evaluation.
- Missing modern and stronger baselines; unclear scalability.
- Lack of ablations to validate design choices.

Recommendation
Score: 4 (reject). While the idea is interesting and results over older baselines are positive, the paper lacks rigorous justification for its optimization procedure, omits key baselines and ablations, and does not address scalability. Strengthening the theory (or using an appropriate optimizer), adding modern baselines, and broadening the empirical evaluation would substantially improve the paper.

Confidence: High.
