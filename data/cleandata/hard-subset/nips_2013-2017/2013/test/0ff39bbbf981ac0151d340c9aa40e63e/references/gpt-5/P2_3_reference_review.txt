Summary
The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual text classification using a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 performs matrix completion on a unified bilingual document-term matrix M0, where parallel pairs are fully observed and monolingual documents have missing entries for the other language’s vocabulary. The objective uses a nuclear norm (low-rank), an L1 term (sparsity which becomes linear under nonnegativity), squared-loss fidelity on observed entries, and L2-regularization toward zero on missing entries, with a nonnegativity constraint. The optimization is done via a projected gradient descent scheme with a gradient step on the smooth part, singular-value shrinkage for the nuclear norm, and projection onto the nonnegative orthant. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) on the recovered matrix to derive a low-dimensional shared representation on which a linear SVM is trained using labeled source data and 100 labeled target instances. On multilingual Amazon reviews (EN/FR/DE/JA across 3 domains), the method outperforms TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA on most of 18 transfer tasks and is robust when the number of parallel documents is as low as 200.

Relation to prior work
The method builds on cross-lingual representation techniques such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA and on matrix completion (trace-norm minimization). The novelty relative to CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA is to “densify” the bilingual document-term matrix by completing missing entries before applying LSI, in contrast to directly operating on the original concatenated matrix. It also relates to SCL-style feature induction with bilingual pivots, albeit here learned via low-rank structure. The optimization approach follows fixed-point/shrinkage methods from nuclear-norm minimization.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling is plausible and the optimization steps are standard. The gradient and updates are consistent with the proposed objective. However, the convergence argument is not fully convincing: the algorithm alternates a gradient step, a nuclear-norm proximal (shrinkage), and a projection onto the nonnegative orthant. In general, prox of a sum of two nonsmooth terms (nuclear norm + indicator of nonnegativity) is not equal to sequential prox steps; the provided non-expansiveness lemmas are insufficient to guarantee convergence to the minimizer of the full composite objective (as opposed to a fixed point of the operator). A more rigorous argument (e.g., via three-operator splitting, Dykstra’s algorithm, or ADMM) or empirical convergence diagnostics would strengthen the claim.
- Modeling assumptions: Treating other-language features in monolingual documents as “missing” rather than truly zero is a strong modeling choice. It effectively hallucinates cross-lingual pseudo counts via low-rank completion. This is central to the approach but deserves justification and ablation (e.g., LSI on M0 vs on completed M*, completion without L1, without nonnegativity, varying ρ).
- Empirical evaluation: The benchmark and setup are appropriate and the gains are consistent across 18 tasks, including low-parallel regimes. However, there is no runtime/memory analysis; repeated SVDs on large matrices can be expensive, and practical scalability is important. Significance testing is not reported; the claim of “significant” improvements should be backed by tests. Reproducibility would benefit from more details (stopping criteria, number of iterations, truncated/randomized SVD settings, normalization/TF-IDF specifics, vocabulary pruning, and whether source+target labeled data are always used for training across methods).
- Baselines: CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA are relevant. Missing are strong modern baselines based on cross-lingual word embeddings or bilingual mappings and SCL-style pivot methods (which are cited but not included experimentally). Even an off-the-shelf MT-then-monolingual baseline would contextualize performance.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Notation is consistent, and the algorithm is easy to follow. Minor issues: the loss c(x,y) is inconsistently formatted (ensure squared norm); describe initialization more concretely; provide algorithmic hyperparameters (stopping, SVD rank truncation) and complexity. Figures/tables (not included here) should include confidence intervals or tests.

Originality
The core idea—matrix completion on a bilingual document-term matrix followed by LSI—is a novel combination of established tools in this specific cross-lingual setting. The individual components are standard, and the theoretical contribution is limited to a basic convergence sketch. The contribution is primarily algorithmic/empirical rather than theoretical.

Significance
The empirical gains across 18 transfer tasks and robustness with small parallel data are promising and practically relevant. If the approach scales, practitioners could benefit from a simple procedure that leverages few parallel documents. However, without stronger theoretical grounding and broader baselines/ablations, it is hard to assess how generally this will improve over more modern cross-lingual representation methods.

Pros
- Simple, intuitive method; leverages small parallel corpora effectively.
- Consistent improvements over reasonable baselines on many tasks.
- Clear algorithm; uses standard optimization machinery.
- Analysis of performance vs amount of parallel data.

Cons
- Convergence proof for the composite nonsmooth problem with projection is insufficient.
- Key modeling choice (missing vs zero) not justified via ablation.
- Scalability and computational cost not discussed; SVD-heavy procedure may be expensive.
- Missing stronger baselines (SCL, CLWE/MT-based) and statistical significance tests.
- Reproducibility details (preprocessing, stopping, SVD truncation) are sparse.

Questions for authors
- Provide runtime, memory, and iteration counts; do you use truncated or randomized SVD?
- Ablate the components: no completion (LSI on M0), completion without L1 or without nonnegativity, effect of ρ and γ.
- Justify the “missing not zero” assumption empirically.
- Compare to SCL and cross-lingual word embedding baselines.
- Clarify convergence guarantees or switch to a method with provable convergence for the composite objective.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The empirical results are encouraging and the idea is neat, but the theoretical justification is incomplete for the proposed solver, the novelty is a modest combination of known techniques, and the experimental section lacks key baselines, ablations, and scalability analysis. Strengthening these aspects could make this a solid contribution.
