## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a reasonably well-motivated and technically sound approach to cross-lingual representation learning. The two-step method – matrix completion followed by LSI – is a logical progression, and the empirical results demonstrate a clear improvement over several baselines. However, the novelty is incremental, and the presentation lacks sufficient depth in justifying design choices and discussing limitations. I recommend **Accept with Minor Revisions**.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the challenging problem of cross-lingual text classification, where labeled data is scarce in the target language. The authors propose a two-step method leveraging unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. First, a concatenated document-term matrix is constructed and completed using matrix factorization, aiming to capture cross-lingual co-occurrence patterns. Second, LSI is applied to the completed matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The paper correctly identifies the relevant prior work, categorizing methods into translation-based and cross-lingual representation learning approaches.  The comparison with related methods like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA is appropriate.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Clear Motivation & Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of cross-lingual text classification and the benefits of reducing reliance on labeled target-language data.
* **Sound Methodology:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI is a sensible approach. Matrix completion is a good way to handle the sparsity inherent in the concatenated document-term matrix, and LSI provides a dimensionality reduction technique for creating meaningful representations.
* **Empirically Validated:** The experimental results on Amazon reviews demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms several baselines consistently. The results highlight the benefit of using even a small amount of unlabeled parallel data.
* **Scalable Optimization:** The projected gradient descent algorithm for matrix completion is a practical consideration, addressing the large-scale nature of the problem.  The inclusion of the algorithm and a convergence analysis adds rigor to the work.
* **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments consider a variety of language pairs and categories, enhancing the generalizability of the findings.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Incremental Novelty:** While the combination of matrix completion and LSI is reasonable, the overall novelty is limited. Matrix completion is a well-established technique, and its application in this cross-lingual context, while effective, doesn't represent a paradigm shift.  The authors don't sufficiently distinguish their approach from existing matrix factorization-based methods in cross-lingual settings.
* **Lack of Justification for Design Choices:** The paper lacks a strong rationale for specific parameter settings (e.g., values for γ, ρ, τ, k). Why were these values chosen?  Were any ablation studies performed to determine their impact?  The explanation of the choice of the least-squared loss function is also missing.
* **Limited Discussion of Limitations:** The authors don’t address potential limitations of the approach. For example: How sensitive is the performance to the quality of the parallel data? What if the parallel data is noisy or biased? How does the method perform when the vocabulary overlap between the languages is very small?
* **Insufficient Detail on the Parallel Data:** The source and quality of the unlabeled parallel data are only briefly mentioned. More detail on how this data was obtained and any pre-processing steps applied would be beneficial.
* **Presentation could be more Concise:** The paper is somewhat verbose, repeating similar ideas in multiple sections. Streamlining the presentation would improve readability. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 in the main text feels slightly out of place; it could be moved to the appendix.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3.1:**  The optimization problem (2) could be further motivated. Why is the ℓ1 norm used for sparsity?  Are there alternatives that were considered?
* **Section 4.1:** The projected gradient descent algorithm is described, but a discussion of its computational complexity would be helpful.
* **Section 5.2:**  The justification for choosing 100 labeled target documents seems arbitrary. Was this number chosen based on a sensitivity analysis or pilot experiments?
* **Figure 1 & 2:** The error bars in the figures are small, suggesting consistent results. This is good, but it would be useful to report the p-values for the comparisons to establish statistical significance.
* **Related Work:**  The related work section would benefit from discussing more recent advances in representation learning, such as pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-RoBERTa). While the approach is different, it's important to position it within the broader landscape of cross-lingual transfer learning.

**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Demonstrably improves upon baseline methods in cross-lingual text classification.
* Presents a technically sound and practically implementable approach.
* Thorough experimental evaluation on a relevant dataset.
* Scalable optimization algorithm.

**Con:**

* Incremental novelty – the combination of existing techniques doesn’t represent a major breakthrough.
* Lack of rigorous justification for design choices and parameter settings.
* Limited discussion of limitations.



**6. Recommendation:**

I recommend **Accept with Minor Revisions**. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above, specifically by:

* Providing a stronger justification for the design choices and parameter settings.
* Discussing the limitations of the approach and potential directions for future research.
* Providing more detail on the parallel data used in the experiments.
* Considering the inclusion of recent pre-trained multilingual models in the related work section.
* Streamlining the presentation for clarity and conciseness.



Addressing these points will strengthen the paper and enhance its contribution to the field of cross-lingual representation learning.
