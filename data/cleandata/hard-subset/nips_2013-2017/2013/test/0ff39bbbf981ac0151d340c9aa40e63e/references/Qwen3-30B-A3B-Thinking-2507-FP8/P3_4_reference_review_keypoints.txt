- Proposes a two-step cross-lingual method: matrix completion to impute missing language features in a concatenated document-term matrix, followed by LSI for low-dimensional representations.  
- Uses unlabeled parallel bilingual documents and evaluates on Amazon cross-lingual sentiment classification across 18 tasks.  
- Strength: Technically sound formulation with convergence proof for the optimization algorithm.  
- Strength: Empirical results show consistent improvements over traditional baselines, especially with limited parallel data (TSL outperforms CL-LSI by ~8.6–14.6%).  
- Strength: Thorough comparison to older related work and clear distinction from CL-LSI by filling missing entries.  
- Weakness: Limited novelty—similar ideas (matrix completion for cross-lingual transfer) exist in recent literature and the method is essentially matrix completion + LSI.  
- Weakness: Fails to compare against modern neural baselines (e.g., multilingual BERT, XLM-R), limiting assessment of current relevance.  
- Weakness: Lacks theoretical analysis showing why matrix completion yields better representations than simpler imputations or ignoring missing values.  
- Weakness: No discussion of computational complexity or scalability for large document-term matrices.  
- Weakness: No exploration of parameter sensitivity; reported results rely on specific hyperparameters without robustness analysis.  
- Specific comment: Claim of strong performance with “very small” parallel data is unsupported—smallest tested set was 200 documents, not 20–50.  
- Specific comment: "Unified vocabulary set" description is unclear (union vs. preprocessing like stemming/lemmatization), harming reproducibility.  
- Specific comment: Reported improvement margins are modest relative to modern SOTA; significance of gains is not convincingly argued.  
- Overall: Technically sound but incremental; does not engage with current neural SOTA and lacks sufficient novelty or practical analysis.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject; revise to include neural baselines, stronger theoretical justification, scalability/parameter analyses, and experiments with very small parallel sets for reconsideration.
