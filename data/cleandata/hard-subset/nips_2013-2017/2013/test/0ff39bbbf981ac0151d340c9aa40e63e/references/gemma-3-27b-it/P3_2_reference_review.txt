## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a conceptually sound and well-executed approach to cross-lingual representation learning. The two-step process of matrix completion followed by LSI is a reasonable strategy for leveraging unlabeled parallel data to bridge the language gap. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating significant improvements over several baselines. While the core ideas aren't revolutionary, the combination and thorough experimental validation make this a strong submission suitable for NIPS.

**1. Summary & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper tackles the challenging problem of cross-lingual text classification, where labeled data is scarce in the target language. It proposes a two-step method: (1) constructing a partially observed document-term matrix using parallel documents and completing it via matrix completion, and (2) applying Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to obtain cross-lingual representations.  

The authors correctly position their work within the broader landscape of cross-lingual learning. They distinguish their approach from methods relying heavily on machine translation (e.g., [17, 21, 22]) by minimizing the need for translating entire datasets.  They also differentiate themselves from existing methods leveraging parallel data like CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], and CL-OPCA [15] by introducing the crucial intermediate step of matrix completion *before* dimensionality reduction. This addresses the potential issue of working with highly sparse matrices and aims to capture more robust co-occurrence patterns. The references cited are relevant and paint a good picture of the state-of-the-art.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides convincing evidence of the method’s effectiveness through experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews. The consistent outperformance of strong baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) is a major strength.
* **Novelty of Approach:** While matrix completion and LSI are individually well-established techniques, their *combination* in this two-step framework for cross-lingual representation learning is novel and well-motivated.  The authors clearly articulate the rationale behind using matrix completion to handle the sparsity and language gap.
* **Detailed Optimization Algorithm:**  The paper presents a projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence analysis for solving the matrix completion problem, demonstrating a strong theoretical understanding and providing practical implementation details.
* **Impact of Parallel Data Size:** The exploration of the effect of varying the size of the parallel corpus (Figure 1 & 2) is valuable, showing the robustness of the method even with limited parallel data.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical formulation is clear, and the explanation of the algorithm is easy to follow.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Matrix Completion Choices:** While the optimization details are good, the paper doesn't deeply discuss the *choice* of using a trace norm regularizer for the matrix completion.  Other options exist, and a justification for this specific choice (e.g., theoretical properties, empirical performance comparison) would be beneficial.
* **Sensitivity Analysis:**  While the effect of parallel data size is shown, a sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters (γ, ρ, k) would strengthen the paper. Understanding how performance changes with different parameter settings would provide more practical guidance for users.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper mentions the need for a scalable optimization algorithm, but doesn't provide any discussion of the computational cost of the proposed method compared to the baselines, especially concerning large-scale datasets. A brief analysis or empirical comparison of running times would be helpful.
* **Limited Dataset Diversity:**  The experiments are confined to the Amazon review dataset. Evaluating the method on other datasets (e.g., news articles, scientific papers) with different characteristics would increase confidence in its generalizability.
* **Missing Negative Results:**  It would be helpful to briefly mention scenarios where this method *might* struggle. For example, does the performance degrade significantly with very dissimilar languages (e.g., English and Chinese)?



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Strong empirical results demonstrating significant performance gains.
* Novel and well-motivated combination of established techniques.
* Detailed theoretical analysis and algorithmic presentation.
* Thorough experimental evaluation, including analysis of parallel data size.
* Clear and well-written presentation.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited discussion of matrix completion choices and hyperparameters.
* Lack of computational complexity analysis.
* Dataset diversity is limited to a single domain.




**5. Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper to NIPS.  The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and delivers compelling empirical results. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly by adding a brief discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity and computational complexity – would further strengthen the paper. While the core ideas aren’t paradigm-shifting, the thoughtful combination and robust validation make this a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning.  I believe it addresses a significant problem and offers a promising solution that others will likely build upon.
