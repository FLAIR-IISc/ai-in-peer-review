Summary
The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual document representation learning that leverages a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 constructs a unified document–term matrix over the concatenated vocabularies of two languages, where unpaired documents are partially observed. It then performs convex matrix completion with a trace-norm (low-rank) penalty, an L1 penalty (which reduces to a linear term under nonnegativity), squared loss on observed entries, and an additional squared penalty on missing entries weighted by ρ, with a nonnegativity constraint. The optimization is tackled by an iterative projected gradient descent scheme that alternates a gradient step on the smooth part, singular value thresholding (SVT) for the nuclear norm, and projection onto the nonnegative orthant. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional shared representation, on which a linear SVM is trained. Experiments on Amazon reviews across 18 English–French/German/Japanese sentiment classification tasks show consistent improvements over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, and robustness when varying the amount of parallel data.

Relation to prior work
The method is conceptually close to prior cross-lingual representation learning using parallel data (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) but differs by first imputing the missing entries via convex matrix completion before dimensionality reduction. It also relates to SCL-type approaches that induce shared features via bilingual pivots (Prettenhofer & Stein), although it does not require bilingual lexicons. The optimization leverages standard nuclear-norm minimization ideas (Candès & Recht; Ma et al.).

Strengths
- Clear problem setting and simple pipeline that can reuse monolingual classifiers.
- Sensible use of unlabeled parallel data to couple vocabularies while incorporating all monolingual documents via matrix completion.
- Convex surrogate (nuclear norm + linear penalty) with a straightforward iterative algorithm, and a convergence discussion.
- Empirical results are consistent across 18 tasks and show robustness to the amount of parallel data; in many cases substantial gains over strong classical baselines (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA).
- The approach appears data-efficient with as few as 200 parallel pairs.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Optimization correctness: Proximal gradient with a nonsmooth term that is the sum of a nuclear norm and an indicator of the nonnegative orthant requires the proximal of the sum. The algorithm applies SVT followed by projection. In general, prox of f+g is not equal to sequential prox_f ∘ prox_g (nor prox_g ∘ prox_f) unless special conditions hold (e.g., separability). The provided convergence argument shows non-expansiveness of the composed operator but does not establish that fixed points minimize the original objective with both nonsmooth terms present. A more principled treatment (e.g., ADMM, Dykstra’s algorithm for sum of proximals, or a single proximal mapping for nuclear norm with nonnegativity constraints) is needed, or a proof that the proposed composition is indeed the correct prox (which is unlikely in general).
- Modeling assumption: The claim that a fully observed document–term matrix is “naturally low-rank” is debatable; such matrices are typically high rank but approximable by low-rank structure. The paper would benefit from empirical spectrum plots or a discussion of approximate low-rankness and the impact of rank selection (k) on performance.
- Regularization of missing entries: Penalizing missing entries toward zero with ρ plays a critical role but is under-analyzed. This can bias imputations toward sparsity and may conflict with cross-lingual coupling. Ablations varying ρ and removing this term would clarify its necessity and effect.
- Lack of ablations: The method has two steps; results comparing (i) CL-LSI on the raw concatenated matrix, (ii) classification directly on the completed matrix without LSI, and (iii) different k would help attribute gains.
- Scalability and practicality: Each iteration requires SVD (even if truncated). There is no discussion of runtime, memory footprint, number of iterations, or comparison of training time vs. baselines. This is important for large vocabularies and corpora.
- Baselines: The set excludes strong translation-based baselines (e.g., MT to project source to target or vice versa) and SCL with bilingual pivots (though cited). Depending on the time frame, bilingual word embedding approaches (e.g., CCA-based embeddings, or bilingual skip-gram) would also be relevant. Including a straightforward MT baseline would strengthen claims.
- Hyperparameter selection: Tuning γ, ρ, and k using labeled target data from one task (EFB) and then reusing them across tasks risks bias; cross-validation per task (or unsupervised criteria) would be preferable.
- Generality: Only Amazon reviews are considered; no results on other cross-lingual tasks (topic classification, news categorization) or non-English pairs. This limits claims of broad applicability.
- Reproducibility: No implementation details (truncated SVD method, stopping criteria, initialization beyond “rank-1 approximation”, number of iterations), no code, and no statistical significance testing.

Quality
The empirical study is thorough within one dataset and shows clear gains. However, the optimization section is the main technical weakness: the convergence proof does not convincingly establish optimality for the posed problem due to handling of the composite nonsmooth term. Lack of runtime/complexity analysis is also a concern.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The objective and algorithm are described clearly. Proof sketches are readable but, as noted, incomplete for the composite nonsmooth case. Figures/tables are referenced but not shown here; ensure they report standard deviations and practical settings. Some minor typos and notation issues exist (e.g., missing exponents in c(x,y)=1/2||x−y||^2).

Originality
Using convex matrix completion to impute cross-lingual document-term matrices before LSI is a novel combination relative to prior CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/OPCA pipelines. The idea of leveraging partial parallelism via completion is interesting. The optimization strategy itself is standard, but its application here is new.

Significance
If validated with correct optimization and stronger baselines, the approach could be a useful addition to cross-lingual representation learning, particularly when only limited parallel data exist. As it stands, the methodological flaw and limited baselines reduce confidence in the generality of the reported gains.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a correct optimization scheme (e.g., ADMM) or a proof that SVT followed by projection is the proximal operator for the sum of nuclear norm and nonnegativity indicator?
- What are runtimes, number of iterations, and memory usage for typical settings (t,d)? Which truncated SVD method is used?
- How sensitive is performance to ρ, γ, and k? Please add ablations, including removing the c(Mij,0) term.
- How does the method compare to (i) machine-translation baselines and (ii) SCL with bilingual pivots?
- Do you have results on another dataset or language pair not involving English?

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, intuitive method with consistent empirical gains on many tasks.
- Novel use of matrix completion to exploit limited parallel data and large monolingual corpora.
- Robustness to small amounts of parallel data.

Cons for acceptance
- Optimization method/proof appears theoretically unsound for the stated objective.
- Missing strong baselines and ablations; limited task/domain coverage.
- No scalability analysis; practicality unclear.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The core idea is promising and empirically encouraging, but the optimization correctness and experimental breadth need strengthening. Addressing the proximal composition issue (or switching to ADMM), adding ablations and stronger baselines, and providing runtime analysis would significantly improve the paper.
