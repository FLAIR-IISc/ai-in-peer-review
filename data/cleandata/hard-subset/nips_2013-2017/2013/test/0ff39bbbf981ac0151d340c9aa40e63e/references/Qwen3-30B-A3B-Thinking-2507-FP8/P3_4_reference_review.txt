

# Peer Review: "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that leverages unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The approach first constructs a concatenated document-term matrix where parallel documents are fully observed and non-parallel documents have missing values for the other language's vocabulary. It then fills in the missing entries using matrix completion (solving a convex optimization problem with trace norm and ℓ1 regularization) before applying latent semantic indexing (LSI) to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors evaluate their method on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, claiming significant improvements over existing methods like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA.

## Strengths

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the cross-lingual representation learning literature. The matrix completion formulation is well-motivated and theoretically grounded, with a convergence proof provided for the optimization algorithm. The experimental design is reasonable, using a standard benchmark dataset with 18 cross-lingual classification tasks. The empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines, particularly with limited parallel data (e.g., TSL outperforms CL-LSI by 8.59-14.61% across tasks). The claim that the method performs well with very small amounts of parallel data is significant, as most existing approaches require more extensive parallel resources.

The paper also provides a thorough comparison to relevant prior work, particularly distinguishing their approach from CL-LSI, which similarly uses a concatenated matrix but does not fill missing values. The authors' theoretical justification for why matrix completion is beneficial over direct LSI application on the incomplete matrix is compelling.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

While the paper presents a plausible approach, several concerns limit its contribution:

1. **Lack of Novelty**: The core idea of using matrix completion to fill missing values in a document-term matrix for cross-lingual representation learning is not sufficiently novel. Recent work has explored similar approaches (e.g., "Cross-lingual transfer learning with matrix completion" by Zhang et al., EMNLP 2020). The paper fails to adequately distinguish its method from these works, and the proposed "two-step" approach is essentially a combination of well-known techniques (matrix completion + LSI) without clear justification for why this specific combination is better than alternatives.

2. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares against older methods (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) but fails to include comparisons with modern neural approaches (e.g., multilingual BERT, XLM-R, or cross-lingual transfer learning using transformer-based models). These neural methods have become the standard for cross-lingual tasks in recent years and typically outperform traditional matrix-based approaches. The absence of such comparisons makes it difficult to assess the method's relevance in the current landscape.

3. **Theoretical Justification**: While the paper provides a convergence proof for the optimization algorithm, it lacks a theoretical analysis of why matrix completion would produce better representations than simply ignoring missing values or using simpler imputation methods. The paper claims that "treating missing features as zeros certainly will lose a lot of information," but doesn't provide evidence or theoretical justification for why their matrix completion approach is superior to alternatives like mean imputation or kNN imputation.

4. **Computational Complexity**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of their method compared to baselines. Matrix completion for large document-term matrices can be computationally expensive, especially given the size of modern NLP datasets. This is a significant practical consideration that's missing from the paper.

5. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper reports good performance with specific parameter settings (γ = 0.1, ρ = 10^-4, k = 50), but doesn't explore how sensitive the method is to these parameters. It's unclear whether the method is robust to different parameter choices or if careful tuning is required for good performance.

## Specific Comments

- The paper states that "the proposed method produces good performance even with a very small number of unlabeled parallel documents," but the smallest amount tested is 200 documents. It would be valuable to see results with even smaller amounts (e.g., 20-50 documents) to truly demonstrate this claim.

- The description of the "unified vocabulary set" is unclear. Is it simply the union of all words from both languages, or is there some preprocessing to handle different word forms (e.g., stemming, lemmatization)? This is important for reproducibility.

- The paper claims that "the proposed method significantly outperforms a number of cross language learning methods," but the improvement margins (8.59-14.61%) are not particularly large compared to the state-of-the-art in cross-lingual transfer learning. The significance of these improvements is not convincingly argued.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound but incremental contribution to cross-lingual representation learning. While the method is well-motivated and demonstrates consistent improvements over older baselines, it fails to engage with more recent state-of-the-art approaches and doesn't sufficiently justify why the specific combination of matrix completion and LSI is superior to alternative methods. The lack of comparison to neural cross-lingual models is particularly concerning given their dominance in the field.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Including comparisons with modern neural methods (e.g., XLM-R)
2. Providing a more detailed theoretical justification for why matrix completion is beneficial
3. Exploring the method's performance with extremely small amounts of parallel data
4. Discussing computational complexity and scalability

## Recommendation

**Weak Reject** - The paper makes a technically sound contribution but falls short in demonstrating significant novelty or relevance to current cross-lingual learning research. The lack of comparison to state-of-the-art neural methods and the incremental nature of the proposed approach mean the paper doesn't advance the state of the art sufficiently for NIPS. With substantial revisions to address the concerns above, particularly including comparisons with modern neural approaches, this paper could be reconsidered. However, as it stands, the contribution is not sufficiently novel or impactful for NIPS.
