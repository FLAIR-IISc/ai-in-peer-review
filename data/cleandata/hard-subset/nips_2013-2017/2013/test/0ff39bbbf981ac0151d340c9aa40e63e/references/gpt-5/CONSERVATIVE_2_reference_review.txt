Summary
The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual text classification. Step 1: build a unified bilingual document-term matrix where non-parallel documents have missing entries; recover missing entries by solving a convex matrix completion problem with a nuclear norm, ℓ1 penalty, and nonnegativity, optimized via a projected gradient descent procedure with singular value shrinkage. Step 2: apply LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional shared representation, then train standard monolingual classifiers. Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews (18 transfer tasks) report improvements over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, and some robustness to small numbers of parallel documents. A convergence argument is provided based on non-expansive operators.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on well-known ingredients: nuclear norm matrix completion (Candes & Recht), soft-thresholding/shrinkage (Ma et al.), CL-LSI (Littman et al.), CL-KCCA/OPCA, SCL (Prettenhofer & Stein) and multilingual topic models. The novelty is primarily the sequencing: impute missing bilingual features, then LSI. The setup resembles prior “dual-language matrix + SVD” pipelines but with an added imputation step instead of zero-padding or translation.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant problem and uses only small parallel corpora.
- Conceptually simple, leveraging established convex formulations.
- Empirically improves over several classical cross-lingual representation baselines on Amazon reviews, with some analysis of varying parallel data size.
- Implementation details are mostly clear; the objective is convex (after relaxation).

Weaknesses
- Limited and somewhat dated baselines. Missing several strong, established baselines:
  - Translate-train/translate-test with MT (even public MT suffices for an informative baseline on Amazon reviews).
  - SCL (cited) is a canonical baseline for this dataset but not reported.
  - Cross-lingual word embedding methods (bilingual/multilingual embeddings) and multilingual pretrained encoders (mBERT/XLM-R) are standard by now; even a bag-of-embeddings or CLS-based SVM baseline would contextualize gains. As-is, it is hard to claim state-of-the-art significance.
- Potentially unfair use of unlabeled data: representation learning uses all documents from both languages, seemingly including target test documents (transductive). This should be either avoided or clearly separated; many baselines are sensitive to transductive vs inductive splits.
- Ablations are lacking. It is essential to show:
  - TSL without step 1 (LSI on the original concatenated matrix with zeros)—to isolate the benefit of completion over CL-LSI in the exact same construction.
  - TSL without step 2 (classification directly on the completed matrix).
  - Effect of each regularization (nuclear norm, ℓ1, nonnegativity, and the ρ penalty on missing entries).
  - Sensitivity to k, γ, ρ, µ beyond one task; currently, parameters are tuned on EFB and reused, which could under-optimize baselines and obscures per-task performance.
- Convergence/optimization concerns:
  - The algorithm applies shrinkage (prox of nuclear norm) followed by projection to the nonnegative orthant. This is not, in general, the proximal operator of the sum (nuclear norm + indicator of nonnegativity). The fixed-point argument via non-expansive mappings is not sufficient to guarantee convergence to the solution of the original composite objective; a rigorous proof requires conditions for the composition/prox-splitting used (or casting the method as a valid proximal algorithm, e.g., Dykstra’s, ADMM, or forward–backward with proper prox). As written, Theorem 1 is not fully convincing.
  - No complexity/runtime analysis. Each iteration requires an SVD; scalability to realistic vocabulary sizes is unclear. Are truncated or randomized SVDs used? What ranks are observed in practice?
- Experimental reporting gaps:
  - No significance testing; only averages and stds. Report p-values or CIs.
  - No error analysis or diagnostic measures (e.g., alignment quality of parallel pairs in the latent space, nearest-neighbor retrieval across languages).
  - Dataset preprocessing lacks detail (vocabulary pruning, stopwords, normalization), which affects reproducibility and completion behavior.
- Conceptual assumptions: The low-rank assumption for the full bilingual document-term matrix is plausible but unexamined. Empirical rank estimates or reconstruction errors would help justify the modeling choice and explain why completion helps cross-lingual alignment rather than hallucinating co-occurrences.

Quality
- The objective is reasonable, but the optimization analysis is incomplete and there are questions about whether the algorithm actually solves the stated problem. Empirical methodology lacks strong baselines, ablations, scalability measurements, and careful train/test separation for representation learning.

Clarity
- Generally clear and readable. Some details are missing (preprocessing, SVD/truncation, runtime). Theoretical section should be revised to provide a correct convergence proof or a more standard proximal framework.

Originality
- The combination of matrix completion with subsequent LSI is incremental. Prior methods already operate on concatenated bilingual matrices; the imputation step is a natural extension. Clear differentiation from CL-LSI on zero-padded matrices and from joint low-rank factorization approaches is needed.

Significance
- Potentially useful in low-parallel-data settings. However, without comparisons to strong, modern baselines and without rigorous optimization guarantees, the claims of broad significance are premature.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add strong baselines: translate-train/test; SCL; bilingual/multilingual word embeddings; mBERT/XLM-R.
- Provide ablations isolating each component and a fair variant of CL-LSI on the exact same input.
- Avoid transductive use of test data in representation learning, or clearly report both inductive and transductive results.
- Provide runtime and scalability analysis; use truncated/randomized SVD; report practical ranks.
- Fix the optimization analysis: use a valid proximal-splitting method (e.g., proximal gradient with the true prox of nuclear norm + nonnegativity via Dykstra/ADMM) and provide a correct convergence proof.
- Release code and detailed preprocessing to ensure reproducibility.

Recommendation
- Reject. While the idea is reasonable and results are promising against older baselines, the methodological novelty is limited, the theoretical analysis is not fully sound, and the experimental evaluation omits key baselines and ablations needed to support strong claims.

Confidence
- High.
