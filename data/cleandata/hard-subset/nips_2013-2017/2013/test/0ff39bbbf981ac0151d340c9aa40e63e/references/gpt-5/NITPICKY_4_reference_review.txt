Summary
The paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-lingual text classification using a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 performs matrix completion on a unified document-term matrix (concatenated vocabularies) where non-parallel rows have many missing entries. The completion solves a convex surrogate minimizing a weighted squared loss on observed entries plus a penalty on missing entries toward zero, with nuclear norm and ℓ1 regularization under nonnegativity. A projected gradient descent with singular-value shrinkage and nonnegativity projection is used. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the recovered matrix to obtain a low-dimensional shared representation, on which a linear SVM is trained. Experiments on Amazon reviews (EN, FR, DE, JA; three domains) show gains over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, and robustness with few parallel pairs.

Strengths
- Clear high-level idea: exploit structured missingness in a bilingual document-term matrix, then induce shared semantics via LSI.
- Uses relatively few parallel pairs and reports robustness to small np.
- Attempts a convergence argument for the iterative solver (non-expansive mapping perspective).
- Empirical comparisons against several classical cross-lingual representation methods on a standard dataset.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Methodology/optimization
1) Incorrect handling of composite non-smooth terms. Problem (2) has two non-smooth terms (nuclear norm and indicator for nonnegativity), and a term µ||M||1 which is non-differentiable at zeros. The algorithm applies (a) a gradient step on g(M) including µ||M||1 (treating it as smooth) with gradient µE, (b) nuclear-norm shrinkage, and (c) projection onto nonnegativity. In general, prox for f1+f2 is not equal to prox_f2(prox_f1(·)). The cited convergence (via non-expansiveness) shows the iteration is a contraction under certain τ, but does not establish that the fixed point solves (2). The citation [11] (SVT) handles one non-smooth term; here there are two. A correct approach would use a principled splitting method (e.g., ADMM, Dykstra’s algorithm, three-operator splitting) and handle the ℓ1 term either via soft-thresholding or include it in the proximal composite. As written, the algorithm/proof gap is substantial.

2) Treating ℓ1 as “smooth linear” under nonnegativity is not correct: ||M||1 is non-differentiable at zero; the gradient µE used in (4) is not a valid subgradient choice at zeros after projection. This undermines both convergence claims and optimality.

3) Structured missingness is not addressed. Matrix completion guarantees typically require incoherence and (approximately) uniform sampling. Here, the missingness is highly structured (entire language-blocks per non-parallel document). No theoretical discussion of identifiability or error bounds under this pattern is provided. The added penalty ρ∑_{Ω̂} c(Mij,0) biases missing entries toward zero; this is a strong prior that conflicts with low-rank completion and may drive trivial solutions for small ρ/τ. No ablation on ρ is provided beyond a coarse grid.

4) Objective design vs. semantics. Nuclear norm plus ℓ2 loss generally yields dense solutions; adding ℓ1 to enforce sparsity conflicts with the low-rank bias, and the post-LSI step further compresses. The rationale for combining these three biases (nonnegativity, sparsity, low-rank, then LSI) is not analyzed. Why not learn the low-dimensional shared space directly (e.g., via nonnegative matrix tri-factorization, joint factorization, OPCA-like constraints, or CCA variants)?

5) Step-size condition and Lipschitz constant. The step-size τ<min(2,2/ρ) is derived elementwise; rigorously, the Lipschitz constant of ∇g is L = max(1,ρ), which yields τ<2/L. That suffices, but the proof ignores the non-smooth ℓ1 and indicator terms and again does not connect fixed points to optimality of (2).

Reproducibility and clarity
6) Missing critical experimental details: no runtime/complexity discussion; no SVD solver details (full vs. partial, randomized SVD), no stopping criteria or iteration counts; vocabulary sizes and matrix dimensions are omitted; pre-processing (tokenization, lowercasing, stopwords, IDF computation across languages) not specified; normalization across languages not discussed (TF-IDF scales can differ by language). Without these, reproducibility is compromised.

7) Baselines are dated and incomplete. Absent are strong modern baselines: cross-lingual word embeddings (e.g., Artetxe et al., Conneau et al. MUSE), bilingual autoencoders, multilingual sentence encoders (LASER), and multilingual transformers (mBERT/XLM-R) with translate-train/translate-test. These are standard for cross-lingual text classification and particularly relevant when only 100 target labels are available. The experimental claim of state-of-the-art is not convincing without them.

8) Fairness/validation. SVM uses libsvm defaults; no hyperparameter tuning on a dev set is reported. CL-KCCA kernel choice and hyperparameters are unspecified. Using the same k across tasks without validation may disadvantage some baselines. Also, it is unclear whether representation learning uses all target documents including test; if so, that is unsupervised and acceptable but should be clearly stated for all methods.

9) Reporting. Table 1 and Figures 1–2 are not included; numerical results cannot be verified. No statistical significance tests are reported.

10) Writing/formatting issues: numerous typos and notation problems: c(x,y)=12‖x−y‖ should be 1/2‖x−y‖^2; I(·) is used for identity but not defined; min(2, 2 ρ ) formatting; repeated “if only if”; inconsistent reference formatting (missing years/venues), e.g., [10] CL-LSI attribution is more typically to Dumais/Landauer/Littman; several conference acronyms are incorrect. These issues hinder clarity.

Empirical scope
11) Single dataset (Amazon) and three domains; no evaluation on other benchmarks (e.g., Reuters RCV2 multilingual, MLDoc, Europarl/TED) to test generality. No ablations (e.g., without ℓ1, without nonnegativity, without the LSI step) to justify design choices.

Relation to prior work
12) The paper positions itself relative to CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA and SCL, but omits a large body of cross-lingual representation learning (bilingual embeddings, joint mapping methods, multilingual pretraining). Even for matrix completion with side constraints, related literature on nonnegative matrix completion and sparse+low-rank modeling is not discussed.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple idea that can leverage small parallel corpora.
- Some empirical gains over classical baselines.
- Attempted theoretical analysis.

Cons for acceptance
- Major methodological flaws in optimization and convergence claims.
- Incomplete and outdated empirical evaluation; missing strong baselines.
- Missing or unclear experimental details and absent figures/tables.
- Numerous clarity/formatting/reference issues.

Questions for authors
- Provide a correct optimization algorithm (e.g., ADMM) and a proof that the iteration converges to the solution of (2) with both nuclear norm and nonnegativity (and properly treating ℓ1).
- Clarify pre-processing and normalization across languages; how is IDF computed?
- Report runtime, iterations, and SVD details; include ablations on µ, ρ, and the necessity of the LSI step.
- Add modern baselines (mBERT/XLM-R translate-train/test; CLWE+SVM).
- Include all missing tables/figures and conduct significance testing.

Recommendation
Reject. Despite a promising high-level idea, the optimization is not sound as presented, empirical comparisons are incomplete by current standards, and the paper has many clarity and formatting issues. With a correct solver, stronger baselines, and clearer reporting, the work could become competitive. Quality: 4/10, Clarity: 5/10, Originality: 5/10, Significance: 4/10. Confidence: high.
