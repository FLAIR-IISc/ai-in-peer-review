

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification by first using matrix completion to fill missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix, then applying LSI to produce low-dimensional representations. While the problem of cross-lingual representation learning is well-motivated, the paper contains several critical technical flaws and inconsistencies that undermine its contribution.

**Technical Flaws**

The most severe issue lies in the convergence analysis (Lemma 1). The paper claims "0 < τ < min(2, 2 ρ )" for convergence, but ρ is a regularization parameter that can be as small as 10^-5. This would make 2/ρ extremely large (200,000), rendering the upper bound for τ essentially 2. However, the paper's statement implies τ < 2ρ, which for small ρ would be an extremely tight constraint (e.g., τ < 2×10^-5 for ρ=10^-5). This is a critical mathematical error that invalidates the convergence proof. The authors should have used "0 < τ < min(2, 2/ρ)" instead.

In the optimization formulation (equation 2), the regularization term for missing features is inconsistent. The paper states "ρ∑(i,j)∈Ω̂ c(Mij, 0)" but then in equation (4), the gradient includes "ρM ◦ Ŷ" which is inconsistent with the regularization term. The authors should clarify the relationship between these two expressions.

The paper incorrectly states that "with nonnegativity constraints M ≥ 0, the non-smooth ℓ1 norm regularizer... is equivalent to a smooth linear function ‖M‖1 = ∑ij Mij." This is false; the ℓ1 norm is non-smooth regardless of nonnegativity constraints. The authors are confusing the ℓ1 norm with the sum of absolute values, which for nonnegative variables does simplify to the sum.

**Formatting and Terminology Inconsistencies**

The paper uses inconsistent notation: "Ω̂" should be "Ω̂" (with a proper hat symbol) or "Ω^c" for the complement set. In the introduction, "a pair of parallel documents are represented" should be "is represented" (subject-verb agreement).

The paper refers to "unlabeled parallel documents" but parallel documents inherently contain content from both languages. The authors should clarify whether these are unlabeled in the sense of lacking sentiment labels, or if they're unlabeled in some other sense.

The paper uses "low-rank" and "low-dimensional" interchangeably, which are distinct concepts (rank vs. dimensionality). This confusion appears throughout the paper.

**Methodological Concerns**

The paper claims "the proposed method produces good performance even with a very small number of unlabeled parallel bilingual documents," but the experiments only test with 200-2000 parallel documents. The paper fails to demonstrate performance with extremely small numbers (e.g., 10 or 50), which would better support this claim.

The comparison with CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA is inconsistent. The paper reports CL-KCCA used k=100 while TSL used k=50, but doesn't explain why different dimensionality was chosen for different methods. This inconsistency could bias the comparison.

**Experimental Weaknesses**

The paper uses only 100 labeled target documents for training, which is extremely low for a classifier. The authors should provide additional experiments with more labeled data to establish the robustness of their approach across different data regimes.

The paper doesn't report computational complexity or runtime comparisons with other methods, which is essential for an algorithm involving matrix completion on large matrices.

**Related Work**

The paper cites [16] as "Cross-language structural correspondence learning" but [16] is actually "Cross-language text classification using structural correspondence learning" by Prettenhofer and Stein, which is different from the method described. This indicates a lack of careful citation.

**Strengths**

The paper does present a novel two-step approach that differs from previous methods by first completing the matrix before performing LSI. The experimental results show consistent improvements over baselines across 18 tasks, which is a positive finding.

**Conclusion**

While the paper addresses an important problem and shows promising empirical results, the technical flaws in the convergence analysis and optimization formulation are severe enough to undermine the paper's credibility. The mathematical errors in Lemma 1 would make the algorithm theoretically unsound for small ρ values, which is a critical flaw. The inconsistent notation, terminology, and experimental design further weaken the contribution.

The paper's main contribution appears to be a novel application of matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning, but the technical execution contains multiple fundamental errors. A paper with such critical mathematical errors should not be accepted at NIPS, regardless of the empirical results.

**Recommendation: Reject**
