Summary
The paper proposes a two-step method (TSL) for cross-language text classification. Given a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix where parallel pairs are fully observed and non-parallel documents are partially observed, the authors first perform matrix completion to impute the missing entries by solving a convex optimization problem with a trace-norm (low-rank) penalty, an ℓ1 penalty (argued to promote sparsity under nonnegativity), squared loss on observed entries, a small regularization on missing entries, and nonnegativity constraints. They solve it with an iterative scheme combining a gradient step for the smooth part, singular value shrinkage for the nuclear norm, and projection onto the nonnegative orthant. They then apply LSI (SVD) to the recovered matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation on which a linear SVM is trained. On Amazon multilingual sentiment classification, TSL outperforms CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA, and a target-only baseline, and remains strong with few parallel documents.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation leveraging a realistic resource setting: a small number of unlabeled parallel pairs plus many non-parallel documents.
- Principled convex objective combining low rank, entrywise regularization, and nonnegativity; simple algorithmic pipeline (prox-like iterations + LSI).
- Extensive empirical evaluation across 18 cross-lingual tasks with ablation on the amount of parallel data; consistent gains over strong classical baselines.
- Hyperparameter selection done on one task and reused, limiting overfitting across tasks.
- Empirical robustness in low-parallel regimes is practically valuable.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Optimization correctness: The algorithm alternates (i) a gradient step for g, (ii) nuclear-norm shrinkage, and (iii) projection to nonnegativity. However, the problem has two nonsmooth terms (nuclear norm and indicator of the nonnegative cone). The proposed update is not the proximal operator of the sum of these nonsmooth terms; composition of prox operators generally does not yield the prox of the sum. The convergence argument relies on non-expansiveness of each operator and cites [11, Thm 4], but that result applies to a different setting (single nonsmooth term with shrinkage). As stated, Theorem 1 does not clearly establish convergence to the minimizer of (2). Please either (a) provide a proof using a suitable three-operator splitting (e.g., Condat–Vu/Davis–Yin) or Dykstra’s method, or (b) absorb the nonnegativity into the prox (prox of nuclear norm with nonnegativity), or (c) remove/justify the projection step theoretically.
- Role of the ℓ1 penalty: Under M ≥ 0, ‖M‖1 = sum Mij is linear and does not induce sparsity patterns in the usual sense; it uniformly shrinks entries. The claim of enforcing sparsity is misleading without further structure. An ablation showing the effect of µ (including µ=0) would clarify whether the ℓ1 term is needed.
- Loss on missing entries: Penalizing missing entries toward zero (ρ∑_(i,j)∈Ω̂ c(Mij,0)) deviates from standard matrix completion and biases the solution toward smaller values across the unobserved block. Some theoretical or empirical justification for this choice (e.g., calibration of ρ, sensitivity curves) is needed, especially because this term and the ℓ1 term act similarly under nonnegativity.
- Two-step redundancy: The completion already encourages low rank via the nuclear norm. Performing a second SVD (LSI) may be redundant; equivalently, the top-k singular vectors of M* could be used directly. An analysis/ablation comparing (i) direct use of top-k factors from the completion step vs. (ii) the two-step approach would strengthen the case for the design.
- Transductive use of target test data: For representation learning, the method uses “all documents from the two languages,” which includes target test documents. While the competing representation learners also do so, the TBOW baseline does not, making some of the improvements versus TBOW conflate representation quality with access to additional unlabeled test data. Please provide an inductive setting (fit representations on training-only unlabeled data) to quantify this effect.
- Missing baselines: Given the dataset and era of related work, Structural Correspondence Learning (SCL) [16] is a highly relevant baseline and should be included. A translate-train or translate-test baseline (MT or dictionary-based) would also contextualize gains, as many prior cross-lingual classification papers report such comparisons.
- Scalability and practicality: Each iteration requires an SVD (or at least truncated SVD) of a very large, sparse matrix. There is no complexity analysis, no mention of truncated/partial SVD, number of iterations, stopping criterion, runtimes, or memory footprint. Without this, it is hard to assess applicability beyond the Amazon dataset.
- Details missing for reproducibility: How are TF-IDF statistics computed across languages (per-language vs. joint IDF)? Document length normalization? Tokenization for non-Latin scripts? Stopping criterion and step-size tuning? These details materially affect results.

Quality
Technically promising but the optimization analysis is incomplete/misapplied given two nonsmooth terms. Empirical evaluation is broad and indicates real gains, but missing key baselines and ablations limit conclusiveness. Scalability and reproducibility details are insufficient.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The optimization section is easy to follow, with helpful lemmas, but the central convergence claim needs clarification or correction. Some experimental details are underspecified.

Originality
The idea of completing a bilingual concatenated document-term matrix before cross-lingual LSI is a novel and interesting twist on established CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA lines. However, it is an incremental combination of known components (nuclear norm completion + LSI) rather than a fundamentally new paradigm.

Significance
If the empirical gains hold under more rigorous baselines and inductive settings, the approach could be useful in low-parallel-data regimes. As presented, the impact is promising but not yet definitive due to the concerns above.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Provide a correct convergence proof or switch to a standard three-operator splitting method handling nuclear norm and nonnegativity jointly.
- Add SCL and a translate-train/test baseline; optionally include a bilingual embedding baseline.
- Report runtimes, iterations, truncated SVD ranks, and memory usage; discuss scalability to larger vocabularies.
- Run an inductive setup where representation learning excludes target test documents.
- Ablate µ (ℓ1), ρ (missing-entry loss), and the LSI second step; report sensitivity curves.
- Clarify TF-IDF computation across languages and preprocessing/tokenization details.
- Discuss when and why cross-lingual correspondences emerge under this completion model (e.g., via analysis of cross-language term correlations in M*).

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a simple, effective idea.
- Strong, consistent empirical results including low-parallel regimes.
- Convex formulation with clear algorithmic steps.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical convergence claim appears incorrect/incomplete for the stated problem.
- Missing key baselines and ablations; transductive setup clouds fair comparison to TBOW.
- No scalability/runtime evidence.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. With a corrected optimization analysis and stronger experimental comparisons/ablations, this work could become a solid contribution.
