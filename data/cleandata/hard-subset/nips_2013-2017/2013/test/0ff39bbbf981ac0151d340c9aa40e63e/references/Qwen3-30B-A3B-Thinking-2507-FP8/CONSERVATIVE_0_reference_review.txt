

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-language text classification that first uses matrix completion to fill missing entries in a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix and then applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors claim their method outperforms existing approaches like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA on Amazon product review datasets.

## Strengths

The paper presents a technically sound formulation of the matrix completion problem with a convex optimization approach and provides a convergence analysis for the proposed projected gradient descent algorithm. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering 18 cross-lingual classification tasks across three languages. The results demonstrate consistent improvements over the baseline TBOW method and the three comparison methods across most tasks.

## Weaknesses

While the technical approach is sound, the paper suffers from several critical weaknesses that undermine its contribution and relevance for NeurIPS:

1. **Lack of meaningful comparison to current state-of-the-art**: The paper compares against methods proposed in 2010-2015 (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA), but fails to compare with modern approaches that have significantly advanced cross-lingual representation learning. Recent work such as mBERT, XLM-R, and other transformer-based models have established new baselines for cross-lingual tasks. The absence of such comparisons makes the claimed "significant outperformance" appear misleading, as the paper is only beating older methods rather than the current state of the art.

2. **Insufficient theoretical justification**: The paper claims matrix completion is necessary to "automatically capture important and robust low-rank information," but fails to provide a clear theoretical argument for why this is superior to directly applying LSI on the concatenated matrix (as done in CL-LSI). The paper acknowledges that CL-LSI uses a similar concatenated matrix approach but doesn't adequately explain why their two-step method is fundamentally better.

3. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper reports average accuracy improvements but doesn't provide statistical significance tests for the performance differences. This is particularly concerning given the relatively small dataset (only 2000 unlabeled parallel documents) and the fact that the improvements are claimed to be "significant."

4. **Limited scalability discussion**: The matrix completion approach is computationally intensive for large-scale problems. The paper doesn't address how this method would scale to modern datasets with millions of documents or more languages, which is a critical limitation for practical applications.

5. **Misleading claims about small data requirements**: While the paper claims their method works "even with a very small number of unlabeled parallel documents," the experimental setup uses 2000 unlabeled parallel documents (which is actually quite substantial for this task). The paper doesn't provide results with truly minimal data requirements (e.g., 100 or 200 documents), making this claim unsubstantiated.

## Relation to Previous Work

The paper correctly identifies the connection to CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA but fails to properly position itself within the broader context of cross-lingual representation learning. The most relevant recent work, such as the XLM-R model (Conneau et al., 2020) which uses a multilingual transformer architecture, is completely absent from the comparison. This omission is particularly problematic for a NeurIPS submission, where demonstrating relevance to current research trends is essential.

## Recommendation

I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper in its current form. While the technical approach is sound, the paper fails to make a compelling case for its novelty and significance. The claimed advantages over existing methods are not properly demonstrated against the current state of the art, and the experimental evaluation lacks the rigor expected for NeurIPS. The paper presents a minor variation of existing techniques without sufficient justification for why this variation is meaningful in the context of modern cross-lingual learning.

The paper would need substantial revision to be considered for NeurIPS, including:
1. Comparison against recent transformer-based cross-lingual models
2. Statistical significance testing of the reported improvements
3. More thorough discussion of the theoretical advantages of the two-step approach
4. A clearer explanation of why this method would be preferable to existing approaches in practical settings

Without these revisions, the paper's contribution appears marginal and fails to advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. While the technical details are well-presented, the lack of meaningful comparison to current research makes it difficult to assess the paper's true value to the field. For NeurIPS, papers should demonstrate clear advancement over the current state of the art, which this paper does not do.
