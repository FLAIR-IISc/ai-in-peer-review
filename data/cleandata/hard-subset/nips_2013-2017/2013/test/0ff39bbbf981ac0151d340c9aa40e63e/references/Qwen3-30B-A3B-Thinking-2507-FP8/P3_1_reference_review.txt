

# Peer Review: "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that first fills missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix using matrix completion, followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors claim that their method significantly outperforms existing approaches (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA, and a target baseline) on 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews. The work builds upon prior cross-lingual learning methods that either translate documents or construct cross-lingual representations using bilingual resources, but introduces matrix completion as a preprocessing step to handle the missing values inherent in the concatenated document-term matrix.

## Strengths

1. **Clear and well-structured presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology, experiments, and conclusions. The mathematical formulation is precise and the algorithm is clearly described.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The method consistently outperforms baselines across 18 different cross-lingual tasks, with improvements of 8.59-14.61% over the target baseline. The results demonstrate the method's effectiveness even with very limited parallel data (as few as 200 documents).

3. **Convergence analysis**: The paper provides a rigorous convergence analysis for the projected gradient descent algorithm, which is a valuable technical contribution.

4. **Practical relevance**: The method's ability to work with a small number of unlabeled parallel documents is a significant practical advantage, as obtaining large amounts of parallel data is often challenging in cross-lingual settings.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

### 1. Insufficient Differentiation from Prior Work (Originality)

While the paper claims novelty, the main contribution (matrix completion followed by LSI) is not clearly distinguished from existing approaches. CL-LSI [10] already uses a concatenated document-term matrix and applies LSI directly. The authors should clarify why filling missing entries *before* LSI is fundamentally better than applying LSI to the incomplete matrix (which is essentially what CL-LSI does). 

The paper states: "different from these methods which apply operations such as LSI, KCCA, and OPCA directly on the original concatenated document-term matrix, our method first fills the missing entries of the document-term matrix using matrix completion, and then performs LSI over the recovered low-rank matrix." However, this distinction is not convincingly argued. The authors should provide evidence that directly applying LSI to the incomplete matrix (as in CL-LSI) performs worse than their two-step approach, rather than just showing their method outperforms CL-LSI.

**Suggestion**: Include an ablation study comparing the performance of LSI directly on the incomplete matrix versus LSI on the completed matrix, to demonstrate the value of the matrix completion step.

### 2. Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art Methods

The paper compares against CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, but these are relatively older methods (2010-2015). The field of cross-lingual representation learning has advanced significantly since then, with neural network approaches becoming dominant. The authors should compare against more recent methods, particularly those based on neural networks or transformers, such as:

- Multilingual BERT (mBERT) [1]
- XLM-R [2]
- LASER [3]
- MUSE [4]

These methods have become the new state-of-the-art for cross-lingual tasks and would provide a more meaningful benchmark. The paper's claim of "significantly outperforming" other methods is weakened by not comparing with these modern approaches.

**Suggestion**: Add a comparison with at least one recent neural network-based cross-lingual method to demonstrate the relative performance of the proposed approach.

### 3. Limited Experimental Scope

The evaluation is restricted to sentiment classification on Amazon product reviews. This is a narrow domain that may not generalize to other cross-lingual tasks. The paper should demonstrate the method's effectiveness on other tasks, such as text classification or named entity recognition, to show broader applicability.

Additionally, the paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the matrix completion step compared to the baselines. For large-scale applications, this could be a significant drawback.

### 4. Inadequate Explanation of Key Choices

The parameter selection process is not thoroughly justified. The authors state they used the EFB task to select parameters (γ = 0.1, ρ = 10⁻⁴, k = 50), but don't explain why EFB was chosen over other tasks. This is particularly concerning since they claim the method works well with small amounts of parallel data, yet they used a specific task for parameter selection that might not generalize.

**Suggestion**: Perform parameter selection across multiple tasks or use a more robust method like cross-validation to select parameters.

### 5. Lack of Theoretical Justification for the Matrix Completion Approach

While the paper provides a convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm, it doesn't provide theoretical justification for why matrix completion would be effective for this specific problem. The authors should explain why the document-term matrix is low-rank and how the missing data structure (only parallel documents provide connections between languages) makes matrix completion appropriate.

## Specific Comments on Methodology

1. **Matrix Completion Formulation**: The optimization problem (2) is well-formulated, but the choice of using ℓ₁ norm for sparsity and trace norm for low-rank is standard. The authors should discuss why they chose these specific regularizers over alternatives like ℓ₂ for the low-rank term.

2. **Non-negativity Constraint**: The paper mentions the non-negativity constraint is important for document-term matrices, but doesn't discuss how this constraint affects the optimization or the quality of the recovered matrix.

3. **Algorithm 1**: The projected gradient descent algorithm is correctly described, but the paper doesn't provide any analysis of how the step size τ affects convergence speed or solution quality.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Add comparison with recent neural methods**: Include at least one comparison with a state-of-the-art neural cross-lingual model to provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

2. **Provide ablation study**: Show the performance difference between applying LSI directly to the incomplete matrix versus the proposed two-step approach.

3. **Expand experimental scope**: Test the method on additional tasks beyond sentiment classification, such as text classification or named entity recognition.

4. **Justify parameter selection**: Explain the rationale for choosing EFB for parameter selection, and consider using a more robust parameter selection method.

5. **Include computational complexity analysis**: Compare the computational requirements of the proposed method with the baselines.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-executed method that demonstrates improved performance over existing cross-lingual learning approaches on a specific benchmark. The technical aspects are sound, and the empirical results are convincing. However, the paper's claim of novelty is undermined by insufficient differentiation from prior work, and the lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods weakens the significance of the contribution.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Better articulation of the novelty compared to existing approaches
2. Comparison with more recent neural network-based cross-lingual methods
3. A more comprehensive experimental evaluation

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** (with major revisions). The paper has merit and the results are promising, but the lack of comparison with modern methods and insufficient justification for the novelty prevent it from being a strong contribution to the field. With the suggested revisions, this paper could make a valuable contribution to cross-lingual representation learning.

## Final Thoughts

The paper addresses an important problem in cross-lingual learning, and the matrix completion approach is a reasonable extension of existing methods. However, in the current state, it represents an incremental improvement rather than a significant advancement. The field has moved beyond the approaches the paper compares against, and the authors should update their evaluation to reflect current state-of-the-art methods.
