

# Peer Review: "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-language text classification that leverages unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to learn cross-lingual representations. The core idea involves constructing a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix where parallel documents have complete entries, while non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language's vocabulary. The authors then formulate a matrix completion problem to fill the missing entries, followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The method is evaluated on 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews across English, French, German, and Japanese.

The paper positions itself as distinct from previous approaches like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, which directly apply LSI, KCCA, or OPCA to the concatenated document-term matrix. The authors argue that their matrix completion step captures important low-rank information from word co-occurrence patterns across languages, which is not fully leveraged by prior methods.

This work relates to NIPS papers such as "Cross-language structural correspondence learning" (Prettenhofer & Stein, 2009) and "Inferring a semantic representation of text via cross-language correlation analysis" (Vinokourov et al., 2005), which also address cross-lingual representation learning. However, this paper introduces a novel matrix completion step that distinguishes it from previous approaches.

## Strengths

### Quality
- The paper presents a technically sound approach with a well-formulated optimization problem for matrix completion. The convex relaxation of the rank minimization problem is appropriate and well-justified.
- The convergence analysis of the projected gradient descent algorithm is thorough and correctly proves the convergence properties.
- The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering 18 different cross-lingual classification tasks across multiple languages and product categories.
- The ablation study on the impact of the number of unlabeled parallel documents provides valuable insights into the method's data efficiency.

### Clarity
- The paper is well-organized with clear sections that logically progress from problem statement to methodology, optimization, and evaluation.
- The mathematical formulation is precise and well-explained, with the algorithm pseudocode making the implementation details accessible.
- The experimental setup is clearly described, including dataset details, baselines, and evaluation metrics.

### Originality
- The core idea of using matrix completion to fill missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix before applying LSI represents a novel approach to cross-lingual representation learning.
- The paper clearly differentiates its contribution from previous work like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA by emphasizing the importance of first completing the matrix to capture cross-lingual co-occurrence patterns.
- The method's effectiveness with minimal parallel data (as few as 200 documents) is a significant practical contribution that distinguishes it from methods requiring larger parallel corpora.

### Significance
- The paper addresses a practical problem in NLP with real-world impact: reducing annotation effort for low-resource languages.
- The demonstrated improvements over strong baselines (8.59-14.61% average accuracy gains) represent a meaningful advancement in the field.
- The method's robustness with small amounts of parallel data makes it particularly valuable for scenarios where parallel data is scarce.

## Weaknesses

### Quality
- The paper does not adequately address whether the matrix completion step is truly necessary. Previous work like CL-LSI also uses parallel documents to build a unified representation, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that matrix completion provides a fundamental advantage over simply using the parallel documents to build a better vocabulary mapping.
- The paper doesn't compare with more recent neural approaches to cross-lingual representation learning (e.g., multilingual BERT, XLM-R) that have become standard in the field since the cited works.
- The parameter selection process (using only the EFB task for hyperparameter tuning) is somewhat ad-hoc and doesn't thoroughly justify why these parameters generalize well across all 18 tasks.

### Clarity
- The paper could better explain why the matrix is expected to be low-rank, which is a key assumption for the matrix completion approach.
- The relationship between the matrix completion step and the final LSI step could be clarified - why does completing the matrix before LSI produce better results than applying LSI directly to the partially observed matrix?

### Originality
- While the two-step approach is novel, the core idea of using matrix completion for cross-lingual representation learning is somewhat similar to techniques used in other domains (e.g., collaborative filtering). The paper could better highlight how this application differs from those other domains.
- The paper cites relevant work but doesn't thoroughly discuss how the proposed method relates to recent advances in cross-lingual representation learning beyond the 2010s.

### Significance
- The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the matrix completion step compared to baselines, which is important for practical adoption.
- The paper doesn't discuss potential limitations or failure cases where the matrix completion approach might not work well (e.g., with very dissimilar languages or when parallel data is extremely scarce).

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper presents a well-motivated approach to cross-lingual representation learning with a clear technical contribution.
- The experimental results demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines.
- The method's effectiveness with minimal parallel data is a valuable practical contribution.
- The convergence analysis and detailed optimization algorithm demonstrate technical rigor.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The novelty claim is somewhat overstated; the core idea of using matrix completion for cross-lingual representation learning is not substantially different from existing approaches.
- The paper doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods in cross-lingual representation learning.
- The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with additional ablation studies on the matrix completion step itself.
- The paper doesn't thoroughly address the computational cost of the proposed approach.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of cross-lingual representation learning with a well-executed methodology and strong experimental results. The two-step approach is novel and demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods, particularly in its ability to work effectively with very limited parallel data.

However, I recommend the authors make the following revisions before final acceptance:
1. Strengthen the comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods in cross-lingual representation learning (e.g., multilingual transformers).
2. Provide a more thorough explanation of why matrix completion is necessary for capturing cross-lingual co-occurrence patterns, and how this differs from simply using parallel documents to build a vocabulary mapping.
3. Include a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to baselines.
4. Clarify the parameter selection process and its generalizability across tasks.

These revisions would strengthen the paper's contribution and make it more relevant to the current state of the art in cross-lingual representation learning, which has advanced significantly since the cited works.

This paper addresses an important problem in NLP with a technically sound approach that demonstrates clear empirical advantages over previous methods. The work is well-executed, well-organized, and makes a valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual representation learning.
