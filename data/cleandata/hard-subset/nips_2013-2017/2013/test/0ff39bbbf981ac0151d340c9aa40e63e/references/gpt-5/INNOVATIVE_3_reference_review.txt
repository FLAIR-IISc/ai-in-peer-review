Summary
The paper proposes a two-step approach to cross-lingual document representation for text classification that leverages a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 treats the bilingual concatenated document-term matrix as partially observed: non-parallel documents are missing all features from the other language; parallel pairs are represented in a unified vocabulary. The authors solve a convex matrix completion problem with a trace norm (low-rank), an L1 term (sparsity, which reduces to a linear penalty under nonnegativity), and squared loss on observed entries plus a regularization term on missing entries, subject to nonnegativity. They devise a projected gradient descent algorithm with singular value shrinkage and provide a convergence argument. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the recovered matrix to obtain dense cross-lingual document embeddings on which monolingual SVMs are trained. On Amazon multilingual sentiment tasks (EN, FR, DE, JA; 3 categories), the method outperforms CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, and remains robust when only a few hundred parallel pairs are available.

Relation to prior work
The method is closest to CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA, which directly apply projections to concatenated bilingual matrices or paired corpora. The novelty is to first complete the partially observed matrix using a convex low-rank+sparse objective, then run LSI, arguing this better captures cross-lingual co-occurrence structure from limited parallel data. The optimization resembles fixed-point continuation/Soft-Impute style nuclear norm minimization with nonnegativity; related proximal/shrinkage methods are cited (Ma et al.), though the closest “Soft-Impute” line of work is not explicitly discussed. Compared to pivot-based SCL and multilingual topic models, this is a different, simpler interlingual signal.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is reasonable and convex. The projected gradient with shrinkage and nonnegativity is standard; the non-expansiveness lemmas lead to a convergence argument. Treating the L1 term as smooth under nonnegativity is fine. The objective also regularizes missing entries toward zero, which is intuitive but could bias solutions if ρ is large (authors tune it small). 
- Empirics: Experiments are clearly set up, hyperparameters are selected on one task and transferred, and results are averaged over multiple splits. The ablation against CL-LSI directly tests the value of the completion step.
- Missing details: No runtime/complexity analysis or scaling strategies (e.g., truncated SVD per iteration, handling very large vocabularies). No sensitivity study for µ and τ. It would help to report the rank of solutions and number of iterations. Using TF-IDF within a completion framework is unconventional; some discussion of why this metric is appropriate (versus counts) would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-structured and readable. The optimization section is clear. A few points need clarification:
- Construction of M0: confirm whether each parallel pair is collapsed into a single row with both languages’ features, or two rows aligned via Ω; implications for duplication and weighting should be discussed.
- Notation and constraints (ρ ≤ 1 in the algorithm but not earlier) should be made consistent.
- Some references lack complete bibliographic details; related matrix completion work more directly tied to Soft-Impute should be cited.

Originality
Moderately novel. The key idea—viewing the bilingual document-term matrix as a partially observed low-rank nonnegative matrix and completing it before LSI—is a clean conceptual shift from directly projecting the original matrix. While each component (nuclear norm completion, shrinkage, LSI) is known, their integration for cross-lingual representation with very limited parallel data is a fresh angle. This is more than incremental over CL-LSI/OPCA in my view, though not a radical departure.

Significance
The approach is simple, convex, and potentially broadly useful for multilingual settings where only small bitext is available. Reported gains over CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA across 18 tasks and robustness with as few as 200 parallel pairs are promising. That said, the empirical scope is limited (one dataset, three categories, four languages), and comparisons omit more recent neural baselines (e.g., multilingual transformers, bilingual word embeddings). If positioned as a classical, resource-lean alternative, the contribution is still valuable; stronger significance would come from scaling experiments and broader baselines.

Pros
- Clear, well-motivated formulation that reframes cross-lingual representation as matrix completion.
- Convex objective with a simple, principled algorithm and a convergence argument.
- Consistent, sizeable empirical gains over established cross-lingual baselines.
- Robust performance with very small quantities of parallel data.
- Minimal supervision and reusable across tasks.

Cons
- Lack of runtime/complexity analysis and scalability discussion; repeated SVDs per iteration plus a final SVD could be costly at realistic vocabulary scales.
- Limited experimental breadth; no comparisons to modern neural cross-lingual methods or NMF tri-factorization baselines; no ablations on µ, ρ, nonnegativity, or the necessity of the second LSI step beyond CL-LSI comparison.
- Ambiguities around the exact construction/weighting of parallel rows; potential bias from regularizing missing entries to zero not fully explored.
- Some missing/partial references and minor notational inconsistencies.

Questions for authors
- How large are t, d in your experiments, and what are typical runtimes/iterations? Are you using truncated SVD per iteration?
- How sensitive is performance to ρ and µ? What happens if µ=0 or without the nonnegativity constraint?
- Do you observe gains if you skip the second LSI step and directly train on M*?
- How do results compare to a multilingual transformer (e.g., mBERT/XLM-R) with 100 labeled target examples and/or with bitext for feature alignment?
- Are parallel pairs single rows or two rows? How do you prevent “double counting” and balance languages?

Recommendation: Weak Accept
This is a neat, conceptually novel twist that yields consistent improvements with very limited parallel data. While the methodology is not complex and the experiments are somewhat narrow, the idea is bold enough and well-executed to merit presentation. Strengthening the paper with scaling data, clearer construction details, and broader baselines would solidify its impact.
