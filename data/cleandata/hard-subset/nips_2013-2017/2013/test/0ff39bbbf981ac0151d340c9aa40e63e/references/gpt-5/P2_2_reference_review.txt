Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses cross-language text classification by inducing a shared representation from a small number of unlabeled parallel documents. The key idea is a two-step pipeline: (1) construct a unified bilingual document-term matrix where parallel pairs are fully observed and non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language; then complete this matrix via a convex formulation combining a nuclear norm (low-rank), an ℓ1 term (sparsity), squared loss on observed entries, and a quadratic penalty on missing entries, with nonnegativity constraints; optimized by a projected gradient descent scheme with singular value thresholding; (2) perform LSI (SVD) on the recovered matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation, on which a monolingual classifier is trained. Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews compare against TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, showing consistent gains and robustness to the number of parallel documents.

The work is positioned against translation-based transfer, multi-view methods, and representation learning methods such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, and multilingual topic models. The closest baselines are CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA on parallel data; the novelty claimed is to first complete the bilingual doc-term matrix (exploiting co-occurrence both within and across languages) before applying LSI.

Quality
- Theory/algorithm: The convex objective is reasonable and clearly stated. The gradient for the smooth part is correct under the nonnegativity constraint, which renders the ℓ1 term a differentiable linear penalty. The optimization uses a projected gradient step on the smooth part, followed by nuclear-norm shrinkage and nonnegativity projection. The convergence argument relies on non-expansiveness of the gradient step, shrinkage, and projection, and cites fixed-point convergence results. However, the proof that the specific composition of shrinkage and projection converges to the minimizer of the objective with both the nuclear norm and the nonnegativity indicator lacks rigor: in general, the proximal operator of a sum of two non-smooth terms (nuclear norm + indicator of nonnegativity) is not equal to sequentially applying their individual proximal operators. A more principled treatment (e.g., projected proximal gradient with a combined prox, Dykstra splitting, or ADMM) and a reference that covers this exact composition would strengthen the theoretical claims. As written, the fixed-point convergence proof is plausible but incomplete regarding optimality for the stated objective.
- Empirics: The experimental setup is clear, using established datasets and baselines. The method consistently outperforms CL-LSI/CL-OPCA/CL-KCCA and is robust with few parallel documents. However, key ablations are missing: (i) LSI directly on the concatenated matrix with missing entries treated as zero, to isolate the value of matrix completion; (ii) using the completed matrix directly without the LSI step; (iii) sensitivity to µ, γ, ρ, and τ. Runtime/scalability are not reported; since each iteration requires an SVD, practical efficiency is a concern. Statistical testing is not provided beyond means and standard deviations.
- Claims are mostly supported by the results presented, but broader generality beyond sentiment classification and beyond English-centric settings is not evaluated.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The construction of the unified matrix, the optimization problem, and the algorithm are described clearly. The notation is consistent. The convergence section is readable, though, as noted, it should more carefully justify the composite proximal step. Minor suggestions: briefly discuss computational complexity per iteration (SVD size and truncation), and clarify how CL-LSI and CL-OPCA are instantiated on partially parallel data to ensure fair comparison.

Originality
The combination of matrix completion with subsequent LSI for cross-lingual representation is, to my knowledge, novel relative to CL-LSI/CL-OPCA/CL-KCCA. Using a convex low-rank + sparsity + nonnegativity completion on a bilingual doc-term matrix with a small number of fully observed parallel rows is an interesting formulation. The paper appropriately cites related cross-lingual representation learning approaches. While low-rank completion and LSI are standard techniques, their integration in this context and the specific regularization choices constitute a nontrivial contribution.

Significance
Cross-lingual text classification with limited parallel data is an important problem. The method appears practically useful, especially its robustness with as few as 200 parallel documents, and produces consistent gains over strong classical baselines. If the theoretical optimization concerns are addressed and the empirical scope broadened (or at least the ablations added), the contribution could be of interest to practitioners and researchers working on cross-lingual representation learning.

Strengths
- Clear problem setup and a simple, elegant two-step solution.
- Convex formulation with nonnegativity, sparsity, and a ridge-like penalty on missing entries.
- Strong empirical gains over CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA across 18 tasks; robust to fewer parallel pairs.
- Method leverages both within- and cross-language co-occurrence without requiring translation of all documents.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete theoretical justification for convergence to the true minimizer when combining shrinkage and nonnegativity projection.
- Missing ablations to isolate the contribution of the matrix completion step versus LSI alone.
- No runtime or scalability analysis; SVD-in-the-loop may be costly at scale.
- Limited baselines: a direct “LSI on concatenated-with-zeros” baseline would be very informative.
- Some parameters (µ) are fixed without sensitivity analysis; only one task used for model selection transferred to all.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide an ablation with LSI on the original concatenated matrix (zeros for missing) and with using M* directly for classification.
- Clarify whether you use truncated SVD in the shrinkage step and report wall-clock times and number of iterations.
- Strengthen the optimization section: either cite a result covering prox of sum of nuclear norm and indicator via your composition, or adopt a standard splitting method with a convergence guarantee to the optimal solution of (2).
- Discuss the effect of the nonnegativity constraint and the ℓ1 term (µ) quantitatively.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a simple, general approach.
- Consistent, sizable empirical improvements and robustness to scarce parallel data.
- Clear exposition and sound convex formulation (modulo the optimization detail).

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical convergence to the stated objective is not fully justified for the proposed operator composition.
- Missing key ablations and scalability analysis limit the strength and generality of the empirical evidence.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea is appealing and empirically strong against classical baselines, but the paper would benefit from tightening the optimization theory and adding ablations and runtime analysis.
