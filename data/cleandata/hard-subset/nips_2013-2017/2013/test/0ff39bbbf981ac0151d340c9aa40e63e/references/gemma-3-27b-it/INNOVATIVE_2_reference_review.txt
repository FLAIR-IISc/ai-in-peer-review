This paper presents a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, leveraging unlabeled parallel data via matrix completion and latent semantic indexing. The core idea – utilizing matrix completion to bridge the vocabulary gap before applying LSI – is intriguing and, importantly, *novel*. While matrix completion has seen widespread use, its application in this specific cross-lingual context, particularly as a pre-processing step for LSI, feels fresh and addresses a known challenge in the field. The empirical results on Amazon reviews demonstrate a clear performance improvement over several baselines, and the observation that the method performs well even with limited parallel data is a significant strength.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning is a genuinely new approach. It’s not simply another adaptation of existing techniques.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms several established methods across a range of tasks. The gains, especially considering the relatively small amount of labeled target data used, are compelling.
*   **Practicality:** The method's effectiveness with limited parallel data is a significant advantage, making it more applicable in real-world scenarios where large parallel corpora are unavailable.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The algorithm is described in detail, and the experimental setup is clearly outlined.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the authors motivate the matrix completion step intuitively, a deeper theoretical justification for *why* this approach works so well would be beneficial. Why is low-rank structure a reasonable assumption in this cross-lingual context beyond the general principle of sparsity?
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The extensive parameter tuning mentioned (γ, ρ, k) suggests potential sensitivity. While they report the selected parameters, a more detailed analysis of how performance varies with different parameter settings would strengthen the work. A sensitivity analysis could reveal robustness or highlight critical parameters.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are limited to sentiment classification on Amazon reviews. While these are a good starting point, evaluating the method on other cross-lingual tasks (e.g., topic classification, information retrieval) would increase confidence in its generalizability.
*   **Optimization Details:** The algorithm description mentions a projected gradient descent algorithm but lacks sufficient detail for reproduction. Providing more specifics on the projection step and convergence criteria would be helpful.
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the chosen baselines are reasonable, including more recent and advanced cross-lingual representation learning methods (e.g., those utilizing pre-trained multilingual language models like BERT or XLM-R) would provide a more rigorous evaluation. A comparison to even a *single* such model would be informative.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is truly novel and offers a promising new direction for cross-lingual representation learning.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods.
*   The method's practicality (performance with limited parallel data) is a significant advantage.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the approach and experimental results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical justification for the approach could be strengthened.
*   The experiments are limited in scope, and the method hasn’t been evaluated on a wide range of tasks.
*   A more detailed discussion of the optimization algorithm and parameter sensitivity is needed.
*   The comparison to state-of-the-art methods, especially those based on pre-trained language models, is lacking.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, I strongly recommend *accepting* this paper. The novelty of the approach and the compelling empirical results outweigh the need for complete theoretical rigor or extensive experimentation. The paper introduces a genuinely new idea to the field of cross-lingual learning, and the preliminary results suggest that this approach has the potential to make a significant impact. I believe this work represents a valuable contribution to NIPS, prioritizing a bold new approach over absolute methodological perfection. The authors should be encouraged to address the weaknesses in future work, but the paper is already worthy of publication.
