Summary
The paper tackles cross-language text classification by learning shared document representations from unlabeled parallel data. The core idea is a two-step pipeline: (1) construct a unified bilingual document-term matrix where parallel document pairs are fully observed across concatenated vocabularies but non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language; recover missing entries by solving a convex matrix completion problem that combines nuclear norm (low rank), an l1 penalty (sparsity with nonnegativity so it becomes linear), squared loss on observed entries, and an l2-type shrinkage toward zero on unobserved entries; (2) apply LSI (SVD) to the recovered matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations on which a linear SVM is trained. The optimization is performed by a projected gradient descent scheme with singular value shrinkage and a nonnegativity projection; convergence is argued via non-expansive operator composition. Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews (EN/FR/DE/JA; 18 transfer tasks; only 100 labeled target examples) show improvements over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, and robustness to small numbers of parallel documents.

Relation to prior work
The approach connects to CL-LSI (LSI on bilingual/dual-language matrices), CL-KCCA (correlation-based projections), CL-OPCA, and bilingual SCL with pivot features. Unlike these, the paper explicitly formulates bilingual representation induction as matrix completion in a concatenated vocabulary space before performing LSI. The optimization builds on trace-norm minimization (Candes & Recht) and SVT/proximal methods (Ma et al.). Compared to multi-view co-training/co-classification and topic models, the contribution is a convex imputation step that leverages limited parallel data and nonnegativity.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation is clear and convex. The algorithm resembles a proximal gradient with nuclear norm shrinkage and projection. However, the convergence argument is not fully rigorous for the stated objective: you apply the proximal operator of the nuclear norm followed by projection (indicator of nonnegativity). In general, prox of f+g is not prox_f followed by prox_g; convergence to the minimizer of the composite objective requires conditions (e.g., generalized forward-backward or ADMM) and a proof that fixed points correspond to optimal solutions. Here, non-expansiveness alone does not ensure convergence to the optimum of (2). This is a significant but likely addressable gap—either tighten the proof, or switch to a known convergent scheme for multiple nonsmooth terms.
- Modeling choices: Adding both nuclear norm and a linear l1 penalty (which is a linear sum under nonnegativity) plus an l2 loss toward zero on unobserved entries is unusual. Some discussion/ablation is needed to justify the role of ρ (shrink-to-zero of missing entries) in conjunction with the sparsity pressure and nuclear norm. The current rationale is intuitive but not empirically dissected.
- Empirics: The experimental protocol is reasonable and results look consistently stronger than baselines, including under limited parallel data. However, key ablations are missing: (i) LSI on the original concatenated matrix vs on the completed matrix (to isolate the gain from completion), (ii) removing the l1 term or the ρ penalty, (iii) runtime and scalability. The dataset is single-domain sentiment; broader tasks would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, with a clear problem setup, algorithm box, and sensible parameter choices. Some places could be clearer:
- Explicitly state the stopping criterion, SVD computation details (full vs partial, randomized), and complexity per iteration.
- Clarify why the linear l1 term under nonnegativity is treated as smooth and how its constant gradient affects step-size/Lipschitz assumptions.
- Provide intuition/examples for what kinds of cross-lingual co-occurrence patterns are recovered by completion (e.g., nearest cross-lingual word associations).
- Include exact numbers in tables/figures (they’re referenced but not present in the text provided).

Originality
The combination of trace-norm matrix completion on a bilingual document-term matrix followed by LSI is, to my knowledge, novel relative to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, and SCL. While both components are known, applying imputation of missing cross-lingual features before spectral dimensionality reduction is a new angle. That said, related families (NMF tri-factorization, collective matrix factorization, joint bilingual embeddings) are close; the paper cites some but does not compare to SCL or NMF-based methods empirically.

Significance
If the convergence and ablations are addressed, the approach is a practical contribution: it appears to reduce reliance on large parallel corpora, improves over strong cross-lingual baselines on a standard benchmark, and is conceptually simple to implement with off-the-shelf SVMs. This could be useful for practitioners working with low-resource parallel data. The impact would be higher with broader tasks/datasets and comparisons to additional modern baselines.

Pros
- Simple, modular two-step method; leverages limited parallel data.
- Convex formulation with reasonable inductive biases (low-rank + sparsity + nonnegativity).
- Strong, consistent empirical gains over several established baselines; robustness with few parallel pairs.
- Clear setup and reasonable hyperparameter protocol (fixed across tasks).

Cons
- Convergence proof is incomplete for the stated composite nonsmooth objective; algorithmic guarantees need tightening or a standard method (e.g., ADMM).
- Missing ablations to attribute gains and justify regularization terms.
- Limited baselines (no SCL pivot method, no NMF tri-factorization, no translation-based baselines).
- Scalability/runtime and SVD details are absent; applicability to larger vocabularies/datasets unclear.
- Evaluation limited to sentiment; generality to other cross-lingual tasks not shown.

Questions for authors
1. Can you provide a rigorous convergence guarantee for minimizing (2) with both nuclear norm and nonnegativity, or switch to a known convergent proximal scheme (e.g., ADMM, generalized forward-backward)?
2. How sensitive are results to ρ and µ? What happens if ρ=0 or µ=0?
3. What is the runtime and memory footprint versus CL-LSI/CL-KCCA? Do you use truncated/randomized SVD?
4. How much of the gain comes from matrix completion versus doing LSI directly (CL-LSI) on the same data?
5. Can you compare to SCL (Prettenhofer & Stein, 2010) and an NMF tri-factorization baseline?

Minor
- Some typos/formatting (e.g., missing squares in norms in the text).
- Please include precise data splits and stopping criteria for reproducibility.

Recommendation
Score: 6/10 (weak accept), contingent on clarifying convergence and adding at least an ablation isolating the contribution of the completion step. The idea is promising and empirically strong, but the theoretical and empirical analysis should be tightened for NeurIPS standards.

Reviewer confidence: medium.
