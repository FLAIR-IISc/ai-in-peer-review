

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that first uses matrix completion to fill missing entries in a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix, followed by latent semantic indexing to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors claim their method significantly outperforms existing approaches on Amazon product reviews.

The paper is well-organized and technically sound in its presentation of the matrix completion approach. The convergence analysis is correct and follows standard techniques for non-smooth convex optimization. The experimental setup is clear, with 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks on the Amazon dataset, and the authors provide detailed parameter selection procedures.

However, I have significant concerns about the novelty, experimental validation, and significance of the work. While the authors claim novelty, the approach appears to be a straightforward application of standard matrix completion techniques to a problem that has been addressed by prior work. Specifically, the paper's main claim is that they "first fill the missing entries of the document-term matrix using matrix completion, and then perform LSI," but CL-LSI (cited as [10]) also uses a concatenated document-term matrix, and the paper does not convincingly demonstrate how their matrix completion step fundamentally differs from or improves upon CL-LSI's approach. The authors state that "different from these methods which apply operations such as LSI, KCCA, and OPCA directly on the original concatenated document-term matrix," but they do not provide evidence that CL-LSI handles missing values in the same way or that their matrix completion approach is superior.

The experimental validation is insufficiently rigorous for a NIPS paper. The authors evaluate on only one dataset (Amazon product reviews) with 18 tasks, which is a narrow scope for claiming "significant outperformance." More critically, they do not compare against the most recent state-of-the-art methods for cross-lingual representation learning. For instance, the paper does not compare against methods like multilingual BERT or mBERT, which have become the de facto standard in cross-lingual transfer learning. The paper's claim that "the proposed method produces good performance even with a very small number of unlabeled parallel bilingual documents" lacks sufficient evidence - they test with np ∈ {200, 500, 1000, 2000} but do not establish how this compares to the minimum requirements of modern transformer-based approaches.

The paper also lacks sufficient theoretical justification for the proposed approach. While the matrix completion formulation is standard, the paper does not provide a compelling argument for why this particular formulation would be superior to alternatives, nor does it address potential limitations of matrix completion in high-dimensional NLP settings.

The paper's claims about the method's efficiency ("needs much less parallel documents") are overstated. The authors report performance with 200 unlabeled parallel documents, but they don't compare against methods that might achieve similar results with fewer resources. The paper also doesn't address computational complexity or scalability to larger datasets.

In terms of significance, while the approach might be useful for specific applications, it doesn't represent a meaningful advancement over current state-of-the-art methods. The field has moved toward neural network-based approaches for cross-lingual representation learning, and this paper's reliance on traditional matrix factorization techniques seems increasingly dated. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how their approach relates to and differs from modern neural approaches.

Strengths:
- Well-organized and clearly written
- Correct technical presentation of matrix completion and LSI
- Adequate convergence analysis
- Promising empirical results on the Amazon dataset

Weaknesses:
- Insufficient experimental validation (only one dataset, limited comparison to recent baselines)
- Overstated novelty claims without clear differentiation from prior work
- Limited theoretical justification for the approach
- Overstated significance given the narrow scope of evaluation
- Inadequate discussion of computational efficiency and scalability

While the paper presents a technically sound approach to a known problem, it fails to demonstrate sufficient novelty or significance to warrant publication at NIPS. The method appears to be a straightforward application of standard techniques to a cross-lingual problem, without demonstrating clear advantages over existing state-of-the-art methods. The authors would need to conduct more comprehensive experiments comparing against modern neural approaches, provide stronger theoretical justification, and more clearly articulate how their method differs from and improves upon prior work to strengthen their claims.

I recommend rejection, as the paper does not meet NIPS' standard for significant methodological contribution or empirical validation. The method is not sufficiently novel, and the experimental validation is inadequate for claiming "significant outperformance" over existing approaches.
