Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles cross-language text classification by learning a shared representation with two steps: (i) fill the missing entries of a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix via convex matrix completion with a trace-norm plus ℓ1 regularizer and squared-loss data terms, under a nonnegativity constraint; (ii) apply LSI (SVD) to the recovered matrix to obtain low-dimensional features for training monolingual classifiers. The approach leverages a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. The paper relates to CL-LSI [Littman et al.], CL-KCCA [Vinokourov et al.], CL-OPCA [Platt et al.], SCL [Prettenhofer & Stein], and multi-view methods [Amini et al.], and to matrix completion with nuclear norm minimization [Candès & Recht; Ma et al.]. The empirical study uses the multilingual Amazon reviews dataset and reports improvements over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA across 18 transfer tasks, including sensitivity to the number of parallel documents.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, practical idea: use low-rank completion to exploit cross-lingual co-occurrence before LSI. 
- Convex formulation with nonnegativity and sparsity fits TF-IDF data; squared losses acknowledge noise and include a tunable penalty on unobserved entries.
- Empirical gains are reported across many language/category pairs and with few parallel documents, indicating robustness.
- Some theoretical consideration: non-expansiveness lemmas and a convergence claim.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Methodology and theory
1) Proximal algorithm and convergence proof: The optimization (2) includes two nonsmooth terms: the nuclear norm and the indicator of the nonnegative orthant. The algorithm performs three steps per iteration: gradient descent on g, singular value shrinkage, then projection onto the nonnegative orthant. Theorem 1 cites non-expansiveness to claim convergence to an optimal solution of (2). However, for forward–backward splitting with a smooth term g and a nonsmooth term f, the update should apply the proximal operator of f once (not the composition of two different proxes) to the gradient step. In general, prox of (γ‖·‖∗ + I{·≥0}) is not equal to prox_{γ‖·‖∗} followed by projection. The provided proof does not establish that fixed points of PC∘Sν∘h coincide with minimizers of (2). The reference [11, Thm. 4] covers shrinkage after a gradient step, not an additional projection. A correct approach would use: (i) proximal gradient with a single prox for the sum (e.g., via Dykstra’s algorithm), (ii) ADMM/split Bregman with a separate variable for the constraint, or (iii) projected subgradient on the dual. As written, the convergence claim to the true optimizer is not justified.

2) Assumptions for matrix completion: Classical guarantees rely on randomness/incoherence and sampling patterns unlikely to hold here: the missingness is highly structured (entire language blocks per non-parallel document). The paper neither analyzes identifiability under such block-missingness nor provides diagnostics when completion fails. A discussion or empirical stress test (e.g., varying percentage of parallel pairs and vocabulary overlap) is needed.

3) Redundancy vs. LSI and missing ablations: The trace norm enforces low rank; the second-stage LSI again applies SVD. An ablation is needed to quantify the contribution of the completion step vs. a single-stage CL-LSI with appropriate weighting/masking. Similarly, show results for: (a) completion without LSI; (b) CL-LSI on the zero-filled matrix with importance weights on observed vs. missing; (c) nonnegativity vs. no nonnegativity; (d) varying ρ, γ, µ.

4) Initialization and handling missingness: “Initialize M as the nonnegative projection of the rank-1 approximation of M0.” How is the rank-1 approximation computed with missing entries? If zeros are imputed, this biases the SVD. Consider Soft-Impute-like warm-starts or explicitly state the procedure.

5) Complexity and scalability: Each iteration requires an SVD of a large t×d matrix; no runtime, memory, or iteration counts are reported. No comparison of computational cost to CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA is given. Randomized/truncated SVD choices and stopping criteria should be specified.

Experimental design and clarity
6) Transductive setting: Representation learning uses “all documents from the two languages,” including test documents, for all methods except TBOW. This is a transductive regime; please clarify explicitly and include a fair transductive baseline for TBOW (e.g., target-only LSI on all target docs, then train on the 100 labeled target docs). Also report an inductive variant that uses only training+parallel documents.

7) Construction of M0: The text says that each parallel pair is represented as a single fully observed row. Does that mean the pair is collapsed into one instance (thus reducing t)? Are those reviews also present as separate monolingual rows elsewhere (double counting)? How are TF-IDF weights normalized across languages to avoid magnitude bias?

8) Baselines and tuning: CL-KCCA details (kernel, regularization, CCA dimensionality selection) are underspecified; OPCA implementation details are also sparse. Using “libsvm default” may disadvantage some baselines; consider a small sweep over C. Report statistical significance.

9) Reproducibility: Table 1 and Figures 1–2 are referenced but not included here; the absolute numbers, variances, number of iterations, and convergence tolerances are essential. No code or preprocessing details (tokenization for Japanese, stopwording, vocabulary pruning) are provided.

10) Hyperparameter sensitivity: µ is fixed at 1e−6 without justification; ρ and γ are tuned only on EFB, then reused. Provide sensitivity curves or at least ranges where performance is stable.

Minor issues and presentation
- Notation inconsistencies and typos: missing/awkward spacing in inequalities (e.g., 0 < τ < min(2, 2 ρ)), sometimes ρ ≤ 1 is claimed but later ρ ∈ {1e−5,…,1}. Clarify.
- Equation (3): calling µ‖M‖1 “smooth” relies on nonnegativity so that ‖M‖1 = sum Mij; this should be stated upfront in 4.1 and the proof should reflect the constrained domain.
- The gradient formula and Lemma 1’s non-expansiveness ignore the constant µE term (it cancels); note this explicitly.
- Many references are incomplete or incorrect (missing years, venues, authors’ names; e.g., “ICCL-ACL”, “Smet” vs. “De Smet”). Please fix.
- Algorithm 1 lacks stopping criteria and SVD computation details (full vs. truncated).

Pros
- Simple, potentially useful idea; leverages few parallel documents.
- Convex objective; nonnegativity appropriate for TF-IDF.
- Broad empirical evaluation across multiple language pairs and categories; promising gains reported.

Cons
- The optimization algorithm/proof is, as written, insufficiently justified for a problem with two nonsmooth terms and a constraint.
- No complexity analysis or runtimes; unclear scalability.
- Important ablations and stronger/fair baselines (transductive TBOW, Soft-Impute/weighted CL-LSI, matrix-factorization/NMF variants) are missing.
- Clarity issues in data construction and preprocessing; references poorly formatted.

Recommendation: Reject. The idea is interesting and empirical results are encouraging, but the methodological gap in the optimizer/convergence, lack of ablations and scalability evidence, and clarity/reference issues are substantial and should be addressed.

Confidence: High.
