

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning using matrix completion on a concatenated document-term matrix built from unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The approach first fills missing entries via matrix completion (using a trace norm and ℓ1 regularization) and then applies LSI to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors evaluate their method on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses a relevant problem in cross-lingual learning with a novel two-step approach that differs from existing methods like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA. The experimental results show consistent improvements over baselines, particularly with limited parallel data. The method is conceptually sound and the empirical evaluation is thorough.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising results, the paper contains numerous technical and methodological inconsistencies that undermine its scientific rigor:

1. **Technical Errors in Formulation:** Equation (1) incorrectly uses "rank(M) + µ‖M‖1" without proper subscript notation (should be "‖M‖₁"). Equation (2) has inconsistent spacing in "M ≥ 0" and "Mij ≥ 0, ∀(i, j) ∈ Ω̂" (spaces should be removed). The gradient calculation in Equation (4) uses "M" for the matrix variable, but the paper also uses "M" for the matrix in the objective function, creating confusion.

2. **Incorrect Technical Claim:** The paper states "a fully observed document-term matrix is naturally low-rank and sparse" (Section 3.1), which is factually incorrect. Document-term matrices are typically high-rank (not low-rank) and sparse. The low-rank property emerges after LSI, not from the raw matrix.

3. **Inconsistent Notation:** The paper uses "Ω̂" for missing entries but doesn't consistently use the correct LaTeX notation (should be \hat{\Omega} or \Omega^{\hat{}}). In Section 3, "M0" is used for the initial matrix, but the optimization variable is also "M," creating ambiguity.

4. **Methodological Flaw:** The combination of trace norm (for rank minimization) and ℓ1 norm (for sparsity) is theoretically questionable since document-term matrices are already sparse. The paper fails to justify why both regularizers are necessary.

5. **Experimental Design Issues:** Parameter selection is performed on a single task (EFB), which risks overfitting to that specific task. The paper claims "the proposed method needs much less parallel documents" but provides no quantitative comparison of the required parallel data volume (e.g., "we need 200 parallel documents instead of 2000" would be helpful).

6. **Missing Critical Analysis:** The paper lacks statistical significance testing for the performance improvements over baselines. The claim "significantly outperforms" should be supported by proper hypothesis testing.

7. **Figure Reference Error:** The paper mentions "Figure 1 and Figure 2" in Section 5.3 but no figures are included in the provided text, which is a critical omission for a conference paper.

8. **Formatting Inconsistencies:** The reference list contains inconsistent punctuation (e.g., "In NIPS,," with two commas) and some references lack complete publication details. The paper also inconsistently uses "language" vs "languages" (e.g., "language barrier" vs "language domains").

9. **Misleading Claim in Related Work:** The paper claims "our approach only requires a small set of unlabeled parallel documents, while these approaches require at least translating all the training documents," but the cited approaches (e.g., [17, 21]) actually require translating the training data, which is different from the parallel documents used in this method.

**Recommendation:** While the paper presents a potentially useful approach for cross-lingual learning, the numerous technical errors, methodological inconsistencies, and lack of rigorous statistical validation undermine its scientific contribution. The paper would benefit from a thorough technical revision addressing these issues before reconsideration. I recommend rejection due to the significant number of technical flaws that prevent proper evaluation of the method's merits.

**Pro/Con Summary:**
- Pro: Novel two-step approach with promising empirical results
- Pro: Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple language pairs
- Con: Numerous technical errors in formulation and notation
- Con: Incorrect technical claims about document-term matrices
- Con: Inadequate statistical validation of results
- Con: Inconsistent experimental design (parameter selection on single task)
- Con: Critical missing figures referenced in text
- Con: Unclear theoretical justification for combined regularizers
