

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning using matrix completion on a concatenated document-term matrix. The approach first fills missing entries in the document-term matrix (where parallel documents have observed values for both languages and non-parallel documents have missing values for the other language) via matrix completion, then applies LSI on the completed matrix.

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a clear methodology for cross-language representation learning that leverages parallel documents in a novel way. The experimental results on Amazon product reviews show consistent improvements over several strong baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) across multiple language pairs. The demonstration of robustness with small amounts of unlabeled parallel data is particularly valuable for practical applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Critical Mathematical Inconsistency:** Equation (2) contains ρ in the summation over Ω̂ without defining ρ in the optimization problem. The parameter ρ appears to be used as a regularization weight, but it's never formally defined in the optimization objective. This is a major technical flaw that undermines the mathematical soundness of the proposed approach.

2. **Misleading Technical Claim:** The paper states "With nonnegativity constraints M ≥ 0, the non-smooth ℓ1 norm regularizer in the objective function of (2) is equivalent to a smooth linear function ‖M‖1 = ∑ij Mij." This is incorrect. While the ℓ1 norm of a nonnegative matrix is indeed ∑ij Mij, this is not "smooth" as claimed. The ℓ1 norm is non-smooth (not differentiable at zero), which is why the paper uses a shrinkage operation in the optimization. This mischaracterization of the ℓ1 norm is a serious technical error.

3. **Parameter Selection Issues:** The paper sets τ = 1 for the gradient descent step size, which is unreasonably large for optimization (typical step sizes are much smaller, like 0.01-0.1). The justification for this choice is absent, and the convergence analysis in Section 4.2 doesn't provide guidance for choosing τ=1.

4. **Ambiguous Data Handling:** The paper states it uses "unlabeled parallel bilingual documents" but doesn't clarify whether these documents have class labels (for sentiment classification) or not. In standard NLP terminology, "unlabeled" means no class labels, but parallel documents in sentiment classification typically do have labels. This ambiguity affects how the matrix completion is performed.

5. **Inconsistent Notation:** The paper uses inconsistent formatting for mathematical notation (e.g., "‖ · ‖1" vs "‖M‖1", "M0" vs "M0" with inconsistent superscript formatting). In Section 3.1, the paper refers to "M0ij" but in the text it's written as "M0ij" in some places and "M0ij" in others.

6. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper doesn't explain why matrix completion would be better than simply using the observed entries (as in CL-LSI). The claim that "a fully observed document-term matrix is naturally low-rank and sparse" is true, but the paper doesn't justify why filling missing values through matrix completion would be superior to the existing methods that work with incomplete matrices.

7. **Parameter Selection Methodology:** Using the single task EFB to select parameters (γ, ρ, k) for all 18 tasks is problematic. This is a form of data leakage since the same data is used for both parameter tuning and testing. The paper should have used cross-validation across tasks or a separate validation set.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a novel approach to cross-language representation learning with promising empirical results, the technical flaws are substantial. The mathematical inconsistencies, particularly the undefined parameter ρ and the misleading claim about the ℓ1 norm, undermine the credibility of the proposed method. The paper would benefit from a thorough mathematical review and clarification of these points.

The experimental section lacks sufficient detail on the implementation of the matrix completion step, particularly how missing values are handled during the optimization process. The paper also fails to provide a clear theoretical justification for why matrix completion would improve performance over existing methods that work directly with incomplete matrices.

**Recommendation:** Reject. While the paper makes a plausible contribution to cross-language representation learning, the technical flaws are significant enough to prevent acceptance. The paper would need to be substantially revised to address the mathematical inconsistencies before it could be considered for publication. The minor methodological details and technical nuances identified here are critical to the validity of the proposed approach, and their omission represents a serious flaw in the paper's technical presentation.
