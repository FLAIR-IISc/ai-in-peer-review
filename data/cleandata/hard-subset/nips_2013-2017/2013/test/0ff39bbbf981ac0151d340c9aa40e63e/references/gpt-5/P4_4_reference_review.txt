Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual text classification that leverages a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 performs matrix completion on a unified document–term matrix spanning the concatenated vocabularies of two languages: parallel pairs are fully observed rows; non-parallel documents are partially observed rows with missing entries for the other language. The completion is posed as a convex optimization with nuclear norm (for low rank), ℓ1 regularization (for sparsity), squared losses on both observed entries and missing entries (the latter weighted by ρ), and nonnegativity constraints. The authors solve this with an iterative scheme combining a gradient step on the smooth part, singular value shrinkage, and nonnegativity projection. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to derive low-dimensional cross-lingual representations, on which monolingual SVMs are trained.

This sits between two known strands: (a) translation-based or multi-view cross-lingual classification (e.g., co-training, SCL, translated learning) and (b) cross-lingual representation learning via CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA on parallel/dual-language corpora. The key difference from CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA is the explicit matrix completion to impute missing cross-language entries before dimensionality reduction, with the aim of exploiting intra- and cross-lingual co-occurrence even with limited parallel data.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Formulation: The convex objective combines nuclear norm, ℓ1 penalty, squared reconstruction loss on observed entries, a quadratic penalty on missing entries, and nonnegativity. The gradient and masking are correctly described; with nonnegativity, the ℓ1 term reduces to a smooth linear term.
- Algorithm: The update combines a gradient step on g(M), singular value thresholding (prox of nuclear norm), and projection onto the nonnegative orthant. While each operator is non-expansive, the composition is not the proximal map of the sum of the nonsmooth terms (nuclear norm + nonnegativity indicator). Convergence claims to the global minimizer of (2) require a method that handles multiple nonsmooth terms (e.g., Dykstra’s algorithm, Douglas–Rachford/ADMM, or proximal splitting for sums of convex functions); see Combettes & Pesquet (2011), Condat (2013), or Parikh & Boyd (2014). The provided argument based solely on non-expansiveness and a reference to [11] is insufficient to establish convergence to the solution of (2); at best it shows convergence to a fixed point of the composed operator. This is a significant theoretical gap that needs either a corrected proof or an algorithm aligned with known theory.
- Complexity/scalability: Each iteration requires an SVD; for large document–term matrices, this is expensive. The paper lacks details on using truncated/partial SVD, per-iteration costs, memory footprint, and wall-clock times or iteration counts. This limits assessment of practicality.
- Modeling choices: The claim that fully observed document–term matrices are “naturally low-rank” is debatable; they are typically high-rank but may have approximate low-dimensional structure. The inclusion of both nuclear norm (promoting low rank) and ℓ1 (promoting sparsity) plus a subsequent LSI seems potentially redundant; an ablation would clarify contributions of each component. The choice and effect of ρ and µ are not analyzed beyond a single setting for µ.
- Experimental protocol: The representation learning step uses “all documents from the two languages,” which appears to include target test documents. This is a transductive setup; it should be clearly stated and justified, and a strictly inductive setting (using only training unlabeled data) should be reported to avoid optimistic estimates.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The optimization setup, gradient, shrinkage, and projection are clearly described. Suggestions:
- Clarify whether the representation learning uses any test documents (transductive) and, if so, add an inductive variant.
- Provide algorithmic details for SVD (truncated method, rank selection per iteration), stopping criteria, and computational costs.
- Expand on initialization and sensitivity to hyperparameters (γ, ρ, µ, k).
- Fix minor typos and standardize notation (e.g., vectors vs. matrices; ensure all symbols are defined once).

Originality
The use of matrix completion to impute a bilingual document–term matrix with partially observed cross-language entries, followed by LSI, appears novel in cross-lingual text classification. The optimization approach (nuclear norm with shrinkage) is standard; the novelty lies in the problem setup and two-step pipeline rather than in algorithmic advances.

Significance
Empirical results on multilingual Amazon reviews indicate consistent gains over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, especially with limited parallel data. This suggests practical value for low-resource cross-lingual settings. However, significance would be strengthened by:
- Including SCL (Prettenhofer & Stein, 2010) as a strong baseline (cited as [16]).
- Reporting inductive results (no test-set access during representation learning).
- Ablations isolating the effect of matrix completion vs. directly applying LSI to the masked matrix, and the roles of ℓ1, nonnegativity, and ρ.
- Runtime/efficiency results to establish scalability.

Pros
- Clear, intuitive two-step framework leveraging limited parallel data.
- Convex formulation with standard components; easy to implement with existing tools.
- Strong empirical performance and robustness to small numbers of parallel pairs.
- Addresses an important problem in cross-lingual classification.

Cons
- Convergence proof is not sound for the stated objective with two nonsmooth terms; algorithmic justification needs correction.
- Potential transductive evaluation inflates performance; inductive results missing.
- Missing strong baseline (SCL) and limited ablation/sensitivity analyses.
- No discussion of computational cost; full SVD per iteration can be prohibitive.
- Some overstatements (e.g., “naturally low-rank” document–term matrices).

Suggestions for improvement
- Revise the optimization to a provably convergent method for γ||M||* + I_{M≥0} + smooth loss (e.g., ADMM or Douglas–Rachford) and provide a correct convergence proof, or remove the nonnegativity constraint from the objective and justify the projection step as a post-processing choice.
- Provide inductive results (no access to test documents in the unsupervised stage).
- Add SCL as a baseline, and include ablations (no ℓ1; no nonnegativity; vary ρ; skip matrix completion).
- Report computational performance and use truncated SVD.
- Analyze learned cross-lingual correspondences qualitatively to support claims.

Overall, the approach is promising and empirically effective, but the theoretical justification and experimental protocol need tightening to meet NeurIPS standards.
