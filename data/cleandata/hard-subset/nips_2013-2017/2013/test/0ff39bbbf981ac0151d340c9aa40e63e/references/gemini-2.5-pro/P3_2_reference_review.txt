**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel two-step learning method (TSL) for cross-language text classification. The core idea is to first construct a unified document-term matrix from source and target language documents, where non-parallel documents have missing entries corresponding to the vocabulary of the other language. The first step addresses this sparsity by formulating and solving a low-rank matrix completion problem to impute the missing values. This step leverages co-occurrence patterns both within and across languages, using a small set of parallel documents as a bridge. The second step applies standard Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) on the completed, dense matrix to learn a low-dimensional, shared semantic space. Experiments on a multilingual sentiment classification task show that TSL significantly outperforms several strong baselines, including CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, particularly when the amount of parallel data is limited.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Approach:** The central contribution—using matrix completion to explicitly model and fill missing cross-lingual information before learning a shared representation—is novel and well-motivated. Standard methods like CL-LSI implicitly treat these missing entries as zeros, which is a strong and often incorrect assumption. This paper's approach of treating it as a principled imputation problem is a clear conceptual improvement.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are convincing. TSL consistently and significantly outperforms four relevant baselines across 18 different cross-lingual classification tasks. The gains are not marginal, with improvements of up to 14.6% in accuracy over the in-domain baseline, demonstrating the method's effectiveness.

3.  **Robustness with Limited Parallel Data:** The analysis in Section 5.3 is a key strength. The paper shows that TSL maintains high performance even with a very small number of parallel documents (e.g., 200), while the performance of competing methods degrades substantially. This is a significant practical advantage, as large, high-quality parallel corpora can be expensive or unavailable for many language pairs. This finding strongly supports the claim that the matrix completion step effectively leverages the available parallel signal.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The methodology is presented logically, progressing from the problem setup to the two main steps and the optimization algorithm.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Justification of the Objective Function:** The objective function for matrix completion in Equation (2) contains regularization terms whose motivations are not well-explained and seem potentially counterintuitive.
    *   The `µ‖M‖1` term: For a non-negative matrix `M`, the L1 norm is simply the sum of its elements (`∑ij Mij`). This term thus penalizes the total "mass" of the completed matrix. While this could be a form of regularization, its role in "enforcing sparsity" on the *completed* matrix `M` is unclear, as the low-rank assumption already implies a dense (or at least non-sparse) structure. A clearer justification for this specific regularizer is needed.
    *   The `ρ∑c(Mij, 0)` term for `(i,j) ∈ Ω̂`: This term penalizes the squared value of the imputed entries, effectively pushing them towards zero. This seems to work in direct opposition to the goal of matrix completion, which is to fill in meaningful non-zero values based on the learned low-rank structure. While it can prevent overfitting, it appears to contradict the primary goal of imputation. How do the authors reconcile the trace norm minimization (which finds the best low-rank approximation) with a term that encourages the imputed values to be zero? A more detailed explanation of the interplay between `γ` and `ρ` would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** The proposed optimization algorithm requires computing an SVD of the full `t x d` matrix `M` in every iteration (for the shrinkage operator). Here, `d` is the size of the *unified* vocabulary, which can be very large (tens or hundreds of thousands). This is computationally expensive. The paper does not discuss the computational complexity or runtime of the proposed method, which is a significant omission. Can this method scale to large vocabularies and document collections? Are there more scalable optimization methods, perhaps based on stochastic or alternating minimization approaches, that could be used?

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The hyperparameters (γ, ρ, k) were selected based on a single task (EFB) and then fixed for all 18 tasks. While this demonstrates some robustness, it is unlikely to be optimal for all language pairs and domains. A discussion on the sensitivity of the model to these parameters, especially the crucial regularization parameters `γ` and `ρ`, would be beneficial.

4.  **Comparison to Modern Methods:** The baselines (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) are relevant but somewhat dated. The field of cross-lingual representation learning has largely moved towards methods based on bilingual word embeddings (e.g., using methods from Mikolov et al., 2013; Artetxe et al., 2017) or pre-trained multilingual models like mBERT. While a direct comparison might be out of scope, acknowledging these more recent lines of work and positioning the proposed method relative to them would provide better context. The paper's contribution is in the matrix-based paradigm, but its significance should be considered in the context of the entire field.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a solid paper with a novel idea, strong empirical results, and a particularly important finding regarding robustness to limited parallel data. The main contribution is the clever application of matrix completion to the cross-lingual setting, which provides a principled way to handle the disjoint feature spaces.

The primary weaknesses lie in the lack of clear motivation for parts of the objective function and the unaddressed concerns about computational scalability. Despite these points, the impressive empirical performance and the practical significance of the method's data efficiency make this a valuable contribution. The paper advances the state-of-the-art within the paradigm of matrix factorization methods for cross-lingual learning.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper's strengths, particularly the novel formulation and strong, data-efficient results, outweigh its weaknesses. The identified weaknesses can be addressed in a final version by providing better justification for the objective function and discussing the computational aspects.
