## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification. The core idea is to leverage unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to construct a concatenated document-term matrix, perform matrix completion to fill in missing values, and then apply Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to induce cross-lingual representations. The authors demonstrate through experiments on Amazon product review sentiment classification that their method outperforms several baselines, including TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, and performs well even with limited parallel data.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The paper positions itself within the broader landscape of cross-lingual learning, specifically addressing the challenge of bridging the language gap.  It acknowledges and contrasts its approach with existing methods that rely on machine translation (e.g., [17, 21, 22]) or direct cross-lingual representation learning using bilingual resources (e.g., [16, 10, 20, 15]).  The key distinction, as highlighted by the authors, is the efficient use of *limited* unlabeled parallel data coupled with matrix completion as a pre-processing step before LSI, differentiating it from methods that directly operate on the sparse concatenated matrix or require translation of all training data.  The references cited appear relevant and demonstrate an understanding of the field.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning is a reasonable and potentially effective approach. Matrix completion specifically addresses the issue of sparse data arising from limited parallel corpora.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over the baselines on the chosen datasets and tasks. The performance gain is significant, particularly given the limited amount of labeled target data used (100 documents).
* **Robustness to Data Size:** The study on the impact of the amount of unlabeled parallel data shows that the method is relatively robust, achieving good performance even with only 200 parallel documents, a significant advantage.
* **Clear Presentation of Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 provides a clear description of the projected gradient descent procedure, making the method reproducible.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a good grasp of the relevant literature and carefully position their work within it.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Theoretical Justification:** While the intuition behind matrix completion is solid, the paper lacks a more rigorous theoretical analysis of *why* this approach is beneficial for cross-lingual learning.  Why is low-rank assumption suitable for this cross-lingual document-term matrix? A deeper discussion of the underlying assumptions would strengthen the contribution.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The parameter tuning process is briefly described but lacks detail.  The selection of γ, ρ, and k seems crucial, and a more systematic exploration of their impact on performance would be valuable.  A sensitivity analysis would reveal if the performance is highly dependent on specific parameter settings.
* **Limited Dataset Diversity:** The experiments are conducted solely on Amazon product reviews. While this is a standard benchmark, the results might not generalize to other domains or text types. Demonstrating performance on a more diverse set of datasets would strengthen the claims of generalizability.
* **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the chosen baselines are relevant, the field has advanced since some of the cited work (e.g., [10]).  A comparison to more recent and potentially stronger cross-lingual representation learning methods, perhaps based on pre-trained multilingual language models (e.g., mBERT, XLM-RoBERTa), would provide a more compelling benchmark.
* **Optimization Details:** While Algorithm 1 is provided, details about the implementation are scarce (e.g., stopping criteria for the projected gradient descent).



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and effective approach to cross-lingual representation learning.
* The empirical results are strong and consistently demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
* The problem addressed is relevant and important in the NLP community.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a deeper theoretical analysis limits the understanding of *why* the method works.
* The limited dataset diversity raises concerns about generalizability.
* A comparison to stronger, more recent baselines is missing.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept** with minor revisions. The authors present a well-motivated and empirically validated method for cross-lingual representation learning. While the theoretical justification could be strengthened and the evaluation broadened, the novelty and effectiveness of the approach, along with the clear presentation, make it a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

*   **Expand the theoretical analysis:**  Discuss the assumptions underlying matrix completion in the context of cross-lingual learning.
*   **Provide more details on parameter tuning:**  Include a more systematic exploration of the impact of different parameters on performance.
*   **Evaluate on more diverse datasets:**  Include results on datasets beyond Amazon product reviews.
*   **Compare to stronger baselines:** Include more recent and state-of-the-art cross-lingual representation learning methods in the evaluation.
*   **Provide implementation details:** Specify stopping criteria and other relevant implementation details for reproducibility.
