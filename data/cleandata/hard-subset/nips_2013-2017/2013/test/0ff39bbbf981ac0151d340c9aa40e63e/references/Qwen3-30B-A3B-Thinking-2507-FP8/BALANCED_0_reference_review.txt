

# Review for "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that first fills missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix using matrix completion, then applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The approach builds on previous work like CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], and CL-OPCA [15], but differs by performing matrix completion before applying LSI, rather than applying these techniques directly to the sparse concatenated matrix. The authors evaluate their method on 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using the multilingual Amazon product reviews dataset, demonstrating consistent improvements over baseline methods.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a well-formulated convex optimization problem for matrix completion with a convergence proof (Theorem 1), addressing a key challenge in handling sparse bilingual document-term matrices. The proposed projected gradient descent algorithm is appropriately designed for the problem.

2. **Empirical Evaluation**: The experimental design is thorough, with 18 cross-lingual tasks across three language pairs (English-French, English-German, English-Japanese) and three product categories. The method consistently outperforms four baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA), with significant improvements (8.59-14.61% accuracy gains) across all tasks.

3. **Practical Significance**: The demonstration that the method works effectively with very small numbers of unlabeled parallel documents (as few as 200) is particularly valuable for low-resource language scenarios, addressing a key practical challenge in cross-lingual learning.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized with clear mathematical formulations and sufficient experimental details. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) and convergence analysis are particularly well-executed.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Recent Advances**: The paper compares against CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA but doesn't include more recent methods like those based on neural networks (e.g., multilingual BERT variants) that have become dominant in cross-lingual representation learning. This makes it difficult to assess the method's position relative to state-of-the-art.

2. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (matrix completion vs. LSI). It would be valuable to see how much of the improvement comes from the matrix completion step versus the overall two-step approach.

3. **Scalability Concerns**: While the paper mentions the matrix is "typically very large," it doesn't provide computational complexity analysis or empirical results on large-scale datasets. The projected gradient descent algorithm may not scale well to modern NLP tasks with millions of documents.

4. **Theoretical Limitations**: The paper assumes the document-term matrix is low-rank, but doesn't provide theoretical guarantees about when this assumption holds or how it might fail for certain language pairs. The choice of hyperparameters (γ, ρ, k) is also somewhat ad-hoc.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance**:
- Novel two-step approach that addresses a key limitation of previous methods (direct application of LSI to sparse matrices)
- Strong empirical results with consistent improvements across multiple tasks
- Demonstrates practical value by working well with minimal parallel data
- Well-executed technical contribution with proper convergence analysis

**Con Acceptance**:
- Limited comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods
- Insufficient exploration of theoretical limitations
- Lack of ablation studies to isolate the contribution of matrix completion
- Potential scalability issues not addressed for large-scale applications

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Update Baselines**: Include comparisons with recent neural cross-lingual methods (e.g., multilingual BERT, mBERT, XLM-R) to better position the contribution relative to current state-of-the-art.

2. **Add Ablation Studies**: Conduct experiments to isolate the contribution of matrix completion versus the overall two-step approach. For example, compare directly applying LSI to the completed matrix versus applying LSI to the original sparse matrix.

3. **Address Scalability**: Provide computational complexity analysis of the proposed method and report runtime or memory usage for larger datasets. Consider discussing potential scaling strategies.

4. **Expand Theoretical Analysis**: Discuss the conditions under which the low-rank assumption holds, and how the method might fail with certain language pairs (e.g., languages with very different grammatical structures).

5. **Clarify Novelty**: More explicitly differentiate the proposed approach from previous work, particularly emphasizing what makes matrix completion before LSI a fundamentally better approach than applying LSI directly.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound approach to cross-lingual text classification with strong empirical results. While the two-step method is novel and shows consistent improvements over previous methods, the limited comparison with more recent state-of-the-art techniques and lack of ablation studies make it difficult to assess the full significance of the contribution. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including comparisons with contemporary neural approaches and providing more detailed analysis of the method's components.

The work is solid and makes a valid contribution to cross-lingual learning, but it doesn't represent a major advance over the current state of the art. I recommend rejection, but with the understanding that the authors could substantially improve their work with the suggested revisions.
