Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses cross-language text classification by proposing a two-step method (TSL) to learn shared document representations from a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 performs matrix completion on a unified bilingual document-term matrix constructed from both languages, where parallel pairs are fully observed and non-parallel documents have missing entries for words from the other language. The objective is convex: trace norm (low rank) + ℓ1 (sparsity) + squared losses on observed and missing entries (with nonnegativity constraints). A projected gradient descent algorithm with singular value shrinkage is presented, along with a convergence argument based on non-expansiveness. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) on the completed matrix to derive a low-dimensional cross-lingual space; monolingual classifiers are then trained. Experiments on the multilingual Amazon Reviews dataset compare TSL to TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, showing consistent gains, including robustness when only a few parallel pairs are available.

This builds on long-standing strands of CLIR/CLTC work: CL-LSI [10], KCCA-based projections [20], OPCA [15], SCL pivots [16], and multi-view/co-training techniques [1,2,22]. The main departure is inserting matrix completion before LSI to impute cross-lingual co-occurrence structure, rather than applying LSI/KCCA/OPCA directly to sparse concatenations.

Strengths:
- Clear, intuitive formulation: casting bilingual concatenated bag-of-words as a partially observed matrix and recovering it with a convex low-rank+sparse objective is natural and well-motivated.
- Sound optimization: the relaxed problem is convex; the algorithm is a standard proximal scheme with shrinkage and nonnegativity projection, with a convergence argument (albeit high level).
- Empirical breadth on the classic Amazon dataset: 18 transfer directions, variance over 10 runs, and a study of performance vs. the number of parallel pairs.
- Practical value: TSL performs well even with scarce parallel data and limited labels in the target, a realistic constraint in some settings.
- The method is simple to implement with widely available primitives (SVD, PGD), and could be of interest to practitioners seeking lightweight cross-lingual representations without heavy supervision.

Weaknesses:
Quality
- Experimental baselines are outdated for NeurIPS standards. Missing comparisons to modern cross-lingual representation methods (aligned fastText/MUSE/VecMap; bilingual/multilingual sentence and word embeddings; multilingual transformers such as mBERT, XLM-R, LaBSE; translate-train/translate-test with strong NMT). These are de facto baselines for cross-lingual text classification and often outperform older CL-LSI/KCCA systems, sometimes without parallel corpora.
- Potential transductive bias: “all documents from the two languages and the additional unlabeled parallel documents” are used to learn representations. If this includes test documents, it should be made explicit and justified; comparable transductive settings should be ensured for all baselines (CL-KCCA appears to use only parallel data), or a non-transductive variant should be reported.
- Limited ablations: no analysis of the contribution of (i) trace norm vs. ℓ1 vs. nonnegativity; (ii) matrix completion without LSI; (iii) LSI on zero-filled matrix (beyond CL-LSI, which is slightly different); (iv) sensitivity to ρ, γ, μ, τ, stopping criteria. Runtime/scalability is not quantified, yet repeated SVDs can be costly for large vocabularies.
- Statistical testing is not reported; some improvements are large, but significance should be assessed.

Clarity
- The algorithmic description is mostly clear, but key practical details are missing: stopping criteria, SVD strategy (full vs. truncated, randomized), per-iteration cost, memory usage, and how large t and d are in practice.
- The construction of “unlabeled parallel reviews” in the used dataset should be clarified (source of alignments; whether machine translation is involved or native parallelism).

Originality
- The idea of low-rank completion of document-term matrices is known, as are nonnegative/trace-norm formulations and subsequent LSI. The novelty lies in their combination for cross-lingual representation learning using a specific masking scheme derived from parallel pairs. This is incremental relative to prior CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA and matrix completion literature.

Significance
- On a legacy dataset with pre-deep baselines, the gains are compelling. However, without comparisons to strong modern cross-lingual encoders and translate-based baselines, it is hard to assess impact for today’s community. The approach may still be attractive in low-resource or compute-constrained scenarios, but this positioning is not substantiated experimentally.

Questions for the authors:
- Do you use any target test documents during representation learning? If so, can you report results in a non-transductive setting to ensure fair comparison with CL-KCCA?
- How does TSL perform vs. translate-train or translate-test using a standard MT system? Versus aligned fastText or multilingual transformers (frozen and fine-tuned)?
- What are the stopping criteria and average runtime per task? Which SVD implementation is used?
- Can you provide ablations removing trace norm, ℓ1, and nonnegativity, and a variant that skips the LSI step?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include strong modern baselines (aligned CLWE, sentence embeddings, mBERT/XLM-R/LaBSE, translate-train/test).
- Control for transductive use of data and report both transductive and inductive results for all methods.
- Add ablations and sensitivity analyses; report statistical significance and compute/memory costs.
- Evaluate beyond sentiment and beyond English↔{FR, DE, JA}, ideally on more languages and tasks (topic classification, news categorization).
- Release code and scripts to enhance reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Simple, convex, and well-explained method with solid empirical gains over classic baselines.
- Robustness with few parallel pairs is appealing for low-resource settings.
- Clear algorithmic contribution that could be of practical interest.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing comparisons to contemporary cross-lingual methods severely limits conclusions about significance.
- Incremental novelty; core components (matrix completion + LSI) are standard.
- Possible transductive advantage and lack of ablations/efficiency analysis.

Recommendation: Reject in current form. The paper could become competitive with comprehensive modern baselines, non-transductive evaluations, ablations, and clearer scalability evidence.
