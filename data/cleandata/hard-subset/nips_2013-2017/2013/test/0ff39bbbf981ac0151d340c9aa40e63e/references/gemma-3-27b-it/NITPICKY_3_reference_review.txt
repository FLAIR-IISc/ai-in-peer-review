## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, leveraging unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The core idea involves matrix completion to fill a concatenated document-term matrix with missing entries, followed by Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to derive low-dimensional representations. The authors demonstrate improvements over several baselines on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews.

While the overall approach has merit and addresses a relevant problem, the paper suffers from a significant number of shortcomings, ranging from superficial technical details to inconsistencies in presentation and a lack of thorough evaluation. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** Cross-lingual learning is a vital area, and reducing reliance on labeled target language data is a desirable goal.
*   **Intuitive Approach:** The idea of utilizing matrix completion to bridge the language gap by leveraging parallel data is conceptually sound.
*   **Empirical Results:** The reported improvements over the baseline methods, particularly TBOW, suggest a positive contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigorous Justification for Algorithm 1:** The presented projected gradient descent algorithm is described without sufficient detail regarding parameter selection (beyond mentioning a search procedure). The claims of convergence (Theorem 1) rely heavily on cited work [11] without providing sufficient context *within the paper* to assess its applicability to this specific matrix completion formulation. The authors state τ ∈ (0,min(2, 2ρ)), but the derivation or explanation for this bound is missing.
*   **Missing Details in Matrix Completion Formulation:** Equation (2) introduces regularization terms but lacks clear justification for the chosen loss functions (least squares) and their parameters (γ, ρ). The rationale behind the ℓ1 norm regularization and its coefficient (µ) is similarly absent. Why these specific choices, and how sensitive are the results to them?
*   **Shallow Experimental Analysis:** While the results demonstrate improvement, the experimental setup is not thoroughly analyzed. The parameter selection process is described vaguely ("running the algorithm 3 times"). The choice of k=50 for CL-LSI/CL-OPCA and k=100 for CL-KCCA feels arbitrary and lacks justification. A more detailed ablation study varying these parameters would strengthen the findings.
*   **Inconsistent Notation:** The use of Ω and Ω̂ to denote observed and missing indices is standard, but then “Ω̂” is later used in Equation (4) to denote a different indicator matrix. This creates confusion.
*   **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** The related work section is comprehensive in *listing* papers, but lacks critical analysis. For instance, the authors claim their approach only needs a “small set” of parallel documents while others require translation of all training data. This distinction isn’t fully substantiated – translation is a pre-processing step; the *core* difference lies in the representation learning technique itself.
*   **Redundant Statements and Verbosity:** The paper contains numerous instances of stating the obvious ("learning from this partially observed matrix directly…will lose a lot of information") which add to the length without contributing to clarity.
*   **Formatting and Typos:** Several minor formatting inconsistencies exist (e.g., inconsistent spacing around equations). There are instances of awkward phrasing and grammatical errors throughout the paper.
*   **Algorithm 1’s Initialization:** Initializing M as the rank-1 approximation of M0 is not explained. Why this specific initialization, and how does it affect convergence?

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is novel and addresses a challenging problem.
*   Empirical results show a performance gain over established baselines.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of rigorous technical details and justification for key algorithmic choices.
*   Insufficient experimental analysis and parameter tuning.
*   Inconsistent notation and writing quality.
*   The significance of the improvement, while present, isn't substantial enough to overcome the technical flaws.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising idea but falls short of NIPS standards due to its lack of technical rigor, insufficient experimental detail, and stylistic weaknesses. The authors demonstrate a proof-of-concept, but significant work is needed to establish the robustness and generalizability of their approach. 

**Recommendation:** **Reject**. While the idea is potentially valuable, the paper requires substantial revision and further investigation before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. I recommend the authors focus on providing a more thorough theoretical analysis, conducting a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, and addressing the clarity and consistency issues throughout the manuscript.
