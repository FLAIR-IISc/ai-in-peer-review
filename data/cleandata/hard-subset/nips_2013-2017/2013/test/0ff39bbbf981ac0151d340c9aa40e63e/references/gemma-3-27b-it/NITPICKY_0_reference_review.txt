## Review of “A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning”

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, utilizing matrix completion to fill missing values in a concatenated document-term matrix followed by Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI). The authors evaluate their method on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, demonstrating improvements over several baselines. While the idea of leveraging matrix completion within a cross-lingual learning framework is intriguing, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet cumulatively substantial, flaws in presentation, technical detail, and experimental rigor.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:** The core concept – addressing the cross-lingual gap by completing a concatenated document-term matrix – builds upon existing work in cross-lingual representation learning, specifically drawing parallels to methods using parallel data like CL-LSI and CL-KCCA.  The paper positions itself as requiring fewer parallel documents than translation-based approaches. This positioning is reasonable, but not sufficiently highlighted or contrasted with the computational costs of matrix completion for very large vocabularies. 

**Strengths:** The idea of utilizing matrix completion to bridge the language gap is a conceptually sound approach. The empirical results *do* demonstrate some improvement over the baselines, suggesting the method holds some promise. The inclusion of an algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is appreciated, although its clarity is questionable (see below). The extensive related work section provides a good overview of the field.

**Weaknesses:** The paper is riddled with issues that, while individually minor, create a concerning overall impression of lack of meticulousness.

*   **Technical Nuances & Justification:** The choice of the ℓ1 norm in the matrix completion objective is not adequately justified. While sparsity is desired, the connection to the specific problem isn’t made clear.  The relaxation of the rank function to the trace norm is standard, but lacks a citation to foundational work in this area (e.g., Fazel et al., 2002). The selection of the least squared loss function c(x,y) feels arbitrary; a justification for this choice over other loss functions is absent. The step size τ in Algorithm 1 is mentioned as being determined, but the reasoning behind this choice or a reference to its derivation is missing.
*   **Algorithm Description & Convergence:** Algorithm 1 lacks sufficient detail. Specifically, the “rank-1 approximation of M0” used for initialization is ill-defined.  What rank-1 approximation is being used (SVD, NMF)? The convergence criteria are not specified – when does the algorithm terminate? The claim of convergence proof rests on Lemma 3, but this lemma is stated without full justification, assuming properties of the operators without explicit proof.
*   **Experimental Setup:**  The parameter selection process is insufficiently detailed. Stating that the parameter setting was determined using EFB is insufficient; the range of values tested for γ, ρ, and k should be explicitly stated.  The rationale for choosing a fixed k=50 for CL-LSI/OPCA and k=100 for CL-KCCA is weak. Why these specific values?  A more systematic parameter search or cross-validation procedure is expected.  The use of only 100 labeled target documents feels unusually small, potentially skewing results. The standard deviation reported in Table 1 is important, but a more detailed statistical significance test (e.g., paired t-test) is required to claim significant outperformance.
*   **Clarity & Presentation:** The writing quality is uneven.  Phrases like “across the range of different np values” are clunky. The description of the matrix completion problem in section 3.1 is overly verbose and difficult to follow. The notation is inconsistent (e.g., using both Ω and Ω̂, and inconsistently defining their contents). Figure 1 & 2 are referenced, but not included in the submission.
* **Minor Errors**: There are several minor inconsistencies throughout the paper, such as inconsistent use of terminology and formatting issues. For example, the references are not formatted consistently.

**Arguments Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel application of matrix completion to cross-lingual learning.
*   Empirical results show some improvement over baselines.
*   Comprehensive related work section.

**Con:**

*   Lack of technical rigor and justification for choices.
*   Insufficient detail in algorithm description and experimental setup.
*   Poor clarity and presentation.
*   Cumulatively, a large number of minor flaws suggest a lack of carefulness.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially interesting idea, the paper suffers from significant shortcomings in technical detail, experimental rigor, and presentation. The numerous minor flaws detract from the overall quality of the work. While the results are encouraging, they are not presented convincingly enough to warrant acceptance.  I recommend **rejection**. The authors should address the identified weaknesses – providing justifications for key choices, clarifying the algorithm, improving the experimental setup, and thoroughly revising the writing – before resubmitting. A more substantial theoretical analysis and a more rigorous experimental evaluation are crucial.
