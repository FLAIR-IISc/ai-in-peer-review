

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification using unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The approach constructs a concatenated document-term matrix with missing values for non-parallel documents, fills in missing entries via matrix completion, and then applies LSI to generate cross-lingual representations. The authors position their work as distinct from prior approaches (like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) that directly apply dimensionality reduction on the partially observed matrix, arguing that their matrix completion step better captures cross-lingual co-occurrence patterns.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a clear technical contribution by introducing a matrix completion step before LSI, which is novel in the context of cross-lingual representation learning. The theoretical foundation with the convex optimization formulation and convergence proof (Theorem 1) is sound and well-executed.

2. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks across three languages (French, German, Japanese) and three product categories (Books, DVD, Music). The results consistently show that the proposed method (TSL) outperforms four baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) by 8.59-14.61% on average.

3. The paper demonstrates the practical advantage of the method by showing it requires significantly fewer unlabeled parallel documents (as few as 200) to achieve good performance, which is particularly valuable for low-resource language scenarios.

4. The writing is clear and well-organized, with appropriate mathematical formalization and detailed experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art:** The paper compares only with four methods (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA, TBOW), but fails to include more recent neural approaches to cross-lingual representation learning (e.g., multilingual BERT variants, XLM-R, or recent work on unsupervised cross-lingual transfer). This omission weakens the significance claim, as the paper doesn't demonstrate how their approach compares to the current state-of-the-art.

2. **Theoretical justification for low-rank assumption:** While the paper assumes the document-term matrix is low-rank, it doesn't sufficiently justify this assumption for cross-lingual scenarios. The connection to the empirical success of low-rank matrix completion in other NLP tasks (e.g., word embedding) is not adequately established.

3. **Lack of failure analysis:** The paper doesn't discuss any failure cases or limitations of the approach. For instance, how does the method perform when the languages are very dissimilar (e.g., English to Japanese vs. English to French)? The paper only shows positive results, which is insufficient for a strong scientific contribution.

4. **Computational considerations:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity or scalability of the matrix completion approach for large-scale datasets. The projected gradient descent algorithm is presented, but there's no discussion of runtime or memory requirements compared to other methods.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Add a comparison with at least two recent neural approaches to cross-lingual representation learning (e.g., XLM-R or mBERT fine-tuned on cross-lingual tasks) to better establish the significance of the proposed method.

2. Expand the discussion of the low-rank assumption with empirical evidence (e.g., by showing the singular value decay in the observed document-term matrix) and cite relevant work that supports this assumption in cross-lingual contexts.

3. Include a section discussing limitations and failure cases, such as when parallel data is scarce for certain language pairs or when languages have very different grammatical structures.

4. Add a brief analysis of computational complexity and runtime comparisons with the baseline methods to help readers understand the practical trade-offs.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear novelty in the two-step matrix completion + LSI framework. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate practical advantages over existing methods. However, the paper's significance is undermined by the lack of comparison with recent neural approaches, which represent the current state-of-the-art in cross-lingual representation learning. Without this comparison, the paper's contribution appears limited to a specific class of traditional methods rather than advancing the field as a whole.

While the technical contribution is valid, the paper falls short in demonstrating how it compares to the most relevant recent work. The significance criterion is not fully satisfied, as the paper does not clearly establish how this method advances the state of the art beyond traditional approaches when compared to modern neural methods.

**Recommendation:**
Reject (with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the above concerns, particularly the comparison with recent neural approaches). The paper has strong technical merit but needs to better position itself within the current landscape of cross-lingual representation learning to be competitive for NeurIPS.
