This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification. The core idea is to leverage unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to construct a unified document-term matrix, fill in missing entries via matrix completion, and then apply latent semantic indexing (LSI) to obtain cross-lingual representations. The authors demonstrate strong performance on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, outperforming several baselines.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a challenging problem in NLP. Cross-language text classification is indeed important, and the problem of leveraging limited labeled target language data with abundant source language data is well-defined. The approach is grounded in established techniques (matrix completion, LSI) and attempts to combine them in a novel way to bridge the cross-lingual gap. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with comparisons to several relevant baselines and a sensitivity analysis regarding the amount of parallel data. The presentation of results is clear, with tables and figures effectively summarizing the findings. The related work section is comprehensive, covering various approaches to cross-lingual learning, including translation-based methods and those relying on bilingual resources. The optimization algorithm is described in detail, including a convergence analysis.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. First, the novelty of the approach is somewhat incremental. Matrix completion and LSI are established techniques, and combining them in this specific context feels more like an application than a fundamental breakthrough. The authors acknowledge similarity to existing methods like CL-LSI and CL-OPCA, but the justification for *why* their two-step approach with matrix completion is superior isn't entirely convincing beyond the empirical results. The benefits of matrix completion aren't fully explained in terms of capturing nuanced relationships beyond simply filling in missing data. A more detailed discussion of the underlying assumptions and theoretical advantages of this two-step approach would strengthen the paper.

Second, the hyperparameter tuning process isn’t fully transparent. While the authors state they used a grid search on the EFB task, the specific parameter ranges explored and the rationale for selecting the final parameters aren’t detailed enough for reproducibility. This is particularly concerning for parameters like gamma, rho and k.

Third, while the experiments are comprehensive, the dataset (Amazon reviews) is somewhat limited in scope.  Performance on other datasets with different characteristics (e.g., news articles, scientific papers) would provide a more robust assessment of the method’s generalizability.  The experiments primarily focus on sentiment classification; evaluating the method on other cross-lingual classification tasks (e.g., topic categorization) would broaden the scope.

Fourth, the motivation for using the specific loss function and regularization terms in the matrix completion step (Equation 2) could be better justified. While standard choices, a more detailed discussion of *why* these specific functions are appropriate for this problem would be valuable.

Finally, the writing quality, while generally clear, occasionally suffers from redundancy. Some sentences and paragraphs repeat ideas already presented, making the paper longer than necessary.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong empirical performance consistently outperforms several strong baselines.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation with a sensitivity analysis.
*   Clear presentation of the method and results.
*   Well-motivated problem and comprehensive related work.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Incremental novelty – combining established techniques without a strong theoretical justification.
*   Lack of transparency in hyperparameter tuning.
*   Limited dataset scope potentially affecting generalizability.
*   Insufficient justification for the choices made in the matrix completion step.
*   Occasional redundancy in writing.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid empirical contribution to cross-language text classification. While the novelty is incremental, the consistent performance gains and thorough experimentation are commendable. However, the lack of deeper theoretical justification and transparency in the experimental setup raise concerns. 

**Recommendation:**

Reject. While the paper demonstrates promising results, the weaknesses regarding novelty, justification and experimental detail prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. I encourage the authors to address these concerns in a revised version, particularly by providing a more compelling theoretical explanation for the benefits of their two-step approach and improving the transparency of their experimental setup. A broader evaluation on diverse datasets would also be beneficial.
