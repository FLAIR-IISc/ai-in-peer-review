- Proposes a two-step method: matrix completion on a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix with missing entries, followed by LSI to learn cross-lingual representations.  
- Evaluated on 18 cross-lingual sentiment tasks (EN/FR/DE/JP; Books/DVD/Music) and shows substantial gains over CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA, and TBOW.  
- Method is robust to small amounts of unlabeled parallel data (performance holds with as few as ~200 parallel documents).  
- Strong technical formulation: clear optimization problem (eq.2) and a convergence proof (Theorem 1) for the projected gradient descent algorithm.  
- Robust experimental design with comprehensive results and analysis of the effect of parallel data size.  
- Clear presentation: well-organized paper, precise math, and reproducible experimental setup; algorithm description is clear.  
- Lacks theoretical justification for the key low-rank assumption—insufficient evidence that the incomplete matrix is low-rank.  
- Limits of applicability not well explored: experiments only on Amazon reviews, leaving domain generalization unclear.  
- Comparison to related work is superficial; authors do not clearly justify why completion+LSI is theoretically superior to directly applying LSI/KCCA/OPCA.  
- No computational complexity or scalability analysis; matrix completion may not scale with large vocabularies or document counts.  
- Motivation for combining ℓ1 (sparsity) and trace-norm (low-rank) regularization is not fully explained.  
- Distinction from CL-LSI (and similar methods that use a concatenated matrix) could be clearer; some technical terms are under-explained.  
- Novelty: applying matrix completion prior to LSI for cross-lingual representation appears new in this context.  
- Novelty concerns: matrix completion has been used in NLP before, and the two-step approach may be seen as a straightforward combination of known techniques.  
- Missed or incomplete citation of recent related work (e.g., recent NIPS paper using matrix completion for cross-lingual tasks).  
- Significance: addresses an important practical problem (low-resource languages) and yields strong empirical improvements, especially in low-data regimes.  
- Significance limits: narrow experimental scope and unclear integration into broader NLP pipelines reduce perceived impact.  
- Overall assessment: solid technical contribution with strong empirical results and theoretical analysis, but needs better theoretical justification, broader comparisons, and scalability discussion.  
- Recommendation: Accept — merits inclusion due to empirical gains, practical value, and sound analysis despite limited novelty and some unresolved issues.
