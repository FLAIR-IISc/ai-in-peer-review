Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses cross-language text classification by learning shared document representations from limited unlabeled parallel data. The main idea is a two-step procedure: (1) construct a unified document-term matrix over the concatenated vocabularies of two languages, where parallel document pairs are fully observed and non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language; fill the missing entries via a convex matrix completion problem with nuclear-norm (low-rank) and ℓ1 (sparsity/scale) regularization under nonnegativity constraints; (2) apply latent semantic indexing (LSI) via SVD to the completed matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations, on which standard monolingual classifiers are trained.

This relates to prior cross-lingual representation learning methods that exploit parallel/comparable data or bilingual lexicons, including CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], CL-OPCA [15], multilingual topic models [19], and pivot-based SCL [16]. Unlike these, which operate directly on concatenated/paired matrices, this work proposes first imputing the missing entries via matrix completion to better leverage cross-lingual co-occurrence structure and then applying LSI. The optimization uses a projected gradient update on the smooth part, nuclear-norm soft-thresholding (shrinkage), and nonnegativity projection, with a non-expansiveness-based convergence argument following Ma et al. [11].

Empirical evaluation is on the multilingual Amazon reviews dataset [16] across 18 source-target/category tasks (English, French, German, Japanese), with 100 labeled target documents and 2000 unlabeled parallel pairs. The proposed method (TSL) is compared to a target-only bag-of-words baseline (TBOW), CL-LSI [10], CL-OPCA [15], and CL-KCCA [20]. Reported results show TSL generally outperforms all baselines and remains robust when the number of parallel pairs is reduced.

Quality
- Formulation: The matrix completion objective is convex: γ||M||* + µ||M||1 + 0.5||PΩ(M−M0)||F^2 + 0.5ρ||PΩ̂(M)||F^2 subject to M ≥ 0. The relaxation from rank to nuclear norm is standard; the quadratic losses on observed and (weighted) missing entries are reasonable (the latter discourages arbitrary imputations). Under nonnegativity, the ℓ1 term reduces to a linear penalty, which is smooth with gradient µE. The modeling assumption that the unified document-term matrix admits a useful low-rank approximation is standard in LSI-style methods, though exact low rank is not realistic; the approach seeks a low-rank approximation.
- Algorithm: The iterative scheme applies a gradient step on the smooth part, nuclear-norm shrinkage, and a nonnegativity projection. Each operator is non-expansive; the composition is non-expansive. The provided lemmas establish non-expansiveness under τ < min(2, 2/ρ). However, the analysis does not fully justify that fixed points of this composition correspond to minimizers of the objective with both nuclear norm and an indicator of the nonnegative orthant. In general, the proximal operator of a sum of nonsmooth functions is not the composition of their proximals; further justification (e.g., via DRS/ADMM or a known projected proximal-gradient variant with convergence guarantees) is needed to claim convergence to an optimizer of (2), not just to a fixed point. As written, Theorem 1 cites [11, Thm. 4], which pertains to nuclear-norm minimization without additional constraints; extending it to include the projection step requires more care.
- Implementation details: The algorithm requires repeated SVDs; practical scalability hinges on efficient truncated SVD and stopping criteria, which are not detailed. Hyperparameters (γ, ρ, k) are tuned on one task and transferred to others; µ is fixed to 1e-6. Sensitivity to µ and runtime/iteration counts are not reported. An ablation comparing “matrix completion + SVM” vs “matrix completion + LSI + SVM” would clarify the contribution of step 2.
- Experiments: The experimental protocol (18 tasks, 10 random splits, averages and standard deviations) is sound. The comparisons cover representative classic baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA). Details of KCCA (kernel choice, regularization) are not specified; this can affect performance. The method consistently outperforms others and is robust with fewer parallel pairs, according to the reported averages. Statistical significance testing is not discussed.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The optimization problem, gradient, and operators are specified. Algorithm 1 is explicit. The convergence section is readable, though, as noted, the optimality claim needs tightening. Some details are missing: computational complexity per iteration, stopping criteria, SVD implementation, and KCCA settings. A brief discussion of why LSI after completion adds value beyond the low-rank completion would help.

Originality
The combination of matrix completion on a bilingual unified document-term matrix with subsequent LSI for cross-lingual representation appears novel relative to CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA and SCL. The idea of imputing missing cross-lingual features from sparse parallel supervision is a distinct contribution. The optimization is a straightforward adaptation of known proximal/shrinkage techniques with a nonnegativity constraint.

Significance
If the empirical gains are robust, the approach is a simple, general alternative to established cross-lingual representation methods, especially when only small parallel corpora are available. The practicality depends on computational cost and availability of even a small parallel set. The work advances the state of the art over the compared classic baselines on the chosen dataset. Broader impact relative to newer cross-lingual embedding or multilingual transformer methods is not assessed.

Pros
- Clear formulation leveraging parallel data to connect vocabularies and impute missing features.
- Convex objective; simple iterative algorithm using standard operators.
- Strong empirical improvements over CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA across many tasks.
- Robust performance with very limited parallel data.

Cons
- Convergence proof does not rigorously establish convergence to a minimizer of the stated problem with both nuclear norm and nonnegativity; additional theory is needed.
- Computational scalability and runtime are not discussed; repeated SVDs can be expensive.
- Missing experimental ablations (e.g., without LSI; different ρ/µ; sensitivity to k).
- KCCA configuration details are lacking; fairness of that baseline is hard to assess.
- Limited discussion on when the low-rank assumption is appropriate and potential failure modes.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide a rigorous convergence guarantee (or a reference) for proximal-gradient with both nuclear norm and nonnegativity, beyond non-expansiveness, or alternatively use a known convergent scheme (e.g., DRS/ADMM)?
- What are the computational costs (iterations, time, matrix sizes), and do you use truncated SVD?
- How sensitive are results to µ and ρ? Why set µ so small?
- What kernels and regularization were used for KCCA?
- How much does step 2 (LSI) improve over using M* directly?

Recommendation
The paper presents a conceptually simple and empirically effective method with clear novelty over classic baselines. The main concern is the incomplete convergence/optimality justification and limited reporting on computational aspects and ablations. Addressing these would strengthen the technical soundness. Overall, the contribution is relevant to NeurIPS topics on representation learning and cross-lingual transfer.
