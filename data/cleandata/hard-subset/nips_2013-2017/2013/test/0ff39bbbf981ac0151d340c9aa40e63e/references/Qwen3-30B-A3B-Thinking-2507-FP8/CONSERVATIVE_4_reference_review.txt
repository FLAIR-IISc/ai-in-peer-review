

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that first constructs a bilingual document-term matrix with missing values for non-parallel documents, then fills in missing entries using matrix completion, and finally applies LSI to produce cross-lingual representations. The authors claim this approach significantly outperforms existing methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA on Amazon sentiment classification tasks.

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

The paper builds on the well-established approach of using bilingual resources for cross-lingual learning, which has been extensively studied in NIPS and other venues (e.g., [10, 15, 20] cited in the paper). The authors' key innovation is using matrix completion to fill missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix before applying LSI, rather than applying LSI directly to the incomplete matrix as in CL-LSI.

## Strengths

1. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a coherent presentation of the methodology.
2. The experimental design is reasonably thorough, with extensive evaluation across 18 cross-lingual tasks.
3. The method shows consistent improvements over baseline methods in the reported experiments.
4. The paper provides a detailed convergence analysis for the proposed optimization algorithm.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The most significant weakness is the absence of comparison with neural approaches that have become the standard for cross-lingual tasks in recent years (e.g., mBERT, XLM, XLM-R). The paper only compares with older, non-neural methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA. For a NIPS paper, this is a major oversight, as the field has moved significantly toward neural approaches for cross-lingual tasks.

2. **Exaggerated performance claims**: The reported performance improvements (up to 14.61% over baselines) are unusually large for a representation learning method. The paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these improvements, making it difficult to assess whether the gains are meaningful or simply due to random variation in the data splits.

3. **Unjustified theoretical claims**: The paper claims that the matrix completion approach "automatically capture important and robust low-rank information based on the word co-occurrence patterns," but provides no theoretical justification for why this should work better than existing approaches for cross-lingual tasks. The low-rank assumption for document-term matrices is already used in CL-LSI, so the paper doesn't convincingly argue why matrix completion would provide additional value.

4. **Insufficient validation**: The paper only tests on the Amazon product review dataset with a limited number of languages (English, French, German, Japanese). It doesn't demonstrate how the method would scale to more languages or to more complex tasks beyond sentiment classification.

5. **Overstated claims about data efficiency**: While the paper claims the method "needs much less parallel documents," the experiments only test with 200-2000 parallel documents, which is actually a relatively large amount for many practical applications. The paper doesn't show performance with extremely small amounts of parallel data (e.g., <100 documents), which would be more compelling for the claim.

## Quality Assessment

The technical approach is sound but not particularly novel. The matrix completion formulation is a standard convex optimization problem that has been well-studied in the literature (e.g., [4, 11]). The paper's contribution appears to be an application of a known technique to a specific problem, rather than a novel method. The experimental results are not sufficiently rigorous to support the strong claims made about performance improvements.

## Originality Assessment

The core idea of using matrix completion for cross-lingual representation learning is not particularly original. The paper cites related work (CL-LSI, etc.), but fails to clearly distinguish its contribution from these existing methods. The authors claim their method "differs" from CL-LSI by first filling missing entries with matrix completion, but CL-LSI already uses a similar dual-language matrix approach. The paper doesn't provide convincing evidence that this small modification leads to significant improvements.

## Significance Assessment

The significance of the work is limited. The paper addresses a well-studied problem, but doesn't advance the state-of-the-art in a meaningful way. The field of cross-lingual learning has moved toward neural approaches, and the paper's focus on traditional matrix-based methods makes it less relevant to current research directions. The claimed performance improvements over older methods don't translate to meaningful gains over contemporary state-of-the-art approaches.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it presents an incremental improvement over existing methods without sufficient validation against the current state-of-the-art. The lack of comparison with neural approaches (which have become the standard for cross-lingual tasks) is particularly problematic for a NIPS submission. The paper's claims about performance improvements are overstated without proper statistical validation, and the novelty of the approach is limited.

The authors should consider comparing their method to recent neural approaches (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R) and providing more rigorous statistical validation of their results before resubmitting to a venue like NIPS.
