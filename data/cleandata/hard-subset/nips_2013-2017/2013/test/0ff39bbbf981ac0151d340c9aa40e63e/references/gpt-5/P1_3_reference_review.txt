Summary
The paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-lingual text classification using a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. Step 1 formulates a matrix completion problem on a unified document-term matrix built from concatenated vocabularies of two languages. Parallel document pairs are treated as fully observed rows (both languages’ features present), while monolingual documents have missing entries for the other language’s features. The completion objective is convex: a trace-norm term (to encourage low rank), an ℓ1 term (sparsity; simplified to a linear term due to nonnegativity), squared losses on observed entries (to denoise), and a Tikhonov-like penalty on missing entries (to avoid overfitting to observed entries), with M ≥ 0. The authors solve it with a projected gradient–shrinkage scheme: a gradient step on the smooth part, followed by singular value thresholding for the trace norm and a projection onto the nonnegative orthant. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual document representations. Linear SVMs are then trained. Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews (EN, FR, DE, JA; 18 transfer directions across 3 domains) show that the method (TSL) improves over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, and is relatively robust to having as few as 200 parallel documents.

Relation to prior work
The paper relates to translation-based transfer, multi-view learning, cross-lingual representation learning via LSI/OPCA/KCCA, SCL-based pivot features, and multilingual topic models. The closest baselines are CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, which operate directly on concatenated/paired representations; the novelty here is to first complete the partially observed concatenated matrix via nuclear-norm-based recovery before applying LSI. This is a plausible extension of prior CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA lines.

Strengths
- Clear modeling idea: leverage parallel pairs to tie vocabularies, then propagate cross-language co-occurrence via a convex low-rank completion with nonnegativity and sparsity.
- Simple, resource-light (needs few parallel pairs), and broadly applicable.
- Empirical coverage: 18 transfer settings across three domains and three target languages; consistent gains over several strong classical baselines; robustness when reducing the number of parallel pairs.
- The formulation is convex (after relaxation), and the algorithm is conceptually straightforward.
- Inclusion of a convergence argument and algorithmic details (operator definitions, step-size conditions).

Weaknesses and questions
- Optimization rigor: The algorithm applies shrinkage (prox for nuclear norm) followed by projection onto the nonnegative orthant after a gradient step on the smooth term. This amounts to a forward–backward splitting with two nonsmooth terms (nuclear norm and indicator of the nonnegative cone). The composition of two proximal operators is not, in general, the proximal operator of the sum; the brief non-expansiveness argument and Theorem 1 do not establish convergence to the minimizer of (2). A more principled approach (e.g., projected proximal gradient with a single nonsmooth term, Dykstra-like proximal splitting, or ADMM) and/or a rigorous proof that the proposed iteration solves (2) is needed.
- Computational aspects are under-specified: no complexity analysis, number of iterations, partial SVD strategy, wall-clock times, or memory footprint. Nuclear-norm methods can be costly on large t × d matrices typical of bag-of-words.
- Ablations are missing: impact of each regularizer (γ, µ, ρ), the nonnegativity constraint, and the two-step design (e.g., performance without matrix completion, or without the LSI step). In particular, why not learn the low-dimensional factors directly (e.g., NMF or constrained matrix factorization) rather than completion + LSI?
- Baseline fairness and detail: CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA configurations are only briefly described; it is unclear whether they were granted access to the same information (e.g., how non-parallel documents are handled, weighting/normalization, and whether CL-LSI was run on exactly the same “dual” rows for parallel pairs and partial rows for others). Hyperparameters were tuned on a single task and transferred; while this favors simplicity, some baselines may be more sensitive to k or kernel choices.
- Generality and breadth: Only sentiment analysis on Amazon reviews; no results on other cross-lingual tasks (e.g., topic classification, CLIR) or other datasets. No error analysis.
- Statistical analysis: mean and standard deviation are reported, but no significance tests.
- Presentation issues: references are incomplete; several entries lack years/pages; some typos and minor notational imprecision (e.g., “ICCL-ACL”, “ℓ1 norm is equivalent to a smooth linear function” deserves a brief justification tied to nonnegativity).

Quality
- The modeling idea is sound and well-motivated. Empirical claims are plausible and reasonably supported by results on a standard benchmark.
- Theoretical contribution is limited: the convergence claim, as stated, is not fully convincing for the stated constrained objective. The algorithm is likely useful in practice but requires either a corrected derivation (e.g., ADMM) or a stronger proof.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-structured. The construction of M0 and the roles of Ω and Ω̂ are explained. The algorithm is presented cleanly.
- Missing implementation details (stopping criteria, SVD strategy, normalization) and baseline configurations detract from reproducibility.

Originality
- Moderate. The combination of nuclear-norm completion on concatenated bilingual doc-term matrices followed by LSI is, to my knowledge, novel relative to CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA and SCL. However, it is a relatively straightforward composition of known tools.

Significance
- For classic cross-lingual text classification settings with limited parallel data, the gains and robustness are encouraging and could be useful to practitioners. The impact would be stronger with broader task coverage, stronger theoretical backing, and more complete comparisons/ablations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a principled optimization scheme and proof for the constrained problem (e.g., ADMM splitting M into variables for nuclear norm and nonnegativity), or justify rigorously that the current operator converges to an optimizer of (2).
- Report runtime, iteration counts, and scalability (partial SVD, approximation rank) versus baselines.
- Add ablations: remove LSI, remove completion (i.e., CL-LSI on the same partially observed matrix with zeros), vary/omit µ, ρ, nonnegativity; sensitivity to k.
- Clarify preprocessing: TF/IDF per language vs. joint, length normalization, vocabulary pruning.
- Expand baselines if possible (e.g., SCL variants, bilingual topic models) and ensure comparable use of parallel data.
- Add significance testing and limited qualitative/error analysis.
- Fix and complete references.

Pro acceptance arguments
- Simple, resource-efficient method with consistent empirical gains and robustness to few parallel pairs.
- Convex formulation and interpretable objective.
- Addresses an important cross-lingual setting where labeled target data is scarce.

Con acceptance arguments
- Theoretical convergence to the exact stated objective is not convincingly established.
- Limited experimental breadth and missing ablations/reporting make it hard to assess where the gains come from and how scalable it is.
- Moderate novelty over established CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA pipelines.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject (borderline). With a corrected/justified optimization algorithm, more thorough experimental analysis (ablations, runtimes), and broader comparisons, this could be a solid contribution.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
