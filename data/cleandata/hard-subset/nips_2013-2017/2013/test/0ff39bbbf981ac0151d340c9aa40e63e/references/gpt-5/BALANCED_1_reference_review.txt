Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles cross-language text classification by learning a shared representation from a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. The core idea is a two-step pipeline: (1) build a unified document-term matrix over the concatenated vocabularies of two languages, where non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language; fill the missing entries via a convex matrix completion objective (trace norm + ℓ1 + squared loss, with nonnegativity); (2) apply LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations, on which a monolingual classifier is trained.

The approach is related to cross-lingual representation learning via parallel/comparable data (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, multilingual topic models), multi-view methods (co-training/co-classification), and translated learning. The novelty is to explicitly treat the concatenated bilingual document-term matrix as partially observed and to complete it with a nuclear-norm-regularized objective before LSI. The optimization uses a projected gradient method with singular value thresholding and nonnegativity projection, with a convergence argument based on non-expansiveness.

Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews (EN/FR/DE/JA, Books/DVD/Music) compare the proposed method (TSL) to TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA. TSL is reported to outperform baselines on most or all of 18 transfer tasks, and to remain robust when only 200 parallel pairs are available.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and intuitive formulation: unify bilingual vocabularies, use parallel pairs to “stitch” spaces by completing missing entries, then denoise via LSI.
- Uses relatively little parallel data and shows robustness as the amount of parallel data decreases.
- Solid empirical gains over several classical cross-lingual methods across many language/category pairs.
- Includes an optimization algorithm with a clear proximal-style structure and a convergence argument; the step-size condition is explicit.
- Experimental protocol fixes hyperparameters chosen on one task and reuses them across tasks, which reduces overfitting to each transfer.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty relative to existing representation learning with parallel data is moderate; the two-step “completion + LSI” is an incremental twist on nuclear-norm-regularized denoising followed by SVD, akin to soft-impute then LSI. The paper would benefit from a deeper comparison to one-step formulations (e.g., missing-data LSI/PMF) or joint objectives.
- Missing or outdated baselines: no comparison to cross-lingual structural correspondence learning (SCL), bilingual word embeddings/VecMap, or modern multilingual encoders (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R, LASER/LaBSE) in zero-shot or with limited target labels. Without these, significance in today’s landscape is unclear.
- Potential transductive setting: representation learning appears to use all target documents (including test) unlabeled. This is common in some prior work but should be explicitly stated and justified; a purely inductive setting (train on source + a pool of unlabeled target not including test) would be more informative.
- Algorithmic details are thin: computational complexity, use of partial SVD, stopping criteria, and wall-clock/runtime scaling with vocabulary and corpus size are not reported. Nuclear-norm methods can be expensive on large term-document matrices.
- Objective and notation issues: the loss c(x,y) is written as 1/2||x−y|| without the square; clarify that it is squared loss. The treatment of the ℓ1 term as µ||M||1 = µ∑ Mij under nonnegativity is delicate: during iterations, M can be negative before projection; then ∇||M||1 = sign(M), not E. The stated gradient uses µE; the projection step may mitigate this in practice, but a rigorous proximal treatment (e.g., keep ℓ1 as nonsmooth with a separate prox) or ensuring nonnegativity before the gradient step would strengthen correctness.
- Limited analysis: no ablation on the contribution of matrix completion vs. direct LSI, no sensitivity to µ, γ, ρ beyond a single chosen setting, no inspection of recovered rank/sparsity, and no qualitative analysis of cross-lingual alignment.
- Dataset assumptions: the source of “unlabeled parallel reviews” is not fully described; true parallel reviews in Amazon are rare without machine translation. Clarify how pairs are obtained and whether domain/category leakage occurs.

Quality
Technically mostly sound, with a plausible convex relaxation and standard proximal operators. The convergence argument via non-expansiveness is reasonable, though the handling of the ℓ1 term under projection should be clarified or adjusted. Empirical methodology is standard but lacks strong modern baselines, runtime reporting, and ablations.

Clarity
Generally well written and easy to follow. Improve precision in the objective (squared loss), provide explicit algorithmic details (partial SVD, stopping), clarify data usage (transductive vs. inductive), and better describe how parallel data are obtained. Minor typos/notation inconsistencies can be fixed easily.

Originality
Moderate. The combination of matrix completion on a block term-document matrix with subsequent LSI is a neat perspective, but adjacent ideas exist in missing-data LSI/soft-impute and prior cross-lingual methods that directly apply SVD/CCA/OPCA to parallel concatenations.

Significance
Potentially useful in low-parallel-data settings, but the absence of comparisons to strong contemporary baselines and lack of scalability evidence limit confidence in impact.

Pros
- Simple, general method; works with few parallel pairs
- Consistent improvements over several classical baselines
- Convergence argument and clear algorithmic structure

Cons
- Missing strong modern baselines (mBERT/XLM-R, CLWE, SCL)
- Limited ablations and scalability analysis
- Objective/gradient handling of ℓ1 under nonnegativity needs clarification
- Likely transductive evaluation; needs clearer framing

Recommendation: Weak reject
The idea is interesting and empirically promising against classical methods, but the paper falls short on modern baselines, ablations, and some technical clarity. Strengthening these points could make the contribution more compelling.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to mBERT/XLM-R (zero-shot and few-shot), LASER/LaBSE sentence embeddings, and bilingual word embeddings (e.g., VecMap) with a linear classifier. Include SCL as a classic strong baseline.
- Provide ablations: (i) LSI without completion; (ii) completion without LSI; (iii) effect of removing ℓ1 or trace norm; (iv) sensitivity to γ, ρ, k. Report recovered rank/sparsity.
- Clarify evaluation protocol (transductive vs. inductive) and run an inductive variant.
- Detail the source/creation of parallel reviews. Explore generalization with comparable corpora or MT-generated pairs.
- Report computational complexity, partial SVD usage, iteration counts, and wall-clock times.
- Tighten the optimization section: correct the loss, address the ℓ1 term rigorously (or justify the projected treatment), and cite proximal gradient theory accordingly.
- Consider extending to a joint objective that integrates completion and dimensionality reduction in one optimization.
