Summary
The paper tackles cross-language text classification by learning a shared representation from a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. The core idea is a two-step pipeline: (1) construct a unified document–term matrix over the concatenated vocabularies of two languages, where non-parallel documents have missing entries; then complete this matrix via a convex optimization that balances low rank (trace norm), sparsity (l1), squared loss on observed entries, and a shrinkage toward zero on missing entries under nonnegativity constraints; (2) run LSI (SVD) on the completed matrix to obtain low-dimensional representations on which a monolingual classifier (SVM) is trained. Experiments on multilingual Amazon reviews (EN–FR/DE/JA) show consistent gains over TBOW and three cross-lingual baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA), including robustness when the number of parallel documents is small.

Relation to prior work: The method builds on cross-lingual representation learning (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA), pivot-based SCL, and multilingual topic models, as well as convex matrix completion (Candes & Recht) and proximal/shrinkage algorithms for nuclear-norm minimization (Ma et al.). The novelty is to explicitly impute missing cross-lingual features via matrix completion before doing LSI, instead of directly applying LSI/CCA on the original or dual-language matrices.

Strengths
- Simple, modular method that can be plugged into standard classifiers; requires only a modest amount of parallel data.
- Empirically strong and consistent improvements across 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks; robustness to small numbers of parallel documents is a valuable property.
- Clear motivation for using completion to exploit cross-lingual co-occurrences that are otherwise absent in a naïvely concatenated matrix.
- Use of nonnegativity and sparsity is coherent with TF-IDF data and promotes interpretability.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical gap: The optimization involves two non-smooth terms (nuclear norm and the indicator of nonnegativity) plus a smooth term. The proposed update alternates a gradient step, nuclear-norm shrinkage, and nonnegativity projection. The convergence proof relies on non-expansiveness to claim convergence to an optimal solution, but this does not generally establish convergence to the minimizer of the sum of non-smooth terms; forward–backward splitting with a single proximal operator does, but composition of two proximal operators is not in general the proximal of the sum. Standard remedies include Dykstra’s algorithm, ADMM, or primal–dual methods with established convergence guarantees. The current proof appears insufficient.
- Scalability and practicality: Each iteration requires an SVD (for shrinkage). There is no complexity analysis, no mention of truncated/partial SVD, iteration counts, or runtimes. Given very large vocabularies, this is critical for feasibility.
- Missing ablations: It is unclear how much of the gain comes from completion vs. LSI. A direct baseline “zero-filled concatenation + LSI” would isolate the benefit of completion. Similarly, ablate ρ (penalty on missing entries), nonnegativity, and the l1 term to understand their roles; compare to “completion only” without the second LSI.
- Baselines: While CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA are standard, the paper omits strong and relevant baselines such as translate-train/translate-test with MT or bilingual dictionaries (which the related work discusses), pivot-based SCL (Prettenhofer & Stein) as an implemented baseline, or bilingual/multilingual word embeddings (if contemporaneous). SVM hyperparameters are left at defaults, which could under-represent some baselines.
- Assumption and identifiability: The text asserts that fully observed document–term matrices are “naturally low-rank,” which is debatable; bag-of-words matrices are high-rank but low-rank approximable. With extremely high missing rates (by construction, half the features per row are missing), nuclear-norm completion may be ill-posed without additional structure. The added squared penalty toward zero on unobserved entries biases the solution; a discussion of this bias and its effect on cross-lingual signal would be useful.
- Reproducibility: Important details are missing (stopping criteria, convergence tolerances, initialization sensitivity, normalization, SVD implementations, computational resources). References are incomplete and have formatting issues.

Quality
Technically, the formulation is sensible and the algorithm is plausible, but the convergence argument is incomplete for the given composite objective. The empirical evaluation is broad and shows strong gains, yet lacks critical ablations and stronger baselines. The claims about needing very few parallel documents are supported by the sensitivity analysis, which is a plus.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and easy to follow, with math and algorithm steps clearly laid out. However, the optimization section would benefit from a more rigorous connection to established proximal splitting frameworks, and the experimental section needs more implementation details for reproducibility. The bibliography needs fixing.

Originality
Combining matrix completion with LSI for cross-lingual representation is, to my knowledge, novel and a reasonable idea. However, it is a relatively straightforward composition of known tools; the main novelty is in the problem setup and empirical demonstration rather than in algorithmic innovation.

Significance
If the gains hold with stronger baselines and in larger settings, the approach could be useful for practitioners who have few parallel documents. The idea may inspire further work integrating structured imputation with cross-lingual representation learning. The current theoretical and experimental omissions, however, limit the impact.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Provide a principled optimization method (e.g., ADMM, Dykstra, or primal–dual) with a correct convergence proof for the objective including both nuclear norm and nonnegativity constraints, or justify conditions under which your operator composition converges to the true minimizer.
- Report computational complexity, use truncated SVD, and provide runtimes and iteration counts.
- Add ablations: zero-fill + LSI, completion without LSI, effect of ρ, µ, nonnegativity.
- Include stronger baselines (translate-train/test, SCL, possibly bilingual embeddings) and tune SVM C.
- Clarify construction of the dual-language inputs for CL-LSI/OPCA in your setup to ensure fairness.
- Provide more implementation details (normalization, stopping criteria), and fix references.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, broadly applicable idea with strong empirical results across many tasks.
- Demonstrates robustness with few parallel documents.
- Potentially impactful for low-resource cross-lingual classification.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete and potentially incorrect convergence justification.
- Missing ablations and some stronger baselines; limited reproducibility details.
- Scalability concerns not addressed.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is promising and empirically encouraging, but the theoretical gap and evaluation shortcomings should be addressed to meet NeurIPS standards. With a stronger optimization treatment, additional baselines/ablations, and clearer scalability analysis, this could become a solid contribution.
