**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a two-step learning method (TSL) for cross-language text classification. The goal is to leverage labeled data from a source language to train a classifier for a target language where labeled data is scarce. The method utilizes a small set of unlabeled parallel documents to bridge the language gap. The first step involves constructing a unified document-term matrix for all documents (source, target, and parallel) using a concatenated vocabulary. This matrix is sparsely populated: parallel documents have entries for both languages, while monolingual documents have missing values for the other language's vocabulary. The authors formulate this as a matrix completion problem, aiming to recover a low-rank, non-negative, and sparse matrix by filling in the missing entries. They solve this using a projected gradient descent algorithm. The second step takes the completed matrix and applies Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) via SVD to project the documents into a low-dimensional, dense, shared semantic space. Standard monolingual classifiers can then be trained on this representation.

The approach relates to previous work like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, which also learn shared representations from parallel or comparable corpora. The key difference and main contribution is the explicit matrix completion step *before* dimensionality reduction. While methods like CL-LSI implicitly handle missing entries by treating them as zeros, this work's central hypothesis is that actively inferring these missing values based on a low-rank assumption will produce a more robust shared representation. The authors validate their method on a standard multilingual sentiment analysis dataset, showing significant improvements over several strong baselines, especially when the amount of parallel data is small.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Formulation:** The core idea of framing the cross-lingual representation problem as one of matrix completion is novel and elegant. Instead of simply concatenating parallel documents and treating non-corresponding parts as zero (as in CL-LSI), the model explicitly treats them as *missing* and seeks to infer their values. This is a more principled way to handle the unified vocabulary and captures the intuition that a document in one language has an implicit representation in the other, which can be recovered from the low-rank structure of the full document collection.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The method is tested on 18 different cross-language tasks, demonstrating its general applicability. It consistently and significantly outperforms four relevant baselines, including strong representation learning methods like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA.

3.  **Robustness to Limited Parallel Data:** The analysis of performance with varying amounts of parallel data (Section 5.3) is a key strength. The results show that TSL maintains a strong performance advantage even with a very small number of parallel documents (e.g., 200). This is a highly desirable practical property, as large parallel corpora are not always available. This finding strongly supports the claim that the matrix completion approach is effective at leveraging limited cross-lingual signals.

4.  **Technically Sound:** The mathematical formulation is sound. The optimization problem in Eq. (2) is a well-motivated relaxation of the rank minimization problem, incorporating sparsity (L1 norm) and non-negativity constraints appropriate for text data. The use of a projected gradient descent algorithm is a standard and scalable approach for this class of problems, and the authors provide a convergence proof, adding to the paper's technical quality.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance)**

1.  **Lack of Discussion on Scalability and Computational Cost:** A major weakness is the absence of any discussion regarding the computational complexity of the proposed method. The optimization algorithm requires computing an SVD at *each iteration* for the shrinkage step. For a large document collection, the document-term matrix M can be very large, making this iterative SVD procedure extremely expensive compared to baselines like CL-LSI, which requires only a single SVD. This practical limitation is not addressed, and no runtime comparisons are provided. This is a crucial omission for a NIPS paper, where scalability is often a key concern.

2.  **Limited Intuition on Completed Matrix:** The paper would benefit from a deeper discussion or qualitative analysis of what the completed matrix M* represents. For a monolingual French document, what is the interpretation of the filled-in values for English words? Are they pseudo-translations, topic indicators, or something else? Providing examples or analysis of the completed entries would offer valuable insight into *why* the method works and build stronger intuition.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The hyperparameters (γ, ρ, k) are tuned on a single task (EFB) and then fixed for all 17 other tasks. While this is a fair evaluation protocol, it leaves the question of the model's sensitivity unanswered. A sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with these parameters would strengthen the paper's claims of robustness. It is possible that the chosen parameters happen to work well for this specific dataset but might be suboptimal for others.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you please comment on the computational cost of your method, particularly the iterative SVD, compared to the baselines? What is the typical number of iterations required for convergence? This seems like a critical practical disadvantage that needs to be discussed.
*   In Section 3.1, the relaxation of the L1 norm to a linear function under non-negativity is mentioned briefly. It might be clearer to explicitly state that `µ||M||_1` becomes `µ * sum(M_ij)` in the gradient calculation `∇g(M)`.
*   The choice of the loss function `c(M_ij, 0)` for the unobserved entries is interesting. This regularizes the missing entries towards zero. What is the intuition behind this, and have you experimented with other forms of regularization for the unobserved entries?
*   The convergence proof relies heavily on the non-expansiveness of the operators and refers to [11]. While sound, providing a bit more high-level intuition for the proof structure within the paper itself could improve clarity for readers not deeply familiar with this specific optimization literature.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a well-written paper presenting a novel and effective method for cross-language representation learning. The core idea is strong, the technical execution is solid, and the experimental results are compelling, particularly the method's robustness with limited parallel data. The primary weakness is the lack of discussion on the significant computational cost and scalability, which is an important consideration for a method intended for large text corpora. Despite this, the novelty of the approach and the strength of the empirical results make this a valuable contribution. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and I recommend acceptance, with the strong suggestion that the authors address the scalability concerns in the final version.
