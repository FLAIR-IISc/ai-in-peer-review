

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning that addresses the challenge of cross-lingual text classification. The authors construct a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix with missing entries for non-parallel documents, then use matrix completion to fill in the missing values before applying latent semantic indexing to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The method differs from prior approaches like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, which apply dimensionality reduction directly on the incomplete matrix. The authors evaluate their method on 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines.

## Strengths
1. **Novel Methodology**: The two-step approach (matrix completion followed by LSI) is a fresh perspective on cross-lingual representation learning, offering a principled way to handle the sparse bilingual document-term matrix. This differs from prior work that either translates documents or applies dimensionality reduction directly on incomplete data.

2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The method significantly outperforms baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) across 18 cross-lingual tasks, with improvements ranging from 8.59% to 14.61%. The ablation study showing effectiveness with as few as 200 unlabeled parallel documents is particularly valuable for practical applications.

3. **Theoretical Rigor**: The paper provides a convergence proof for the projected gradient descent algorithm used in matrix completion, which adds theoretical credibility to the proposed optimization approach.

4. **Clear Problem Formulation**: The authors clearly articulate the cross-lingual representation learning problem and the limitations of existing approaches, providing a solid motivation for their method.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation**: The experiments are restricted to sentiment classification on Amazon product reviews. The authors do not evaluate on other NLP tasks (e.g., named entity recognition, machine translation) or more diverse datasets, which limits the perceived significance of the approach. More importantly, they fail to compare with recent neural approaches like multilingual BERT or XLM-R, which have become the new state-of-the-art in cross-lingual representation learning.

2. **Insufficient Theoretical Justification**: While the paper formulates the matrix completion problem, it lacks a clear theoretical argument for why their approach would produce better representations than direct application of LSI on the incomplete matrix. The claim that "a fully observed document-term matrix is naturally low-rank and sparse" is insufficient justification for the matrix completion step.

3. **Ambiguous Mathematical Formulation**: The optimization problem in section 3.1 (equation 2) is not sufficiently motivated. The rationale for using specific regularizers (trace norm, ℓ1 norm) and the choice of the loss function c(Mij, M0ij) is unclear. The transition from the non-convex problem (1) to the convex relaxation (2) could be better explained.

4. **Lack of Computational Analysis**: The paper does not discuss the computational complexity or scalability of the proposed method compared to baselines. Given the size of the document-term matrices in real-world applications, this is an important consideration for practical adoption.

## Recommendation
This paper presents a novel approach to cross-lingual representation learning with strong empirical results. However, the limited evaluation scope (only sentiment classification on one dataset, no comparison with neural state-of-the-art methods) significantly limits the paper's significance for the NIPS community. The theoretical justification for the matrix completion step is also insufficient.

I recommend **weak rejection**. The paper has merit in its methodology and experimental results, but the lack of comparison with recent neural approaches (which have largely superseded traditional matrix-based methods for cross-lingual tasks) and the limited evaluation scope prevent it from making a significant contribution to the field at this time.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Expand Evaluation**: Compare with recent neural cross-lingual models (e.g., multilingual BERT, XLM-R) on multiple NLP tasks beyond sentiment classification. This would better demonstrate the method's relevance to current research trends.

2. **Strengthen Theoretical Justification**: Provide a clearer argument for why matrix completion would lead to better representations than direct LSI on incomplete data. A theoretical analysis of the representation quality would be valuable.

3. **Clarify Formulation**: Improve the mathematical exposition in section 3.1, particularly the rationale for the specific optimization formulation and the choice of regularizers.

4. **Add Computational Analysis**: Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method and compare it with baselines, including training and inference times.

5. **Consider Broader Applications**: Discuss how the method could be extended to other cross-lingual tasks beyond sentiment classification.

With these improvements, particularly the addition of comparisons with recent neural approaches and expanded evaluation, this paper could become a strong candidate for NIPS. As it stands, however, the limited scope of the evaluation and lack of comparison with state-of-the-art neural methods make it difficult to justify its acceptance at NIPS.
