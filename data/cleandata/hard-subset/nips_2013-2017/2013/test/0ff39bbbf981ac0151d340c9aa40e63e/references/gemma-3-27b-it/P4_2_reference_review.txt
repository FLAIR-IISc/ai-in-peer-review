This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning, aiming to improve cross-lingual text classification performance. The core idea is to leverage unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to first complete a partially observed document-term matrix via matrix completion, and then apply Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to derive cross-lingual representations. The method is evaluated on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews.

The paper builds on a substantial body of work in cross-lingual learning. It correctly identifies key approaches, including machine translation-based methods (e.g., [18, 23]), domain adaptation techniques [17, 9, 21], multi-view learning [22, 2, 1, 13, 12] and methods exploiting bilingual resources [16, 10, 20, 15, 19, 14]. The authors accurately position their work with respect to these existing methods, highlighting how it differs – specifically, requiring less parallel data than approaches relying on full translation. The comparison to CL-LSI [10], CL-OPCA [15], and CL-KCCA [20] is particularly relevant, and the emphasis on matrix completion *before* LSI is a novel aspect. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The two-step approach of matrix completion followed by LSI is a reasonable and potentially effective combination, and represents a novel contribution within the cross-lingual representation learning field. Applying matrix completion to address the sparse document-term matrix formed from limited parallel data appears promising.
* **Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method consistently outperforms several strong baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) across the majority of tasks. The consistent improvements suggest the effectiveness of the approach.
* **Robustness to Parallel Data Size:**  The experiments investigating the impact of the size of parallel data show the method's robustness, performing well even with a limited amount of parallel data (200 documents).
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the problem of cross-language text classification and motivates the need for improved methods.
* **Detailed Algorithm Description:** Algorithm 1, detailing the projected gradient descent method for matrix completion, provides sufficient detail for implementation. The convergence analysis, although brief, is a positive aspect.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Optimization Algorithm Details:** While Algorithm 1 is presented, many practical details are missing. The choice of parameters (γ, ρ, τ) isn't thoroughly justified beyond stating they were chosen via cross-validation on one task.  A more detailed discussion of parameter sensitivity would strengthen the results. The specific implementation of the projected gradient descent (e.g., stopping criteria, handling of numerical instability) is not described.
* **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While the baselines are reasonable, the comparison could be strengthened by including more recent and state-of-the-art cross-lingual learning methods. For example, methods based on pre-trained multilingual language models (like mBERT or XLM-R) are now common and would provide a more challenging benchmark.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** It would be valuable to include ablation studies to determine the contribution of each step in the proposed method. For example, what is the performance gain from matrix completion compared to directly applying LSI to the sparse matrix?
* **Figure Resolution and Readability:** Figures 1 and 2 have small text and axes labels, making them difficult to read.
* **Repetitive Language:**  There are instances of repetitive phrasing and overly verbose descriptions throughout the paper.  Conciseness would improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The proposed method demonstrates a clear and statistically significant improvement over several strong baselines.
* The two-step approach is a novel contribution that addresses the challenges of sparse data in cross-lingual representation learning.
* The method is robust to the amount of available parallel data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of detailed implementation specifics for the optimization algorithm and parameter selection.
* The absence of a comparison to more recent and powerful baselines (e.g., multilingual pre-trained models).
* The limited ablation studies prevent a thorough understanding of the contribution of each component.




**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and supported by convincing experimental results. While there are some weaknesses regarding implementation details and baseline comparisons, the strengths of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding algorithmic details and comparisons to stronger baselines.  Specifically, the authors should provide more information on the parameter selection process and consider including a comparison to a multilingual pre-trained language model.
