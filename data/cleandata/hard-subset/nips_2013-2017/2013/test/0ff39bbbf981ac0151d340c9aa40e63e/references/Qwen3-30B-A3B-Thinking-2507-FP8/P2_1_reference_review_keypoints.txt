- Main idea: Proposes a two-step cross-lingual representation method that fills missing entries in a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix via convex matrix completion, then applies LSI for low-dimensional representations.  
- Evaluation: Tested on cross-lingual sentiment classification using Amazon reviews across 3 categories and 4 languages, outperforming four baselines on 18 tasks.

Strengths
- Technical soundness: Convex matrix completion with trace-norm relaxation and a proven convergence result for the projected gradient algorithm (Theorem 1).  
- Experimental rigor: Extensive experiments showing consistent, significant improvements (8.6%–14.6%) over baselines across tasks.  
- Ablation: Analysis shows robustness to limited parallel data, working with as few as 200 parallel documents.  
- Clarity: Well-organized paper with precise mathematical formulation and clearly described algorithm and experimental setup.  
- Originality: Novel combination of matrix completion to impute missing bilingual entries before applying LSI, distinguishing it from prior CL-LSI-like methods.  
- Significance: Addresses an important problem for low-resource languages and shows improved performance with fewer parallel documents.

Weaknesses
- Missing SOTA comparisons: No comparison to modern transformer-based cross-lingual models (mBERT, XLM-R), which are current standards.  
- Parameter selection: Parameters tuned on a single task (EFB) and applied universally without sufficient justification or sensitivity analysis.  
- Theoretical gaps: Lacks deeper theoretical justification for choosing trace-norm plus ℓ1 regularization specifically for cross-lingual settings.  
- Incremental novelty: Does not convincingly show why matrix completion + LSI is superior to simpler missing-data strategies (e.g., zero/mean imputation).  
- Limited evaluation scope: Only evaluated on sentiment classification (Amazon reviews), not on other cross-lingual tasks like NER or MT.  
- Scalability concerns: No analysis of computational scalability to more languages or larger document collections.

Overall assessment
- Technically sound and empirically strong, but the significance claim is weakened by omission of comparisons to transformer-based methods; paper is novel but needs broader evaluation.

Recommendation
- Conditional accept (minor revisions) if authors add comparisons to transformer baselines; otherwise reject if they cannot address that major omission.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to mBERT/XLM-R (and similar transformers).  
- Run parameter sensitivity analyses across multiple tasks.  
- Include ablations comparing matrix completion to simple imputation strategies.  
- Provide scalability analysis for larger/more multilingual datasets.  
- Discuss limitations and applicability relative to modern neural cross-lingual methods.

Final verdict
- Strong technical contribution but falls short of NIPS significance bar until comparisons to current transformer-based cross-lingual models are included.
