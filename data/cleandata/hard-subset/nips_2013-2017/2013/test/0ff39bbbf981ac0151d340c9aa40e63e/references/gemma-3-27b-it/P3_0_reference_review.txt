## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a reasonably well-executed approach to cross-lingual representation learning, building upon established techniques like matrix completion and LSI. While the idea isn't groundbreakingly novel, the combination and application to the cross-lingual setting, along with the empirical validation, are solid contributions. I believe the paper is above the NIPS acceptance threshold, leaning towards a weak accept. There are areas for improvement in clarity and a more critical discussion of the limitations, but the positive results and thorough experimental setup justify publication.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the important problem of cross-lingual text classification, where labeled data is scarce in the target language. The authors propose a two-step method: 1) matrix completion to fill in missing values in a concatenated document-term matrix constructed from parallel and non-parallel documents, and 2) latent semantic indexing (LSI) on the completed matrix to derive cross-lingual representations. 

The work builds upon a well-trodden area within cross-lingual learning. The authors correctly position their work relative to approaches leveraging machine translation (e.g., [17, 21]) and those building cross-lingual representations through bilingual resources (e.g., [16, 10]). They appropriately highlight the novelty as lying in the combination of matrix completion *before* LSI, a step not taken in prior work like CL-LSI. The connection to matrix completion and its potential benefits for capturing co-occurrence patterns is clearly articulated.  The references cited are relevant and provide a good context for the proposed method.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Solid Empirical Results:** The experimental results on the Amazon review dataset are compelling. The proposed method consistently outperforms several strong baselines, including CL-LSI, CL-OPCA and CL-KCCA. The substantial gains, particularly on tasks with limited labeled data, are noteworthy.
* **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The paper includes a comprehensive experimental setup, including comparisons with multiple baselines, parameter tuning, and analysis of the impact of parallel data size. The repeated experiments and reporting of standard deviations add to the robustness of the findings.
* **Clear Motivation & Intuition:** The motivation for using matrix completion is well-explained, emphasizing its ability to leverage co-occurrence information from parallel data to bridge the language gap. The two-step approach is intuitively appealing and easy to understand.
* **Scalable Algorithm:** The use of a projected gradient descent algorithm for matrix completion is practical, addressing the scalability issues often associated with matrix factorization techniques on large datasets. The convergence analysis, while potentially dense for some readers, demonstrates mathematical rigor.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of matrix completion and LSI is a reasonable idea, it's not a fundamentally new paradigm. The core building blocks are well-established techniques. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of *why* this combination is particularly effective compared to simply applying these techniques independently or in other sequences.
* **Matrix Completion Justification:** The choice of the specific matrix completion formulation (objective function in (2)) feels somewhat arbitrary. The authors mention sparsity regularization via the ℓ1 norm but do not provide strong justification for this choice over other regularization techniques.  A brief discussion of alternatives and their trade-offs would be valuable.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper briefly mentions parameter tuning but lacks a deeper analysis of parameter sensitivity. Understanding how the performance varies with different values of γ, ρ, and k would strengthen the results and provide practical guidance for users.
* **Limited Dataset Diversity:** The experiments are solely based on the Amazon review dataset.  While this is a common benchmark, the generalizability of the findings to other domains and languages is uncertain.  Results on at least one additional dataset would be highly beneficial.
* **Clarity in Section 4:** While mathematically correct, Section 4 (Optimization Algorithm) is quite dense.  The notation and steps could be explained more clearly with more intuitive explanations.  Linking the equations back to the practical implications would make it more accessible.



**4. Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical justification for matrix completion and the optimization algorithm are well-presented. The experimental results are convincing and support the claims. However, the lack of detailed analysis on parameter sensitivity and dataset diversity weakens the overall quality slightly.
* **Clarity:** The introduction and experimental sections are well-written and easy to follow. However, Section 3 and especially Section 4 require more attention to clarity. The mathematical notation is dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
* **Originality:** The originality is moderate.  The combination of techniques is not entirely novel, but the application to the cross-lingual setting and the empirical results contribute a new perspective.
* **Significance:** The results are significant in the context of cross-lingual learning. The method outperforms existing approaches, particularly in low-resource settings.  The potential for reducing the need for labeled data in target languages is valuable.




**5. Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Strong empirical results consistently outperform competitive baselines.
* Thorough experimental setup with parameter tuning and statistical significance.
* Clear motivation and intuition behind the proposed approach.
* Scalable optimization algorithm.

**Con:**

* Limited novelty – the core techniques are well-established.
* Lack of in-depth parameter sensitivity analysis.
* Limited dataset diversity.
* Section 4 (Optimization) is difficult to follow.




**Overall Recommendation:** **Weak Accept.**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should focus on improving the clarity of Section 4, adding a more critical discussion of parameter sensitivity, and briefly addressing the limited dataset diversity. Strengthening the discussion of the novelty and potential limitations would also enhance the paper's impact. While not a groundbreaking contribution, the work is solid, well-executed, and offers a valuable addition to the field of cross-lingual representation learning.
