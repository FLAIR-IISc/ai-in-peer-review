This paper presents a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, leveraging unlabeled parallel bilingual data via matrix completion and LSI. The core idea – using matrix completion to ‘fill in’ a concatenated document-term matrix to bridge the language gap – is genuinely novel and represents a worthwhile direction for cross-lingual transfer learning. While not a radical departure from existing approaches (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, etc.), the matrix completion step feels like a fresh perspective, particularly given its potential to capture subtle co-occurrence patterns not easily modeled by direct projection methods. The experimental validation on Amazon reviews appears solid, with consistent outperformance of several baselines.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation is a novel approach. The use of matrix completion to explicitly model cross-lingual relationships via co-occurrence patterns is a strong point.
*   **Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates significant improvements over several strong baselines, providing concrete evidence of the method's effectiveness. The fact that it performs well even with limited parallel data is particularly encouraging.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The algorithm is clearly presented, and the experimental setup is well-defined.
*   **Theoretical grounding**: While not a primary focus, the inclusion of a convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm strengthens the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Optimization Details:** The discussion of the projected gradient descent algorithm, while present, feels somewhat dense and lacks intuitive explanation. The selection of parameters (γ, ρ, τ) could benefit from more justification, even if it involved a small grid search.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** While the authors mention scalability as a motivation for their chosen algorithm, a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity, and potential bottlenecks for very large datasets would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Ablation Study:** While comparisons to baselines are comprehensive, there is limited ablation study investigating the contribution of each step (matrix completion *vs.* LSI).
*   **Limited Dataset Diversity:** Experiments are restricted to the Amazon reviews dataset. While a strong testbed, results on other datasets (e.g., news articles, scientific abstracts) could strengthen the generalizability claims.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is genuinely novel and presents a promising new direction for cross-lingual learning.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The theoretical treatment, although not exhaustive, provides a level of rigor.
*   The paper addresses a challenging problem and offers a practical solution with potential for real-world impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The optimization details could be clearer and more thoroughly justified.
*   The lack of ablation study limits understanding of the relative importance of each component.
*   The reliance on a single dataset raises concerns about generalizability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper with a novel and potentially significant contribution. While the optimization details and lack of extensive ablation studies are weaknesses, they are not critical flaws. The strength of the core idea, coupled with the strong empirical results, outweighs these concerns. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program. I would rate this as a **Strong Accept**. The novelty and potential impact of the work are compelling, and while further refinement is possible, the paper already represents a significant step forward in the field of cross-lingual representation learning. I'm willing to overlook the minor limitations given the strength of the central concept.
