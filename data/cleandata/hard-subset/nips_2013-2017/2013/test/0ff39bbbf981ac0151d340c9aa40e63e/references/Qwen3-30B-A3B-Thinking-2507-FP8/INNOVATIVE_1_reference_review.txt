

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning that addresses the challenge of language barriers in cross-lingual text classification. The authors construct a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix where parallel documents are fully observed and non-parallel documents have missing values for the other language's vocabulary. Their key insight is to first solve a matrix completion problem to fill in missing entries based on observed co-occurrence patterns, then apply latent semantic indexing to the completed matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. This approach differs from prior work like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA that directly apply dimensionality reduction to the original concatenated matrix without addressing the missing values.

The paper makes a strong novelty claim by introducing matrix completion as a preprocessing step for cross-lingual representation learning. While previous work has used concatenated document-term matrices for cross-lingual tasks (as cited in the related work), none have proposed filling in the missing entries through matrix completion before dimensionality reduction. This is a clean, conceptually simple yet powerful idea that directly addresses the fundamental problem of sparse data in cross-lingual settings.

The experimental evaluation on Amazon product reviews across four languages demonstrates that the proposed method (TSL) consistently outperforms existing baselines including CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, and a target-only baseline. The results show substantial improvements of 8.59-14.61% over the baseline across 18 different cross-language sentiment classification tasks. Crucially, the method demonstrates robust performance even with very limited unlabeled parallel data (as few as 200 documents), which is a significant practical advantage.

Strengths:
1. The core idea of using matrix completion to fill missing entries before LSI is genuinely novel and conceptually elegant.
2. The performance gains over existing methods are substantial and statistically significant across multiple tasks.
3. The method's ability to work with minimal parallel data is a major practical contribution.
4. The paper clearly articulates the distinction between their approach and prior work in the related work section.

Weaknesses:
1. The theoretical justification for why matrix completion would work particularly well for this problem could be strengthened. The paper shows the method works empirically but doesn't deeply analyze why the low-rank structure captured by matrix completion is especially relevant to cross-lingual representation learning.
2. The computational complexity analysis is limited. Given the matrix completion step, it's unclear how the method scales to larger datasets compared to the existing methods.
3. The paper doesn't thoroughly explore failure cases or limitations of the approach (e.g., how it handles extreme vocabulary mismatches between languages).

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The experimental setup is appropriate, and the results are convincingly presented with proper statistical analysis.

This work stands out as a significant contribution to cross-lingual learning because it introduces a fundamentally new approach to handling the missing data problem inherent in cross-lingual representation learning. While the method itself is relatively straightforward, the insight of applying matrix completion as a preprocessing step before dimensionality reduction is novel and effective. The performance gains are substantial enough to suggest this could become a standard approach in the field.

Compared to other NIPS submissions, this paper offers a clear conceptual advance rather than just an incremental improvement. The authors have successfully identified a key limitation in existing approaches (handling missing values in bilingual document-term matrices) and proposed a novel solution that addresses it effectively. The method is simple enough to be widely applicable yet produces significant performance gains.

While the paper could benefit from a deeper theoretical analysis of why matrix completion works well for this specific problem, the empirical results are compelling enough to suggest this is a valuable contribution. As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be a strong candidate for acceptance. The core idea is sufficiently novel and impactful to warrant publication, and the experimental results convincingly support the claims.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to better address the theoretical aspects and limitations of the method. The paper makes a clear contribution that advances the state of the art in cross-lingual learning, and its simplicity and effectiveness make it likely to be widely adopted and built upon by the community.
