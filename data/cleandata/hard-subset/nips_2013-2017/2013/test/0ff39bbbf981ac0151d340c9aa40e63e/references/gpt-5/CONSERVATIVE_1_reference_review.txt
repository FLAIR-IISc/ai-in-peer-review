Summary
The paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-lingual text classification using a small number of unlabeled parallel document pairs. Step 1 performs matrix completion on a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix (rows are documents, columns are the union vocabulary; non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language) using a convex objective with nuclear norm (rank surrogate), an ℓ1 term (which becomes linear under nonnegativity), squared losses on observed entries, and a small quadratic penalty on missing entries, with a nonnegativity constraint. A projected gradient descent algorithm with singular value shrinkage and nonnegativity projection is used, with a convergence argument based on non-expansiveness. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the recovered matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations, followed by a linear SVM for classification. On multilingual Amazon review sentiment tasks (18 source-target-category pairs), the method is reported to outperform TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, and to be relatively robust when the number of parallel pairs is small.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned against translation-based approaches (EM self-training, bi-weighting), multi-view/co-training methods, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, and multilingual topic models. The main claimed novelty is completing the bilingual document-term matrix before applying LSI, rather than directly applying LSI/OPCA/KCCA to the observed data or parallel concatenations.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, leveraging a well-studied convex matrix completion formulation to exploit co-occurrence within and across languages without relying on full translation.
- Uses widely adopted Amazon multilingual sentiment datasets and evaluates across 18 tasks, with some sensitivity analysis to the number of parallel pairs.
- Clear description of the optimization procedure and a convergence argument following prior work on non-expansive mappings.
- The empirical gains reported over CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA are consistent across most tasks, and the method appears to degrade less when parallel data are scarce.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical soundness/theory:
- The convergence proof appears to extend Ma et al. (2009) to a constrained problem with two nonsmooth terms (nuclear norm and indicator of nonnegativity). The algorithm applies shrinkage then projects onto the nonnegative orthant. In general, the proximal of a sum is not the composition of individual proximals; non-expansiveness alone does not establish convergence to the minimizer of the stated convex problem. A more rigorous justification (e.g., via proximal splitting with two nonsmooth terms, ADMM, or establishing that the composition is a valid proximal for this specific structure) is needed. As written, optimality for (2) is not convincingly established.
- The claim that a “fully observed document-term matrix is naturally low-rank” is questionable. Bag-of-words matrices are high-dimensional and sparse; while low-rank approximations are useful, assuming low rank a priori (so that completion is well-posed) merits discussion or citations beyond intuition. Under what conditions does the combination of nuclear norm + small penalty on unobserved entries recover meaningful cross-lingual co-occurrences?
- The role of the ρ regularizer is underexplored. With ρ as small as 1e-4, the method effectively encourages aggressive filling of missing entries; with larger ρ it biases toward zeros. No ablation is provided to show sensitivity to ρ, µ, or γ, nor evidence that the learned M* is indeed low rank/sparse as intended.

Empirical validation:
- Baselines are limited. Strong and widely used baselines such as Structural Correspondence Learning (SCL; already cited), bilingual/multilingual word embeddings (e.g., projection-based alignment), and modern multilingual encoders (mBERT/XLM-R) are absent. Even for the period matching your references, SCL is a notable omission; for post-2016, cross-lingual embeddings are standard. This substantially weakens the significance of the empirical claims.
- Key details are missing: kernel choice and parameters for KCCA, tokenization and IDF computation across languages (separate vs. joint), normalization, stopping criteria, iteration counts, and wall-clock time. Nuclear norm methods require repeated SVDs on a potentially huge matrix (t × d with d the union vocabulary), raising scalability concerns. No complexity analysis or runtime comparison is provided.
- Fairness of comparisons: TBOW is a very weak baseline with only 100 target labels. The competing cross-lingual methods use source labels; a stronger baseline would include machine-translation-based training on translated source data, or lexicon-based projection baselines. Hyperparameters are tuned on a single task (EFB) and transferred; while that avoids per-task cherry-picking, it is unclear whether the chosen parameters disadvantage certain baselines (e.g., KCCA).
- Methodological ablations are missing: What is the gain from matrix completion beyond simply applying LSI to the observed concatenated matrix (with missing entries at zero)? What if one uses masked/weighted low-rank factorization (EM for SVD with missing data, weighted NMF), which directly handles missingness without an explicit imputation step? These are closer to your objective and would isolate the contribution of the completion step.

Clarity and reproducibility:
- The paper is generally readable, but several choices are not justified (e.g., why two-step vs. a single masked low-rank model; why nonnegativity is critical). The convergence section borrows lemmas but does not clearly tie fixed-point convergence to optimality of (2).
- Important experimental artifacts (tables/figures) are not present here; assuming they exist in the full paper, please report statistical significance, include per-task numbers, and provide error bars in the plots.

Significance and originality:
- The two-step idea (impute then LSI) is a relatively straightforward combination of established tools; similar problems are often approached via masked low-rank factorization/end-to-end objectives. Without stronger baselines and deeper analysis, it is difficult to conclude that this advances the state of the art.

Pros for acceptance
- Clean idea with a convex objective; practically useful if it indeed works with few parallel pairs.
- Broad evaluation across 18 tasks on a standard dataset with some robustness analysis.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty relative to existing low-rank/masked factorization methods; missing comparisons to strong baselines (notably SCL and modern cross-lingual representations).
- Theoretical justification for the proposed optimizer solving the stated problem is incomplete.
- Scalability and implementation details are insufficiently addressed.
- Lack of ablations to isolate the contribution of matrix completion vs. direct LSI.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a rigorous optimization framework (e.g., proximal splitting/ADMM) with a proof that converges to the optimal solution of (2), or adjust the objective to match the algorithm’s fixed point.
- Add strong baselines: SCL, dictionary-based projection, bilingual embeddings (e.g., VecMap/MUSE), and, if allowed, multilingual transformers. Include MT-based baselines.
- Add ablations: no-completion (direct LSI), masked SVD/NMF, effect of ρ/γ/µ, with convergence plots, ranks, and sparsity of M*.
- Report scalability: vocabulary sizes, ranks, iteration counts, runtime, and memory.
- Clarify preprocessing (tokenization, stopwording, IDF computation), kernel choices in KCCA, and ensure hyperparameter selection is fair.

Overall recommendation: Reject
The paper presents a reasonable combination of established techniques with promising empirical results, but falls short on theoretical rigor, breadth of baselines, and ablations needed to convincingly establish novelty and significance.

Confidence: High.
