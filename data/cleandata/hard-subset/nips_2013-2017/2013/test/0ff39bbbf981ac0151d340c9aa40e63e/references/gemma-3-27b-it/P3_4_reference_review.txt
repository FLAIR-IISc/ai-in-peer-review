## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a conceptually sound and well-executed approach to cross-lingual representation learning. The two-step process of matrix completion followed by LSI is a reasonable strategy to address the challenges of sparse data and cross-lingual alignment. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating significant improvements over several baselines. While the approach isn’t radically novel in its individual components, the combination and application to this specific problem are worthwhile. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of cross-lingual text classification, where labeled data is scarce in the target language. The authors propose a two-step method: first, they construct a partially observed document-term matrix leveraging unlabeled parallel data and fill in missing values using matrix completion. Second, they apply Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to derive cross-lingual document representations. This approach aims to capture co-occurrence patterns across languages without relying heavily on machine translation.

The authors correctly situate their work within the existing literature. They differentiate their approach from methods relying on machine translation (e.g., [17, 21, 22]) by minimizing the need for translating large amounts of data. They also distinguish themselves from other cross-lingual representation learning methods that directly apply techniques like LSI, KCCA, or OPCA to the concatenated matrix [10, 15, 20] by strategically employing matrix completion *before* dimensionality reduction.  The connection to matrix completion techniques [4, 11] is also well established.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Sound Methodology:** The two-step approach is well-motivated. Matrix completion effectively addresses the sparse data problem inherent in using parallel corpora and the language barrier.  LSI then provides a robust dimensionality reduction technique.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper provides extensive experimental results on a standard benchmark dataset (Amazon reviews).  The consistent outperformance of TSL over several strong baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) demonstrates its effectiveness.
* **Robustness to Parallel Data Size:** The experiments demonstrating performance with varying amounts of parallel data (Figure 1 & 2) are valuable. Showing good results even with limited parallel resources is a significant strength.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical formulation of the matrix completion problem is clear, and Algorithm 1 is easy to follow. The inclusion of a convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm is a plus.
* **Thorough Comparison:** Comparing against a diverse set of baselines (including a simple TBOW baseline) strengthens the validity of the results.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty:** While the *combination* of matrix completion and LSI is effective, neither technique is novel in itself. The paper would benefit from a deeper discussion of how the specific choices of matrix completion parameters (γ, ρ) influence the quality of the learned representations.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The parameter selection process (reported on EFB task only) is somewhat opaque. The rationale behind the chosen values for γ, ρ, and k isn't fully explained.  A sensitivity analysis, even limited, would strengthen the findings.
* **Computational Cost:**  Matrix completion can be computationally expensive, especially with large vocabularies. The paper doesn't provide details on the computational resources required for the experiments, making it difficult to assess the scalability of the approach.
* **Limited Error Analysis:**  The paper focuses on overall accuracy. Analyzing specific types of errors (e.g., misclassifications related to specific sentiment-bearing words) could provide valuable insights into the limitations of the method.



**4. Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 3.1:**  The choice of the ℓ1 norm for sparsity in the matrix completion problem is reasonable, but a brief justification for this choice would be helpful. Are other sparsity-inducing regularizers (e.g., ℓ0 norm approximation) considered?
* **Section 4.1:**  The projected gradient descent algorithm is described, but the choice of step size τ seems somewhat arbitrary. How sensitive is the algorithm's performance to this parameter?
* **Section 5.2:**  The description of the experimental setup and parameter selection is slightly unclear. Clarify how the same parameters were used across all 18 tasks.
* **Figure 1 & 2:**  The error bars are quite large in some cases. This suggests significant variability in the results. Consider discussing the potential sources of this variability.
* **Computational Complexity:** Provide a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method, particularly the matrix completion step.




**5. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Demonstrates a clear improvement over existing methods on a challenging cross-lingual text classification task.
* The proposed methodology is sound and well-motivated.
* The paper is well-written, organized, and includes a convergence analysis.
* The experimental results are convincing and highlight the robustness of the approach.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the approach is incremental.
*  The parameter selection process is not fully transparent.
* Lack of detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability.



**6. Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, and the contributions are valuable to the field of cross-lingual learning. The authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above by:

* Providing a more detailed discussion of parameter selection and sensitivity.
*  Briefly discussing the computational cost and scalability of the approach.
*  Including a brief error analysis to provide further insights into the method’s limitations.
