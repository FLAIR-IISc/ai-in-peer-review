This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual text classification, leveraging unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The core idea involves matrix completion to fill a concatenated document-term matrix with missing values (due to language differences), followed by Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) for dimensionality reduction and representation learning. While the problem addressed – cross-lingual text classification – is a valid and important one in NLP, and the authors correctly identify the challenges related to the language barrier, the proposed approach feels incremental and lacks compelling justification for its complexity.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly outlines the method. The organization is logical, and the related work section provides a reasonable overview of existing techniques. However, clarity suffers slightly from the level of detail devoted to the optimization algorithm (Section 4), which feels disproportionately lengthy and doesn't contribute significantly to understanding the core contribution. The experimental setup is adequately described.

The primary weakness lies in the originality and significance of the work. Matrix completion and LSI are established techniques. Combining them, while potentially beneficial, doesn’t represent a groundbreaking innovation. The authors claim novelty in applying matrix completion *before* LSI in this context, but the rationale for this specific order isn’t sufficiently convincing. Why would completing the matrix *before* dimensionality reduction be superior to performing dimensionality reduction first and then attempting completion on a lower-dimensional space? This isn't explored. Furthermore, the performance gains demonstrated in the experiments, while statistically significant, aren’t substantial enough to justify the added complexity of the two-step process compared to existing methods like CL-KCCA or CL-OPCA. The performance improvements are largely incremental, and the benefits do not convincingly outweigh the computational cost of matrix completion, particularly for large datasets. 

The extensive detail on the optimization algorithm feels unnecessary for a NIPS paper. While mathematical rigor is appreciated, the focus should be on the *impact* of the method, not the intricacies of its implementation. The convergence analysis is standard and doesn’t offer novel insights.

The experiments, while reasonably comprehensive, primarily focus on sentiment classification with Amazon reviews. While this is a common benchmark, it’s limited. Exploring different datasets, classification tasks, or language pairs would strengthen the generalization of the findings. The analysis of the impact of the number of parallel documents is positive, but expected – more data generally helps. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant problem in NLP.
* The method is technically sound and well-implemented.
* Empirical results demonstrate improvements over baseline methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The approach lacks significant originality; it's an incremental combination of existing techniques.
* The performance gains are modest and do not justify the added complexity.
* The optimization details overshadow the core contribution.
* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.
* The rationale for the specific two-step approach (matrix completion *before* LSI) is not convincingly established.



Overall, this paper presents a solid but uninspired contribution. While technically correct and well-executed, it falls short of the novelty and significance expected for a NIPS publication. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should consider focusing on a more substantial innovation or providing a more compelling justification for the benefits of their specific approach. I would be more receptive to a revised version if the authors could demonstrate a significant performance advantage on more diverse datasets or provide a strong theoretical argument for why their method outperforms alternative approaches.
