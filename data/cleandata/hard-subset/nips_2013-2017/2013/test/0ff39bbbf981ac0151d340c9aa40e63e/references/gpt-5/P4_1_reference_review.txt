Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses cross-language text classification by learning shared document representations from a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. The proposed two-step method (TSL) (i) constructs a unified document-term matrix over the concatenated vocabularies of two languages, where non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language; (ii) performs matrix completion with a convex surrogate (trace norm + ℓ1 penalty, squared loss on observed entries, Tikhonov regularization on missing entries, and non-negativity constraints) optimized via a projected gradient descent scheme with singular value thresholding; and (iii) applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations on which standard monolingual classifiers are trained. Experiments on the Amazon multilingual reviews dataset (EN–FR/DE/JA; Books/DVD/Music) compare against TBOW, CL-LSI [Littman et al.], CL-OPCA [Platt et al.], and CL-KCCA [Vinokourov et al.], showing consistent gains, especially with few parallel documents.

The approach relates to prior cross-lingual representation learning that leverages parallel/comparable corpora: CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], CL-OPCA [15], topic models [19], and SCL with bilingual pivots [16]. Unlike these, TSL explicitly imputes missing entries in a bilingual matrix before dimensionality reduction. The optimization setup builds on trace-norm regularization and singular value thresholding (e.g., Ma et al. [11]) and standard matrix completion theory [4].

Strengths
- Clear problem setup and intuitive formulation: viewing non-parallel documents as partially observed rows and addressing cross-lingual co-occurrence via matrix completion is a coherent idea.
- Solid empirical gains across 18 tasks, with especially strong performance when the amount of parallel data is small (np=200), highlighting robustness to scarce bilingual supervision.
- Sensible baselines and consistent evaluation protocol (same SVM, dimensionalities selected once and reused; multiple random splits).
- The optimization algorithm is simple to implement and scales via first-order methods; the LSI step is standard and widely used.

Weaknesses and detailed assessment
Quality (technical soundness):
- Optimization correctness: The objective includes a nonsmooth trace norm and a non-negativity constraint (indicator function), in addition to a smooth term. The proposed iteration applies a gradient step on the smooth part, then the proximal for the trace norm (shrinkage), then a separate projection onto the nonnegative orthant. In general, prox(f+g) ≠ prox(f)∘prox(g); convergence guarantees from [11] (for trace norm without additional constraints) do not directly apply. The proof offered relies on non-expansiveness of the composed operators to claim convergence to an optimal solution, but non-expansiveness alone does not ensure convergence to the minimizer of the stated composite objective. A correct approach would use a three-operator splitting (forward–backward–forward), Dykstra’s algorithm, or ADMM to handle the sum of a proximable function and an indicator constraint with rigorous guarantees. This is the primary theoretical weakness.
- Regularization design: With M ≥ 0, the ℓ1 term becomes linear (μ∑ij Mij). Its practical role is unclear given the ρ-penalty on missing entries already shrinks towards zero; an ablation (μ=0 vs μ>0) is warranted to show necessity.
- Transductive setting: Representation learning uses all documents from both languages, including the target test set; this is common in CL-LSI/CCA but should be stated explicitly and reflected in baseline choices (e.g., a monolingual semi-supervised baseline that also uses unlabeled target documents).
- Missing computational details: No complexity analysis, runtime, convergence criteria, or number of iterations are reported. Given repeated SVDs per iteration, scalability to large vocabularies and corpora is a practical concern.
- Statistical reporting: Only averages and standard deviations are provided; no statistical significance tests are reported.

Clarity:
- Generally well organized, with clear notation and algorithm pseudocode. Some notational glitches (e.g., equation formatting) and minor language issues exist.
- Important experimental details are missing: preprocessing steps (stopword removal, vocab cutoff), vocabulary sizes, sparsity levels, convergence tolerance, and how SVD is computed (full vs truncated).
- The distinction between the proposed LSI step and CL-LSI should be clarified: CL-LSI is applied directly to concatenated (dual-language) documents, whereas TSL applies LSI after matrix completion over partially observed rows.

Originality:
- Combining matrix completion with LSI for cross-lingual representation appears to be a novel and reasonable synthesis; however, each component is standard. Related multi-view completion/imputation literature (e.g., collective matrix factorization, co-regularized factorization, semi-supervised NMF/tri-factorization) could be discussed more thoroughly to delineate novelty.
- Theoretical contribution is limited; the current convergence argument is not rigorous as stated.

Significance:
- On the classic Amazon dataset, reported improvements over CL-LSI/CL-OPCA/CL-KCCA are meaningful, especially under low parallel data. This suggests practical utility when small amounts of parallel data are available.
- However, the paper lacks comparisons to strong modern cross-lingual baselines (e.g., multilingual transformers such as mBERT/XLM-R, multilingual sentence embeddings such as LASER/LaBSE, or CLWE alignment methods) that are widely used for cross-lingual text classification. Without these, it is hard to assess contemporary impact.
- Generalization beyond the chosen dataset and languages is not demonstrated.

Constructive suggestions
- Replace the current optimization with a method that has guarantees for the sum of trace norm and non-negativity constraint (e.g., proximal Dykstra, ADMM, or three-operator splitting), and revise the convergence proof accordingly. Alternatively, drop the explicit constraint and use a nonnegativity-promoting regularizer with a single proximal step if appropriate.
- Provide computational complexity, wall-clock runtimes, SVD implementation details (e.g., PROPACK/ randomized SVD), and convergence diagnostics.
- Add ablations for μ and ρ, and for the two-step design (LSI with vs. without completion).
- Clarify the transductive setting and include a monolingual semi-supervised baseline that uses the same unlabeled target data.
- Include comparisons to modern cross-lingual representation methods (mBERT/XLM-R fine-tuning, adapters, sentence embeddings, CLWE alignment), and translation-based baselines (MT + monolingual classifier), to position the contribution in today’s landscape.
- Report additional diagnostics: alignment of parallel pairs in the learned space, sensitivity to k, and robustness across categories/languages.
- Improve clarity in equations and add missing experimental details.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear and intuitive formulation; practical and empirically effective on a standard benchmark.
- Demonstrated robustness with very limited parallel data.
- Simple pipeline that can be reused with off-the-shelf classifiers.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Theoretical issues with the optimization/proof; current convergence claim is not justified.
- Missing comparisons to strong contemporary baselines, limiting significance.
- Incomplete reporting of computational aspects and experimental details.
- Moderate novelty (combination of known components).

Recommendation
At present, due to the theoretical gap and missing contemporary baselines, I recommend rejection. With a corrected optimization method/proof, stronger baselines, and fuller empirical and computational analysis, the work could be competitive.
