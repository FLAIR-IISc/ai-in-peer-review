

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning that addresses the challenge of bridging language gaps in cross-lingual text classification. The core idea is novel: instead of directly applying latent semantic indexing (LSI) to a partially observed bilingual document-term matrix (as done in prior work like CL-LSI), the authors first use matrix completion to fill in missing entries based on co-occurrence patterns across languages, then apply LSI to the completed matrix. This approach leverages a small set of unlabeled parallel documents to create a more robust cross-lingual representation.

The paper makes a clear distinction from related work in Section 2, particularly from CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], and CL-OPCA [15], which apply dimensionality reduction directly to the concatenated document-term matrix without addressing the missing entries. The authors' matrix completion approach is theoretically sound and well-motivated by the low-rank nature of document-term matrices. The proposed projected gradient descent algorithm (Algorithm 1) provides a scalable solution to the non-smooth convex optimization problem, with a solid convergence analysis (Theorem 1).

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks on Amazon product reviews across English, French, German, and Japanese. The results (Table 1 and Figures 1-2) show consistent improvements over baselines: TSL outperforms CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA across all 18 tasks, with gains of 8.59-14.61% over the target baseline (TBOW). Most impressively, TSL achieves strong performance even with very limited parallel data (200 documents), while other methods degrade significantly. This practical advantage with minimal parallel data is particularly valuable for real-world applications where labeled parallel data is scarce.

**Strengths:**
1. **High novelty:** The matrix completion approach to filling missing entries in bilingual document-term matrices before LSI is a fresh perspective that differs significantly from prior methods.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The consistent performance gains across multiple language pairs and domains are convincing.
3. **Practical significance:** The method works well with minimal parallel data, addressing a key practical constraint in cross-lingual learning.
4. **Clear technical contribution:** The mathematical formulation and optimization algorithm are well-developed and properly justified.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited theoretical justification:** While the matrix completion approach is motivated by the low-rank property of document-term matrices, the paper doesn't provide a rigorous argument for why matrix completion would outperform direct LSI on the observed data (e.g., through error bounds).
2. **Comparison with recent methods:** The paper doesn't compare against more recent cross-lingual representation learning approaches that might use neural networks (e.g., multilingual BERT), though this is partly justified by the paper's focus on matrix-based methods.
3. **Ablation studies:** The paper doesn't include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (matrix completion vs. LSI).

**Originality Assessment:**
The paper's core idea—using matrix completion to fill missing entries in bilingual document-term matrices before applying LSI—is genuinely novel. While the general concept of using bilingual documents for cross-lingual learning is established, the specific two-step approach (completion followed by LSI) has not appeared in prior NIPS or related work. The distinction from CL-LSI [10] is particularly clear and well-articulated.

**Significance Assessment:**
The method represents a meaningful advancement in cross-lingual learning, with clear practical benefits (better performance with less parallel data). The approach is applicable to a wide range of cross-lingual tasks and could inspire similar techniques in other representation learning domains. The paper demonstrates a clear improvement over the state-of-the-art, not just incremental gains.

**Quality and Clarity:**
The paper is technically sound, with a well-structured presentation of the method, algorithm, and experiments. The mathematical formulations are precise, and the experimental setup is thorough. The writing is clear and accessible to NIPS readers.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend acceptance. This paper presents a novel and impactful approach to cross-language representation learning that addresses a meaningful problem in NLP. While the theoretical justification could be strengthened, the methodological novelty and strong empirical results outweigh this limitation. The paper's focus on a small set of unlabeled parallel documents is particularly valuable for practical applications. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper's contribution significant and worthy of publication at NIPS.

The paper makes a clear, valuable contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning, with a fresh perspective that could inspire future work. The experimental results are convincing, and the method is both theoretically grounded and practically useful. I believe this paper would be of great interest to the NIPS community.
