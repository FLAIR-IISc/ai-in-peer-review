## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification. The method addresses the language barrier by first constructing a concatenated document-term matrix with missing values representing documents not having a parallel translation. It then employs matrix completion to impute the missing values, leveraging co-occurrence patterns, followed by Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on cross-lingual sentiment classification with Amazon product reviews, showing superior performance to several baselines, even with limited parallel data.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a reasonable and well-motivated approach to cross-lingual representation learning. The idea of leveraging matrix completion to bridge the vocabulary gap and capture cross-lingual relationships is intuitive. The experimental results are promising, demonstrating a consistent performance gain over existing methods. However, the paper lacks significant theoretical novelty, and the empirical gains, while consistent, aren't overwhelmingly large.  The writing is generally clear, but could benefit from more concise explanations and a more critical discussion of limitations. 

**Detailed Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality (7/10):** The technical aspects of the method – matrix completion and LSI – are well-established. The application to cross-lingual learning is sensible. The optimization algorithm details are provided, including convergence analysis, which is a strong point. However, the theoretical justification for *why* this specific matrix completion approach (with the chosen regularization) is particularly well-suited to cross-lingual learning feels weak. The experiments are well-designed, but the parameter selection is somewhat opaque ("performed model parameter selection" – more detail is needed here).  While the authors mention observing convergence, more details on the convergence criteria would be beneficial. The loss function chosen for matrix completion also appears somewhat arbitrary.
* **Clarity (7/10):** The paper is generally well-organized and written. However, some sections (specifically Section 4 - Optimization) are dense and could benefit from simplification.  The notation is consistent, and the flow of the approach is easy to follow. The motivation for certain design choices (e.g., the choice of the ℓ1 norm in matrix completion) could be explained more clearly.  The presentation of the experimental setup is good, but could be more concise, perhaps by using a table to summarize the task configurations.
* **Originality (6/10):** The core techniques (matrix completion, LSI) are not new. The novelty lies in the specific application and combination of these techniques to the cross-lingual setting. While this is a valid contribution, it’s incremental rather than groundbreaking. The paper acknowledges related work reasonably well, but there is a slight tendency to downplay the similarity to existing cross-lingual representation learning methods.  For example, mentioning the relationship to techniques like Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) more explicitly would be helpful.
* **Significance (7/10):** The problem of cross-lingual learning remains important, and any improvement in performance is valuable. The results demonstrate a consistent improvement over established baselines. The finding that the method performs well even with limited parallel data is particularly encouraging. However, the magnitude of the performance gains is not dramatic. It's unclear whether this method would be preferred over simpler, more efficient approaches in many practical scenarios.  The impact of this method will likely be as one piece of the puzzle in a larger cross-lingual learning system.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear motivation and well-defined problem.
*   Solid experimental validation on a standard dataset.
*   Strong performance compared to several baselines.
*   Detailed optimization algorithm and convergence analysis.
*   Demonstrated robustness with limited parallel data.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Lack of substantial theoretical novelty.
*   Incremental contribution – relies heavily on existing techniques.
*   The choice of regularization terms in matrix completion feels somewhat arbitrary.
*   Parameter selection process lacks detailed explanation.
*   The magnitude of performance gains is not substantial.
*   Limited discussion of the computational complexity of the matrix completion step (especially scalability to large datasets).



**Arguments Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem in NLP.
*   Presents a technically sound and well-validated method.
*   Demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines.
*   The convergence analysis adds credibility.

**Con:**

*   Lacks significant theoretical novelty.
*   The incremental nature of the contribution may not meet the high bar for NIPS.
*   The performance gains are not transformative.
*   The computational cost could be a limitation for large-scale applications.




**Recommendation:**

**Weak Accept.**

While this paper doesn't present a revolutionary new idea, it’s a solid and well-executed contribution. The method is sensible, the experiments are thorough, and the results are encouraging.  I believe it meets the minimum quality standards for NIPS, but it's not a standout paper.  I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above – specifically providing a more convincing justification for the matrix completion approach, elaborating on the parameter selection process, and discussing the computational complexity and scalability of the method.
