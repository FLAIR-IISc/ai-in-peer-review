

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that leverages unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The authors first construct a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix where parallel documents are fully observed and non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language's vocabulary. They then solve a matrix completion problem to fill in missing entries, followed by applying latent semantic indexing (LSI) to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The method is evaluated on 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews across four languages.

The paper positions itself as different from prior work (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) by first completing the matrix before applying LSI, rather than applying LSI directly to the incomplete matrix. The authors claim their method significantly outperforms baselines and requires fewer parallel documents.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Statement**: The paper effectively articulates the cross-language text classification problem and the language barrier challenge, providing a solid foundation for the proposed method.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The matrix completion formulation (Equation 2) is well-motivated, and the convergence analysis (Theorem 1) provides theoretical grounding for the optimization algorithm. The use of a projected gradient descent algorithm for large-scale matrix completion is appropriate.

3. **Practical Contribution**: The demonstration that the method works well with very small amounts of unlabeled parallel data (200 documents) is a significant practical contribution, as parallel data is often scarce in cross-lingual applications.

4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation**: The paper conducts extensive experiments across 18 different language pairs and categories, with careful analysis of the impact of parallel data quantity (Figures 1-2).

5. **Good Related Work**: The related work section comprehensively covers relevant approaches and clearly positions the proposed method within the literature, distinguishing it from prior work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares only to four methods (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA), but the field has advanced significantly since these methods were proposed. Recent state-of-the-art approaches like multilingual BERT (mBERT), XLM-R, and other transformer-based cross-lingual models should have been included for comparison. Without this, the claimed "significant outperformance" lacks context.

2. **Insufficient Theoretical Justification**: While the paper provides a convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm, it lacks theoretical justification for the specific combination of regularization terms (γ‖M‖* + µ‖M‖1) and the use of least squares loss. Why is this specific formulation better than alternatives?

3. **Parameter Selection Process**: The paper selects parameters based on the EFB task and then uses the same parameters for all tasks. This is potentially problematic as different language pairs may require different parameter settings. The paper should have used a more rigorous validation procedure.

4. **Ambiguous Vocabulary Construction**: The paper states "the unified vocabulary set of the two languages" but doesn't explain how the vocabulary is unified or how the parallel documents connect the vocabularies. This is critical for understanding how the method bridges the language gap.

5. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (matrix completion vs. LSI) or to understand why matrix completion improves performance over direct application of LSI.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound in its formulation and optimization approach, and the empirical results are well-presented. However, the quality is limited by the lack of comparison to more recent methods and the insufficient theoretical justification for the specific regularization approach. The paper doesn't fully address potential weaknesses of the proposed method (e.g., sensitivity to the quality of the parallel data, performance on low-resource languages).

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The main ideas are presented clearly, and the technical details are sufficiently explained. However, some aspects could be clarified:

- The transition from the document-term matrix to the matrix completion problem could be more clearly explained.
- The rationale for using least squares loss for missing entries (c(Mij, 0)) rather than other approaches (e.g., zero imputation or more sophisticated methods) could be better justified.
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed description of how the unified vocabulary is constructed.

## Originality Assessment

The two-step approach (matrix completion followed by LSI) is a reasonable idea that builds on existing work. However, the novelty is somewhat incremental rather than groundbreaking. The paper cites relevant prior work but doesn't sufficiently establish how this specific combination of techniques represents a significant advance over existing methods. The paper would have been stronger with a more thorough comparison to recent approaches that might have similar characteristics.

## Significance Assessment

The significance is somewhat limited by the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods. The practical advantage of working with small amounts of parallel data is valuable, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how this compares to recent neural approaches that might also work with limited parallel data. The 8.59-14.61% accuracy improvement over the baseline is notable, but without context of current state-of-the-art, it's difficult to assess the true significance of this contribution.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The paper presents a technically sound approach with a clear contribution in the specific context of using minimal parallel data, and the experimental results are promising. However, the lack of comparison to more recent methods significantly limits the paper's impact and makes it difficult to assess the true novelty and significance of the contribution.

## Specific Recommendations for Improvement

1. Include comparisons to at least two recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual methods (e.g., mBERT, XLM-R, or other transformer-based approaches) to properly contextualize the results.

2. Provide a more detailed explanation of how the unified vocabulary is constructed and how parallel documents bridge the language gap.

3. Conduct ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the matrix completion step versus direct application of LSI.

4. Justify the specific choice of regularization terms (γ‖M‖* + µ‖M‖1) more thoroughly, perhaps with a small theoretical analysis or empirical comparison of different regularization strategies.

5. Use a more rigorous parameter selection procedure (e.g., cross-validation across multiple tasks) rather than selecting parameters based on a single task.

## Final Assessment

The paper makes a reasonable contribution to the field of cross-lingual representation learning, particularly in the context of limited parallel data. While the method is well-motivated and the experiments are thorough within the scope presented, the lack of comparison to current state-of-the-art methods limits the paper's significance for a venue like NIPS. With the recommended revisions, this paper could make a stronger contribution to the field.
