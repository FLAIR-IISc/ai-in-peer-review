

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that addresses the core challenge of bridging language barriers in cross-language text classification. The key insight is to construct a unified document-term matrix with missing values for non-parallel documents, then use matrix completion to recover the low-rank structure before applying latent semantic indexing. The authors claim this approach significantly outperforms existing methods like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, particularly when only small amounts of parallel data are available.

The paper's main contribution is conceptually novel in the context of cross-lingual representation learning. While prior work (like CL-LSI) applies LSI directly to the concatenated document-term matrix, this paper recognizes that the matrix is highly incomplete (with missing values for non-parallel documents) and proposes to first fill these missing values through matrix completion. This is not merely a technical tweak but a fundamental shift in how to handle the data structure, treating the problem as a matrix completion task rather than directly applying dimensionality reduction to incomplete data. The authors provide a convex optimization formulation with a projected gradient descent algorithm and prove convergence, adding theoretical rigor to the approach.

The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines across 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks on Amazon product reviews. The paper shows that the proposed method achieves 8.59-14.61% accuracy gains over the baseline and outperforms all other methods, with particularly strong performance when only 200 unlabeled parallel documents are available. This practical advantage for low-resource settings is significant, as it addresses a major limitation of existing approaches that often require substantial parallel data.

The paper excels in originality, which is the reviewer's highest priority. The approach is genuinely novel in the cross-lingual learning space, not just a minor variation of existing techniques. The authors clearly distinguish their work from related approaches in the literature (particularly CL-LSI), and the method represents a new direction in how we think about cross-lingual representation learning. While the paper doesn't claim to solve all problems in the field, it introduces a significant new concept that could inspire future work.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important practical challenge in NLP: reducing annotation effort for low-resource languages. The method's ability to work with very small amounts of parallel data makes it particularly valuable for real-world applications where parallel data is scarce. The results demonstrate a demonstrable advance over the state of the art, and the approach could be readily adopted by practitioners.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient technical detail to reproduce the results. The mathematical formulation is sound, and the convergence analysis adds credibility. Minor weaknesses include limited analysis of *why* the matrix completion approach works better than alternatives (e.g., why not use simple imputation?), and the paper doesn't discuss computational scalability for very large datasets, though these are secondary to the core novelty.

This paper stands out from the typical incremental improvements common in the field. It presents a bold, novel approach to a well-known problem that fundamentally changes how we think about the data structure in cross-lingual learning. While the methodology is not perfect (the matrix completion approach could potentially be improved), the novelty and significance outweigh these minor issues.

The paper's contribution is clearly distinct from previous NIPS work in the area. While prior papers have explored cross-lingual representation learning (e.g., CL-LSI, CL-KCCA), none have proposed using matrix completion as a preprocessing step before applying LSI. The authors have thoroughly reviewed the literature and positioned their work appropriately.

In summary, this paper introduces a genuinely novel approach that addresses a significant challenge in cross-lingual learning with practical advantages. The experimental results support the claim of superiority, and the method represents a new direction that could inspire future research. While not perfect, the novelty and significance of the work make it a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.

Strengths:
- Genuine novelty in approach (matrix completion as preprocessing for cross-lingual representation)
- Clear distinction from related work
- Strong experimental results with significant accuracy gains
- Practical advantage (works well with very small parallel data)
- Sound theoretical foundation

Weaknesses:
- Limited analysis of why matrix completion works better than alternatives
- Minimal discussion of computational scalability
- Some technical details in optimization section could be more accessible

Recommendation: Accept. The paper's novel concept and strong empirical results make it a significant contribution to the field, aligning well with NIPS' emphasis on innovative ideas over incremental improvements.
