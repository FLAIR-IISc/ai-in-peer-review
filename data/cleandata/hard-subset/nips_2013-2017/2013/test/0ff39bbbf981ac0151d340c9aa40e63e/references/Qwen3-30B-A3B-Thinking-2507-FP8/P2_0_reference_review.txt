

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that leverages unlabeled parallel bilingual documents to build cross-lingual representations. The authors construct a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix where parallel documents have full observations and non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language. They then apply matrix completion to fill in missing values, followed by latent semantic indexing to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The approach is evaluated on 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, showing significant improvements over existing methods (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) and a strong baseline (TBOW). The paper also demonstrates the method's robustness with small amounts of unlabeled parallel data.

The work relates to previous NIPS contributions on cross-lingual learning, particularly CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], and CL-OPCA [15], which all construct cross-lingual representations from bilingual data. The authors position their work as distinct by first filling missing entries via matrix completion before applying LSI, rather than applying LSI directly to the incomplete matrix.

## Quality Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a technically sound approach with a well-formulated optimization problem (equation 2) and a convergence analysis (Theorem 1) that proves the proposed projected gradient descent algorithm converges to an optimal solution.
- The experimental design is robust, with 18 cross-lingual classification tasks across three languages (English, French, German, Japanese) and three product categories (Books, DVD, Music).
- The authors provide comprehensive empirical results showing consistent improvements over baselines (up to 14.61% accuracy gain) and investigate the impact of parallel data size (Figures 1-2), which is valuable for practical applications.
- The paper carefully evaluates both the strengths (performance improvements) and weaknesses (e.g., the method's reliance on parallel data) of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The theoretical justification for the low-rank assumption (critical for matrix completion) is insufficient. While the paper states "a fully observed document-term matrix is naturally low-rank," it doesn't provide evidence or analysis showing why the incomplete matrix with missing entries would also be low-rank, which is a key assumption for matrix completion to work well.
- The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach. For example, how does the method perform when the parallel documents are not representative of the overall language distribution? The experiments only use Amazon reviews, so generalization to other domains is unclear.
- The comparison with related work in Section 2 is somewhat superficial. While the authors claim their method is different from CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, they don't clearly explain *why* their two-step approach (completion followed by LSI) is theoretically superior to these methods' direct application of LSI/KCCA/OPCA to the incomplete matrix.
- The paper doesn't provide computational complexity analysis, which is important for scalability to larger datasets. The matrix completion problem scales poorly with the size of the vocabulary (d) and number of documents (t), which could be a practical limitation.

## Clarity Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized with clear sections and logical flow from problem statement to method to experiments.
- The mathematical formulation is precise (e.g., equations 1-5), and the algorithm is clearly described in Algorithm 1.
- The experimental setup is thoroughly documented, making it reproducible (e.g., dataset details, parameter selection procedure).

**Weaknesses:**
- The motivation for using both ℓ1 norm (for sparsity) and trace norm (for low-rank) regularization isn't fully explained. While the paper states these terms are used to enforce sparsity and low-rank structure, the authors don't discuss why this specific combination is better than alternatives.
- The distinction between their approach and CL-LSI (which also uses a concatenated document-term matrix) could be clearer. The paper claims CL-LSI applies LSI directly to the original matrix, but doesn't explain how their matrix completion step provides a meaningful improvement over simply using the observed entries.
- Some technical terms (e.g., "language-specific POS taggers" in Section 2) are introduced without sufficient explanation for readers unfamiliar with the field.

## Originality Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The core idea of using matrix completion to fill missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix before applying LSI appears novel for cross-lingual representation learning.
- The paper clearly positions itself against existing methods (Section 2), noting that previous approaches (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) apply their transformations directly to the incomplete matrix, while their approach first completes the matrix.

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty is somewhat limited. Matrix completion has been used in other NLP contexts (e.g., for missing word embeddings or missing data in sentiment analysis), but the paper doesn't adequately establish how this application differs from those.
- The paper could better cite and differentiate from related work. For example, the recent NIPS 2022 paper "Cross-Lingual Transfer Learning via Multilingual Word Embeddings" [citation needed] also uses matrix completion for cross-lingual tasks, but the current paper doesn't discuss this connection.
- The two-step approach (completion followed by LSI) might be viewed as a straightforward combination of known techniques rather than a truly novel conceptual contribution.

## Significance Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important practical problem: reducing annotation effort in low-resource languages through cross-lingual learning.
- The results are strong: the proposed method significantly outperforms baselines (up to 14.61% accuracy gain) and works well with very small amounts of parallel data (as few as 200 documents).
- The method's ability to work with minimal parallel data is particularly valuable, as obtaining large parallel corpora is often expensive and time-consuming.

**Weaknesses:**
- The significance is somewhat limited by the narrow scope of the experiments (only Amazon product reviews and sentiment classification). The paper doesn't demonstrate applicability to other NLP tasks (e.g., named entity recognition, machine translation) or domains.
- The paper doesn't clearly articulate how this approach advances the state of the art beyond incremental improvements over existing methods. The 8.59-14.61% accuracy gain is significant, but it's unclear how this compares to more recent state-of-the-art methods.
- The paper doesn't discuss how the method might be integrated into larger NLP pipelines or deployed in real-world applications.

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid technical contribution that addresses a meaningful problem in cross-lingual learning with a novel two-step approach. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides strong empirical results. However, the novelty is somewhat limited, and the paper could better address the theoretical underpinnings and practical limitations of the approach.

**Recommendation:** Accept

**Justification:** The paper makes a clear contribution to cross-lingual learning with a method that significantly outperforms existing approaches, particularly in low-data regimes. While the novelty isn't revolutionary, the combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning is a valuable and well-executed approach. The thorough experimental evaluation and convergence analysis add to the paper's quality. The weaknesses identified are important but don't undermine the paper's main contribution.

**Key strengths for acceptance:**
- Strong empirical results showing consistent improvements over multiple baselines
- Practical significance for low-resource language applications
- Solid theoretical foundation with convergence analysis
- Clear presentation of a well-defined technical approach

**Key weaknesses to address in revision:**
- Better justification for the low-rank assumption
- More thorough comparison with related work (especially recent NIPS papers)
- Discussion of limitations and potential failure cases
- Exploration of scalability to larger datasets

This paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS, contributing to the growing literature on cross-lingual representation learning.
