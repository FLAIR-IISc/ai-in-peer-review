- Proposes a two-step cross-lingual representation method: convex matrix completion to fill a unified bilingual document–term matrix, then LSI/SVD to get a shared low-dimensional space.
- Treats non-parallel documents as partially observed rows and uses a small set of parallel documents to bridge vocabularies across languages.
- Matrix completion objective combines nuclear norm (low rank), ℓ1 norm (sparsity), least-squares fit on observed entries, a regularizer on missing entries (ρ), and non-negativity constraints.
- Optimization via projected gradient descent with singular-value shrinkage and non-negativity projection; convergence argued using non-expansiveness of the composite operator.
- After completion, standard LSI produces a cross-lingual representation usable by monolingual classifiers.
- Closely related to CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA and SCL/topic models, but novel in applying matrix completion prior to dimensionality reduction.
- Notes missing empirical comparison to emerging cross-lingual embeddings (e.g., Klementiev et al., Mikolov et al.) which could be complementary baselines.
- Empirical evaluation: 18 EN–FR/DE/JP Amazon sentiment tasks, 100 labeled target examples, parallel data 200–2000 pairs, hyperparameters tuned on one task and reused.
- Reports consistent outperformance of TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA, with largest gains when parallel data is scarce; accuracy reported as mean±std over 10 splits.
- Optimization problem is well-motivated and convex after relaxation; algorithm and convergence argument are appropriate and standard.
- Strengths include robustness with low parallel data, clear performance margins, and a reproducible experimental setup with relevant baselines.
- Main critique: missing runtime/complexity and memory analysis (per-iteration cost, truncated SVD use) to support scalability claims.
- Important clarifying point: representation learning uses all unlabeled target documents (including test instances), i.e., the setting is transductive and may inflate results.
- Requested ablations: (i) matrix completion alone vs completion+LSI, (ii) effects of each regularizer (nuclear norm, ℓ1, nonnegativity, ρ).
- Statistical reporting should include significance testing (paired tests) for key improvements, not only mean±std.
- Ask for focused sensitivity analysis to ρ (bias on missing entries), γ, and k, since bias toward zero may hurt rare features.
- Clarity suggestions: better motivate matrix completion in Introduction via low-rank semantics, define “observed” vs “missing” early with a toy example, and explain nonnegativity’s role after shrinkage.
- Highlight that all documents from both languages are used in representation learning and make that explicit.
- Originality: novel and elegant application of convex matrix completion to cross-lingual pre-processing; combination of known components is practically meaningful.
- Significance: empirically useful preprocessing step, especially under limited parallel data, and compatible with many monolingual classifiers; broader impact would be stronger with comparisons to embeddings and tasks beyond sentiment.
- Pros for acceptance: principled convex formulation with convergence, strong empirical improvements, robustness with small parallel corpora, simple and compatible method.
- Cons against acceptance: lacks runtime/scale analysis, transductive setting concern, missing comparisons to cross-lingual embeddings, and unsubstantiated theoretical justification for low rank in document-term matrices.
- Suggested improvements: add wall-clock and memory benchmarks and SVD implementation details, include ablation suite and inductive-only experiments, compare to cross-lingual embeddings, provide qualitative nearest-neighbor examples, and evaluate multi-language/general-task transfer.
- Overall verdict: solid, well-executed paper with a simple effective idea that performs well in low-parallel regimes but needs scalability analysis and deeper ablations to be a stronger NIPS contribution.
