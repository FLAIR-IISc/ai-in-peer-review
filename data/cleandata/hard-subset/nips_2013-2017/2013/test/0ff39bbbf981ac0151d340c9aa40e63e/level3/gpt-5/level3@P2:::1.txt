Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual text classification. Step 1 formulates a matrix completion problem on a unified document-term matrix that concatenates vocabularies of two languages: parallel document pairs have complete rows; non-parallel documents have missing features for the other language. The objective combines a trace norm (low rank), an l1 norm (sparsity), squared loss on observed entries (with nonnegativity), and squared loss toward zero on missing entries, and is optimized by a projected gradient descent algorithm with shrinkage; convergence is established. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation on which a standard linear SVM is trained.

The approach is positioned against translation-based baselines and cross-lingual representation methods that use parallel or comparable data, notably CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA. Unlike CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA that operate directly on concatenated or paired data, this work first imputes the missing block entries via matrix completion, then reduces dimensionality.

On Amazon multilingual sentiment (Books/DVD/Music across English, French, German, Japanese), the method outperforms TBOW and generally outperforms CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, especially with few parallel documents.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple and practically appealing: the unified incomplete matrix captures both within- and cross-language co-occurrence; completion exploits low-rank structure, followed by a well-understood semantic reduction (LSI).
- A scalable projected gradient method with convergence guarantees is clearly described, including the shrinkage and nonnegativity projection.
- Strong empirical results across 18 adaptation directions, with notable robustness when parallel data are scarce.
- The paper is generally well written and situates itself among CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA and related cross-lingual learning literature.

Weaknesses and questions for authors
- Rationale for the LSI step: After estimating M*, why is a further low-dimensional projection necessary? Is the motivation computational (M* becomes denser despite l1), statistical (denoising), or both? Why LSI specifically rather than PCA (with centering), NMF, or continuing the rank control within the completion step (e.g., setting γ to achieve the desired effective rank)? An ablation comparing (i) using M* directly, (ii) LSI on the original incomplete M0 (treating missing as zero), and (iii) LSI on only the fully observed parallel rows would help isolate the benefit of completion versus LSI.
- Low-rank assumption: The paper states that a fully observed document-term matrix is “naturally low-rank and sparse.” While sparsity is clear, the low-rank claim is not obvious for BoW data; rank can be full though often approximable by low-rank. Please clarify the modeling assumption: is the objective leveraging approximate low-rank with nuclear norm as a regularizer? What intuition supports low-rank here (e.g., shared latent topics/sentiments across languages)? Also, the missingness pattern is highly structured (entire language blocks per non-parallel document). Typical matrix completion guarantees assume random missingness; please discuss identifiability under this pattern and whether the added l2 penalty on missing entries toward zero (ρ-term) is primarily a regularizer or a prior.
- Intuition vs. CCA-based methods: CCA feels natural for bilingual alignment. Why does TSL often outperform CL-KCCA? Is the gain mostly from leveraging monolingual co-occurrence from all documents (including non-parallel ones) during completion, whereas CCA uses only parallel pairs? Any analysis (e.g., nearest-neighbor alignment quality across languages, or topic coherence) that sheds light on where the advantage comes from would be valuable.
- Experimental setup clarity: Please confirm the protocol: for each task, (i) representation learning uses all documents from both languages plus 2k unlabeled parallel pairs; (ii) training uses all labeled source-language documents (4k English or 2k in other languages?), plus 100 labeled target documents; (iii) test is on the remaining ≈1.9k target documents; and (iv) Section 5.3 varies the number of parallel pairs from 200 to 2000 while keeping all other data fixed. It would help to explicitly separate and report the roles of external data: labeled source-language data vs. unlabeled parallel data.
- Inconsistency to address: In Fig. 1, CL-KCCA appears to outperform TSL for EFM and EGM at 2k parallel documents, whereas Table 1 reports TSL ahead on average. Please reconcile (e.g., differences in averaging, variance, or parameter settings).
- Runtime and scalability: Please provide running-time and memory comparisons for TSL (including the SVD) versus CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, as a function of vocabulary size, number of documents, and number of parallel pairs. How many iterations are typical, and what is the wall-clock time per dataset?
- Parameter sensitivity: A brief sensitivity plot for γ, ρ, and k would be helpful, especially since you select parameters on EFB and transfer them to all tasks.
- Statistical significance: You report means and standard deviations; please add significance tests where differences are small.

Quality
- The optimization is sound and the convergence argument for the projected gradient with shrinkage and nonnegativity projection is appropriate. More discussion on identifiability under structured missingness would strengthen the theory. Experiments are thorough across languages/categories, but additional ablations and runtime comparisons are needed.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized. The experimental protocol and the role of each data source should be spelled out more explicitly. Minor editorial fixes below.

Originality
- The combination—matrix completion on a bilingual concatenated matrix followed by LSI—appears novel relative to CL-LSI/CL-KCCA/CL-OPCA and multi-view methods that either translate or directly project paired data.

Significance
- The empirical gains, especially in low-parallel-data regimes, are practically meaningful for cross-lingual classification. If runtime is competitive, the approach could be adopted by practitioners.

Minor editorial suggestions
- Before Eq. (2), change (ij) to (i,j).
- Remove “such as” before Eqs. (4) and (5).
- Define all symbols upon first use (e.g., clarify Ω, Ω̂, Y, Ŷ inline).
- Briefly state how τ is chosen in practice and whether backtracking is used.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, general method with convergence guarantee.
- Strong and robust empirical performance, especially with limited parallel data.
- Uses both parallel and non-parallel documents effectively.

Cons for acceptance
- Modeling assumptions (low-rank, structured missingness) need clearer justification.
- Some inconsistencies/ambiguities in experimental reporting.
- Missing runtime/scalability analysis and ablations to disentangle where gains come from.

Overall recommendation
- Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the experimental protocol, reconciling the CL-KCCA discrepancy, and adding runtime comparisons and brief ablations/justifications for the LSI step and low-rank assumption.
