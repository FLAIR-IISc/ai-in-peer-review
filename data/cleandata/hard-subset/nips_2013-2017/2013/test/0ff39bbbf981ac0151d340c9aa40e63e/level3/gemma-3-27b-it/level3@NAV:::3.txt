## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, leveraging matrix completion to address the disjoint feature space problem inherent in cross-lingual text classification. The authors first construct a partially observed document-term matrix using parallel bilingual documents and then apply matrix completion to fill in missing values, followed by Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation. This approach builds upon prior work in cross-lingual learning, such as machine translation-based methods [18, 6], and representation learning using bilingual resources [16, 10].  The paper positions itself within the landscape of methods like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA [10, 15, 20] but differentiates itself by using matrix completion *before* applying dimensionality reduction.

The core idea is novel and potentially promising. Matrix completion is a well-controlled non-linear inference technique, and its application to bridge cross-lingual feature spaces is a worthwhile exploration. The paper is clearly written and the proposed algorithm is well-defined, including a convergence analysis for the optimization procedure. The experimental setup, while somewhat fragmented (as the authors acknowledge by spreading details across the text and Table 1), provides reasonable comparisons against several baselines on the Amazon product review dataset.

However, a significant quality concern exists. The reported improvements over baseline methods, particularly CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, might be attributable to the scaling effect of LSI rather than the benefit of the proposed matrix completion step. The authors exclusively use a linear SVM as a classifier, which is known to be sensitive to feature scaling.  This introduces a potential confound: the matrix completion and subsequent LSI might simply be re-scaling features in a way that happens to benefit a linear SVM, without necessarily learning a better underlying representation. 

To address this concern, the authors *must* evaluate the method with a classifier that is insensitive to per-feature rescaling, such as a boosted decision tree (e.g., readily available in Weka, as suggested by the reviewer guidelines).  Without this evaluation, the claim of superior performance remains questionable. Furthermore, the clarity could be improved by explicitly stating whether the "unified document-term matrix" uses language-agnostic terms or language-specific surface forms—this is a crucial detail for understanding the method.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning.
*   Clear and well-defined algorithm with convergence guarantees.
*   Demonstrated improvement over baselines (though robustness is questionable – see below).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Results may be artifacts of LSI scaling benefiting a linear SVM.
*   Lack of evaluation with a classifier insensitive to feature scaling.
*   Ambiguity in the description of the unified document-term matrix.



**Recommendation:**  Reject with request for major revisions. The authors should perform the suggested experiments with a non-scaling-sensitive classifier and clarify the nature of the terms used in the unified document-term matrix.  If these revisions demonstrate robust improvements, the paper would become a strong contribution.
