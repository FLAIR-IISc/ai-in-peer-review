This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning, addressing the challenge of disjoint feature spaces in multilingual text classification. The core idea is to first employ matrix completion to fill missing values in a concatenated document-term matrix constructed from parallel bilingual documents, and then apply Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to induce a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation. This representation is subsequently used for sentiment classification. The method is evaluated on Amazon product reviews, demonstrating performance gains over several baseline cross-language learning approaches, particularly when the amount of parallel data is limited.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in cross-language text classification. Approaches leveraging machine translation [18, 6, 23] or bilingual lexicons are noted, as are methods constructing cross-lingual representations using resources like bilingual word pairs [16], comparable corpora [10, 20, 15], and multilingual resources [3, 14]. The authors correctly position their work within this landscape, drawing connections to methods like CL-LSI [10], CL-OPCA [15], and CL-KCCA [20], while highlighting the novelty of employing matrix completion *before* dimensionality reduction via LSI. The related work section is comprehensive and provides appropriate citations.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning appears to be a novel contribution. Matrix completion provides a principled way to infer relationships between languages, and the subsequent LSI step offers dimensionality reduction and noise filtering.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method consistently outperforms several established baselines, particularly in low-resource scenarios (small amounts of parallel data).
* **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a convergence analysis for the projected gradient descent algorithm used to solve the matrix completion problem, lending technical rigor to the approach.
* **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of cross-language text classification and the challenges posed by differing feature spaces.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Potential Confounding Factor:** The reported improvements could be, at least partially, attributable to the scaling properties of LSI when applied to a more complete matrix. The use of a linear SVM as the classifier might be overly sensitive to these scaling effects. It's unclear if the gains are due to the improved representation *itself*, or simply LSI being more effective when operating on a denser matrix.
* **Limited Classifier Evaluation:** Relying solely on a linear SVM limits the generalizability of the conclusions. The authors acknowledge the importance of robust evaluation, but do not explore alternative classifiers.
* **Clarity Concerns:** The description of the “unified document-term matrix” is somewhat ambiguous. It's unclear whether the vocabulary consists of language-agnostic concepts or language-specific surface forms. Clarifying this point would improve understanding.
* **Fragmented Experimental Setup:** The description of the experimental setup is spread across multiple sections, particularly Table 1, making it difficult to grasp the details at a glance. A more consolidated presentation would be beneficial.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear mathematical formulation and a convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm. However, the potential confounding factor related to the classifier choice raises concerns about the robustness of the conclusions. While the results are promising, more thorough evaluation is needed to confirm the effectiveness of the proposed method independent of LSI’s scaling properties.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the aforementioned ambiguities regarding the vocabulary and the fragmented experimental setup detract from its clarity. Addressing these points would significantly enhance readability.

**Originality:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning is a novel approach. The authors clearly differentiate their work from existing methods in the related work section.

**Significance:** If the improvements are demonstrably robust and not simply artifacts of LSI scaling, the proposed method holds significant potential. Matrix completion is a well-established technique for handling missing data, and its application to cross-lingual representation learning is a promising direction. The ability to effectively leverage limited parallel data is particularly valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel approach to cross-lingual representation learning.
* Strong empirical results on a standard dataset.
* Theoretical justification for the optimization algorithm.
* Potential to address a challenging problem in low-resource settings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Concerns about the robustness of the results due to the use of a linear SVM.
* Ambiguity in the description of the unified vocabulary.
* Fragmented experimental setup description.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns raised regarding the choice of classifier. Specifically, they should evaluate the method using a classifier less sensitive to per-feature rescaling, such as a boosted decision tree (e.g., using Weka’s implementation). Furthermore, the authors should clarify the nature of the vocabulary used in the unified document-term matrix and consolidate the description of the experimental setup into a single, coherent section. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase confidence in the reported results.
