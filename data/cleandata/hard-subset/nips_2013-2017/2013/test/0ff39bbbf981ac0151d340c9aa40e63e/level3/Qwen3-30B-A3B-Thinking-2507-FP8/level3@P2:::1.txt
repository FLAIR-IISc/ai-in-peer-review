

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification that addresses the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages. The authors construct a unified bilingual document-term matrix where parallel documents have complete rows and non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language's vocabulary. They then solve a matrix completion problem with low-rank and sparsity constraints using a projected gradient descent algorithm, followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) to produce cross-lingual representations. The method is evaluated on Amazon product reviews across multiple language pairs, showing significant improvements over baseline methods, particularly when parallel data is limited.

The work builds on previous NIPS contributions such as CL-LSI (from the 2009 NIPS proceedings) and CL-KCCA (from the 2010 NIPS proceedings), which also address cross-lingual representation learning. While these prior methods apply LSI, OPCA, or KCCA directly on the concatenated document-term matrix, this paper's key innovation is the two-step process: first completing the matrix to fill missing entries, then applying LSI on the completed matrix. This differs from approaches like CL-OPCA and CL-KCCA that use different projection methods directly on the original sparse matrix.

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound method with convergence guarantees for the optimization algorithm. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with 18 cross-language sentiment classification tasks across three language pairs and three product categories. The results consistently show that the proposed method (TSL) outperforms baselines, especially when parallel data is limited (e.g., 8.59-14.61% accuracy improvement over the baseline). However, some theoretical aspects require clarification: the claim that a fully observed document-term matrix is low-rank seems questionable given the sparsity of document representations and that documents aren't obvious linear combinations of each other. The paper would benefit from addressing this concern or providing stronger justification. Additionally, the experimental setup needs clarification: it's unclear whether the 2k unlabeled parallel documents are used in the matrix completion step for all experiments, and the training data composition (4k labeled source, 100 labeled target, 2k unlabeled parallel) should be explicitly stated.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized, though several minor editorial issues need correction: "(ij)" should be "(i,j)" before Equation (2), and "such as" should be removed before Equations (4) and (5). The authors should also explicitly distinguish how external data is used: labeled source documents (for classification) versus unlabeled parallel documents (for representation learning). The inconsistency between Figure 1 (showing CL-KCCA outperforming TSL for EFM/EGM at 2k parallel documents) and Table 1 (where TSL consistently outperforms CL-KCCA) needs explanation.

**Originality:** The two-step approach (matrix completion followed by LSI) is a novel combination that effectively addresses the challenge of sparse bilingual document-term matrices. The paper properly references related work, including several NIPS papers from 2009-2012, and clearly differentiates their method from prior approaches. The key insight that matrix completion can better capture cross-lingual word co-occurrence patterns than direct application of LSI or KCCA is well-motivated.

**Significance:** The method is significant because it achieves better cross-language classification performance with much less parallel data than competing methods, making it particularly valuable for practical applications where parallel data is scarce. The results demonstrate that TSL can achieve good performance with as few as 200 unlabeled parallel documents, while other methods show significant performance drops with limited parallel data. This addresses an important practical challenge in cross-language learning.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees
- Thorough experimental evaluation across multiple language pairs
- Demonstrates significant performance gains, especially with limited parallel data
- Simple yet effective approach that could be widely adopted

**Weaknesses:**
- Theoretical justification for the low-rank assumption needs strengthening
- Experimental details about data usage require clarification
- Inconsistency between Table 1 and Figure 1 for specific tasks
- Lack of running-time comparison with baseline methods
- Insufficient explanation for why LSI is used after matrix completion (vs. other dimensionality reduction techniques)

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel, effective approach to cross-language representation learning that builds on previous NIPS work while offering significant improvements. The main issues to address are the theoretical justification for the low-rank assumption, clarification of experimental details, and resolution of the inconsistency between Table 1 and Figure 1. The requested clarifications (particularly regarding the low-rank assumption and the LSI step) would strengthen the paper significantly.

The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates meaningful improvements over existing approaches. With the requested clarifications, this paper would make a valuable contribution to the field of cross-language learning.
