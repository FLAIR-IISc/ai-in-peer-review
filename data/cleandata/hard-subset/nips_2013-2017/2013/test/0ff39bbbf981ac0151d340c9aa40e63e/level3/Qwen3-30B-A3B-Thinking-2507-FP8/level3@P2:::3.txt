

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that first uses matrix completion to fill missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix and then applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to derive low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The method is evaluated on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews across English, French, German, and Japanese. The authors claim their approach significantly outperforms baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA, and a target-only baseline) especially with limited parallel data.

**Originality and Significance**: The core idea of using matrix completion to bridge language-specific feature spaces is novel within the context of cross-lingual learning at NIPS. While prior work (e.g., CL-LSI, CL-KCCA) uses direct dimensionality reduction on concatenated matrices, this paper’s two-step approach—filling missing entries *before* LSI—is a meaningful technical contribution. If validated, it could provide a principled way to leverage sparse parallel data, addressing a key challenge in low-resource cross-lingual transfer. The significance is high if the method’s robustness is confirmed.

**Quality**: The technical formulation is sound. The matrix completion problem (Eq. 2) is well-motivated (low-rank + sparsity), and the projected gradient descent algorithm (Algorithm 1) includes a convergence proof (Theorem 1). However, a critical quality concern arises in the experimental evaluation. The reported improvements over baselines (e.g., +8.59%–14.61% over TBOW) may stem from LSI’s inherent scaling properties rather than the matrix completion step. Specifically:
- The paper uses linear SVMs, which are highly sensitive to feature scaling (e.g., LSI’s singular value projection).
- Baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) also use LSI/PCA/CCA *before* SVMs, but the proposed method’s *additional* LSI step could amplify scaling effects.
- **Critical flaw**: The authors do not test whether the improvement persists with classifiers *insensitive* to feature rescaling (e.g., boosted decision trees). Without this, the results could be an artifact of LSI’s scaling, not the matrix completion. The authors should replicate experiments with a non-SVM classifier (e.g., Weka’s AdaBoost) to rule this out.

**Clarity**: The paper’s clarity is hampered by two issues:
1. Ambiguous description of the "unified document-term matrix." It is unclear whether the vocabulary is language-agnostic (e.g., translated terms) or language-specific (e.g., surface forms). The phrase "columns correspond to the word features from the unified vocabulary set" lacks specificity—does this involve translation, or are terms treated as distinct per language? This ambiguity affects reproducibility.
2. Experimental details are fragmented across Table 1 and the text. Parameter selection (e.g., γ, ρ, k) is described only for EFB, but not for other tasks. A consolidated experimental setup section would greatly improve readability.

**Strengths**: The method is conceptually clean, the matrix completion formulation is theoretically grounded, and the results *do* demonstrate consistent outperformance over baselines on 16/18 tasks. The convergence guarantee for the optimization algorithm adds technical rigor.

**Weaknesses**: The primary weakness is the unaddressed confounding factor in the evaluation (LSI scaling artifact). Without testing with a robust classifier, the claimed superiority of the *matrix completion step* is unverified. The "small parallel data" advantage (Fig. 2) is compelling *if* the method is robust, but the current evidence is insufficient. Additionally, the paper does not discuss computational scalability for large vocabularies (e.g., 10k+ terms), which could limit real-world applicability.

**Recommendation**: **Weak Reject**. The core idea is novel and potentially impactful, but the experimental validation is incomplete. The paper would be acceptable *only* if the authors address the critical flaw by:
1. Re-running experiments with a non-SVM classifier (e.g., boosted trees) to confirm results are not scaling artifacts.
2. Clarifying the vocabulary construction (language-agnostic vs. language-specific) and consolidating experimental details.

**Pro/Con Summary**:
- **Pro**: Novel two-step framework; theoretically grounded optimization; strong empirical results *if* artifacts are ruled out.
- **Con**: Unverified experimental claim due to SVM scaling bias; ambiguous methodology; fragmented presentation.

This work has promise but currently lacks sufficient evidence to support its main claim. The proposed solution (classifier robustness test) is straightforward and would strengthen the contribution substantially. As-is, the quality of the empirical validation is insufficient for acceptance at NIPS.
