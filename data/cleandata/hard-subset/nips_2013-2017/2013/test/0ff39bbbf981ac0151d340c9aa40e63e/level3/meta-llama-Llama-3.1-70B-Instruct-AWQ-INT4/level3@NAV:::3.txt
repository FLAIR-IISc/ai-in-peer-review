This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-language representation learning. The method first uses matrix completion to create a unified document-term matrix for all documents in two languages, and then applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) on the completed matrix to induce a low-dimensional cross-lingual document representation. The proposed method is evaluated on cross-language sentiment classification tasks on Amazon product reviews and demonstrates significant improvement over other cross-language representation learning methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a thorough review of related work and clearly explain the proposed method. The experimental setup is well-described, and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner.

The proposed method is technically sound, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the optimization algorithm used to solve the matrix completion problem. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the results.

However, there are some concerns regarding the quality of the results. The authors use a linear SVM as the monolingual classification model, which may not be the most suitable choice for this task. The authors should consider using other classification models, such as boosted decision trees, to evaluate the robustness of the proposed method. Additionally, the authors should provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the preprocessing steps used to create the document-term matrix.

The originality of the paper is high, as the proposed method is a novel combination of matrix completion and LSI. The authors clearly explain how the proposed method differs from previous contributions and provide a thorough review of related work.

The significance of the paper is high, as the proposed method has the potential to improve the performance of cross-language text classification tasks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on a real-world dataset, and the results are promising.

However, there are some clarity issues in the paper. The authors use ambiguous phrasing when describing the "unified document-term matrix." It is unclear whether the terms are language-agnostic or language-specific surface forms. Additionally, the experimental setup description is fragmented and spread across Table 1, making it hard to follow.

Overall, the paper is well-written and proposes a novel method for cross-language representation learning. However, the authors should address the concerns regarding the quality of the results and provide more details on the experimental setup. Additionally, the authors should clarify the ambiguous phrasing and improve the organization of the experimental setup description.
