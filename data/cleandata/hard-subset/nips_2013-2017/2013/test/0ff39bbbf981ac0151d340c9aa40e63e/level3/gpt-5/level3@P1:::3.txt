This paper proposes a two-step cross-lingual representation learning method that (i) completes a concatenated bilingual document–term matrix via a convex matrix completion objective (trace norm + ℓ1 with nonnegativity and squared-loss penalties on observed and missing entries), and (ii) applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional shared representation for downstream classification with a linear SVM. The key idea is to leverage a small set of unlabeled parallel documents to couple the feature spaces of two languages while exploiting within- and cross-language co-occurrence patterns. The optimization is solved with a projected (proximal) gradient method with singular value shrinkage and nonnegativity projection; a convergence argument based on non-expansiveness is provided. Experiments on 18 English–{French,German,Japanese} Amazon sentiment tasks show consistent gains over TBOW, CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], and CL-OPCA [15], with especially good performance when parallel data is scarce.

Relation to prior work. The paper is well-situated with respect to machine-translation-based approaches [18, 23], domain adaptation [17, 21], multi-view/co-training methods [1, 2, 22], and cross-lingual representation learning via CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], CL-OPCA [15], topic models [19], and SCL [16]. Unlike CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA that operate directly on the observed concatenated matrix or paired projections, this work first imputes missing entries in a unified bilingual matrix using a controlled convex program, then does LSI. The imputation step is the main novelty and is plausibly beneficial when parallel data is limited.

Quality. The formulation is sound: the convex relaxation with trace norm and linear (ℓ1 under nonnegativity) regularization is standard; adding squared losses on both observed entries and missing entries (the latter toward zero via ρ) is reasonable to mitigate overfitting. The proximal-gradient algorithm (gradient on smooth part, singular value thresholding, nonnegativity projection) is appropriate, and the convergence sketch is consistent with known results. Two points merit further scrutiny:
- Potential confound from feature scaling. LSI produces an orthonormal basis and implicit rescaling that can benefit linear SVMs. The strong gains over OPCA/CCA might partly reflect the choice of classifier rather than the superiority of the completion step. While the paper compares against CL-LSI, it would help to isolate whether the improvement over CL-LSI comes from the denser, completed matrix (vs. mere scaling/denoising effects).
- Missing ablations and robustness checks. It would strengthen the evidence to add: (a) “matrix completion only” (no LSI) vs. “LSI only” vs. “completion+LSI”; (b) sensitivity to µ, γ, ρ, and k; (c) robustness to different TF-IDF/normalization schemes; and (d) runtime and scalability (per-iteration SVD on large matrices can be costly).

Clarity. The paper is generally readable, but two issues reduce accessibility:
- Unified document–term matrix: please clarify explicitly whether the columns correspond to language-specific surface forms concatenated across languages (appears so), or to language-agnostic units. A small illustrative example would help.
- Experimental setup: the description is fragmented (partly in the text, partly in Table 1). Consolidate the dataset splits, number of labeled target instances, parallel data sizes, and chosen hyperparameters in one place for reproducibility.

Originality. Combining convex matrix completion with LSI for cross-lingual representation appears novel. The use of nonnegativity constraints and an explicit quadratic penalty on missing entries is a thoughtful variant likely to matter in sparse TF-IDF settings.

Significance. If the gains over strong CL baselines persist under additional controls, the contribution is meaningful: the approach offers a principled, resource-light alternative that performs well with few parallel documents—a common regime in multilingual NLP. The formulation is generic and could transfer beyond sentiment to other text classification tasks.

Suggestions for improvement.
- Control for scaling effects: include classifiers less sensitive to per-feature rescaling, e.g., boosted decision trees (e.g., AdaBoostM1/LogitBoost in Weka), random forests, or cosine-similarity nearest-neighbor. Report whether TSL’s gains over CL-LSI/OPCA/CCA persist. Alternatively, standardize features post-projection across methods to equalize scaling.
- Add ablations (completion vs. LSI vs. both) and report per-task gaps to pinpoint where completion helps most (e.g., low np).
- Report computational costs (iterations, wall-clock, SVD rank growth) and explore truncated/ randomized SVD to support scalability claims.
- Expand related comparisons to include SCL [16] and recent multilingual topic/model alignment baselines if feasible on this dataset.
- Clarify the choice and effect of ρ (penalizing unobserved entries toward zero) and µ; provide a brief sensitivity plot.

Pros for acceptance.
- Clear, principled formulation with convergence guarantees.
- Novel two-step combination; simple to implement with standard proximal tools.
- Strong empirical performance across 18 tasks, especially in low-parallel-data regimes.
- Uses only unlabeled parallel data; avoids full translation of training sets.

Cons for acceptance.
- Possible confound with LSI-induced scaling and linear SVM; limited classifier diversity.
- Missing ablations and limited hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.
- Clarity issues about the unified vocabulary and experimental reporting.
- No runtime/scalability evidence; feasibility on larger vocabularies remains unclear.

Overall assessment. A neat and potentially impactful idea with promising results. With additional controls to rule out scaling artifacts and clearer experimental reporting, this would be a strong contribution. I lean positive, conditional on the suggested analyses (especially classifier robustness and ablations) to solidify the empirical claims.
