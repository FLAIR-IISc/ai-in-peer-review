This paper proposes a novel two-step learning method for cross-language text classification. The core idea is to bridge the feature space gap between two languages by leveraging unlabeled parallel documents. First, the method constructs a unified document-term matrix containing documents from both source and target languages. For non-parallel documents, the feature entries corresponding to the other language's vocabulary are treated as missing. The paper then formulates a matrix completion problem to recover these missing entries, optimizing an objective that encourages a low-rank and sparse solution. This optimization is solved using a projected gradient descent algorithm. In the second step, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to the completed matrix to induce a low-dimensional, dense cross-lingual representation. Standard monolingual classifiers can then be trained on this shared representation.

This work relates to a significant body of research on cross-lingual representation learning. It differs from methods like Cross-Lingual LSI (CL-LSI), CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, which typically operate directly on a concatenated matrix of parallel documents. The key novelty here is the explicit matrix completion step to infer a full bilingual representation for *all* documents (parallel and non-parallel) before performing dimensionality reduction.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper presents a technically sound method. The formulation of the matrix completion problem as a convex optimization problem with trace norm and L1 regularization is well-motivated. The authors provide a convergence proof for their projected gradient descent algorithm, which lends theoretical support to their approach. The experimental results on the Amazon product review dataset are strong and demonstrate that the proposed method, TSL, consistently outperforms several relevant baselines, including CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA. The improved performance in settings with a very small number of parallel documents is a particularly notable strength.

However, a key assumption needs more justification. The paper states that "a fully observed document-term matrix is naturally low-rank." While this is a common assumption in matrix completion literature, its application to document-term matrices is not obvious. Documents are typically sparse vectors, and it is not immediately clear why they should be well-approximated as linear combinations of a small number of basis documents. Providing more intuition or justification for this low-rank assumption would strengthen the paper's theoretical foundation.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and the proposed method is clearly described. However, several points could be clarified to improve the manuscript and aid reproducibility.

1.  **Motivation for LSI:** After solving for the optimal matrix M*, the paper applies LSI for dimensionality reduction. The motivation for this second step is unclear. Is M* computationally difficult to work with because it becomes relatively dense despite the L1 penalty? Why was LSI chosen over other techniques like PCA? A brief discussion of this design choice would be helpful.
2.  **Experimental Setup:** The description of the experimental setup is ambiguous. Could the authors please confirm the exact data splits for training and testing? Is it correct that for representation learning and classification model training, you use: 4000 labeled source documents, 100 randomly sampled labeled target documents, and the full set of 2000 unlabeled parallel documents? Is the testing then performed on the remaining 1900 target documents? Also, please confirm that the experiments in Section 5.3 vary the number of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2000, while keeping other data fixed.
3.  **Data Usage:** It would be clearer to explicitly distinguish the two ways external data are leveraged: (1) labeled source-language data for supervised training, and (2) unlabeled parallel data for representation learning.
4.  **Minor Editorial Suggestions:**
    *   In the paragraph preceding Eq. (2), please change `(ij)∈Ω` to `(i,j)∈Ω` for consistency.
    *   In Section 4.1, the phrase "such as f(M) = ..." seems misplaced before the decomposition.
    *   Similarly, the phrases "such as" before Eqs. (4) and (5) could be removed for clarity.

**Originality:**
The approach of combining matrix completion with LSI for cross-language representation learning is novel. While the components themselves (matrix completion, projected gradient descent, LSI) are established techniques, their application and combination to solve this specific problem is original. The paper does a good job of situating its contribution with respect to prior work, clearly distinguishing its method from related approaches that do not perform the explicit completion step.

**Significance:**
The paper addresses the important and practical problem of cross-language classification, particularly in low-resource scenarios. The strong empirical results, especially the robustness to a small number of parallel documents, suggest the method could be highly useful for practitioners.

To better assess the method's practical significance, a few points could be addressed:
1.  **Computational Cost:** The paper does not include a comparison of the running time. How does the projected gradient descent algorithm's runtime compare to that of CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA? This information is crucial for understanding the trade-offs of the proposed method.
2.  **Intuition:** The paper would benefit from more intuition about *why* the proposed method outperforms strong baselines like CL-KCCA. CCA-based methods seem like a very natural fit for finding shared representations across two views (languages). What is it about enforcing a global low-rank structure on the combined document-term matrix that is more effective?
3.  **Result Inconsistency:** There appears to be a minor inconsistency in the results. In Figure 1, for tasks EFM and EGM with 2000 parallel documents, CL-KCCA appears to perform slightly better than or equal to TSL. However, in Table 1 (which uses 2000 parallel documents), TSL outperforms CL-KCCA on these tasks. Please clarify this discrepancy.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and intuitive two-step method for a significant problem in NLP.
*   The method is technically sound and supported by a convergence guarantee.
*   Demonstrates strong and consistent empirical improvements over several relevant baselines.
*   Shows particular strength in the practical setting where parallel data is limited.

**Arguments for Rejection (or Points to Address in Rebuttal):**
*   The theoretical assumption of the low-rank nature of document-term matrices is not well-justified.
*   Key details of the experimental setup are unclear, hindering reproducibility.
*   The paper lacks a discussion on computational complexity and runtime comparisons.
*   There is an apparent inconsistency in the reported experimental results.

Overall, this is a solid paper with a simple, effective method and promising results. My recommendation leans towards acceptance, provided the authors can satisfactorily address the points of clarification regarding the experimental setup, results, and the motivation for their design choices.
