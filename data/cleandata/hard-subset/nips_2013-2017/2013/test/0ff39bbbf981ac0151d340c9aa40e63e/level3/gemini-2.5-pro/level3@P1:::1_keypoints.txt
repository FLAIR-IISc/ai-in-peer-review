- Proposes TSL: a two-step method that first completes a bilingual document-term matrix (low-rank + sparse) then applies LSI to learn a shared semantic space for cross-language classification.  
- Matrix completion uses convex relaxation (trace norm + L1) solved by projected gradient descent with a provided convergence guarantee.  
- Empirical results are strong: TSL outperforms baselines on 18 cross-language sentiment tasks and works well with few unlabeled parallel documents.  
- Strength: method is simple, intuitive, and novel in applying matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning.  
- Strength: theoretical convergence guarantee adds technical soundness.  
- Strength: robustness and practical utility in low-resource scenarios (good performance with small parallel corpora).  
- Weakness: The core low-rank assumption for the fully observed document-term matrix is not justified or intuitively explained.  
- Weakness: Lack of intuition/theoretical reason why global matrix completion should outperform methods that directly optimize cross-language correlations (e.g., CL-KCCA).  
- Weakness: Motivation for the second LSI step after matrix completion is unclear; need ablation or justification for using LSI vs. alternatives or raw low-rank factors.  
- Experimental clarity needed: precise description of the training/test splits and which data are used for representation learning vs. classifier training.  
- Experiments should explicitly distinguish contributions from labeled source data versus unlabeled parallel data.  
- Results inconsistency: Table 1 vs. Figure 1 show conflicting performance for CL-KCCA vs. TSL at np=2000 for some tasks—please clarify.  
- Computational concerns: missing comparison of runtime and complexity—matrix completion via iterative PGD may be slower than SVD/closed-form baselines.  
- Minor editorial fixes: index notation `(ij)` → `(i, j)` and remove awkward "such as" phrasing around definitions in Section 4.1 and equations (4),(5).
