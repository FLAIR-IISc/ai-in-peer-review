This paper proposes a two-step approach to cross-lingual representation learning: (1) recover a unified bilingual document–term matrix via convex matrix completion with trace-norm and sparsity regularization optimized by a projected gradient descent scheme with convergence guarantees, and (2) apply LSI on the recovered matrix to obtain a shared low-dimensional semantic space for classification. The work is well situated relative to prior NIPS and IR/NLP work: unlike CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA and multilingual topic models that operate directly on concatenated or paired corpora, the key novelty is to first complete missing cross-lingual features so that LSI can better exploit within- and cross-language co-occurrence. On Amazon Reviews (EN/FR/DE/JA), the method outperforms bag-of-words, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA across 18 transfer tasks, and its learning curves show strong robustness with as few as 200 parallel documents.

Quality: The formulation is sound, convex, and paired with a clear proximal-style algorithm and proof of convergence. Experimental design is careful: consistent parameter selection, appropriate monolingual SVM classifier, and thorough comparisons. Results are strong and stable, especially in low-parallel-data regimes. Suggestions: report runtime and memory profiles (SVD cost per iteration) to substantiate scalability; add an ablation isolating the effect of matrix completion (e.g., LSI on zero-filled or naïvely imputed matrices) to sharpen causal attribution; discuss sensitivity to γ, ρ, and k beyond the single-task selection.

Clarity: The paper is well organized and reproducible; algorithmic details and selection procedures are reported. Minor suggestion: better motivate matrix completion in the Introduction with intuitive examples of how completing cross-lingual co-occurrences can resolve sparsity and align topics, and clarify why nonnegativity and ℓ1 regularization are desirable here.

Originality: The application of convex matrix completion as a preprocessing step for cross-lingual representation learning is novel and clearly differentiated from prior CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA pipelines.

Significance: The improvements over strong cross-lingual baselines and robustness with limited parallel data make this a useful contribution likely to be adopted. The approach appears portable to other tasks (e.g., category transfer, topic induction); demonstrating results beyond sentiment and outside Amazon would strengthen the case.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, principled two-step formulation with convergence guarantees
- Consistent, sizable gains over strong mono- and cross-lingual baselines
- Robust performance with scarce parallel data
- Clear, reproducible presentation

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited task and domain scope; no runtime/scale analysis
- Missing ablations isolating completion vs. LSI
- Introduction could better motivate the matrix completion choice and design choices
