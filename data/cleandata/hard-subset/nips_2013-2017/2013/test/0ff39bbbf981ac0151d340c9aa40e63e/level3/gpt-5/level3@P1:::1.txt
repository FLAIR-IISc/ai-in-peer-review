This paper proposes a two-step approach to cross-language text classification. Step 1 formulates a convex matrix completion problem on a unified document–term matrix constructed from both languages: observed entries correspond to terms present in a document’s own language, while entries for the other language are missing unless the document has a parallel translation. The objective combines a trace-norm (low-rank) penalty, an ℓ1 penalty (sparsity), squared loss on observed entries, a weak quadratic regularizer on missing entries, and nonnegativity constraints. The authors solve it via a projected gradient descent scheme with singular value thresholding (shrinkage) and nonnegativity projection, and provide a convergence proof based on non-expansive operators. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to produce a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation, on which a linear SVM is trained. Experiments on 18 Amazon review transfer tasks (English↔{French,German,Japanese} across three domains) show consistent gains over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, with particularly strong performance when few parallel documents are available.

Relation to prior work: The approach contrasts with translation-based pipelines (e.g., Dai et al., Wan et al.), and with representation learning methods that operate directly on concatenated or paired data: CL-LSI (Littman et al.), CL-KCCA (Vinokourov et al.), and CL-OPCA (Platt et al.). The novelty is to first impute missing cross-lingual features via a convex completion problem that leverages within- and cross-language co-occurrences, and only then apply LSI; prior methods apply LSI/CCA/OPCA directly to observed/paired data.

Strengths
- Simple, coherent method with a principled convex formulation and a convergence guarantee.
- Strong empirical results across 18 tasks; robustness when parallel data is scarce (np=200).
- Makes effective use of both monolingual co-occurrence structure and limited parallel data.
- Clear articulation of the optimization algorithm and convergence argument.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- The low-rank assumption for a fully observed document–term matrix is asserted as “natural,” but not justified. Real document–term matrices are extremely sparse and high-rank in the ambient space; the intended claim is likely low intrinsic semantic rank. Please articulate the modeling assumption (e.g., topic/LSA view) and when it is expected to hold in the cross-lingual setting.
- Why is a second-stage LSI necessary after completion? Is it for denoising, dimensionality control, or computational efficiency because M* densifies despite the ℓ1 term? Why LSI vs. PCA/NMF or directly training on M*? An ablation comparing: (a) LSI on the incomplete matrix (no completion), (b) completion only (no LSI), (c) completion+PCA/NMF, would sharpen the contribution.
- Data usage/protocol should be explicitly and unambiguously stated. Please confirm: for each task, you use all source-labeled documents (4k in English; 2k in other languages), 100 labeled target documents for training, all remaining target documents for testing (e.g., 1.9k), and 2k unlabeled parallel documents between EN and the other language. In Section 5.3, do you vary only the number of unlabeled parallel documents (np ∈ {200,500,1000,2000}) while keeping the labeled sets fixed?
- Figure/Table consistency: You note that TSL generally outperforms baselines, but Figure 1 suggests CL-KCCA surpasses TSL for EFM and EGM at np=2000, whereas Table 1 shows TSL ≥ CL-KCCA on average. Please reconcile (e.g., variance, different k, or averaging across repeated random splits).
- Runtime and scalability: The method requires repeated SVDs per iteration (shrinkage). Please report wall-clock times and complexity vs. CL-LSI (single SVD), CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA (solving generalized eigenproblems with paired data). Do you use truncated/approximate SVD? How do t, d, and k affect runtime?
- Hyperparameters and optimization details: Sensitivity of performance to γ, ρ, k, and τ; stopping criterion and typical number of iterations; effect of the nonnegativity constraint and ℓ1 term (e.g., ablations dropping each). Note τ must satisfy 0 < τ < min(2, 2/ρ); with ρ as small as 1e-5, τ=1 is fine, but please state how τ was chosen in general and whether backtracking/line search helps.
- Use of external data: It would help to disentangle and report the contributions of (1) labeled source data and (2) unlabeled parallel data. For example, show performance with only target labels, with and without parallel data; show how much of the gain comes from adding source labels given the learned representation.
- Intuition relative to CCA-based methods: CCA directly maximizes cross-view correlation on paired data. Your completion step integrates all documents (paired and unpaired) via shared low-rank structure plus nonnegativity and sparsity, potentially leveraging richer monolingual co-occurrence than CCA (which ignores unpaired data). It would be valuable to add a brief conceptual comparison and, if possible, a toy example illustrating when completion+LSI has an advantage.

Quality
- Technically sound convex relaxation and correct convergence argument. Some modeling assumptions (low-rankness) need clearer justification; more thorough ablations and runtime analysis would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
- Generally well written; the algorithm and loss are easy to follow. Please clarify the experimental protocol, reconcile Figure/Table differences, and add the suggested ablations. Minor edits below.

Originality
- The combination of convex completion on a cross-lingual unified matrix followed by LSI is a simple but novel twist relative to prior CL-LSI/CCA/OPCA pipelines and translation-based transfer.

Significance
- Solid gains across many language pairs and robustness with limited parallel data suggest practical utility. Extending beyond Amazon reviews and adding ablations/runtime would broaden impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled method with convergence proof
- Consistent accuracy gains, especially with few parallel documents
- Uses both monolingual and limited bilingual signal effectively
- Simple to implement with standard components (SVT, SVD, SVM)

Cons against acceptance
- Incomplete justification of low-rank assumption and need for LSI post-completion
- Missing ablations and runtime comparisons
- Some ambiguity in the experimental protocol and a figure/table inconsistency

Minor editorial suggestions
- Before Eq. (2), replace “(ij)” with “(i,j)”.
- Remove “such as” before Eqs. (4) and (5).
- Define symbols once and use consistently (e.g., Y, Ŷ, E); ensure table/figure captions specify settings (np, k).

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the protocol, reconciling Figure/Table discrepancies, and adding brief ablations and runtime reporting.
