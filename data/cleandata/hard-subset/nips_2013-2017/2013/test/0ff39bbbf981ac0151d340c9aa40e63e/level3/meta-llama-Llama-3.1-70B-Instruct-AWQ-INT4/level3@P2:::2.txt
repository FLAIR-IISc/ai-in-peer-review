This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-language representation learning, which is a critical challenge in natural language processing. The method first formulates a matrix completion problem to produce a complete parallel document-term matrix for all documents in two languages, and then induces a low-dimensional cross-lingual document representation by applying latent semantic indexing on the obtained matrix.

The proposed method is related to previous work in the literature, which has demonstrated successful performance of cross-language learning systems on various cross-language text classification problems. However, most of these approaches require translating all the training documents in one language domain into the other language domain, which can be time-consuming and may not always produce accurate translations. In contrast, the proposed method only requires a small set of unlabeled parallel documents, making it more efficient and scalable.

The authors provide a thorough discussion of the related work in the field, including methods that transform the training or test data from one language domain into the other language domain using machine translation tools or bilingual lexicons, and methods that construct cross-lingual representations by exploiting bilingual word pairs, parallel corpora, and other resources. They also highlight the limitations of these approaches and motivate the need for a new method that can effectively learn cross-lingual representations from a small set of parallel documents.

The proposed method is evaluated on a set of cross-language sentiment classification tasks on multilingual Amazon product reviews. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method significantly outperforms a number of other cross-language representation learning methods, including state-of-the-art methods. The results also show that the proposed method produces good performance even with a very small number of unlabeled parallel bilingual documents.

The paper is well-organized and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method, including the matrix completion step and the latent semantic indexing step. They also provide a detailed discussion of the optimization algorithm used to solve the matrix completion problem. The experimental results are thoroughly presented, including the learning curves and the comparison with other methods.

The originality of the paper lies in its novel application of matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning. The authors demonstrate that matrix completion can be an effective preprocessing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification, and potentially other tasks. The significance of the paper is that it shows that matrix completion can be used to learn effective cross-lingual representations from a small set of parallel documents, which can be useful in many real-world applications.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound. The authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method and the optimization algorithm used to solve the matrix completion problem. They also provide a thorough discussion of the related work in the field and a detailed presentation of the experimental results. The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-organized and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method and the optimization algorithm used to solve the matrix completion problem. However, the use of matrix completion in the introduction could be better motivated. The authors could provide more background information on matrix completion and its applications in other fields, and explain why it is a suitable method for cross-lingual representation learning.
