This paper proposes a novel two-step learning method (TSL) for cross-language text classification. The core idea is to bridge the feature space gap between a source and target language by leveraging a set of unlabeled parallel documents. The method first constructs a unified document-term matrix containing documents from both languages. This matrix is inherently incomplete, as monolingual documents have missing values for the vocabulary of the other language. The first step of the proposed method is to recover a complete, low-rank, and sparse version of this matrix by formulating and solving a matrix completion problem. The optimization uses a convex relaxation with trace norm and L1 regularization, solved via a projected gradient descent algorithm for which a convergence guarantee is provided. The second step applies Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to induce a low-dimensional, shared semantic representation for all documents. Standard monolingual classifiers can then be trained on this representation.

The approach relates to prior work in cross-lingual representation learning, such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, which also utilize parallel corpora to create a shared space. However, the key difference and contribution of this work lies in the explicit matrix completion pre-processing step. While other methods operate directly on the sparse, concatenated matrix (often treating missing values as zeros), this paper hypothesizes that first inferring the missing feature values will lead to a more robust shared representation.

**Strengths:**
*   The proposed method is simple, intuitive, and novel in its application of matrix completion to the cross-lingual representation problem. It presents a clean, two-step procedure that combines well-understood techniques in a new way.
*   The paper provides a theoretical convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm used to solve the matrix completion problem, which adds to the technical soundness of the work.
*   The experimental results are strong and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach. TSL consistently outperforms several relevant baselines across 18 different cross-language sentiment classification tasks. Particularly impressive is its strong performance even with a small number of unlabeled parallel documents, highlighting its robustness and practical utility in low-resource scenarios.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Clarification:**
The paper is generally well-written, but several conceptual and experimental details could be clarified to strengthen the contribution.

1.  **Motivation and Justification:**
    *   The central assumption of the matrix completion step is that the "fully observed document-term matrix is naturally low-rank." This claim is not obvious and requires more justification. Document-term matrices are typically very sparse, and it is not immediately clear why a document's feature vector should be well-approximated by a linear combination of a small number of basis documents. Further intuition or theoretical justification for this low-rank assumption would be beneficial.
    *   The paper would be stronger if it provided more intuition on why the proposed TSL method outperforms competitors. For instance, methods like CL-KCCA directly optimize for correlated projections between the two languages, which seems like a very natural approach for this problem. Why is enforcing a global low-rank structure via matrix completion a more effective strategy for capturing cross-lingual semantics?
    *   The motivation for the second step (LSI) is not fully clear. After obtaining the completed matrix M*, why is a further dimensionality reduction step necessary? Is it because M*, despite the L1 regularization, becomes relatively dense, making it computationally expensive for the subsequent classifier? Or is it to smooth the representation and reduce noise? An ablation study without the LSI step, or a discussion on why LSI was chosen over alternatives like PCA or using the low-rank factors of M* directly, would clarify the role of this second step.

2.  **Experimental Clarity:**
    *   The experimental setup in Section 5.2 could be described more precisely. To confirm my understanding: for each task, the model is trained using all documents from the source language (e.g., 4000 English reviews) plus 100 randomly chosen labeled documents from the target language. The representation learning step (both steps of TSL) uses these documents plus the 2000 unlabeled parallel documents. The model is then tested on the remaining 1900 documents from the target language. Is this interpretation correct?
    *   The paper uses two types of external data to augment the limited labeled target data: (1) labeled source-language data and (2) unlabeled parallel data. The experiments could be clearer if the contributions of these two sources were more explicitly distinguished.
    *   There appears to be an inconsistency in the results. In Table 1 (using 2000 parallel documents), TSL outperforms CL-KCCA on the EFM and EGM tasks. However, in Figure 1, at the np=2000 mark, CL-KCCA appears to have a slightly higher mean accuracy than TSL for these same two tasks. Please clarify this discrepancy.
    *   A comparison of the computational complexity and running time would be a valuable addition. Solving the matrix completion problem via iterative projected gradient descent seems potentially more time-consuming than the closed-form or SVD-based solutions of CL-LSI and CL-OPCA. A practical discussion of training times would help assess the trade-offs of the proposed method.

**Minor Editorial Suggestions:**
*   In the paragraph just before equation (2), the index set notation `(ij)` should likely be `(i, j)` for consistency.
*   In Section 4.1, the phrase "such as f(M) = ..." seems out of place, as f(M) is defined by the objective, not an example. Similarly, in the descriptions of equations (4) and (5), the leading "such as" could be removed for clarity.

In summary, this paper presents a promising and effective method for cross-lingual representation learning. The core idea is elegant and the empirical results are compelling. However, the paper could be significantly improved by providing stronger justification for its core assumptions, clarifying experimental details, and discussing the practical computational aspects of the approach. Addressing these points would strengthen the paper's contribution and its impact on the field.
