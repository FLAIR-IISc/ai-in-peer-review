This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, combining matrix completion with latent semantic indexing (LSI). The core idea is to address the disjoint feature space problem in cross-lingual text classification by first completing a document-term matrix constructed from parallel bilingual data, and then inducing a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation via LSI. The authors demonstrate strong performance on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon reviews, outperforming several baselines including state-of-the-art methods, particularly when parallel data is limited.

The paper builds upon a significant body of work in cross-lingual text classification. Previous approaches, as outlined in the Related Work section, broadly fall into two categories: machine translation-based methods (e.g., [18, 6, 23]) and methods that directly construct cross-lingual representations (e.g., [16, 10, 20, 15]). This work distinguishes itself by leveraging matrix completion, a technique not previously applied to this specific problem. While methods like CL-LSI [10] also aim to create cross-lingual representations from document-term matrices, they operate directly on the sparse, incomplete matrix, whereas this paper proposes a preprocessing step to impute missing values.  The authors appropriately cite relevant work and position their contribution within the existing literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning is a novel contribution. This approach offers a unique way to bridge the language gap by inferring relationships between words across languages based on limited parallel data.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling. The proposed method consistently outperforms several strong baselines, including state-of-the-art techniques like CL-KCCA and CL-OPCA. The consistent gains demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* **Thorough Experiments:** The experiments are well-designed and comprehensive. The authors evaluate the method on 18 different cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks and include an ablation study demonstrating the impact of the amount of parallel data.
* **Reproducibility:** The paper is remarkably well-organized and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. All parameters and the parameter selection procedure are clearly reported, a significant strength.  The use of a standard SVM classifier and the referencing of the libsvm package further enhances reproducibility.
* **Theoretical Justification:**  The paper provides a convergence analysis for the projected gradient descent algorithm used for matrix completion, adding theoretical grounding to the proposed method.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The method is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Motivation for Matrix Completion:** While the paper clearly explains *how* matrix completion is used, the initial motivation for choosing this technique could be strengthened.  The introduction states the goal is to bridge feature spaces, but doesn’t explicitly articulate *why* matrix completion is a particularly suitable method for this task beyond the mention of low-rank structure. Elaborating on the underlying assumptions that make matrix completion effective in this context would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** Matrix completion can be computationally expensive, particularly for large datasets. While the authors address scalability with the projected gradient descent algorithm, a discussion of the computational cost relative to other methods would be valuable.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** Although the parameter selection procedure is described, a more in-depth analysis of the sensitivity of the results to parameter choices could provide further insight.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to cross-lingual representation learning. The empirical results are convincing, the experiments are thorough, and the paper is well-written and reproducible. The minor weakness regarding motivation for matrix completion is easily addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and effective technique for cross-lingual representation learning.
* The empirical results demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and reproducible.
* The theoretical analysis adds credibility to the proposed approach.
* The findings have the potential to impact various cross-lingual NLP tasks beyond sentiment classification.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The initial motivation for choosing matrix completion could be slightly strengthened.
* The computational cost of matrix completion for very large datasets is not fully addressed.



**Recommendation:**

Strong Accept. This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of cross-lingual NLP and is worthy of acceptance to NIPS. The potential impact of this work is substantial, and the paper is of high quality in terms of technical soundness, clarity, originality, and significance.
