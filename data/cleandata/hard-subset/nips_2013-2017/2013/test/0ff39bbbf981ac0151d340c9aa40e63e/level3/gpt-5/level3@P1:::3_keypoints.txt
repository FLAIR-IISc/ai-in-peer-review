- Proposes a two-step cross-lingual method: convex matrix completion of a concatenated bilingual document–term matrix, then LSI to obtain shared representations for linear SVM classification.  
- Completion objective uses trace norm + ℓ1 with nonnegativity and squared-loss penalties, including a quadratic penalty ρ on missing entries.  
- Optimization solved with a proximal/projected gradient method using singular value thresholding and nonnegativity projection; a convergence argument is provided.  
- Leverages a small set of unlabeled parallel documents to couple feature spaces and exploit within- and cross-language co-occurrence patterns.  
- Empirical results on 18 EN–{FR,DE,JP} Amazon sentiment tasks show consistent gains over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, especially when parallel data is scarce.  
- Main novelty is imputing missing entries via convex matrix completion before applying LSI, which is plausibly helpful in sparse TF-IDF settings.  
- The convex formulation and proximal algorithm are sound and align with known theoretical results.  
- Potential confound: improvements may partly arise from LSI-induced feature scaling benefiting linear SVMs rather than the completion step itself.  
- Missing ablations: need comparisons of “completion only” vs “LSI only” vs “completion+LSI” to isolate effects.  
- Need robustness checks and sensitivity analyses for µ, γ, ρ, k, TF-IDF/normalization choices, and dataset/task variability.  
- Scalability concern: per-iteration SVD can be costly—report runtime, iterations, SVD rank growth, and evaluate truncated/randomized SVD.  
- Clarity issues: unclear whether unified matrix columns are concatenated language-specific surface forms; include a small illustrative example.  
- Experimental reporting is fragmented—consolidate dataset splits, labeled target counts, parallel sizes, and hyperparameters for reproducibility.  
- Suggested controls: use classifiers less sensitive to feature scaling (boosting, random forests, cosine-NN) or standardize features post-projection, add per-task gaps and sensitivity plots.  
- Pros for acceptance: principled formulation with convergence guarantees, simple-to-implement two-step method, strong low-parallel-data performance, and resource-light requirements.  
- Cons for acceptance: possible scaling artifact, limited classifier diversity, missing ablations/sensitivity analysis, clarity and runtime/scalability gaps.  
- Overall assessment: promising and potentially impactful; recommend acceptance conditional on additional controls (classifier robustness, ablations) and clearer experimental reporting.
