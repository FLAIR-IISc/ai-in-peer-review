This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning. The method first constructs a unified document-term matrix for source and target languages. It then uses matrix completion to infer the missing feature values for monolingual documents, effectively creating a dense multilingual vector representation for every document. In the second step, it applies Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to this completed matrix to induce a low-dimensional shared semantic space for classification. This approach extends prior work like CL-LSI and CL-OPCA by introducing matrix completion as an explicit, non-linear inference step to bridge the lexical gap before dimensionality reduction.

The primary strength of the paper is its originality. The idea of using matrix completion to explicitly generate complete cross-lingual document vectors is a new and elegant contribution to the field. If proven robust, the approach is significant because matrix completion is a principled technique for inference, and its application here is a creative way to leverage statistical co-occurrence patterns both within and across languages from parallel corpora. The experimental results, particularly the method's strong performance with limited parallel data, suggest a potentially valuable contribution.

However, the paper has a significant weakness in its experimental validation that raises concerns about the quality and robustness of the results. The exclusive use of a linear SVM classifier makes it difficult to ascertain whether the performance gains stem from the novel matrix completion step or from an incidental artifact of feature scaling. The LSI step intrinsically rescales features, and it is plausible that this particular scaling is simply more favorable to a linear SVM than the scaling produced by baselines like OPCA or CCA. To substantiate the paper's central claim, the authors must decouple the quality of the representation from the classifier's sensitivity to scaling. This could be achieved by evaluating with a classifier insensitive to per-feature rescaling, such as boosted decision trees or random forests.

The paper’s clarity could also be improved. The term “unified document-term matrix” is ambiguous; the authors should clarify whether terms are language-agnostic or simply a concatenation of language-specific surface forms. Additionally, the description of the experimental setup is fragmented across the text and Table 1, making it difficult to follow and hindering reproducibility.

In conclusion, this paper presents a neat and promising idea. However, the current evidence is not sufficient to rule out a critical experimental confound. The authors should strengthen their claims by conducting additional experiments with scale-invariant classifiers to confirm that the observed improvements are indeed due to their proposed method.
