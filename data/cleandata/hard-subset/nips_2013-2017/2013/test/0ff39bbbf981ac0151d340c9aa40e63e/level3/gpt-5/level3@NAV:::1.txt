This paper proposes a simple two-step pipeline for cross-language text classification: (i) construct a unified, partially observed document–term matrix across two languages and complete it via a projected gradient descent (PGD) procedure with trace-norm (low-rank) and L1 (sparsity) regularization; (ii) apply LSI to the completed matrix and train a standard classifier in the reduced space. The algorithm comes with a convergence guarantee (via non-expansive operator composition) and is evaluated on Amazon multilingual sentiment tasks. The method builds directly on matrix completion foundations (e.g., nuclear-norm minimization) and relates to cross-lingual representation learning via CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA; the novelty is to first impute cross-lingual structure by completion and only then reduce dimensionality.

Quality: The optimization is technically sound and the convergence argument is clear. Empirical results are strong overall and particularly compelling at low parallel-data regimes. However, several clarifications would improve rigor: (1) Please justify the low-rank premise for fully observed document–term matrices. Sparsity of bag-of-words does not imply low rank, and documents are not obvious linear mixtures; provide empirical rank/spectral decay plots or theory tying translation-induced co-occurrence to approximate low rank. (2) Why perform an additional LSI step after estimating M*? Is it mainly computational because M* becomes relatively dense despite sparsity regularization? Why LSI rather than PCA/OPCA on M* (or a direct nuclear-norm-constrained classifier)? An ablation (completion-only vs LSI-only vs both) would help.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written, but please: fix (ij) to (i,j) before Eq. (2); remove “such as” before Eqs. (4) and (5); state stopping criteria, iteration counts, and typical ranks selected; and report wall-clock time and scalability. A running-time comparison of PGD vs CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA would be valuable.

Originality: The completion-then-LSI decomposition is a novel and appealing twist relative to prior cross-lingual projections applied directly on concatenated matrices.

Significance: Gains over strong baselines suggest practical impact, especially when parallel data are scarce. Please also: (a) explicitly separate and report the two external signals—labeled source data aiding labeled target vs unlabeled parallel data; (b) confirm the training/testing protocol (4k labeled source, 100 labeled target, 2k unlabeled parallel; remainder ~1.9k target for test), and that §5.3 varies parallel data from 200 to 2k; (c) reconcile the discrepancy where CL-KCCA outperforms TSL in Figure 1 (EFM/EGM at 2k) but not in Table 1; (d) offer intuition for why TSL often beats CCA methods in cross-lingual settings.

Pros: Simple, principled, effective; convergence guarantee; strong results with little parallel data.
Cons: Low-rank assumption not fully justified; missing runtime/scalability and ablations; some experimental details unclear.

Overall: Promising and likely to interest the community, contingent on clarifications and additional analysis.
