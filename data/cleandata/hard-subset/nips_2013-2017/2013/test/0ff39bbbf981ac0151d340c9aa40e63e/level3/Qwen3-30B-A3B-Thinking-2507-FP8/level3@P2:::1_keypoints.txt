- Proposes a two-step method (matrix completion with low-rank + sparsity via projected gradient descent, then LSI) for cross-language text classification to handle disjoint feature spaces.
- Constructs a unified bilingual document-term matrix with missing entries for non-parallel documents and fills them via matrix completion.
- Evaluated on 18 cross-language sentiment tasks (three language pairs, three product categories) showing consistent improvements, especially with limited parallel data.
- Shows large accuracy gains over baselines (e.g., 8.59–14.61% improvement) and good performance with as few as 200 unlabeled parallel documents.
- Builds on prior NIPS work (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) but novelly separates completion and LSI rather than applying projections directly to the sparse matrix.
- Presents a technically sound optimization algorithm with convergence guarantees.
- Questions the theoretical claim that a fully observed document-term matrix is low-rank and requests stronger justification for this assumption.
- Experimental setup is unclear about use of the 2k unlabeled parallel documents and exact training data composition; these details should be explicitly stated.
- Several minor editorial/clarity fixes needed (notation before Eq.(2), removal of "such as" before Eqs.(4),(5), and clearer distinction of labeled source vs. unlabeled parallel data).
- Inconsistency between Figure 1 and Table 1 (CL-KCCA vs TSL results for specific tasks) requires explanation.
- Lacks running-time comparisons with baselines and insufficient rationale for choosing LSI after completion versus other dimensionality reduction methods.
- Strengths: clear theoretical basis, thorough experiments, significant gains with limited parallel data, and a simple adoptable approach.
- Weaknesses: weak low-rank justification, unclear experimental details, figure/table inconsistency, missing runtime comparisons, and limited explanation for the LSI choice.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions contingent on clarifying the low-rank assumption, experimental details, and resolving the Figure/Table inconsistency.
