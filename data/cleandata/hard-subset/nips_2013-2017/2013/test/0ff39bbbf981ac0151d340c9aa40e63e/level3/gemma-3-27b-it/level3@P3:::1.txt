This paper proposes a two-step matrix completion approach for cross-domain classification, leveraging parallel bilingual documents to bridge feature spaces. The method first constructs a partially observed document-term matrix, completes it using projected gradient descent with low-rank and sparsity constraints, and then applies Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) for dimensionality reduction before training a classifier. The authors provide a convergence guarantee for their projected gradient descent algorithm and demonstrate favorable empirical results on cross-lingual sentiment classification.

The core idea is sound and addresses a crucial challenge in cross-lingual learning – bridging the gap between disjoint feature spaces. The combination of matrix completion and LSI is interesting, offering a potentially effective way to capture latent semantic relationships across languages. The theoretical convergence guarantee is a strong positive point, indicating careful consideration of the method’s properties. Overall, the paper is well-written and clearly presents the proposed approach.

However, several technical points and experimental details require clarification. Specifically, the motivation for employing LSI *after* estimating the completed matrix M is unclear. While the authors mention computational convenience due to potential density despite sparsity enforcement, it would be helpful to elaborate. Why LSI specifically, instead of alternatives like PCA? PCA might be more directly interpretable and computationally efficient for dimensionality reduction given the focus on variance capture. A more detailed justification of this design choice is needed.

Furthermore, the use of external data should be explicitly distinguished and reported separately. Currently, the description conflates the benefits of labeled source-language data assisting labeled target data with the impact of unlabeled parallel data. Reporting performance metrics separately for these two types of external resources will provide a clearer understanding of their individual contributions.

The experimental setup also lacks sufficient detail. The authors state training utilizes 4k labeled source docs, 100 labeled target docs, and 2k unlabeled parallel docs, with testing on 1.9k target docs, but confirming these numbers is crucial. Moreover, it’s important to clarify if Section 5.3 varied the number of unlabeled parallel docs from 200 to 2k across *all* tasks or only a subset.

An inconsistency exists between Figure 1 and Table 1. Figure 1 suggests CL-KCCA outperforms TSL for EFM and EGM at 2k unlabeled documents, whereas Table 1 does not show the same trend. Resolving this discrepancy is vital for a reliable assessment of the results.

From a theoretical standpoint, the claim that a fully observed document-term matrix would be inherently low-rank needs further justification. While the vocabulary size is often large, documents aren't necessarily simple linear combinations of other documents, especially when considering semantic content. Elaborating on the assumptions leading to this expectation would strengthen the argument.

I'm curious about the intuition behind TSL often outperforming other methods, particularly CCA. CCA seems naturally suited for multiview cross-language tasks, focusing on correlations between languages. TSL’s primary driver appears to be enforcing low-rank structure through matrix completion. How does this difference explain the observed performance gap? A comparative discussion highlighting the strengths of each approach would be valuable.

Finally, a running-time comparison would be informative. How does the projected gradient descent algorithm's runtime compare to that of CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA? Knowing the computational cost of the proposed method is crucial for assessing its practicality.

Minor editorial suggestions: change “(ij)” to “(i,j)” before Eq. (2), and remove “such as” before Eqs. (4) and (5) for cleaner presentation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound with a convergence guarantee.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance on cross-lingual sentiment classification.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in cross-lingual learning.
*   Clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of justification for using LSI after matrix completion.
*   Insufficient detail in experimental setup.
*   Inconsistency in reported results (Figure 1 vs. Table 1).
*   Unclear explanation for the low-rank assumption of document-term matrices.
*   Missing runtime comparison.



I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field, but the aforementioned clarifications and elaborations are necessary before I can confidently recommend acceptance. I would rate this as a **weak accept** pending significant revision addressing these concerns.
