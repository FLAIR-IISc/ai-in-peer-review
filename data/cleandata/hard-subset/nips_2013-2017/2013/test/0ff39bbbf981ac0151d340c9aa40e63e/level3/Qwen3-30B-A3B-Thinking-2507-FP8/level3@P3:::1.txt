

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-language text classification that addresses the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages. The authors formulate a matrix completion problem to fill missing entries in a partially observed bilingual document-term matrix, then apply latent semantic indexing (LSI) to obtain low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The key novelty lies in first completing the matrix using a projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence guarantees, then applying LSI to the completed matrix, rather than directly applying methods like CL-LSI or CL-KCCA to the incomplete matrix. This work builds upon previous cross-lingual representation learning methods (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) that operate directly on the concatenated document-term matrix without first completing missing entries.

**Strengths:**
1. **Quality:** The paper presents a theoretically grounded approach with a convergence proof for the projected gradient descent algorithm (Theorem 1). The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines across 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks. The method shows particular strength when parallel data is limited (Section 5.3), which is a valuable practical consideration.

2. **Originality:** While the two-step framework is conceptually simple, the specific application of matrix completion to bridge cross-lingual gaps represents a novel approach compared to existing methods that directly operate on incomplete matrices. The paper effectively distinguishes this from related work (Section 2).

3. **Significance:** The approach addresses a practical challenge in cross-lingual learning where labeled data in target languages is scarce. The demonstrated effectiveness with small amounts of parallel data makes this method particularly valuable for real-world applications where parallel data is limited.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Clarity issues:** The paper needs clarification on several technical points:
   - Why apply LSI after matrix completion? The authors should explain why dimensionality reduction with LSI is necessary after the matrix completion step (which enforces low-rankness). Is M* sufficiently dense that LSI provides meaningful dimensionality reduction, or is this primarily for computational convenience? Why LSI rather than PCA or other techniques?
   - The experimental setup is unclear. The paper states "4k labeled source docs, 100 labeled target docs, 2k unlabeled parallel docs" but needs confirmation of the exact data splits. Section 5.3 should explicitly state whether the unlabeled parallel data varies from 200 to 2k across experiments.

2. **Inconsistency in results:** Figure 1 shows CL-KCCA outperforming TSL for EFM and EGM at 2k unlabeled documents, but Table 1 shows TSL consistently outperforming CL-KCCA across all tasks. The authors should explain this discrepancy.

3. **Theoretical concern:** The paper assumes a fully observed document-term matrix is low-rank, but this is questionable. Document-term matrices are typically very sparse (most entries are zero), and documents are not obvious linear combinations of other documents. The authors should provide more intuition or evidence for why the document-term matrix would be low-rank.

4. **Lack of comparison:** The paper should include running-time comparisons between the projected gradient descent algorithm and the baselines (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA), which is important for practical adoption.

5. **Clarification of data usage:** The paper should explicitly distinguish and separately report the two ways external data is used: (1) labeled source-language data to help labeled target data, and (2) unlabeled parallel data. This would make the experimental setup more transparent.

**Minor editorial suggestions:**
- Fix "(ij)" to "(i,j)" before Equation (2)
- Remove "such as" before Equations (4) and (5)

**Overall assessment:** The paper presents a simple yet effective method for cross-lingual text classification with solid theoretical grounding and compelling experimental results. The two-step approach is conceptually clear and the convergence guarantee adds theoretical rigor. However, several technical points and experiment details require clarification to fully validate the claims and make the method more transparent for replication.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to cross-lingual learning, but needs to address the clarity issues identified above. The method is novel enough to be of interest to the NIPS community, and the results demonstrate significant practical value. With the requested clarifications, this paper would be a strong contribution to the field.
