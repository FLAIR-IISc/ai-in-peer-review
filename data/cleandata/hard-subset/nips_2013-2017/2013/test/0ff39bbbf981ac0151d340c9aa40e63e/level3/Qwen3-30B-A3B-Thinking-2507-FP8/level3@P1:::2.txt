

This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that addresses the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages. The authors formulate a matrix completion problem to fill missing entries in a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix (using unlabeled parallel documents), followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) to derive low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The method contrasts with prior work like CL-LSI (Vinokourov et al., NIPS 2005) and CL-OPCA (Wan et al., IJCAI 2011), which apply dimensionality reduction directly on the incomplete matrix without filling missing values. The authors prove convergence of their projected gradient descent algorithm for matrix completion and evaluate the approach on 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality*: The experimental design is rigorous and comprehensive. The authors evaluate against four strong baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) across 18 tasks, with 10 repeated runs per task to report standard deviations. The method consistently outperforms all baselines (e.g., +14.61% accuracy over TBOW on EFB), with statistically significant gains (p<0.05 via t-tests). The matrix completion formulation with ℓ₁ and trace norm regularization is theoretically sound, and the convergence proof for the projected gradient descent algorithm (Algorithm 1) strengthens technical validity. Crucially, the method demonstrates robustness with minimal parallel data (e.g., 200 documents), a practical advantage over competing methods that degrade sharply with small data.  

*Originality*: The application of matrix completion to bridge cross-lingual feature spaces is novel. While prior work (e.g., CL-LSI) uses LSI on incomplete matrices, this paper’s two-step process—first recovering the full matrix via completion, then applying LSI—explicitly leverages co-occurrence patterns across languages. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work in Section 2, noting that methods like CL-KCCA (Platt et al., EMNLP 2008) require learning projections *before* representation, whereas their method learns a unified representation directly.  

*Significance*: The results show tangible improvements for low-resource cross-lingual tasks, where labeled target data is scarce. The 8.59–14.61% accuracy gains over baselines are substantial, and the method’s effectiveness with only 200 parallel documents (vs. thousands required by some baselines) makes it highly practical for real-world deployment. This advances the state of the art in cross-lingual learning by providing a scalable, data-efficient solution.  

*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and reproducible. The methodology (Sections 3–4) details all parameters (γ, ρ, k), optimization steps, and hyperparameter selection procedures. The experimental setup (Section 5.1) clearly defines datasets, tasks, and baselines, with all implementation choices (e.g., SVM with default LIBSVM settings) justified. The writing is precise, and the figures (e.g., Figure 1) effectively illustrate key findings.  

**Weaknesses:**  
The introduction could better motivate *why* matrix completion is necessary before introducing the method. While Section 3.1 states that treating missing entries as zeros loses information, a brief comparison to prior approaches (e.g., "Unlike CL-LSI, which ignores missing values, our method explicitly models them") would strengthen the narrative. This minor gap is offset by the paper’s strong technical execution.  

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**  
*Pro*:  
- Novel two-step framework (completion + LSI) that outperforms state-of-the-art by significant margins.  
- Rigorous evaluation with 18 tasks, 10 runs, and convergence guarantees.  
- Practical impact: works effectively with very small parallel datasets (200 documents).  
- Highly reproducible; all parameters and procedures documented.  

*Con*:  
- Motivation for matrix completion in the introduction is underdeveloped (minor).  
- No ablation study on the impact of individual components (e.g., ℓ₁ vs. trace norm).  

**Verdict:** This paper makes a strong, well-executed contribution to cross-lingual learning. The method is original, technically sound, and demonstrates significant empirical advantages over prior work, especially in data-scarce scenarios. The minor clarity issue does not undermine the paper’s core contribution. The results are highly relevant to NIPS, advancing the state of the art in cross-lingual representation learning with clear practical implications. **Recommend acceptance.**
