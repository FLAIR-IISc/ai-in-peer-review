The paper "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning" proposes a new approach to cross language text classification by exploiting a set of parallel bilingual documents. The authors formulate a matrix completion problem to produce a complete parallel document-term matrix for all documents in two languages and then induce a low-dimensional cross-lingual document representation by applying latent semantic indexing on the obtained matrix. The proposed method is evaluated by conducting a set of experiments with cross language sentiment classification tasks on Amazon product reviews.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear overview of the challenges in cross language text classification and the limitations of existing approaches. The proposed two-step method is novel and has the potential to improve the performance of cross language text classification systems. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the related work and clearly explain the differences between their approach and existing methods.

However, there are some concerns regarding the quality of the results. The improvements achieved by the proposed method might be due to the latent semantic indexing (LSI) scaling rather than the matrix completion step. To address this concern, the authors should evaluate their method using a classifier that is insensitive to per-feature rescaling, such as boosted decision trees.

The paper is generally well-written, but there are some clarity issues. The authors use ambiguous phrasing when describing the "unified document-term matrix," making it unclear whether the terms are language-agnostic or language-specific surface forms. Additionally, the experimental setup description is fragmented and spread across Table 1, making it hard to follow.

In terms of originality, the idea of using matrix completion to create multilingual row vectors and then applying CL-LSI on those multilingual vectors appears to be new. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the related work and clearly explain the differences between their approach and existing methods.

The significance of the paper lies in the fact that matrix completion is a well-controlled non-linear inference technique. If the results are robust, the approach is interesting and has the potential to improve the performance of cross language text classification systems.

Overall, the paper presents a neat idea, but the authors should try classifiers beyond linear SVM to rule out scaling artifacts. With some revisions to address the quality concerns and clarity issues, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel approach to cross language text classification.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of the related work and clearly explain the differences between their approach and existing methods.
* The proposed method has the potential to improve the performance of cross language text classification systems.
* The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear overview of the challenges in cross language text classification and the limitations of existing approaches.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The results may not be robust, and the improvements achieved by the proposed method might be due to the LSI scaling rather than the matrix completion step.
* The paper has some clarity issues, such as ambiguous phrasing and a fragmented experimental setup description.
* The authors should evaluate their method using a classifier that is insensitive to per-feature rescaling to rule out scaling artifacts.

Recommendations:

* Evaluate the proposed method using a classifier that is insensitive to per-feature rescaling, such as boosted decision trees.
* Revise the paper to address the clarity issues and provide a clearer description of the experimental setup.
