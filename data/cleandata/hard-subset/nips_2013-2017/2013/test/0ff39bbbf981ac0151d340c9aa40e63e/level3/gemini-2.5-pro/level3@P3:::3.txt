This paper proposes a novel two-step method for learning cross-lingual document representations for text classification. The core idea is to first construct a unified document-term matrix where documents are represented by the concatenated vocabulary of two languages. For monolingual documents, the features corresponding to the other language are treated as missing values. The authors then apply matrix completion to infer these missing values, creating a dense, multilingual vector for each document. In the second step, standard Cross-Lingual Latent Semantic Indexing (CL-LSI) is applied to this completed matrix to learn a low-dimensional shared representation for classification. This approach is related to prior work like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, which also learn shared representations from parallel corpora. The key difference and main contribution is the introduction of matrix completion as a preceding step to densify the document representations, which aims to better capture cross-lingual co-occurrence patterns.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound in its formulation of the matrix completion problem and the use of a projected gradient descent algorithm for optimization. The experimental results, as presented, show a significant improvement over several strong baselines on a cross-lingual sentiment classification task.

However, there is a major concern regarding the robustness of these results. The reported improvement might be an artifact of the experimental setup rather than the novelty of the proposed method. The method relies on LSI (which is based on SVD) for the final dimensionality reduction step, and a linear SVM is used as the sole classifier for evaluation. LSI implicitly rescales the feature space, and linear SVMs are known to be sensitive to the scaling of input features. In contrast, two of the main baselines, CL-OPCA and CL-KCCA, do not necessarily produce representations with the same scaling properties. Consequently, the observed performance gain could simply be due to LSI's scaling being more compatible with a linear SVM than the representations produced by the baseline methods. This potential confound makes it difficult to attribute the improvement solely to the matrix completion step, which is the paper's central contribution. Without ruling this out, the primary claims of the paper are not fully supported.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written, but could be improved in two areas:
1.  The phrasing "unified document-term matrix" is ambiguous. It is not explicitly stated whether the terms in the unified vocabulary are language-specific surface forms (e.g., English 'good' and French 'bon' are distinct columns) or if they are mapped to some language-agnostic space. The former seems to be the case, but this should be clarified to avoid confusion.
2.  The description of the experimental setup is fragmented. Details about the datasets, parameter settings, training/testing splits, and evaluation protocol are scattered throughout Section 5 and the caption of Table 1. This makes the experimental procedure difficult to follow and reproduce. A consolidated paragraph or table detailing all aspects of the setup would greatly enhance the paper's clarity.

**Originality**

The core idea of using matrix completion to create dense multilingual document vectors before applying a dimensionality reduction technique like LSI appears to be novel. It is an interesting combination of familiar techniques to tackle the problem of bridging language-specific feature spaces, especially for documents that are not part of a parallel corpus. The related work section is adequate and correctly positions the contribution relative to previous approaches.

**Significance**

If the results can be shown to be robust, the approach is quite significant. Matrix completion is a well-controlled and principled non-linear inference technique. Applying it in this context offers a new and potentially powerful method for leveraging parallel data to enrich monolingual representations. The reported strong performance, particularly when the amount of parallel data is small, would be a valuable contribution to low-resource cross-lingual learning.

**Summary and Recommendation**

This paper introduces a neat and original two-step approach for cross-lingual representation learning. The idea of using matrix completion to densify representations is compelling. However, the experimental evaluation is undermined by a potential confound: the observed improvements may stem from the favorable interaction between LSI's feature scaling and the linear SVM classifier, rather than the effectiveness of the matrix completion step itself.

To strengthen the paper and validate its claims, the authors should re-evaluate their method using a classifier that is insensitive to per-feature rescaling. An excellent choice would be an ensemble of decision trees, such as AdaBoost or Random Forests (which are readily available in toolkits like Weka or scikit-learn). If the performance gains persist with such a classifier, it would provide much stronger evidence for the paper's central claim.

Overall, this is a promising paper with a novel idea, but the current evaluation is not sufficient to warrant acceptance. I recommend rejection for now but would be very positive about a revised version that addresses the experimental concerns.
