This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, tackling the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages. The core idea is to first complete a partially observed document-term matrix built from parallel bilingual documents using matrix completion, and then apply Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to induce a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation. The matrix completion step is optimized via a projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence guarantees. The method is evaluated on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, demonstrating superior performance compared to several baselines, particularly when limited parallel data is available.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research in cross-lingual learning. The paper appropriately positions itself within the field, referencing key approaches such as machine translation-based methods [18, 6, 23], methods leveraging bilingual word pairs [16], and techniques employing comparable corpora [10, 20, 15]. The authors correctly identify these approaches and distinguish their work by focusing on matrix completion as a preprocessing step to enhance cross-lingual representations, especially in low-resource parallel data scenarios. Connections to related work leveraging LSI in cross-lingual settings [10] are also clearly articulated.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of matrix completion to address the cross-lingual feature space gap is a novel contribution. While matrix completion is well-established in other domains, its use in this context, combined with LSI, presents a fresh perspective.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling. The proposed method consistently outperforms a diverse set of baselines, including strong contenders like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA. The improvement in performance, particularly with limited parallel data, is significant.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors demonstrate a commitment to rigorous evaluation. They consider multiple cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks, providing a comprehensive assessment of the method's performance. The inclusion of learning curves showcasing the impact of varying amounts of parallel data adds further value.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithm, based on projected gradient descent, is well-motivated and comes with convergence guarantees. The detailed description of the optimization process and parameter selection is commendable.
* **Clarity & Reproducibility:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The methodology is clearly explained, and all key parameters and selection procedures are reported, enhancing reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Motivation for Matrix Completion:** While the benefits of matrix completion are clearly demonstrated empirically, the initial motivation in the introduction could be strengthened. A more intuitive explanation of *why* matrix completion is a suitable approach for bridging the cross-lingual feature gap would be helpful.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** Although parameter selection is explained, a discussion of the method’s sensitivity to parameter settings would be beneficial. While the authors provide selected parameters, understanding how performance changes with variations in γ, ρ, and k would increase confidence in the robustness of the results.
* **Limited Task Domain:** The experiments are confined to sentiment classification on Amazon reviews. Exploring the method's effectiveness on other cross-lingual tasks (e.g., document categorization, topic modeling) would broaden the scope and demonstrate generalizability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to cross-lingual representation learning.
* The experimental results are strong and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods, particularly in low-resource scenarios.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.
* The contribution addresses a relevant and challenging problem in natural language processing.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of deeper exploration of parameter sensitivity could raise concerns about the method’s robustness.
* The limited task domain might limit the perceived broader impact of the work.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of cross-lingual learning. The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and demonstrably effective. The strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the minor weaknesses. I believe this paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS 2013.

**Score:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)
