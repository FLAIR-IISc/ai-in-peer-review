## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a novel two-step approach to cross-language text classification. The method first completes a partially observed document-term matrix, constructed from parallel and non-parallel documents across two languages, using projected gradient descent with convergence guarantees. It then applies Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to the completed matrix to derive a low-dimensional, cross-lingual document representation suitable for training a classifier. The core idea of leveraging matrix completion to bridge the cross-lingual feature space is interesting and potentially valuable, particularly in low-resource scenarios. 

The authors appropriately situate their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers in cross-lingual transfer learning, including approaches based on machine translation, bilingual resources, and multi-view learning (e.g., CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA). The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the methodology. The theoretical guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm is a significant strength. Experimental results on Amazon reviews demonstrate competitive performance, consistently outperforming several baselines.

However, several points require clarification. The motivation for using LSI *after* matrix completion is not fully explained. While computational efficiency is a plausible reason given the potential density of the completed matrix despite sparsity enforcement, a more explicit discussion is needed. Why LSI specifically, rather than alternatives like PCA which might be more directly tied to variance explained, would also be helpful. 

The experimental setup lacks detail. Specifically, confirmation is needed that training involved 4k labeled source documents, 100 labeled target documents, 2k unlabeled parallel documents, with testing on the remaining 1.9k target documents. It’s also unclear if Section 5.3 systematically varied the number of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2k. Furthermore, the inconsistency between Figure 1 and Table 1 regarding CL-KCCA’s performance (outperforming TSL for EFM/EGM at 2k unlabeled in Figure 1, but not in Table 1) needs resolution.

A fundamental question arises regarding the assumption of low-rank structure in a fully observed document-term matrix. Given sparsity and the lack of obvious linear combinations between documents, the rationale for this assumption warrants more explanation.  Intuitively, why does TSL frequently outperform methods like CCA, which seem naturally suited for multi-view cross-lingual tasks? A discussion relating the low-rank enforcement in matrix completion to the benefits offered by other techniques would be insightful. Finally, a comparison of the running time of the projected gradient descent algorithm to the other methods (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) would enhance the practical assessment of the proposed approach.

Minor editorial points: Change "(ij)" to "(i,j)" before Eq. (2), and remove "such as" before Eqs. (4) and (5).

**Overall:** This is a promising method that is simple, effective, and generally well-presented. However, addressing the technical questions and clarifying experimental details are crucial for a stronger evaluation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
