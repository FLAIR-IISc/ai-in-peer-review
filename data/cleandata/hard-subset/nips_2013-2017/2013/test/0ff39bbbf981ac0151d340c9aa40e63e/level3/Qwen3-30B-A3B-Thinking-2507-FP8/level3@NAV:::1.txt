

This paper proposes a two-step matrix completion approach for cross-lingual sentiment classification. The authors construct a partially observed document-term matrix using parallel bilingual documents, complete it via low-rank and sparse matrix completion with projected gradient descent (with convergence guarantees), and then apply LSI to obtain cross-lingual representations. The method is evaluated on Amazon product reviews across multiple language pairs.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution by combining matrix completion with LSI for cross-lingual representation learning. The convergence analysis for the projected gradient descent algorithm is a notable strength. The experimental results demonstrate consistent improvements over baseline methods (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA), particularly when parallel data is limited, which is a significant practical advantage.

However, several clarifications are needed. First, the paper does not explain why LSI is applied after matrix completion, especially since the matrix completion enforces sparsity. Is this purely for computational convenience (given that M* becomes relatively dense), and why LSI rather than PCA or other dimensionality reduction techniques? Second, the experimental setup requires clarification: the paper states "4k labeled source docs, 100 labeled target docs, 2k unlabeled parallel docs" but this needs verification as it conflicts with the description in Section 5.3 where unlabeled parallel data varies from 200 to 2k. Third, there's an inconsistency between Figure 1 (where CL-KCCA outperforms TSL at 2k unlabeled data for EFM/EGM) and Table 1 (where TSL consistently outperforms CL-KCCA), requiring clarification.

The theoretical justification for the low-rank assumption in document-term matrices needs strengthening, as it's unclear why a fully observed document-term matrix would be low-rank given document sparsity and non-linear relationships between documents. Additionally, the paper should provide intuition for why TSL outperforms methods like CL-KCCA (which seems more natural for multiview cross-lingual tasks) and include running-time comparisons with other methods.

While the paper is generally clearly written and the method is simple and effective, these technical and experimental clarifications are necessary for full evaluation. The approach shows promise for cross-lingual learning with limited parallel data, but the current presentation requires refinement to fully demonstrate its significance. The paper meets quality standards but needs improvements in clarity and completeness before acceptance.
