## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step matrix completion approach for cross-lingual representation learning. The authors address the challenge of bridging feature spaces across languages by first constructing a partially observed document-term matrix encompassing both languages, completing it via projected gradient descent with sparsity and low-rank constraints, and then applying Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) for dimensionality reduction before classification. The method is evaluated on cross-lingual sentiment classification with Amazon product reviews, demonstrating competitive performance, particularly with limited parallel data.

The core idea is sound and builds upon existing work in cross-lingual learning, such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, by introducing a matrix completion step. The theoretical guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm is a valuable contribution, and the experimental results are promising. The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the proposed method. 

However, several points require clarification. The motivation for using LSI *after* matrix completion is unclear. While the authors mention computational convenience, a more detailed explanation of why LSI is preferred over alternatives like PCA, given the potential for relatively dense matrices despite sparsity enforcement, is needed. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from explicitly distinguishing and reporting results for the two ways external data is used: labeled source-language data aiding target-language classification, and unlabeled parallel data for matrix completion. 

Regarding the experimental setup, confirmation is needed regarding the training data size: 4k source, 100 target, 2k parallel, and testing on 1.9k target. It's also crucial to clarify if Section 5.3 systematically varies the amount of unlabeled parallel data from 200 to 2k. An inconsistency exists between Figure 1 and Table 1: CL-KCCA outperforms TSL for EFM and EGM at 2k unlabeled data in Figure 1, but not in Table 1. 

A theoretical question arises: why would a fully observed document-term matrix be inherently low-rank, considering the sparsity and the lack of obvious linear combinations between documents?  The authors should address this assumption.  Furthermore, while TSL performs well, the intuition behind its success compared to methods like CCA, which seem naturally suited for multi-view cross-lingual tasks, is not fully explained.  Finally, a running-time comparison of the projected gradient descent algorithm against CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA would strengthen the evaluation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel application of matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning.
*   Theoretical convergence guarantee for the optimization algorithm.
*   Strong empirical performance, especially with limited parallel data.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of justification for the choice of LSI.
*   Unclear experimental details and inconsistencies in results.
*   Missing theoretical discussion on the low-rank assumption.
*   Absence of runtime comparison.



Overall, this is a promising paper with a novel approach. Addressing the points raised above would significantly strengthen the contribution and increase confidence in the results. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions.
