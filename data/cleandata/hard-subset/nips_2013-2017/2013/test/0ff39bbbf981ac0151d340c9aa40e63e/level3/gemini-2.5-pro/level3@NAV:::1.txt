This paper proposes a novel two-step learning method (TSL) for cross-language text classification. The core idea is to first construct a single, incomplete document-term matrix spanning both source and target language vocabularies. This matrix is then completed using a projected gradient descent algorithm that enforces low-rank and sparsity constraints. Finally, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to the completed matrix to generate a low-dimensional representation for training a standard classifier. This work relates to previous methods in cross-lingual representation learning, such as CL-LSI and CL-KCCA, but its novelty lies in explicitly treating the problem as matrix completion *before* dimensionality reduction, rather than applying techniques like LSI or CCA directly to the sparse, concatenated matrix.

The paper's primary strengths are its simplicity and strong empirical performance. The method is elegant and outperforms several competitive baselines, especially when the amount of unlabeled parallel data is small. The inclusion of a convergence guarantee for the optimization algorithm provides welcome theoretical grounding. The paper is also generally well-written and clearly presented.

However, several points require clarification to strengthen the contribution. First, the central assumption that a fully observed document-term matrix is low-rank needs more justification. Given the inherent sparsity of these matrices, the intuition for why documents should be linear combinations of a few basis documents is not obvious. Further, it is unclear why LSI is necessary after matrix completion. Is this because the completed matrix M* becomes dense, making LSI a computational convenience? The choice of LSI over alternatives like PCA should also be motivated. On the experimental side, the setup is ambiguous. The authors should confirm the training configuration (4k labeled source, 100 labeled target, 2k unlabeled parallel) and test set (remaining 1.9k target). It would also be beneficial to explicitly report the separate contributions of labeled source data versus unlabeled parallel data. An apparent inconsistency exists where CL-KCCA outperforms TSL in Figure 1 (for EFM/EGM) but not in Table 1. Finally, providing a running-time comparison against the baselines would be valuable.

Overall, this is a promising paper with a simple and effective method. Its acceptance would be strengthened by addressing the questions regarding theoretical motivation, experimental details, and key design choices. Minor editorial fixes include changing (ij) to (i,j) before Eq. (2) and removing "such as" before Eqs. (4) and (5).
