This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-language representation learning, which is a critical component of cross-language text classification. The method first formulates a matrix completion problem to produce a complete parallel document-term matrix for all documents in two languages, and then induces a low-dimensional cross-lingual document representation by applying latent semantic indexing on the obtained matrix. The proposed method is evaluated by conducting a set of experiments with cross-language sentiment classification tasks on Amazon product reviews, and the experimental results demonstrate that the proposed learning method outperforms a number of other cross-language representation learning methods, especially when the number of parallel bilingual documents is small.

The proposed approach is related to previous work in the literature that addresses cross-language text classification by transforming the training or test data from one language domain into the other language domain by using machine translation tools or bilingual lexicons, and constructing cross-lingual representations by using readily available auxiliary resources such as bilingual word pairs, comparable corpora, and other multilingual resources. However, the proposed approach only requires a small set of unlabeled parallel documents, while these approaches require at least translating all the training documents in one language domain.

The proposed two-step learning method exploits parallel documents to learn a low-rank document-term matrix by automatically filling the missing entries via matrix completion. The method then produces cross-lingual representations by applying the latent semantic indexing method over the learned matrix. The authors provide a convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm used to solve the matrix completion problem.

The experimental results show that the proposed method significantly outperforms a number of cross-language learning methods. The method is also robust to the size of the unlabeled parallel data, and achieves great performance with only 200 unlabeled parallel documents. However, several technical points and experiment details need clarification.

One question is why the authors reduce the feature dimension with LSI after estimating M. Is this for computational convenience because M* becomes relatively dense despite sparsity enforcement, and why LSI instead of alternatives like PCA? Additionally, it would be helpful to explicitly distinguish and separately report the two ways external data are used: (1) labeled source-language data to help labeled target data, and (2) unlabeled parallel data.

The experimental setup is also unclear, and it would be helpful to confirm the training and testing data. Specifically, is the training data 4k labeled source docs, 100 labeled target docs, 2k unlabeled parallel docs, and is the testing data the remaining 1.9k target docs? Additionally, does Section 5.3 vary the unlabeled parallel docs from 200 to 2k?

There is also an inconsistency in the results, where CL-KCCA outperforms TSL in Figure 1 for EFM and EGM at 2k unlabeled but not in Table 1. It would be helpful to clarify this discrepancy.

From a theoretical perspective, it is unclear why a fully observed document-term matrix would be low-rank, given sparsity and that documents are not obvious linear combinations of other documents. Additionally, it would be helpful to provide intuition on why TSL often outperforms other methods, especially CCA. CCA feels natural for multiview cross-language tasks, whereas matrix completion’s main driver appears to be enforcing low-rank — how does this compare to other techniques?

Finally, it would be helpful to include a running-time comparison of the projected gradient descent algorithm with other methods, such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA.

Overall, the method is simple, effective, and generally clearly presented, but several technical points and experiment details need clarification.

Strengths:

* The proposed method is simple and effective for cross-language representation learning.
* The method is robust to the size of the unlabeled parallel data.
* The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed learning method outperforms a number of other cross-language representation learning methods.

Weaknesses:

* The experimental setup is unclear, and it would be helpful to confirm the training and testing data.
* There is an inconsistency in the results, where CL-KCCA outperforms TSL in Figure 1 for EFM and EGM at 2k unlabeled but not in Table 1.
