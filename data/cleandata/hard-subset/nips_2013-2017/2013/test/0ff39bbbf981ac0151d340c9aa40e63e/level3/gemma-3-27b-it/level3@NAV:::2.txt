## Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper presents a novel two-step approach to cross-lingual representation learning, combining matrix completion with latent semantic analysis (LSA). The core idea is to leverage parallel bilingual documents to fill in a sparse document-term matrix, effectively bridging the feature space gap between languages, before applying LSA for dimensionality reduction. The authors demonstrate strong performance on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon reviews, consistently outperforming several baselines including state-of-the-art methods, particularly with limited parallel data. 

The work builds upon existing literature in cross-lingual learning, addressing the challenge of disjoint feature spaces. Approaches like machine translation (Dai et al., 2007) and exploiting bilingual resources (Gliozzo, 2005) are well-established. This paper distinguishes itself by proposing a unique application of matrix completion, a technique typically used in recommender systems, to this cross-lingual context. The authors appropriately position their work relative to related techniques like CL-LSI (Littman et al., 2001) and CL-KCCA (Vinokourov et al., 2005), highlighting the advantages of their two-step process.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear mathematical formulation of the matrix completion problem and a well-defined optimization algorithm based on projected gradient descent, including a convergence analysis. The experimental setup is thorough, comparing against a comprehensive set of baselines, including bag-of-words, cross-lingual LSA, and more recent dimensionality reduction techniques.  Importantly, the paper demonstrates excellent reproducibility, meticulously reporting all parameters and selection procedures.  The learning curves clearly demonstrate the method's stability and effectiveness even with small amounts of parallel data, a crucial advantage in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses:** While the performance gains are significant, the initial motivation for using matrix completion in the introduction could be strengthened. More explicitly connecting the inherent low-rank structure of document-term matrices to the benefits of matrix completion would be beneficial.  

**Overall:** This is a strong contribution to the field of cross-lingual representation learning. The proposed method is novel, effective, and well-supported by experimental results. The clarity and reproducibility of the work are commendable. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
* Novel application of matrix completion to a challenging NLP problem.
* Significant performance improvement over strong baselines.
* Robustness to limited parallel data.
* Thorough experimental evaluation and clear presentation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
* The motivation for matrix completion could be more explicit.



**Recommendation:** Strongly Accept. This paper meets the criteria for NIPS publication in terms of quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
