The paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-language representation learning, aiming to bridge the feature spaces of different languages by exploiting a set of parallel bilingual documents. The approach first formulates a matrix completion problem to produce a complete parallel document-term matrix for all documents in two languages, and then induces a low-dimensional cross-lingual document representation by applying latent semantic indexing on the obtained matrix.

The proposed method is technically sound, as it is well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the matrix completion problem and the projected gradient descent algorithm used to solve it. The convergence guarantees of the algorithm are also provided, which ensures the stability of the method.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The authors provide sufficient background information and related work to help readers understand the context and significance of the proposed method. The experimental setup and results are also clearly presented, making it easy to reproduce the results.

The proposed method is original and novel, as it combines matrix completion and latent semantic indexing to learn cross-lingual representations. The authors also provide a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The results are significant, as they show that the proposed method outperforms other cross-language learning methods, especially when the number of parallel bilingual documents is small. The learning curves also demonstrate strong and stable performance, even with relatively small amounts of parallel/unlabeled data.

The experiments are thorough and compare against bag-of-words, cross-lingual LSA, and two state-of-the-art dimensionality reduction methods. The paper is well-organized and reproducible, with all parameters and selection procedures reported.

One minor suggestion for improvement is to better motivate the use of matrix completion in the Introduction. While the authors provide a clear explanation of the matrix completion problem, it would be helpful to provide more context on why this approach is necessary and how it addresses the challenges of cross-language learning.

Overall, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and original. The proposed method is significant, and the results are thorough and well-presented. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of natural language processing and cross-language learning.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and original method for cross-language representation learning.
* The method is technically sound and well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results.
* The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and easy to follow.
* The results are significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The experiments are thorough and compare against state-of-the-art methods.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from a clearer motivation for the use of matrix completion in the Introduction.
* Some readers may find the technical details of the matrix completion problem and the projected gradient descent algorithm challenging to follow.

Rating: 9/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
