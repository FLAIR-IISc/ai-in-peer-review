

# Review of "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a novel two-step approach for cross-lingual representation learning that first solves a matrix completion problem on a partially observed bilingual document-term matrix, followed by latent semantic analysis to produce low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors frame the problem as learning from a sparse matrix where parallel documents provide the only connections between language-specific vocabulary spaces. This work extends previous cross-lingual learning approaches like CL-LSI [10] and CL-KCCA [20] by introducing matrix completion as a preprocessing step to fill missing entries before applying dimensionality reduction. Unlike translation-based approaches [18, 23], the method requires only a small set of unlabeled parallel documents rather than translating all training data.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-justified optimization approach. The authors formulate a convex optimization problem for matrix completion that incorporates rank minimization, sparsity, and nonnegativity constraints. They provide a convergence proof for their projected gradient descent algorithm (Algorithm 1), which is particularly valuable given the non-smooth nature of the optimization problem. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing against multiple baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) across 18 cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks on Amazon reviews. The results consistently show that the proposed method outperforms all baselines, with particularly significant improvements (8.59-14.61% average accuracy gains) when using only 100 labeled target documents.

The paper excels in clarity and reproducibility. The methodology is well-structured, with clear mathematical formulations and algorithm descriptions. All key parameters (γ, ρ, k) are thoroughly reported with their selection procedures, and the experimental setup is detailed enough for replication. The learning curves (Figure 1 and 2) compellingly demonstrate the method's stability and effectiveness even with minimal parallel data (as few as 200 documents), which is a significant practical advantage.

The originality of the work is notable: while matrix completion has been applied to recommendation systems [4, 11], its application to cross-lingual representation learning represents a novel perspective. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous approaches by emphasizing that matrix completion automatically captures cross-lingual co-occurrence patterns before dimensionality reduction, rather than applying LSI or other methods directly on the incomplete matrix.

The significance of this work is substantial. It demonstrates that matrix completion can serve as an effective preprocessing step for cross-lingual tasks, potentially extending to other applications beyond sentiment analysis. The paper's findings address a critical challenge in cross-lingual learning—bridging disjoint feature spaces with minimal parallel data—showing practical utility for resource-constrained scenarios.

Minor weaknesses include a slightly underdeveloped motivation for matrix completion in the introduction. While the paper later justifies the approach through the low-rank assumption of document-term matrices, a more explicit connection to prior work on matrix completion in NLP would strengthen the introduction. Additionally, the paper could better discuss computational scalability for larger datasets, though the convergence proof suggests the method is theoretically sound.

The paper's strengths far outweigh these minor weaknesses. The technical quality is excellent, with rigorous optimization and convergence analysis. The experimental design is thorough and well-justified, with appropriate baselines and comprehensive results. The method's practical advantages (small data requirements, strong performance) make it valuable for real-world applications where parallel data is scarce.

The paper addresses all four NIPS criteria effectively:
- Quality: Strong theoretical foundation with convergence proof, robust experimental validation
- Clarity: Well-organized with clear algorithms and detailed parameter selection
- Originality: Novel application of matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning
- Significance: Demonstrates practical utility with significant performance gains on a meaningful task

This paper represents a solid contribution to cross-lingual learning that advances the state of the art with both theoretical and practical significance. The method is both elegant and effective, providing a clear path for future work in cross-lingual representation learning. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
