## Review of “A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning”

This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning, combining matrix completion with Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI). The core idea is to address the disjoint feature space problem in cross-lingual text classification by first completing a partially observed document-term matrix built from parallel bilingual documents, and then applying LSI to induce a shared, low-dimensional representation. This approach leverages matrix completion as a controlled non-linear inference technique to bridge the language gap. The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the challenges of cross-lingual learning and positioning the proposed method within the existing literature, referencing relevant work in machine translation, bilingual lexicons, and cross-lingual representation learning (e.g., CL-LSI, CL-OPCA).

The strength of this work lies in its novelty. The combination of matrix completion and CL-LSI appears to be a new approach, and the theoretical justification for using matrix completion to address the sparse, cross-lingual data is sound. The experimental results on Amazon product reviews demonstrate promising performance, consistently outperforming several baselines, particularly when the amount of parallel data is limited. The inclusion of an optimization algorithm with convergence guarantees is also a positive aspect. 

However, a significant concern is the robustness of the reported improvements. The authors primarily evaluate with a linear SVM classifier. As highlighted in the paper’s limitations, the observed gains could be attributable to the scaling properties of LSI rather than the effectiveness of the matrix completion step itself. LSI inherently performs a form of feature scaling, which might disproportionately benefit a linear classifier. To address this, I strongly recommend evaluating the method with a classifier *insensitive* to per-feature rescaling, as suggested by the conference guidelines – boosted decision trees (available in Weka) would be a suitable choice. 

Clarity is somewhat hampered by ambiguous phrasing. The term “unified document-term matrix” is unclear; it’s crucial to explicitly state whether the terms are language-agnostic or language-specific surface forms. Additionally, the experimental setup description is fragmented, scattered across the text and Table 1, making it difficult to quickly grasp the experimental details. Consolidating this information into a dedicated subsection would improve readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach combining matrix completion and LSI.
*   Promising experimental results on a standard dataset.
*   Theoretical justification for the method.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Potential for results to be an artifact of LSI scaling.
*   Lack of evaluation with classifiers insensitive to feature scaling.
*   Clarity issues regarding terminology and experimental setup.



**Overall:** This is a neat idea with potential. However, the authors need to address the robustness concern by performing additional experiments with alternative classifiers. If the performance gains hold with a classifier insensitive to scaling, the paper would represent a significant contribution. As it stands, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the inclusion of the suggested experiments.
