Summary
This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning that leverages a small set of unlabeled parallel documents. The first step formulates cross-lingual feature bridging as a matrix completion problem over a unified document-term matrix: parallel document pairs are fully observed across both vocabularies, while monolingual documents have missing entries for the other language. The authors relax rank minimization to trace norm, add an L1 penalty and nonnegativity, and fit observed entries with squared loss while softly regularizing missing entries. They develop a projected gradient descent algorithm with shrinkage (for the trace norm) and nonnegativity projection, and provide a convergence argument based on non-expansiveness. The second step applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation on which a linear SVM is trained.

The work is positioned with respect to translation-based approaches and cross-lingual representation learning methods such as CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], CL-OPCA [15], multilingual topic models [19], and SCL [16]. Unlike prior work that directly applies LSI/KCCA/OPCA to concatenated bilingual matrices or relies heavily on translation resources, this paper first completes the missing bilingual features via a convex matrix completion problem, then performs LSI. Experiments on multilingual Amazon sentiment classification (EN↔FR/DE/JA; Books/DVD/Music) show consistent gains over TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, especially when parallel data is scarce.

Quality
- Methodology: The convex relaxation (trace norm + L1 + nonnegativity) with squared losses is reasonable and well motivated for inducing low-rank, sparse, nonnegative doc-term matrices. The projected gradient descent with shrinkage is standard for trace-norm regularization; the gradient is derived correctly, and the step-size conditions are explicit. The convergence argument is clear in spirit (non-expansive operator composition), though one technical detail would benefit from clarification: composing shrinkage (prox for the trace norm) with a separate nonnegativity projection is not, in general, the proximal operator of the sum of the two nonsmooth terms; please clarify conditions under which convergence to the true minimizer of (2) is guaranteed (beyond fixed-point convergence), or cite a result covering this composition.
- Empirics: The experimental setup is careful: fixed parameter selection on one task, consistent k across methods, and multiple runs with means and standard deviations. The learning-curve study varying the number of parallel documents is particularly informative and supports the claim of robustness with limited parallel data.
- Completeness: A useful ablation is missing: LSI directly on the unified matrix with zeros for missing entries (i.e., TSL without the completion step) to isolate the contribution of matrix completion. Likewise, “completion only” (no LSI) would test whether the second step is strictly necessary. Runtime/complexity and iteration counts are not reported; given the per-iteration SVD, scalability results (e.g., truncated SVD, wall-clock times) would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
The paper is well organized and readable. Algorithmic details and hyperparameters are reported, and convergence lemmas are stated cleanly. Two minor suggestions:
- Better motivate matrix completion in the Introduction: explicitly argue why the unified bilingual doc-term matrix should be approximately low-rank (e.g., shared latent topics across languages) and how parallel rows constrain cross-lingual co-occurrences.
- Elaborate on the role and intuition of ρ (regularization on missing entries) and µ (sparsity) and provide guidance on their selection. Reporting typical vocabulary sizes and the rank k selected would also help replication.

Originality
Applying convex matrix completion as a preprocessing step to bridge disjoint cross-lingual vocabularies appears novel relative to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, and SCL. The formulation that treats monolingual rows as partially observed bilingual vectors is an elegant way to exploit limited parallel data while using all available monolingual data.

Significance
The method yields consistent improvements across 18 EN↔FR/DE/JA sentiment tasks and is particularly strong in the low-parallel-data regime, which is practically important. The approach is general and likely applicable beyond sentiment (e.g., topic classification, retrieval). The reliance on a small number of parallel documents makes it attractive for low-resource settings.

Pros
- Clear, principled two-step formulation; sound convex relaxation and simple optimization.
- Strong empirical gains over competitive baselines, including CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA.
- Robust performance with few parallel documents; informative learning-curve analysis.
- Reproducible setup with parameters and selection procedures reported.

Cons
- Theoretical detail: convergence of the specific composition (shrinkage + nonnegativity projection) to the minimizer of (2) needs a more rigorous justification or citation.
- Missing ablations to isolate the benefit of matrix completion vs. LSI alone, and the necessity of nonnegativity/L1.
- Limited evaluation scope (one dataset/domain); comparisons to SCL [16] and multilingual topic models [19] in practice would strengthen claims.
- No runtime/scalability analysis despite per-iteration SVD.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: (i) LSI on the unified matrix with zeros for missing entries; (ii) completion without LSI; (iii) effect of removing nonnegativity or L1.
- Provide computational details (truncated SVD, per-iteration time, total runtime) and memory footprint.
- Clarify the convergence proof for the constrained, doubly nonsmooth objective or reformulate via a single proximal operator (e.g., proximal of trace norm + indicator of nonnegativity).
- Expand evaluation to another cross-lingual dataset or task, and consider adding SCL and a multilingual topic model as baselines.
- Strengthen the motivation for low rank and the role of ρ and µ in practice.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and well-executed paper with a simple, novel idea that delivers tangible gains and is likely to influence subsequent cross-lingual representation learning work. With minor theoretical clarification and a few additional experiments, it would make a strong NIPS contribution. I recommend acceptance.
