This paper proposes a novel two-step method for learning cross-lingual document representations, aiming to improve cross-language text classification when labeled data in the target language is scarce. The core idea is to leverage a small set of unlabeled parallel documents to bridge the vocabulary gap between two languages. First, a "unified document-term matrix" is constructed for all documents (parallel and non-parallel) from both source and target languages. For non-parallel documents, the term entries corresponding to the other language are considered missing. The authors then formulate this as a matrix completion problem, recovering a complete, low-rank document-term matrix by inferring the missing values. In the second step, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to this completed matrix to produce a low-dimensional, dense cross-lingual representation. Standard monolingual classifiers can then be trained on this shared representation. This approach differs from prior work like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, or CL-KCCA, which typically apply dimensionality reduction directly to a concatenated (but not completed) document-term matrix. The proposed method is evaluated on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using the multilingual Amazon product reviews dataset, showing superior performance over several baselines, particularly when the number of parallel documents is small.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Originality:** The core idea of framing the cross-lingual representation problem as a matrix completion task appears to be novel. Combining this well-established technique with a subsequent LSI step is a creative way to address the problem of disjoint feature spaces and data sparsity.
*   **Significance:** If the method's effectiveness is robustly demonstrated, the contribution could be significant. Matrix completion offers a principled, non-linear inference technique to estimate the missing cross-lingual term associations. This provides a more sophisticated way to leverage parallel corpora compared to simply concatenating documents before applying linear projections, which is common in methods like CL-LSI. The approach is elegant and could inspire further work in this direction.
*   **Empirical Results:** The experiments show consistent and sometimes substantial improvements over several relevant baselines across 18 different tasks. The analysis of performance with varying amounts of parallel data is particularly compelling, suggesting the method is more data-efficient than its competitors.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

*   **Quality of Evaluation:** The primary weakness of this paper lies in the experimental validation. The reported improvements may not be due to the intrinsic superiority of the representation learning method itself, but rather an artifact of the choice of classifier. The authors use a linear SVM for all experiments. Linear SVMs are known to be sensitive to the scaling of individual features. The proposed method (TSL), as well as the baselines (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA), all produce different low-dimensional representations. It is entirely plausible that the feature space produced by the TSL method (matrix completion followed by SVD) has scaling properties that are simply more favorable for a linear SVM than the feature spaces produced by OPCA or KCCA. Therefore, the observed performance gain might be attributed to this scaling artifact rather than a genuinely better cross-lingual representation. This is a critical confounding variable that undermines the main claims of the paper.
*   **Clarity:** The paper could be improved in terms of clarity in a few areas. The description of the "unified document-term matrix" in Section 3 is slightly ambiguous. It is not explicitly stated how the unified vocabulary is constructed. The reader is left to assume it is a simple concatenation of the source and target language vocabularies, but this should be stated clearly. Additionally, the description of the experimental setup is fragmented across the main text of Section 5.2 and the caption for Table 1, making it difficult to quickly grasp the full details of the training and testing protocol.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

To address the major concern about the quality of the evaluation, I strongly recommend that the authors re-evaluate their method using a classifier that is insensitive to monotonic per-feature rescaling. A good choice would be a tree-based ensemble method like boosted decision trees (e.g., AdaBoost with C4.5, available in Weka) or Random Forests. If the proposed TSL method still outperforms the baselines when using such a classifier, it would provide much stronger and more convincing evidence that the representation itself is superior, effectively ruling out the scaling artifact hypothesis. This experiment is crucial for validating the paper's central claims.

For clarity, please explicitly define the construction of the unified document-term matrix. Is it a simple concatenation of language-specific surface forms? Are any cognates or shared vocabulary items handled in a special way? Furthermore, I suggest reorganizing the description of the experimental setup into a more self-contained subsection that details the dataset splits, the number of labeled source/target documents used for training, the number of test documents, and the specific parameter selection strategy, rather than scattering this information.

In summary, the paper introduces a neat and original idea for cross-lingual representation learning. However, the current experimental evidence is not fully convincing due to the potential confounding factor of classifier sensitivity to feature scaling. If the authors can provide results with a scale-invariant classifier that support their claims, this would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a solid contribution to the field.
