This paper proposes a two-step representation learning method for cross-language text classification by exploiting a set of parallel bilingual documents. The approach is simple, effective, and generally clearly presented. The authors provide a convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm used to solve the matrix completion problem, and the experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms several other cross-language representation learning methods, especially when the number of parallel bilingual documents is small.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a novel two-step method for cross-language representation learning. The approach first constructs a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix and then learns a low-rank document-term matrix by automatically filling the missing entries via matrix completion. The authors use a projected gradient descent algorithm to solve the matrix completion problem, which is a scalable and efficient optimization algorithm. After obtaining the low-rank document-term matrix, the authors apply latent semantic indexing to produce a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation of the documents.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the proposed method. The experimental setup is also well-designed, and the results demonstrate the efficacy and robustness of the proposed method. However, there are a few technical points and experiment details that need clarification.

One of the main strengths of the paper is its ability to handle a small number of parallel bilingual documents. The authors demonstrate that the proposed method can achieve good performance even with only 200 unlabeled parallel documents. This is a significant advantage over other methods, which often require a large number of parallel documents to achieve good performance.

However, there are a few limitations to the paper. One of the main limitations is the lack of a clear explanation for why the authors reduce the feature dimension with LSI after estimating the low-rank document-term matrix. The authors mention that this is for computational convenience, but it would be helpful to provide more details on this.

Another limitation is the lack of a clear distinction between the two ways external data are used: (1) labeled source-language data to help labeled target data, and (2) unlabeled parallel data. The authors should explicitly distinguish and separately report the two ways external data are used.

The experimental setup is also unclear in some places. For example, the authors should confirm the training setup, including the number of labeled source documents, labeled target documents, and unlabeled parallel documents. Additionally, the authors should clarify whether Section 5.3 varies the number of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2000.

There are also a few inconsistencies in the paper. For example, the authors mention that CL-KCCA outperforms TSL in Figure 1 for EFM and EGM at 2k unlabeled, but this is not reflected in Table 1. The authors should clarify this inconsistency.

The paper raises several theoretical questions. For example, why would a fully observed document-term matrix be low-rank, given sparsity and that documents are not obvious linear combinations of other documents? The authors should provide more intuition on this.

The paper also raises several questions about the comparison with other methods. For example, why does TSL often outperform other methods, especially CCA? The authors should provide more insight into this.

Finally, the authors should provide a running-time comparison between the projected gradient descent algorithm and other methods, such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed method is effective and efficient. However, there are a few technical points and experiment details that need clarification. With some revisions to address these limitations, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of cross-language text classification.
