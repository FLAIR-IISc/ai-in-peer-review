Summary
This paper proposes a two-step approach for cross-language representation learning. Step 1 formulates completion of a unified bilingual document–term matrix as a convex optimization with trace norm (to promote low rank), L1 (to promote sparsity), and nonnegativity constraints, with squared loss on observed entries and a small penalty on unobserved entries. A projected gradient–shrinkage algorithm with convergence guarantees is used to solve it. Step 2 applies LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation for standard monolingual classifiers. Experiments on cross-lingual sentiment classification with multilingual Amazon reviews compare against TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, showing consistent gains, especially with few parallel documents.

Relation to prior work
The paper is clearly positioned relative to CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, SCL, and multilingual topic models: like those methods, it uses parallel/comparable data to learn a shared space; unlike them, it first imputes missing cross-language features via matrix completion before dimensionality reduction. The optimization connects to established matrix completion literature (e.g., Candès & Recht; Ma et al.). This appears to be a novel combination in the cross-lingual text setting.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling choice—convex relaxation with trace norm + L1 and nonnegativity—is reasonable, and the algorithm is a proximal-style gradient method with shrinkage and projection. The convergence argument mirrors prior fixed-point analyses for nuclear norm minimization. One detail that would benefit from clarification is optimality under the composition of shrinkage and projection: proximal of a sum is not generally the composition of proximals; the current proof uses non-expansiveness, but please make explicit the conditions under which fixed points of PC(Sν(h(·))) correspond to minimizers of (2), or cite a precise result covering the trace-norm-plus-indicator case.
- Empirical validation: The empirical gains are encouraging and the analysis of performance vs. the number of parallel documents is valuable. However, there is a potential confound: much of the improvement might come from the LSI post-processing (including implicit rescaling/whitening) rather than from the matrix completion step per se. The current baselines (CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA) are not perfectly matched, and the study lacks ablations to isolate where the gains originate.
- Missing ablations/controls:
  - LSI on the same unified matrix without matrix completion (i.e., treat missing entries as zeros or use simple imputation) to quantify the marginal contribution of completion.
  - Classification directly on the completed M* (no LSI) to test whether completion alone adds predictive value.
  - Consistent feature normalization across methods (e.g., row L2-normalization, optional whitening) to reduce scaling artifacts.
  - Use classifiers less sensitive to per-feature rescaling (e.g., boosted decision trees or random forests in Weka, k-NN with cosine), to ensure the superiority is not an artifact of LSI scaling favoring linear SVMs.
  - Statistical significance tests across the 10 runs.
- Efficiency: Please report runtime, memory, and number of SVDs per run, as scalability is important for large vocabularies.

Clarity
- The “unified document–term matrix” needs a precise definition. Are columns language-specific surface forms from each language concatenated (so the same concept in different languages is represented by different columns), or are there shared/merged columns for translated terms? The text implies the former (language-specific vocabularies concatenated), but the phrasing could be misread.
- The experimental setup is hard to follow because key details are dispersed (e.g., dataset splits, parameter selection for each method, dimensionalities, and selection protocol). Please consolidate into one subsection: preprocessing; construction of Ω/Ω̂; how parallel/non-parallel rows are formed; training/validation/test splits; hyperparameter grids; model selection protocol; and whether the same splits are used across methods.
- Minor: define all symbols at first use; ensure consistent notation for indicators Y, Ŷ.

Originality
- The idea of leveraging matrix completion to impute cross-lingual features before learning a shared low-rank space is, to my knowledge, new in this domain. The formulation and algorithmic instantiation are straightforward but nontrivial.

Significance
- If the gains remain after the suggested controls, the contribution is significant: it shows a well-principled, low-data approach that leverages a small number of parallel documents to bridge languages, with a convex formulation and convergence guarantees. This would be attractive to practitioners dealing with scarce parallel data. Without the ablations, it is difficult to attribute the gains specifically to the completion step, which weakens the impact.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation linking matrix completion with cross-lingual representation learning.
- Convergence-guaranteed algorithm with an interpretable update.
- Strong empirical performance across 18 tasks and robustness with few parallel documents.
- Novel angle relative to CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA.

Cons
- Potential scaling/LSI confound; missing ablations to attribute gains to matrix completion.
- Clarity issues regarding the unified matrix and the experimental protocol.
- Limited discussion of computational cost and scalability.
- Convergence argument could better justify optimality under projection+shrinkage composition.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: (i) LSI without completion on the identical matrix; (ii) completion without LSI; (iii) normalized features across all methods.
- Evaluate at least one classifier less sensitive to feature rescaling (e.g., gradient-boosted trees, random forests) to rule out SVM scaling artifacts.
- Report time/memory and sensitivity to γ, ρ, µ, k.
- Clarify the exact construction of the unified vocabulary and Ω/Ω̂ and consolidate the experimental description.
- Consider releasing code to facilitate reproducibility.

Recommendation
Borderline reject without additional analyses; the idea is neat and potentially impactful, but stronger controls are needed to convincingly attribute the improvements to the proposed matrix completion step rather than to the LSI/feature scaling. With the suggested experiments and clarifications, I would lean toward acceptance.
