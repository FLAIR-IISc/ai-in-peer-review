## Review of “A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning”

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification leveraging unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The core idea is to construct a concatenated document-term matrix, complete missing entries via matrix completion with a projected gradient descent algorithm (with convergence guarantees provided), and then induce a low-dimensional cross-lingual representation using Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI). The method is evaluated on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, demonstrating superior performance compared to several baselines, particularly with limited parallel data.

The problem of cross-language text classification is well-motivated and significant, and the proposed approach builds upon prior work in representation learning for this task. Previous NIPS papers have tackled this challenge through machine translation [18, 6], bilingual lexicons [23], and exploiting resources like bilingual word pairs [16] and comparable corpora [10, 20]. The authors correctly situate their work within this landscape, referencing relevant papers and highlighting the novelty of their two-step approach.  The use of matrix completion to bridge the feature space gap is a reasonable strategy.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The matrix completion formulation and the provided convergence analysis for the projected gradient descent algorithm suggest a technically sound approach. The use of the trace norm and sparsity regularization are appropriate for this problem.
* **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms several baselines, including CL-LSI, CL-OPCA and CL-KCCA, particularly in low-resource settings.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The two-step approach is clearly articulated.
* **Convergence Guarantee:** Providing a convergence guarantee for the optimization algorithm is a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Motivation for LSI:** The motivation for applying LSI *after* the matrix completion step is not fully clear. Why is dimensionality reduction necessary when the completed matrix is already relatively dense due to the sparsity enforcement? Why LSI specifically, rather than alternatives like PCA which might better preserve variance?
* **Data Usage Distinction:** The paper doesn’t clearly distinguish the roles of labeled source language data (for training) and unlabeled parallel data (for representation learning). Reporting results separately for the impact of each would be insightful.
* **Experimental Details & Inconsistency:** The experimental setup requires clarification. Specifically, confirming that training utilizes 4k labeled source documents, 100 labeled target documents, 2k unlabeled parallel documents, and testing is performed on the remaining 1.9k target documents would be helpful. Furthermore, Section 5.3 states the number of unlabeled parallel documents is varied from 200 to 2k, but it would be beneficial to explicitly state this applies to *all* experiments. A slight inconsistency exists: Figure 1 shows CL-KCCA outperforming TSL for EFM and EGM at 2k unlabeled parallel documents, while Table 1 reports TSL's superior performance. This discrepancy should be investigated.
* **Low-Rank Assumption:** The claim that a fully observed document-term matrix is "naturally low-rank and sparse" is questionable. While sparsity is reasonable, the low-rank assumption requires further justification, considering documents aren't necessarily simple linear combinations of each other.
* **Intuition & Comparison to CCA:** The paper would benefit from a deeper intuition regarding why TSL often outperforms other methods like CL-KCCA. CCA feels naturally suited for cross-language representation learning, while TSL's primary driver appears to be enforcing low-rank structure. A discussion of how this differs conceptually would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost:** A comparison of running times between TSL (including the projected gradient descent) and the other methods (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) is missing. Understanding the computational cost is important, especially for larger datasets.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to cross-language representation learning.
* The empirical results demonstrate a significant improvement over existing methods, particularly in low-resource scenarios.
* The convergence guarantee provides a solid theoretical foundation.
* The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Several technical points and experimental details require clarification.
* The motivation for specific design choices (e.g., LSI) is not fully explained.
* A discussion of the computational complexity is missing.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising approach to cross-language representation learning. The method is simple yet effective, and the experimental results are encouraging. However, addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly clarifying the experimental details, justifying the use of LSI, and providing a computational analysis – would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors addressing these concerns.
