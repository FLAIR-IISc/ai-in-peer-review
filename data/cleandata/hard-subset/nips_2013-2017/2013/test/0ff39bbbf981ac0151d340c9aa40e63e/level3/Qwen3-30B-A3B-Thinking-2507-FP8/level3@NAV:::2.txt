

This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that combines matrix completion with latent semantic analysis to bridge feature spaces across languages. The authors formulate a matrix completion problem to fill missing entries in a concatenated bilingual document-term matrix constructed from unlabeled parallel documents, followed by LSI to derive low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The approach differs significantly from prior work such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, which apply dimensionality reduction directly to the incomplete matrix rather than first recovering missing values.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-justified projected gradient descent algorithm for matrix completion, accompanied by a rigorous convergence proof. The experimental evaluation on Amazon product reviews across multiple language pairs (English-French, English-German, English-Japanese) thoroughly compares against multiple baselines (TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA), showing consistent improvements of 8.59-14.61% in classification accuracy. Notably, the method achieves robust performance even with minimal parallel data (as few as 200 documents), which addresses a critical challenge in cross-lingual learning.

The paper excels in clarity and reproducibility, with complete parameter descriptions, clear methodology, and well-structured experiments. The motivation for matrix completion in the introduction could be strengthened to better position this novel application against existing approaches, but this is a minor issue.

The work demonstrates significant originality through its innovative use of matrix completion as a preprocessing step for cross-lingual representation learning—a creative application of established techniques to a persistent problem in multilingual NLP. The significance is substantial, as the approach provides a practical solution for cross-lingual sentiment classification with limited parallel data, potentially extending to other cross-lingual tasks.

While the paper is technically sound and well-executed, the main limitation is that it focuses solely on sentiment analysis. Future work could explore the method's applicability to more diverse NLP tasks. Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances cross-lingual learning with a novel, effective approach, demonstrating strong empirical results and theoretical grounding. The paper is well-suited for NIPS and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of representation learning.
