This paper proposes a two-step cross-lingual representation learning pipeline: (1) formulate completion of a unified bilingual document-term matrix via a convex trace-norm + ℓ1, nonnegativity-regularized objective solved with projected gradient descent, producing multilingual row vectors for all documents; (2) apply cross-lingual LSI (SVD) on the completed matrix to obtain a low-dimensional shared space and train a monolingual classifier. The work is positioned against prior cross-lingual representation methods such as CL-LSI (Littman et al.), CL-CCA/CL-KCCA (Vinokourov et al., NIPS), and OPCA (Platt et al.), as well as SCL (Prettenhofer & Stein). The optimization builds on established matrix completion theory (Candès & Recht; Ma et al.) with a convergence argument for the proximal PGD scheme.

Quality: The formulation is technically sound and the optimization is appropriate and scalable. Empirically, the method outperforms CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA on Amazon sentiment under limited parallel supervision. However, the main empirical claim risks a confound: linear SVMs are sensitive to per-feature rescaling, and LSI’s orthogonal components with singular-value scaling can favor SVMs over OPCA/CCA. The observed gains may therefore reflect LSI scaling choices rather than the matrix-completion step. To establish robustness, the authors should (i) evaluate with classifiers insensitive to per-feature rescaling (e.g., boosted decision trees in Weka), (ii) include ablations: MC-only (no LSI) and LSI-only (on the original concatenated matrix), (iii) standardize or ℓ2-normalize features post-projection across all methods, and (iv) report sensitivity to k and scaling (with/without singular values).

Clarity: Two issues hinder reproducibility. (1) “Unified document-term matrix” is ambiguous: are columns language-specific surface forms concatenated per language, or language-agnostic terms (e.g., via a dictionary)? How exactly is a “pair of parallel documents” collapsed into a single row—concatenation, sum, or other fusion? Please formalize with indexing and an example. (2) The experimental setup is fragmented (details scattered across Table 1 and text). Consolidate data splits, preprocessing (TF-IDF, normalization), hyperparameter selection, and classifier settings in one subsection.

Originality: Combining matrix completion with CL-LSI for cross-lingual representation appears novel, and the non-linear inference via completion is a fresh angle relative to OPCA/CCA/LSI-only baselines.

Significance: If the improvements persist under scale-invariant evaluation, the approach would be impactful: matrix completion is a well-controlled non-linear mechanism to propagate cross-lingual co-occurrence using few parallel pairs.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel, simple pipeline with solid optimization and convergence.
- Promising accuracy in low-parallel settings; broad applicability.

Cons against acceptance:
- Potential scaling artifact with linear SVM; missing scale-robust evaluations.
- Ambiguities in data construction and experimental reporting.

Overall: Neat idea; I recommend additional classifier evaluations (e.g., boosted trees in Weka) and clearer exposition before acceptance.
