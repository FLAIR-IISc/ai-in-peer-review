This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language text classification, leveraging matrix completion to bridge feature spaces followed by latent semantic indexing (LSI) for dimensionality reduction. The core idea of using matrix completion to handle sparse cross-lingual data is novel and potentially valuable. The method is evaluated on Amazon product review data, demonstrating improvements over several baselines. However, I have concerns regarding the robustness of these results and potential artifacts introduced by the use of LSI with a linear SVM classifier.

The approach builds on existing work in cross-lingual learning, which broadly falls into translation-based methods (e.g., [18, 6, 23]) and representation learning methods utilizing bilingual resources ([16, 10, 20, 15]). The proposed method fits within the latter category, specifically drawing inspiration from CL-LSI [10] but differs by first applying matrix completion to ‘fill in’ the cross-lingual document-term matrix. The authors correctly position their work within this landscape, referencing relevant prior art.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning appears to be a new approach. Matrix completion is a well-controlled non-linear inference technique, and its application to this problem is a promising direction.
* **Significance (Potential):** If the reported performance gains prove robust, the method could be significant, particularly in scenarios with limited parallel data, as highlighted by the experimental results.
* **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of cross-lingual text classification due to disjoint feature spaces.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a convergence analysis for their projected gradient descent algorithm, lending credibility to their implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Quality/Robustness Concern:** The most significant concern is the potential lack of robustness in the reported results. The paper notes that LSI scaling could be responsible for the improvements, rather than the matrix completion step itself.  Specifically, LSI, when paired with a linear SVM, can outperform methods like OPCA/CCA due to its inherent feature rescaling. This confound needs to be rigorously addressed.
* **Classifier Dependence:** The exclusive use of a linear SVM as the classifier is a limitation.  Linear SVMs are sensitive to feature scaling.  A classifier insensitive to per-feature rescaling, such as a boosted decision tree implementation (e.g., those available in Weka), is crucial to disentangle the effects of matrix completion/LSI from the impact of LSI’s scaling.
* **Clarity – Unified Document-Term Matrix:** The phrase "unified document-term matrix" is ambiguous. It is unclear whether the terms represent language-agnostic concepts or the surface forms specific to each language. This distinction is important for understanding the underlying assumptions of the method.
* **Clarity – Experimental Setup:** The description of the experimental setup is fragmented. Key details, such as parameter settings and data splits, are dispersed throughout the paper, particularly within Table 1. A more consolidated and detailed description of the experimental procedure would greatly improve reproducibility and understanding.

**Further Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Ablation Study:** An ablation study, comparing the performance of LSI applied directly to the original sparse matrix versus LSI applied to the matrix completion output, would help isolate the contribution of the matrix completion step.
* **Parameter Sensitivity Analysis:**  A more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the method to the various hyperparameters (γ, ρ, k) would be beneficial.
* **Alternative Matrix Completion Methods:** Exploring other matrix completion techniques, beyond the one presented, could broaden the scope of the work.
* **Scalability Discussion:** While the authors mention scalability, a more concrete discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm, and its limitations for very large datasets, would be valuable.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The idea is novel and potentially impactful.
* The theoretical analysis provides some assurance of the algorithm's correctness.
* The experimental results are promising, demonstrating improvements over strong baselines.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The robustness of the results is questionable, due to the potential for LSI scaling artifacts.
* The reliance on a single classifier (linear SVM) limits the generalizability of the findings.
* Clarity issues regarding the experimental setup and key terminology hinder full understanding.



**Overall:**

This is a neat idea with the potential to contribute to the field of cross-lingual learning. However, the authors need to address the concerns regarding the robustness of their results and the potential confounding effects of LSI scaling. I recommend rejection in its current form, but strongly encourage the authors to revise and resubmit after addressing these issues, particularly by evaluating with classifiers less sensitive to feature scaling and conducting a more thorough analysis of the contributions of matrix completion versus LSI.
