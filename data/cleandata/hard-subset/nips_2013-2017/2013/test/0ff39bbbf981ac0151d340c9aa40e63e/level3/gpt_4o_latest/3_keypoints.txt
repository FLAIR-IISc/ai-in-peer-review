- Proposes a two-step translingual representation method: matrix completion to fill a unified document-term matrix, then CL-LSI for low-dimensional cross-lingual representations.  
- Leverages a small set of parallel bilingual documents to handle disjoint feature spaces in cross-language text classification.  
- Evaluated on Amazon cross-language sentiment classification and shows superior performance to existing methods, especially with limited parallel data.  
- Innovative methodology combining matrix completion and CL-LSI to infer missing entries in a sparse bilingual document-term matrix.  
- Matrix completion effectively captures low-rank and sparse structures in the unified matrix.  
- Empirical results consistently outperform baselines like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA across multiple tasks.  
- Method is robust with small amounts of parallel data, reducing dependence on large bilingual corpora.  
- Proposed optimization algorithm includes convergence guarantees, adding theoretical rigor.  
- Concern that performance gains may be due to LSI scaling properties that favor linear SVMs rather than the method itself.  
- Needs validation with classifiers less sensitive to feature rescaling (e.g., boosted decision trees) to confirm generalizability.  
- Presentation is dense; the unified document-term matrix construction and experimental setup need clearer explanations and visual aids.  
- Related work discussion should more explicitly differentiate the approach from prior methods like CL-LSI and CL-KCCA.  
- Recommendation: validate with additional classifiers, clarify matrix construction/experiments, and provide more explicit comparisons to related work.  
- Pro: Novel combination of techniques, strong empirical performance, and reduced need for extensive parallel data.  
- Con: Potential reliance on LSI effects, clarity issues, and limited evaluation with non-linear classifiers.  
- Overall: Promising and valuable contribution that requires further validation and clearer exposition to fully realize its impact.
