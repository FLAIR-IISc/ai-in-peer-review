This paper proposes a novel two-step method for learning cross-lingual document representations from parallel corpora. The core idea is to first construct a unified document-term matrix for both source and target languages, where non-parallel documents have missing entries for the vocabulary of the other language. The authors frame the problem of filling these entries as a matrix completion task, which they solve using a projected gradient descent algorithm to recover a low-rank matrix. In the second step, standard Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA) is applied to this completed matrix to derive low-dimensional, dense cross-lingual representations. This work builds upon the lineage of cross-lingual LSA (CL-LSA) and other projection-based methods but distinguishes itself by explicitly treating the lack of cross-lingual information as a missing data problem to be solved via matrix completion, rather than an implicit feature of the data.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The claims are strongly supported by extensive experiments on a standard cross-lingual sentiment classification dataset. The evaluation is thorough, comparing against a monolingual baseline and several strong cross-lingual methods, including state-of-the-art techniques like CL-OPCA and CL-KCCA. The learning curve analysis, which demonstrates the method's robustness even with small amounts of parallel data, is a particularly compelling piece of evidence for its efficacy.

*   **Originality & Significance:** The primary contribution lies in the novel application of matrix completion as a preprocessing step for cross-lingual representation learning. While the constituent techniques are not new, their combination is elegant and well-motivated. The significance of this work is clear: it demonstrates that explicitly imputing missing cross-lingual term information can yield substantial performance gains over methods that directly perform dimensionality reduction on the sparse, concatenated matrix. The results, which show consistent outperformance against strong baselines, advance the state-of-the-art on this task and suggest a promising direction for other cross-modal learning problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. A minor suggestion would be to strengthen the motivation for matrix completion in the Introduction. While the method is clear, the introduction could more explicitly frame the problem of missing term information in non-parallel documents as a natural fit for matrix completion, which would better set the stage for the proposed solution.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and effective method for cross-lingual representation learning. The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. I recommend acceptance.
