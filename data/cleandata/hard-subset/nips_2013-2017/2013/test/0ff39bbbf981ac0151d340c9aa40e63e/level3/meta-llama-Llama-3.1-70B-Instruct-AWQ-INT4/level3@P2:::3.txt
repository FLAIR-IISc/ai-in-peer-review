This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-language representation learning, which addresses the challenge of cross-language text classification by bridging the feature spaces of different languages using a set of parallel bilingual documents. The method first formulates a matrix completion problem to produce a complete parallel document-term matrix for all documents in two languages, and then induces a low-dimensional cross-lingual document representation by applying latent semantic indexing on the obtained matrix.

The paper relates to previous work in the literature that addresses cross-language text classification by transforming training or test data from one language domain into the other language domain using machine translation tools or bilingual lexicons, or by constructing cross-lingual representations using readily available auxiliary resources such as bilingual word pairs, comparable corpora, and other multilingual resources. However, the proposed approach is distinct in that it only requires a small set of unlabeled parallel documents, whereas other approaches require at least translating all the training documents in one language domain.

The technical soundness of the paper is good, and the authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their method. The use of matrix completion to create multilingual row vectors, followed by the application of latent semantic indexing, is a well-motivated and technically sound approach. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the optimization algorithm used to solve the matrix completion problem, including a convergence proof.

However, there are some concerns regarding the quality of the results. The improvements achieved by the proposed method over other comparison methods may be due to the scaling properties of latent semantic indexing rather than the proposed method itself. To address this concern, the authors should evaluate their method using a classifier that is insensitive to per-feature rescaling, such as boosted decision trees.

The significance of the paper is high, as it proposes a novel approach to cross-language representation learning that is based on a well-controlled non-linear inference technique. The idea of using matrix completion to create multilingual row vectors is interesting and has the potential to be applied to other tasks beyond cross-language text classification.

The originality of the paper is also high, as the authors propose a new approach that is distinct from previous work in the literature. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise presentation of their method and results.

However, there are some clarity issues that need to be addressed. The phrasing regarding a "unified document-term matrix" is ambiguous, and it is unclear whether the terms are language-agnostic or language-specific surface forms. Additionally, the experimental setup description is fragmented and spread across Table 1, making it hard to follow.

Overall, the paper proposes a neat idea, but the authors should try classifiers beyond linear SVM to rule out scaling artifacts. With some revisions to address the clarity issues and concerns regarding the quality of the results, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of cross-language representation learning.
