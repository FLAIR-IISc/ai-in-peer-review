This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-language representation learning, which bridges the feature spaces of different languages by exploiting a set of parallel bilingual documents. The method first formulates a matrix completion problem to produce a complete parallel document-term matrix for all documents in two languages, and then induces a low-dimensional cross-lingual document representation by applying latent semantic indexing on the obtained matrix.

The proposed method is evaluated by conducting a set of experiments with cross-language sentiment classification tasks on multilingual Amazon product reviews. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed learning method outperforms a number of other cross-language representation learning methods, especially when the number of parallel bilingual documents is small.

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed method and its components. The optimization algorithm used to solve the matrix completion problem is well-explained, and the convergence analysis is thorough. The experimental results are also well-presented, and the authors provide a clear comparison of their method with other state-of-the-art methods.

However, there are some concerns regarding the quality of the results. The authors use a linear SVM classifier, which may not be the most suitable choice for this task. The results may not be robust, and the improvements might be due to the LSI scaling rather than the proposed method. Additionally, the positive results could be an artifact of LSI scaling outperforming OPCA/CCA for a linear SVM.

To address these concerns, the authors should evaluate their method with a classifier that is insensitive to per-feature rescaling, such as boosted decision trees available in Weka. This would help to determine whether the proposed method is truly effective or if the results are due to the choice of classifier.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to advance the state of the art in cross-language text classification. The proposed method is interesting because matrix completion is a well-controlled non-linear inference technique. If the results are robust, the approach could be useful for practitioners and researchers in the field.

The novelty of the paper is also a strong point. The idea of using matrix completion to bridge the feature spaces of different languages is new and innovative. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their method differs from previous contributions, and the related work section is thorough.

However, there are some clarity issues in the paper. The phrasing about a "unified document-term matrix" is ambiguous, and it is unclear whether terms are language-agnostic or language-specific surface forms. Additionally, the experimental setup description is fragmented and spread across Table 1, making it hard to follow.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their method and its components. However, there are some concerns regarding the quality of the results, and the authors should address these concerns by evaluating their method with a different classifier. With some revisions to address these concerns, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of cross-language text classification.
