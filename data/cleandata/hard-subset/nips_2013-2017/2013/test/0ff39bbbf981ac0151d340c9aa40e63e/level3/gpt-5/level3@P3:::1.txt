Summary
The paper proposes a simple two-step approach for cross-language text classification. Step 1 constructs a single bilingual document–term matrix across source and target languages, with rows corresponding to documents and columns to the unified vocabulary; entries missing for non-parallel documents are inferred via a convex matrix completion objective that combines nuclear norm (low-rank), ℓ1 (sparsity), nonnegativity, and squared losses on both observed entries and (weighted) missing entries. The authors solve this via a projected gradient method with singular-value shrinkage and prove convergence under a standard stepsize condition. Step 2 applies LSI (truncated SVD) to the completed matrix M*, yielding low-dimensional cross-lingual document vectors for standard monolingual classification. On Amazon review sentiment datasets (EN–FR/DE/JA), the method (TSL) generally outperforms CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, especially with few parallel documents.

Relation to prior work: The method is close in spirit to representation-learning approaches that leverage parallel or comparable corpora (e.g., CL-LSI [Littman et al.], CL-KCCA [Vinokourov et al.], CL-OPCA [Platt et al.]) and to SCL [Prettenhofer & Stein], but differs by first imputing a completed bilingual document–term matrix via convex matrix completion (Candès & Recht; Ma et al.) before dimensionality reduction.

Strengths
- Conceptually simple, modular pipeline (completion + LSI) that is easy to reproduce and plug into existing classifiers.
- Solid optimization: convex surrogate, clear algorithm (projected gradient with shrinkage), and a clean convergence guarantee to an optimal solution.
- Empirical performance is consistently strong, particularly when the number of parallel documents is small; this addresses an important practical regime.
- Well-situated in the literature; comparisons to strong baselines.

Weaknesses and questions
- Modeling assumption: The paper asserts a “fully observed document–term matrix is naturally low-rank and sparse.” Sparsity is clear, but low-rank is not obvious: bag-of-words matrices are often high-rank because documents are not linear combinations of a few others. Please justify when low-rank should hold (e.g., topic models implying approximate low-rank structure, shared bilingual topics, or correlations induced by sentiment polarity), and discuss how violations affect completion.
- Why LSI after completion? After filling missing entries, M* may be relatively dense (despite ℓ1 and nonnegativity), so dimensionality reduction likely aids both computation and de-noising. Please clarify whether the primary motivation is computational efficiency, statistical denoising, or both. Also, why LSI rather than PCA (on the document covariance), NMF (consistent with nonnegativity), or OPCA? Since LSI is effectively truncated SVD on tf-idf, a brief rationale and, if possible, a small comparison would help.
- Data usage clarity: The paper mixes two external signals: (1) labeled source-language data assisting labeled target data, and (2) unlabeled parallel data. Please distinguish and report their contributions explicitly (e.g., ablations: TSL w/wo parallel data; TSL with target-only labels vs adding source labels in the final classifier).
- Experimental setup transparency: Please confirm the training protocol: for each task, you use 4000 labeled source docs, 100 labeled target docs, 2000 unlabeled parallel docs for representation learning, and test on the remaining 1900 target docs. In Section 5.3, do you vary the number of unlabeled parallel docs from 200 to 2000 while keeping all else fixed?
- Result inconsistency: In Figure 1, CL-KCCA appears to outperform TSL for EFM and EGM at 2000 parallel documents, while Table 1 suggests TSL ≥ CL-KCCA on average. Please reconcile (different random splits? hyperparameters? averaging across categories?).
- Runtime and scalability: Each projected gradient iteration requires an SVD (for shrinkage), which can dominate runtime for large t×d. Please provide wall-clock times and per-iteration costs versus CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA, along with k and SVD ranks used (full vs partial), and convergence criteria/iterations. A complexity discussion (in terms of t, d, rank k) would be useful.
- Sensitivity to ρ on missing entries: Penalizing missing entries toward zero is a modeling choice. Some intuition/sensitivity analysis for ρ would strengthen the story.
- Minor editorial issues:
  - Change (ij) to (i,j) before Eq. (2).
  - In Section 4.1, remove “such as” before Eqs. (4) and (5); simply define g(M) and Sν(·).
  - A few typos/wording (e.g., “documentterm” → “document-term”).

Quality
- The optimization component is technically sound with a correct convergence argument for the convex surrogate. The main open technical point is the validity and impact of the low-rank assumption on document–term matrices. Experiments are thorough across many language/category pairs, with standard deviations reported, though some setup details and a runtime comparison are missing.

Clarity
- Writing is generally clear and well-organized. Providing a schematic of the two-step pipeline, clarifying the data usage and train/test splits, and adding the requested rationale for LSI would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- The components (matrix completion, LSI) are known, but their combination for cross-lingual representation by imputing a bilingual bag-of-words matrix first appears novel and well-motivated. The convergence proof in this specific setting is a plus.

Significance
- Cross-lingual sentiment classification remains important. The demonstrated gains, especially in low-parallel-data regimes, and the method’s simplicity make it likely to see adoption or to inspire follow-on work.

Pros for acceptance
- Simple, effective method with strong results and convergence guarantees.
- Robust when parallel data is scarce.
- Clear relation to and improvement over standard baselines.

Cons for acceptance
- Questionable low-rank assumption needs better justification.
- Missing runtime and scalability analysis.
- Some inconsistencies and ambiguities in experimental reporting.

Suggestions for the camera-ready
- Justify low-rank assumption; add sensitivity to ρ; provide runtime vs CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA.
- Clarify the role of LSI vs alternatives and why dimensionality reduction is necessary after completion.
- Separate and report the contributions of source labels vs parallel data; confirm/train-test details; reconcile Figure 1 vs Table 1.
- Apply minor editorial fixes listed above.

Overall assessment
A well-executed, generally clear paper proposing a simple yet effective two-step approach with a solid optimization backbone and strong empirical results. With clarifications on modeling assumptions, experiment details, and runtime, this would make a solid NIPS contribution.
