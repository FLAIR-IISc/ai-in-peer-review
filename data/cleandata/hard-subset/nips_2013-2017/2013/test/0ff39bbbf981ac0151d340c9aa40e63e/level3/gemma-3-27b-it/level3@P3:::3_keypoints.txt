- Proposes a two-step cross-language classification: matrix completion to fill cross-lingual document-term matrix, then LSI for dimensionality reduction.
- Novel use of matrix completion to handle sparse cross-lingual data, potentially valuable.
- Evaluated on Amazon product reviews and reports improvements over several baselines.
- Core concern: reported gains may not be robust and could be artifacts of LSI’s feature rescaling.
- Method builds on representation-learning approaches and is related to CL-LSI but differs by pre-filling via matrix completion.
- Strength: combination of matrix completion and LSI appears novel and promising.
- Strength: could be significant in low-parallel-data scenarios if results hold.
- Strength: problem statement is clearly articulated.
- Strength: provides convergence analysis for the projected gradient descent implementation.
- Weakness: improvements may be confounded by LSI scaling rather than matrix completion’s contribution.
- Weakness: exclusive use of linear SVM (sensitive to feature scaling) limits conclusions; need classifiers insensitive to per-feature scaling.
- Weakness: ambiguity over “unified document-term matrix” (language-agnostic concepts vs surface forms).
- Weakness: experimental setup description is fragmented, with parameter settings and splits dispersed, harming reproducibility.
- Suggestion: perform an ablation study comparing LSI on the original sparse matrix vs on matrix-completed output to isolate effects.
- Suggestion: provide parameter sensitivity analysis for γ, ρ, and k.
- Suggestion: explore other matrix completion techniques to broaden the study.
- Suggestion: give a concrete scalability and computational-complexity discussion for large datasets.
- Argument for acceptance: novel idea, theoretical backing, and promising empirical improvements.
- Argument against acceptance: questionable robustness due to LSI artifacts, reliance on a single classifier, and clarity/reproducibility issues.
- Overall recommendation: interesting but recommend rejection in current form; encourage revision addressing robustness, classifier sensitivity, and clearer experiments.
