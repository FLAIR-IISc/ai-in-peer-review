

# Review: "A Novel Two-Step Method for Cross Language Representation Learning"

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning that first uses matrix completion to fill missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix and then applies CL-LSI on the completed matrix to create low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. The authors frame this as a solution to the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages in cross-language text classification. The approach builds on related work like CL-LSI [10] and CL-OPCA [15], but differs by first completing the matrix before applying dimensionality reduction rather than directly applying LSI or OPCA to the sparse matrix.

The paper makes a novel contribution by using matrix completion to bridge vocabulary gaps between languages, which appears to be a new approach in the cross-lingual representation learning literature. The authors provide theoretical convergence guarantees for their projected gradient descent algorithm, which is a technical strength. The experimental results on Amazon product reviews demonstrate that their method outperforms several baselines, particularly when the number of parallel bilingual documents is limited.

**Strengths:**
- The matrix completion approach offers a principled way to handle missing data in bilingual document-term matrices, which is a significant challenge in cross-lingual learning.
- The theoretical convergence analysis for the optimization algorithm provides solid technical grounding.
- The experimental results are comprehensive, with 18 cross-lingual classification tasks and ablation studies on varying amounts of parallel data.
- The novelty of the approach is clear, as the authors distinguish their method from existing techniques like CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA.

**Weaknesses:**
The primary concern is the robustness of the reported results. The authors use linear SVMs for classification, which are sensitive to feature scaling. Their method involves matrix completion followed by LSI, both of which produce feature vectors that are scaled by the LSI process. The observed performance gains could be artifacts of this scaling process rather than evidence of the method's superiority. The authors note that "LSI scaling" outperforms OPCA/CCA for linear SVMs, but this is not properly tested.

Specifically, the claim that their method outperforms CL-LSI (which also uses LSI) is questionable because both methods apply LSI to the completed or original matrix. The key difference is the matrix completion step, but the results could be driven by the LSI process itself rather than the completion step. The authors should evaluate with classifiers insensitive to feature scaling (e.g., boosted decision trees or random forests) to rule out this artifact.

The paper contains two clarity issues: First, the description of the "unified document-term matrix" is ambiguous. It's unclear whether terms are language-agnostic (e.g., using a common vocabulary) or language-specific (e.g., keeping separate word forms for each language). This is critical for understanding the matrix completion approach. Second, the experimental setup is fragmented across multiple sections and Table 1, making it difficult to follow the exact configuration of the experiments.

**Recommendations:**
1. Conduct experiments with classifiers that are insensitive to feature scaling (e.g., boosted decision trees in Weka) to determine if the performance gains are robust to the LSI scaling artifact.
2. Clarify the nature of the "unified vocabulary" in the document-term matrix - are terms language-agnostic or language-specific?
3. Consolidate the experimental setup description into a single, clear section rather than spreading it across Table 1 and the text.
4. Include ablation studies that isolate the contribution of the matrix completion step versus the LSI step.

**Significance and Originality:**
The approach is significant if robust, as matrix completion provides a well-controlled non-linear inference technique for cross-lingual representation learning. The novelty appears to be genuine, with no clear overlap with existing NIPS papers. However, the significance is contingent on the robustness of the results, which requires further validation.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper presents a neat idea with potential for cross-lingual learning, but the current evaluation does not adequately rule out a scaling artifact as the source of the performance gains. The quality of the work is compromised by this methodological concern, despite the technical soundness of the matrix completion approach. The clarity issues further reduce the paper's accessibility.

**Recommendation:** Weak Accept. The paper has genuine novelty and a solid theoretical foundation, but the results require validation with more robust classifiers to confirm the claimed performance gains. If the authors can provide these additional experiments, the paper would be significantly stronger. As is, the paper's contribution is questionable due to the potential scaling artifact, but the underlying idea is interesting enough to warrant a weak accept with the expectation that the authors address these concerns in a revision.
