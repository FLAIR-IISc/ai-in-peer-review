Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a simple and effective two-step approach for cross-lingual representation learning: (i) formulate the concatenated bilingual document–term matrix (with many missing entries for non-parallel documents) as a matrix completion problem, solved via a convex surrogate with nuclear norm (for low rank), L1 (for sparsity), nonnegativity constraints, and squared losses on observed and missing entries; (ii) run latent semantic indexing (LSI/SVD) on the recovered matrix to produce a shared low-dimensional space in which standard monolingual classifiers can be trained. The optimization is carried out by a projected gradient descent algorithm with singular value shrinkage and a nonnegativity projection, and the paper provides a convergence proof.

Relative to prior cross-lingual work such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA, and SCL-based approaches, the novelty is to insert a principled matrix completion step before the dimensionality reduction, explicitly leveraging the structure induced by parallel documents to impute cross-lingual co-occurrences. This differs from methods that directly apply LSI/KCCA/OPCA to the raw concatenated matrix (which treats all unobserved features as zeros). The approach is also related to multilingual topic models, but here the low-rank assumption is enforced via convex regularization with an explicit optimization procedure and convergence guarantees.

Empirical results on parallel Amazon reviews across English, French, German, and Japanese (18 source–target–domain tasks) show consistent improvements over bag-of-words in the target language and three strong cross-lingual baselines (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA). Learning curves indicate robust performance even when only a few hundred parallel documents are available.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated method: a two-step pipeline that is conceptually simple yet addresses a real drawback of treating missing entries as zeros in cross-lingual matrices.
- Solid optimization: projected gradient with shrinkage, nonnegativity projection, and a convergence analysis. Practical parameter constraints are spelled out.
- Strong and stable empirical performance: outperforms mono- and cross-lingual baselines on 17/18 tasks and ties on the remaining one; maintains accuracy with as few as 200 parallel pairs.
- Thorough experiments and fair setup: multiple languages, domains, and careful parameter selection; comparisons against representative and state-of-the-art dimensionality reduction baselines.
- Reproducibility: hyperparameters, selection procedures, and implementation details are reported.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Motivation in the Introduction: Although the method is sound, the paper could better motivate why matrix completion is the right pre-processing step for cross-lingual learning. A brief intuitive example (e.g., how imputation reconstructs cross-lingual co-occurrence structure that LSI alone cannot see when missing entries are treated as zeros) or a schematic would strengthen the pitch.
- Transductive setting and baselines: Representation learning uses all documents (including unlabeled target/test). This is common in semi-supervised transfer, but the baseline TBOW does not benefit from target-side unlabeled data. Consider adding (a) a target-only LSI baseline using the same unlabeled target data; (b) an inductive variant that learns the representation on training-only pools to quantify any transductive gains; and (c) a bilingual topic-model baseline (e.g., coupled pLSA or bilingual LDA), which is cited but not evaluated.
- Ablations: An ablation showing (i) LSI on the incomplete matrix (CL-LSI), (ii) matrix completion without the LSI step (training on M* directly), and (iii) the full two-step pipeline would clarify the contribution of each component. A sensitivity analysis for γ, ρ, and k would also help.
- Computational aspects: The method requires repeated SVDs for shrinkage and a final SVD for LSI. Please report wall-clock times, memory footprint, and whether partial/randomized SVD is used. Discussion of scalability to larger vocabularies and corpora would be useful.
- Loss on missing entries: The use of a quadratic penalty c(Mij, 0) with weight ρ is intuitive but may bias toward small values. Some justification for the chosen ρ range and its impact on recall of cross-lingual terms would help.
- Generalization to new documents: Clarify how to embed new, unseen documents without re-solving the matrix completion problem (e.g., a fixed projection using Vk and a simple mapping from bag-of-words to the latent space).

Quality
Technically sound and well-executed. The optimization is principled with a convergence guarantee; the empirical evidence is strong and covers multiple languages and domains. Additional ablations and runtime/sensitivity analyses would further strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is well-written, organized, and largely reproducible; algorithmic details and parameter ranges are provided. Minor clarity suggestion: strengthen the Introduction’s motivation for matrix completion and add a brief intuitive example.

Originality
Applying matrix completion (nuclear norm + sparsity + nonnegativity) as a pre-step to cross-lingual LSA is a novel and interesting combination relative to prior CL-LSI/KCCA/OPCA and SCL approaches.

Significance
The results convincingly show that matrix completion can be an effective pre-processing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification, especially with small amounts of parallel data. The idea is generic and likely transferable to other cross-lingual tasks (e.g., topic classification, retrieval) and possibly to multi-view learning beyond language.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong empirical gains over solid baselines; robustness with limited parallel data.
- Simple, general method with a convergence guarantee and clear practical impact.
- Thorough experimentation and clear reporting.

Arguments against acceptance
- Transductive advantage over some baselines and missing ablations may inflate perceived gains.
- Limited discussion of computational scalability and sensitivity.
- Missing comparison to multilingual topic models in experiments.

Questions for the authors
1) How does performance change if the representation is learned only from training pools (inductive setting)?
2) Can you provide wall-clock times and memory usage, and detail any use of partial/randomized SVD?
3) What is the effect of removing the LSI step and training directly on M*?
4) How are new documents embedded at test time without re-running matrix completion?

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a well-founded, novel application of matrix completion to cross-lingual representation learning with convincing empirical benefits and good clarity/reproducibility.

Reviewer confidence
High.
