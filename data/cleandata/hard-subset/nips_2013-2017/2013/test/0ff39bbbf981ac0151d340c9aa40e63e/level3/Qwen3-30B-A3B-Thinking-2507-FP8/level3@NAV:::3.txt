

This paper proposes a novel two-step method for cross-language representation learning using matrix completion followed by CL-LSI. The approach constructs a unified document-term matrix where parallel documents form fully observed rows while non-parallel documents have missing values for the other language's vocabulary. Matrix completion fills these missing entries, and LSI is applied to the completed matrix to generate cross-lingual representations. This differs from prior work like CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], and CL-OPCA [15], which apply dimensionality reduction directly to the incomplete matrix rather than first completing it.

The paper's strengths include a genuinely novel methodological contribution, sound theoretical development of the projected gradient descent algorithm with convergence guarantees, and promising empirical results showing significant improvements over baselines, especially with limited parallel data. The matrix completion step represents a well-controlled non-linear inference technique that could be valuable for cross-lingual learning.

However, a critical quality concern undermines the results' robustness. The authors use linear SVM as the classifier, which is highly sensitive to feature scaling. The positive results could simply reflect that LSI scaling outperforms OPCA/CCA for linear SVM, rather than the matrix completion step providing meaningful improvements. The authors should evaluate with classifiers insensitive to per-feature rescaling (e.g., boosted decision trees in Weka) to rule out this artifact. The paper also contains clarity issues: the description of the "unified document-term matrix" is ambiguous regarding whether terms are language-agnostic or language-specific, and the experimental setup is fragmented across Table 1, making it difficult to follow.

While the idea is interesting and the technical content is sound, the evaluation methodology fails to adequately validate the claimed advantages. The paper would benefit from additional experiments with more robust classifiers to establish whether the improvements are truly due to the proposed method. Given the current evaluation, the results lack sufficient robustness to support the significance claims. I recommend rejection pending these additional experiments.
