This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning, addressing the challenge of disparate feature spaces across languages in tasks like text classification. The core idea is to leverage parallel bilingual documents to complete a document-term matrix, effectively ‘filling in’ missing word features for documents not directly translated. This completed matrix is then subjected to Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to generate a low-dimensional, cross-lingual document representation suitable for classification. The authors demonstrate performance gains on cross-lingual sentiment classification tasks using Amazon product reviews, outperforming several baseline methods, especially when limited parallel data is available.

This work builds upon a substantial body of research in cross-lingual learning. The paper correctly identifies existing approaches that rely on machine translation ([18, 6, 23]), bilingual lexicons, and the construction of cross-lingual representations using resources like bilingual word pairs [16], comparable corpora [10, 20, 15], and multilingual resources [3, 14].  Related methods like CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20] and CL-OPCA [15] are also appropriately positioned.  The novelty lies in the specific combination of matrix completion as a pre-processing step to enhance the effectiveness of LSI, rather than applying LSI directly to the sparse concatenated matrix. This appears to be a unique approach within the existing literature.

The paper demonstrates several strengths. The proposed method appears conceptually sound, addressing the feature space discrepancy problem with a mathematically principled approach – matrix completion. The experimental results are promising, showing consistent improvement over established baselines, and the authors highlight the method’s effectiveness even with limited parallel data. The convergence analysis of the projected gradient descent algorithm is a valuable addition, demonstrating theoretical guarantees for the optimization process.

However, there are also some weaknesses that warrant consideration. While the results are encouraging, it is unclear whether the observed improvements are genuinely attributable to the matrix completion step or are simply a consequence of the scaling inherent in LSI. Applying LSI, even to a sparse matrix, inherently rescales features, which could disproportionately benefit linear classifiers like SVMs.  This scaling could artificially inflate performance relative to methods like CL-OPCA and CL-KCCA, which may rely on different feature scaling strategies. 

To address this concern, the authors should evaluate the proposed method with a classifier less sensitive to per-feature rescaling. The suggestion to utilize boosted decision trees (e.g., those available in Weka) is a strong one, as these methods are less affected by the magnitude of individual features. Demonstrating consistent performance gains across different classifier types would significantly strengthen the claim of a genuine improvement.

Clarity is also an area for improvement. The description of the “unified document-term matrix” is somewhat ambiguous. It is unclear whether the matrix employs language-agnostic concepts or directly utilizes language-specific surface forms. Clarifying this point would improve understanding. Furthermore, the experimental setup is fragmented; details are spread across the text and Table 1, making it difficult to get a concise overview of the experimental design. Consolidating this information into a single section would enhance readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The proposed method is novel and addresses an important problem in cross-lingual learning.
*   Experimental results show consistent improvement over several baselines.
*   The theoretical analysis of the optimization algorithm provides valuable insight.
*   The method appears robust, even with limited parallel data.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The reported gains may be an artifact of the chosen classifier (linear SVM) and the scaling properties of LSI.
*   Clarity issues related to the unified document-term matrix and experimental setup hinder full understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising paper with a novel approach to cross-lingual representation learning. While the results are encouraging, the potential confound related to classifier scaling necessitates further investigation. If the authors can demonstrate robust performance with a classifier less sensitive to feature rescaling, this paper would be a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on addressing the concerns regarding classifier choice and clarifying the experimental details.  The potential significance of applying a well-controlled non-linear inference technique like matrix completion to this problem is considerable.
