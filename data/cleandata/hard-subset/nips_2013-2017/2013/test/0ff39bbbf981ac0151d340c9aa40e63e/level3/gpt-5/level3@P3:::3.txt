Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a two-step approach to cross-lingual document representation for classification using a small number of parallel documents. Step 1 formulates a convex matrix completion problem over a bilingual “unified” document–term matrix that concatenates the vocabularies of two languages and treats all entries for the “other” language in non-parallel documents as missing. The objective combines a trace norm for low rank, an L1 term, nonnegativity, and squared-loss penalties for observed and missing entries (with a tunable ρ). A projected gradient descent algorithm with singular value shrinkage is used, with convergence guarantees. Step 2 applies cross-lingual LSI (via SVD) to the completed matrix to produce multilingual document vectors, on which a monolingual classifier (linear SVM) is trained. Experiments on Amazon cross-lingual sentiment (EN–FR/DE/JP) compare against TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, reporting consistent gains, especially with few parallel documents.

This sits at the intersection of cross-lingual representation learning (e.g., CL-LSI/Littman et al., CL-CCA/Vinokourov et al., OPCA/Platt et al., SCL/Prettenhofer & Stein) and matrix completion (Candès & Recht; Ma et al.). Unlike prior CL methods that operate directly on the original concatenated document–term matrix, this work first imputes missing entries via a controlled convex program, then runs LSI.

Strengths
- Novelty: Using convex matrix completion to infer cross-lingual co-occurrence structure prior to CL-LSI appears new. The nonnegativity and explicit missing-entry regularization are well-motivated for TF-IDF data.
- Methodological soundness: The optimization objective is convex; the algorithm has a clear proximal/shrinkage interpretation with a convergence proof referencing standard results.
- Empirical scope: Results cover 18 EN–FR/DE/JP sentiment transfer tasks and vary the amount of parallel data. Gains are largest in the low-parallel regime, an important practical setting.
- Potential significance: If robust, combining a well-controlled non-linear inference step (matrix completion) with LSI could be a general recipe for multilingual representation learning and beyond.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Possible scaling confounds with the classifier: All methods are evaluated with a linear SVM, which is sensitive to per-feature scaling. LSI has specific scaling/whitening effects (the paper uses Z = M* Vk, i.e., a projection onto orthonormal directions without multiplying by singular values), whereas OPCA/CCA produce projections with different scale properties. Positive results, especially vs. OPCA/CCA, may reflect LSI’s favorable scaling for SVM rather than the matrix-completion step per se.
- Attribution of gains: While TSL is compared to CL-LSI, which partially isolates the effect of completion, it remains unclear whether TSL’s improvements over CL-LSI are robust to scale-normalized evaluation (e.g., per-dimension standardization or norm-normalization) or to classifiers that are inherently insensitive to feature rescaling. Without such controls, the headline improvements could be partly artifacts of the LSI–SVM interaction.
- Clarity issue 1 (terminology): “Unified document–term matrix” is ambiguous. Do columns correspond to language-agnostic terms (shared across languages) or language-specific surface forms concatenated into one space? The narrative suggests the latter, but the wording “unified vocabulary” could be misread as a cross-lingual lexicon mapping. Please make explicit that columns are language-specific surface forms, with no lexical unification beyond parallel-document structure, and specify how TF-IDF is computed (IDF across both languages or per-language?).
- Clarity issue 2 (experimental description): The setup is overly fragmented (details spread across text and Table 1). Important choices—normalization of rows before SVD, whether singular values are used in the projection (Z = USk vs. M*Vk), per-document length normalization pre/post LSI, and SVM input normalization—are not clearly stated but materially affect results.
- Missing robustness checks: No experiments with classifiers insensitive to per-feature rescaling (e.g., boosted decision trees in Weka, Random Forests) or with explicit standardization of learned features. Hyperparameter sensitivity beyond k, γ, ρ is limited; the choice µ = 1e−6 is fixed without justification. Computational scalability (wall-clock, memory, SVD regime) is not reported for larger corpora.

Quality
- The optimization component is technically sound with appropriate references and a clear proximal algorithm. The empirical section is extensive, but the central claim—matrix completion improves cross-lingual representations—would be much more convincing with scale-robust evaluations and ablations:
  - LSI on the original matrix vs. LSI on the completed matrix with matched normalization.
  - TSL with ρ = 0 (i.e., no missing-entry regularization) and with/without nonnegativity.
  - Linear SVM with per-feature z-scoring and per-example L2-norm, as well as scale-insensitive classifiers (e.g., AdaBoostM1 with decision trees in Weka).
  - Sensitivity to k and to the number of power iterations/truncated SVD tolerance.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized, but the two clarity issues above hamper reproducibility. Please also detail:
  - Tokenization, stopwording, stemming per language.
  - Whether IDF is computed jointly across languages or separately.
  - Whether rows are length-normalized before SVD and whether features are centered.
  - Exact SVD variant (truncated, randomized) and stopping tolerances.

Originality
- The two-step idea (convex matrix completion followed by CL-LSI) appears novel relative to CL-LSI/OPCA/CCA and multilingual topic models. The connection to low-rank inference is well articulated.

Significance
- Potentially impactful if the gains persist under scale-robust evaluation and with non-SVM classifiers. The approach could seed further work on structured imputation for multilingual and multi-view learning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add evaluations with classifiers insensitive to per-feature scaling, e.g., boosted decision trees (Weka), Random Forests. Report whether TSL’s advantage persists.
- Normalize features across methods: try z-scoring each learned dimension and L2-normalizing document vectors before SVM. Report the impact.
- Provide ablations to isolate the contribution of matrix completion versus LSI and of each regularizer (trace norm, L1, nonnegativity, ρ on missing entries).
- Clarify the “unified document–term matrix” and all preprocessing/normalization choices; consolidate the experimental setup in one subsection rather than distributing details across Table 1 and the text.
- Report training time, number of SVDs per iteration, and memory footprint to support the scalability claim.

Pros and cons for acceptance
- Pros: Novel and principled idea; solid optimization; promising results, especially with few parallel documents; potential for broad use.
- Cons: Possible scaling artifacts with linear SVM; unclear attribution of gains; missing scale-robust evaluations; some clarity gaps in the setup.

Overall
A neat and potentially significant idea. However, to be confident the improvements reflect the proposed matrix-completion step rather than LSI/feature-scaling interactions with a linear SVM, the authors should evaluate with classifiers insensitive to per-feature rescaling (e.g., boosted decision trees in Weka) and include the suggested ablations. If these controls confirm the gains, this would make a strong NIPS contribution.
