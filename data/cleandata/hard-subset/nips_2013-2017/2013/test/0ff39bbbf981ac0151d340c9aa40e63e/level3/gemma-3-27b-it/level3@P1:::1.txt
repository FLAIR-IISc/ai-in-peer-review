This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-language representation learning, addressing the challenge of disjoint feature spaces in multilingual text classification. The approach constructs a concatenated document-term matrix, utilizes matrix completion with a projected gradient descent algorithm to fill missing values (leveraging parallel bilingual documents), and then applies Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) to induce a low-dimensional, cross-lingual document representation. The authors provide a convergence guarantee for their optimization algorithm and demonstrate strong empirical performance on cross-language sentiment classification tasks, particularly when parallel data is limited.

The work builds on a significant body of research in cross-lingual learning. Previous methods, such as those utilizing machine translation [18, 6, 23] or bilingual resources [16], often require substantial translation or rely on pre-existing dictionaries. Other approaches construct cross-lingual representations via methods like CL-LSI [10], CL-KCCA [20], and CL-OPCA [15].  This paper distinguishes itself by focusing on a matrix completion framework to bridge the language gap using *unlabeled* parallel data, a scenario relevant when labeled resources are scarce. The authors correctly position their work within this landscape, referencing key publications and highlighting how their method differs by minimizing the reliance on extensive translation or pre-existing bilingual resources.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed method appears theoretically sound, particularly the convergence guarantee for the projected gradient descent algorithm. The formulation of matrix completion as a regularized optimization problem is well-motivated.
* **Empirical Results:**  The experimental results are compelling, consistently demonstrating that the proposed method outperforms several baselines, especially when the amount of parallel bilingual data is small. This is a significant advantage in many real-world scenarios.
* **Clarity of Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The two-step approach is clearly articulated. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 is helpful.
* **Novelty:** The combination of matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning is a novel contribution.  The method’s ability to work effectively with limited parallel data is a notable strength.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Motivation for LSI:** While the paper justifies matrix completion, the choice of LSI for dimensionality reduction following completion is less clearly motivated. Why LSI specifically, and not alternatives like PCA? Is it primarily for computational convenience given the potentially increased density of the completed matrix?  A more detailed explanation of this design choice would be beneficial.
* **Data Usage Clarification:**  The paper would benefit from explicitly distinguishing and reporting results for the two distinct ways external data is used: (1) labeled data from the source language to assist with labeled data in the target language, and (2) unlabeled parallel data. Presenting separate results would provide a clearer understanding of each component’s contribution.
* **Experimental Setup Clarity:** The experimental setup, particularly regarding the training data, requires clarification.  Please confirm that the setup involves training with 4000 labeled source documents, 100 labeled target documents, 2000 unlabeled parallel documents, and then testing on the remaining 1900 target documents. Also, please confirm if Section 5.3 systematically varies the number of *unlabeled* parallel documents from 200 to 2000.
* **Inconsistency in Results:** There appears to be a discrepancy between Table 1 and Figure 1. Specifically, in Figure 1, CL-KCCA outperforms TSL for the EFM and EGM tasks at 2000 unlabeled parallel documents, while Table 1 shows TSL consistently outperforming CL-KCCA. This inconsistency should be addressed.
* **Low-Rank Assumption:** The justification that a fully observed document-term matrix is “naturally low-rank” requires further elaboration. Given the sparsity of document representations and the lack of direct linear relationships between documents, why should we expect such a strong low-rank property?
* **Intuition for Performance:**  While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks a deeper intuition for *why* TSL often outperforms other methods, particularly those based on Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA). CCA seems like a natural approach for cross-lingual tasks. Understanding how the low-rank enforcement in matrix completion drives the superior performance would be valuable.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper does not provide any discussion of the computational cost of the proposed method. A comparison of running times for projected gradient descent versus the other methods (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) would be useful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to cross-lingual representation learning.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate significant improvements over existing methods, particularly in low-resource scenarios.
* The authors provide a convergence guarantee for their optimization algorithm.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Several technical points and experimental details require clarification.
* The motivation for the choice of LSI is not fully explained.
* The paper lacks a discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a promising approach. The method is simple, effective, and generally clearly presented. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly clarifying the experimental setup, providing more intuition for the performance gains, and discussing computational complexity – would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.  I recommend acceptance contingent on addressing these points.
