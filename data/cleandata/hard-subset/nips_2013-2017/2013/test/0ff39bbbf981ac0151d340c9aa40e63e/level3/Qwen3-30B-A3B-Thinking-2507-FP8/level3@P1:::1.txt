

This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning that addresses the challenge of disjoint feature spaces across languages by exploiting unlabeled parallel bilingual documents. The authors first construct a unified document-term matrix where parallel documents are fully observed while non-parallel documents have missing entries for the other language. They then formulate and solve a matrix completion problem to recover a low-rank, sparse matrix, followed by applying latent semantic indexing to obtain cross-lingual representations. The approach differs from prior work such as CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, and CL-OPCA by first completing the matrix before performing dimensionality reduction.

The paper makes several notable contributions. The two-step methodology is conceptually simple yet effective, and the authors provide theoretical guarantees for their projected gradient descent algorithm, which is a significant strength. The experimental results on Amazon product reviews demonstrate consistent improvements over baseline methods, particularly when parallel data is limited (8.59-14.61% absolute improvement over TBOW). The method's robustness to small amounts of parallel data is especially valuable for practical applications where labeled parallel data is scarce.

Several technical points require clarification. First, the authors need to explicitly distinguish between the two ways external data is used: labeled source-language data (for classification) versus unlabeled parallel data (for representation learning). The experimental setup description is unclear regarding the exact data splits: 4,000 labeled source documents, 100 labeled target documents, 2,000 unlabeled parallel documents, and 1,900 test target documents. Section 5.3's variation of unlabeled parallel documents from 200 to 2,000 needs verification.

The theoretical justification for the low-rank assumption requires more discussion. The paper states that "a fully observed document-term matrix is naturally low-rank and sparse," but this isn't intuitively clear given that documents typically represent sparse vectors without obvious linear relationships. The authors should provide more intuition about why this assumption holds in cross-lingual contexts.

A significant inconsistency exists between Table 1 and Figures 1-2: CL-KCCA outperforms TSL on tasks EFM and EGM in Figure 1 (with 2,000 parallel documents) but TSL consistently outperforms CL-KCCA in Table 1 across all tasks. The authors should reconcile this discrepancy.

The paper also fails to explain why LSI is used after matrix completion rather than other dimensionality reduction techniques like PCA. The authors should clarify whether the low-rank enforcement during matrix completion already produces a compact representation that would make LSI redundant, or if LSI is necessary for computational reasons.

Regarding the choice of matrix completion over other approaches, the authors should provide more intuition about why their method outperforms CL-KCCA (which uses a different approach to learn cross-lingual projections). The paper states that "TSL outperforms both CL-KCCA and CL-OPCA across all the 18 tasks," but doesn't explain why the low-rank matrix completion approach would be superior to the kernel CCA method.

Minor editorial issues include inconsistent notation (e.g., (ij) vs. (i,j) before Eq. 2), and unnecessary "such as" before equations 4 and 5. The paper would benefit from a brief running-time comparison to establish computational efficiency relative to the other methods.

Overall, this is a solid paper that makes a meaningful contribution to cross-lingual learning. The method is simple, well-motivated, and demonstrates strong empirical performance. The theoretical guarantees for the optimization algorithm add significant value. However, the paper would be substantially stronger with clearer explanations of the methodological choices and resolution of the inconsistencies in the experimental results. The reviewers' concerns about the theoretical justification, experimental details, and comparison to CL-KCCA are valid and should be addressed.

Strengths: Novel two-step approach, strong empirical results, theoretical convergence guarantees, clear problem statement, practical value for limited parallel data scenarios.

Weaknesses: Inconsistent experimental results (Table 1 vs. Figures 1-2), insufficient theoretical justification for low-rank assumption, unclear data splits and usage, lack of comparison to alternative dimensionality reduction methods, missing running-time analysis.

Pro: The method is simple, theoretically grounded, and demonstrates clear empirical advantages, especially with limited parallel data.

Con: The paper contains inconsistencies that need clarification, and the theoretical foundation for key assumptions requires strengthening.
