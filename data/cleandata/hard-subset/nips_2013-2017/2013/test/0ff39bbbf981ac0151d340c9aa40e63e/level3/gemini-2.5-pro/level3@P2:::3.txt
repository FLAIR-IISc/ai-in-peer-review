### **Summary**

The paper proposes a novel two-step method for learning cross-lingual document representations to aid in cross-language text classification. The method leverages a set of unlabeled parallel documents to bridge the gap between two languages. First, it constructs a unified document-term matrix where documents are represented by the concatenated vocabularies of both languages. For monolingual documents, the term features from the other language are treated as missing values. The authors then formulate this as a matrix completion problem, using a projected gradient descent algorithm to recover a complete, low-rank matrix. In the second step, Latent Semantic Indexing (LSI) is applied to this completed matrix to derive a low-dimensional, dense cross-lingual representation. This final representation can be used with any standard monolingual classifier. This approach builds upon prior work in cross-lingual representation learning like Cross-Lingual LSI (CL-LSI) and Cross-Lingual Kernel CCA (CL-KCCA), but its key innovation lies in the explicit matrix completion step to infer missing cross-lingual information before dimensionality reduction.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper addresses a well-defined and important problem. The proposed method of combining matrix completion with LSI is technically interesting. The convergence analysis for the projected gradient descent algorithm provides theoretical grounding for the optimization procedure.

However, the primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation, which may not be robust enough to fully support the claims. The performance gains shown could be an artifact of the interaction between the learned representations and the specific choice of classifier (a linear SVM). The final step of the proposed method (TSL) uses LSI, which produces a scaled feature representation. Methods like OPCA and KCCA, used as baselines, produce projections with different scaling properties. A linear SVM's performance is sensitive to the scaling of its input features. It is therefore plausible that the superior performance of TSL is due to the SVM favoring the LSI-scaled representation, rather than the contribution of the matrix completion step itself. This represents a significant experimental confound that makes it difficult to isolate the benefit of the core technical contribution.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation and high-level approach are easy to follow. However, clarity could be improved in two areas:
1.  The description of the "unified document-term matrix" in Section 3 is ambiguous. It is not explicitly stated whether terms from different languages are treated as distinct features (e.g., separate columns for the English word "book" and the French word "livre"). This should be clarified, as it is fundamental to the problem setup.
2.  The description of the experimental setup is fragmented across the text and the caption for Table 1. This makes it difficult for the reader to fully grasp the experimental conditions (e.g., number of labeled source/target documents for training, number of test documents) without piecing together information from multiple places. A single, self-contained description of the data splits would greatly improve clarity and reproducibility.

**Originality:**
The core idea of formulating the cross-lingual learning problem as one of matrix completion appears to be novel. While matrix completion and LSI are both well-known techniques, their combination in this two-step fashion to create cross-lingual representations is a new contribution. The paper does a good job of situating this approach relative to prior work.

**Significance:**
The work addresses a significant and practical problem. If the results are shown to be robust, the approach is quite interesting because it applies a principled, non-linear inference technique (matrix completion) to bridge the language gap. The empirical results, which suggest the method works well even with very few parallel documents, would be of high value to researchers and practitioners working on low-resource language problems. However, the significance of the contribution is currently moderated by the potential flaw in the experimental design.

### **Suggestions for Improvement**

To strengthen the paper and address the main weakness, I strongly recommend that the authors validate their results using a classifier that is insensitive to per-feature rescaling. An excellent choice would be a tree-based ensemble method like boosted decision trees (e.g., AdaBoost with decision stumps or trees), which are available in standard toolkits like Weka or scikit-learn. If the proposed TSL method maintains its performance advantage with a scale-invariant classifier, the evidence for the effectiveness of the matrix completion step would be much more compelling.

### **Arguments for/against acceptance**

**Pro:**
-   The paper presents a novel and elegant idea, combining matrix completion and LSI for cross-lingual representation learning.
-   The problem is important and highly relevant to the NIPS community.
-   The initial empirical results are promising and show a clear advantage over several baselines, particularly in a setting with limited parallel data.

**Con:**
-   The experimental validation may be flawed. The observed gains could be an artifact of the linear SVM's sensitivity to feature scaling rather than the novelty of the proposed method. This key confound undermines the paper's central claims.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper introduces a neat and original idea for an important problem. However, the experimental evidence provided is not fully convincing due to the potential confound related to the choice of classifier. While the idea is interesting, the work requires stronger empirical validation to be considered a complete and robust scientific contribution.
