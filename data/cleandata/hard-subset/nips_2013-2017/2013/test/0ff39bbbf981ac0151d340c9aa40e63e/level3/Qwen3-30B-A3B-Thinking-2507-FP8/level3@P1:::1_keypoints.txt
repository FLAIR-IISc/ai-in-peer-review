- Proposes a two-step cross-lingual representation method: matrix completion to fill missing entries in a unified document-term matrix, then LSI for dimensionality reduction.
- Differs from prior work (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) by completing the matrix before performing dimensionality reduction.
- Provides theoretical convergence guarantees for a projected gradient descent algorithm—a significant technical contribution.
- Demonstrates strong empirical gains on Amazon product reviews, especially with limited parallel data (8.59–14.61% absolute improvement over TBOW).
- Method is robust and practically valuable when parallel labeled data is scarce.
- Experimental setup and data usage are unclear: need explicit distinction between labeled source data (classification) and unlabeled parallel data (representation learning).
- Unclear exact data splits (claimed: 4,000 labeled source, 100 labeled target, 2,000 unlabeled parallel, 1,900 test target) and need to verify Section 5.3’s varying unlabeled parallel sizes (200–2,000).
- The low-rank assumption for the full document-term matrix lacks sufficient intuition and theoretical justification in the cross-lingual context.
- Significant inconsistency between Table 1 and Figures 1–2: CL-KCCA appears to outperform TSL in some figures but TSL wins in Table 1; this must be reconciled.
- Unclear rationale for applying LSI after matrix completion versus using other dimensionality reduction methods (e.g., PCA) or relying on the low-rank completion alone.
- Needs more intuition and explanation for why matrix completion outperforms approaches like CL-KCCA (kernel CCA) despite empirical claims of superiority.
- Minor editorial issues: inconsistent notation ((ij) vs. (i,j)), unnecessary phrasing before equations—requires polish.
- Missing running-time / computational-efficiency comparison against baseline methods.
- Overall assessment: solid, simple, well-motivated approach with theoretical backing and strong empirical performance.
- Main weaknesses to address: experimental inconsistencies, theoretical justification for key assumptions, unclear experimental details, and lack of methodological comparisons.
