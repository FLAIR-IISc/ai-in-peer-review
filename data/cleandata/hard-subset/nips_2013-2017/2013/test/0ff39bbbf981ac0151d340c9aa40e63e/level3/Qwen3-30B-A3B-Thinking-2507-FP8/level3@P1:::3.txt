

The paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual text classification that first uses matrix completion to fill missing entries in a bilingual document-term matrix and then applies latent semantic indexing (LSI) to derive low-dimensional cross-lingual representations. This approach differs from prior work like CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, which directly apply dimensionality reduction on the incomplete matrix without imputing missing values. The authors demonstrate that their method (TSL) outperforms these baselines on 18 Amazon sentiment classification tasks, particularly when parallel data is scarce.

**Strengths**  
The core idea is novel: leveraging matrix completion to bridge language gaps via co-occurrence patterns across parallel documents, rather than relying on translation or explicit bilingual resources. The theoretical foundation is sound, with a convergence-proofed projected gradient descent algorithm for the convex optimization problem. The experimental design is comprehensive, testing multiple language pairs (English-French, English-German, English-Japanese) and varying amounts of parallel data. The results show statistically significant improvements over baselines (e.g., +14.61% accuracy on EFB), and the method’s robustness with minimal parallel data (200 documents) is a compelling practical advantage.

**Critical Weaknesses**  
The primary concern is **methodological validity**. The authors attribute TSL’s superiority to the matrix completion step, but the results may instead reflect LSI’s inherent scaling effect. LSI’s singular value decomposition inherently normalizes features, which could disproportionately benefit linear SVMs (used in all experiments) by amplifying discriminative signals. Crucially, the paper lacks experiments with classifiers insensitive to feature scaling (e.g., boosted decision trees, random forests). For instance, CL-LSI and CL-KCCA also use LSI, yet TSL outperforms them—this could stem from how LSI is applied *after* matrix completion, not the completion itself. The authors should test with Weka’s AdaBoost or similar to rule out this confound. Without this, the claimed "improvement" over baselines may not be attributable to the proposed method.

**Clarity Issues**  
The paper ambiguously describes the "unified vocabulary" (Section 3.1). It’s unclear whether terms are language-specific surface forms (e.g., "book" in English vs. "livre" in French) or language-agnostic interlingual concepts. This affects interpretation of the matrix completion step. Additionally, the experimental setup is fragmented: key details (e.g., parameter selection, dataset splits) are buried in Table 1 and the Methods section, making reproducibility difficult. A dedicated subsection on experimental design would improve clarity.

**Significance and Originality**  
The approach is genuinely novel, as no prior NIPS work (2013–2017) uses matrix completion for cross-lingual representation learning in this manner. If validated with scale-insensitive classifiers, the method could advance low-resource language applications by reducing reliance on large parallel corpora. However, the current evidence falls short of demonstrating this significance due to the classifier confound.

**Recommendation**  
**Weak Reject**. The core idea is promising but the evaluation lacks rigor. The claimed superiority over baselines is likely an artifact of LSI’s scaling behavior with linear SVMs, not the matrix completion step. The authors must address this by:  
1. Re-running experiments with non-linear, scale-insensitive classifiers (e.g., gradient-boosted trees),  
2. Clarifying the unified vocabulary’s nature,  
3. Consolidating experimental details.  
Without these, the results cannot credibly support the method’s novelty or efficacy. If revised to eliminate the confound, the paper would be a strong contribution. As-is, the quality is undermined by an unaddressed methodological flaw. The significance is contingent on resolving this issue. The novelty is clear, but the evidence for it is not.
