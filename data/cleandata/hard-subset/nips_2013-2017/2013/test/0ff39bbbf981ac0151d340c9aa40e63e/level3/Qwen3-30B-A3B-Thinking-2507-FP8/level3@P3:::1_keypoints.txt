- Proposes a novel two-step method: complete a partially observed bilingual document-term matrix via projected gradient descent (with convergence proof), then apply LSI to obtain cross-lingual representations.
- Theoretically grounded: provides convergence guarantees for the projected gradient descent algorithm (Theorem 1).
- Empirically strong: shows consistent improvements over baselines across 18 cross-lingual sentiment tasks, especially when parallel data is limited.
- Originality: applying matrix completion before LSI to bridge cross-lingual gaps is a novel departure from methods that operate on incomplete concatenated matrices.
- Practical significance: useful for scenarios with scarce labeled target-language data and limited parallel corpora.
- Clarity needed: justify applying LSI after matrix completion—does LSI add value beyond the low-rank completion, and why choose LSI over PCA or alternatives?
- Experimental clarity needed: specify exact data splits (e.g., 4k source, 100 target, 2k unlabeled) and whether unlabeled parallel data varies from 200 to 2k in Section 5.3.
- Result inconsistency: reconcile Figure 1 (CL-KCCA outperforming TSL on EFM/EGM at 2k unlabeled) with Table 1 (TSL outperforming CL-KCCA across tasks).
- Theoretical concern: question the low-rank assumption for document-term matrices—provide intuition or evidence given typical sparsity and document heterogeneity.
- Missing evaluation: include runtime comparisons between the projected gradient descent and baselines (CL-LSI, CL-KCCA, CL-OPCA) for practical adoption.
- Data usage clarity: explicitly distinguish and report the separate roles of labeled source data vs. unlabeled parallel data in experiments.
- Minor edits: fix "(ij)" to "(i,j)" before Eq. (2) and remove "such as" before Eqs. (4) and (5).
- Overall assessment: a simple, effective, and theoretically supported contribution that merits acceptance with minor revisions to address clarity and experimental details.
