- Proposes a two-step method: matrix completion to fill a unified document-term matrix across languages, then LSI to obtain cross-lingual document representations.  
- Aims to improve cross-language text classification when target-language labeled data is scarce by leveraging a small set of unlabeled parallel documents.  
- Unified document-term matrix treats missing cross-language term entries for non-parallel documents and is recovered via low-rank matrix completion.  
- Final low-dimensional representations are produced by applying LSI (SVD) to the completed matrix and used with standard monolingual classifiers.  
- Differs from prior work (CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, CL-KCCA) by completing missing entries before dimensionality reduction rather than directly projecting concatenated matrices.  
- Evaluated on multilingual Amazon reviews for cross-lingual sentiment classification across 18 tasks, showing superior performance, especially with few parallel documents.  
- Strength: Novel framing of cross-lingual representation as a matrix completion problem combined with LSI is creative and principled.  
- Strength: Potentially significant contribution if robust, offering a more sophisticated way to infer cross-lingual term associations and greater data efficiency.  
- Strength: Empirical results show consistent improvements and compelling analysis of performance versus amount of parallel data.  
- Weakness: Experimental validation may be confounded by classifier choice (linear SVM), since per-feature scaling could favor the proposed representation.  
- Weakness: Description of the unified document-term matrix construction is ambiguous and should be explicitly stated.  
- Weakness: Experimental setup details are fragmented across sections and captions, reducing clarity and reproducibility.  
- Suggestion: Re-run experiments with classifiers insensitive to monotonic per-feature rescaling (e.g., Random Forests or boosted trees) to rule out scaling artifacts.  
- Suggestion: Explicitly define how the unified vocabulary is constructed (concatenation, handling of cognates/shared tokens) and consolidate experimental protocol into a single clear subsection.  
- Summary: Idea is neat and original, but stronger validation with scale-invariant classifiers and clearer methodological descriptions are needed to convincingly support the claims.
