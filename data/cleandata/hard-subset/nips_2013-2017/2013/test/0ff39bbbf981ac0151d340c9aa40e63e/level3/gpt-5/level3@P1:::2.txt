This paper proposes a two-step method for cross-lingual representation learning: (1) complete a unified bilingual document–term matrix via convex matrix completion, then (2) apply LSI/SVD to induce a shared low-dimensional semantic space where standard monolingual classifiers can operate. The key idea is to treat non-parallel documents as partially observed rows in the concatenated vocabulary, and to exploit a small set of parallel documents to “bridge” vocabularies across languages. The matrix completion objective combines a nuclear norm (for low rank), an ℓ1 norm (for sparsity), least-squares data fit on observed entries, and a regularizer on missing entries, with non-negativity constraints. A projected gradient descent algorithm with singular value shrinkage and non-negativity projection is used, and convergence is argued via non-expansiveness of the composite operator. After completion, LSI produces the cross-lingual representation.

Relation to prior work: The method is closest to CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, all of which learn cross-lingual mappings from parallel/comparable corpora, but those operate directly on the original concatenated matrix; here, matrix completion precedes dimensionality reduction. The approach also relates to SCL with pivot features and multilingual topic models. The paper clearly situates itself among translation-based adaptation, multi-view co-training/co-classification, and projection-based methods. One notable nearby line not compared empirically is cross-lingual word/paragraph embeddings (e.g., Klementiev et al., 2012; Mikolov et al., 2013), which could be acknowledged as complementary baselines.

Empirical evaluation is on 18 English–French/German/Japanese sentiment classification tasks (Amazon reviews), with 100 labeled target examples and parallel data varying from 200 to 2000 pairs. The proposed method consistently outperforms TBOW, CL-LSI, CL-OPCA, and CL-KCCA, with especially strong gains when parallel data is scarce. Hyperparameters are chosen on one task and reused, and accuracy (mean ± std over 10 splits) is reported.

Quality: The optimization problem is well-motivated and convex after relaxation; the algorithm is appropriate and the convergence argument follows standard results. The experimental setup is reasonable and the comparisons are to relevant, strong baselines. Strengths include robustness at low parallel data and clear performance margins. Areas for improvement:
- Provide computational complexity and runtimes (per iteration cost, use of truncated SVD, memory footprint), and compare to CL-LSI/OPCA/KCCA to support claims of scalability.
- Clarify the learning setting: representation learning uses all unlabeled target documents (including test instances), which is transductive. Consider reporting an additional setting where only source + parallel + labeled target are used for representation learning to assess generalization.
- Add ablations: (i) matrix completion alone vs. completion+LSI to justify the two-step design; (ii) effect of each regularizer (nuclear norm, ℓ1, nonnegativity, and the ρ term on missing entries).
- Report statistical significance (e.g., paired tests) for key improvements, not only mean ± std.
- Discuss sensitivity to ρ (bias toward zero on missing entries may hurt rare features), γ, and k in a focused analysis.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, well-organized, and reproducible: objectives, algorithms, and hyperparameter selection are clearly stated. Minor suggestions:
- Better motivate matrix completion in the Introduction: give intuition and cite why document-term matrices, though high-dimensional, often admit low-rank approximations due to latent semantic structure.
- Explain the role of nonnegativity with shrinkage (nuclear-norm soft-thresholding can produce negatives; projection may interact with low-rank structure).
- Explicitly define “observed” vs “missing” in the unified matrix early and give a small toy example for intuition.
- Make explicit that all documents from both languages are used in the representation-learning stage.

Originality: Using convex matrix completion to impute cross-lingual co-occurrence structure prior to LSI is a novel and elegant twist on prior projection-based approaches. The algorithmic components are known, but their combination and application to cross-lingual representation learning are original and practically meaningful.

Significance: The empirical gains—especially under limited parallel data—suggest this is a useful preprocessing step for cross-lingual sentiment classification and potentially other tasks. Because the output is a shared representation, the approach can plug into many monolingual classifiers. Broader impact would be strengthened by comparisons to cross-lingual embedding methods and by demonstrating transfer beyond sentiment (e.g., topic categorization or retrieval).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled formulation with convergence-guaranteed algorithm.
- Strong, consistent empirical improvements over established baselines.
- Robust performance with small parallel corpora.
- Thorough experimental protocol and reasonable parameter selection strategy.
- Conceptually simple and compatible with standard classifiers.

Cons against acceptance:
- Lacks runtime/scale analysis and deeper ablations to isolate contributions of each component.
- Transductive setting may inflate performance; needs a strictly inductive variant.
- Missing comparisons to cross-lingual embeddings that were emerging contemporaneously.
- Theoretical justification for low rank in document-term matrices is asserted but not substantiated or empirically probed.

Suggestions to improve the paper:
- Add wall-clock and memory comparisons; describe SVD implementation (e.g., randomized/truncated).
- Include an ablation suite and an inductive-only setting.
- Compare to cross-lingual embeddings (e.g., Klementiev et al., 2012; Mikolov et al., 2013) or discuss complementarities.
- Provide qualitative analyses: examples of recovered cross-lingual associations; nearest neighbors across languages in the learned space.
- Discuss applicability beyond sentiment and to more than two languages.

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed paper with a simple idea that works notably well in low-parallel regimes. With additional analysis of scalability and ablations, it would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
