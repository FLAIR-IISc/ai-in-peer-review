Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses unsupervised structure and parameter learning for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), a family of generative, compositional models used in vision and activity recognition [1,3,7] and related to stochastic grammars in NLP [8,9] and sum-product networks (SPNs) [10]. The authors provide a unified, context-free formalization of AOGs that abstracts over the domain (text, images, events). The key contribution is an iterative, bottom-up structure learning method that starts from a memorization grammar (one And-rule per training example under an Or over examples) and greedily inserts And-Or fragments—each fragment introduces one And-node with multiple Or-children—to increase the posterior P(G|X) under a sparsity prior and a Viterbi (best-parse) likelihood approximation [20,21].

Technically, the paper proposes: (i) to search over “And-Or fragments” that jointly introduce composition (And) and reconfiguration (Or), rather than learning And- and Or-structure separately; (ii) to evaluate the posterior gain of a candidate fragment using sufficient statistics computed once from the data: an n-gram tensor of configurations and a context matrix of configuration/context co-occurrences, assuming unambiguous parses; (iii) to derive maximum-likelihood Or-rule parameters within the fragment directly from the reduction counts; and (iv) to incorporate a description-length style prior penalizing grammar size. The authors argue that searching over And-Or fragments mitigates data sparsity and redundancy issues observed when learning And or Or structure in isolation (cf. [14–18]).

Empirically, the approach is applied to two domains. For event grammars (indoor activities), three variants are evaluated: learning from discretized ID sequences (as in [18]) and two variants grounded to atomic actions with both temporal “followed-by” and “co-occurring” relations. Performance is measured by F-measure over overlapping event intervals and compared to [15,18]. For image grammars, the method is evaluated on a synthetic animal-face sketch dataset (F-measure, KL divergence to a known ground-truth grammar) and a real dataset from [17] (perplexity), with comparisons against [17]. Results suggest competitive or improved accuracy and substantially lower perplexity on the real image dataset.

Quality
- Soundness: The overall framework—greedy posterior-improving fragment insertion under a size prior and Viterbi likelihood—is reasonable and consistent with prior work in grammar induction and SPN structure learning. The derivation of posterior gain using sufficient statistics is appealing for efficiency. However, several assumptions/approximations are strong and under-specified:
  - The unambiguity assumption is crucial for the factorized likelihood-gain formula; the paper does not quantify how violations affect correctness or propose a remedy (e.g., n-best parses or soft counts).
  - The use of Viterbi likelihood can bias learning; while motivated by [20,21], empirical sensitivity analysis is not provided.
  - The prior term depends on sa and so (“bits to encode”), but their choice/material impact is not fully described; α is fixed at 0.5 without justification across domains.
  - The claim that “adding only And-Or fragments can construct any context-free AOG” is plausible but lacks a formal proof in the main text.
- Methodological completeness: The search strategy (greedy/beam with restarts) is pragmatic; complexity and scaling characteristics are only briefly discussed. Pseudocode and derivations are deferred to the supplement.
- Empirics: Cross-domain evaluations are a strength. Yet, details are missing/limited:
  - Event experiments: The datasets, train/test splits, and exact numbers for the baselines and variants (Table 2) are not fully shown here; statistical significance is not reported. Some advantages may stem from using richer input (action-grounded representation) than baselines restricted to IDs; the authors partly address this via their “method 1”, but a direct head-to-head on identical inputs and ablations (And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or fragments) would clarify where gains originate.
  - Image experiments: Synthetic results appear favorable; real-data perplexity differences are large, but smoothing and evaluation protocols need more detail. Runtime and search parameters (beam width, restarts) are not reported.

Clarity
The high-level ideas and motivations are clearly stated, and the unified AOG definition is helpful. The learning process is well illustrated conceptually (figures). However, some key components are only sketched:
- Formal definitions of the “coherence” measures and their connection to the likelihood-gain factorization rely on [22] and the supplement; including a concise definition in the main text would improve readability for ML audiences.
- Notation in the likelihood-gain expression is dense; a simple worked example would aid comprehension.
- Hyperparameter choices (α, sa, so) and their roles should be specified upfront.

Originality
Jointly proposing And-Or fragments as the primitive for structure search, with an explicit posterior-gain objective derived from sufficient statistics, is novel relative to prior AOG learning that treats And/Or separately [17,18] and to classical grammar induction by model merging/biclustering [14–16]. The unifying, domain-agnostic formulation and cross-domain application add to originality. Related work on SPN structure learning (e.g., Poon & Domingos 2011 [10]; Gens & Domingos 2013, UAI) is closely connected and should be discussed, as the fragment search resembles discovering decomposable/product and sum structure.

Significance
Learning compact, generalizable AOGs without annotations is important for vision and activity understanding. If robust, the fragment-based search that ties structure learning to sufficient statistics can be impactful. The empirical gains, especially on real-image perplexity, are promising. The practical significance would be strengthened by scalability analyses, ablations, and comparisons to more recent grammar/SPN structure learners.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, unified formulation of context-free AOGs applicable across domains.
- Conceptual novelty in using And-Or fragments to jointly learn composition and reconfiguration.
- Efficient posterior-gain evaluation via sufficient statistics; practical search strategies.
- Cross-domain experiments show competitive to superior performance; substantial perplexity reduction on real images.

Cons for acceptance
- Reliance on strong assumptions (unambiguity; Viterbi) without analysis of their impact.
- Prior/encoding costs (sa, so, α) under-specified; sensitivity not studied.
- Limited empirical diagnostics: no ablations on fragment choice, no runtime/scale metrics, and incomplete reporting of event results and significance.
- Incomplete positioning w.r.t. SPN structure learning literature and other modern grammar induction methods.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a brief proof or constructive argument that And-Or fragment insertions can generate any context-free AOG.
- Define and justify the coherence measures in the main text; discuss how ambiguity affects the sufficient-statistics formulation.
- Report sensitivity to α, sa, so and to beam size; include runtime and scaling with dataset/grammar size.
- Add ablations (And-only, Or-only, And-Or) and an analysis of failure cases.
- Expand related work to include SPN structure learning (e.g., Gens & Domingos 2013) and Bayesian grammar induction beyond those cited.

Overall recommendation
A solid and interesting contribution to unsupervised structure learning for AOGs with encouraging cross-domain results. With stronger empirical diagnostics and clearer treatment of assumptions and related work, this would be a strong NeurIPS paper. As is, it is technically plausible and novel but would benefit from additional rigor and analysis.
