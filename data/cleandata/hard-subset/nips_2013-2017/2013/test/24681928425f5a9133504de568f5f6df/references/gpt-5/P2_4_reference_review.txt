Summary
The paper proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of context-free Stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs) from unannotated data. The key idea is to iteratively insert And-Or fragments—small subgrammars that simultaneously introduce a new And-node and its child Or-nodes—thereby learning compositions and alternative configurations jointly. The objective is a posterior combining a sparsity/MDL-style prior over grammar size and a Viterbi (best-parse) likelihood, which the authors argue favors compact, relatively unambiguous grammars. To make search efficient, they derive sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor that counts configurations covered by a candidate fragment and a context matrix that counts co-occurrences of configurations with their contexts. Under an unambiguity assumption, the likelihood gain of adding a fragment factorizes into coherence measures of these statistics, yielding a fast scoring mechanism amenable to greedy/beam search. Experiments on event parsing and image grammar learning show improvements over prior AOG structure learning methods.

Relation to prior work: The approach extends classic grammar induction (e.g., Bayesian model merging, Stolcke & Omohundro; unsupervised PCFG induction, Klein & Manning) and recent AOG learning for images/events (e.g., Si & Zhu; Si et al.) by unifying the search for And- and Or-rules via And-Or fragments. It is also conceptually related to structure learning in sum-product networks (SPNs), to which AOGs are closely connected.

Strengths
- Clear algorithmic contribution: searching over And-Or fragments to jointly learn compositions and alternatives addresses data scarcity and redundancy issues seen when learning And- or Or-fragments separately.
- Principled objective: maximizing posterior with an MDL-like prior and Viterbi likelihood is well-motivated (supported by prior observations on Viterbi training/unambiguity bias).
- Efficient scoring via sufficient statistics: the n-gram tensor and context matrix coherence reduce re-parsing costs, making greedy/beam search practical.
- Cross-domain applicability: same framework applied to events (temporal relations) and images (spatial relations), with performance gains over prior AOG learners.
- Empirical comparisons: on a synthetic image dataset with known ground truth grammar and on real image/event datasets, the method performs comparably or better than baselines.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Unambiguity assumption: The derivation of posterior gain relies on grammar unambiguity. Real-world grammars are often ambiguous; the paper does not quantify how violations affect correctness or performance. The reliance on Viterbi likelihood partially mitigates but does not remove this gap.
- Prior specification: Grammar size is measured using per-symbol code lengths sa and so, and α is a hyperparameter. The paper fixes α (e.g., 0.5) but does not justify or analyze sensitivity to α, sa, so, which can strongly influence structure selection.
- And-rule parameterization: And-rule probabilities/energies depend on relations; the learning/estimation of these energy terms is not described in sufficient detail to reproduce results, especially across modalities with different relation types.
- Scalability and search complexity: While the sufficient statistics help, constructing n-gram tensors across relation types can be expensive. There is no complexity analysis or scaling experiment (e.g., growth with n, number of relations, or larger datasets).
- Generality vs. domain engineering: The framework is “agnostic” to data types only up to the need to define relation sets and context construction, which are nontrivial and domain-specific. The paper could better specify how contexts are defined and how relation types are selected and validated.
- Evaluation gaps: 
  - Event experiments involve small splits (55/6), and the third method modifies the learned grammar post hoc by turning top-level And-nodes into Or-nodes; this is ad hoc and its effect is not isolated.
  - Real image perplexity evaluation mentions smoothing but omits details; perplexity is reported without confidence intervals.
  - Missing ablations: no direct comparison of learning only And-fragments, only Or-fragments, and And-Or fragments within the same framework to quantify the claimed advantages.
  - Few diagnostics on learned grammar ambiguity, compactness vs. accuracy trade-off, or sensitivity to α and beam width.
- Clarity issues: Some derivations are deferred to the supplement; figures (e.g., 1 and 2) are dense and hard to parse; the definition of “context” in the context matrix and the exact encoding cost model need clearer, reproducible specification.

Assessment by review criteria
- Quality: The approach is technically sound at a high level and supported by experiments, but key assumptions (unambiguity) and hyperparameter choices are not thoroughly examined. Reproducibility is hindered by missing details on energy term learning, context construction, and smoothing.
- Clarity: Generally well-organized, with a coherent story and sensible motivation. However, some definitions and derivations are under-specified in the main text; figures could be improved for readability.
- Originality: The unified And-Or fragment search and the sufficient-statistics-based posterior gain are novel and nontrivial extensions over prior AOG/SPN structure learning and classical grammar induction.
- Significance: Learning compact, expressive grammars across modalities is important. The method appears to advance the state of the art for AOG structure learning with promising empirical gains, though broader adoption would benefit from stronger scalability and ablation evidence.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Quantify the impact of ambiguity: How does the posterior gain approximation behave when grammars are ambiguous? Can you report ambiguity metrics and performance deltas?
- Provide ablations: Compare And-only, Or-only, and And-Or fragment searches; show sensitivity to α, sa, so, beam width, and n.
- Clarify implementation details: exact definitions of contexts; how relations are represented and how And-rule energies are estimated; smoothing method for perplexity; runtime and memory profiles vs. dataset size and n.
- Discuss scalability: complexity analysis and experiments on larger datasets; heuristics for pruning candidate fragments.
- Improve figures and include more parsing examples to illustrate learned structures.

Verdict
Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution with a clear methodological advance and encouraging results. Some theoretical and empirical gaps (unambiguity assumption, missing ablations/sensitivity, and limited scalability analysis) prevent a strong accept. I lean toward acceptance provided the authors address these concerns in the camera-ready.
