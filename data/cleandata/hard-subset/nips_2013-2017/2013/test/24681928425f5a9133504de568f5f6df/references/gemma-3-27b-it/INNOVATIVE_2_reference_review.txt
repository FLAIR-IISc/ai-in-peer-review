This paper presents a novel and ambitious approach to unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, applicable across diverse data types. The core idea – jointly learning grammar structure *and* parameters, and formulating this as posterior maximization using And-Or fragments – is a significant departure from existing methods that typically fix the structure or treat composition/reconfiguration separately. The authors successfully provide a unified formalization and demonstrate promising results on both event and image grammars. This work feels like a genuinely new direction, and despite some areas for improvement, the boldness and potential impact warrant strong consideration.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core contribution is highly novel. Learning both structure and parameters simultaneously, and the use of And-Or fragments, addresses a crucial limitation of previous approaches. This is not merely incremental work.
*   **Generality:** The grammar formalization is impressively general, extending beyond traditional NLP applications to images and events without modification. This broad applicability is a major strength.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:** The formulation based on posterior probability maximization, and the derivation of sufficient statistics, demonstrate a solid theoretical foundation.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The results on event and image grammars are competitive or superior to existing methods, suggesting the effectiveness of the approach. The synthetic image experiments, in particular, are convincing.
*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The authors clearly articulate the challenges of unsupervised grammar learning and position their work within the existing literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** The algorithm and its underlying theory are complex. While the authors make an effort to explain them, a deeper understanding requires significant effort from the reader.
*   **Scalability:** While the authors claim reasonable runtime, the scalability to very large datasets or grammars isn't fully addressed. The discussion of computational efficiency could be expanded.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the hyperparameter α is not thoroughly investigated. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the results.
*   **Limited Baselines:** The comparison with existing methods is somewhat limited. More recent and sophisticated approaches, especially in the image grammar domain, could be included.
*   **Evaluation Metrics:** Perplexity, while a standard metric, can be difficult to interpret in the context of image grammars. More visually interpretable evaluation metrics would be helpful.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The work introduces a truly novel and potentially impactful approach to unsupervised grammar learning.
*   The theoretical foundation is strong, and the algorithm is well-motivated.
*   The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.
*   The generality of the framework is a significant advantage.
*   Even if some details require further refinement, the core idea is sufficiently compelling to warrant acceptance.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The complexity of the approach may limit its adoption by the broader research community.
*   The scalability to large datasets is not fully demonstrated.
*   The evaluation could be more comprehensive, including a wider range of baselines and metrics.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly lean towards acceptance. This paper presents a significant step forward in unsupervised grammar learning. While the method is complex and could benefit from further scalability analysis and more extensive evaluation, the novelty, theoretical grounding, and promising empirical results outweigh the weaknesses. The approach has the potential to inspire new research in diverse areas, including natural language processing, computer vision, and robotics. This paper aligns well with NIPS’s focus on innovative and impactful research. I would encourage the authors to address the points raised regarding scalability and evaluation in a future version of the work.



**Rating:** 8.5/10 (Strong Accept)
