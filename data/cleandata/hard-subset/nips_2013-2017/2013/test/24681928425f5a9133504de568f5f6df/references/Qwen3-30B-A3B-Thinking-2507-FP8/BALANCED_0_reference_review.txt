

# Review: Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars

This paper proposes an unsupervised approach for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars. The authors introduce a novel "And-Or fragment" that unifies the learning of compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules), addressing a key limitation of prior work that typically learns these components separately. The approach optimizes the posterior probability of the grammar, using sufficient statistics to efficiently compute likelihood gains. The paper demonstrates the approach on event grammars (learning from human activity videos) and image grammars (learning from animal face sketches), showing competitive or better performance than prior methods.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Unified Approach**: The central contribution—learning And-rules and Or-rules through unified And-Or fragments—addresses a genuine limitation in previous structure learning approaches. By avoiding separate searches for And-fragments and Or-fragments, the method is more robust to data scarcity and ambiguity, as the authors convincingly argue.

2. **Formalization and Generality**: The paper provides a clear, agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars applicable to various data types (text, images, events), which is a useful contribution beyond the specific applications demonstrated.

3. **Technical Soundness**: The derivation of sufficient statistics for efficient posterior gain computation is technically sound and well-supported. The likelihood gain formulation is particularly elegant, relating to coherence measures of n-gram tensors and context matrices.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments on event and image grammars demonstrate practical utility, with the approach outperforming prior work on a synthetic event dataset (where it handles action subtype variations better) and showing significant improvements on both synthetic and real image grammars.

## Weaknesses and Suggestions

1. **Insufficient Differentiation from Prior Work**: The paper claims novelty in learning both structure and parameters in a unified way but doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from Si et al. (2013) [17], which also learns image grammars. The paper mentions [17] but doesn't explicitly contrast the two methods. To strengthen the contribution claim, the authors should clarify how their posterior probability optimization framework differs from [17]'s approach (which appears to use a different objective function).

2. **Limited Experimental Scope**: The event grammar experiments use only 61 videos total (55 training, 6 testing), which is quite small for meaningful statistical evaluation. The synthetic dataset for the second experiment is also limited. The authors should either increase dataset size, include more diverse applications, or provide more thorough statistical analysis of their results to strengthen the significance claim.

3. **Evaluation Metrics**: The event grammar evaluation uses "purity" and "inverse purity" without sufficient explanation of why these are appropriate metrics. Standard NLP metrics like precision, recall, and F1-score would provide clearer interpretation of results. The authors should clarify the choice of metrics and consider standard alternatives.

4. **Computational Considerations**: The paper mentions the algorithm runs in "a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples," but doesn't provide computational complexity analysis or scalability analysis for larger datasets. For a method targeting practical applications, this is a significant omission.

5. **Theoretical Justification**: While the paper argues that And-Or fragments avoid problems with separate And/Or fragment learning, a more formal theoretical justification (beyond empirical results) would strengthen this claim. For instance, a proof showing that the unified approach converges to the optimal grammar structure under certain conditions would be valuable.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to unsupervised structure learning of grammars, with a novel unified approach that addresses a genuine limitation in prior work. The technical execution is sound, and the empirical results are promising. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by better differentiating from Si et al. (2013), expanding the experimental validation, and providing more comprehensive theoretical justification.

While the paper is strong and makes a meaningful contribution, the current version has some important limitations in experimental scope and differentiation from prior work that prevent it from being a clear "top-tier" NeurIPS contribution. With the suggested revisions—particularly clarifying the relationship to [17] and expanding the experimental validation—the paper would be significantly stronger.

**Decision**: Weak Accept (with major revisions)

**Major Revisions Needed**:
1. Clearly contrast the proposed method with Si et al. (2013) [17], explaining why the posterior probability optimization framework represents a distinct contribution.
2. Expand experimental evaluation with larger datasets or more thorough statistical analysis.
3. Provide more standard evaluation metrics for the event grammar experiments.
4. Include computational complexity analysis and scalability discussion.
