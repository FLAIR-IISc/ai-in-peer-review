Summary
The paper studies unsupervised structure learning for context-free Stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs), proposing to jointly induce compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules) via “And-Or fragments.” The objective maximizes a sparsity-regularized posterior, using Viterbi likelihood to approximate P(X|G). Starting from a trivial memorizing grammar, the method greedily/beam-search inserts And-Or fragments that increase the posterior. The authors derive an expression for the posterior gain under an “unambiguity” assumption, decomposing likelihood gain into two coherence-like factors computed from sufficient statistics (an n-gram tensor of within-fragment configurations and a context matrix), and a prior gain that penalizes grammar size via a code-length proxy. They show applications to event grammars (two datasets, compared to ID-based approaches) and image grammars (synthetic with known ground truth and a real animal-faces dataset), claiming improved performance over prior work (e.g., Si & Zhu 2013; Si et al. 2011; Wang et al. 2013).

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on stochastic grammars for images/events (Zhu & Mumford; Jin & Geman; Zhao & Zhu), event parsing (Ivanov & Bobick; Ryoo & Aggarwal; Si et al.), unsupervised grammar induction (Stolcke & Omohundro; Solan et al.; Tu & Honavar), and SPNs (Poon & Domingos). The main novelty is the use of And-Or fragments to unify discovering compositions and alternatives, with a posterior-gain scoring computable from sufficient statistics.

Strengths
- Ambitious unified formulation of context-free AOGs applicable across modalities (images, events) and a search-based structure learning strategy that interleaves compositions and alternatives.
- Clear high-level motivation for And-Or fragments over separate And- or Or-fragment searches; plausible efficiency and robustness benefits under data scarcity/ambiguity.
- Derivation of a data-driven posterior-gain score from sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor and context matrix) is attractive for scalable search.
- Cross-domain experiments (events, images); on the real image dataset, perplexity appears substantially better than the compared baseline.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and completeness
- And-rule probabilities/energies are not specified. The text says And-rule probabilities are “specified by the energy terms defined on the relations,” but there is no explicit parameterization, no learning/update rule, and no description of how these energies contribute numerically to P(A→⋯). In the likelihood-gain derivation, P(A→⋯) appears but is never concretely defined, making the core scoring ill-specified.
- The posterior-gain derivation relies on a strong unambiguity assumption and Viterbi likelihood. The method neither enforces unambiguity nor checks it post hoc; no discussion of the bias this induces or failure modes when ambiguity is present. This undermines the claimed efficiency/accuracy of the sufficient-statistics computation.
- The likelihood-gain equation contains apparent typographical/notation errors (e.g., repeated ‖RDi(aij)‖ in the numerator; exponents like CM[e,c] CM[e,c] are ambiguous). The sets RD and RDi(aij) are insufficiently defined; “contexts” in CM are only described informally as “surrounding patterns,” not a formal function of the parse or data. As written, the formula is not reproducible.
- The prior model begins with P(G) ∝ e^{-α‖G‖}, but later the prior gain uses code-length terms sa and so without defining how ‖G‖ relates to these, nor how sa/so are chosen. These become free hyperparameters with non-trivial impact on the search, yet no guidance or sensitivity analysis is provided.
- The claim “adding only And-Or fragments can construct any context-free AOG” is plausible but unproven in the paper; similarly, the factorization of likelihood gain into tensor/matrix coherence “as defined in [22]” is asserted without definitions or derivations in the main text.
- The initial grammar is claimed to “lead to the maximal likelihood on the training data,” but this is not justified. Under Viterbi likelihood, other memorizing grammars with different probability allocations could yield the same or higher likelihood; a proof or a precise statement of conditions would help.

Algorithmic clarity and reproducibility
- Critical practical constraints are deferred to the supplement: exact pseudocode, how candidate relations are enumerated, how n (arity) and fragment sizes are bounded, beam width, number of random restarts, and how contexts are defined for CM. Without these, the method is not reproducible.
- Computational complexity is not analyzed. The assertion “a few minutes for 5000 samples with >10 atomic patterns” lacks details (machine specs, grammar size, candidates per iteration, number of iterations).
- For event data, the paper “changes top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes” at the end to decouple multiple events per video. This is ad hoc and changes the generative semantics post hoc. Using CYK is stated, but the grammar includes co-occurrence relations beyond simple string concatenation; it is unclear how CYK applies unless the model is reduced to CNF on string terminals, which is not described.

Experimental issues
- Event datasets are small (61 videos; 55/6 split). No variance/confidence intervals. F-measure computation details (matching policy, tolerance on boundaries) are not provided. The compared baselines are limited; no modern structured or neural baselines; no ablations (e.g., And-Or fragments vs separate And/Or searches; effect of unambiguity assumption).
- Synthetic image experiments: the true grammar and data generation process are not specified (grammar size, branching factors, parameter settings), making the reported F/KL improvements in Fig. 7 uninterpretable.
- Real image perplexity: smoothing method is unspecified; whether perplexity uses Viterbi or marginalized probabilities is unclear; the parsing/inference procedure used to evaluate probabilities is not stated. Only one baseline is reported.
- Hyperparameters: α is fixed to 0.5 for synthetic images; values for events and real images are not reported; no sensitivity analysis.

Presentation and formatting
- Numerous formatting/typographical problems hinder comprehension: broken/misplaced figures (Fig. 1 appears garbled, “initial gramm r”); repeated unlabeled symbols (“S S 2”); illegible equations; Table numbers referenced (Table 2) without content in the provided text; inconsistent notation (‖·‖ used for both size and cardinality); references with missing venue/year or malformed entries; inconsistent terminology (nodes vs rules vs fragments).
- The “unified” claim is not empirically supported on text data (only images and events are demonstrated).

Pros for acceptance
- Interesting idea to jointly induce compositions and alternatives via And-Or fragments.
- Attractive use of sufficient statistics for fast scoring (if the derivation is sound).
- Cross-domain applicability with some empirical gains.

Cons for acceptance
- Core probabilistic model (And-rule energies) and the posterior-gain derivation are insufficiently specified and contain errors; key assumptions (unambiguity) are strong and unenforced.
- Reproducibility is low: many essential algorithmic details are missing or relegated to the supplement.
- Experimental evaluation is limited, with small datasets, weak baselines, missing ablations, and unclear evaluation procedures.
- Significant presentation/formatting issues and incomplete references.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper introduces a potentially useful search primitive (And-Or fragments) and a promising scoring idea, but the current presentation has substantial technical gaps, ambiguous definitions, and experimental shortcomings. I encourage the authors to (i) precisely formalize And-rule probabilities/energies and the full likelihood; (ii) provide a rigorous, error-free derivation of the posterior-gain score (including context definition), discussing the impact of ambiguity; (iii) detail the algorithm and complexity; (iv) improve experiments with stronger baselines, ablations, and clearer evaluation protocols; and (v) fix formatting and references. With these addressed, the contribution could be significant.
