## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to unsupervised learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs), a powerful framework for modeling compositional and reconfigurable data like images and event sequences. The authors address the challenging problem of simultaneously learning both the grammar structure *and* its parameters from unannotated data.  Their method iteratively inserts "And-Or fragments" into a starting trivial grammar, optimizing a posterior probability that balances model complexity with likelihood. A key contribution is the unified learning of composition and reconfiguration, coupled with a theoretically grounded efficiency improvement using sufficient statistics. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach on both event grammar learning and image grammar learning, achieving competitive or superior performance to existing methods.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of stochastic grammar learning. They acknowledge and build upon prior work in both Natural Language Processing (NLP) – particularly structure learning from unlabeled data – and recent advances in applying SAGs to visual data.  They explicitly distinguish their work from approaches that fix the grammar structure and focus solely on parameter estimation (e.g., [11, 12, 10, 13]), and from methods that learn compositions and reconfigurations separately ([17, 18]). The connections to related concepts like sum-product networks [10] and hierarchical Dirichlet processes are also appropriately noted.  The references provided seem comprehensive and relevant.

**Strengths:**

* **Unified Framework:** The key strength is the unified approach to learning both composition (And-rules) and reconfiguration (Or-rules) via And-Or fragments. This is a significant improvement over previous methods, likely contributing to robustness and efficiency, especially with limited data.
* **Principled Optimization:**  Explicitly optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar, rather than relying on heuristics, is a strong point.  The use of a sparsity prior (penalizing grammar size) is a sensible approach to prevent overfitting.
* **Theoretical Justification:** Deriving sufficient statistics to efficiently compute the posterior gain demonstrates a strong theoretical understanding of the problem and a commitment to scalable algorithms. This is a substantial contribution.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both event and image grammars are well-designed and demonstrate competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods. The results on the more complex event grammar dataset (with action subtypes) are particularly convincing.
* **Generalizability:** The authors emphasize the data-agnostic nature of their formalization, making the approach applicable to a wide range of data types.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The use of diagrams (Figures 1 & 2) is helpful in illustrating the learning process and the And-Or fragment concept.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the authors mention efficiency improvements, the overall algorithm appears complex.  The paper doesn’t fully convey the practical challenges involved in implementing and tuning the various parameters (e.g., beam search width, the α hyperparameter). More detail on implementation choices and computational costs would be helpful.
* **Sensitivity to Initial Grammar:** The performance might be sensitive to the initial grammar, even though it's designed to generate all training samples. A brief discussion of how the algorithm behaves with variations in the initial grammar would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison:** While comparison to existing methods is present, the choice of baselines feels somewhat limited, particularly in the image grammar experiments.  Comparing against more recent and sophisticated image parsing techniques (even if not specifically grammar-based) would strengthen the claims of novelty and significance.
* **Lack of Qualitative Examples:** Showing qualitative examples of the learned grammars (e.g., visualizing the learned structures for specific images or event sequences) would make the results more intuitive and accessible.
* **Heuristic Aspects Remain:** Although the authors optimize the posterior, the greedy/beam search for And-Or fragments is still a heuristic. It’s not fully clear how the search space is pruned and whether the algorithm is prone to getting stuck in local optima.



**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded approach to a challenging problem.
* The unified learning framework and efficient optimization are significant contributions.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
* The work aligns well with the NIPS focus on machine learning and representation learning.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The algorithm is complex, and its practical implementation challenges aren’t fully addressed.
* The comparison to existing methods is somewhat limited.
* The reliance on heuristics (beam search) raises concerns about potential local optima.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. The authors have addressed a challenging problem with a novel and well-motivated approach, backed by theoretical analysis and promising empirical results.  While the algorithm's complexity and the limited scope of comparisons are minor concerns, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with a request for the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing more detail on implementation and computational aspects, expanding the comparison to more recent baselines, and including qualitative examples of the learned grammars.  



**Confidence:** 8/10
