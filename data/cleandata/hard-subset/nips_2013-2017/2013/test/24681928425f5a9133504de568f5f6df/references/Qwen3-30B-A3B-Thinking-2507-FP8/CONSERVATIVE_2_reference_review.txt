

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper proposes a new approach for unsupervised learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs), focusing on learning both grammar structure and parameters. The authors introduce "And-Or fragments" as a unified way to learn compositions and reconfigurations, claiming this approach is more efficient and robust than previous methods that learn these aspects separately. The paper presents theoretical formalization of SAGs, an algorithm for structure learning, and experimental validation on event and image grammars.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper provides a clear and unified formalization of Stochastic And-Or Grammars that is agnostic to data type.
2. The algorithm framework is well-structured and logically presented.
3. The experimental evaluation on event and image grammars shows reasonable results, with the proposed approach achieving comparable or better performance than two previous methods on the datasets tested.
4. The paper includes detailed theoretical derivations of the likelihood gain and prior gain for the learning procedure.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of comprehensive baseline comparison:** The paper compares only against two previous methods ([17, 18]), which are themselves not necessarily state-of-the-art. The authors fail to demonstrate how their approach compares to more established methods in the field, such as the widely used probabilistic context-free grammar learning approaches [11, 12] or sum-product network learning techniques [10]. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the claimed improvements are meaningful.

2. **Insufficient evidence for claimed advantages:** The paper asserts that the And-Or fragments approach is "more efficient and robust" than previous methods, but provides no ablation studies to isolate the contribution of this specific innovation. For instance, it doesn't compare the performance of the proposed method with versions that use only And-fragments or Or-fragments. The single experiment on the second dataset (where the method outperforms previous approaches) could be explained by the specific dataset characteristics rather than the proposed method.

3. **Limited experimental validation:** The paper only evaluates on two datasets (event grammars and image grammars), with only 5000 training samples total. The real-world image dataset has only 320 images, which is quite small for modern computer vision tasks. The synthetic dataset for images is also limited in scope. The paper does not provide any analysis of the scalability of the approach or its performance on larger, more challenging datasets.

4. **Theoretical justification gaps:** The paper relies on Viterbi likelihood approximation, which the authors acknowledge "can be interpreted as combining the standard likelihood with an unambiguity bias" [21]. However, the paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for why this approximation is appropriate for the specific problem of grammar learning, nor does it explore the trade-offs of using Viterbi likelihood versus standard likelihood.

5. **Questionable novelty:** The core idea of learning grammar structure through iterative refinement is not new [14, 15, 16]. The paper's main contribution appears to be the "And-Or fragments" formulation, but this is not clearly differentiated from previous work. The paper cites [17, 18] as recent work on And-Or grammars, but doesn't sufficiently explain how their approach differs in a meaningful way from these prior methods.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a coherent approach to an interesting problem, it fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of novelty and advantage over established methods. The experimental validation is limited in scope and lacks appropriate baselines for comparison. The paper makes strong claims about the efficiency and robustness of the proposed method without providing the necessary empirical evidence to back them up.

The authors would need to substantially strengthen their paper by:
1. Comparing against a broader set of established baselines (including more recent work in grammar learning)
2. Providing ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the And-Or fragments approach
3. Demonstrating the approach on larger, more challenging datasets
4. Providing clearer theoretical justification for the Viterbi likelihood approximation
5. More thoroughly differentiating their approach from previous work on grammar learning

Without these improvements, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence that this is a meaningful advancement in the field, and thus does not meet NIPS's standards for technical rigor and significance.
