

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper proposes an approach for unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, extending previous work on grammar learning for images and events. The authors introduce "And-Or fragments" as a unified mechanism for learning both compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules), claiming this approach is more efficient and robust than previous methods that learn these components separately. The paper presents a formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and an algorithm that iteratively improves the grammar's posterior probability by adding And-Or fragments.

**Strengths:** The paper provides a clear and unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type (text, images, events). The algorithm is well-structured, and the authors provide sufficient theoretical justification for their approach, particularly regarding the use of sufficient statistics to efficiently compute posterior gains. The experimental results on event and image grammar learning show promising results compared to previous approaches, with the proposed method achieving better performance on more complex datasets.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the paper's claims, I have significant concerns about the novelty, validation, and significance of the proposed approach. The core contribution—using "And-Or fragments" instead of separate And and Or fragments—appears to be an incremental extension of previous work rather than a substantial methodological advance. The paper fails to provide convincing evidence that this approach is fundamentally superior to existing methods. For instance, the claim that "reasonable And-Or fragments usually increase the posterior probability" lacks rigorous theoretical justification or empirical demonstration.

The experimental evaluation is notably limited. The authors compare against two previous methods on two datasets (event and image grammars), but the baselines are not state-of-the-art and do not include more recent approaches. The paper claims "comparable or better performance" but provides minimal quantitative evidence of the magnitude of improvement. The results in Table 2 show only modest improvements on the first dataset and a slight advantage on the second, which is insufficient to justify the novelty claim.

More critically, the paper does not adequately address the fundamental question: Why is this approach better than simply learning And and Or fragments separately? The authors acknowledge that learning Or-fragments alone is problematic but don't provide strong evidence that their unified approach overcomes these issues. The paper lacks ablation studies demonstrating the necessity of the And-Or fragment approach versus learning the components separately.

The paper also lacks thorough discussion of limitations. For example, the authors note that their approach assumes each training sample consists of a single pattern generated from the target grammar, but they don't discuss how this limitation affects performance on real-world data where samples often contain multiple patterns. The paper also doesn't address how the method scales to larger datasets or more complex grammars.

**Overall assessment:** While the paper presents a technically sound approach that builds on established work in grammar learning, the contribution appears incremental rather than groundbreaking. The experimental validation is insufficient to support the paper's claims of significant improvement over previous methods. The paper lacks the rigorous comparison, ablation studies, and theoretical justification required to demonstrate that the proposed approach represents a meaningful advance in the field.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. The paper's main contribution is not sufficiently novel or validated to warrant publication at NIPS. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their experimental validation, provide more comprehensive comparisons with recent baselines, and offer stronger theoretical justification for their approach to demonstrate that it represents a significant methodological advance rather than a minor extension of existing work.

**Pro-con list:**
- *Pro:* Clear formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars; well-structured algorithm; some promising experimental results
- *Con:* Insufficient experimental validation; limited comparison to recent baselines; unconvincing justification for core innovation; lacks ablation studies; minimal discussion of limitations
