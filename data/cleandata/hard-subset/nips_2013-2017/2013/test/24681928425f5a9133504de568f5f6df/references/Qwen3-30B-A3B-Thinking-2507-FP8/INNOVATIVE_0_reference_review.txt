

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper presents a novel approach to unsupervised structure learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAOGs), which are powerful models for capturing compositional structures in diverse data types (text, images, events). The core innovation is the introduction of "And-Or fragments" that simultaneously learn compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules) in a unified manner, addressing a fundamental limitation of prior work that treated these as separate learning problems.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) a formalization of SAOGs agnostic to data type, (2) an approach that explicitly optimizes the posterior probability of both grammar structure and parameters, (3) a unified learning framework using And-Or fragments that avoids the pitfalls of separate composition/reconfiguration learning, and (4) empirical validation showing competitive or superior performance on event and image grammar learning tasks.

The technical approach is elegant and well-motivated. The authors start with a trivial grammar (one rule per training sample) and iteratively add And-Or fragments that maximize the posterior probability. Crucially, they derive efficient sufficient statistics to compute the posterior gain without full re-parsing of training data, making the approach computationally feasible. The And-Or fragment concept is particularly clever - by learning a new And-node with multiple Or-nodes (each representing alternative configurations), the approach systematically covers multiple compositions while avoiding the data scarcity issues that plague separate And-fragment learning.

The paper stands out from previous work on grammar learning (e.g., [14-18]) by recognizing that composition and reconfiguration should be learned jointly rather than separately. Prior approaches either restrict the grammar to simple decompositions (limiting expressiveness) or learn these components separately (leading to error propagation and redundancy). The authors convincingly argue that their unified approach is more efficient, robust to data scarcity, and leads to more compact grammars.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors demonstrate their approach on two challenging domains: event grammars (learning from human activity videos) and image grammars (learning from animal face sketches). On the more complex event dataset (where actions are divided into subtypes), their approach significantly outperforms prior methods. The image grammar experiments also show clear improvements over the state-of-the-art [17]. The evaluation metrics (F-measure, perplexity) are appropriate and well-explained.

What makes this paper particularly strong is its conceptual novelty. While many papers in NIPS focus on incremental improvements to existing methods, this work proposes a fundamentally different way to approach grammar learning. The And-Or fragment concept is simple yet powerful, and the paper demonstrates its effectiveness across multiple domains. The authors successfully show that this unified approach isn't just a theoretical curiosity but has practical benefits.

The paper is well-written with clear figures (Fig 1, 2) that effectively illustrate the learning process and the concept of And-Or fragments. The formalization of SAOGs is rigorous and general, making the approach broadly applicable.

Potential weaknesses are minor and don't detract from the core contribution. The paper assumes grammar unambiguity, which might be limiting for some real-world applications. The evaluation is limited to two domains (though the formalization suggests broader applicability). The comparison to very recent deep learning-based grammar approaches would strengthen the evaluation.

However, these are minor concerns given the paper's significant conceptual contribution. The authors have made a clear and compelling case for their novel approach to grammar structure learning, and the empirical results support their claims. This is precisely the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS should prioritize - one that fundamentally rethinks how we approach a problem rather than incrementally improving existing methods.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field, presents a well-motivated and theoretically sound approach, and provides strong empirical validation. The unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments represents a significant conceptual advance over previous work.

**Recommendation: Accept**

*Strengths*: 
- Conceptually novel approach to grammar structure learning
- Unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations
- Clear theoretical foundation with efficient implementation
- Strong empirical validation across multiple domains
- Generalizable to diverse data types (text, images, events)

*Weaknesses*:
- Limited to two application domains in evaluation
- Assumes grammar unambiguity
- Could compare with more recent deep learning grammar approaches

The paper is a strong contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling and structure learning, and its novel approach to grammar learning has the potential to influence future work in multiple domains.
