- Proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars using "And-Or fragments" that unify And- and Or-rule learning.  
- Explicitly optimizes the grammar posterior and efficiently computes likelihood gains via sufficient statistics.  
- Demonstrates competitive or superior results on event grammars (human activity videos) and image grammars (animal face sketches).  
- Builds on prior grammar structure learning and extends recent And-Or grammar work, unifying composition and reconfiguration learning.  
- Strength: technically sound with clear theoretical derivations for posterior, likelihood, and prior gains.  
- Strength: experimental design is thorough, comparing to relevant baselines on synthetic and real datasets using F-measure and perplexity.  
- Strength: exceptionally clear and well-organized presentation with formal definitions, illustrative figures, and reproducible supplementary material.  
- Strength: original—And-Or fragments offer a meaningful advance that addresses data scarcity and unifies previous separate procedures.  
- Strength: significant—provides a practical, broadly applicable approach to compositional structure learning with demonstrated empirical benefits.  
- Weakness: limited discussion and analysis of scalability to larger datasets and greater model complexity.  
- Weakness: depends on Viterbi likelihood (assumes grammar unambiguity); handling ambiguous grammars is not addressed.  
- Weakness: image evaluation relies only on perplexity; lacks qualitative analyses or visualizations of learned grammars.  
- Recommendation: accept—strong contribution that meets NIPS standards and is of broad interest.  
- Arguments for acceptance: clear technical contribution, solid theory, practical implementation, comprehensive evaluation, novel unified approach, and excellent writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: insufficient scalability analysis, reliance on unambiguity assumption, and limited image-evaluation metrics.
