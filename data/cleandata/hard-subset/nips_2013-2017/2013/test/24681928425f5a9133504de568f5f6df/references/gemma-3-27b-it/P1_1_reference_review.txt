## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to unsupervised learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs). Unlike previous work that often relies on manually specified structures or separate learning of composition and reconfiguration, the authors propose a method to learn *both* the structure and parameters of a SAG directly from unlabeled data.  Their approach iteratively inserts new “And-Or fragments” into an initially trivial grammar, optimizing a posterior probability that balances model complexity and data likelihood.  The key contribution lies in a unified learning framework that combines composition and reconfiguration, along with a computationally efficient optimization procedure leveraging sufficient statistics. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on both event grammar and image grammar learning tasks, achieving competitive or superior performance compared to existing techniques.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on a rich history of work in stochastic grammars, originally developed for natural language processing (NLP).  It acknowledges and positions itself within the field of unsupervised grammar induction, referencing key works in NLP ([11, 12, 14, 15, 16]) and image modeling ([1, 3, 10, 13, 17, 18]).  The authors appropriately highlight how their approach distinguishes itself from prior methods by learning structure *and* parameters jointly and by utilizing And-Or fragments for more robust and efficient learning, particularly addressing limitations when dealing with scarce or ambiguous data.  The connection to Sum-Product Networks [10] is also well-placed.  They correctly cite relevant work on structure learning like [17, 18] and demonstrate improvement over these baselines.

**Strengths:**

* **Unified Framework:** The core strength of the paper lies in the elegant unification of composition and reconfiguration through the use of And-Or fragments. This is a conceptually strong idea that addresses a key limitation of previous methods.
* **Principled Optimization:** The approach focuses on maximizing a well-defined posterior probability, incorporating a sparsity prior to prevent overfitting.  The efficient computation of this posterior gain using sufficient statistics is a significant technical contribution.
* **Generality:** The authors successfully abstract away from specific data types (text, images, events), providing a general framework for learning compositional structures. This is highlighted in the formalization of SAGs.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on both event and image data demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results show improvements over existing methods, especially in the more challenging event grammar learning task.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the key concepts and algorithms.  The figures are helpful in illustrating the learning process and grammar structures.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Relations:** While the authors mention the possibility of specifying different types of relations in And-rules, the implementation and learning of these relations are not thoroughly discussed. How are these relations defined and parameterized?  More details on this aspect would be beneficial.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the sparsity hyperparameter α is not extensively explored.  A more thorough sensitivity analysis would provide greater confidence in the robustness of the approach.
* **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons are made to existing methods, the field of unsupervised grammar learning is relatively sparse.  A broader comparison, perhaps including methods from different areas like Bayesian nonparametrics or hierarchical clustering, would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Scalability:** The authors state the algorithm runs "reasonably fast" on moderate-sized datasets.  However, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and scalability of the approach, especially for very large datasets or high-dimensional data, would be valuable.
* **Incomplete Details:** Certain aspects of the implementation, such as the Viterbi likelihood approximation and the smoothing procedure used for perplexity calculation, could benefit from more detailed explanation.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem.
* The unified framework for learning composition and reconfiguration is a significant contribution.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on diverse datasets.
* The clarity of writing and presentation are commendable.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  The limitations regarding the complexity of relations, hyperparameter sensitivity, and scalability warrant further investigation.
*  The comparison to existing methods could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. The proposed approach is technically sound, conceptually elegant, and empirically validated. While some areas could benefit from further investigation and clarification, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I believe this paper is suitable for acceptance at NIPS.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly regarding the details of relation learning and hyperparameter sensitivity).
