### Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

**Summary**

This paper presents a novel, unsupervised approach for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs). The authors propose a general, data-agnostic formalization of AOGs and an iterative, bottom-up learning algorithm that optimizes the posterior probability of the grammar given the data. The core contribution is the concept of learning "And-Or fragments," which unifies the discovery of compositional structures (And-rules) and configurable alternatives (Or-rules). This contrasts with prior work that often learns these components separately, which can be inefficient and error-prone. The algorithm starts with a trivial grammar that overfits the training data and greedily adds the And-Or fragment that yields the highest gain in the grammar's posterior probability. A key technical contribution is an efficient method to calculate this gain using sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices), avoiding costly re-parsing at each step. The method is validated on challenging tasks of learning event grammars from activity videos and image grammars from animal faces, demonstrating significant performance improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Conceptual Elegance**: The central idea of introducing "And-Or fragments" as the atomic unit of structure learning is highly original and compelling. It provides a principled solution to a key challenge in grammar induction: how to jointly discover compositional patterns and their variations. This unified search is more robust to data scarcity and ambiguity than searching for And-rules and Or-rules separately, as persuasively argued by the authors.

2.  **Principled Objective and Efficient Optimization**: The work is well-grounded in a Bayesian framework, aiming to maximize the posterior probability `P(G|X)`. The use of a sparsity prior to encourage compact grammars is standard and effective. The derivation of the posterior gain, factorizing it into likelihood and prior components that can be computed efficiently from sufficient statistics, is a significant technical strength. This makes the greedy search procedure practical for reasonably large datasets.

3.  **Generality**: The proposed AOG formalism and learning algorithm are data-agnostic. The authors successfully demonstrate this by applying the exact same framework to two very different domains: temporal event sequences and spatial image patches. This highlights the power and flexibility of the approach as a general tool for unsupervised structure discovery.

4.  **Strong Empirical Performance**: The experimental results are convincing. The proposed method significantly outperforms relevant prior work on both synthetic and real-world datasets. For instance, the perplexity on the real animal faces dataset is nearly halved compared to the baseline [17], which is a substantial improvement. The ablation-like comparison of the three methods for event grammar learning provides valuable insight into the benefits of learning directly from atomic actions and discovering relations simultaneously.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors**

1.  **Clarity on the Search Procedure**: While the paper explains *what* is being optimized (posterior gain of an And-Or fragment), it is less clear on *how* the best fragment is found in practice. The paper mentions constructing n-gram tensors and searching for sub-tensors. The search space for such fragments (over different n, different relations, and all possible subsets of terminals/non-terminals) seems combinatorially large. More details on how this search is constrained or implemented would be crucial for reproducibility and for understanding the computational complexity. Is the search limited to contiguous elements in the data? Are there heuristics used to propose candidate fragments?

2.  **Impact of Approximations**: The method relies on the Viterbi likelihood, which is a common and practical approximation but ignores parsing ambiguity. The derivation of the posterior gain further assumes "grammar unambiguity." A brief discussion on the potential limitations of these assumptions would strengthen the paper. For example, in domains with highly ambiguous structures, could this lead the greedy search to suboptimal grammars?

3.  **Scalability**: The paper reports that the algorithm is fast on the tested datasets. However, the complexity appears to depend heavily on the number of terminal symbols, the length of data samples, and the size `n` of the n-grams considered. How would the approach scale to problems with much larger vocabularies (e.g., natural language) or higher-dimensional data (e.g., high-resolution images where the number of potential patches is vast)?

4.  **Handling of Multiple Events**: In the event grammar experiments, the late-stage modification of changing top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes to handle multiple independent events seems ad-hoc. This modification alters the grammar structure and the generative probability of the data. How does this final step align with the posterior optimization framework that was used to build the grammar up to that point? Does it invalidate the posterior gain calculations made during learning?

**Detailed Comments**

-   The factorization of the likelihood gain into tensor/matrix coherence measures is an interesting connection and provides a nice intuition for what the algorithm is optimizing for: patterns that exhibit context-free behavior both internally and externally.
-   The experiments on the synthesized event dataset are well-designed, showing that the method is robust to the kind of noise/variation expected from unsupervised feature clustering (e.g., multiple clusters for the same action).
-   The outperformance of "method 3" over "method 2" in the event experiments strongly supports the core claim that joint learning of different compositions (here, "co-occurring" and "followed-by") is superior to a staged approach.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. The proposed method for learning And-Or grammars is novel, principled, and demonstrably effective across different domains. The core idea of using And-Or fragments is a genuine advance over prior art. While there are minor weaknesses regarding the clarity of the search procedure and the justification for certain modeling choices, the strengths in originality, technical quality, and empirical results are substantial. This work is likely to be influential and inspire further research in structured probabilistic modeling. I strongly recommend acceptance.
