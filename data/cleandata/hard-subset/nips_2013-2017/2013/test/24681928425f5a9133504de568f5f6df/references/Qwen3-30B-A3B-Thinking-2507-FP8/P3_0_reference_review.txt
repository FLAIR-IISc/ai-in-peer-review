

# Peer Review: "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

## Summary
This paper proposes an unsupervised approach to learn both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars, which model compositional and reconfigurable patterns across various data types (text, images, events). The key innovation is the introduction of "And-Or fragments" that simultaneously learn compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules), addressing limitations in prior work that learned these components separately. The approach starts from a trivial grammar and iteratively adds fragments to optimize the posterior probability of the grammar, using efficient sufficient statistics to compute gains.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars as agnostic to data types is well-motivated and provides a unifying framework for applications across domains. The paper correctly identifies the key limitation of previous approaches (separate learning of compositions and reconfigurations) and offers a principled solution through And-Or fragments.

2. **Algorithmic Innovation**: The derivation of sufficient statistics for efficiently computing the posterior gain is technically sound. The insight that And-Or fragments systematically cover multiple compositions alleviates data scarcity issues common in And-fragment-only approaches is particularly valuable.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments on event grammars and image grammars demonstrate clear advantages over prior work. The results on the synthetic dataset (Figure 7) and real animal face dataset (Table 3) show consistent improvements in F-measure and perplexity, respectively. The analysis of the "subtypes" experiment (Section 4.1) provides insightful evidence for the approach's robustness.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical Gaps**: The paper lacks sufficient theoretical grounding for key claims. While the posterior gain formula is presented, the assumptions (e.g., grammar unambiguity) are not rigorously justified. The connection between the derived coherence measures and the actual grammar quality needs deeper exploration. The supplementary material would be essential for full evaluation, but it's not available for review.

2. **Experimental Limitations**: 
   - The comparison with prior work is limited to two methods ([17, 18]), but the most recent and relevant work on And-Or grammars (e.g., [17] is from 2013, while the authors cite [18] from 2011) should be compared against more recent approaches.
   - The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the unified And-Or fragment learning versus the posterior optimization approach.
   - The Viterbi likelihood approximation (3.1) is not adequately justified for cases where grammars are ambiguous, which is common in real-world data.

3. **Implementation Details**: The computational complexity of the algorithm is not discussed, and the claim that "our prototype implementation can finish within a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples" lacks context. How does this compare to prior methods? The paper doesn't provide runtime comparisons.

4. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims "our approach is capable of learning from different types of data" but only evaluates on event and image data. The generality claim would be strengthened by at least a brief mention of text data experiments.

## Comparison to Related Work

The paper correctly positions itself against prior work on structure learning (e.g., [14-16] for NLP, [17-18] for And-Or grammars). However, it doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from [17], which also uses a fragment-based approach for image grammars. The key difference appears to be the unified learning of And-Or fragments versus the sequential learning approach in [17], but this distinction isn't clearly articulated.

## Overall Assessment

This is a solid contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning for compositional models. The unified And-Or fragment approach addresses a genuine limitation in prior work, and the empirical results are convincing. However, the paper would benefit from addressing the theoretical gaps and experimental limitations noted above.

**Quality**: 7/10 - Technically sound but with theoretical gaps. Claims are partially supported by experiments but lack sufficient depth.

**Clarity**: 8/10 - Well-organized and clearly written, but some key algorithmic details are missing.

**Originality**: 7/10 - The unified fragment approach is novel, but the core idea builds directly on [17] and [18].

**Significance**: 7/10 - The approach has practical value for compositional modeling, but the scope is limited to specific domains.

## Recommendation

**Accept with minor revisions**. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning for compositional models. The main weaknesses are the theoretical gaps and limited experimental comparisons, which could be addressed with minor revisions. The authors should:

1. Clarify the theoretical assumptions and provide more justification for the Viterbi approximation
2. Compare against more recent approaches to And-Or grammar learning
3. Include a runtime analysis and ablation study
4. Provide more context for the computational claims

The paper is well-positioned to advance the state of the art in compositional modeling, and with these revisions, it would be a strong contribution to NIPS. The unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments represents a significant step forward from previous approaches that treated these as separate learning problems.
