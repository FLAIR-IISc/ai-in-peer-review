This paper proposes an unsupervised learning approach for Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs), aiming to learn both the structure and parameters of the grammar from unannotated data. SAGs, traditionally used in NLP but extended to images and events, offer a compositional and reconfigurable framework for modeling complex data. The core contribution lies in a novel algorithm that iteratively inserts "And-Or fragments" – simultaneously learning compositions and reconfigurations – coupled with an efficient posterior probability optimization using sufficient statistics. This work builds upon previous structure learning methods for both natural language grammars and, more recently, image/event grammars, addressing limitations like the need for manual structure specification or separate learning of compositions and reconfigurations.

**Strengths:**

* **Unified Framework:** The approach offers a principled and unified way to learn both the structure *and* parameters of SAGs, a significant advancement over methods relying on pre-defined structures.
* **And-Or Fragments:** The introduction of And-Or fragments is a clever idea, promoting efficient and robust learning by jointly modeling compositions and reconfigurations, particularly helpful with scarce or ambiguous data. This is a key differentiating factor from existing work.
* **Posterior Probability Optimization:** Explicitly optimizing the posterior probability, rather than relying on heuristics, is a strong theoretical foundation for the learning process. The use of sufficient statistics enhances computational efficiency.
* **Generality:** The formalization of SAGs as data-type agnostic is a valuable contribution, allowing the same learning approach to be applied to diverse data types (text, images, events).
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results on both event and image grammars demonstrate competitive, and in some cases superior, performance compared to existing approaches.  The ablation study with the different application methods of the learning algorithm (Methods 2 & 3) provides insightful understanding of the design choices.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, with a good explanation of the core concepts and the proposed algorithm. Figures are helpful in illustrating the process.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Algorithm:** While the authors mention efficiency gains, the algorithm seems relatively complex. The description of the likelihood gain computation, while theoretically sound, could benefit from more intuition or simplification.  A step-by-step example illustrating the gain calculation would be helpful.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the sparsity parameter *α* and the specifics of the greedy/beam search could significantly impact the results. The paper doesn’t provide enough details on how these parameters were selected or tuned, and a sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison:**  While comparisons are made to existing work, they are somewhat limited. Comparing against more recent or state-of-the-art methods, where applicable, would strengthen the evaluation.  Specifically for image grammars, exploring more sophisticated quantization methods or considering the impact of different atomic pattern representations would be valuable.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of grammar unambiguity, while justified for efficiency, could be a limitation in certain applications.  Discussing the potential impact of this assumption and exploring methods to relax it would be worthwhile.
* **Scalability Concerns:** Although experiments are performed on reasonably sized datasets, the scalability of the approach to very large datasets or complex grammars is not explicitly addressed. A discussion of potential bottlenecks and mitigation strategies would be helpful.

**Originality:**

The paper presents a novel combination of ideas. While the use of grammars for unsupervised learning isn’t new, the specific approach of simultaneously learning structure and parameters through And-Or fragments and posterior optimization is original. The generalization of SAGs to be data-type agnostic is also a meaningful contribution. The related work section adequately covers relevant literature.

**Significance:**

The work has the potential to be significant.  Unsupervised structure learning is a crucial challenge in many areas of machine learning. A method that can learn both the structure and parameters of expressive models like SAGs, while addressing data scarcity and ambiguity, would be highly valuable. The applications to event and image grammars demonstrate the practicality of the approach, and the generality of the framework suggests wider applicability.  The unification of composition and reconfiguration learning is a key step towards more robust and interpretable models.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to unsupervised structure learning.
* Significant improvement over existing methods in the experimental settings.
* Potential for broad applicability across different data types.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Algorithm complexity and potential hyperparameter sensitivity.
* Limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
* Scalability concerns for very large datasets.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to unsupervised structure learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars. The strengths of the work – particularly the introduction of And-Or fragments and the posterior probability optimization – outweigh the weaknesses.  While further investigation into scalability, hyperparameter sensitivity, and comparison to more recent methods would be beneficial, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:**

Accept with minor revisions. Specifically, I recommend:

1.  Providing a more intuitive explanation of the likelihood gain computation, potentially with a worked example.
2.  Discussing the sensitivity of the approach to hyperparameters and providing a brief sensitivity analysis.
3.  Including a discussion of the scalability of the approach and potential bottlenecks.
4.  If possible, comparing against more recent or state-of-the-art methods in the experimental domains.
