

# Review: "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach for unsupervised structure learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs), which are powerful models for capturing compositional and reconfigurable patterns across various data types. The authors propose a unified framework that learns both grammar structure and parameters by optimizing the posterior probability, using "And-Or fragments" to simultaneously learn compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules). The method is evaluated on event grammars for video action recognition and image grammars for object scene modeling, showing significant improvements over previous approaches.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with a solid theoretical foundation. The authors provide clear derivations of the likelihood gain and prior gain for adding And-Or fragments, which enables efficient greedy search. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with both synthetic and real datasets, and the results consistently show improvements over baselines (SZ [17] for image grammars, previous methods for event grammars). The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the Viterbi approximation for likelihood, and provide a reasonable justification for this simplification. The algorithm runs efficiently (within minutes on 5000 samples), and the prototype implementation is well-described.

One minor weakness is the lack of detailed computational complexity analysis, though the practical efficiency is demonstrated. The paper also doesn't fully explore how the Viterbi approximation affects theoretical guarantees, which could be a valuable addition for readers interested in the theoretical properties of the approach.

## Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with excellent use of figures (Figures 1-3) to illustrate the core concepts of grammar learning and And-Or fragments. The formal definition of SAGs is precise and accessible to readers familiar with grammatical models. The algorithm description is clear, though the derivation of sufficient statistics might be challenging for readers without strong probabilistic modeling background. The writing is generally clear, and the paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

The authors could improve accessibility by adding a brief intuitive explanation of the sufficient statistics calculation before presenting the formal derivation. A small example showing how the context matrix (Figure 2c) is constructed would also help readers understand this key component.

## Originality
This work makes a significant contribution to unsupervised structure learning of grammars. While previous approaches have focused on learning compositions or reconfigurations separately (e.g., [14-16] for natural language, [17-18] for And-Or grammars), the authors' key innovation is the unified "And-Or fragment" approach that simultaneously learns both aspects. The paper clearly articulates how this differs from prior work in Section 3.3, with a helpful comparison table. The formalization of SAGs as agnostic to data type (text, images, events) is also a valuable contribution that broadens the applicability of the framework.

The paper properly cites related work, including recent NIPS papers on sum-product networks (which are closely related to And-Or grammars [10]), though it could better connect to these works in the discussion.

## Significance
The paper addresses an important problem in unsupervised learning of structured representations with clear applications in computer vision and video analysis. The demonstrated improvements over previous methods on both image and event grammars suggest the approach could be widely applicable across multiple domains. The unified framework for grammar learning is a significant advance that could benefit many areas of machine learning.

The results on the synthetic and real datasets provide strong evidence that the approach advances the state of the art. The paper's ability to handle both spatial structures (images) and temporal structures (events) with the same framework is particularly noteworthy, suggesting broader applicability than previous work.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a strong contribution to the field with a technically sound approach, clear presentation, and significant empirical results. The unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments is a novel and valuable idea that advances the state of the art in unsupervised grammar learning.

## Minor Suggestions
1. Include a brief ablation study showing the contribution of each component of the proposed method (e.g., the And-Or fragment vs. separate And/Or learning).
2. Provide a more explicit connection to recent NIPS work on sum-product networks (which are directly related to the And-Or grammar framework).
3. Add a short discussion of the limitations of the Viterbi approximation for likelihood.

## Conclusion
This paper presents a technically sound, novel, and significant contribution to unsupervised structure learning. The unified And-Or fragment approach offers a more efficient and robust way to learn grammatical structures compared to previous methods, with demonstrated improvements across multiple domains. The work would be of substantial interest to the NIPS community and makes a strong contribution to the field of structured representation learning.
