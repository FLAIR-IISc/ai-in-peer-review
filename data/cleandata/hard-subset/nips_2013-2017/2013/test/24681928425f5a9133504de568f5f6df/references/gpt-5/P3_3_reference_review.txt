Summary
The paper tackles unsupervised structure and parameter learning for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs). It proposes: (i) a unified, context-free AOG formalization agnostic to modality (text, images, events); (ii) a bottom-up structure learning algorithm that adds And-Or fragments—each comprising a new And-node, several new Or-nodes, and their rules—so that composition (And) and reconfiguration (Or) are learned jointly; (iii) a posterior objective with a sparsity prior over grammar size and Viterbi likelihood; and (iv) an efficient scoring of candidate fragments via sufficient statistics computed from data: an n-gram tensor over configurations and a context matrix, yielding a closed-form likelihood-gain approximation and simple MLEs for Or-rule probabilities. Search uses greedy/beam strategies with random restarts; a prior term penalizes fragment size and rewards reductions. Experiments on event grammars (human activity videos) and image grammars (synthetic and real animal faces) show improvements over prior structure learning work for AOGs.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to unsupervised grammar induction in NLP (Baker, Klein & Manning; Viterbi training benefits per Spitkovsky et al.), Bayesian model merging (Stolcke & Omohundro), and ADIOS (Solan et al.). It builds on AOGs for images and events (Zhu et al.; Si & Zhu), and is related to SPNs (Poon & Domingos), particularly decomposable SPNs, by using context-free decompositions with Or choices. The main departure from prior AOG structure learning (e.g., Si & Zhu; Si et al.) is the joint induction of And-Or fragments rather than separately mining compositions and alternatives, and the explicit posterior scoring with sufficient statistics. The tensor/matrix “coherence” interpretation links to biclustering literature.

Strengths
- Idea: Joint And-Or fragment induction is elegant and addresses data sparsity and redundancy issues seen when learning And and Or structure separately. The sufficiency-statistics view makes the search practical.
- Objective: Clear Bayesian objective with a sparsity prior; Viterbi likelihood and unambiguity bias are reasonable and supported by NLP literature.
- Generality: The formalization and algorithm are modality-agnostic; the same machinery is applied to events and images, with different relation types.
- Efficiency: The posterior-gain formula avoids repeated re-parsing, and the algorithm appears to run in minutes on nontrivial datasets.
- Empirics: On events, the grounded approach (learning co-occurrence and followed-by jointly) outperforms baselines, especially under artificially increased ambiguity. On images, significant gains over [17] on both synthetic (F1, KL) and real (perplexity) data.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumptions and theory:
  - The likelihood-gain derivation relies on “assuming grammar unambiguity.” This is strong for realistic AOGs (ambiguity is common). How sensitive is the scoring to ambiguity? Any correction or bounds when ambiguity exists?
  - Viterbi likelihood and duplicate-sample aggregation: the factor that adjusts likelihood based on identical samples after reductions is unusual; please justify more formally how this arises from the model definition S → Ai with uniform probabilities and whether this introduces dataset-composition artifacts (e.g., sensitivity to duplicated training instances).
  - No guarantees are provided on search quality (e.g., monotonic posterior increase per fragment is claimed to be “usually” true). Can you prove each accepted fragment increases the posterior under your approximations? Any consistency results or identifiability conditions?
  - The claim that any context-free AOG can be constructed by adding And-Or fragments is plausible but not shown; a sketch or citation would help.
- Parameter learning:
  - Or-rule probabilities are given by simple counts, but And-rule “energy terms” are said to be specified by relations; how are these parameters learned or set? Are there continuous parameters (e.g., relative-position Gaussians) and is there any estimation or regularization? If And-rule energies are fixed templates, this is a limitation that should be explicit.
  - The coding lengths sa and so in the prior are hyperparameters; how chosen, and how sensitive are results to them and to α? An ablation or sensitivity plot would help.
- Clarity and reproducibility:
  - The key derivations (likelihood-gain factorization into tensor/context coherence, definitions of “context,” construction of RD, CM) are deferred to the supplement. The main paper would benefit from a concise theorem with precise definitions and complexity claims.
  - Search details are under-specified: candidate generation (n values, relation types), pruning, beam width, random restarts, and stopping criteria. Provide pseudocode or a flow diagram in the main text.
  - Inference for images is not described (how is parsing done with spatial relations? any dynamic programming or heuristic search?). For events, you switch top-level And to Or at the end and use CYK; is an analogous trick used for images?
  - Perplexity smoothing on real images: which smoothing method and parameters?
- Empirical scope:
  - Baselines are limited. For NLP-style induction, adaptor grammars/HDP-PCFGs or SPN structure learners would be informative comparators given the stated generality; for events, recent structured sequence models; for images, other compositional models/SPN learners.
  - Dataset scale is modest (61 videos; 320 images). It would help to test on larger or noisier settings and include robustness to detection noise.
  - Missing ablations: And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or fragments; effect of the context-matrix term; impact of enforcing co-occurrence-first (method 2) vs joint (method 3); effect of the unambiguity assumption being violated.

Significance and originality
The joint And-Or fragment induction with efficient posterior-gain scoring is a neat and potentially impactful idea for structure learning in compositional models, bridging grammar induction and SPN-like architectures across modalities. While the empirical evaluation is not broad, the method improves over strong prior AOG learners and could spur further work. The paper would be substantially strengthened by clarifying the theoretical assumptions and by richer ablations/baselines.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, but several core technical definitions are too terse for reproduction without the supplement. Figures illustrating fragments and tensors are helpful but could be simplified; some captions appear garbled.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and practically useful joint fragment learning
- Clear Bayesian objective; efficient sufficient-statistics–based scoring
- Cross-domain applicability demonstrated; good empirical gains
- Potential to influence compositional model induction beyond AOGs

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi approximations without analysis
- Incomplete specification of And-rule parameter learning and inference
- Limited baselines, small datasets, and missing ablations
- Several crucial derivations and algorithmic details relegated to supplement

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on clarifying the likelihood-gain derivation, the handling of ambiguity, and And-rule parameterization, and adding at least an ablation on fragment types and a sensitivity analysis to α, sa, so. My confidence is moderate.
