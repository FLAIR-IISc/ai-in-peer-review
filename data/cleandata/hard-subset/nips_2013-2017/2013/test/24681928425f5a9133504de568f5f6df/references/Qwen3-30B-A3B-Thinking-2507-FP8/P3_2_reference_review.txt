

# Peer Review: "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper presents a unified approach for unsupervised structure learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAOGs), addressing a significant gap in the literature where most prior work either fixes grammar structure or learns compositions and reconfigurations separately. The authors formalize SAOGs in a data-type-agnostic manner and propose a novel learning algorithm that iteratively inserts And-Or fragments to optimize the posterior probability of the grammar.

**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

The paper builds on previous work in stochastic grammars for natural language [8,9], sum-product networks [10], and And-Or grammars for images [1,3] and events [7]. It correctly identifies that most prior approaches to grammar learning either fix the grammar structure (e.g., [10,13]) or learn compositions and reconfigurations separately [14-18]. The authors position their work as extending [17,18] by introducing a unified learning framework that addresses the limitations of previous approaches, particularly regarding data scarcity and ambiguity.

**Strengths**

The paper's main strengths are its formalization of SAOGs as agnostic to data type, the efficient computation of posterior gain using sufficient statistics (via n-gram tensors and context matrices), and the empirical validation across two distinct domains (event and image grammars). The theoretical foundation for the posterior gain computation is sound, and the paper effectively demonstrates how the proposed And-Or fragments unify the learning of compositions and reconfigurations.

The experimental results are convincing, showing competitive or better performance than prior approaches on both synthetic and real datasets. The authors' analysis of the second event dataset (with subtype divisions) is particularly insightful, showing how their approach avoids learning erroneous compositions when faced with ambiguous data. The scalability demonstration (5000 samples on a desktop in minutes) is also a significant practical contribution.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper is technically sound, it has several notable weaknesses. First, the comparison with the most recent relevant work is insufficient. The paper cites [17,18] but does not compare with more recent approaches like those in ICML 2023 or NeurIPS 2022 that also address grammar learning, such as the work by [22] on neural grammars. The paper claims to "improve upon" [17,18], but without direct comparison to more recent methods, this claim lacks sufficient support.

Second, the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why the unified And-Or fragment approach is significantly better than separate learning of compositions and reconfigurations. While the authors argue it's more robust to data scarcity, they don't provide direct ablation studies showing the comparative performance of the unified approach versus separate approaches on the same datasets.

Third, the theoretical analysis of the posterior gain computation is somewhat superficial. While the paper references supplementary material for the full derivation, the main paper lacks sufficient detail about the assumptions and limitations of the coherence measures they use. For instance, how do these measures behave when the training data contains noise or when the true grammar is not context-free?

Fourth, the empirical evaluation on real-world data is limited. The image grammar experiments use only one real dataset (animal faces), and the event grammar experiments rely on synthetic data with known structure. The paper would be stronger with more diverse real-world applications.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a solid technical contribution with clear theoretical foundations and promising empirical results. The unified learning approach for compositions and reconfigurations is a meaningful improvement over previous work, and the efficient computation of posterior gain using sufficient statistics is a valuable technical contribution.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1) More thorough comparison with recent literature on grammar learning
2) Additional ablation studies demonstrating the superiority of the unified approach
3) More comprehensive real-world validation beyond the single image dataset
4) Deeper theoretical analysis of the coherence measures

**Recommendation**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning, with clear theoretical foundations and strong empirical validation. While the weaknesses noted above are important, they are not fundamental flaws that undermine the paper's contribution. With the suggested revisions (particularly the additional comparisons and ablation studies), the paper would be an excellent addition to NeurIPS.

**Key Points for Revision:**

1. Add a comparison with recent grammar learning approaches from NeurIPS/ICML 2022-2023
2. Include ablation studies showing performance differences between unified and separate learning approaches
3. Expand the discussion of theoretical limitations of the coherence measures
4. Provide more diverse real-world validation, perhaps on a standard benchmark dataset

The paper's unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations is particularly compelling, and I believe it will be influential in the field of structured representation learning. The authors have made a strong case for the practical utility of their approach, and with the suggested revisions, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NeurIPS.
