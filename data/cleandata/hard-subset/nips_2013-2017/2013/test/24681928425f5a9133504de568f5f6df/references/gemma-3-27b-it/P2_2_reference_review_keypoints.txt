- Tackles unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs) via iterative addition of And-Or fragments to maximize grammar posterior.
- Formalizes SAGs in a data-agnostic way and derives efficient posterior gain computation using sufficient statistics.
- Extends prior unsupervised grammar learning to images and events, learning composition and reconfiguration simultaneously.
- Introduces And-Or fragments as a unified approach that is more principled and data-efficient than separate learning strategies.
- Provides solid theoretical foundations and efficient derivations for the proposed method.
- Claims generality beyond NLP, applying SAGs to both image and event domains.
- Shows promising empirical results, matching or outperforming baselines, notably on a complex event dataset.
- Paper is generally well written with clear explanations of concepts and derivations.
- Algorithm is complex with many components (fragments, context matrices, n-gram tensors) and needs clearer explanation and visualization of the search process.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., α, beam width) is underexplored and lacks tuning/sensitivity analysis.
- Limited ablation studies prevent isolating the impact of specific components like fragment learning versus separate strategies.
- Synthetic image dataset is simplistic; real-image experiments are limited to a dataset used in prior work.
- Insufficient implementation details (Viterbi, image quantization, compute resources) hinder reproducibility.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, theoretically sound, unified learning strategy with effective empirical performance.
- Arguments against acceptance: algorithmic complexity, lack of thorough hyperparameter/ablation analysis, limited datasets, and reproducibility concerns.
- Overall assessment: solid and significant contribution with strong theory and promising results; reviewer recommends Strong Accept (7/10).
- Recommendations: add search visualizations, hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, ablation studies, computational complexity discussion, implementation details, and evaluations on more complex real-world datasets.
