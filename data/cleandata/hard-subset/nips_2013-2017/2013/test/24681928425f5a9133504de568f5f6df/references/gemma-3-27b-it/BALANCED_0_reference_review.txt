This paper presents a novel approach to unsupervised structure learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs). The authors address a challenging problem – learning both the structure *and* parameters of SAGs from unannotated data – which is crucial for applying these powerful models to diverse domains like images and events. The core idea of learning "And-Or fragments" to simultaneously induce compositionality and reconfiguration is a significant contribution. The authors carefully formalize SAGs, providing a general framework applicable to various data types. They also present an efficient optimization procedure leveraging sufficient statistics and a well-motivated sparsity prior. Empirical evaluation on event and image grammars demonstrates competitive, and often superior, performance compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The unified approach of learning And-Or fragments is a genuinely novel idea, addressing a key limitation of previous methods that treat composition and reconfiguration separately.
* **Generality:**  The abstract formalization of SAGs makes the approach applicable to a wide range of data types beyond natural language, a significant strength.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The optimization procedure based on posterior probability maximization and the use of sufficient statistics are theoretically well-grounded.  The connection to tensor/matrix coherence measures provides insightful justification for the algorithm's efficiency.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both event and image grammars provide compelling evidence of the approach's effectiveness. The results consistently outperform or match prior work. The use of both synthetic and real-world datasets is commendable.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and algorithmic steps.  The inclusion of figures helps illustrate the learning process and the structure of the grammars.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the paper presents a clear algorithm, the implementation details, particularly concerning the greedy/beam search for And-Or fragments and the computation of coherence measures, appear complex. More details or pseudocode beyond the current supplementary material would be beneficial.  The practical scalability to very large datasets or high-dimensional data is not fully explored.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the sparsity hyperparameter α is not thoroughly investigated.  A more detailed analysis of its influence on performance and generalization would strengthen the results.
* **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:**  The comparison to existing methods is limited.  While the chosen baselines are relevant, it would be valuable to compare against more recent and potentially stronger approaches, especially those employing deep learning techniques for structure learning (e.g., variational autoencoders for grammar induction).
* **Interpretation of Learned Grammars:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a deeper qualitative analysis of the *learned* grammars themselves. Visualizing or interpreting the structure of the resulting grammars, especially for the image data, would provide greater insight into the model's learning process and its ability to capture meaningful patterns.
* **Viterbi Likelihood Approximation:**  The reliance on Viterbi likelihood as an approximation of the true likelihood is mentioned, but the potential impact of this approximation on the learned grammar's quality isn’t fully discussed.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning.
* The approach is theoretically sound and empirically validated on relevant datasets.
* The generality of the framework makes it applicable to a wide range of problems.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The complexity of implementation and potential hyperparameter sensitivity might limit the practical applicability of the approach.
* The comparison to strong baselines could be more comprehensive.
* The lack of deeper qualitative analysis of the learned grammars leaves some questions unanswered.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. The theoretical foundations are solid, the empirical results are compelling, and the paper is generally well-written. While some aspects could be further strengthened, the contributions are significant enough to warrant acceptance.  I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest:

1.  **Expand the supplementary material** to provide more detailed pseudocode for the And-Or fragment search and coherence measure computation.
2.  **Include a more thorough analysis** of the hyperparameter α, including its impact on performance and generalization.
3.  **Consider including a comparison** to one or two more recent and competitive baselines, if feasible.
4.  **Add a section with a qualitative analysis** of the learned grammars, particularly for the image data.  Visualize the structures and provide some interpretative insights.
5. **Discuss the limitations** of the Viterbi approximation and potential impact on the learned grammars.
