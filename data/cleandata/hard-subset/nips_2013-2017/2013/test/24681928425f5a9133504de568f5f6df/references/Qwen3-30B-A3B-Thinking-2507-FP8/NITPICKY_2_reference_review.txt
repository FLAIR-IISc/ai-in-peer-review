

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper proposes an unsupervised approach for learning both the structure and parameters of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAOGs). The authors present a unified formalization of SAOGs and propose an algorithm that iteratively adds And-Or fragments to the grammar to maximize its posterior probability. The approach is evaluated on event grammars and image grammars, showing competitive results compared to prior work.

## Strengths

The paper makes a clear contribution to the growing literature on grammar-based representations for structured data. The formalization of SAOGs as agnostic to data type (text, images, events) is well-motivated and consistent with recent work on probabilistic grammars for structured data. The unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations (as opposed to separate learning of And-fragments and Or-fragments) is a theoretically sound improvement over prior methods. The experimental results on event grammars and image grammars are promising, with clear demonstrations of the approach's effectiveness.

## Weaknesses

Despite the promising ideas, the paper contains several technical inconsistencies and presentation issues that undermine its credibility:

1. **Theoretical gaps**: The claim that "by adding only And-Or fragments, our algorithm is still capable of constructing any context-free And-Or grammar" lacks any proof or reference. This is a significant theoretical gap that should be addressed.

2. **Formalization errors**: In section 3.2, the paper states "for each xi there is also an And-rule Ai → ai1ai2 . . . ain where aij (j = 1 . . . n) are the terminal nodes representing the set of atomic patterns contained in sample xi." This is incorrect - aij should be terminal nodes representing individual atomic patterns, not "the set of atomic patterns." The grammar definition in section 2 correctly states terminal nodes represent atomic patterns, not sets of patterns.

3. **Figure inconsistencies**: Figure 1(a) shows an initial grammar with "S" as a nonterminal node, but section 2 explicitly defines S as the start symbol (not a nonterminal node). This is a fundamental confusion in the grammar representation.

4. **Experimental design**: The paper uses only 6 samples (10%) for testing on the 61-video dataset. This small test set size (especially with 55 training samples) makes the reported performance metrics highly unstable and potentially misleading. The paper should either use a more standard train-test split (e.g., 80/20) or report confidence intervals.

5. **Terminology confusion**: The paper uses "And-Or fragments" without clearly defining what constitutes a "fragment" in the context of grammars. A fragment is a subgraph, but the paper doesn't specify the structural constraints or boundaries.

6. **Presentation issues**: In Table 1, the "Natural language grammar" row states "Deterministic 'concatenating' relations" - the word "Deterministic" appears to be redundant. Figure 2(c) presents a context matrix but fails to explain how contexts are defined or calculated.

7. **Lack of statistical significance**: The paper claims "significant outperformance" but provides no statistical tests or confidence intervals to support this claim, making it difficult to assess whether the improvements are meaningful.

8. **Missing comparison**: The paper doesn't adequately distinguish its approach from Si et al.'s "Unsupervised learning of event and-or grammar and semantics from video" (ICCV 2011), which is cited as related work but not clearly contrasted.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to SAOG learning with promising empirical results, the numerous technical inconsistencies and presentation errors are substantial enough to undermine the paper's credibility. The formalization errors are particularly concerning as they indicate fundamental misunderstandings of grammar structure. The small test set size for the event grammar evaluation further weakens the experimental claims.

The paper's contribution is novel in its unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations, but the lack of rigorous theoretical justification and the presentation errors prevent the contribution from being fully validated. The authors should thoroughly revise the paper to address these issues before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.

I recommend rejection due to the significant technical errors and presentation issues that prevent proper assessment of the paper's contribution. The authors would benefit from a more careful revision focusing on the formal definitions and experimental methodology before resubmission.
