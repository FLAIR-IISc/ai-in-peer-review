### Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper presents a novel approach for the unsupervised learning of both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs). The authors formulate the problem within a Bayesian framework, aiming to find the grammar that maximizes the posterior probability given the data, incorporating a sparsity prior to encourage compact and generalizable grammars. The core of the proposed method is an iterative, bottom-up algorithm that begins with a trivial grammar (representing each training sample explicitly) and progressively introduces new grammar rules to increase the posterior.

The key innovation lies in the primitive used for structure search: the "And-Or fragment." This fragment jointly models a compositional rule (an And-rule) and a set of alternative sub-configurations (Or-rules), unifying the search for compositionality and reconfigurability. This contrasts with prior work in grammar induction, such as [14, 15, 17, 18], which often learns these two aspects separately or relies on heuristics. The authors argue that their unified approach is more robust to data scarcity and ambiguity. A significant technical contribution is the derivation of an efficient method to calculate the gain in posterior probability for adding a candidate fragment, which relies on a set of sufficient statistics (an n-gram tensor and a context matrix) and avoids costly re-parsing of the training data at each step. The authors demonstrate the generality of their domain-agnostic formulation by applying it successfully to learn event grammars from video data and image grammars from both synthetic and real images, showing superior performance compared to relevant baselines [15, 17, 18].

### Strengths

1.  **Principled and Novel Learning Framework:** The paper's main strength is its principled approach to structure learning. Framing the problem as the maximization of grammar posterior is well-founded. The central idea of introducing "And-Or fragments" is a novel and elegant solution to the challenge of jointly learning compositional (And) and alternative (Or) structures. This unified search primitive is well-motivated and convincingly argued to be more efficient and robust than learning And- and Or-rules separately, especially when data is sparse.

2.  **Efficient Optimization:** The derivation of the posterior gain in terms of sufficient statistics is a strong technical contribution. By showing that the gain can be computed from n-gram and context counts without re-parsing the entire dataset, the authors make the greedy search for the best fragment computationally feasible. The connection drawn between likelihood gain and tensor/matrix coherence measures provides additional insight into what the model is capturing.

3.  **Generality and Broad Applicability:** The formulation of AOGs and the learning algorithm is deliberately data-agnostic, depending only on a set of terminal nodes and relations between them. This is a significant advantage, and the paper effectively demonstrates this generality by applying the same core algorithm to two distinct domains: temporal event sequences and spatial image arrangements. This suggests the method could be a valuable tool for structure discovery in a wide range of applications.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness. The approach achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on all tested datasets. In particular, the significant reduction in perplexity on the real-world animal faces dataset compared to the baseline from [17] is a strong result. The comparison of different learning strategies on the event datasets also provides valuable insights into the benefits of the flexible, fully grounded approach (Method 3).

### Weaknesses

1.  **Scalability of the Search Procedure:** The paper proposes using greedy or beam search to find the best And-Or fragment by searching through sub-tensors of n-gram statistics. However, the combinatorial nature of this search is a potential concern for scalability. The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the search space's complexity and how the algorithm would perform on problems with larger vocabularies of terminals, longer sequences, or more complex n-ary relations. The claim of running "within a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples" is encouraging, but more details on the search constraints and complexity analysis are needed to assess its applicability to larger-scale problems.

2.  **Impact of Key Approximations:** The method relies on two important approximations. First, it optimizes the Viterbi likelihood instead of the true marginal likelihood. While this is a common and sometimes effective heuristic [20], it biases the model towards unambiguous grammars and may perform poorly in domains where ambiguity is inherent. Second, the efficient calculation of the posterior gain relies on an assumption of "grammar unambiguity." The paper should discuss the implications of this assumption more thoroughly. It is not clear how violations of this assumption, which are likely to occur during the learning process, would affect the search and the quality of the learned grammar.

3.  **Dependence on Preprocessing:** In both the event and image experiments, the learning process begins with a set of pre-defined or pre-learned atomic patterns (terminals). For instance, the image grammar experiments rely on the method from [17] to quantize images and learn atomic patches. The overall system performance is therefore contingent on the quality of this initial step. An analysis of the method's sensitivity to the quality of the terminal vocabulary would strengthen the evaluation.

### Constructive Suggestions

1.  **Elaboration on Search Complexity:** To address scalability concerns, I suggest the authors include a more detailed description of the search algorithm for finding optimal And-Or fragments. Providing a complexity analysis, even for a simplified case, and discussing the practical heuristics used to constrain the search (e.g., limits on `n`, the number of children in an And-rule) would significantly improve the paper's clarity and completeness.

2.  **Discussion of Approximations:** The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of the limitations imposed by the Viterbi likelihood and the unambiguity assumption. It would be helpful to mention when these approximations are expected to hold and what potential failure modes they might introduce. Briefly discussing how the framework might be extended to use the full likelihood (e.g., with an Inside-Outside-style computation) as a direction for future work would also be valuable.

3.  **Further Analysis in Experiments:** In the event grammar experiments, it is noted that Method 3 performs better on the more complex dataset because the sub-typed actions help avoid learning erroneous rules. This is an interesting finding and could be elaborated on further, as it provides insight into how the granularity of terminals affects structure learning.

### Decision

This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. The proposed "And-Or fragment" is a novel and well-motivated concept that elegantly unifies the learning of compositional and alternative structures. The technical derivation for efficient search is solid, and the empirical results are strong and convincing across multiple domains. While there are some concerns about scalability and the impact of approximations, these points represent avenues for future research rather than fatal flaws. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and presents ideas that are likely to be influential. I recommend acceptance.
