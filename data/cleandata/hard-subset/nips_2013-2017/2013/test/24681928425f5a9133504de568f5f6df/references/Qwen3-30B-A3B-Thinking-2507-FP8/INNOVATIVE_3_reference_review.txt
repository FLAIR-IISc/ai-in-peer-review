

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper introduces a novel approach to unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, which unifies the learning of compositional (And-rules) and reconfigurational (Or-rules) structures through "And-Or fragments." The authors present a formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type (text, images, events), and propose an algorithm that iteratively builds grammars by adding these unified fragments to optimize posterior probability.

The key novelty lies in the "And-Or fragment" concept, which represents a significant departure from previous approaches that treated composition and reconfiguration as separate learning problems. By learning these aspects jointly, the authors claim their method is more robust to data scarcity and leads to more compact grammars. The paper's theoretical foundation for efficiently computing posterior gains using sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) is elegant and well-motivated.

The paper makes a strong case for the significance of this work by demonstrating competitive performance on both event grammars (from activity videos) and image grammars (from synthetic and real animal face datasets), outperforming previous methods in more complex scenarios. The ability to learn from raw atomic patterns rather than pre-processed ID sequences (as in prior work) is particularly valuable for real-world applications.

This work relates to several NIPS papers on probabilistic grammars, sum-product networks, and unsupervised learning. It builds upon the foundational work of Zhu et al. on stochastic grammars for images [1, 3] and extends the recent work on structure learning of And-Or grammars [17, 18]. The paper's unification of composition and reconfiguration learning represents a conceptual advance beyond previous approaches that treated these as separate challenges.

The paper's main strength is its bold conceptual contribution: the And-Or fragment framework. This is not merely an incremental improvement but a new way of thinking about grammar structure learning. The authors effectively argue that previous approaches were limited by their separate treatment of And-rules and Or-rules, and their unified approach provides both theoretical and empirical benefits.

I find the paper technically sound, with clear theoretical motivation and sufficient experimental validation across two distinct domains. The algorithm's efficiency (processing 5000 samples in minutes) and the principled way of handling prior probability through grammar size are particularly impressive.

The paper's main weakness is that the experimental results, while showing improvement, don't demonstrate revolutionary performance gains over previous methods. The perplexity improvement on the real image dataset (67.5 vs 129.4) is significant but not dramatic. However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novel concepts over marginal performance gains, I view this as a minor issue.

Another potential limitation is that the paper doesn't fully explore the theoretical guarantees of the algorithm, though the authors acknowledge the greedy search approach as an approximation. The paper's focus on practical utility rather than theoretical completeness aligns well with the paper's overall goals.

The writing is clear and well-organized, with helpful examples (Figures 1-2) that illustrate the grammar learning process. The supplementary material appears to contain the necessary details for reproducibility.

In terms of significance, this work opens a new direction in grammar learning that could potentially influence research in structured prediction, compositional modeling, and unsupervised representation learning across multiple domains. The agnostic formalization of And-Or grammars to different data types is particularly valuable for future work.

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance in unsupervised grammar learning. While the methodological details are sound, the true value lies in the novel perspective it offers on grammar structure learning. The paper's focus on unifying composition and reconfiguration through And-Or fragments is a bold and promising direction that addresses a fundamental limitation in previous approaches.

The paper meets NIPS standards for originality and significance. It presents a genuinely new concept that challenges conventional approaches to grammar learning. While the experimental results are solid rather than groundbreaking, the novelty of the approach justifies its acceptance.

Strengths:
- Novel unified framework for grammar structure learning (And-Or fragments)
- Theoretical foundation for efficient posterior optimization
- Demonstrated utility across two distinct domains (events, images)
- Agnostic formalization applicable to multiple data types

Weaknesses:
- Performance gains, while significant, aren't revolutionary
- Limited theoretical guarantees for the greedy search approach
- Some details require supplementing with supplementary material

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The conceptual novelty and bold approach to grammar structure learning represent a meaningful contribution to the field. The paper's focus on a new direction rather than incremental improvements aligns well with NIPS' emphasis on innovative research. The authors have successfully demonstrated the viability of their approach while opening up new research avenues.
