This paper presents a novel and ambitious approach to unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, aiming to simultaneously learn both the grammar’s structure and its parameters from unlabeled data. The authors elegantly formalize stochastic And-Or grammars in a data-agnostic way, and their key innovation lies in the introduction of “And-Or fragments” – a unified mechanism for learning compositional and reconfigurable structures, addressing limitations of previous methods that treated these aspects separately. The theoretical formulation, especially concerning the efficient computation of posterior gains using sufficient statistics and the connection to tensor/matrix coherence, is commendable. The empirical evaluation on event and image grammars demonstrates promising results, competitive with or exceeding existing approaches, particularly on a more challenging synthetic event dataset.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of learning both structure *and* parameters simultaneously, coupled with the And-Or fragment approach, is a significant departure from existing methods. This is the paper’s strongest asset.
* **Generality:** The data-agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars is a powerful feature, opening the door to applications across diverse data types.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the posterior gain formulation and its connection to coherence measures demonstrate a strong theoretical understanding.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while not extensive, provide evidence that the approach is viable and competitive. The improved performance on the more complex event dataset is particularly encouraging.
* **Clear Writing (mostly):** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with clear explanations of the key concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experimental section feels somewhat constrained. While results are positive, they are limited to relatively small datasets and a specific set of tasks (event and image grammars). A wider range of datasets and tasks would strengthen the claims of generality.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The effect of the α parameter on performance isn't thoroughly investigated. Its optimal setting likely depends on the specific dataset and could influence the results considerably.
* **Complexity of Implementation:**  The method appears complex to implement, and the authors don’t fully address the computational challenges, relying on mentioning efficiency without detailed analysis.
* **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The comparison is primarily with [17, 18], which are somewhat dated. A comparison to more recent, state-of-the-art unsupervised structure learning methods (even if adapted to this specific grammar formalism) would be beneficial.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** The explanation of the likelihood gain formulation, while theoretically sound, could benefit from more intuitive explanations and visual aids.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* This paper tackles a challenging and important problem – unsupervised learning of compositional models.
* The proposed approach is genuinely novel and offers a significant advance over existing methods.
* The theoretical framework is well-developed and provides a solid foundation for the approach.
* The empirical results, though limited, are promising and support the claims of the paper.
* The potential impact of this work is significant, as it could enable the automatic discovery of underlying structures in complex data.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The experimental validation is somewhat limited and doesn't fully demonstrate the generality of the approach.
* The computational complexity of the method and its implementation challenges aren’t fully addressed.
* The comparison to recent, strong baselines is lacking.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution.  I am particularly impressed by the theoretical elegance and the innovation of the And-Or fragment approach. While the experimental validation could be more comprehensive, the paper presents a compelling case for the value of this new methodology. The strengths, particularly the novelty and theoretical soundness, outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors consider expanding the experimental evaluation in future work. I believe this paper has the potential to stimulate significant research in the area of unsupervised structure learning and compositional modeling.
