Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses unsupervised structure and parameter learning for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), a formalism that combines compositional (And) and reconfigurable (Or) structure and has been used in images and events. The authors (1) provide a unified, context-free definition of AOGs, agnostic to the modality; (2) propose an iterative, bottom-up algorithm that starts from a trivial grammar that exactly generates the training set and repeatedly inserts “And-Or fragments” to maximize the grammar’s posterior under a sparsity prior; and (3) derive efficient scoring of candidate fragments using sufficient statistics computed from the data under a Viterbi-likelihood approximation and an unambiguity assumption. The likelihood gain factorizes into coherence measures of an n-gram tensor (counts of configurations) and a context matrix (counts of configuration-context co-occurrences). The authors show how to set Or-rule probabilities from counts, include a prior penalty proportional to grammar size, and search greedily/with beam. Experiments on activity parsing (two variants of the same dataset) and image parsing (synthetic and real animal faces) suggest gains over prior AOG structure-learning methods.

This extends prior grammar induction techniques in NLP (e.g., EM/inside-outside for SCFGs [Baker 1979], Klein & Manning 2004; Bayesian model merging [Stolcke & Omohundro 1994]; ADIOS [Solan et al. 2005]; biclustering-based grammar induction [Tu & Honavar 2008]) and builds on AOGs for images and events [Zhu & Mumford 2006; Zhao & Zhu 2011; Si & Zhu 2013; Si et al. 2011]. It is also related to structure learning in sum-product networks [Poon & Domingos 2011] and uses Viterbi/unambiguity regularization ideas [Spitkovsky et al. 2010; Tu & Honavar 2012]. Compared to prior AOG structure learners [Si & Zhu 2013; Si et al. 2011], the key novelty is to add And-Or fragments that unify discovering compositions and reconfigurations with a single posterior objective and computable sufficient statistics.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The learning objective (posterior with size prior) and Viterbi approximation are standard and reasonable. The derivation of the fragment likelihood gain and its factorization into tensor/matrix “coherence” is plausible but key details are deferred to the supplement. The assumption of grammar unambiguity is strong and central to the tractability of the sufficient-statistics computation; the practical impact when the true grammar is ambiguous is not analyzed. The method provides closed-form updates for Or-rule probabilities from reduction counts. However, the treatment of And-rule probabilities/energy terms is underspecified in the main text—how are relation energies learned or normalized across alternative And-rules? The prior uses fixed per-symbol code lengths (sa, so), but the definition of grammar size and encoding scheme is not fully specified, which affects reproducibility and comparisons.
- Completeness and evaluation: The algorithm is well-motivated and described at a high level, with search via greedy/beam and random restarts. There are no formal guarantees (e.g., convergence to a local optimum is by construction, but no bounds on approximation error from the Viterbi/unambiguity assumptions). Complexity analysis of candidate fragment enumeration (n-gram tensor construction, context matrix construction across relation types) is not provided. Experiments are positive but limited: event datasets are small, and comparisons are restricted to methods operating on ID sequences. The real-image evaluation uses perplexity with smoothing, but no ablations (e.g., And-Or fragments vs. separate And/Or search, effect of α, beam width) or statistical tests.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-structured, with helpful figures and intuitive explanations of fragments, reductions, and context. Some notational elements require clarification:
- Precisely define grammar size and encoding lengths sa and so.
- Specify how And-rule energies/probabilities are parametrized and estimated.
- Define “context” formally for temporal and spatial settings, and how contexts are enumerated for the context matrix.
- The claim that And-Or fragments can construct any context-free AOG is important; a sketch proof or reference would help.
Figure readability and some variable definitions in equations could be tightened. Pseudocode is in the supplement; a brief outline in the main text would aid clarity.

Originality
Learning And-Or fragments that jointly capture compositions and reconfigurations, coupled with a posterior objective and efficient scoring via sufficient statistics, appears novel within the AOG literature. The use of Viterbi/unambiguity biases is known, as are biclustering/coherence notions (e.g., [22]), but their integration here is a meaningful contribution. The modality-agnostic formalization is useful, though conceptually consistent with prior AOG frameworks.

Significance
If robust, the approach can simplify structure learning for rich compositional/reconfigurable models across modalities. The gains over prior AOG learners on synthetic and real data are promising. However, broader impact is tempered by the strong unambiguity assumption, limited experimental scope, and the lack of comparisons to more general grammar/SPN structure learners or stronger vision/event baselines. Demonstrating scalability, robustness to noise in atomic detections, and benefits on larger, diverse datasets would increase significance.

Pros
- Unified formalization and learning framework applicable to text, images, and events.
- Joint And-Or fragment induction mitigates data scarcity and redundancy issues of separate And/Or searches.
- Clear objective (posterior with sparsity prior), efficient scoring from sufficient statistics, and closed-form Or-rule estimation.
- Empirical improvements over prior AOG structure learning on both events and images.

Cons
- Critical assumptions (Viterbi likelihood, grammar unambiguity) not analyzed for violation; limited theoretical guarantees.
- And-rule energy/probability learning unspecified; grammar size/encoding not rigorously defined.
- Experimental scope modest; baselines limited; no ablations/sensitivity analyses; fairness concerns for event baselines that operate on IDs only.
- Computational complexity and scalability characteristics not characterized.

Questions for the authors
- How are And-rule probabilities/energies parameterized and learned? Are relation energy parameters optimized, or are And-rule probabilities implicit?
- How sensitive are results to α, beam width, and the unambiguity assumption? What happens if multiple parses exist?
- What is the precise definition of context, and how is the context space controlled to avoid sparsity in the context matrix?
- Can you provide complexity analysis for constructing n-gram tensors and searching fragments across relation types?
- Do And-Or fragments strictly suffice to generate any context-free AOG? Please include a proof or reference.

Suggestions
- Clarify grammar size/encoding and And-rule parameter learning; include a brief derivation of the likelihood gain in the main text.
- Add ablations (And-Or vs. separate And/Or; effect of α; beam size), sensitivity to noise, and runtime/scale results.
- Broaden baselines (e.g., HDP-PCFG variants, SPN structure learning) where applicable and ensure event baselines use comparable inputs.
- Report statistical significance and include larger-scale datasets if possible.

Overall assessment
A solid and potentially impactful contribution to unsupervised AOG structure learning with a clear idea—And-Or fragments—and promising results. Addressing the noted clarity and evaluation gaps would strengthen the paper.
