

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper proposes an unsupervised approach for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars, with a focus on unifying the learning of compositions and reconfigurations through "And-Or fragments." The approach optimizes the posterior probability of the grammar and is evaluated on event grammars and image grammars.

## Strengths
The paper presents a theoretically motivated approach that addresses a genuine limitation in previous grammar learning methods, where compositions and reconfigurations were learned separately. The And-Or fragment concept is conceptually novel and addresses data scarcity issues. The experimental results on event and image grammars are promising, showing competitive or better performance than prior approaches. The formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars as agnostic to data type is a useful contribution.

## Weaknesses

**1. Formalization Inconsistencies:** The paper claims to provide a "formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to the types of atomic patterns and their compositions," but this is undermined by inconsistent definitions. The grammar definition in Section 2 relies on unspecified "relations" in And-rules, with no formal definition provided. The Natural Language Grammar example in Table 1 mentions "Deterministic 'concatenating' relations" without explaining what this means in the grammar's formal structure.

**2. Algorithmic Ambiguities:** The learning algorithm's description lacks critical details. The paper states "we try to reduce each training sample using the new grammar rules and update the top-level And-rules accordingly" but never explains how this reduction is performed or how the top-level rules are updated. The restriction on "types of grammar fragments" mentioned in Section 3.2 is never specified, making reproducibility difficult.

**3. Mathematical Errors:** The likelihood gain formula in Section 3.3 contains a clear typo: "‖RDi(aij)‖‖RDi(aij)‖" should be "‖RDi(aij)‖/‖RD‖" or similar. The notation is inconsistent (using "RD" and "RDi" without clear definitions), and the derivation's dependence on the supplementary material is not properly acknowledged in the main text.

**4. Ambiguous Evaluation Metrics:** The paper uses "purity" and "inverse purity" for event grammar evaluation without clear definitions. The description "purity (the percentage of the identified event durations overlapping with the annotated event durations)" is ambiguous - it could mean the percentage of identified events that overlap with annotated events, or the percentage of annotated events that overlap with identified events. This ambiguity makes it difficult to assess the reported F-measures.

**5. Experimental Details Omissions:** The real image dataset experiment (Section 4.2) uses 10-fold cross-validation but doesn't specify how the folds were created or if the data was stratified by class, which could introduce bias. The paper also fails to clarify how the "atomic patterns" were learned for the real dataset, despite claiming to use the same approach as [17].

## Additional Concerns

The paper cites [17] as a comparison for image grammar learning, but doesn't mention more recent work (e.g., [24] which is cited in the paper itself). The reference to "Z. Si, M. Pei, B. Yao, S.-C. Zhu, ICCV, 2011" contains a typo (should be "Z. Si, S. Zhu" as in the paper's own citation).

Figure 2's caption is incomplete, failing to explain what "context1", "context2", etc. represent in the context matrix. Table 1 has inconsistent punctuation in the "Relations in And-rules" column, with some entries capitalized and others not.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to grammar learning with promising empirical results, the technical inconsistencies and omissions significantly undermine the paper's quality. The formalization is incomplete, key algorithmic details are missing, and critical mathematical expressions contain errors. The ambiguities in evaluation metrics and experimental details further reduce confidence in the reported results.

The paper's contributions are not sufficiently novel compared to related work in grammatical inference (e.g., [14,15,16]), and the claimed "agnostic" formalization is compromised by the lack of precise definition of core concepts. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous mathematical treatment, clearer algorithmic specification, and careful attention to the details of the formalization.

Given these substantial technical issues that affect reproducibility and credibility, I recommend rejection. The paper contains promising ideas but fails to present them with the technical rigor expected at NIPS. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address these concerns before it could be considered for acceptance.
