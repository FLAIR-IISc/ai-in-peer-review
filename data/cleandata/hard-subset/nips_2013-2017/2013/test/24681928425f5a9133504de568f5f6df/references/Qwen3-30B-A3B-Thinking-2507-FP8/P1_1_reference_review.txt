

# Peer Review: "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

## Summary
This paper proposes an unsupervised learning approach for stochastic And-Or grammars that simultaneously learns both grammar structure and parameters from unannotated data. The authors introduce "And-Or fragments" as a unified mechanism to learn both compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules), addressing limitations in previous approaches that handled these separately. The method optimizes the posterior probability of the grammar, using sufficient statistics to efficiently compute likelihood gains. The approach is evaluated on event grammars (from human activity videos) and image grammars (from synthetic and real animal face datasets), showing competitive or better performance than prior work.

## Strengths

**Originality (High):** The paper's core contribution—using And-Or fragments to unify the learning of compositions and reconfigurations—is novel and well-motivated. Previous work [14-18] typically learns And-fragments and Or-fragments separately, which can lead to inefficient search and redundant grammars. The authors correctly position their work as addressing this limitation while building on prior structure learning approaches.

**Quality (High):** The paper provides a clear formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type, making the approach broadly applicable. The theoretical derivation of likelihood gain using context matrices and n-gram tensors is sound, and the empirical results are well-designed. The method's ability to optimize both structure and parameters simultaneously is a significant technical contribution.

**Significance (High):** Stochastic And-Or grammars have been successfully applied to various domains (images, events), but manual specification of grammar structures is challenging. This unsupervised approach addresses a practical bottleneck in applying these models to new domains, with demonstrated improvements over prior methods on both event and image grammars.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized with clear definitions, helpful examples (Figures 1-3), and a logical flow. The algorithm is described with sufficient detail, and the supplementary material appears to contain the necessary derivations.

## Weaknesses

**Computational Complexity (Moderate):** While the authors claim the algorithm "runs reasonably fast," they provide little quantitative evidence. A comparison of computational complexity with previous methods would strengthen the paper, especially since grammar learning can be computationally intensive.

**Assumption of Grammar Unambiguity (Moderate):** The paper relies on Viterbi likelihood, which assumes grammar unambiguity. This is acknowledged in the text, but the authors don't adequately address how the method would perform when this assumption is violated in real-world data.

**Event Grammar Modification (Moderate):** The authors modify their algorithm to handle multiple unrelated events per video by changing top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes. While reasonable, this modification isn't thoroughly justified, and it's unclear how generalizable this approach is to other multi-event scenarios.

**Limited Discussion of Fragments (Moderate):** The paper doesn't fully explain why certain And-Or fragments are preferred over others, beyond their posterior gain. A deeper analysis of what makes a "good" fragment (beyond just statistical co-occurrence) would add theoretical depth.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of unsupervised grammar learning with clear theoretical and empirical advantages over previous approaches. The unified And-Or fragment approach is particularly compelling and addresses a meaningful limitation in prior work.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Include a computational complexity analysis comparing the proposed method with previous approaches.

2. Expand the discussion on handling ambiguous grammars and potential extensions to address this limitation.

3. Provide more detailed justification for the event grammar modification, including how it might generalize to other multi-event scenarios.

4. Consider adding a small case study showing examples of learned And-Or fragments and why they were selected.

## Overall Assessment

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning for grammatical models. The unified approach to learning both compositions and reconfigurations is novel and well-supported by both theory and experiments. The method's broad applicability across different data types (text, images, events) and its demonstrated performance improvements over prior work make it a strong candidate for NIPS. While some minor clarifications would strengthen the paper, these do not undermine the core contribution.

The paper advances the state of the art in grammatical modeling by providing a more efficient and robust approach to unsupervised grammar learning, which will likely be of interest to researchers working on compositional models, structured prediction, and unsupervised learning across multiple domains. The work aligns well with NIPS' focus on theoretical and empirical advances in machine learning.
