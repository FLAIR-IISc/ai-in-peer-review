Summary
This paper tackles unsupervised structure and parameter learning for context-free stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) across modalities (text, images, events). The key idea is to start from an initial grammar that exactly reproduces the training data and iteratively add And-Or fragments—each comprising one And node with several Or children—so as to maximize a posterior objective combining Viterbi likelihood and a sparsity prior on grammar size. The authors derive an efficient approximation of the marginal gain in posterior per candidate fragment using sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor (co-occurrence of child choices) and a context matrix (co-occurrence of configurations with their surrounding context). This yields closed-form Or-rule probabilities and a prior gain term that encourages compactness. Empirical results on event parsing (indoor activities) and image grammars (synthetic animal face sketches; real animal faces) show improvements over prior AOG structure learning baselines.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on a long line of grammar induction and AOG research: model merging and MDL-style PCFG induction (Stolcke & Omohundro; Solan et al.; Tu & Honavar), SPNs and their decomposability (Poon & Domingos) and And-Or templates for vision (Si & Zhu). Compared to earlier AOG structure learning for images/events [17, 18], this paper learns composition (And) and reconfiguration (Or) jointly via And-Or fragments, which is a substantive algorithmic change. The use of Viterbi likelihood echoes unambiguity-regularized grammar induction (Spitkovsky et al.; Tu & Honavar). The “coherence” perspective connects to biclustering.

Strengths
- Unified framework: a clean, modality-agnostic formalization of context-free AOGs that accommodates different relation types (temporal, spatial).
- Joint search over And and Or via And-Or fragments: this is a practical and conceptually appealing way to alleviate data sparsity and redundancy compared to separate And- or Or-fragment induction.
- Efficient scoring: formulating posterior gain via sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor + context matrix) avoids repeated reparsing, with an intuitive factorization into “within-fragment” and “against-context” coherence.
- Empirical evidence: on a synthetic image grammar (with ground truth) the method reportedly outperforms [17]; on real images, perplexity is substantially lower; on events, the version grounded in atomic actions and learning both “co-occurring” and “followed-by” relations performs best among tested methods.

Weaknesses and questions
Quality
- Assumptions: The derivation of likelihood gain and use of sufficient statistics assume grammar unambiguity and rely on Viterbi parses. Many AOGs are inherently ambiguous; please quantify how violations affect scoring. An ablation comparing Viterbi vs. marginal likelihood (even on small data) would strengthen the case.
- Parameter learning: And-rule probabilities are said to be “specified by energy terms defined on the relations,” but the paper provides no details on parameterization (e.g., discrete bins vs. continuous Gaussians for spatial offsets/temporal gaps), estimation (MLE/MAP), sharing, or regularization. This is critical for reproducibility and for understanding generalization.
- Prior/MDL: The sparsity prior uses sa and so (bits per RHS symbol) and α. How are these set? Is there sensitivity analysis? A more principled MDL scheme that includes data encoding length (not just model size) would be more convincing.
- Search: The candidate fragment generation and pruning strategy is under-specified. How are relation types and n chosen? How are contexts defined across different relations? What beam widths and restart schedules are used? How do you prevent overlapping/conflicting reductions? A complexity analysis (or at least empirical scaling with n, grammar size, and |X|) is needed.
- Theory: Claims that any context-free AOG can be constructed with And-Or fragments and that likelihood gain factorizes into two coherence measures need precise statements and at least proof sketches.

Clarity
- The exposition has several notation and readability issues (e.g., figures are hard to parse; definitions of RD, RDi(aij) appear abruptly; some equations are cramped). A self-contained derivation of the gain formula in the main text (with a small running example) would help.
- The description of how contexts are represented for different relation types (temporal vs. spatial) is too terse. Include concrete definitions or diagrams.
- In the event experiments, the late switch from top-level And to Or to handle multiple events per video is a nontrivial modeling change; describe its effect on training objective and parsing.

Originality
- The joint And-Or fragment concept and the sufficient-statistics-based scoring are novel in the AOG context. The work is distinct from prior AOG induction [17,18] and PCFG induction. However, the paper would benefit from a deeper comparison to SPN structure learning methods (which are closely related to AOGs) and to MDL-based grammar induction beyond [14–16].

Significance
- If robust, the approach could be a useful, general-purpose structure learner for compositional-reconfigurable models across modalities. The improvements on the vision tasks are promising.
- That said, the experimental scope and baselines are limited. For events, comparisons are only to ID-based grammar learners; please add non-grammar unsupervised baselines (e.g., HDP-HMM/Semi-Markov, motif discovery) and state-of-the-art event grammars if feasible. For images, compare to SPN structure learning and other hierarchical tiling/scene grammar methods.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide details and ablations for: (i) parameterization and learning of And-rule relation energies; (ii) sensitivity to α, sa, so; (iii) effect of Viterbi vs. marginal likelihood; (iv) beam width/restarts; (v) And-only, Or-only, and And-Or ablations.
- Add runtime and scaling plots; report beam sizes and candidate counts.
- Strengthen baselines and report full numbers (Table 2 is referenced but values are not present here). Include parse accuracy or structural similarity metrics where ground truth is known.
- Clarify the construction of contexts, n-gram tensors across relation types, and the smoothing used for perplexity on real images.

Pros
- Conceptually clean, modality-agnostic framework
- Practical joint search over And/Or; efficient scoring with sufficient statistics
- Promising empirical gains on two domains

Cons
- Heavy reliance on Viterbi and unambiguity; unclear robustness
- Insufficient detail on relation modeling and parameter learning
- Search procedure and complexity under-specified
- Limited baselines/ablations; some evaluation details missing

Recommendation: Weak reject. The paper presents a compelling idea with encouraging results, but key methodological details, theoretical claims, and experimental breadth need strengthening for NeurIPS. With clarifications, additional baselines, and ablations, this could become a strong contribution.
