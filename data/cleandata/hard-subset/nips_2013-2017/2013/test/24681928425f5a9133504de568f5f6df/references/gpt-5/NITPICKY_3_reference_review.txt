Summary
The paper proposes an unsupervised method for learning both structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) from unannotated data. The key idea is to grow the grammar bottom-up by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments: one new And-node with multiple sibling Or-nodes whose alternatives are existing nodes. The objective is the posterior P(G|X) with an MDL-like sparsity prior and a Viterbi likelihood approximation. The authors derive a fast scoring function for candidate fragments using sufficient statistics computed from the data: an n-gram tensor over child choices and a context matrix, which factorize the likelihood gain (under an unambiguity assumption) into “coherence” measures. They apply the approach to event grammars (two human activity datasets) and image grammars (synthetic and real), reporting improvements over prior AOG structure learning for images/events.

Strengths
- Clear motivation to unify learning of compositions (And) and reconfigurations (Or) via And-Or fragments, addressing brittleness of learning them separately.
- Uses a posterior objective with an explicit sparsity prior; the learning criterion is principled compared to heuristic merging.
- Sufficient-statistics formulation for evaluating fragment gain is computationally attractive; the context/fragment coherence intuition is appealing.
- Empirical results show nontrivial improvements over prior AOG learning approaches on the tested datasets.
- Attempts to be domain-agnostic (text/images/events) and to relate to SCFGs and SPNs.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Central assumption: grammar unambiguity. The scoring derivation and sufficient-statistic factorization rely on unambiguity, which is unrealistic for most grammars in language, images, or events. The method also uses Viterbi likelihood (another ambiguity-reducing bias). There is no analysis of how violations of unambiguity affect the score, nor empirical ablations quantifying robustness.
- Incomplete probabilistic specification. And-rules are said to be parameterized by “energy terms defined on relations,” but the paper never specifies how these energies are learned, normalized, or combined in the likelihood; the likelihood gain depends on P(A→...), but the paper only derives Or-rule probabilities. It is unclear whether And-rule probabilities are fixed, learned jointly, or cancelled by the scoring surrogate.
- Prior/MDL details are under-specified. The grammar size ‖G‖ and the encoding unit costs sa and so are not defined precisely. Without a coding scheme, the prior is ambiguous and can dominate or under-regularize. Sensitivity to α, sa, so is not explored; α is fixed to 0.5 without justification.
- Theoretical claims without proof. “Capable of constructing any context-free And-Or grammar by adding And-Or fragments only” is asserted but not proven (or even sketched). The factorization of likelihood gain into tensor/matrix “coherence” relies on [22], which is a biclustering survey and does not define a coherence metric per se; the exact measure used is not specified.
- Search complexity and scalability. Constructing n-gram tensors for different n and relation types over all samples is potentially exponential in n and alphabet size. The beam/greedy search space and pruning are not characterized; no complexity analysis is given. The “minutes on a desktop” claim is not reproducible (no hardware specs, implementation details, or scaling plots).
- Experimental design issues:
  - Event datasets rely on annotated atomic actions and precise start/end times; this is not end-to-end unsupervised from raw data. The second dataset artificially splits actions into subtypes, which confounds model difficulty and may advantage the proposed method.
  - Fairness of baselines: comparisons are only to specific AOG/event grammar learners ([15,18,17]); stronger, widely used unsupervised grammar learners (e.g., adaptor grammars, HDP-PCFGs, PCFG-LA, Bayesian model merging variants) or modern SPN structure learners (e.g., LearnSPN/Gens & Domingos 2013) are not included.
  - Metrics and protocols are underspecified. For the synthetic image grammar experiment, how is the “true grammar” used to compute F-measure/KL? How are samples drawn/approximated? For the real images, perplexity estimation includes smoothing, but the scheme is not defined. No standard errors or significance tests are reported.
  - Ad hoc post-processing: converting top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes “right before termination” to handle multiple events per video is heuristic and changes the model class post hoc. The use of CYK assumes context-free relations; with temporal “co-occurring” and spatial relations, this needs justification.
- Clarity/organization and notational issues:
  - Several equations are hard to parse and appear inconsistent (e.g., the likelihood gain expression includes products of counts with unclear normalization; duplicated ‖RD‖ terms; undefined symbols). The derivations are deferred to supplementary material not available for review.
  - The definition of “context” in the context matrix is vague; different choices can change the objective drastically.
  - Figures are difficult to interpret (garbled labels in Fig. 1/2; missing axis explanations in Fig. 7; Table 1 incomplete). Many references are poorly formatted or incomplete.
  - No pseudocode, no algorithmic details on beam width, restarts, or stopping criteria.
- Claims of generality are not substantiated. No text experiments are shown; the formalism claims to be domain-agnostic, but all experiments are in images/events and depend on domain-specific preprocessing (quantization, action annotations).

Quality
- Mixed. The high-level idea is reasonable and the empirical results are promising, but important probabilistic and algorithmic details are missing or rely on unrealistic assumptions. Evaluation is limited and not fully reproducible.

Clarity
- Below NeurIPS standards. Definitions, notation, and figures need significant cleanup. Key quantities (‖G‖, sa/so, coherence) and derivations should be in the main text or clearly summarized.

Originality
- Moderate. Combining And/Or discovery into a single fragment is a nice twist on grammar merging. However, structure learning for AOGs and SPNs is established; connections to existing SPN learning are not fully explored or compared.

Significance
- Potentially useful for AOG learning, but broader impact is limited by assumptions and incomplete evaluation. Missing comparisons to stronger baselines reduce confidence in the empirical gains.

Questions for authors
- How are And-rule probabilities/energies defined and learned? How do they enter the likelihood gain?
- Precisely define ‖G‖, sa, so and report sensitivity to α.
- Provide a proof/sketch that And-Or fragments can generate any context-free AOG.
- Define the “coherence” measures used and justify the factorization rigorously without unambiguity, or quantify degradation empirically.
- Detail the construction of contexts, n-gram tensors, beam search parameters, and computational complexity.
- Expand baselines to include SPN structure learners and Bayesian PCFG/AOG learners; report statistical significance.

Minor comments and formatting
- Fix figure/table rendering and captions; ensure all symbols are defined.
- Provide pseudocode and hardware/runtime details.
- Clean up references (missing years, venues, duplicated commas).
- Clarify the synthetic image grammar generation and KL estimation procedure.

Recommendation
- Lean reject. The paper contains a promising idea and encouraging results, but the current presentation has too many technical and methodological gaps, heavy reliance on unrealistic assumptions, and limited/misaligned baselines. With complete derivations, stronger comparisons, clarified modeling of And-rule probabilities, and improved clarity, this work could be competitive.
