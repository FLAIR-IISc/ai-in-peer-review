This paper proposes an unsupervised approach to learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs). SAGs are presented as a general framework capable of modeling compositional and reconfigurable patterns in diverse data types (text, images, events). The core contribution lies in an iterative algorithm that adds "And-Or fragments" to a grammar, optimizing a posterior probability that balances model complexity and data likelihood. This contrasts with previous work that often fixes the grammar structure or learns compositions and reconfigurations separately. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on event and image grammars, achieving competitive or superior performance compared to existing approaches.

The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the motivations and contributions. The formalization of SAGs as a general model is a significant step, unifying previous formulations tailored to specific data types. The proposed learning algorithm, particularly the use of And-Or fragments, is a novel idea that addresses limitations of earlier work. The empirical evaluation, though limited in scope, provides convincing evidence of the approach's effectiveness. The authors relate their work to relevant literature in natural language processing, computer vision, and event recognition, demonstrating a solid understanding of the field.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The unified formulation of SAGs and the And-Or fragment-based learning algorithm are novel contributions.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The approach is based on a well-defined probabilistic framework and leverages statistical principles (posterior probability optimization, sufficient statistics). The derivation of the likelihood gain is particularly noteworthy.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The use of figures (e.g., Figure 1, 2) helps illustrate key concepts.
*   **Significance:** Learning compositional structures from unlabeled data is a challenging and important problem. SAGs offer a powerful framework for addressing this challenge, with potential applications in various domains. The performance improvements on both event and image grammar learning suggest the practical relevance of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Scalability:** While the authors mention efficiency improvements, the computational complexity of the learning algorithm, especially for large datasets or complex grammars, is not thoroughly discussed. The scalability to higher-dimensional data or more intricate structures remains an open question.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the hyperparameter α (sparsity prior) on the learning process is not extensively explored. A more detailed sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Baselines:** The comparison to prior work is somewhat limited, particularly on the image grammar learning task. Including a wider range of state-of-the-art image parsing methods would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The practical aspects of applying the learned grammars (e.g., inference speed, memory requirements) are not addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to unsupervised structure learning.
*   The proposed method demonstrates promising results on two different datasets.
*   The unified formulation of SAGs is a significant contribution to the field.
*   The paper addresses a challenging and important problem with potential for broad impact.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The scalability of the algorithm is a concern.
*   The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the practical considerations of using the learned grammars.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong contribution that addresses a significant problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. While some limitations exist, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. The paper is of high quality and is suitable for acceptance to NIPS, pending minor revisions to address the concerns regarding scalability, hyperparameter sensitivity, and the scope of the experimental evaluation. Specifically, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity, a sensitivity analysis of the sparsity parameter, and a comparison to more recent image parsing methods would enhance the paper's impact.
