**Summary:**
This paper proposes a method for the unsupervised learning of both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs). The core problem is that manually defining a grammar structure is difficult, and learning parameters for a fixed, overly general grammar is often intractable. The authors formulate this as a Bayesian inference problem, seeking to maximize the posterior probability of the grammar given the data, using a sparsity prior to encourage compact models. The main technical contribution is a bottom-up, iterative learning algorithm that searches for and adds "And-Or fragments" to the grammar. This novel fragment type is designed to jointly learn compositional rules (And-nodes) and alternative reconfigurations (Or-nodes), which the authors argue is more robust and efficient than learning them separately. The method is presented as a general framework applicable to various data types and is evaluated on event grammar learning from video and image grammar learning from synthetic and real datasets.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles the challenging and important problem of unsupervised structure learning for compositional, probabilistic models. AOGs are a powerful and expressive model class, and developing effective, data-driven methods for learning their structure is a valuable research direction.
2.  **Novel Search Strategy:** The central idea of learning via "And-Or fragments" is novel and well-motivated. The argument that separate learning of And-rules and Or-rules can be inefficient or error-prone, especially with sparse data, is compelling. Unifying this search into a single, more complex operator is an interesting and principled approach to structure search.
3.  **Principled Objective:** The learning process is framed around maximizing a well-defined objective function—the posterior probability of the grammar. This is a more rigorous foundation than purely heuristic or ad-hoc rule induction methods.
4.  **Strong Empirical Results on Image Grammars:** The experiments on both synthetic and real image datasets demonstrate a significant performance improvement over a directly comparable and highly relevant prior method [17]. The substantial reduction in perplexity on the real animal faces dataset is a strong piece of evidence supporting the proposed approach's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Methodological Soundness and Approximations:** The paper's claim of optimizing a posterior probability is weakened by significant approximations made for tractability.
    *   The use of the Viterbi likelihood instead of the true marginal likelihood is a major simplification. While this has been used in prior work, it fundamentally changes the objective function. It introduces a heuristic "unambiguity bias" that is not well justified from a purely Bayesian perspective. The paper should discuss the potential drawbacks and biases introduced by this choice.
    *   The derivation of the posterior gain relies on an "unambiguity assumption." This is a strong assumption that may not hold in practice, and its impact on the learning algorithm's convergence or the quality of the learned grammar is not analyzed. For a top-tier venue like NIPS, the reliance on such unexamined approximations is a notable weakness.
2.  **Limited Baselines and Experimental Scope:** While the comparison against [17] for image grammars is strong, the overall experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
    *   The baselines for the event grammar learning task [15, 18] are relevant but not the most recent. The comparison would be more convincing if benchmarked against more contemporary sequence modeling or grammar induction techniques.
    *   The paper misses an opportunity to situate its work within the broader context of modern deep generative models. While AOGs offer interpretability that many deep models lack, a comparison of generative quality (e.g., sample likelihood on held-out data) against models like VAEs or autoregressive models would provide a valuable benchmark for the model's representational power.
3.  **Clarity on Key Derivations:** The derivation of the likelihood gain, which is the technical core of the search procedure, is summarized very briefly in the main paper and deferred to the supplementary material. Providing more intuition or a clearer sketch of this derivation in the main text would significantly improve the paper's self-containedness and allow the reader to better assess the method's mechanics and assumptions. The connection to tensor/matrix coherence measures is mentioned but not sufficiently explained to offer real insight.
4.  **Evaluation of Event Grammar Learning:** The results on the event grammar task are less convincing than on the image task. The performance on the first dataset is only "competitive," and the outperformance on the second, more complex dataset is not supported by statistical significance testing. Furthermore, the fact that three different pre-processing methods were tested, with one performing poorly, suggests that the overall pipeline may be sensitive to data representation choices, which somewhat undermines the claim of a general, data-agnostic framework.

**Conclusion:**
The paper introduces an interesting and novel idea—the "And-Or fragment"—for unsupervised grammar induction. The motivation is sound, and the results on image grammar learning are impressive. However, the work's methodological rigor is compromised by the use of significant and under-analyzed approximations (Viterbi likelihood, unambiguity assumption). While these are pragmatic choices, they distance the algorithm from the principled Bayesian objective it claims to optimize. Coupled with a somewhat limited set of baselines, particularly for the event modeling task, the paper feels more like a strong workshop contribution presenting a promising new heuristic rather than a complete, rigorously validated method suitable for NIPS. The core idea is good, but it requires a more thorough theoretical analysis of its approximations and a more robust and broad empirical evaluation to be fully convincing.
