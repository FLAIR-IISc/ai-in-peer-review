

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper presents a formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to the data type (text, images, events), and proposes an unsupervised learning approach that simultaneously learns grammar structure and parameters. The authors build upon prior work on structure learning of natural language grammars [14-16] and And-Or grammars for images [17] and events [18], addressing the limitation of previous approaches that learn compositions and reconfigurations separately.

The key contribution is the introduction of "And-Or fragments" that unify the search for new compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules). The authors formalize the learning problem as optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar, and derive efficient sufficient statistics for computing the likelihood gain and prior gain during grammar expansion. The approach starts from a trivial grammar that perfectly fits the training data and iteratively adds And-Or fragments to improve the posterior probability. The paper demonstrates the approach on event grammars (using human activity datasets) and image grammars (using synthetic and real animal face datasets), showing competitive or better performance than prior methods.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper provides a clear and unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that can model different data types (Table 1, Section 2), which is valuable for cross-domain applications.
2. The theoretical derivation of the likelihood gain and prior gain (Section 3.3) is technically sound and provides a principled approach to grammar learning, avoiding heuristic search methods used in prior work.
3. The experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets demonstrates practical utility, with significant improvements over [17] on image grammars (Table 3) and better performance on a more challenging event grammar dataset (Table 2).
4. The paper clearly articulates how the proposed approach differs from previous work (e.g., "Unlike some previous approaches that rely on heuristics... we explicitly optimize the posterior probability").
5. The algorithm is well-organized with clear figures (Figures 1-3) illustrating the learning process and the And-Or fragment concept.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper claims to "learn both structure and parameters" but doesn't sufficiently distinguish this from [17, 18], which also claim to learn structure. The novelty in the structure learning approach (using And-Or fragments) could be more clearly contrasted with prior work.
2. The scalability of the approach to larger datasets isn't thoroughly addressed; the experiments use relatively small datasets (5000 training samples for images, 61 videos for events), and the paper doesn't discuss computational complexity in relation to data size.
3. The theoretical analysis of the posterior gain (Section 3.3) is mathematically dense without sufficient intuitive explanation, potentially limiting accessibility for readers unfamiliar with grammatical inference.
4. The evaluation on event grammars is limited to two datasets, with the second being a synthetic modification of the first, which may not fully demonstrate the method's generalizability.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pros:*
- Provides a theoretically grounded approach to unsupervised structure learning that unifies composition and reconfiguration learning
- Demonstrates significant improvements over prior work on image grammars (Table 3)
- The unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars is valuable for cross-domain applications
- The approach is more robust to data scarcity than previous methods (as claimed in the paper)

*Cons:*
- The novelty claim could be strengthened by more explicit comparison to [17, 18]
- Limited experimental validation on diverse, large-scale datasets
- Theoretical analysis lacks intuitive explanation for broader accessibility
- The algorithm's computational complexity isn't thoroughly analyzed

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of unsupervised grammar learning. The formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars is clear and generalizable across data types, and the proposed learning approach offers a principled way to optimize grammar structure and parameters. The experimental results are convincing, showing clear improvements over prior methods on both image and event grammar learning tasks.

The paper addresses an important problem in unsupervised learning of compositional structures, with potential applications in multiple domains. The method's ability to learn from unannotated data and its unified approach to structure learning represent meaningful advances over previous work.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with appropriate citations to relevant prior work. The theoretical contributions are substantial and well-supported by the experimental results. While the novelty could be more precisely articulated in relation to [17, 18], the approach represents a significant improvement in the state of the art for unsupervised structure learning of stochastic grammars.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a technically sound contribution with clear novelty in the unified approach to grammar structure learning, and the experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over prior methods. The work aligns well with NIPS' focus on foundational contributions to machine learning with strong theoretical and empirical grounding.
