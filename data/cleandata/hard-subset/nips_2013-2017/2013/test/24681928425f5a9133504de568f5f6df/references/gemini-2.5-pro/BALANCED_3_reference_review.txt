### Summary

This paper presents a novel unsupervised learning algorithm for inducing the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars from unannotated data. The key idea is to move beyond prior work that either assumes a fixed grammar structure or learns compositional (And-rules) and reconfigurable (Or-rules) aspects separately. Instead, the authors propose an iterative, bottom-up learning procedure that searches for and incorporates "And-Or fragments"—sub-structures that jointly define a new composition of alternatives. The learning process is framed as a principled optimization of the grammar's posterior probability, which combines a Viterbi likelihood term with a complexity-penalizing prior. A major technical contribution is the derivation of an efficient method to calculate the posterior gain of adding a candidate And-Or fragment, which relies on sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) and avoids costly re-parsing of the entire dataset at each step. The domain-agnostic nature of the framework is demonstrated through experiments on learning both event grammars from video data and image grammars from collections of animal faces, where the proposed method shows competitive or superior performance against relevant baselines.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Principled Approach:** The central contribution—learning And-Or fragments to unify the search for compositions and reconfigurations—is a significant and elegant idea. It directly addresses a key limitation of previous structure learning methods (e.g., [15, 17, 18]) which often handle these two aspects disjointly, potentially leading to suboptimal or redundant grammars. Framing the learning process as the optimization of a well-defined objective function (grammar posterior) provides a solid theoretical foundation, moving beyond more heuristic-driven approaches.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Efficiency:** The derivation of the posterior gain, factorized into likelihood and prior components, is a strong technical contribution. Showing that the likelihood gain can be efficiently computed from pre-compiled sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) is crucial for the algorithm's feasibility. This makes the greedy/beam search practical, whereas a naive approach of re-parsing the data for every candidate fragment would be computationally prohibitive.

3.  **Generality and Significance:** The proposed formalization of And-Or grammars and the learning algorithm are domain-agnostic. This is a major strength, as it presents a general tool for unsupervised structure discovery. The successful application to two distinct and challenging domains—temporal event structures and spatial image structures—convincingly demonstrates this flexibility. Unsupervised structure learning for compositional models is a fundamental problem in machine learning, and this work represents a clear advance, with potential applications in language, vision, and beyond. The connection to sum-product networks also makes the work relevant to the broader graphical models community at NeurIPS.

4.  **Solid Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, covering both synthetic and real-world datasets in two different modalities. The quantitative comparisons against relevant prior work ([17, 18]) are favorable and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, particularly on the more complex datasets (e.g., the second event dataset and the real image dataset). The ablation-like study of three different methods for the event grammar task provides useful insights into the model's capabilities.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity on the Search Procedure:** The paper is less clear on the practical implementation of the search for optimal And-Or fragments. Section 3.3 states that the algorithm searches within n-gram tensors for sub-tensors with high posterior gain using greedy search or beam search. This description is too high-level for reproducibility. It would be very beneficial to include:
    *   A more detailed explanation of how candidate fragments (i.e., sub-tensors and their corresponding contexts) are generated and enumerated. What is the search space and how is it traversed?
    *   Pseudocode of the overall learning algorithm, including the inner loop that identifies the best fragment to add at each iteration. This could be placed in the supplementary material if space is a concern.

2.  **Reliance on Viterbi Approximation and Unambiguity Assumption:** The method approximates the true likelihood with the Viterbi likelihood and relies on an "unambiguity assumption" to derive the efficient update for the posterior gain. While this is a common and often effective simplification, its implications should be discussed more thoroughly.
    *   How does the Viterbi approximation affect the ability to model inherently ambiguous phenomena where multiple parses are plausible?
    *   What are the failure modes when the grammar becomes highly ambiguous and the core assumption is violated? A brief discussion of these limitations would strengthen the paper and provide a more balanced perspective.

3.  **Lack of Qualitative Results:** The evaluation is entirely quantitative (F-measure, perplexity, KL-divergence). While these metrics are important, they do not provide intuition about the quality of the learned structures. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including qualitative examples, such as:
    *   Visualizations of some learned And-Or fragments from the image and event grammar experiments. What meaningful compositions (e.g., "eye pair" from "left eye" and "right eye") or reconfigurations (e.g., different types of "making a phone call") were discovered?
    *   Example parses of a few test images or event sequences using the learned grammars. This would help readers understand what the model is actually capturing.

4.  **Scalability Concerns:** The paper mentions the algorithm is fast on the tested datasets. However, the complexity seems to depend on constructing n-gram tensors. The size of these tensors grows exponentially with `n` (the number of children in an And-rule) and polynomially with the vocabulary size. A brief discussion on the computational complexity and the practical limits of the approach for larger-scale problems (e.g., larger vocabularies, deeper hierarchies) would be a valuable addition.

### Decision

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, principled, and effective algorithm for a fundamental and challenging problem in unsupervised learning. The core idea of jointly learning compositional and reconfigurable structure via "And-Or fragments" is a significant contribution. The technical development is sound, and the empirical results are convincing across multiple domains. The weaknesses, primarily related to clarity on implementation details and a lack of qualitative results, are addressable and do not undermine the core contributions. The paper's strengths in originality, significance, and technical quality make it a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS.

**Recommendation: Accept**
