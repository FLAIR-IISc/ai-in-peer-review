- Proposes an iterative unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs) from unannotated data.  
- Introduces “And-Or fragments” to jointly induce compositional and reconfiguration aspects of grammars.  
- Optimizes the posterior probability of the full grammar and leverages sufficient statistics for efficient learning.  
- Presents a unified formalism for SAGs applicable to text, images, and events.  
- Empirically evaluates on event and image grammars, showing comparable or superior performance to selected baselines.  
- Builds on prior stochastic grammar and Bayesian model merging work, unifying composition and reconfiguration learning.  
- Connects conceptually to sum-product networks and existing grammar learning literature.  
- Strength: unified, cross-modal formalism that advances conceptual understanding of SAGs.  
- Strength: simultaneous structure and parameter learning enables flexible model adaptation.  
- Strength: And-Or fragments improve efficiency and robustness in learning.  
- Strength: Posterior optimization with derived sufficient statistics provides a principled learning framework.  
- Strength: Clear writing and well-organized presentation.  
- Weakness: Scalability to high-dimensional data or large datasets is not thoroughly analyzed.  
- Weakness: Limited sensitivity analysis for key hyperparameter α (sparsity prior).  
- Weakness: Comparisons to more recent or state-of-the-art methods, especially in image tasks, are limited.  
- Weakness: Assumption of grammar unambiguity may be restrictive and lacks discussion of impacts.  
- Weakness: Event grammar experiments use a small dataset (55 samples), limiting claims of generalizability.  
- For acceptance: novel, theoretically sound approach with promising empirical results and potential cross-domain impact.  
- Against acceptance: concerns about scalability, limited sensitivity analysis, and incomplete comparisons to recent methods.  
- Recommended revisions: add computational complexity/scalability discussion, sensitivity analysis for α, broader comparisons, discussion of unambiguity assumption, and larger-scale experiments.
