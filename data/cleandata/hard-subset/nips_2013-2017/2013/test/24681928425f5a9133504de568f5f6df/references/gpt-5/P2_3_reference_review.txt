Summary
The paper proposes an unsupervised method to jointly learn the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs) across modalities (text, images, events). The key idea is to grow the grammar from a trivial initial model by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments—collections that include a new And-node with multiple sibling Or-nodes—so that both composition (And) and reconfiguration (Or) are induced together. The search is guided by an explicit posterior objective combining a sparsity prior on grammar size and a Viterbi approximation to the likelihood. A main technical contribution is deriving efficient estimates of the posterior gain of candidate fragments using sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor (co-occurrences of child choices within a fragment) and a context matrix (co-occurrences with surrounding context). Under an unambiguity assumption, the likelihood gain factorizes into two coherence terms of these statistics. Experiments on event parsing and image grammar induction show improvements over prior AOG structure-learning approaches.

Relation to prior work
This builds on AOGs for images/events and on unsupervised grammar induction (e.g., Bayesian model merging, PCFG induction, Viterbi training). Compared to prior AOG structure learning [Si & Zhu; Si et al.], which uses more templated or heuristic structure search, the paper optimizes an explicit posterior and proposes a joint And-Or fragment operation. The connection to sum-product networks is acknowledged; however, SPN structure learning literature could be discussed in more depth (e.g., Gens & Domingos 2013; Rooshenas & Lowd 2014), as the proposed factorization and fragment search has conceptual overlap with decomposable architectures.

Quality
- Soundness: The objective and search strategy are reasonable, and deriving a closed-form posterior gain using sufficient statistics is a clear advance for efficiency. However, the derivation critically relies on (i) Viterbi likelihood and (ii) an unambiguity assumption to avoid re-parsing. The practical validity and impact of unambiguity on learned structures are not empirically probed. Similarly, the claim that any context-free AOG can be constructed using only And-Or fragments should be clearly proved (the text says “it can be shown”). The encoding prior uses per-symbol code lengths sa and so; the choice and sensitivity of these constants are not discussed.
- Empirics: The image experiments include a synthetic study with F-measure and KL divergence to a known ground-truth grammar (good), and a real dataset where perplexity is lower than [17]. Event experiments compare to two baselines and variants of the proposed method. While results are promising, baselines are relatively narrow (older AOG works and ID-sequence approaches). Missing comparisons include general-purpose structure learners (e.g., PCFG induction for temporal sequences; SPN structure learning) and ablations isolating the benefit of And-Or fragments versus separate And/Or search. There is no sensitivity analysis (alpha, beam width), error analysis, or report of runtime scaling on larger grammars/samples.
- Reproducibility: Key algorithmic details are sketched but some are deferred to supplementary material (derivation, pseudocode). Implementation choices for relation types, smoothing for perplexity, and how contexts are defined in different domains need more specifics.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with a unified AOG formalization. The And-Or fragment notion and the sufficient-statistics view are clearly motivated. Some presentation issues remain:
- Several figure captions/labels are garbled; clearer, self-contained diagrams of fragments, tensors, and context matrices would help.
- Define precisely the “context” columns used in the context matrix across modalities.
- Explain how energy terms in And-rules are parameterized in practice and how probabilities are computed from energies.
- State and discuss the unambiguity assumption up front and its practical implications/violations.
- Describe smoothing used for perplexity and the choice of code lengths sa and so.

Originality
The joint And-Or fragment operation and the posterior-gain computation via tensor/matrix coherence are novel and interesting. The explicit unified treatment of composition and reconfiguration addresses a known pain point in prior structure learning for AOGs. The formalization across modalities is useful, although leveraging Viterbi training and sparsity priors are established ideas. The work is distinct from prior AOG structure learning and older Bayesian model merging methods.

Significance
If robust, the proposed strategy could influence structure learning for compositional probabilistic models beyond AOGs. Showing comparable or better performance on both events and images is encouraging. However, the scope of datasets is modest, and missing comparisons and analyses limit confidence in generality and scalability. Stronger evidence on larger-scale, diverse domains (e.g., standard PCFG induction benchmarks, richer video datasets) would increase impact.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, principled objective with an efficient search criterion based on sufficient statistics.
- Conceptually clean And-Or fragment that unifies composition and reconfiguration; likely to reduce redundancy and improve robustness.
- Cross-domain applicability demonstrated (events and images).
- Solid synthetic evaluation against ground-truth grammars; consistent improvements over prior AOG structure-learning baselines.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi approximations without empirical analysis of their effects.
- Limited baselines; no comparison to contemporary structure learners (PCFG/SPN).
- Incomplete clarity on some implementation details; figures need improvement.
- No ablation/sensitivity/runtimes to support efficiency and robustness claims; scalability evidence limited.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a proof (or clear sketch) that any context-free AOG can be built from And-Or fragments.
- Analyze sensitivity to alpha, beam width, and code lengths; report runtime and scaling with grammar size and sample length.
- Include ablations: separate And-only and Or-only fragment searches to quantify the benefit of joint fragments.
- Evaluate the impact of violations of unambiguity; does the coherence-based gain remain predictive?
- Add baselines from PCFG/SPN structure learning where applicable, and consider a standard NLP induction benchmark.
- Clarify how contexts are defined per domain and how And-rule energies are parameterized/learned.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper presents a thoughtful and potentially impactful approach to unsupervised structure learning for AOGs, with a principled objective and efficient search criterion, and shows cross-domain benefits. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarifying assumptions would solidify the contribution.
