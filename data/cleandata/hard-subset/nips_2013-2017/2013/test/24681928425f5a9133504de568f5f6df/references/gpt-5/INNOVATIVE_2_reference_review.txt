Summary
The paper proposes an unsupervised method to learn both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) from unannotated data. The key idea is to iteratively add And-Or fragments—small subgraphs consisting of one new And-node and a set of sibling Or-nodes—to optimize the grammar’s posterior under a sparsity prior. This unifies the discovery of compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules) and contrasts with previous strategies that search for And- and Or-structures separately. The method approximates the likelihood by Viterbi likelihood and introduces an efficient posterior-gain computation based on sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor over fragment configurations and a context matrix, yielding a factorization into coherence terms. The approach is presented as modality-agnostic and evaluated on event parsing (two activity datasets) and image parsing (synthetic with known ground truth and a real animal-face dataset), outperforming prior AOG structure-learning baselines.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on stochastic grammars for images and events (Zhu & Mumford; Si & Zhu; Pei et al.) and on unsupervised grammar induction in NLP (Baker, Klein & Manning, Stolcke & Omohundro, Solan et al., Tu & Honavar). It positions AOGs as closely related to decomposable SPNs (Poon & Domingos) and adopts Viterbi training and unambiguity bias (Spitkovsky et al.; Tu & Honavar). Compared to prior AOG structure learning for images/events (e.g., [17, 18]), which use more heuristic or decomposed searches, this work’s novelty is the unified And-Or fragment insertion with explicit posterior optimization and efficient gain evaluation. The MDL-like sparsity prior is in line with Bayesian model merging traditions, but here instantiated for AOGs with sufficient statistics tailored to candidate fragments.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: And-Or fragments provide a clean unification of learning composition and reconfiguration, addressing data scarcity and ambiguity that plague separate And- or Or-only searches. The claim that any context-free AOG can be assembled by such fragments is appealing.
- Principled objective: Optimizing a posterior with a size penalty, using Viterbi likelihood, is sensible and consistent with grammar induction practice.
- Efficient scoring: The posterior gain formula, using n-gram tensors and context matrices and factorizing into coherence measures, is elegant and practically useful. It reduces re-parsing costs and gives an interpretable criterion (intra-fragment and fragment-vs-context “context-freeness”).
- Modality-agnostic formalization: A single framework applied to events and images, with different relation types (temporal vs spatial), is valuable.
- Empirical results: Competitive on the standard event dataset and improved performance on a harder variant; substantial improvements on the synthetic image grammar (both F1 and KL) and on real images (perplexity halved vs [17]). Runtime claims suggest practicality.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumption of unambiguity: The efficient gain computation hinges on grammar unambiguity, which often fails in practice for compositional models. The paper should quantify how often ambiguity arises, how violations impact the gain estimate, and whether unambiguity regularization is used during learning.
- Limited baselines: For images, comparison is only to [17]; for events, only to [15, 18]. Given the connection to SPNs, a comparison to structure-learning SPNs (e.g., Gens & Domingos, 2013) or modern PCFG/SPN induction would strengthen claims. Likewise, comparisons to recent AOG learning for vision would be informative.
- Experimental scale and metrics: Datasets are relatively small (61 videos; 320 images). The real-image evaluation uses perplexity with post-hoc smoothing; more task-driven metrics (e.g., parse accuracy, segmentation/part localization) would better demonstrate utility. Ablations (And-only, Or-only, And-Or; with/without context coherence; different fragment arities) are missing.
- Search complexity and guarantees: Searching high-order sub-tensors is combinatorially challenging. While greedy/beam search “works,” the paper lacks complexity analysis, sensitivity to beam width, and failure cases. The claim that any context-free AOG can be constructed from And-Or fragments should be accompanied by at least a proof sketch.
- Hyperparameters and MDL prior: The prior uses α, sa, so as code lengths; these choices affect results. Provide guidance or data-driven selection (e.g., MDL-based coding scheme), and sensitivity analyses.
- Clarity and presentation: Some figures look garbled in the submission (e.g., Fig. 1–3), making it hard to follow examples. The derivation details and the precise definition of “coherence” used should be at least summarized in the main text.

Questions for the authors
- How robust is the posterior-gain approximation when parses are ambiguous? Can you report the proportion of samples with multiple Viterbi parses during training and test?
- What fragment arities n were considered in practice, and how does performance/runtime scale with n?
- Did you try SPN structure learning as a baseline on the image data? If not, why is it unsuitable?
- How are sa and so chosen? Would a universal coding scheme give a parameter-free prior?
- Can you provide ablations showing the contribution of the n-gram tensor vs context matrix factors?

Pros
- Unifies composition and reconfiguration learning via And-Or fragments.
- Principled posterior objective with efficient, interpretable gain scoring.
- Modality-agnostic framework demonstrated on events and images.
- Empirically improves over prior AOG structure-learning methods.

Cons
- Relies on unambiguity; practical impact not quantified.
- Limited baselines and small-scale datasets; lack of ablations.
- Incomplete discussion of search complexity and theoretical guarantees.
- Some clarity issues and dependence on pre-quantized terminals.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The core idea—learning AOGs by inserting And-Or fragments scored by a coherent posterior-gain criterion—is novel and promising, and the cross-modal demonstrations, while modest in scale, are encouraging. With stronger baselines, clearer derivations, and some ablations, this could be a solid contribution. Given NeurIPS’s interest in compositional and structured generative models, I lean toward acceptance for the conceptual advance.

Confidence
Moderate.
