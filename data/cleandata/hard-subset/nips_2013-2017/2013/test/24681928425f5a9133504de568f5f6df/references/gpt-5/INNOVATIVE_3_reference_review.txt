Summary
This paper proposes an unsupervised method to jointly learn the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) across modalities (text, images, events). The key idea is to start from a trivial “memorizing” grammar and iteratively insert And-Or fragments—each comprising one And-rule and multiple sibling Or-rules—chosen to maximize posterior probability under a sparsity prior and Viterbi (best-parse) likelihood. The authors derive an efficient posterior-gain objective based on sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor over candidate configurations and a context matrix over configuration–context co-occurrences. This gain decomposes into two coherence terms (in the sense of biclustering/coherence measures), plus a prior term penalizing grammar size. The approach unifies learning of compositions (And) and reconfigurations (Or), avoiding pitfalls of searching And- and Or-only fragments separately. Experiments on two activity datasets and two image datasets show competitive or improved performance versus prior AOG learning methods.

Relation to prior work
- Builds on stochastic grammars/AOGs for images and events [Zhu & Mumford; Zhao & Zhu; Pei et al.] and connects to decomposable SPNs.
- Extends grammar induction ideas (Bayesian model merging, ADIOS/Solan et al., Tu & Honavar biclustering PCFGs) to the And-Or setting and to multi-modal data.
- Improves over recent AOG structure learning for images/events [Si & Zhu; Si et al.] by unified And-Or fragment induction and explicit posterior optimization.
- Uses Viterbi training/unambiguity bias in the spirit of Spitkovsky et al.

Strengths
- Novelty: The And-Or fragment concept is a compelling, unified primitive that jointly introduces new compositions and their alternative realizations. This is a clear advance over separate And/Or search heuristics.
- Principled objective: Maximizes posterior with a sparsity prior; the sufficient-statistics formulation (n-gram tensor + context matrix) is elegant and computationally attractive, with a nice connection to coherence measures.
- Cross-modal formulation: A clean, type-agnostic definition of AOGs and a learning framework that transfers across events and images.
- Practicality: Greedy/beam search guided by closed-form posterior gains; reported runtimes are reasonable.
- Empirical signal: On event parsing, the method is competitive or better, especially when directly grounded to atomic actions and when co-occurrence and temporal relations are learned jointly. On image grammars, the method substantially outperforms a strong AOG baseline in both synthetic F1/KL and real-image perplexity.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumption of unambiguity: The posterior-gain derivation relies on grammar unambiguity and Viterbi likelihood. Real data can be highly ambiguous; it is unclear how robust the method is when this assumption is violated. Some theoretical or empirical stress tests would help.
- Limited breadth of evaluation: No experiments on text (despite the generality claim), and the event experiments rely on pre-segmented atomic actions; performance under noisy detectors is not assessed. The “synthetic subtype” dataset may not fully stress generalization.
- Baselines: Comparisons focus on prior AOG works. It would strengthen the case to compare to stronger grammar-induction baselines (e.g., HDP-PCFG/adaptor grammars, EM/Viterbi PCFGs) where applicable, and to structured prediction methods for events.
- Methodological details: Several core pieces are deferred to the supplement: derivation of the likelihood gain, exact construction of context matrices/relations, and pseudocode. The search procedure (beam width, restarts, stopping) and hyperparameter choices (α, sa, so) are under-specified, and there is no sensitivity analysis.
- Expressivity and relations: The approach presumes a fixed palette of relation types per domain (e.g., followed-by, co-occurs, spatial). How these are defined/chosen matters, but guidance is limited. Also, the context-free restriction may be limiting for many vision/event structures.
- Ablations: There is no ablation comparing And-Or fragments to And-only or Or-only searches, or evaluating the impact of the coherence-based gain vs. a simpler frequency-based heuristic.
- Reproducibility: No code/data release; some evaluation details (e.g., smoothing for perplexity) are brief.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to α and to the encoding costs sa, so? Are these set via MDL/description length or tuned on validation?
- How is n chosen for the n-gram tensor? Do you cap n adaptively? What is the effect on runtime and quality?
- How do you define “context” for images and events precisely, and how large is the context window?
- Have you tested robustness to ambiguous/overlapping parses or noisy atomic detections?
- Can the unambiguity assumption be relaxed (e.g., approximate expected gains under a packed forest)?
- Could you show ablations: And-only vs. Or-only vs. And-Or fragments; with/without context-coherence term?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a principled sensitivity/ablation study, and clarify search hyperparameters and stopping.
- Include at least one text experiment (or a more diverse event/image benchmark) to substantiate the modality-agnostic claim.
- Compare to stronger grammar-induction baselines and to non-grammar structured models for events.
- Discuss and, if possible, empirically test violations of the unambiguity assumption.
- Provide more details on relation sets and context construction; include qualitative parse trees/part layouts.
- Release code and scripts to compute the sufficient statistics and posterior gains.

Overall assessment
This paper introduces a neat and, in my view, genuinely novel idea: learning And-Or fragments by optimizing a posterior gain computed from simple sufficient statistics. The unification of composition and reconfiguration learning is both conceptually clean and empirically effective. While some assumptions (unambiguity/Viterbi) and evaluation breadth leave room for improvement, the approach opens a promising direction for scalable, domain-agnostic grammar induction beyond language. Given the novelty and potential impact, I am inclined to recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifications and added analysis.
