- Proposes unsupervised structure and parameter learning for stochastic And-Or grammars by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments into an overfitted grammar and optimizing a sparsity-regularized posterior using Viterbi likelihood.
- Main technical contribution: efficient posterior-gain computation using an n-gram tensor and a context matrix, yielding a factorization into two “coherence” terms and closed-form Or-rule probability estimates.
- Fragment search is greedy or small-beam with stop-when-no-gain; experiments on event and image data show improvements over prior AOG methods that fixed structure or separated composition/reconfiguration.
- Situates work relative to stochastic grammars, PCFG induction, Viterbi training, and SPNs; key novelty is unified induction of compositions and reconfigurations and sufficient-statistics-based posterior optimization.
- The unifying cross-modal AOG formalization is plausible but demonstrated only on images and events, not broadly across modalities.
- Bottom-up fragment insertion under a size-penalizing prior and Viterbi likelihood is reasonable; derivations and factorization are compelling but many details are deferred to the supplement.
- Important technical details missing or unclear: full likelihood-gain derivation, exact “context” definition, handling of relations/energies in And-rules, and proofs relying on an “unambiguity” assumption.
- Prior encoding uses per-symbol lengths (sa, so) but the encoding scheme and sensitivity are unspecified.
- The unambiguity assumption and Viterbi approximation may bias learning; their empirical effects are not ablated or analyzed.
- Search procedure lacks complexity analysis, beam-size details, convergence diagnostics, and scalability characterization for large alphabets or richer relations.
- Experiments show positive results on modest-sized event and image datasets, but rely on annotated atomic actions and primarily compare to AOG-specific baselines.
- Missing comparisons to broader structure-learning baselines (e.g., HDP-PCFG, Bayesian model merging, SPN learning) and missing ablations (And-only vs Or-only, Viterbi vs full likelihood, unambiguity effects).
- Statistical significance, variability (error bars), and analyses of grammar compactness/interpretability are not reported.
- Paper is generally readable and well organized, but reproducibility needs: formal grammar-size/encoding definitions, explicit relation parameterizations, precise context definition, and detailed search/pseudo-code.
- Some figures are cluttered or corrupted and pseudo-code mentioned is absent from the main text.
- Novelty: And-Or fragment induction unifying composition and reconfiguration is a meaningful contribution; sufficient-statistics gain computation and coherence interpretation are novel in this setting.
- Potential impact: if robust, the approach could yield more compact, generalizable grammars for vision and event understanding, but broader adoption needs clearer algorithms and stronger empirical validation.
- Pros: principled unified structure learning, efficient sufficient-statistics-based gain computation, cross-modal framing with improved perplexity/KL/F-measure over prior AOGs.
- Cons: strong reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi approximations without ablations, deferred derivations and ambiguous context/prior encoding, limited baselines and dataset scale, unclear search complexity and hyperparameter sensitivity, no code release.
- Key questions for authors include precise context definition, values and sensitivity of sa/so/α, parameterization and estimation of relation energy terms, beam widths/restarts/runtime complexity, frequency/impact of ambiguity, and requested ablation studies.
- Suggestions: move key derivations and pseudo-code into main text, add broader baselines and ablations with error bars, analyze grammar size/interpretability and sensitivity to priors, clarify prior encoding (MDL), provide complexity analysis and scalability discussion, and release code.
- Overall recommendation: Weak Accept — principled and novel approach with promising empirical gains but needs clearer exposition and stronger experimental validation.
