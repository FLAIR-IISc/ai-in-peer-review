

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper proposes an unsupervised approach for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars, which are powerful models for representing compositional patterns in data. The authors formalize stochastic And-Or grammars in a data-agnostic manner, making their approach applicable to various domains including text, images, and events. The key innovation is the introduction of "And-Or fragments" that unify the learning of compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules), addressing limitations in previous approaches that learned these separately. The paper builds upon prior work on stochastic grammars in NIPS [1, 3, 7, 10] and structure learning for natural language grammars [14, 15, 16], while improving upon recent work on And-Or grammars for images [17] and events [18].

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a clear and general formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type, which is a significant conceptual contribution. This formalization enables the approach to be applied across different domains without modification.

2. **Novel Learning Approach:** The unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations via And-Or fragments is a compelling technical contribution. The authors demonstrate theoretically and empirically that this approach is more efficient and robust to data scarcity than previous methods that learn And- and Or-rules separately.

3. **Computational Efficiency:** The paper's use of sufficient statistics to compute posterior gains efficiently is well-motivated and addresses a key practical challenge in grammar learning. The derivation of the likelihood gain as a product of coherence measures (n-gram tensor and context matrix) is particularly elegant.

4. **Strong Empirical Results:** The approach is evaluated on both event grammars (using human activity datasets) and image grammars (both synthetic and real datasets). The results consistently show competitive or better performance than previous methods, with the most significant improvements on more challenging datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Computational Complexity:** While the paper mentions the algorithm runs in "a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples," it lacks a thorough analysis of computational complexity as the grammar size grows. For larger datasets or more complex grammars, the computational burden could become prohibitive, and the paper doesn't address this limitation.

2. **Evaluation Metrics:** The evaluation on the image dataset uses perplexity as the primary metric, but it's unclear how this relates to downstream tasks like object recognition or scene understanding. The paper would benefit from demonstrating how the learned grammars improve performance on these tasks.

3. **Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper doesn't compare with more recent approaches to structured learning, such as neural grammars or other deep generative models that can learn compositional structure. This limits the context for evaluating the significance of the contribution.

4. **Scalability and Practical Considerations:** The paper doesn't discuss how the approach scales to very large datasets or more complex patterns. For instance, the real image dataset contains only 320 images, which is relatively small for modern computer vision benchmarks.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1. The authors should expand the evaluation to include downstream task performance (e.g., classification accuracy using the learned grammars as features) to better demonstrate practical significance.

2. A more detailed analysis of computational complexity and scalability would strengthen the paper. It would be valuable to include runtime comparisons with different dataset sizes and grammar complexities.

3. The authors should consider comparing with more recent approaches to structured learning, such as those using neural networks to learn grammatical structures. This would help contextualize the contribution more fully.

4. The paper could benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding the assumption that each training sample consists of a single pattern (which they address with a modification for videos with multiple events).

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. The unified approach to learning grammar structure via And-Or fragments is novel and well-justified, with strong empirical results across two different domains. The paper addresses an important problem in machine learning with clear theoretical and practical value.

While the paper has some limitations regarding computational complexity and evaluation metrics, these are relatively minor compared to the strength of the core contribution. The approach is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated, making it a valuable addition to the literature on stochastic grammars.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field with a novel and well-executed approach to unsupervised grammar learning. The strengths clearly outweigh the weaknesses, and the paper meets all four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance) at a high standard for NIPS.
