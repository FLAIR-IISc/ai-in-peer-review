## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning, specifically within the framework of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs). The authors address a challenging problem – learning both the structure *and* parameters of SAGs from unannotated data – and propose a principled approach based on maximizing the posterior probability of the grammar. The theoretical grounding is strong, the algorithm is well-motivated, and the empirical results are promising, demonstrating competitive or superior performance on event and image grammar learning tasks. While the writing could be slightly more concise, the paper is generally clear and well-organized. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of inducing stochastic And-Or grammars without labeled data.  SAGs offer a powerful way to model compositional and reconfigurable structures found in various data types (language, images, events).  Traditionally, learning SAGs involves either fixing the grammar structure and learning parameters or manually crafting the structure. This work moves beyond these limitations by learning both simultaneously.  The authors build upon previous work in natural language grammar induction (e.g., Stolcke & Omohundro, Liang et al.) and recent efforts in structure learning for images/events (Si et al., Pei et al.). Crucially, they move beyond separate learning of compositions and reconfigurations by introducing “And-Or fragments,” a unified approach that addresses limitations in prior work, particularly concerning data scarcity and ambiguity. The authors correctly position their work as a generalization and improvement upon existing techniques.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Principled Approach:** The core strength lies in the explicit optimization of the posterior probability of the grammar, combined with a sparsity prior to avoid overfitting. This is a significant improvement over heuristic-based approaches.
* **And-Or Fragments:** The introduction of And-Or fragments is a clever and effective way to simultaneously learn compositions and reconfigurations, addressing a key limitation of previous methods. This unified approach demonstrably improves robustness and efficiency.
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The derivation of the posterior gain calculation and its efficient formulation using sufficient statistics is a significant theoretical contribution.  The link to tensor/matrix coherence measures provides valuable insight.
* **Generality:**  The paper provides a unified definition of SAGs applicable to various data modalities, making the approach broadly applicable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both event and image grammars are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The results consistently outperform or match state-of-the-art techniques. The synthetic image data provides a strong baseline.
* **Clear Explanation of Algorithm:** The iterative bottom-up learning process is explained in a clear and accessible manner, supplemented by helpful diagrams (Figures 1 & 2).

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Notation:** While the mathematical formulation is rigorous, the notation is dense and can be difficult to follow without careful reading.  A more intuitive presentation of some equations could improve readability.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The influence of the hyperparameter α (sparsity prior) is not extensively explored. A more thorough sensitivity analysis would strengthen the findings.
* **Computational Cost:**  Although the authors claim efficiency, the computational cost, particularly for large datasets, is not thoroughly discussed.  A comparison of runtime with competing methods would be valuable.
* **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** While the paper showcases successes, a discussion of scenarios where the method struggles (e.g., highly ambiguous data, complex structures) would provide a more balanced assessment.
* **Writing Style:** The writing is sometimes verbose and could be more concise. For example, some sections repeat information unnecessarily.



**4. Detailed Comments & Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Section 3.2 (Algorithm Framework):**  Clarify how the "greedy search or beam search" is implemented in practice.  What are the stopping criteria?
* **Section 3.3 (And-Or Fragments):**  Elaborate on the restrictions imposed on the types of grammar fragments that can be added.  Why were these specific restrictions chosen?
* **Section 4.1 (Learning Event Grammars):**  Explain the choice of the "co-occurring" relation. What benefits does it offer compared to solely relying on the “followed-by” relation?
* **Section 4.2 (Learning Image Grammars):** Provide more details about the image quantization process used for the real dataset. How does this pre-processing step affect the results?
* **Tables and Figures:**  Ensure that all figures and tables are clearly labeled and readily interpretable. Consider adding error bars to the experimental results.

**5. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Strong theoretical foundation and principled approach.
*   Novel and effective And-Or fragment concept.
*   Competitive or superior empirical performance.
*   Broad applicability across different data modalities.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in unsupervised learning.

**Con:**

*   Dense notation and occasional verbosity.
*   Limited analysis of parameter sensitivity and computational cost.
*   Lack of detailed discussion of failure cases.



**6. Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly concerning clarity and additional analysis, would further strengthen this already compelling work. This paper represents a significant advancement in unsupervised structure learning and has the potential to impact various fields dealing with compositional data.
