

# Review: Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars

This paper introduces a novel approach to unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or grammars by proposing a unified framework for learning both grammar structure and parameters. The key innovation is the introduction of "And-Or fragments" that simultaneously learn compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules), addressing a critical limitation in prior work that often learned these aspects separately.

The authors provide a formal, data-type-agnostic definition of stochastic And-Or grammars that extends beyond previous applications to images and events. Their core contribution is a structure learning algorithm that optimizes the posterior probability of the grammar rather than relying on heuristics. The approach starts from a trivial initial grammar and iteratively adds And-Or fragments that maximize the posterior gain, which the authors efficiently compute using sufficient statistics from training data.

This work significantly advances the field of grammar learning by solving the problem of learning both compositionality and reconfigurability in a unified manner. Previous approaches either learned these aspects separately (leading to inefficiencies and error propagation) or used restrictive assumptions about the grammar structure. The authors demonstrate that their unified approach is more robust to data scarcity and ambiguity, which is a critical advantage in practical applications.

The paper is technically sound with clear formalization, solid theoretical analysis of likelihood and prior gains, and a well-designed algorithm. The empirical evaluation on both event grammars (with human activity videos) and image grammars (synthetic and real animal faces) shows strong results, with the proposed approach outperforming previous methods in several cases. The third method (directly grounding to atomic actions and learning different relations simultaneously) demonstrated particularly strong performance, especially on the more complex dataset.

The paper's strengths are substantial: the unified learning approach through And-Or fragments is genuinely novel and addresses a fundamental limitation in prior work. The formalization is general enough to apply to multiple data types (text, images, events), and the algorithm's efficiency through sufficient statistics is well-justified. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate the approach's advantages over previous work.

I note that the evaluation is limited to two domains (event sequences and images), though the formalization suggests broader applicability. The paper could have included more discussion about hyperparameter sensitivity (though α was fixed to 0.5 in experiments), and the scalability to extremely large datasets isn't fully addressed. However, these are minor concerns given the paper's core contributions.

In terms of significance, this work represents a meaningful step toward more general compositional modeling. The unified approach to structure learning could influence future work in grammar-based models across multiple domains. The authors' insight that learning And and Or structures jointly avoids the pitfalls of separate learning (data scarcity for And-rules, posterior decrease for Or-rules) is particularly compelling.

Compared to previous NIPS work on grammar learning (such as the sum-product network approach [10] and previous And-Or grammar work [1, 3, 7]), this paper makes a clear conceptual leap by unifying the learning of compositionality and reconfigurability. It's not merely an incremental improvement to existing methods but a fundamentally new direction in grammar learning.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The figures (particularly Figures 1-3) effectively illustrate the learning process and the key concepts.

In summary, this paper presents a bold and novel approach to grammar structure learning that addresses a significant limitation in prior work. The unified And-Or fragments framework is theoretically sound, practically efficient, and empirically validated across two distinct domains. While the evaluation could be expanded to additional data types, the core contribution is substantial and represents a meaningful advancement in the field.

Given my emphasis on novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, this paper should be accepted. It introduces a significant new concept (And-Or fragments) that fundamentally changes how grammar structure learning can be approached, with strong empirical validation to support its claims. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field that would be valuable to the NIPS community and has the potential to influence future work in compositional modeling.

**Recommendation: Accept**
