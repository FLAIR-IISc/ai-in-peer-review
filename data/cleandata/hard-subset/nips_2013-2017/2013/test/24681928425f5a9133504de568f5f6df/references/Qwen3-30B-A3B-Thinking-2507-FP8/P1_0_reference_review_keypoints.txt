- Paper proposes an unsupervised algorithm to jointly learn structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars by iteratively adding And-Or fragments.  
- Formalizes stochastic And-Or grammars in a data-agnostic way and derives posterior optimization with sufficient statistics for efficient likelihood gains.  
- Strong theoretical foundation and technical soundness with clear derivations (Sections 2 and 3.3).  
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation on synthetic and real datasets for event and image grammars, showing consistent improvements over prior work [17,18].  
- Clear, well-organized presentation with illustrative examples and detailed algorithm description (supplementary material complements reproducibility).  
- Novel unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations via And-Or fragments, improving over methods that learn And/Or separately.  
- Significant practical impact for domains needing compositional structure learning (vision, NLP, activity recognition), enabling grammar learning without manual rule specification.  
- Minor clarity issue: relation between the context matrix and the grammar learning procedure is unclear and needs a concrete example.  
- Minor quality issue: runtime claims lack empirical runtime analysis or comparisons to prior methods and scalability evaluation.  
- Minor originality issue: connections to sum-product networks and other compositional models are mentioned but underdeveloped.  
- Limitations: paper does not address extensions to non-context-free grammars and comparisons to more recent grammar-learning methods beyond [17,18].  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel unified approach, rigorous theory, strong experiments, and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited computational complexity analysis, weaker connection to related models, and restricted experimental comparisons.  
- Overall recommendation: recommend acceptance — strong contribution with only minor weaknesses.  
- Specific suggestions: add runtime scaling analysis, expand discussion of sum-product networks, include a concrete context-matrix→fragment example, and discuss possible extensions to non-context-free grammars.
