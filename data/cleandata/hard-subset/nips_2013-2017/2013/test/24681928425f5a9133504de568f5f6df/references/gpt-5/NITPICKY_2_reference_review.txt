Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an unsupervised structure-learning method for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) that targets both grammar structure (rules) and parameters (probabilities/energies). Starting from a trivial, sample-memorizing initial grammar, the method iteratively inserts And-Or fragments—each consisting of an And-rule over newly introduced Or-nodes and their alternative expansions—to maximize a sparsity-regularized posterior under a Viterbi-likelihood approximation. The key claimed technical contribution is a fast evaluation of the posterior gain for a candidate fragment using “sufficient statistics” computed from the training data: an n-gram “tensor” over configurations and a “context matrix” over configuration–context co-occurrences, with a factorization into two coherence-like terms. The authors argue learning And- and Or-structure jointly via fragments alleviates data-scarcity and redundancy issues. Experiments on event sequences and image grammars suggest improvements over prior And-Or grammar structure learning methods [Si & Zhu 2013; Si et al. 2011] and over an image grammar approach [Wang et al. 2013].

This work connects to: Bayesian model merging and PCFG induction [Stolcke & Omohundro, Klein & Manning], Viterbi EM/unambiguity bias [Spitkovsky et al., Tu & Honavar], AOGs for vision/events [Zhu & Mumford; Si & Zhu], and decomposable SPNs [Poon & Domingos]. The idea of learning mid-level AOG fragments resembles grammar fragment induction in NLP, but the explicit joint And-Or fragment search appears novel in this context.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for learning compact, reconfigurable, compositional grammars without enumerating an intractable rule set; the And-Or fragment notion is a reasonable design choice to unify composition and alternatives.
- Posterior optimization with a sparsity prior is principled; using Viterbi likelihood is empirically sensible in grammar induction.
- Attempt to derive fast posterior-gain evaluation from counts is appealing and could make greedy/beam search feasible.
- Cross-domain applicability (events and images) is interesting; the real-image perplexity numbers indicate a substantial gain over [17].
- The paper positions AOGs as a unified formalism, with pointers to SPNs and SCFGs.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Technical clarity and correctness:
- Key math is underspecified or relies on unverified assumptions. The posterior-gain equation (Sec. 3.3) contains ambiguous notation (e.g., ‖RD‖n‖RD‖, products over c and e) and is not self-contained; the derivation is deferred to the supplement. Precise definitions of RD, contexts c, the mapping from reductions to Or/And probabilities, and the independence assumptions used are necessary in the main text to assess correctness.
- The “unambiguity” assumption is strong and unrealistic for AOGs in vision and events; the paper should discuss when/how it holds, and the bias it induces beyond standard Viterbi training.
- The factorization into two “coherence” measures is asserted with a citation to [22], which is a survey of biclustering algorithms, not a definitive source of a specific coherence measure used here. Please define the measures precisely and cite the original definitions.
- The claim “by adding only And-Or fragments, our algorithm can construct any context-free And-Or grammar” needs at least a proof sketch (normal-form argument). As stated, it is not substantiated.
- The prior P(G) uses an ad hoc size model with sa, so “bits per symbol” but no coding scheme. If this is MDL-inspired, please define the code. Otherwise α, sa, so are free knobs; sensitivity and principled selection are not discussed.
- Mixing “energy terms” and probabilities for And-rules is confusing. Are And-rule probabilities globally normalized or unnormalized energies? How are energy parameters learned? The paper seems to avoid parameter learning beyond Or-rule MLE; this should be made explicit.
- CYK parsing is mentioned for event detection, but the grammars can contain “co-occurring” relations (not pure string concatenation) and spatial/temporal relational constraints. The context-free reduction to CYK needs to be formalized (what is the yield, and what is the parsing semiring?).

Algorithmic details and scalability:
- The search space is huge even with fragments; complexity is not analyzed. How are n and relation types chosen? What is the beam size, restart policy, and stopping rule? What is the cost of constructing and storing all n-gram tensors and context matrices? For images, what constitutes a “context” and how large is the context space?
- The initial grammar memorizes each sample via a bespoke And-rule with relations; this can be massive. Memory/runtime implications and any pruning are not reported.
- The assertion that “reasonable And-Or fragments usually increase posterior” is empirical; provide guarantees or counterexamples. Without proofs, search may be brittle.

Experiments:
- Important quantitative results are missing. Table 2 with event F-measures is referenced but not provided. Only qualitative claims are made for the three methods; no numbers, variances, or statistical tests. This is a major omission.
- The train/test split for events uses only 6 test samples, which is very small. No cross-validation, no confidence intervals.
- The modification that converts top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes to handle multiple events per video is ad hoc and changes the model after learning; its effect should be quantified.
- For images, perplexity is reported with “smoothing” but the method is unspecified. Perplexity is sensitive to smoothing; include details and sensitivity analysis.
- Hyperparameters (α, sa, so, beam size) are largely unspecified or fixed without justification (α=0.5 on synthetic). No ablations on fragment type (And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or) beyond qualitative claims; no sensitivity to unambiguity/Viterbi choice.
- Baselines are limited. For language-like induction, comparisons to Bayesian PCFG induction or split-merge PCFGs (where applicable) would strengthen claims. For images/events, include more recent structure-learning baselines if possible.

Presentation and formatting:
- Several figures (Fig. 1, Fig. 2) are difficult to parse and contain garbled text (e.g., repeated “S S”, misaligned symbols). Some equations show typographical artifacts (double norms, exponents). References are inconsistently formatted; some entries miss publication details. Ensure all tables/figures referenced (Table 2, Figure 3) are present and legible.
- The definition of “context” for the context matrix is vague (“surrounding patterns”); please formalize for both sequences and images.
- The claimed connection to decomposable SPNs is not formalized; a brief mapping would help.

Pros
- Conceptually appealing And-Or fragment induction that unifies composition and reconfiguration.
- Posterior objective with sparsity prior; Viterbi approximation aligns with prior grammar induction successes.
- Cross-domain demonstrations; strong perplexity improvement on real images over [17].

Cons
- Critical derivations and definitions are missing or unclear; reliance on strong unambiguity assumptions.
- Incomplete experiments (missing Table 2), limited baselines, weak statistical validation.
- Several ad hoc choices (top-level conversion, smoothing, prior coding) without analysis.
- Presentation/formatting issues impede understanding and reproducibility.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea has merit, but the current submission lacks sufficient technical clarity and complete empirical evidence. With full derivations in the main text (or a much-expanded supplement), clearer definitions, stronger, reproducible experiments (including the missing results), and improved presentation, this could become a solid contribution.

Confidence
Moderate.
