Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses unsupervised induction of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), proposing a unified, context-free formalization applicable across modalities (text, images, events). The key algorithmic idea is to jointly learn structure and parameters by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments—each comprising a new And-node whose children are new Or-nodes, which in turn select among existing nodes—rather than separately searching for And-only or Or-only fragments. The objective is a posterior over grammars with a sparsity prior on grammar size and a Viterbi (best-parse) likelihood, in line with prior observations that Viterbi training can help in unsupervised grammar learning. The authors derive an efficient approximation to the posterior gain for candidate fragments using sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor of covered configurations and a context matrix capturing surrounding patterns; these lead to factorized “coherence” terms inspired by biclustering. They give a constructive argument that And-Or fragments suffice to build any context-free AOG. Experiments on two event datasets (indoor activities; and a harder variant with split action subtypes) and two image settings (synthetic with known ground truth grammar; real animal faces) suggest the method compares favorably to prior structure-learning approaches for events [Solan et al., Tu & Honavar; Si et al. for events] and images [Si & Zhu].

The work builds on several strands: Bayesian model merging and grammar induction in NLP [Stolcke & Omohundro; Solan et al.; Tu & Honavar], AOGs in vision and events [Zhu & Mumford; Zhao & Zhu; Si et al.], and connections to decomposable sum-product networks [Poon & Domingos]. The main novelty is the unified And-Or fragment search with an explicit posterior gain criterion based on sufficient statistics.

Strengths
- Clear high-level motivation: jointly learning compositions (And) and alternatives (Or) reduces redundancy and improves robustness under data scarcity/ambiguity compared to learning them separately.
- General framework spanning modalities; the abstract definition of terminals, nonterminals, and relations is appealing and potentially impactful.
- Derivation of an efficient, count-based posterior-gain approximation via n-gram tensors and context matrices, with a useful interpretation as coherence measures.
- Empirical gains over prior methods on both event and image data; on real images, perplexity is substantially reduced compared to [Si & Zhu, PAMI 2013].
- Practicality: greedy/beam search with random restarts; reported runtimes are modest for moderate-sized datasets.

Weaknesses and detailed assessment
Quality
- The likelihood-gain derivation assumes grammar unambiguity and relies on Viterbi likelihood. Many interesting grammars are ambiguous; it would help to quantify how violations affect the approximation and results (e.g., via synthetic experiments varying ambiguity).
- The treatment of And-rule probabilities/energies is not fully specified. The paper derives optimal Or-rule probabilities from reduction counts, but it is unclear how the energy terms for And-rules are parameterized/estimated during fragment insertion and globally. This is critical for likelihood computation and reproducibility.
- The prior over grammar size uses e^{-α‖G‖} but the exact coding scheme is under-specified (sa and so are “bits per RHS node” yet fixed constants). Results’ sensitivity to α, sa, so is not reported.
- The adjustment to handle multiple events per video (converting top-level Ands to Ors at the end) is ad hoc relative to the generative model and breaks the single-pattern-per-sample assumption. A more principled multi-instance formulation (e.g., a top-level repetition operator) would strengthen the story.
- Search is greedy/beam without guarantees; an ablation comparing And-Or vs And-only vs Or-only learning would substantiate the claimed benefits.

Clarity
- Generally well written at a high level, but several definitions are underspecified:
  - “Relations” in And-rules (temporal, spatial) are only qualitatively described; the energy parameterization and how relations interact with the context matrix are unclear.
  - “Context” of a configuration is informal (“surrounding patterns”); a precise definition across modalities would aid reproducibility.
  - Pseudocode is deferred to the supplement; including a concise algorithm sketch in the main paper would help.
- Figures could be clearer; Figure 2’s numbers appear to be counts but are not labeled; Figure 1’s subfigures are hard to follow without more guidance.

Originality
- The And-Or fragment idea and the posterior-gain computation via coherence of configuration and context statistics are novel (to my knowledge) within AOG induction. The constructive claim that only And-Or fragments suffice is also nice.
- However, connections to structure learning in SPNs (e.g., Gens & Domingos, UAI 2013) and other grammar-learning frameworks (e.g., Adaptor Grammars, HDP-PCFGs) could be discussed and compared more thoroughly.

Significance
- Promising cross-domain modeling capability with empirical improvements is valuable. That said, the experimental scope is modest: small to mid-sized datasets; no NLP benchmarks despite claims of generality; limited downstream evaluation (e.g., detection/segmentation accuracy beyond purity measures).
- Scalability to larger vocabularies and longer sequences/images remains to be demonstrated, especially given the initial grammar and fragment candidate explosion.

Suggestions for improvement
- Specify and evaluate the parameterization/estimation of And-rule energies; include a complete likelihood expression used in scoring.
- Analyze sensitivity to α, sa, so; report the effect of beam width and restarts.
- Add ablations: And-only, Or-only, and And-Or to validate the central design choice; also test without the context term to show its contribution.
- Quantify robustness to ambiguity and to violations of context-freeness via controlled synthetic studies.
- Include comparisons to SPN structure learners and to Bayesian PCFG/HDP-PCFG baselines; add at least one NLP grammar induction benchmark to support modality-agnostic claims.
- Consider a principled top-level multi-instance model (e.g., repetitive Or or a Poisson number of events) rather than post-hoc conversion.
- Improve clarity: formal definition of “context,” algorithm pseudocode in main text, and clearer figures with legends.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean, unified approach to AOG structure induction with a novel And-Or fragment mechanism.
- Efficient scoring via sufficient statistics and coherence; practical and potentially scalable.
- Solid empirical gains over relevant baselines in two domains.

Arguments for rejection
- Important modeling and estimation details (especially And-rule energies) are under-specified; reliance on unambiguity/Viterbi without analysis.
- Limited experimental breadth (no NLP), modest dataset sizes, and ad hoc handling of multi-event videos.
- Lack of ablations and comparisons to closely related structure-learning frameworks (e.g., SPNs, Bayesian PCFGs).

Recommendation
Weak reject. The core idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper needs clearer specification of the probabilistic model and parameter learning, broader and more rigorous empirical validation, and ablations to substantiate its central claims. I encourage the authors to address these points; with stronger experiments and clearer modeling, this could be a valuable contribution.
