

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper proposes an unsupervised learning approach for stochastic And-Or grammars that learns both grammar structure and parameters. The key innovation is the use of "And-Or fragments" to unify the learning of compositions and reconfigurations, which the authors claim makes their approach more efficient and robust than previous methods that learn these components separately. The paper evaluates on event grammars and image grammars, claiming "comparable or better performance" than previous approaches.

**Strengths:** The paper provides a clear formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type, which is a useful contribution. The motivation for their approach is well-justified by the limitations of previous grammar learning methods, particularly the challenge of learning from scarce data. The experimental evaluation covers two different domains (event and image grammars), which demonstrates the approach's versatility.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising motivation, the paper falls short on several critical dimensions required for NIPS acceptance.

First, the experimental validation is insufficiently rigorous. The authors claim their approach "achieves comparable or better performance" than previous methods, but they only compare against two prior approaches ([15, 18]) that are not the strongest baselines available. Crucially, they do not compare against the recent work by Si et al. [17], which is directly related to their image grammar experiments and is cited as prior work. This omission is problematic as [17] represents the current state-of-the-art for image grammar learning. Without this comparison, the claim of "better performance" lacks proper context.

Second, the paper provides minimal statistical evidence to support their performance claims. Results are reported as averages over a few runs without standard deviations or confidence intervals. For instance, Table 2 shows F-measures without any indication of statistical significance. This is particularly concerning given the authors' claim that their method "outperforms previous approaches on the more complicated second dataset" - a claim that requires stronger statistical backing.

Third, the theoretical justification for the approach is somewhat superficial. The authors claim that using And-Or fragments "alleviates the data scarcity problem" but provide no empirical evidence to support this claim. They also fail to explain why their approach is fundamentally better than simply using a different prior or regularization scheme with existing structure learning methods.

Fourth, the paper lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors mention that their prototype implementation runs "within a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples," but this is not a meaningful scalability claim. They don't specify the exact hyperparameters used (e.g., the value of α), nor do they provide sufficient detail about the computational complexity of their approach.

Finally, the paper does not adequately address the relationship between their work and prior literature. While they cite [17] as related work, they fail to clearly articulate how their approach differs from this work in a way that justifies their claim of being an improvement. The paper reads as an incremental extension rather than a significant methodological advance.

**Quality:** The paper has a reasonable theoretical foundation but lacks sufficient experimental validation. The claims about performance improvements are not adequately supported by rigorous comparisons against appropriate baselines.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with good figures illustrating the grammar learning process. However, the lack of detail on implementation specifics and hyperparameters hinders reproducibility.

**Originality:** The core idea of using And-Or fragments to unify composition and reconfiguration learning is not entirely novel. While the authors claim to improve upon previous approaches, they don't sufficiently demonstrate how their approach differs from the related work they cite.

**Significance:** The paper addresses a meaningful problem in grammar learning, but the significance is undermined by the insufficient experimental validation and lack of clear comparison with the most relevant prior work. The claimed improvements do not represent a substantial advance over existing methods.

**Overall Recommendation:** I cannot recommend acceptance at NIPS. While the problem is interesting and the paper is well-written, the lack of rigorous experimental validation against appropriate baselines and the insufficient theoretical justification for the proposed approach fall below NIPS's high standards. The paper presents an incremental improvement rather than a significant methodological advance. To strengthen the paper, the authors would need to: (1) conduct comprehensive comparisons against the strongest relevant baselines (including [17] for image grammars), (2) provide statistical evidence for their performance claims, (3) offer a clearer theoretical justification for their approach, and (4) provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The paper's claims of "better performance" and "more efficient and robust" learning require much stronger evidence before they can be accepted as a meaningful contribution to the field. Without this, the paper fails to meet the quality and significance thresholds required for NIPS publication.
