

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper introduces an unsupervised learning approach for stochastic And-Or grammars that simultaneously learns both grammar structure (rules) and parameters from unannotated data. The authors present a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type (text, images, events), addressing a limitation of prior work that often required domain-specific adaptations. The key innovation is the use of "And-Or fragments" to unify the learning of compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules), in contrast to previous approaches that handled these separately. The method optimizes the posterior probability of the grammar, using sufficient statistics to efficiently compute the posterior gain when adding new fragments, and iteratively refines the grammar from an initial trivial structure.

The paper builds on previous work in stochastic grammar learning, particularly the structure learning of natural language grammars [14, 15, 16] and recent approaches for image [17] and event grammars [18]. Unlike prior methods that either fix the grammar structure or learn compositions and reconfigurations separately, this work introduces a more efficient and robust approach that handles both aspects simultaneously. The authors also provide a formal justification for their approach by showing that And-Or fragments can construct any context-free And-Or grammar.

The technical contribution is well-grounded. The paper provides a clear formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars (Section 2) and a rigorous derivation of the sufficient statistics for efficient posterior gain computation (Section 3.3). The algorithm is properly motivated by the need to avoid the limitations of separate And-fragment and Or-fragment learning (e.g., data scarcity issues for And-fragments and posterior probability decreases for Or-fragments). The experimental evaluation on event grammars (human activity videos) and image grammars (animal face sketches) demonstrates competitive or superior performance compared to previous methods [17, 18]. The results are presented with appropriate metrics (F-measure for event parsing, perplexity for image grammars) and include ablation studies showing the importance of the And-Or fragment approach.

The paper is clearly written with well-organized sections and helpful examples (Figures 1-3). The technical content is accessible to NIPS readers familiar with probabilistic modeling and grammar-based approaches. The authors properly contextualize their work against previous NIPS publications on grammars (e.g., [1, 3, 7, 10]) and provide a thorough comparison with relevant prior work.

Strengths:
- The unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars is general and applicable across domains (text, images, events).
- The And-Or fragment approach effectively unifies composition and reconfiguration learning, addressing a key limitation of prior methods.
- The theoretical justification for using sufficient statistics to compute posterior gain is sound and well-explained.
- The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with both synthetic and real datasets, and includes ablation studies.
- The paper provides clear examples and illustrations that aid understanding of the approach.

Weaknesses:
- The comparison with other grammar learning approaches could be more thorough, particularly with respect to alternative representations or learning paradigms.
- The theoretical analysis of the algorithm's convergence properties or optimality guarantees is limited.
- The discussion of computational complexity and scalability to larger datasets is superficial.
- The paper doesn't explore the limitations of the approach in more complex scenarios (e.g., very large grammars or highly ambiguous data).

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning for compositional models. The unified approach to learning grammar structure and parameters is novel and well-justified, with strong empirical results demonstrating its effectiveness across multiple domains. The technical soundness is high, with proper theoretical grounding and thorough experimental validation. The significance is evident in the ability to learn grammar structures from unannotated data across different application domains.

Pro:
- Novel unified approach to learning grammar structure and parameters
- Strong theoretical foundation with efficient sufficient statistics
- Comprehensive evaluation across two different domains
- Clear presentation and well-motivated technical contributions

Con:
- Limited comparison with alternative grammar learning approaches
- Superficial discussion of computational complexity
- Limited exploration of the approach's limitations in complex scenarios

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a significant contribution to the field of grammar-based models. The approach advances the state of the art in unsupervised structure learning of stochastic grammars and demonstrates practical utility through thorough empirical evaluation. The novelty is clear and significant, with a well-justified departure from previous approaches. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses an important problem in statistical learning with a theoretically grounded and empirically validated solution.
