

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

## Summary
This paper proposes an unsupervised approach for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars from unannotated data. The authors present a formalization of these grammars that is agnostic to data type (text, images, events), and develop an algorithm that iteratively adds "And-Or fragments" to optimize the grammar's posterior probability. This approach unifies the learning of compositions (handled by And-rules) and reconfigurations (handled by Or-rules), addressing limitations of previous approaches that learn these aspects separately. The method is evaluated on event recognition and image parsing tasks, showing competitive or better performance than prior work.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides a clear formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and derives the likelihood and prior gains efficiently using sufficient statistics. The theoretical foundations are solid, with well-motivated derivations of the posterior gain calculation.

2. **Conceptual Contribution**: The unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments is a significant conceptual advance over previous methods that treat these as separate learning problems. This approach directly addresses the data scarcity problem associated with learning compositions and the ambiguity problem in learning reconfigurations.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments on event grammars and image grammars are well-designed and demonstrate the approach's effectiveness. The comparison with relevant baselines (e.g., [17, 18]) shows clear improvements, particularly in the more challenging second dataset for event recognition.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written with clear organization. The examples in Table 1 and Figure 1 effectively illustrate the grammar concepts. The algorithm description is detailed enough for reproducibility.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation**: While the paper claims to be data-agnostic, the experiments are limited to two domains (event recognition and image parsing). More diverse evaluations across different data types would better substantiate this claim and strengthen the paper's contribution.

2. **Lack of Statistical Significance**: The results (Tables 2 and 3) show performance improvements but lack statistical significance testing. Without p-values or confidence intervals, it's difficult to assess whether the observed improvements are meaningful or just due to random variation.

3. **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of the approach. For instance, how does the algorithm scale to larger grammars or more complex data? What are the computational constraints of the method? How sensitive is the approach to the hyperparameter α?

4. **Incomplete Baseline Comparison**: The comparison with [17] in the image grammar experiments is limited. It would be valuable to include a more comprehensive comparison with other relevant methods in the image grammar domain, such as [10] (sum-product networks) or [13] (hierarchical space tiling).

5. **Viterbi Likelihood Justification**: The paper uses Viterbi likelihood instead of standard likelihood but doesn't provide a thorough justification for this choice. The paper mentions it "has been empirically shown to lead to better grammar learning results" but doesn't explain why it's appropriate for their approach.

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of grammatical structure learning, with a clear conceptual advance in unifying the learning of compositions and reconfigurations. The theoretical foundation is solid, and the experimental results are promising. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the limitations mentioned above.

The approach has clear significance for domains where compositional structure learning is important, such as computer vision, natural language processing, and activity recognition. The ability to learn from unannotated data is particularly valuable for real-world applications.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance of this paper, but with the following suggestions for improvement:

1. Include statistical significance testing for the experimental results to better demonstrate the robustness of the improvements.

2. Add a more thorough discussion of the approach's limitations, including computational complexity and scalability.

3. Expand the experimental evaluation to include additional datasets or domains to better demonstrate the data-agnostic nature of the approach.

4. Provide a more detailed justification for using Viterbi likelihood in the learning framework.

5. Include a more comprehensive comparison with relevant baselines in the image grammar domain.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. With these relatively minor improvements, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations is particularly noteworthy and represents a significant step forward in unsupervised grammatical structure learning.
