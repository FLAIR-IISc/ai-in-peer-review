Summary
The paper tackles unsupervised structure and parameter learning for context-free stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs). It proposes to grow a grammar from a trivial initial grammar by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments (a new And-node with several Or-children, each selecting among existing subparts), maximizing a sparsity-regularized posterior with Viterbi likelihood. A key claim is that the posterior gain of a candidate fragment can be computed efficiently via sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor of the fragment’s configurations and a context matrix tallying co-occurrence with surrounding “contexts,” yielding a factorization into “coherence” measures. Experiments on event parsing and image modeling compare against prior AOG structure-learning approaches, reporting improvements (F1 for events; perplexity for images).

Strengths
- Unifies the induction of composition (And) and reconfiguration (Or) via a single fragment, which is conceptually appealing and empirically reasonable.
- Attempts explicit posterior optimization under a sparsity prior rather than heuristic structure growth.
- The sufficient-statistics formulation is promising for efficiency, if correct; leveraging Viterbi likelihood is a practical choice.
- Cross-domain positioning (events and images) is aligned with the And-Or formalism’s generality; real and synthetic datasets are used.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
Quality
- Critical assumptions are under-specified or unrealistic. The posterior-gain computation assumes grammar unambiguity; AOGs for images/events are typically ambiguous. There is no sensitivity analysis to ambiguity violations, nor a fallback when multiple parses exist.
- Key derivations are omitted. The likelihood-gain equation and its factorization into tensor/matrix “coherence” are deferred; the paper provides neither a proof sketch nor enough definitions to verify correctness. The definition of “context” is vague (“surrounding patterns”) and task-dependent; how it is constructed for images vs. events is not specified precisely.
- Parameter learning is incomplete. The paper sets Or-rule probabilities to relative frequencies of reductions but does not specify how And-rule probabilities/energies over relations are parameterized or estimated (e.g., continuous relative positions for images, temporal intervals for events). Without this, the likelihood is ill-defined or implicitly uniform.
- Prior/MDL is underspecified. The grammar-size prior depends on sa and so (bits per RHS symbol) and α; their values, rationale, and sensitivity are not discussed, yet they strongly affect model selection. It is unclear whether relation specifications and parameters are included in the code length.
- Search space and complexity are weakly addressed. The number and arity of candidate fragments, allowed relation types, and how continuous relation spaces are discretized are not rigorously described. Claims of “few minutes on 5k samples” lack detail (hardware, n, beam width, memory), and no scaling curves are provided.
- The claim “adding only And-Or fragments can construct any context-free And-Or grammar” is made without proof.
- The initial grammar enumerates each training sample exactly, which can be extremely large; memory/runtime implications are not analyzed.
- Event experiments: fairness and pipeline concerns. Baselines ([15, 18]) operate on ID sequences only; the best variant here directly models atomic actions with both co-occurrence and followed-by relations, which the baselines cannot exploit—raising comparability concerns. The synthetic splitting of sitting/standing into subtypes is ad hoc and may favor the proposed method. The top-level conversion of And-nodes to Or-nodes at the end to handle multiple events per video is also ad hoc and alters the learned structure.
- Evaluation reporting is insufficient. For events, the exact F1 numbers and variance are missing in the provided text (Table 2 not visible); for images, perplexity is reported as single numbers without variance and with smoothing unspecified. For synthetic image grammar experiments, the ground-truth grammar and how KL divergence is computed are not described here; Figure 7 results are not reproducible from the text. No statistical significance tests, ablations (e.g., And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or), or hyperparameter studies are provided.

Clarity
- Numerous formatting and figure issues impede understanding: Table 1 and Figures 1–3 appear garbled (symbols like “a a2 5”, stray numbers, duplicated “S S”), axes/units missing, and captions incomplete. References are inconsistently formatted, with missing years/publishers and extra punctuation. Notation is occasionally inconsistent (e.g., “AndOr” vs “And-Or”).
- Several key definitions are informal (e.g., “context,” relations in And-rules across domains). Pseudocode is referenced but not present; many crucial steps are deferred to the supplement.

Originality
- Learning AOG structure by alternating composition and reconfiguration is not new per se; the novel aspect is the unified And-Or fragment insertion with an approximate posterior-gain computation. Related work on Bayesian model merging, SPNs, and prior AOG structure learning is cited, but the differences remain partially qualitative. The expressivity claim and the sufficiency/coherence factorization, if properly formalized, could be novel; as presented, they are underspecified.

Significance
- If validated, the fragment-based posterior optimization could be a useful heuristic for scalable AOG induction. However, due to the strong assumptions, missing derivations, and limited, uneven evaluation, it is difficult to assess impact. The current empirical evidence, without ablations and with fairness concerns, does not convincingly demonstrate clear state-of-the-art advances.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem (unsupervised structure induction for compositional models).
- Conceptually neat unification of And/Or via fragments; potentially efficient sufficient statistics idea.
- Cross-domain experiments indicate potential.

Cons for acceptance
- Derivations and key assumptions (unambiguity, context definition) are not rigorously justified; parameter learning for And-rules is unclear.
- Evaluation lacks detail, ablations, and statistical rigor; fairness to baselines is questionable.
- Scalability, complexity, and prior design are underdeveloped.
- Significant clarity and formatting issues.

Questions for authors
- Precisely define “context” and show how CM is constructed for events vs images; provide the full derivation of the likelihood-gain factorization and conditions under which it holds.
- Describe the parameterization and learning of And-rule relation energies/probabilities; are continuous relations discretized?
- Prove or justify the expressivity claim about building any context-free AOG using only And-Or fragments.
- Analyze sensitivity to α, sa, so; include these in the MDL code length of relations/parameters.
- Provide runtime/memory scaling and the exact search settings (n, beam width, candidate generation).
- Add ablations (And-only, Or-only, And-Or) and ambiguity stress tests.
- Clarify smoothing used for perplexity; report variances and statistical tests.

Recommendation
Reject in its current form. The paper has an interesting idea with potential, but the theoretical justifications are incomplete, several core modeling/details are missing, the empirical evaluation is not rigorous enough, and the manuscript suffers from substantial clarity/formatting issues. Addressing the above could make this a strong submission in the future.
