Summary
The paper tackles unsupervised structure learning for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), a generalization of context-free grammars related to sum-product networks, used to model compositional and reconfigurable structure across modalities (language, images, events). The authors propose to jointly learn grammar structure and parameters by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments—each comprising one new And-rule and multiple sibling Or-rules—into a trivial initial grammar that exactly generates the training set. The objective is a MAP criterion with a sparsity prior on grammar size and a Viterbi (best-parse) likelihood, promoting compact, unambiguous grammars. A key technical contribution is an efficient posterior-gain computation for candidate fragments based on sufficient statistics: (i) an n-gram tensor over configurations covered by the fragment and (ii) a context matrix capturing co-occurrence of configurations with their contexts. Under a grammar unambiguity assumption, the likelihood gain factorizes into coherence measures of these statistics; optimal Or-rule probabilities follow from counts. A prior gain term trades off fragment size against the number of reductions it enables. Search over fragments is greedy or beam-based with random restarts. Experiments on event grammars (two activity datasets) and image grammars (synthetic and real animal-face sketches) show improvements over prior AOG structure learners [Si & Zhu 2013; Si et al. 2011] and a quantized-scene model, with better F-measures and perplexities.

Strengths
- Clear, principled MAP objective with an explicit sparsity prior; practical Viterbi likelihood aligns with prior evidence for unsupervised grammar induction.
- Joint induction of composition and reconfiguration via And-Or fragments is elegant and addresses known pitfalls of alternating And/Or-only searches.
- Efficient sufficient-statistics view (n-gram tensor and context matrix) that reduces re-parsing and yields closed-form Or-rule probabilities.
- Conceptually unifying framework applicable to sequences (events) and 2D structures (images), with empirical gains over relevant baselines.
- Sensible search heuristics (greedy/beam) with demonstrable runtime claims, and a simple modification to handle multiple events per video.

Weaknesses
- The central likelihood-gain derivation relies on a strong unambiguity assumption and is deferred to the supplement; implications when the learned grammar is ambiguous are not analyzed.
- Limited empirical scope: relatively small datasets (61 videos; 320 images), few baselines, and no evaluations on natural language despite claims of generality.
- Important design choices are under-specified: how relations are parameterized/encoded (especially for images), how contexts are defined across modalities, how continuous relations are discretized, and how sa/so and α are selected; sensitivity analyses are missing.
- No ablation comparing And-Or fragments to separate And-only or Or-only searches within the same framework to validate the claimed robustness and compactness.
- No theoretical guarantees (e.g., consistency, convergence, approximation) or formal proof that any context-free AOG can be constructed solely from And-Or fragments (claimed but not shown).
- Evaluation metrics sometimes indirect (perplexity with smoothing) and parsing assumptions (CYK for event parsing) may not carry over cleanly to general AOGs with rich relations.

Quality
- Technically plausible and mostly sound at the algorithmic level, but key assumptions (unambiguity; context-freeness with rich relations) and deferred derivations weaken rigor. Empirical evidence supports usefulness, though broader benchmarks and ablations would strengthen claims.

Clarity
- Overall organization is reasonable, but several details are only in the supplement. Definitions of “context,” relation schemas, and data preprocessing (e.g., vectorization vs. IDs) need clearer exposition. Some figures appear garbled; algorithm pseudocode in the main text would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- The unified And-Or fragment search and sufficient-statistics factorization are novel and distinct from prior AOG structure learners and PCFG/SPN structure learning. The work builds on and extends prior grammar induction (e.g., Viterbi training, Bayesian model merging) in a new AOG setting.

Significance
- Moderate. Structure learning for AOGs is important for interpretable, compositional modeling across modalities. The approach appears to improve prior AOG learners and could spur follow-on work, but current scale and breadth of evaluation limit immediate impact.

Detailed comments and questions
- Please provide the full derivation of the likelihood gain under unambiguity in the main text (or summarize key steps) and discuss how violations of unambiguity affect the estimate and search.
- Clarify how “context” is defined for images and events and how contexts are enumerated efficiently. Are contexts tied to parent nonterminals or local neighborhoods?
- Specify how relations in And-rules are represented and learned: discrete templates vs. parameterized functions; how are continuous spatial/temporal relations quantized; are there energy terms beyond multiplicative probabilities?
- Justify the use and selection of α, sa, so; report sensitivity analyses and whether model selection is needed.
- Provide complexity analysis for computing the n-gram tensors/context matrices and for the fragment search; what are the bottlenecks and memory costs?
- Add ablations: (i) And-only vs. Or-only vs. And-Or fragments; (ii) with/without context matrix term; (iii) Viterbi vs. full likelihood if feasible.
- Expand baselines: PCFG/SPN structure learning (where applicable), modern grammar induction baselines, and stronger image compositional models.
- Report variability (std/CI) across random restarts; clarify smoothing used for perplexity and ensure fair comparison.
- The claim that any context-free AOG can be constructed via And-Or fragments needs at least a sketch proof.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually neat and practically useful unification of composition/reconfiguration induction.
- Efficient sufficient-statistics formulation; closed-form parameter updates.
- Positive empirical results on two modalities with improvements over prior AOG learners.

Cons for acceptance
- Heavy reliance on assumptions; limited theoretical backing and missing proofs.
- Narrow, small-scale evaluation and sparse baselines; missing ablations and sensitivity.
- Some clarity/reproducibility issues and modality-specific implementation details left implicit.

Recommendation
- Weak Accept, confidence: 0.7. The idea of And-Or fragment induction with a posterior-gain criterion and sufficient statistics is novel and promising, with encouraging cross-modal results. With stronger derivations, ablations, and clearer presentation, this could be a solid contribution to compositional generative modeling.
