Summary and contributions
The paper proposes an unsupervised method to jointly learn the structure and parameters of context-free stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs) from unannotated data. The key idea is to iteratively add And-Or fragments—each consisting of a new And node whose children are new Or nodes, with each Or node selecting among existing terminals/nonterminals—thus unifying the induction of compositions (And) and reconfigurations (Or). The method optimizes a posterior composed of a sparsity prior over grammar size and a Viterbi likelihood. A main technical contribution is an efficient estimate of the posterior gain of a candidate fragment using sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor of fragment configurations and a context matrix, yielding a product of two “coherence” factors (in the sense of biclustering). Greedy or beam search over candidate fragments is used, with random restarts. Experiments on event parsing from videos and image grammar induction (synthetic and a real animal-face dataset) show competitive or improved results over prior AOG learning methods.

Relation to prior work
This work builds on: (i) AOGs for images and events (Zhu et al.; Si & Zhu; Pei et al.), (ii) unsupervised grammar induction (Stolcke & Omohundro’s Bayesian model merging; Solan et al.; Tu & Honavar), and (iii) SPNs (Poon & Domingos). The proposed fragment search addresses limitations of learning only And- or only Or-fragments, and connects to structure learning in SPNs (not cited: Gens & Domingos, 2013, and follow-ups) and to PCFG split-merge (e.g., Petrov et al.). The Viterbi objective relates to known unambiguity/Viterbi training biases in grammar induction (Spitkovsky et al.; Tu & Honavar).

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear algorithmic idea: And-Or fragments provide a principled search unit that couples composition and reconfiguration. The sufficient-statistics formulation for posterior gain is elegant and computationally attractive.
- The posterior combines a size prior (MDL-like) with Viterbi likelihood; the prior term includes both cost of new rules and savings from reductions, which is sensible.
- Cross-domain applicability is demonstrated (temporal events and spatial image structures), with improvements over prior AOG learners in both synthetic and real settings.

Weaknesses/technical concerns:
- The derivation of likelihood gain assumes grammar unambiguity. This is a strong assumption for AOGs (especially with reconfigurations). It is unclear how violations affect the estimator or search; the paper should clarify whether “unambiguity” means unique Viterbi parses (almost surely), and how the method behaves when multiple near-ties exist.
- Parameter learning for And-rules is underspecified. The paper states And-rule probabilities are given by energy terms over relations, but only gives closed-form estimates for Or-rule probabilities. How are relation parameters (e.g., spatial/temporal relation costs) defined, estimated, or regularized? In the reported experiments, are And-rule energies uniform, discretized, or learned? This impacts both soundness and reproducibility.
- The notion of grammar “size” and the encoding costs sa and so are treated as constants; their selection materially affects the prior and the search. No sensitivity analysis is provided, and α seems fixed ad hoc. Without showing robustness to these hyperparameters, it is hard to assess generality.
- Search space and scalability: Constructing and scanning n-gram tensors across n and relation types could be expensive as vocabularies grow. There is no complexity analysis nor empirical runtime scaling beyond a single anecdote. Practical constraints on n, beam width, and restarts should be discussed and evaluated.
- Evaluation on events uses pre-segmented atomic actions and a small dataset, with an additional synthetic variant. Table 2 is referenced but numerical results are not presented in the text; statistical variability and significance are not reported. More modern baselines (e.g., hierarchical temporal models, SPN-based, or neural segmentation) and ablations (And-only, Or-only, And-Or) would strengthen claims.
- Image grammar evaluation: the synthetic study is appropriate but details on how KL and precision/recall are estimated from grammars are sparse. On the real dataset, perplexity is the only metric; smoothing method and variance across folds are not described. It would help to show qualitative parses and learned structures to assess interpretability.

Clarity
- The formalization of AOGs is useful, but the presentation mixes context-free assumptions with relation-dependent energies that may induce context sensitivity; more precise conditions under which the model remains context-free would help.
- The likelihood gain formula uses nonstandard notation (RD, RDi(aij), CM, e,c indices) without a fully self-contained derivation. A compact proof sketch in the main text (even if full details are in the supplement) would improve readability.
- The claim that any context-free AOG can be constructed by successive And-Or fragments should be supported by a proof sketch.
- Experimental sections omit key implementation details (parameterization of relations, hyperparameters, search settings).

Originality
- The unified And-Or fragment search is a novel and useful design choice that advances prior structure learning for AOGs and, conceptually, for SPN-like models.
- The posterior-gain estimator via coherence of configuration tensors and context matrices is an interesting contribution.
- While related to earlier Bayesian model merging and biclustering-based PCFG induction, the proposed approach differs in its fragment granularity and cross-domain generality.

Significance
- If the unambiguity and parameterization issues are clarified and addressed, the approach could be a practical tool for learning structured generative models across modalities.
- The improvements over prior AOG learners on images are promising; however, broader baselines and ablations are necessary to establish state-of-the-art impact.

Pros
- Clear motivation for And-Or fragments; elegant sufficient-statistics scoring.
- Joint structure/parameter learning with an MDL-like prior.
- Cross-domain demonstrations; strong perplexity gains on real images.

Cons
- Unclear treatment/assumptions for And-rule parameters and grammar ambiguity.
- Sensitivity to prior hyperparameters not explored.
- Limited baselines and missing quantitative details; small event datasets.
- Scalability and complexity not analyzed.

Questions for the authors
1) How are the energy parameters for And-rule relations defined and learned in the two domains? Are they fixed, discretized, or estimated from data?
2) What precisely is the unambiguity assumption? Does the method require unique Viterbi parses for all training samples? How does the scoring behave when multiple parses tie?
3) How sensitive are results to α, sa, so, beam width, and the maximum n? Please provide ablations.
4) Can you report runtimes and memory usage versus number of terminals and samples?
5) Can you provide an ablation comparing And-only, Or-only, and And-Or fragment search on the same tasks?
6) Please justify the claim that any context-free AOG can be constructed by repeated And-Or fragments.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The core idea is promising and methodologically interesting, with encouraging empirical results, but the paper needs clearer treatment of And-rule parameterization, ambiguity assumptions, and more thorough evaluation/ablations to reach a strong accept.
