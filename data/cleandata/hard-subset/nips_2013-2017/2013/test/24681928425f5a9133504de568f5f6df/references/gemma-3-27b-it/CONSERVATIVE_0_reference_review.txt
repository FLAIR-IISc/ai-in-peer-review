This paper presents an unsupervised learning approach for stochastic And-Or grammars, aiming to simultaneously learn both the structure and parameters of the grammar from unannotated data. The authors extend prior work in natural language grammar learning and adapt it to image and event data, proposing an “And-Or fragment” learning strategy that jointly induces compositions and reconfigurations. The core idea is to iteratively insert fragments into a starting trivial grammar, maximizing the posterior probability via efficient computation of sufficient statistics.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the problem and proposed solution. The formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars is a positive step towards a unified framework, and the motivation for simultaneously learning structure and parameters is sound. However, my overall assessment is skeptical, primarily due to a lack of compelling evidence demonstrating a significant advantage over established methods and the complexity introduced by the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Unified Framework:** The attempt to provide a general formulation for stochastic And-Or grammars applicable to various data types (text, images, events) is commendable.
*   **Principled Approach:** Optimizing the posterior probability, albeit approximated, is a sound strategy for structure learning.
*   **And-Or Fragments:** The concept of learning And-Or fragments to unify composition and reconfiguration is potentially beneficial.
*   **Sufficient Statistics:** Deriving sufficient statistics for efficient computation is a positive contribution, although the details are relegated to the supplementary material.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** While the combination of ideas isn't trivial, the individual components—stochastic grammars, structure learning, sufficient statistics—are all well-established areas. The paper doesn't present a fundamentally new theoretical breakthrough.
*   **Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments, while reasonably thorough, don't provide a convincing demonstration of superiority. On the event grammar dataset, performance is comparable to existing methods. The improvement on the more complex event dataset is noted, but the justification relies heavily on the dataset modification and its interaction with the proposed method, which feels post-hoc. The image grammar results are better but compare against a single prior work (SZ [17]). A comparison to more standard image parsing techniques (e.g., those based on Markov Random Fields or deep learning approaches) is missing.
*   **Complexity:** The proposed approach appears significantly more complex than existing methods. The benefit of this complexity (i.e., improved performance) isn't adequately demonstrated. The increased computational burden of jointly learning structure and parameters is acknowledged but not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of grammar unambiguity, while justified for efficiency, may be restrictive. The impact of this assumption on the learned grammar’s expressiveness is not discussed.
*   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper doesn’t include ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of individual components (e.g., the And-Or fragment strategy) to the overall performance.
*    **Overly Optimistic Claims:** The claim of “comparable or better performance” in the introduction feels exaggerated given the experimental results.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a principled approach to unsupervised structure learning.
*   The derived sufficient statistics may be a valuable contribution for efficient learning.
*   The unified framework could potentially facilitate cross-domain applications.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The novelty is incremental.
*   The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate significant improvement over established methods.
*   The increased complexity of the approach is not adequately justified.
*   The reliance on specific dataset modifications to show performance gains is questionable.
*   The assumption of grammar unambiguity could limit expressiveness.

**Overall:**

I find this paper to be technically sound but lacking in significant novelty and compelling experimental validation. The increased complexity of the proposed approach doesn't appear to be justified by the observed performance gains. While the paper might be of interest to researchers specifically working on stochastic grammars, it doesn’t represent a substantial advance that warrants acceptance to NIPS. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should consider simplifying their approach, conducting more comprehensive experiments, and providing a more convincing justification for the added complexity. A comparison with more standard image parsing methods is essential, and ablation studies would help isolate the contribution of individual components.
