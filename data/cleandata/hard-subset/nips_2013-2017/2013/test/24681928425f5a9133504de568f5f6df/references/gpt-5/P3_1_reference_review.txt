Summary
The paper proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) from unannotated data. The authors give a unified, context-free formulation of AOGs agnostic to data type (text, images, events), and introduce an iterative bottom-up structure learning procedure. The key idea is to insert And-Or fragments—each comprising one And-rule with multiple Or-children whose alternatives are drawn from existing nodes—so that compositions (And) and reconfigurations (Or) are learned jointly. The objective is the posterior of the grammar under a sparsity prior exp(−α‖G‖), with likelihood approximated by Viterbi (unambiguity-biased) scores. A central technical contribution is a closed-form approximation of the posterior gain of adding a fragment using sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor counting configurations and a context co-occurrence matrix, avoiding full reparsing. Experiments on event parsing and image modeling show improvements over prior AOG learners.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on AOGs for images/events (Zhu & Mumford; Si & Zhu; Pei et al.) and connects to PCFG induction (Baker; Klein & Manning), Bayesian model merging/MDL (Stolcke & Omohundro), and decomposable SPNs (Poon & Domingos). Compared to prior AOG structure learning (Si & Zhu 2013; Si et al. 2011), the novelty is the unified fragment search that couples And/Or discovery, explicit posterior optimization with a sparsity prior, and efficient posterior-gain estimation via sufficient statistics. The use of Viterbi likelihood and unambiguity regularization is consistent with NLP grammar induction practice (Spitkovsky et al.; Tu & Honavar).

Strengths
- Conceptual contribution: learning And and Or structure jointly via And-Or fragments is elegant, reduces redundancy, and plausibly mitigates data sparsity relative to learning them separately.
- Objective-driven: optimizes a clear posterior criterion with an MDL-like prior; provides a computable gain proxy using counts, avoiding repeated global parsing.
- Generality: unified formalization and empirical demonstrations across two distinct domains (temporal events and spatial images).
- Empirical evidence: solid improvements over [17,18] and competitive with NLP-inspired baselines on the event data; large perplexity gains on the real image dataset.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strong assumptions: The likelihood-gain derivation assumes unambiguity and Viterbi parses; many real AOGs are inherently ambiguous. It is unclear how robust the method is when ambiguity is present.
- Parameter learning for And-rules is underspecified. And-rule probabilities are said to be “energy terms on relations,” but the paper does not explain how these energies are parameterized or estimated. In the gain formula, P(A→…) appears, but it’s unclear what value is used during search.
- “Agnostic to data type” claim relies on domain-specific relation and context definitions. The paper does not precisely define “context” for images or events nor how relation parameters are handled; the sufficient statistics depend critically on these choices.
- Search complexity and scalability: Searching over n-gram tensors across relation types can explode combinatorially; the paper lacks complexity analysis, settings for n, and beam widths. Initialization with a per-sample And-rule that reproduces each datum exactly is memory-intensive for larger datasets.
- Theoretical guarantees: The claim that any context-free AOG can be constructed by composing And-Or fragments is asserted but not proven; a constructive argument or reference is needed.
- Evaluation scope: Event experiments are small-scale (61 videos; 55 train/6 test) with limited baselines (mostly ID-sequence learners). No ablations of key choices (And-Or vs separate And/Or search, Viterbi vs EM, effect of α, fragment sizes). Statistical significance and runtime/memory are not reported.
- Handling multiple events per video by retroactively flipping top-level And to Or is ad hoc and may distort learned structure.
- The “coherence” factorization (to biclustering notions) is intriguing but not fully developed; the definitions and implications for fragment quality are not clear.

Quality
- Generally sound, but key technical pieces need clarification: precise likelihood-gain derivation under ambiguity; learning of And-rule energies; definitions of context; and proof of expressivity via And-Or fragments. Experimental methodology is reasonable but limited; add ablations and runtime.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear at a high level, with helpful figures. However, important implementation details (context definition, energy parameterization, search settings) are missing, making reproduction difficult. Some equations/typesetting are hard to parse.

Originality
- The And-Or fragment idea and posterior-gain computation via sufficient statistics are novel within AOG structure learning. The unification across domains is conceptually appealing.

Significance
- If substantiated and better evaluated, the approach could be impactful for grammar-based structure learning in vision and events. Current experimental scope is modest; broader, more rigorous evaluation would strengthen significance.

Questions for the authors
1) How are And-rule “energy terms” parameterized and learned? Are they fixed, hand-specified, or estimated during structure learning? What is P(A→…) used in the gain?
2) What exactly constitutes a “context” column in the context matrix for images and for events? How large is the context, and how sensitive are results to this choice?
3) How are the reductions RD computed without reparsing under ambiguity? Do you maintain dynamic program tables or local caches?
4) What values of n and relation types were used in practice? Beam width? How does runtime scale with |X|, average sample length, and the number of relation types?
5) Sensitivity to α, sa, so: how were they chosen? Any MDL interpretation or cross-validation?
6) Can the method be extended to allow ambiguity (e.g., unambiguity regularization rather than hard assumption)? What breaks in the gain derivation otherwise?
7) Please provide a proof or constructive argument that iterated And-Or fragments can realize any context-free AOG.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a detailed section on And-rule energy models and their estimation.
- Precisely define “context” and give domain-specific instantiations; include ablations on context size.
- Include ablations comparing: And-Or vs And-only/Or-only fragment search; Viterbi vs EM; effect of α; fragment size n; search beam width.
- Provide runtime and memory profiles and scaling plots; clarify initialization overhead.
- Strengthen evaluations: larger event datasets, stronger baselines (e.g., SPN structure learning, modern grammar induction), statistical tests, and qualitative parses.
- Move key derivations/proofs to the supplement and summarize main assumptions in the paper.

Pros
- Elegant joint And/Or structure learning with a clear objective.
- Efficient approximate gain using sufficient statistics.
- Cross-domain applicability demonstrated.
- Promising empirical gains.

Cons
- Critical modeling and algorithmic details missing (And-rule parameters, context).
- Strong unambiguity/Viterbi assumptions; unclear robustness.
- Limited evaluation scope and ablations; scalability not characterized.
- Some theoretical claims lack proof.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying parameter learning and adding ablations/runtime analyses. The idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the paper needs stronger technical and empirical substantiation.
