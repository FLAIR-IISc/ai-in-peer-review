This paper presents a method for the unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, encompassing both their structure and parameters. The key idea is to perform a bottom-up, iterative search that starts with a trivial grammar perfectly fitting the training data and progressively introduces new grammar rules to increase the grammar's posterior probability. The central contribution is the concept of learning "And-Or fragments" as atomic units. An And-Or fragment simultaneously introduces a new compositional rule (an And-rule) and a set of alternative sub-structures (via Or-nodes and their corresponding Or-rules). This unified approach contrasts with prior work that often learns compositions and reconfigurations separately (e.g., [17], [18]). The learning algorithm is framed as an optimization problem maximizing the posterior probability of the grammar, which combines a Viterbi likelihood term with a sparsity prior on the grammar size. A significant technical contribution is the derivation of an efficient method to calculate the gain in posterior probability for adding a candidate fragment, which relies on sufficient statistics (an n-gram tensor and a context matrix) and avoids re-parsing the entire dataset at each step. The proposed method builds upon a line of research in grammar induction from natural language ([14], [15]) and computer vision ([1], [17]). The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on two distinct tasks: learning event grammars from human activity videos and learning image grammars from synthetic and real animal face images.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Novelty:** The main conceptual contribution—the induction of unified "And-Or fragments"—is novel and well-motivated. By learning compositional and reconfigurable aspects of the grammar simultaneously, the approach addresses a key challenge in structure learning, namely the difficulty of learning alternatives (Or-rules) in isolation, especially with scarce or ambiguous data. This unified search appears to be more robust and efficient than decoupled approaches.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. The learning process is framed within a principled Bayesian framework that optimizes a well-defined objective function (the grammar posterior). The derivation of the posterior gain, which decomposes into a likelihood gain and a prior gain, is a strong point. The formulation of the likelihood gain in terms of sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) is elegant and crucial for making the search process computationally tractable. The insight that this gain relates to tensor and matrix coherence measures provides a nice connection to other machine learning concepts.

3.  **Significance and Generality:** The framework is presented in a domain-agnostic manner, applicable to any data that can be represented compositionally. This is effectively demonstrated through strong empirical results in two different and challenging domains: temporal event modeling and spatial image modeling. In both cases, the proposed method achieves results that are comparable to or significantly better than recent, specialized approaches ([17], [18]). This suggests the method could have a broad impact and be useful to researchers in various fields that utilize grammatical models.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation for learning And-Or fragments is clearly explained by contrasting them with simpler And-fragments and Or-fragments. The overall iterative learning algorithm is well-illustrated in Figure 1.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Reproducibility and Algorithmic Details:** While the high-level framework is clear, crucial details about the search for candidate And-Or fragments are lacking. The paper states that the search is conducted over n-gram tensors constructed for "different values of n and different And-rule relations." This suggests a potentially vast search space. The paper would be significantly strengthened by providing a more detailed description of this search procedure. For instance, how are candidate compositions (potential And-rules) generated? How are the corresponding sub-tensors identified? What is the practical range of `n` considered? Without these details, it is difficult to assess the computational complexity and to reproduce the results. While some details may be in the supplementary material, the core algorithm should be sufficiently detailed in the main paper.

2.  **Impact of Approximations:** The method relies on two key approximations: the use of Viterbi likelihood instead of the full likelihood, and the assumption of "grammar unambiguity" for deriving the likelihood gain. While using the Viterbi likelihood is a common practice, a discussion of its potential limitations would be beneficial. More importantly, the impact of the grammar unambiguity assumption should be addressed. Real-world data can often be parsed in multiple ways, and it is unclear how violations of this assumption would affect the accuracy of the computed posterior gain and the final learned grammar.

3.  **Scalability:** The paper reports fast running times on the datasets used, but a more thorough analysis of the algorithm's scalability is warranted. The size of the initial grammar is linear in the number of training samples. The complexity of finding the best And-Or fragment appears to depend on the number of possible n-grams and contexts, which could be very large for long sequences or large vocabularies of terminal symbols. A formal complexity analysis or empirical evaluation of performance as a function of dataset size, sequence length, and vocabulary size would make the contribution more complete.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   The core idea of learning unified And-Or fragments is novel, principled, and addresses a clear limitation of prior work.
*   The derivation of an efficient objective for the search procedure based on sufficient statistics is a strong technical contribution.
*   The method's generality is successfully demonstrated on two distinct and relevant problem domains (events and images).
*   The empirical results show significant improvements over state-of-the-art competing methods.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

*   Lack of specific details on the search algorithm for finding candidate fragments hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the true computational cost.
*   The paper does not adequately discuss the potential impact of its key assumptions, namely the Viterbi approximation and grammar unambiguity.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with a novel core idea, strong technical contributions, and compelling experimental results. The weaknesses primarily relate to a lack of algorithmic detail and discussion of limitations, which could be addressed in a final version. The strengths, particularly the novelty and significance of the proposed learning framework, outweigh these weaknesses.
