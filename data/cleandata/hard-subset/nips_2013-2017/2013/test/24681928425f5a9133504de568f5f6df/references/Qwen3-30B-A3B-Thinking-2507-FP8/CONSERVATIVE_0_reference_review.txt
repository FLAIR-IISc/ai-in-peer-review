

**Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"**

This paper proposes an approach for unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, which are used to model compositional structures in data (e.g., images, events). The authors claim to learn both grammar structure and parameters by iteratively adding "And-Or fragments" that optimize the posterior probability of the grammar.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type (text, images, events).
- The theoretical foundation is sound, with a derivation of sufficient statistics for efficient computation of posterior gains.
- The experimental evaluation on event and image grammars demonstrates reasonable performance compared to prior methods.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Insufficient empirical validation:** The paper claims "comparable or better performance" but lacks statistical significance testing, error bars, or detailed ablation studies. Table 2 shows marginal improvements (e.g., F-measure differences of 0.05-0.10), which may not be meaningful without statistical validation. The authors should demonstrate that these differences are not due to random variation.

2. **Inadequate baseline comparison:** The paper only compares to two older methods ([15, 18]), both of which are limited to learning from ID sequences. The authors claim their third method (applying to vector sequences) outperforms these, but fail to compare against more recent or stronger baselines that might have been developed since those papers. For instance, the paper doesn't compare to recent approaches that use neural networks for grammar learning.

3. **Questionable theoretical claims:** The assertion that "reasonable And-Or fragments usually increase the posterior probability" is not sufficiently supported. The paper lacks experiments showing how often their approach fails to increase the posterior probability or what conditions lead to failure. This is a critical claim that needs empirical validation.

4. **Methodological limitations:** The use of Viterbi likelihood instead of full likelihood (as noted in Section 3.1) is a common approximation but not thoroughly justified. The paper does not discuss the trade-offs or limitations of this approximation, which could significantly impact the quality of the learned grammars.

5. **Overstated novelty:** The "And-Or fragments" approach is presented as a significant innovation, but it appears to be a minor extension of prior work. The paper cites [17] as previous work on structure learning for And-Or grammars but doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs substantially. The relationship to [17] needs much more careful elaboration.

6. **Limited scalability analysis:** The paper mentions that their implementation runs "within a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples," but provides no scalability analysis. For real-world applications, it's crucial to understand how the approach scales to larger datasets and more complex grammars.

**Significance and Impact:**
While the problem of learning compositional structures is well-motivated, this paper's contribution has limited significance for the NIPS community because:
- Stochastic And-Or grammars are not widely adopted in mainstream machine learning.
- The improvements over baselines are marginal and not statistically significant.
- The paper doesn't demonstrate how this approach could be applied to more challenging real-world problems.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper presents a technically sound approach to unsupervised grammar learning, but the evidence provided for the claimed advantages is insufficient. The significance of the contribution is limited by the niche nature of the problem and the marginal improvements over baselines. The novelty is overstated compared to existing work, and the paper lacks the rigorous validation expected for NIPS.

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. The paper would need substantially more empirical validation, including statistical significance testing, comparison against more recent baselines, and a clearer articulation of how this work differs from prior research. Without these improvements, the evidence supporting the main claims is insufficient for publication at NIPS.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Conduct statistical significance tests for all reported results.
2. Compare against more recent baselines, including methods that have been developed since the cited papers.
3. Provide a detailed analysis of failure cases and limitations of the proposed approach.
4. Clarify the relationship to prior work, particularly [17], and provide a more precise justification for the novelty claims.
5. Discuss the trade-offs of using Viterbi likelihood versus full likelihood in more depth.

While the paper has a solid theoretical foundation, the lack of rigorous validation and the modest nature of the claimed improvements make it difficult to recommend acceptance for NIPS.
