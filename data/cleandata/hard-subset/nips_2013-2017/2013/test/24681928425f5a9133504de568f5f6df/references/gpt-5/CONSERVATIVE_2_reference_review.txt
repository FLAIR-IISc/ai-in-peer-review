Summary
This paper addresses unsupervised structure learning for stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs). It proposes: (i) a unified, context-free formalization of stochastic AOGs purportedly agnostic to modality (text, images, events); (ii) a greedy/beam-search procedure that starts from a memorization grammar and iteratively inserts And-Or fragments (jointly adding an And-rule with several sibling Or-rules) to maximize a sparsity-regularized posterior; (iii) an approximation to posterior gain based on Viterbi likelihood and an unambiguity assumption, yielding “sufficient statistics” in the form of n-gram tensors and a context matrix whose “coherence” factorizes the likelihood gain; (iv) small-scale experiments on event data (with annotated atomic actions) and synthetic/real image datasets, reporting improvements over two prior AOG structure-learning baselines.

Relation to prior work
Learning by starting from a large grammar and merging/compacting is established (e.g., Bayesian Model Merging; MDL/merging for PCFGs). The use of Viterbi training and unambiguity bias echoes prior unsupervised grammar induction. AOGs and SPNs are well-known; structure learning for SPNs and for image/event AOGs has prior art. The paper’s main novelty is searching for joint And-Or fragments with a posterior objective and a coherence-based approximation for likelihood gain.

Strengths
- Attempts a unified formalization across modalities and a single learning framework.
- Emphasizes explicit posterior optimization with a sparsity prior instead of purely heuristic structure proposals.
- Joint induction of compositions and reconfigurations via And-Or fragments is a reasonable idea that could reduce redundancy and mitigate data sparsity.
- The “sufficient statistics” formulation is computationally appealing if valid; reported runtimes are modest.
- Empirical results show gains over selected AOG baselines for events and images.

Weaknesses
Quality/technical soundness
- Heavy reliance on Viterbi likelihood and an unambiguity assumption to derive the gain formula. Many real grammars are ambiguous, especially with Or-choice; the paper does not quantify the impact when this assumption fails. No ablations comparing exact vs Viterbi or with/without unambiguity regularization.
- The likelihood-gain derivation is deferred; the main text gives a complex expression without adequate definitions (e.g., contexts for spatial/temporal fragments, how they are enumerated, avoiding double counting) or guarantees. Claims about factorization into coherence measures are not substantiated in the paper.
- Parameter learning is underspecified: And-rule probabilities/energies “defined on relations” are central, yet the paper only gives a closed-form for Or-rule probabilities. How are And-rule energy parameters learned/regularized? Are they shared across relations? How is P(A→a1…an) set in practice in the gain?
- The prior/MDL term uses sa and so (“bits per symbol”) as fixed constants. Values and sensitivity are not reported; α is fixed to 0.5 ad hoc. There is no analysis of posterior sensitivity to these coding choices.
- The context-free assumption for spatial/temporal relations is nontrivial. For images, relative positions can induce long-range constraints; for events, “co-occurring” and “followed-by” relations may violate context-freeness. The paper does not state the exact independence assumptions required, nor how CYK applies with both relation types.
- Search is greedy/beam with random restarts; no guarantees and no diagnostics for local optima are provided.

Clarity
- Several figures are garbled and hard to interpret; key algorithmic details (exact fragment search over relations, construction of tensors/matrices, pseudocode) are relegated to the supplement.
- Important notation (RD, contexts, encoding length) is not made crisp; symbols are reused inconsistently.
- Smoothing used for perplexity is unspecified.

Originality
- The core idea of joint And-Or fragments is incremental relative to established grammar induction and AOG structure learning; the use of MDL-like priors and Viterbi training is standard. The claimed coherence factorization may be novel, but is insufficiently developed and justified.

Significance/empirical validation
- Baselines are narrow (two AOG-specific methods). No comparisons to broader, established structure-learning approaches: e.g., Bayesian/MDL PCFG induction (HDP-PCFG, Adaptor Grammars), EM/Viterbi PCFG, or SPN structure learning. This makes it hard to assess generality and progress beyond the AOG niche.
- The event experiments are not truly unsupervised end-to-end: atomic actions and their time boundaries are annotated; method 1 further collapses frames into ID sequences, losing structure. Test sets are tiny (6 videos), with no confidence intervals or statistical tests.
- The real-image experiment reports perplexity after unspecified smoothing; there is no evaluation of parsing/segmentation accuracy or qualitative analyses beyond one figure.
- No ablations to isolate the contribution of And-Or fragments vs And-only/Or-only, the coherence-based gain, or the “co-occurring” vs “followed-by” relation learning order. No sensitivity analyses for α, sa, so, beam width, n, or relation inventories.
- Claims of modality-agnosticism are not validated on text corpora, where strong baselines exist.

Questions for authors
1. Provide the full derivation of the likelihood gain, clearly defining contexts for spatial/temporal data and how ambiguity is handled. How robust is the approximation when the grammar is ambiguous?
2. How are And-rule energy parameters defined and learned? Are they local potentials with learned weights? How are they regularized?
3. What exact values of sa and so were used, and why? Please report sensitivity to α, sa, so.
4. How are relation types chosen per fragment, and what is the search space over n and relation templates? What is the computational complexity?
5. For the real-image perplexity, specify the smoothing and show likelihood calibration, standard errors, and qualitative parse examples.
6. Can you compare against broader baselines (e.g., SPN structure learning, PCFG induction on sequence data) and add ablations?

Pros
- Clear motivation for unifying composition and reconfiguration.
- Posterior objective with sparsity prior; efficient statistics if derivation holds.
- Some empirical gains over prior AOG-specific methods; potentially practical.

Cons
- Key assumptions (unambiguity, Viterbi) and derivations insufficiently justified; parameter learning incomplete.
- Limited, small-scale evaluation without strong baselines, ablations, or statistical rigor.
- Clarity issues and reliance on supplement for essential details.
- Generality claims not supported by cross-domain experiments.

Recommendation
Reject. While the direction is interesting and grounded in established grammar-induction ideas, the current submission does not meet the bar for methodological soundness and experimental validation. Strengthening the theoretical development, clarifying parameterization, broadening and deepening the empirical evaluation (with ablations and stronger baselines), and improving clarity would substantially improve the paper. Confidence: high.
