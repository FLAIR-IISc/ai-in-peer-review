This paper proposes an unsupervised learning approach for stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs), extending previous work in natural language processing and image modeling to a more general framework. The core idea is to iteratively learn both the structure and parameters of a SAG by adding “And-Or fragments” – simultaneously considering compositional and reconfiguration possibilities – optimizing a posterior probability based on sufficient statistics derived from the data. The authors demonstrate empirical success on event and image grammars, achieving comparable or better performance than existing methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:** The unified formalization of SAGs, independent of data type (text, images, events), is a significant strength. This allows the application of the same learning framework to diverse problems.
* **Unified Structure Learning:**  The simultaneous learning of compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules) via And-Or fragments is a well-motivated approach, potentially addressing the error-prone nature of separate learning. The authors convincingly argue this improves robustness, especially with limited data.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The explicit optimization of the posterior probability, coupled with the derivation of sufficient statistics, provides a strong theoretical underpinning.  The connection to n-gram coherence and the use of a sparsity prior are sensible choices.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both event and image grammars demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparison to existing methods (even if limited in number) provides a baseline and validates the improvements. The results on the more challenging synthetic event data are particularly encouraging.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The authors make a concerted effort to explain the technical details clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Baselines:** The comparison to existing work is somewhat limited. While the chosen baselines ([17, 18]) are relevant, a more comprehensive evaluation against state-of-the-art methods for event and image parsing (even if they don't directly learn grammars) would strengthen the claims.  Specifically, for event recognition, comparing to methods employing Hidden Markov Models or more recent deep learning approaches would be valuable.
* **Complexity of Algorithm:** While the authors claim efficiency gains, the algorithm appears quite complex.  The derivation of the likelihood gain, involving context matrices and n-gram tensors, is not trivial. A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity (e.g., scalability with data size and grammar complexity) would be helpful.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the hyperparameter α (sparsity penalty) isn't extensively discussed.  An ablation study or sensitivity analysis would reveal its influence on performance and generalization.
* **Visualization of Learned Grammars:**  While the paper describes the learning process, a more visual representation of the learned grammars themselves would aid understanding.  Illustrating the structure of the learned And-Or fragments and their corresponding relationships would be insightful.  For the image grammars, showing examples of how the learned grammar parses sample images would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of grammar unambiguity, used to simplify the likelihood gain calculation, is strong. A discussion of the potential impact of this assumption on the results would be appropriate.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The work presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to unsupervised structure learning of SAGs.
* The generality of the proposed framework is a significant contribution.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on two different datasets.
* The writing is clear and the presentation is well-organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The limited comparison to existing methods weakens the claims of superior performance.
* The computational complexity of the algorithm and its sensitivity to hyperparameters require further investigation.
* The lack of detailed visualization of the learned grammars hinders understanding.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in unsupervised learning. The proposed approach is novel, theoretically well-founded, and empirically validated.  While the limitations regarding baselines, complexity, and visualization exist, they do not fundamentally detract from the contribution.  I believe the paper warrants acceptance, with a request for the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above in a revised version. Specifically, I recommend including more comprehensive experimental comparisons, a discussion of the algorithm’s scalability, and a more visual presentation of the learned grammars.



**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions)
