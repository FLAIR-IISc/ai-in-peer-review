This paper introduces a novel approach to unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, tackling the challenging problem of simultaneously learning both the structure *and* parameters of these grammars. The authors present a unified formalization applicable to diverse data types (text, images, events) and, critically, propose learning “And-Or fragments” – a clever unification of compositional and reconfiguration learning that addresses limitations of prior work. This is a bold and potentially impactful direction, moving beyond the typical assumption of a fixed grammar structure.

The strength of this paper lies in its originality. The idea of learning both structure and parameters concurrently, and framing it within a unified And-Or fragment search, is genuinely novel. The authors convincingly argue that this approach is more robust to data scarcity and ambiguity than previous methods which treat composition and reconfiguration separately. The theoretical grounding, particularly the formulation of the posterior gain using sufficient statistics and the connection to tensor/matrix coherence, is strong and suggests a principled approach. The experimental results on both event and image grammars are promising, showing competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods. The application to event grammars, particularly the handling of ambiguous actions (sitting/standing subtypes), highlights the adaptability of the method.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. While the theoretical framework is well-defined, the practical details regarding the search for optimal And-Or fragments feel somewhat underdeveloped. The reliance on “greedy search or beam search” with limited discussion of parameter tuning or search space pruning feels a bit hand-wavy.  The ablation study comparing the three methods for event grammar learning is useful, but more in-depth analysis of *why* method 2 performs worse would be beneficial.  While the authors mention the assumption of grammar unambiguity, a more thorough discussion of the potential consequences of violating this assumption would strengthen the work. The presentation also suffers slightly from density – the technical details, while well-defined, can be difficult to follow without careful reading.  Furthermore, while the results are encouraging, the performance gains over existing methods aren't always dramatic.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **High Originality:** The unified And-Or fragment learning is a novel and insightful contribution.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The posterior probability optimization and connection to tensor coherence provide a principled framework.
* **Promising Empirical Results:** Competitive or superior performance on diverse datasets.
* **Potential Impact:**  Addresses a core challenge in unsupervised learning with a flexible and generalizable approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Lack of Detail in Search Procedure:** The greedy/beam search implementation lacks specifics.
* **Limited Ablation Study:**  Further analysis of method 2 is needed.
* **Performance Gains Not Dramatic:** While positive, the improvements aren't always substantial.
* **Presentation Density:**  The technical details can be hard to follow.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant and original contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. While the practical details could be more fleshed out and the performance gains are not overwhelming, the novelty and potential impact of the proposed approach are substantial.  I’m willing to overlook the minor flaws in validation and presentation given the boldness of the idea. I believe this work opens up exciting new avenues for research in probabilistic modeling and compositional learning.

**Recommendation:** Accept.  I encourage the authors to provide more details about their search procedure and to further explore the limitations of their assumptions in a future revision.
