Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles unsupervised structure learning for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) across modalities (text, images, events). It proposes a unified, context-free AOG formalization and an iterative bottom-up algorithm that starts from a trivial grammar that exactly generates the training set, then repeatedly inserts And-Or fragments to maximize the posterior P(G|X) ∝ e^{-α||G||} ∏ P(xi|G), approximated with Viterbi likelihood. The key design choice is to induce And-Or fragments that jointly introduce one And-rule and multiple sibling Or-rules (capturing both composition and reconfiguration), rather than searching for And- and Or-fragments separately. The posterior gain of a candidate fragment is computed efficiently from sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor of configurations and a context matrix of configuration-context co-occurrences. Under an unambiguity assumption, the likelihood gain factorizes into tensor/matrix “coherence” terms, and the prior gain penalizes grammar size via a simple coding-length model.

Empirically, the method is applied to activity/event grammars on two datasets (one perturbed) and to image grammars on a synthetic dataset with known ground-truth grammar and a real animal faces dataset. It is compared primarily to And-Or image/event grammar learners [17, 18], showing improved F1 for event parsing and improved F1/KL (synthetic) and perplexity (real) for images.

The work relates to unsupervised grammar induction (e.g., Bayesian model merging [14], ADIOS [15], unsupervised PCFGs [12], HDP-PCFGs [9]), image/event AOG learning [1,3,17,18], and SPNs [10]. The proposed fragment search and posterior objective can be viewed as an MDL-flavored structure learning approach adapted to AOGs and related to statistical structure learning in SPNs, but with a distinct fragment-based search guided by coherence measures.

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation: manual AOG design is hard; full rule sets are intractable; separate composition/reconfiguration searches can be brittle.
- Coherent objective: posterior with a sparsity prior and Viterbi likelihood is reasonable and aligns with prior observations on unambiguity bias.
- Interesting technical idea: And-Or fragment induction and the derivation of an efficient posterior gain using precomputed sufficient statistics; the factorization into tensor/matrix coherence is insightful.
- Generality: unified AOG formalism and experiments on spatial (images) and temporal (events) domains.
- Synthetic evaluation with ground-truth grammar is welcome and shows substantial gains over [17].

Weaknesses and detailed assessment
Quality
- The posterior gain derivation relies on an unambiguity assumption and Viterbi training; the implications when grammars are ambiguous are not analyzed. Many real AOGs will be ambiguous.
- The prior term uses fixed code lengths (sa, so) and α, but these choices are under-specified. No sensitivity analysis is provided; α is fixed to 0.5 in image experiments without justification.
- Search procedure is only sketched (greedy/beam with random restarts). No complexity analysis, convergence behavior, or empirical ablations (e.g., beam width, restarts, runtime vs. dataset size/grammar size).
- The definition of “context” for the context matrix is crucial, yet not precisely specified for each domain (which neighborhoods/relations are used in events/images?).
- Event experiments: only 6 test videos; evaluations aggregate over a small test set. The three application variants are interesting, but the restrictions (e.g., “co-occurring” learned first) are heuristic; no ablation showing how And-Or fragments specifically outperform separate And/Or searches.
- Real-image perplexity is smoothed but the smoothing method is not described; perplexity evaluation across models with different supports can be delicate.

Clarity
- The high-level algorithm is clear, but important details are deferred to the supplement (e.g., full derivation, pseudocode). Given the centrality of the posterior gain and sufficient statistics, a more self-contained presentation would help.
- Figures appear dense and are hard to parse as presented; the And-Or fragment example (Fig. 2) and the contexts would benefit from a small concrete toy example worked through end-to-end.
- Notation inconsistencies and occasional typos (e.g., figure captions, symbols) make sections harder to follow.

Originality
- Combining And- and Or-fragment induction into unified And-Or fragments guided by posterior gain and coherence-based sufficient statistics is novel in the AOG literature to my knowledge, and a meaningful extension of prior work [17,18], as well as classical grammar merging [14,15,16].
- The explicit connection to tensor/matrix coherence as a surrogate for context-freeness is an interesting conceptual contribution.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for grammar-based modeling in vision and activity understanding by providing a more robust structure learner than prior heuristics; the cross-domain formalization is appealing.
- However, empirical validation is limited in scope: baselines are narrow (mostly [17,18]); no comparison to broader structure learners (e.g., HDP-PCFGs/adaptor grammars on text, SPN structure learning) even on controlled settings; no large-scale or more diverse datasets; limited ablations.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to α, sa, so? Can you justify/code-length choices and provide a sensitivity plot?
2. How are “contexts” defined concretely for events/images? How local/global are they, and how does this choice affect the likelihood gain?
3. What happens when the unambiguity assumption is violated? Do the coherence measures still correlate with true posterior gain?
4. Can you report runtime, number of iterations, and beam size vs. dataset size, and show posterior/grammar-size trajectories?
5. How do And-Or fragments compare empirically to learning with separate And- and Or-fragments on the same datasets?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled structure learning approach for AOGs with an interpretable objective.
- Cross-modal applicability demonstrated; strong synthetic results and improvements over prior AOG learners.
- Potentially useful practical algorithm; good idea density.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited experimental breadth and weak baselines beyond prior AOG learners; small test sets.
- Key assumptions (unambiguity) and hyperparameter choices not scrutinized; missing sensitivity/ablation studies.
- Some important technical details deferred; clarity can be improved.

Recommendation: Weak reject
The paper presents a promising and original approach with encouraging results, but the current empirical validation and analysis are not yet at the standard I would expect for NeurIPS. Strengthening experimental breadth (more datasets, stronger baselines, ablations and sensitivity analyses), clarifying the role of contexts and assumptions, and providing more algorithmic detail would make this a strong submission.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add ablations: (i) And-only, Or-only, And-Or; (ii) with/without context-matrix term; (iii) beam width and restarts; (iv) sensitivity to α, sa, so. Plot posterior vs. iteration and grammar size.
- Evaluate on additional benchmarks and against broader baselines (e.g., HDP-PCFG/adaptor grammars on synthetic/text, SPN structure learning on images).
- Analyze ambiguity: measure ambiguity of learned grammars and assess robustness when ambiguity is present.
- Clarify context definitions per domain and provide a small, fully worked example of computing the n-gram tensor, context matrix, posterior gain, and selected fragment.
- Report computational complexity and wall-clock runtimes; discuss scalability.
- Improve figure clarity and notation; include key derivations in the main text if space permits.
