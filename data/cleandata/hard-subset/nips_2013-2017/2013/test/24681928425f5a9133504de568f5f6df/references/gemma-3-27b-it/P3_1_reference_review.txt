## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs), a powerful framework for modeling compositional and reconfigurable data. The authors present a novel approach that jointly learns both the structure *and* parameters of a SAG, contrasting with previous work often fixing the grammar structure. Their method iteratively inserts “And-Or fragments” – simultaneously incorporating composition and reconfiguration – optimizing a posterior probability based on sufficient statistics derived from the data. The contribution lies in a unified learning framework, efficient optimization, and empirical validation on event and image grammars.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader context of stochastic grammar learning, drawing connections to work in natural language processing (stochastic context-free grammars), sum-product networks, and recent advances in learning grammars for images and events.  It effectively differentiates itself from methods relying on fixed grammar structures (e.g., [11, 12, 10, 13]) and highlights improvements over existing structure learning approaches like [17, 18]. The acknowledgement of earlier work on Bayesian model merging [14, 15, 16] is relevant, but a more detailed discussion of how this approach adapts those techniques to the specific challenges of SAGs would be beneficial.  The connection to biclustering [22] in the likelihood gain formulation is a strong point, demonstrating awareness of related techniques.

**Strengths:**

* **Unified Framework:**  The key strength is the simultaneous learning of grammar structure and parameters.  This is a significant improvement over methods that treat these as separate steps, potentially avoiding error propagation.
* **And-Or Fragments:**  The introduction of And-Or fragments is a clever design choice. By jointly modeling composition and reconfiguration, the approach is more robust to data scarcity and ambiguity.
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The formulation of the posterior probability optimization and the derivation of sufficient statistics are solid contributions, demonstrating a deep understanding of the underlying theory. The connection to coherence measures from biclustering provides a strong justification for the likelihood gain calculation.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both event and image grammars demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, outperforming existing methods on more challenging datasets (event grammars with action subtypes).
* **Generality:** The authors successfully abstract the SAG formalism to be agnostic to the specific data type, increasing its potential applicability.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The examples (Table 1, Figures 1 & 2) are helpful in understanding the concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability Discussion:** While the paper mentions efficiency, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability to larger datasets would be valuable.  The authors claim the prototype runs “reasonably fast,” but concrete timings and analysis for different data sizes are missing. The dependence on greedy/beam search suggests potential limitations in exploring the full grammar space.
* **Prior Specification (α):** The choice of α = 0.5 for the sparsity prior seems somewhat arbitrary. A sensitivity analysis or justification for this value would strengthen the results.  How does the performance change with different values of α?
* **Relation Types:** The paper primarily focuses on spatial/temporal relations. While mentioned, a more in-depth discussion of how to learn and represent different *types* of relations within the And-rules would be beneficial, especially for more complex data.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with [17, 18] is somewhat limited as these methods primarily focus on ID sequences in the event grammar setting.  Comparing against methods that directly learn from atomic actions would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Dataset Size:** The dataset sizes used in the experiments are relatively small.  Demonstrating performance on larger, more realistic datasets would further validate the approach.




**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel and theoretically sound approach to unsupervised SAG learning.
* Addresses a significant challenge in the field of compositional modeling.
* Empirical results demonstrate competitive or superior performance.
* Clear and well-written presentation of the ideas.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Limited discussion of scalability and computational complexity.
* Somewhat arbitrary choice of hyperparameter (α) without sensitivity analysis.
* Comparison against alternative methods is somewhat limited.
* Dataset sizes could be larger for more robust evaluation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. The unified framework and the introduction of And-Or fragments are particularly noteworthy. While some weaknesses exist (primarily related to scalability and a more thorough comparative evaluation), the strengths significantly outweigh them. I believe this paper is a worthy addition to NIPS, with the potential to inspire further research in this area.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions – addressing scalability discussion, sensitivity analysis of α, and potentially expanding the comparative evaluation).
