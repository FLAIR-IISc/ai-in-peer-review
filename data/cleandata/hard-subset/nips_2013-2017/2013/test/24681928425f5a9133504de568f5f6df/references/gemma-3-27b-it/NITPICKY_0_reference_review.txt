This paper presents an unsupervised approach to learning stochastic And-Or grammars, extending prior work in natural language processing and image modeling. The core idea is to iteratively refine a grammar by adding “And-Or fragments” – simultaneously learning compositional and reconfiguration rules – guided by maximizing the posterior probability of the grammar given the data. The authors claim several contributions: a data-agnostic formalization, posterior optimization with sufficient statistics, unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations, and empirical validation on event and image grammars.

While the proposed approach appears promising in its ambition, and the paper is clearly written, several shortcomings – many of which are surprisingly granular given the potential significance of the work – raise serious concerns. 

First, the formalization, while aiming for generality, lacks sufficient precision. The definition of “relations” within And-rules remains vague. The paper states these relations “configure how sub-patterns form the composite pattern,” but provides little concrete guidance on *what* these relations can be, or how they are learned. This ambiguity extends to the implementation details. The examples (Table 1) are helpful, but don’t fully address the flexibility (or lack thereof) in defining these relations across different data types.

Second, the claim of optimizing the posterior probability is undermined by several simplifying assumptions. Specifically, the use of Viterbi likelihood, while acknowledged, is treated too casually. The authors state it “leads to better grammar learning results,” but offer limited justification beyond citation [20, 21]. A more thorough discussion of the potential biases introduced by this approximation is necessary. Furthermore, the sparsity prior (α‖G‖) feels arbitrarily chosen; a sensitivity analysis with different α values would be valuable. 

Third, the “And-Or fragment” concept, central to the contribution, suffers from several inconsistencies. The derivation of the likelihood gain, reliant on “sufficient statistics” and matrix/tensor coherence, is overly complex and lacks clear intuition. The connection between the coherence measures and the actual posterior gain isn’t sufficiently elaborated. The justification for the likelihood gain formulation feels hand-wavy, and the reliance on "grammar unambiguity" as a simplifying assumption is potentially restrictive.

Fourth, the experimental evaluation is problematic. The event grammar experiments suffer from a somewhat artificial setup – synthesizing a more complicated dataset by subdividing existing actions. This feels contrived and doesn't strongly demonstrate generalization. The image grammar experiments, while showing improved perplexity compared to [17], lack a comparative analysis of the *structure* of the learned grammars. Were the grammars qualitatively different? Did the proposed method uncover more meaningful compositions? The evaluation metrics (perplexity) are also somewhat limited in their ability to assess the quality of the learned structure. The use of a synthetic image dataset, while allowing for ground truth comparison, lacks real-world complexity.

Fifth, numerous minor inconsistencies and stylistic issues detract from the overall polish. The figure captions are often incomplete or lack sufficient detail. Equation formatting is inconsistent. The writing, while generally clear, occasionally veers into jargon without adequate explanation. For example, the term “RD” is introduced without full definition until later in the text.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to structure learning that attempts to address limitations of existing methods.
*   Clear presentation of the problem and proposed solution.
*   Empirical results demonstrate some improvement over existing methods.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of precision in the formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars.
*   Overly complex and poorly justified derivation of the likelihood gain.
*   Contrived experimental setup and limited evaluation metrics.
*   Numerous minor inconsistencies and stylistic issues.
*   Simplifying assumptions (Viterbi likelihood, grammar unambiguity) are not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential of the idea, the paper suffers from numerous technical shortcomings and lacks the rigor expected of a NIPS publication. The proposed approach requires substantial further development and more convincing empirical validation before it can be considered a significant contribution. I recommend **rejection**. While the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the field, the paper, in its current state, fails to meet the quality standards for NIPS. A major revision addressing the concerns outlined above might warrant reconsideration, but the current manuscript is simply too flawed to accept.
