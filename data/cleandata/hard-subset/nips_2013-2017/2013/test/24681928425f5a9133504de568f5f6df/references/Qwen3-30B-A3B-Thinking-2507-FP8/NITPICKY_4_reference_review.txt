

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper proposes an unsupervised learning approach for stochastic And-Or grammars that jointly learns grammar structure and parameters. The authors introduce a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and propose learning via iterative insertion of And-Or fragments, claiming to unify composition and reconfiguration learning. The approach is evaluated on event and image grammar learning tasks, with claimed improvements over prior work.

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a clear, unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type (text, images, events).
- The And-Or fragment concept is a novel and potentially valuable extension to previous structure learning approaches.
- The empirical results on event and image grammars appear promising, with competitive or better performance than baselines.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological inconsistencies:** The paper claims to optimize "the posterior probability" but the mathematical formulation in Section 3.1 is inconsistent. The objective function is written as P(G|X) ∝ P(G)P(X|G), but the subsequent discussion uses Viterbi likelihood P(X|G) instead of the standard likelihood. The paper doesn't properly explain how the Viterbi approximation affects the posterior calculation, creating ambiguity in the optimization target.

2. **Incomplete technical details:** The paper states that "the posterior gain is equal to the product of the likelihood gain and the prior gain" (Section 3.3) but fails to provide a clear mathematical derivation showing why these factors are multiplicative. The sufficient statistics mentioned are not properly defined, and the paper doesn't explain how these statistics are derived from the training data.

3. **Lack of empirical evidence for key claims:** The paper claims that "the resulting grammar is often more compact" but provides no empirical evidence of grammar size reduction. The statement that "reasonable And-Or fragments usually increase the posterior probability" is asserted without supporting evidence or analysis.

4. **Formatting and presentation issues:** 
   - Inconsistent spacing around mathematical symbols (e.g., "‖G‖" vs "‖G‖")
   - Table 1 has inconsistent formatting in the "Relations in And-rules" column
   - Figure 2(c) is mislabeled as "context matrix" when it appears to be a co-occurrence matrix
   - The paper mentions "Figure 1" but the reference is inconsistent with the actual figure numbering

5. **Experimental evaluation gaps:** The paper reports F-measures without defining them in the context of their evaluation. The claim that "our approach significantly outperforms the competing approach" in Tables 2 and 3 lacks statistical significance testing. The paper uses "purity" and "inverse purity" without defining these terms, making evaluation difficult to interpret.

6. **Insufficient comparison with related work:** The paper cites [17] as a competing approach but doesn't clearly explain how their method differs from it beyond the And-Or fragment concept. The connection to sum-product networks [10] mentioned in the introduction is not properly developed.

**Major concerns:**
The paper's claim that "the search for Or-fragments can be error-prone" is not supported with evidence. The paper doesn't provide a theoretical justification for why the And-Or fragment approach is superior to separate And/Or fragment learning, beyond anecdotal claims. The paper also fails to address how the proposed method scales to larger grammars or more complex data, despite claiming to handle "exponential" rule spaces.

The most concerning issue is the paper's failure to properly define the optimization target. The use of Viterbi likelihood (which incorporates an unambiguity bias) without proper justification or analysis of its impact on the posterior probability undermines the theoretical foundation of the approach. This is a significant methodological flaw in a paper that claims to optimize the posterior probability.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper presents an interesting formalization and a potentially useful extension to grammar learning, the methodological inconsistencies, lack of technical detail, and insufficient empirical evidence for key claims prevent it from meeting NIPS quality standards. The paper would benefit from a clearer mathematical foundation, more rigorous experimental validation, and better presentation of results. As currently written, the paper contains several critical technical flaws that undermine its main contributions.

I recommend rejection due to these methodological and presentation issues, despite the potentially interesting core idea. The paper would benefit from significant revision before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
