- Proposal: Unsupervised method to learn structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars via novel "And-Or fragments".
- Key idea: And-Or fragments jointly capture compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules), unlike prior separate approaches.
- Algorithm: Iteratively grows a grammar from a trivial start, adding fragments to optimize posterior probability using efficient sufficient statistics.
- Theoretical contribution: Provides a data-type-agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and addresses prior limitations.
- Algorithmic innovation: Derives sufficient statistics for efficient posterior gain computation and claims reduced data scarcity issues versus And-only fragments.
- Empirical validation: Shows improvements on synthetic, event, and image datasets (better F-measure and perplexity; subtypes experiment supports robustness).
- Theoretical gaps: Posterior gain assumptions (e.g., unambiguity) and links between coherence measures and grammar quality are insufficiently justified; supplementary material missing.
- Experimental limitations: Comparisons limited to older methods, lacks ablation studies isolating contributions, and Viterbi likelihood approximation is not well-justified for ambiguous grammars.
- Implementation details missing: No complexity analysis or runtime comparisons; runtime claim (few minutes on desktop for 5000 samples) lacks context.
- Overstated generality: Claims applicability to multiple data types but evaluates only on events and images; text experiments absent.
- Related work: Positions against prior structure learning work but does not clearly distinguish from fragment-based [17]; novelty over [17]/[18] not sharply articulated.
- Overall assessment: Solid contribution addressing a real limitation; technically sound but with theoretical and experimental weaknesses.
- Scores: Quality 7/10, Clarity 8/10, Originality 7/10, Significance 7/10.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
- Requested revisions: Clarify theoretical assumptions and justify Viterbi approximation, compare to more recent And-Or grammar methods, add runtime analysis and ablation study, and provide more context for computational claims.
