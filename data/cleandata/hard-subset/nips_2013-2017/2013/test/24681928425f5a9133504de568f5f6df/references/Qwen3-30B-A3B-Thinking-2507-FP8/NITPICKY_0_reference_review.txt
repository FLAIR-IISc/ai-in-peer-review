

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper proposes an unsupervised approach for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars. The authors present a unified formalization of these grammars that is agnostic to the type of data being modeled (text, images, events), and propose an iterative algorithm that learns grammar fragments in a unified And-Or manner rather than separately learning compositions and reconfigurations.

**Strengths:**
The paper provides a clear formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and presents a novel algorithm that unifies the learning of compositions and reconfigurations. The experimental results on event grammars and image grammars show competitive performance compared to previous approaches. The paper's theoretical justification for using And-Or fragments rather than separate And-fragments or Or-fragments is reasonably well-motivated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Critical formatting and presentation issues:** The paper references multiple figures (Figures 1, 2, 3) that are not included in the provided text. This severely undermines the ability to assess the algorithm's mechanics and the examples presented. The lack of visual illustrations for key concepts like the And-Or fragment (Figure 2) is particularly problematic.

2. **Mathematical notation inconsistencies:** The paper uses inconsistent spacing around mathematical operators (e.g., "P (G|X)" vs "P(G|X)") and uses non-standard notation like "‖RDi(aij)‖" without defining what this notation represents. This makes the technical content difficult to follow.

3. **Insufficient experimental justification:** The paper states "We used 55 samples of each dataset as the training set and evaluated the learned grammars on the remaining 6 samples" for the event grammar experiments. With only 6 test samples, the evaluation lacks statistical significance and is highly susceptible to sampling bias. The paper fails to report confidence intervals or statistical tests to support its claims.

4. **Missing technical details:** The paper claims "the posterior gain can be formulated based on a set of sufficient statistics" but fails to provide the complete derivation in the main text (only referencing "supplementary material"). The paper also doesn't define key terms like "‖RDi(aij)‖" clearly.

5. **Inconsistent terminology:** The paper uses inconsistent hyphenation ("And-Or" vs "And-Or") and inconsistent capitalization ("And-Or grammars" vs "and-or grammars"), which creates confusion.

6. **Overstated claims:** The paper claims "our approach is capable of learning from different types of data, e.g., text, images, events" but only demonstrates results on images and events, not text. The text grammar evaluation is notably absent.

7. **Lack of comparison to relevant work:** The paper cites [17, 18] as previous work on image and event grammars but doesn't clearly articulate how their approach differs from these works. The comparison to [17] in the image grammar experiments is presented without sufficient detail about the baseline approach.

8. **Implementation details missing:** The paper states "our prototype implementation can finish running within a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples" but provides no details about the hardware, software, or specific implementation choices that would allow reproducibility.

**Overall assessment:**
While the paper presents an interesting approach to grammar learning with a theoretically sound motivation, the numerous presentation flaws, insufficient experimental validation, and technical omissions significantly undermine the paper's contribution. The lack of figures, inconsistent notation, and questionable evaluation methodology make it difficult to assess the validity of the claims. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. The paper's technical content has merit, but the numerous presentation flaws, insufficient experimental validation, and lack of detail in critical sections make it difficult to evaluate the claims. The paper would require substantial revision to address these issues before it could be considered for publication. The minor flaws identified here collectively outweigh the paper's conceptual contributions.
