Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) from unannotated data. It formalizes a context-free subclass of AOGs that is agnostic to modality (text, images, events), and introduces an iterative, bottom-up structure learning algorithm that adds And-Or fragments—each comprising an And-rule over several Or-nodes—to maximize the posterior of the grammar under a sparsity prior. The likelihood is approximated with Viterbi likelihood, and the posterior gain of adding a fragment is computed using sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) under an unambiguity assumption. The method learns Or-rule probabilities from counts and balances prior gain (model size) against likelihood gain (coherence). Experiments on event parsing and image modeling report improvements over prior AOG structure learning approaches.

This work relates to (i) grammar induction in NLP (e.g., Stolcke & Omohundro 1994; Solan et al. 2005; Klein & Manning 2004) and Viterbi/unambiguity biases (Spitkovsky et al. 2010; Tu & Honavar 2012); (ii) image/event AOGs (Zhu & Mumford 2006; Si & Zhu 2013; Si et al. 2011) and compositional models; and (iii) tractable probabilistic models such as sum-product networks (Poon & Domingos 2011), to which AOGs are closely related. The idea of jointly inducing composition and reconfiguration via And-Or fragments is a notable departure from prior split searches for And- and Or-fragments (e.g., Si et al., 2011; 2013).

Strengths
- Unified and clear formalization of context-free AOGs across modalities.
- Principled objective: posterior maximization with a sparsity prior; Viterbi likelihood is a standard and empirically useful approximation for grammar induction.
- Key algorithmic idea: learn And-Or fragments that couple composition and reconfiguration, addressing data scarcity and redundancy issues seen when learning And/Or rules separately.
- Efficient scoring via sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor and context matrix); intuitive coherence interpretation.
- Practical, iterative procedure with greedy/beam search and random restarts; runs in minutes for moderate-sized datasets.
- Empirical results show gains over prior AOG structure learning for events ([18]) and images ([17]), including a synthetic image setting where ground truth permits KL/F1 assessment and a real dataset with reduced perplexity.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality
- The unambiguity assumption is central to the efficient posterior gain calculation. It is unclear when this holds for learned AOGs with Or choices, and how violations affect correctness. No diagnostics or regularization are reported beyond adopting Viterbi likelihood.
- Parameter learning for And-rule “energy terms” (relations) is underspecified. The derivations yield Or-rule probabilities from reductions, but how are the relation energies learned or estimated (e.g., relative position models in images, temporal relations in events)? Are they fixed, discretized, or optimized?
- The prior term uses encoding costs sa and so and a hyperparameter α. There is no sensitivity analysis or principled selection procedure; results may depend on these choices.
- No ablations: e.g., And-Or fragments vs (i) And-only then Or-only, (ii) And-Or without context coherence, (iii) different n values, (iv) beam width/random restarts.
- Statistical significance of reported improvements is not provided; error bars are absent.
- Scalability beyond 5k samples with >10 atoms is untested; the number of candidate fragments can grow rapidly with n and relation types.

Clarity
- Some essential algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement (pseudocode, full derivations). The main text could be clearer on:
  - How contexts are defined per modality (events vs images) and enumerated.
  - How relations in And-rules are parameterized and scored during fragment evaluation.
  - The exact candidate fragment generation process, constraints on relations, and the value(s) of n explored.
  - How reductions are performed when multiple occurrences/overlaps exist (greedy conflicts, tie-breaking).
- The modification to switch top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes to handle multiple events per video is ad hoc; discuss implications for the objective and parsing.

Originality
- The And-Or fragment concept that jointly induces composition and reconfiguration and the posterior-gain decomposition into tensor/matrix coherence are novel and interesting. The ability to construct any context-free AOG from these fragments is stated but not proved in the main text.

Significance
- The approach has potential to make structure learning of compositional, reconfigurable models more robust and practical across modalities. However, the experimental validation is limited in scope: event experiments use small datasets and baselines restricted to [15, 18]; image experiments compare only to [17]. Given the claim of a general framework, broader comparisons are warranted.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a proof (or a clear reference) that any context-free AOG can be constructed via sequences of And-Or fragments as defined.
- Clarify and empirically evaluate the impact of the unambiguity assumption. Add an unambiguity regularizer or a post-hoc ambiguity check; report ambiguity metrics on learned grammars.
- Specify and evaluate the learning/estimation of And-rule relation parameters. For images, describe the relative-position model (parametric form, discretization) and its training; for events, specify temporal relation modeling and probabilities.
- Add ablations: (i) fragment type (And-Or vs separate And then Or), (ii) with/without context coherence factor, (iii) varying n, (iv) beam size/restarts, (v) priors (α, sa/so).
- Expand baselines: grammar induction (e.g., Bayesian Model Merging, Klein & Manning 2004) and structure learning for SPNs (e.g., Gens & Domingos 2013) on analogous representations; for images, include compositional or tractable models beyond [17].
- Report statistical significance and variance across splits; include runtime and scaling curves.
- Provide qualitative analyses: visualize learned fragments, parse trees, and failure cases; discuss common error modes (e.g., spurious merges).
- Detail evaluation protocols: smoothing method for perplexity; how quantization and terminal inventories are fixed; how detection thresholds are selected in event parsing.
- Discuss limitations: context-free restriction, dependence on discretized atoms, and potential extension to mildly context-sensitive structures or continuous relations.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled and novel structure learning mechanism that unifies composition and reconfiguration.
- Efficient scoring with interpretable sufficient statistics.
- Cross-modal applicability with promising empirical gains over prior AOG learning.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on unproven/unquantified unambiguity assumption; incomplete parameter learning story for relations.
- Limited baselines and experimental breadth; lack of ablations and significance tests.
- Important algorithmic details deferred or unclear, making reproduction difficult.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The core idea is compelling and potentially impactful, but the current submission lacks sufficient clarity, breadth of evaluation, and analysis of key assumptions to meet the NeurIPS bar. Addressing the above suggestions would substantially strengthen the contribution.
