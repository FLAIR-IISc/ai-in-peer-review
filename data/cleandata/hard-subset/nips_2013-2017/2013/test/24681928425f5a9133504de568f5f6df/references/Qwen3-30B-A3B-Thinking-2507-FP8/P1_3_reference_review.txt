

# Peer Review: Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars

## Summary
This paper introduces an unsupervised learning approach for Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAOGs) that simultaneously learns grammar structure and parameters. SAOGs extend traditional stochastic grammars to model compositional structures in diverse data types (text, images, events) through And-nodes (representing compositions) and Or-nodes (representing reconfigurations). The authors propose a unified method that learns "And-Or fragments" (combining both And and Or structures) rather than separate And-fragments and Or-fragments, which they argue is more efficient and robust to data scarcity. The approach iteratively refines a grammar from a trivial initial structure by adding And-Or fragments that maximize the posterior probability of the grammar. The paper evaluates the approach on event grammars (from human activity videos) and image grammars (from synthetic and real animal face datasets), showing competitive or better performance than previous methods.

The work builds upon prior research in structure learning of grammars [14,15,16] and extends recent work on And-Or grammars for images [17] and events [18]. It differs from previous approaches by explicitly optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar structure and parameters, rather than relying on heuristics.

## Strengths

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear and significant contribution by proposing a unified approach to learning both composition and reconfiguration in grammars through And-Or fragments. This unification addresses limitations of previous work that treated these aspects separately, which the authors convincingly argue leads to more efficient and robust learning. The formalization of SAOGs as agnostic to data type is also a valuable contribution that broadens the applicability of the framework.

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound and well-justified. The authors provide a rigorous derivation of the posterior gain calculation, showing how it can be computed efficiently using sufficient statistics. The empirical evaluation is thorough, with well-designed experiments on two distinct domains (event and image grammars) using appropriate metrics (F-measure, perplexity). The comparison with relevant baselines [17,18] is fair and demonstrates the advantages of the proposed method.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The formal definition of SAOGs (Section 2) is precise, and the learning algorithm (Section 3) is effectively illustrated with figures (Figures 1-3). The problem definition and motivation are well articulated, and the experimental setup is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

**Significance:** The problem of unsupervised structure learning of grammars is important for modeling compositional structures across multiple domains. The proposed approach has the potential to be widely applicable beyond the specific domains tested, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over previous methods, particularly on more challenging datasets.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Domain Validation:** While the paper claims the framework is agnostic to data type, the empirical evaluation is limited to two domains (event and image grammars). Showing results on a third domain, such as natural language processing, would strengthen the claim of generality and provide more compelling evidence for the framework's broad applicability.

**Algorithmic Complexity:** The paper states the approach runs "reasonably fast" (within minutes on a desktop for 5000 samples) but lacks a thorough analysis of computational complexity. A formal complexity analysis would help readers understand the scalability of the approach to larger datasets or more complex grammars.

**Assumption of Grammar Unambiguity:** The likelihood gain calculation relies on the assumption of grammar unambiguity, which might not hold in many real-world scenarios. The paper doesn't discuss how sensitive the results are to this assumption or provide analysis of how often this assumption is violated in practice.

**Comparison with Sum-Product Networks:** The paper notes the connection to sum-product networks (SPNs) [10] but doesn't clearly articulate how this approach differs from or improves upon SPNs, which also model compositional structures. A more detailed comparison would help clarify the unique contribution.

**Lack of Ablation Study:** While the paper compares three methods of applying their approach (ID sequences vs. vector sequences), it doesn't provide an ablation study to isolate the contribution of each key component (e.g., the And-Or fragments vs. the posterior probability optimization).

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning for compositional models. The unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments is novel and well-justified, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over previous methods.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Novel unified approach to structure learning that addresses limitations of previous methods
- Rigorous theoretical foundation with efficient computation of posterior gain
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation across two domains with clear improvements
- Well-written and well-organized paper with clear contributions

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Limited validation to two data domains (would benefit from a third)
- Lacks formal computational complexity analysis
- Assumes grammar unambiguity without discussing its limitations
- Could better differentiate from sum-product networks

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic grammars and compositional modeling. The strengths substantially outweigh the weaknesses, and the paper is technically sound with clear empirical validation. The limited domain validation is a minor concern given the paper's focus on a general framework, and the other weaknesses could be addressed in a camera-ready version.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper addresses an important problem with a novel, well-motivated approach that is both theoretically sound and empirically effective. It advances the state of the art in unsupervised structure learning for compositional models and has clear potential for broader application across multiple domains.
