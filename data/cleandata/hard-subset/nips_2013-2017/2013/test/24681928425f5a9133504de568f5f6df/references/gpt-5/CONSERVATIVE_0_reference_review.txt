Summary
The paper proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of context-free stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) across modalities (images, events, potentially text). The key idea is to grow the grammar by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments—each comprising one And-node and several Or-nodes—chosen to maximize the posterior under a sparsity prior. The likelihood is approximated by the Viterbi likelihood with an unambiguity bias. To avoid full reparsing, the authors derive a posterior-gain computation based on sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor over candidate configurations and a context matrix over configuration-context co-occurrences. They also give closed-form estimates for Or-rule probabilities within a fragment. Experiments on (i) event grammar learning from videos (using pre-annotated atomic actions) and (ii) image grammar learning on a synthetic animal-face sketch dataset and a real animal-face dataset show improvements over prior AOG structure-learning methods [17,18] and a classic grammar-induction baseline [15] under F-measure/KL (synthetic) and perplexity (real images).

Strengths
- Clear, modular framework for structure learning with an explicit posterior objective combining Viterbi likelihood and an MDL-like sparsity prior.
- The And-Or fragment formulation unifies learning of compositions and reconfigurations, plausibly improving robustness over separate And/Or searches; the sufficient-statistics view (tensor/matrix coherence) is elegant and potentially efficient.
- Empirical results on selected datasets show substantial improvements over prior AOG-specific learners [17,18]; synthetic study includes KL to a known ground truth.
- The formalization is modality-agnostic and could be of interest to communities modeling compositional structures (AOGs/SPNs).

Weaknesses
- Strong modeling/derivation assumptions with limited justification:
  - Viterbi training and an explicit unambiguity assumption drive the likelihood and the gain computation. Ambiguity is common in grammars for images/events; no empirical assessment of ambiguity or sensitivity to this assumption is provided.
  - The claim that And-Or fragments suffice to construct any context-free AOG is asserted but no proof or constructive procedure is included.
- Limited empirical validation relative to broad claims:
  - Event experiments rely on annotated atomic actions (with start/end times). This weakens the “unsupervised” claim and sidesteps one of the hardest parts of the pipeline. The test set is very small (6 videos), and no variance/error bars or statistical tests are reported.
  - Baselines are narrow: mostly earlier AOG learners [17,18] and a classical grammar induction approach [15]. Absent are stronger and more established structure-learning baselines (e.g., Bayesian PCFG/HDP-PCFG/adaptor grammars for language; SPN structure learning; hierarchical tiling/scene grammars; segmental models or HSMMs for events; modern generative image models). Without these, it is hard to gauge significance beyond the AOG subliterature.
  - No ablations to isolate contributions: And-Or fragments vs And-only/Or-only, effect of the sparsity prior α and encoding costs sa/so, beam width/greedy choices, Viterbi vs full likelihood, with/without the context-coherence term.
- Reproducibility and clarity gaps:
  - Many core derivations and algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement; the search space restriction, the set of relations allowed in And-rules, construction of tensors/contexts, and smoothing used for perplexity are underspecified in the main text.
  - No complexity analysis, scaling curves, or runtime/memory profiles beyond a single anecdotal runtime. No code.
- Scope vs claims:
  - Although the formalism is advertised as agnostic, there are no NLP experiments; the event setup uses engineered atomic actions; the image pipeline depends on a specific quantization. It is unclear how robust the method is to the choice/quality of terminals.

Quality
Technically plausible but several key steps rely on strong approximations (Viterbi, unambiguity) and heuristics (greedy/beam with restricted fragments). The posterior-gain derivation appears reasonable given the assumptions, but formal guarantees (consistency, convergence, approximation quality) are lacking. Experimental support is limited in breadth and statistical rigor.

Clarity
Generally well-organized with intuitive figures, but important details are deferred. Some notation and figure labeling could be improved, and the encoding-cost prior (sa, so) should be justified and analyzed. A more explicit algorithmic description in the main text would help reproducibility.

Originality
The unification via And-Or fragments and the sufficient-statistics factorization into tensor/matrix coherence are novel combinations of ideas building on Bayesian model merging, biclustering, and SPN/AOG literature. The overall novelty is moderate.

Significance
Potentially useful for practitioners already invested in AOGs/SPNs, but current empirical evidence is too narrow to conclude broad impact. Stronger, more comprehensive comparisons and ablations are needed to establish the method as a go-to approach for unsupervised structure learning.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide proofs (or at least sketches) for expressivity (constructing any context-free AOG) and clear conditions under which the posterior-gain factorization is valid.
- Measure and report grammar ambiguity; compare Viterbi vs sum-product likelihood during learning.
- Add ablations (fragment types, α/sa/so sensitivity, beam width) and report grammar size vs performance.
- Substantially broaden baselines (SPN structure learning; HDP-PCFG/adaptor grammars; segmental/HSMMs; modern image generative models) and include statistical tests.
- Include scaling experiments and release code.
- If claiming modality-agnosticism, add at least one NLP grammar induction experiment.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean framework for unified structure learning in AOGs with an efficient scoring mechanism.
- Promising empirical gains over prior AOG-specific methods, including on a synthetic dataset with ground truth.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on untested assumptions (unambiguity, Viterbi) and lack of theoretical guarantees.
- Limited and narrow empirical validation; weak baselines; no ablations; small test sets; unclear statistical significance.
- Reproducibility concerns and missing details in the main text.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is interesting, but the current paper does not meet the level of empirical rigor and comparative evaluation expected for NeurIPS. With stronger baselines, ablations, theoretical support, and clearer presentation, this could be competitive in a future iteration.

Confidence: medium.
