Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) from unannotated data across modalities (text, images, events). The authors formalize a context-free subclass of stochastic AOGs unified across domains, where And-rules encode decompositions with relations (e.g., spatial, temporal) and Or-rules encode alternatives. The learning objective maximizes a posterior with a sparsity prior over grammar size and uses Viterbi likelihood for tractability and an unambiguity bias.

The key algorithmic idea is to iteratively add And-Or fragments (a new And-node with a product of Or-nodes), rather than searching for And- or Or-fragments separately. The posterior gain from adding a fragment is approximated using sufficient statistics derived from the training data: an n-gram tensor counting configurations under the fragment and a context matrix counting configuration–context co-occurrences. Under an unambiguity assumption, the likelihood gain factorizes into products tied to tensor/matrix “coherence” (referencing biclustering measures), and the prior gain follows an MDL-style penalty proportional to grammar size (with encoding lengths sa, so). Or-rule probabilities are estimated by relative counts of reductions.

Experiments include: (a) event grammars learned from human activity videos, with three application variants (ID-based and action-grounded with “co-occurring” and “followed-by” relations). Evaluation uses purity/inverse purity F-measure against annotations; comparison is made to prior grammar induction approaches that operate on ID sequences [15, 18]. (b) image grammars learned from a synthetic animal-face sketch dataset with known ground-truth grammar (measuring F1 and KL divergence) and a real animal-face dataset [17] (measuring perplexity via 10-fold CV). The method reportedly matches or exceeds baselines, with larger gains on the synthetic and real image datasets over [17].

This work builds on and extends: unsupervised grammar induction (e.g., Bayesian model merging [14], ADIOS-style [15], iterative biclustering [16]); And-Or models in vision and events [1, 3, 7, 17, 18]; Viterbi/unambiguity biases in grammar learning [20, 21]; and relates to SPNs [10].

Quality
- Technical soundness: The fragment-based posterior optimization is plausible and well-motivated. The use of And-Or fragments unifies composition and reconfiguration learning, addressing data sparsity and redundancy issues present in separate And/Or searches. The derivation of posterior gain relies on (i) Viterbi likelihood, (ii) an unambiguity assumption, and (iii) the availability of sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor and context matrix). The paper provides a formula for Or-rule probabilities from reduction counts. However:
  - The parameterization and learning of And-rule probabilities/energies over relations are not specified in detail (beyond their role in likelihood gain). It is unclear how relation-specific parameters are estimated or regularized.
  - The prior (exp(-α‖G‖)) instantiated via encoding lengths sa and so is MDL-like but ad hoc; the coding scheme is not fully specified, and the claim that reductions decrease grammar size needs a precise definition of grammar length (e.g., sum of RHS lengths across rules) to be fully convincing.
  - The statement that adding only And-Or fragments can construct any context-free AOG is important but lacks proof or a constructive sketch in the main text.
  - The unambiguity assumption is strong; while the authors cite unambiguity regularization, the impact on real-world ambiguous grammars and on inference is not analyzed.
  - Complexity and scalability: although runtimes are stated (minutes for 5k samples), the search space size, beam settings, and sensitivity to n and relation types are not analyzed.

- Empirical evaluation: The synthetic image experiment is appropriate and demonstrates advantages over [17]. The real image results show notable perplexity improvements. The event experiments use small test sets (6 videos), and the comparisons to ID-only methods are partially alleviated by including an ID-based variant of the proposed method; nonetheless, effect sizes and statistical variability (e.g., confidence intervals) are not reported. Ablations isolating the contribution of And-Or fragments vs separate And- or Or- searches are missing.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-structured and clear at a high level. The unified definition of AOGs is useful. Important details are deferred to the supplementary material (full derivation of likelihood gain, pseudocode), which limits reproducibility from the main text. Specific areas needing clarification include: precise definition of grammar size; parameterization and learning of And-rule energy terms; how non-overlap and relation constraints are enforced during parsing; search constraints on fragment types; smoothing used in perplexity computation; and how CYK parsing is applicable when relations include “co-occurring” beyond purely sequential structure.

Originality
The focus on And-Or fragments to jointly induce compositions and reconfigurations, and the posterior gain formulation via tensor/matrix statistics under Viterbi/unambiguity, appear novel relative to prior AOG structure learning in images/events [17, 18] and classic grammar induction [14–16]. The cross-domain formalization adds value.

Significance
Learning compact, generalizable AOGs across modalities is important. If robust, the approach could influence structure learning for compositional probabilistic models and relate to SPN structure learning. Current empirical evidence is promising, with strong gains in image domains; event results are suggestive but limited by dataset size and evaluation scope.

Strengths
- Unified, domain-agnostic formalization of context-free AOGs.
- Principled objective (posterior) with a practical approximation via Viterbi and sufficient statistics.
- And-Or fragment search that jointly learns compositions and alternatives, addressing sparsity and redundancy.
- Competitive to superior empirical results, especially on image datasets; synthetic evaluation against ground truth is well-chosen.
- Reasonable runtime on moderately sized datasets.

Weaknesses
- Critical details of And-rule parameter learning and relation modeling are under-specified.
- Prior/coding-length formulation lacks rigor and sensitivity analysis (α, sa, so).
- Reliance on unambiguity; limited discussion of ambiguity handling and its effect.
- Limited analysis of search complexity and sensitivity (n, relations, beam size).
- Event experiments small and comparisons partially constrained by baselines; no statistical significance reporting.
- Claim of completeness of And-Or fragments lacks proof in main text.

Questions for the authors
- How are And-rule probabilities/energies over relations parameterized and estimated? Are there learned parameters per relation type, and how are they regularized?
- What is the exact definition of grammar size and coding scheme underlying sa and so? How sensitive are results to α, sa, so?
- Can you provide a proof or constructive sketch that And-Or fragments suffice to construct any context-free AOG?
- How does the method behave with ambiguous grammars? Is there an explicit unambiguity regularizer or pruning?
- What are the search settings (n values, relation sets, beam width), and how sensitive are results to these?
- In the event setting, how is CYK applicable when relations include “co-occurring”? Is the grammar over sequences or sets?

Suggestions for improvement
- Include a formal MDL coding scheme and sensitivity analysis for α, sa, so.
- Detail the And-rule parameter learning and enforcement of relation constraints.
- Add ablations: And-only, Or-only, and separate-stage baselines; with and without unambiguity assumption.
- Provide a proof (or in the supplement) of completeness of And-Or fragments.
- Report confidence intervals and effect sizes; expand event evaluation or include additional datasets.
- Clarify inference algorithms per domain (parsing in 2D images vs 1D sequences) and complexity.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated approach to joint structure learning in AOGs with cross-domain applicability.
- Solid improvements on image grammar learning including a real dataset; synthetic study against ground truth is compelling.
- Clear algorithmic contribution (And-Or fragments with posterior gain via sufficient statistics).

Arguments against acceptance
- Important technical details are missing or deferred (And-rule learning, MDL prior, completeness proof).
- Strong assumptions (unambiguity) and limited analysis of their implications.
- Event experiments are limited in scope and scale; statistical reporting is sparse.
- Limited complexity/sensitivity analysis and ablations reduce confidence in robustness.
