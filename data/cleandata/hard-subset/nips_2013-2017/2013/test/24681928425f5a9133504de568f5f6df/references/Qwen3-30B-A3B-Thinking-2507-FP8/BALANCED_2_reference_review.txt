

# Review: Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars

This paper proposes an unsupervised learning approach for Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAOGs), which are powerful models for representing compositional and reconfigurable patterns across different data types. The authors present a unified formalization of SAOGs that is agnostic to data type (text, images, events), and introduce a novel learning algorithm that simultaneously learns grammar structure and parameters by optimizing the posterior probability. The key innovation is the use of "And-Or fragments" to unify the learning of compositions and reconfigurations, avoiding the limitations of previous approaches that treat these as separate processes.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The paper provides a clear formalization of SAOGs and a theoretically grounded learning algorithm that explicitly optimizes the posterior probability. The derivation of sufficient statistics for efficient computation of posterior gain is well-executed and represents a significant technical contribution. The use of And-Or fragments to unify composition and reconfiguration learning is novel and addresses a key limitation in prior work.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach on two distinct domains (event grammars for human activity recognition and image grammars for object recognition) with thorough experimental design. The comparison with relevant baselines ([17, 18]) is well-justified, and the results demonstrate consistent improvements, particularly on more challenging datasets.

3. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The formal definitions, algorithm description, and experimental results are presented clearly. The figures (particularly Figure 1 and Figure 2) effectively illustrate the key concepts and learning process.

4. **Generalizability:** The unified formalization of SAOGs that works across different data types is a significant contribution that extends the applicability of the approach beyond previous work limited to specific domains.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Recent Work:** While the paper compares well to previous work on SAOG structure learning, it does not adequately address more recent approaches to grammar learning, such as neural grammar models or differentiable approaches. This omission weakens the claim of novelty in the context of the broader field.

2. **Practical Limitations:** The assumption that each training sample consists of a single pattern (rather than multiple patterns) is a significant limitation for many applications. While the authors mention a simple modification to handle multiple events per sample, this seems ad hoc and doesn't address the more general case where samples contain multiple unrelated patterns.

3. **Computational Complexity:** The paper states that the algorithm "runs reasonably fast," but provides no detailed analysis of computational complexity or scalability. For larger datasets or more complex grammars, the greedy/beam search approach might become computationally prohibitive.

4. **Interpretability:** The paper demonstrates empirical success but doesn't explore whether the learned grammars are interpretable or provide meaningful insights about the data structure. This is a missed opportunity to highlight the potential value of the approach beyond just performance metrics.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1. The authors should expand their related work section to include recent approaches to grammar learning, particularly those using neural networks or differentiable methods. This would better position their work within the current landscape.

2. A more thorough analysis of the computational complexity and scalability of the approach would strengthen the paper. The authors could include experiments showing how runtime scales with dataset size.

3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the interpretability of the learned grammars. For instance, they could show examples of grammars learned from image data and discuss whether the learned structures align with human understanding of object composition.

4. The authors should consider addressing the limitation of the single-pattern-per-sample assumption more thoroughly. One approach could be to explicitly model multiple patterns per sample during the learning process rather than modifying the grammar after learning.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of grammar learning. The unified approach to learning grammar structure and parameters using And-Or fragments is novel, well-motivated, and technically sound. The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate clear advantages over previous methods. While the paper has some limitations regarding comparison to recent work and practical assumptions, these are not severe enough to undermine the technical contribution.

The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, clarity, and significance. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling with compositional structures. I recommend acceptance, with the understanding that addressing the suggested improvements in a revised version would further strengthen the paper. The work has the potential to influence future research in grammar-based modeling across multiple domains.
