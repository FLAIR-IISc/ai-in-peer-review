Summary
The paper addresses unsupervised structure learning for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), a context-free generalization of stochastic grammars closely related to sum-product networks. The authors propose to learn both structure and parameters from unannotated data by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments—each fragment introduces one new And-node with several new Or-children—into an initially overfitted grammar that exactly reproduces the training set. They optimize the posterior of the grammar under a sparsity prior, using Viterbi likelihood to approximate the data likelihood (motivated by prior work on unambiguity bias). A main technical contribution is an efficient computation of the posterior gain of a candidate fragment using sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor counting covered configurations and a context matrix counting configuration-context co-occurrences. This yields a factorization of the likelihood gain into two “coherence” terms and closed-form estimates for Or-rule probabilities. Search over fragments is done greedily or with a small beam, and the process stops when no fragment increases the posterior. Experiments on human activity data (event grammars) and image data (synthetic with ground truth and a real animal faces dataset) show improvements over prior AOG learning methods that either fix structure or separately learn compositions/reconfigurations.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well-situated with respect to: stochastic grammars for vision and events (Zhu & Mumford; Si & Zhu; Pei et al.), PCFG induction (Stolcke & Omohundro; Solan et al.; Tu & Honavar), Viterbi training for grammar induction (Spitkovsky et al.; Tu & Honavar), and SPNs (Poon & Domingos). The key differentiator from previous AOG structure learning (e.g., Si & Zhu; Si et al.) is the unified induction of compositions and reconfigurations via And-Or fragments and the explicit posterior optimization with sufficient statistics, rather than heuristic rule growth or pre-specified rule sets. The claimed generality across modalities (text/images/events) is plausible but not fully demonstrated beyond images and events.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The bottom-up fragment insertion under a size-penalizing prior is reasonable, and the Viterbi likelihood choice is supported by prior work. The derivation of the posterior gain, its factorization into coherence measures, and the closed-form Or-rule probabilities are compelling, but many crucial details are deferred to the supplement (e.g., full likelihood-gain derivation, exact definition of “context,” handling of relations/energies in And-rules, and proofs under the “grammar unambiguity” assumption). The prior term uses per-symbol encoding lengths (sa, so), but the encoding scheme and sensitivity are not specified.
- Assumptions and limitations: The unambiguity assumption and Viterbi approximation can bias learning; their empirical effects are not ablated. The search is greedy/beam with random restarts, but no complexity analysis, beam sizes, or convergence diagnostics are reported. The method’s ability to scale to large alphabets or richer relation sets is not characterized.
- Experiments: Results on two event datasets (one augmented to simulate detector variability) and two image settings are positive. However, datasets are modest in size; event experiments rely on annotated atomic actions; and comparisons are limited primarily to prior AOG-specific baselines (for events, methods that operate on ID sequences; for images, SZ [17]). Missing are broader structure-learning baselines (e.g., HDP-PCFG, Bayesian model merging, SPN structure learning), and ablations (And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or fragments, Viterbi vs standard likelihood, with/without unambiguity assumption). Statistical significance and variability (error bars) are not shown.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable, with motivating figures. That said, several elements need clarification for reproducibility: formal definition of grammar size and encoding lengths; explicit specification of relations and their parameterization (energies) in And-rules for each modality; precise definition of the “context” used in the context matrix; details of the search procedure (candidate generation, pruning, beam width, restarts); and parsing/inference procedures when relations impose spatial/temporal constraints. Some figures appear cluttered or partially corrupted; pseudo-code is mentioned but not present in the main paper.

Originality
The core idea of And-Or fragment induction that unifies composition and reconfiguration is a meaningful contribution relative to prior separate And/Or rule growth. The efficient posterior gain computation via n-gram tensors and context matrices and its interpretation as coherence is novel in this setting, though related in spirit to biclustering/co-clustering and tensor mining. The unifying formalization of context-free AOGs across modalities is useful but incremental.

Significance
If robust, the approach could be impactful for unsupervised structure learning in vision and event understanding, yielding more compact, generalizable grammars than prior heuristic methods. The cross-modal applicability is promising, but broader adoption will depend on clearer algorithmic details, stronger baselines, and demonstrations on larger, noisier datasets. The current empirical scope, while encouraging, is not yet definitive.

Pros
- Unified and principled structure learning via And-Or fragments with explicit posterior optimization.
- Efficient sufficient-statistics-based gain computation with closed-form Or-rule probabilities.
- Cross-modal formalization; positive results on both events and images; improved perplexity and KL/F-measure over prior AOG learning.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi approximations, without ablations.
- Important derivations/details deferred; ambiguity in context definition and encoding prior.
- Limited baselines and dataset scale; unclear search complexity and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- No analysis of learned grammar compactness/interpretability vs performance; no code availability.

Questions for authors
1) Precisely define the “context” used in the context matrix for events and images. How is it constructed and bounded? 
2) What are sa and so, and how sensitive are results to these choices and to α?
3) How are relations and their energy terms parameterized and estimated in And-rules? Are there learned continuous parameters?
4) What are beam widths, restart counts, and runtime/complexity as a function of data size and fragment arity?
5) How often does the unambiguity assumption hold in practice? What happens when parses are ambiguous?
6) Could you report ablations (And-only, Or-only, And-Or; Viterbi vs standard EM; with vs without context coherence factor)?

Suggestions
- Bring key derivations into the main text and provide pseudo-code.
- Add broader baselines (e.g., HDP-PCFG/BMM, SPN structure learning) and ablations; report error bars.
- Analyze grammar size, number of rules, and interpretability across iterations; study sensitivity to α, sa, so.
- Clarify and justify the prior encoding; consider MDL more formally.
- Provide complexity analysis and discuss scalability; release code if possible.
- Demonstrate generality on one small NLP dataset to substantiate modality-agnostic claims.

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The paper offers a principled and novel approach to AOG structure learning with promising empirical gains. With clearer exposition and stronger experimental validation, it could be a solid contribution to NIPS.
