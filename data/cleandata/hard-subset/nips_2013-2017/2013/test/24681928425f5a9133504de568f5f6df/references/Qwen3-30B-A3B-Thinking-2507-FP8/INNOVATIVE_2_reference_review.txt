

# Review: Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars

This paper proposes a novel approach to unsupervised learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars by introducing "And-Or fragments" that unify the learning of composition (And-rules) and reconfiguration (Or-rules) in a single framework. The authors present a formalization of Stochastic And-Or Grammars that is agnostic to data types, and develop an algorithm that starts from a trivial grammar and iteratively adds And-Or fragments to optimize the posterior probability of the grammar.

The key novelty lies in the concept of And-Or fragments - a single structural unit that contains both And-nodes and Or-nodes, allowing the algorithm to learn compositions and reconfigurations in a unified manner. Previous approaches had to learn these components separately, which the authors argue leads to inefficiency and fragility when data is scarce. The paper convincingly argues that And-Or fragments avoid the pitfalls of separate And-fragment and Or-fragment learning, and the theoretical derivation of likelihood and prior gains shows how these fragments can be efficiently evaluated.

The paper makes a strong case for its approach through thorough experimental validation on two distinct domains: event grammars (for activity recognition) and image grammars (for object modeling). The results demonstrate competitive or better performance compared to previous methods, with particularly strong results on the more challenging synthetic dataset where actions were divided into subtypes. The third method of applying their approach (directly grounding to atomic actions without restricting learning order) consistently outperformed the others, supporting their claim about the advantage of unifying relation learning.

The theoretical foundation is solid, with clear derivations of the likelihood gain and prior gain that enable efficient computation of the posterior gain. The algorithm is well-described and the implementation details suggest reasonable computational efficiency (minutes on 5000 samples).

This work builds on the authors' previous work on And-Or grammars [1, 3, 7, 17, 18] and extends the literature on structure learning of grammars [14, 15, 16] in a significant way. The formalization of Stochastic And-Or Grammars as data-agnostic models is particularly valuable, as it enables application to multiple domains without major modifications.

The paper's strengths are its novel conceptual contribution (the And-Or fragments), its theoretical grounding, and its empirical validation across two distinct domains. The unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations is a bold new direction that could influence how grammatical structures are learned in various domains.

Minor weaknesses include:
1. The explanation of why previous approaches struggled with separate And/Or learning could be more detailed.
2. The performance gains over previous methods, while consistent, are not revolutionary (e.g., the F-measure improvements in Table 2 are modest).
3. The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of the approach (e.g., how it handles extremely large datasets or highly complex structures).

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find the paper's core contribution to be highly significant. The And-Or fragments concept is a genuinely novel idea that addresses a meaningful problem in grammar learning, and the paper demonstrates its viability across multiple domains. The authors have provided sufficient theoretical foundation and empirical validation to support their claims.

The significance of this work is substantial. Stochastic grammars have been used to model compositional structures in various domains, but manual grammar specification is challenging. This unsupervised learning approach removes that bottleneck, potentially enabling broader application of grammatical models in computer vision, natural language processing, and activity recognition. The data-agnostic formalization makes it particularly appealing for cross-domain applications.

Compared to previous NIPS papers on grammatical models (e.g., [10] on sum-product networks, [17, 18] on And-Or grammars), this paper offers a genuinely new approach to structure learning rather than incremental improvements. It represents a significant conceptual advance in the field.

In conclusion, this paper presents a bold new approach to unsupervised grammar learning with strong theoretical foundation and empirical validation. While the performance gains are not revolutionary, the novelty of the approach and its potential to influence future work in grammatical models makes it a valuable contribution. The paper meets all the key criteria for NIPS acceptance: it's original, significant, well-written, and technically sound.

I recommend acceptance with high confidence.
