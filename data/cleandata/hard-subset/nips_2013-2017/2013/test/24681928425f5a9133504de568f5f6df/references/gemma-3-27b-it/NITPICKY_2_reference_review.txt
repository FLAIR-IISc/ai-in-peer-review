This paper proposes an unsupervised learning approach for stochastic And-Or grammars, aiming to jointly learn both the grammar structure and parameters. The approach iteratively inserts new “And-Or fragments” into the grammar, optimizing a posterior probability that balances grammar complexity and data likelihood. The authors claim contributions in formalizing the grammar, optimizing posterior probability, unifying composition and reconfiguration learning, and demonstrating empirical effectiveness on event and image grammars.

While the problem addressed is relevant and the idea of learning both structure and parameters simultaneously is appealing, the paper suffers from a concerning number of issues, ranging from unclear definitions and explanations to a lack of rigorous experimental evaluation and superficial comparison to existing work. 

The formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars, while presented as a unified definition, relies heavily on previously established concepts without sufficient novelty to warrant specific emphasis. The terminology, particularly regarding "relations" within And-rules, remains vague. What constitutes a "relation," how are they parameterized, and how does the choice of relation impact learning? These questions are not addressed sufficiently. The statement that the approach is “agnostic to the types of atomic patterns” feels unsubstantiated; the examples in Table 1 simply illustrate different *applications* of the same grammar, not a fundamentally data-independent formalism.

The core algorithm, based on iteratively adding And-Or fragments, presents several concerns. The justification for the choice of And-Or fragments – aiming to mitigate issues with separate composition and reconfiguration learning – lacks convincing elaboration. Why are And-Or fragments inherently superior to exploring And or Or fragments independently, particularly given the claim of a potential for complex grammar structures? The discussion surrounding the “sparsity prior” (α term in the objective) feels perfunctory, lacking any sensitivity analysis or justification for the chosen value.

The formulation of the likelihood gain, while presented with some mathematical detail, relies on several assumptions (grammar unambiguity) that may not hold in practice. The connection to “tensor/matrix coherence measures” feels tacked on, lacking a clear explanation of how these measures relate to the underlying grammar learning process. The description of the efficient computation also requires deeper justification. Is the computational complexity significantly lower than alternative approaches, and under what conditions? 

The experimental evaluation is particularly weak. The event grammar experiments, while using a dataset with increased complexity, are hampered by the limited number of samples (6 training, 6 testing). The comparison to previous approaches [15, 18] is insufficient. The authors only state that their approach is “competitive” or “outperforms,” without providing statistical significance tests or detailed analysis of the performance differences. Moreover, comparing to approaches that operate on different data representations (ID sequences vs. vector sequences) makes the comparison less meaningful. The image grammar experiments, while showing promising results on the synthetic dataset, lack detail regarding the parameter settings and the specific quantization method used. The real image experiments are similarly lacking in detail; the perplexity metric is presented without any discussion of its implications or comparison to baseline image models. The claim that perplexity is significantly improved compared to [17] relies on a single number with no error bars or statistical testing. The lack of qualitative results (e.g., visualizing the learned grammars) further hinders the evaluation.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* **Lack of Clarity:** Many concepts are vaguely defined, hindering understanding and reproducibility.
* **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The experiments are limited in scope, lack statistical significance, and provide insufficient detail for proper evaluation.
* **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of learning grammar structure is not entirely novel and the proposed “And-Or fragment” approach lacks a strong justification.
* **Superficial Comparison to Related Work:**  Comparisons to existing methods are often incomplete or based on different data representations.
* **Missing Details:** The paper lacks crucial details about the implementation and parameter settings. 



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Relevant Problem:** The unsupervised learning of stochastic grammars is a challenging and important research area.
* **Potential for Future Work:** The proposed approach, if refined and validated, could contribute to more flexible and expressive grammar learning.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite addressing a relevant problem, the paper's numerous weaknesses – particularly the lack of clarity, insufficient experimental validation, and superficial comparison to related work – outweigh its potential contributions. I recommend **rejection**. A significantly revised version with a more rigorous presentation, a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, and a clearer justification of the proposed approach would be required for reconsideration. The authors need to address the specific concerns raised above and demonstrate the genuine advantages of their method over existing techniques.
