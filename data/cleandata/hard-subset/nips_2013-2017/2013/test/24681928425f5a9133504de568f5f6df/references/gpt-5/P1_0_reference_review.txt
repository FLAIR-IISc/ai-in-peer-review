Summary
The paper proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of context-free Stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs) from unannotated data (text, images, events). Unlike prior work that either fixes the grammar structure and learns parameters or learns And- or Or-structure separately, the authors iteratively induce And-Or fragments—each comprising an And-node with multiple child Or-nodes—thereby jointly capturing compositionality (And) and reconfigurability (Or). They optimize a posterior objective combining a sparsity prior over grammar size with a Viterbi (best-parse) likelihood. A key technical contribution is an efficient estimate of posterior gain for a candidate fragment using sufficient statistics computed once from the data: an n-gram tensor over covered configurations and a context matrix over configuration-context co-occurrences. Under an unambiguity assumption, the likelihood gain factorizes into coherence measures over these statistics, yielding closed-form Or-rule probabilities and a simple prior gain based on coding lengths. The algorithm starts from a trivial grammar that memorizes the training set and greedily/beam-search inserts fragments until no posterior gain is possible. Applications to human activity event grammars and image grammars (synthetic and real animal faces) show comparable or improved performance over prior AOG structure learning methods [Si & Zhu 2013; Si et al. 2011].

Relation to prior work
The work builds on stochastic grammars in NLP (SCFGs), image grammars [Zhu & Mumford; Zhao & Zhu], and event grammars [Pei et al.], and is closely related to sum-product networks (SPNs). For structure learning, it extends Bayesian model merging [Stolcke & Omohundro], grammar induction from sequences [Solan et al.; Tu & Honavar], and recent AOG structure learning in images/events [Si & Zhu; Si et al.]. The unification via And-Or fragments and the posterior-gain formulation via tensor/matrix coherence (connecting to biclustering coherence) are the main differentiators.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The posterior gain decomposition (likelihood and prior) is plausible under stated assumptions (Viterbi training and unambiguity). The sufficient statistics and factorization into coherence terms are elegant and computationally attractive. However, several aspects need clarification or strengthening:
  - Unambiguity assumption: Many grammars for images/events are inherently ambiguous. The practical impact of this assumption on correctness and bias is not evaluated; it may systematically prefer overly “deterministic” structures.
  - Prior coding lengths: The prior uses coding lengths sa and so but their settings (beyond α) are not justified; sensitivity to these choices is not explored.
  - Search and complexity: The space of fragments is large (n, relation types, sub-tensor search). While greedy/beam search with random restarts “works,” there is no complexity analysis, pruning strategy description, or ablation on beam size/restarts.
  - The claim that any context-free AOG can be constructed via And-Or fragments should be stated precisely and proved or referenced.
  - Context definition: How contexts are formed in practice (especially for images: spatial neighborhoods? for events: surrounding actions/time windows?) is under-specified, yet central to the likelihood gain.
  - Experimental evaluation is positive but limited in baselines and diagnostics; see below.

- Empirical support: On synthetic images, the method substantially improves F and KL-divergence over [17], and on real images reduces perplexity. On events, the third variant (simultaneous learning of relations) performs best and outperforms [15,18] on the more challenging dataset. Missing pieces:
  - Baselines: No comparison to SPN structure learning or PCFG induction baselines for sequences; no fixed-structure AOG baseline with parameter learning to isolate structure-learning gains.
  - Ablations: No study of And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or fragments; no evaluation of the coherence-based scoring vs naive frequency scoring; no sensitivity to α, sa, so, beam size.
  - Robustness: No analysis of effect of ambiguity, noise in atomic detectors, or mis-specified relation sets.
  - Runtime/scaling: Claims of “minutes” on 5K samples are encouraging; more systematic scaling plots would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The unified AOG definition and motivation are clear. However:
- Some figures and equations are hard to parse (e.g., the likelihood gain expression and Figure 2 labeling). Provide numbered equations, clean notation, and a small running example working through the gain computation.
- Precisely define “context” and “relations” in And-rules, and give concrete instantiations for each domain.
- Include pseudocode in the main text or summarize key steps; currently it relies on the supplement.
- Detail smoothing used for perplexity and the CYK setup for multi-event parsing.

Originality
The idea of learning And- and Or-structure jointly via And-Or fragments and scoring them with a posterior that can be computed from sufficient statistics is novel and useful. Relating the gain to tensor/matrix coherence is also original. While grounded in known ideas (Viterbi training, MDL-like priors, model merging), the combination and generality across modalities are new.

Significance
AOGs are a powerful abstraction for compositional and reconfigurable structures in sequences, images, and events. A practical, general-purpose structure learner is valuable. If the approach scales and the assumptions hold well enough in practice, this could impact work on interpretable generative models and structured perception. The current empirical scope is modest but promising.

Questions and suggestions
- Quantify the effect of ambiguity: how often are multiple parses observed, and how does relaxing unambiguity (e.g., n-best parses) change results?
- Provide ablations: And-only/Or-only/And-Or; remove the context-coherence factor; vary α, sa, so.
- Clarify how relation types are enumerated and validated; how many were allowed in each domain?
- Compare to SPN structure learning baselines and a fixed-structure AOG with parameter learning.
- Include a complexity analysis and practical pruning strategies for candidate fragment generation.
- Report final grammar sizes and parsing accuracies to support the “compactness” claim.
- Release code/statistics if possible.

Pros
- Clear problem and impactful goal: unsupervised structure learning of AOGs across domains.
- Elegant scoring via sufficient statistics; closed-form Or-rule probabilities.
- Joint learning of composition and reconfiguration reduces data sparsity issues.
- Positive empirical results on both events and images.

Cons
- Relies on unambiguity and Viterbi likelihood; limited analysis of their impact.
- Baseline and ablation coverage is limited; no SPN/PCFG comparisons.
- Some key implementation details (contexts, relations, fragment search) are under-specified.
- Prior design (coding lengths) and sensitivity are not justified.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper presents a coherent, novel, and potentially broadly useful approach with encouraging results. Strengthening clarity and empirical analysis (especially ablations and broader baselines) would make it a solid accept.

Confidence
Medium. I am familiar with grammar induction and structured generative models, less so with recent AOG event parsing specifics.
