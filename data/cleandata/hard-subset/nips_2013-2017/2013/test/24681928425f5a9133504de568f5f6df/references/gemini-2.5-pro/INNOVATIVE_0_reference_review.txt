This paper addresses the challenging problem of unsupervised structure learning for Stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs). The authors propose a novel, bottom-up algorithm that learns both the grammar's structure (rules) and its parameters from unannotated data. Departing from prior work that often relies on fixed structures or learns compositional and reconfigurational rules in separate, potentially error-prone stages, this paper introduces the core concept of "And-Or fragments." These fragments jointly model a composition of elements (an And-rule) and the alternative choices for each element in that composition (a set of Or-rules). The algorithm starts from a trivial grammar that simply memorizes the training data and iteratively incorporates the And-Or fragment that yields the largest increase in the grammar's posterior probability, effectively balancing model likelihood with a sparsity-inducing prior. A key technical contribution is an efficient method for calculating this posterior gain using sufficient statistics, avoiding the need for expensive re-parsing at each step. The method's effectiveness and domain-agnostic nature are demonstrated through strong empirical results on learning both event grammars from video and image grammars.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its originality and the conceptual elegance of its central idea. The introduction of "And-Or fragments" as the atomic unit of learning is a significant and bold contribution to the field of grammar induction. This unified approach directly tackles a fundamental weakness of many previous structure learning methods, which struggle with the interdependency between discovering compositional patterns and grouping alternatives. By searching for both simultaneously, the proposed algorithm is inherently more robust to data scarcity and ambiguity, as evidenced by the improved performance on the more complex synthesized event dataset. This is precisely the kind of novel conceptual framework that can open up new directions for research in compositional and hierarchical modeling.

The work is technically solid and well-motivated. The formulation of the problem within a Bayesian framework that optimizes the grammar's posterior is principled. The derivation of the posterior gain, and its connection to tensor/matrix coherence measures, is a clever piece of analysis that makes the proposed greedy search computationally feasible. This moves beyond heuristic-driven structure search towards a more grounded optimization procedure.

The clarity of the paper is commendable. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, defining the AOG formalism in a general way, and illustrating the learning process (Figure 1 is very helpful). The experimental evaluation is another strength. By testing the method on two distinct and challenging domains—temporal event modeling and spatial image modeling—the authors provide compelling evidence for the generality and power of their approach. The comparisons against relevant and recent baselines (e.g., [17, 18]) are direct and show significant performance gains, particularly in the image grammar experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

The paper's weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract from the significance of its core contribution.

1.  The learning algorithm relies on a greedy or beam search to find the best And-Or fragment to add at each iteration. As with all such heuristic search methods, it is not guaranteed to find the globally optimal grammar structure. While this is an understandable and practical choice given the intractable search space, a brief discussion of potential failure modes or local optima would have strengthened the paper.

2.  The use of the Viterbi likelihood is a well-known approximation to the true marginal likelihood. The authors justify this choice by citing its empirical success and its interpretation as an "unambiguity bias." While this is a perfectly reasonable approach, it is an approximation nonetheless.

3.  The modification made for the event grammar experiments—changing the top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes at the end of learning to handle multiple independent events in a single video—feels somewhat ad-hoc. While it appears to work, it suggests the base framework may not perfectly map to all problem settings without small adjustments.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Novelty:** The concept of learning "And-Or fragments" is a fundamental and powerful new idea for grammar induction.
*   **Significant Impact:** This work provides a new, principled, and more robust way to learn compositional models from raw data. It has the potential to be highly influential for researchers in computer vision, NLP, and activity recognition who are interested in structured, interpretable models.
*   **Principled Approach:** The learning is guided by the optimization of a clear Bayesian objective, not just a collection of heuristics.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The method is shown to significantly outperform relevant prior work across two different data modalities, demonstrating its effectiveness and generality.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The search procedure is heuristic (greedy/beam search) and may not find the optimal solution.
*   The work relies on the Viterbi likelihood approximation.
*   Minor ad-hoc modifications were needed for one of the experimental domains.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The arguments for acceptance, particularly the high degree of novelty and potential for impact, far outweigh the minor methodological weaknesses. This paper is not an incremental improvement; it proposes a genuinely new and better way to think about unsupervised structure learning for this important class of models. The identified weaknesses are largely pragmatic concessions to computational tractability that are common in this research area. This work represents a significant conceptual advance and is an excellent fit for NIPS.
