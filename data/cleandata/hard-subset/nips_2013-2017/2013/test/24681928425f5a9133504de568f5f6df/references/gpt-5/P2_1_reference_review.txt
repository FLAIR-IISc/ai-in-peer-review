Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles unsupervised structure and parameter learning for stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs), a generalization of PCFGs and a close relative of decomposable SPNs, used for images, videos, and events. The key idea is to grow the grammar from a trivial initial grammar by greedily/beam-searching And-Or fragments that simultaneously introduce a new And-node (composition) and its child Or-nodes (reconfigurations). The objective is a posterior over grammars with a sparsity prior and Viterbi likelihood. The authors derive an efficient posterior-gain computation using sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor that counts covered configurations and a context matrix that counts configuration-context co-occurrences. Under an unambiguity assumption, the likelihood gain factorizes into coherence terms of these statistics; the prior gain penalizes fragment size and rewards reductions. The method is evaluated on event grammars (two activity datasets) and image grammars (synthetic animals and a real animal faces dataset), showing improvements over prior AOG structure-learning methods.

This builds on classic grammar induction approaches (e.g., Bayesian model merging, ADIOS, unsupervised PCFG learning) and recent AOG/SPN work in vision and events. The unification via And-Or fragments extends prior separate treatments of compositions vs alternatives [17,18] and connects to biclustering-based regularizers used previously by the authors in PCFG learning.

Strengths
- Clear, general formalization of context-free AOGs that is agnostic to modality, and a learning algorithm applicable to images and events.
- Conceptual novelty: searching for And-Or fragments unifies composition and reconfiguration, alleviating data sparsity and redundancy issues associated with searching And-only or Or-only fragments.
- Principled objective and efficient scoring: maximizing posterior with a sparsity prior; closed-form Or-rule probabilities; efficient posterior gain via sufficient statistics and coherence measures, avoiding repeated reparsing.
- Empirical evidence on two domains. On real images, perplexity substantially improves over [17]; on events, F-measure is competitive or better than [15,18], with grounded representations that capture co-occurrence and ordering.
- Practicality: greedy/beam search with random restarts; claimed minutes on moderate-sized datasets.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions and bias: The unambiguity assumption is central to the sufficient-statistics derivation; combined with Viterbi training, this introduces an unambiguity bias. The paper does not analyze how violations (inevitable in real AOGs) affect correctness or performance, nor provide diagnostics.
- Evaluation limitations:
  - Events: “Unsupervised” structure learning assumes known atomic actions with start/end times; this is closer to unsupervised grammar induction given gold terminals. Test sets are tiny (6 videos), so statistical significance of F-measure differences is unclear.
  - Images: Only one real dataset and one baseline [17]; missing comparisons to SPNs or other hierarchical compositional models. Perplexity smoothing details are underspecified.
  - No ablations for key choices (And-Or fragments vs And-only/Or-only, beam width, α, coherence components), nor sensitivity to noise in atomic detections.
- Clarity/organization: Some formulas and figures appear garbled in the excerpt (e.g., Figure 1), and important derivations are deferred to the supplement. The encoding-length prior requires choices of sa and so; how these are set in practice is not fully specified.
- Theoretical guarantees: No consistency, approximation, or convergence guarantees for the greedy/beam search, nor complexity bounds beyond anecdotal runtime. The search over n-gram tensors and sub-tensors could be expensive; the paper could better characterize when it scales.
- Generality claim vs experiments: The framework is presented as modality-agnostic but evaluated only on events and images; a small text experiment (e.g., unsupervised PCFG induction) would strengthen the claim and connect to cited NLP work.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The objective and fragment design are well motivated; the posterior gain factorization and optimal Or-rule probabilities are plausible, but key derivations hinge on unambiguity and are in the supplement. Claims are mostly supported, though broader comparisons and ablations are missing.
- Completeness: The work is more than a position paper, but the empirical section could be more thorough (ablation, statistical testing, more baselines).
- Honesty: The paper discusses limitations implicitly (data scarcity, ambiguity), but does not directly discuss the impact of the unambiguity assumption.

Clarity
- Generally understandable, but the exposition would benefit from:
  - Clearer statement and discussion of the unambiguity assumption and its practical implications.
  - Explicit description of how sa, so, and α are chosen; include sensitivity analysis.
  - Cleaner figures and self-contained derivations for the likelihood gain or at least more intuition in the main text.
  - Pseudocode in the main text for reproducibility.

Originality
- The And-Or fragment search and the posterior-gain computation via coherence of n-gram tensors/context matrices are novel and nontrivial contributions relative to prior AOG structure-learning and classic grammar induction methods.

Significance
- If robust, the approach could be valuable for learning compact, interpretable compositional models in vision and events. The real-image perplexity improvements are promising. However, broader empirical validation and analysis are needed to firmly establish state-of-the-art impact.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the unambiguity assumption? Can you measure ambiguity in learned grammars and correlate with performance?
- Provide ablations: And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or fragments; effect of beam width; effect of α; impact of coherence terms separately.
- Clarify how sa and so are set and report results under different settings.
- Report runtime and memory as a function of n and dataset size; include complexity discussion of sub-tensor search.
- For events, add larger test splits and confidence intervals; for images, compare to SPNs or related hierarchical models; detail smoothing used for perplexity.
- Consider adding a small NLP experiment to support modality-agnostic claims.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept to weak accept. The paper presents a principled, novel approach to unsupervised AOG structure learning with promising results and a potentially broad impact. Strengthening empirical validation, clarifying assumptions, and improving presentation would make it a solid NeurIPS contribution.
