Summary
The paper tackles unsupervised structure and parameter learning for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), focusing on a context-free subclass applicable to different modalities (text, images, events). The key idea is to jointly induce compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules) by inserting And-Or fragments: a new And-node expands into several new Or-nodes, each selecting among existing nodes. Learning starts from an initial “memorization” grammar that exactly generates the training samples; the algorithm iteratively adds fragments to maximize a posterior consisting of a sparsity prior over grammar size and a Viterbi-likelihood approximation. Assuming unambiguity, the posterior gain of a candidate fragment can be computed from sufficient statistics (an n-gram tensor over configurations and a context matrix), enabling greedy/beam search. Experiments on event grammars (two small human-activity datasets) and image grammars (synthetic sketches with known ground truth; a small real animal-face dataset) show improved F-measure/KL on synthetic images and lower perplexity than a prior AOG learner; performance on events is competitive or better than two prior ID-sequence-based methods.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on AOGs for images and events and classic grammar induction (e.g., Bayesian model merging, biclustering) and is related to structure learning for SPNs. The joint And-Or fragment construction is presented as more robust than separately searching And- or Or-fragments. The use of Viterbi likelihood echoes Viterbi training in unsupervised parsing. However, several closely related structure-learning lines (e.g., SPN structure learning by Gens & Domingos, 2013; later SPN literature) are not cited or compared, and the connection to HDP/MDL PCFG induction beyond the few citations is not deeply developed.

Strengths
- Clear high-level goal and a unified formulation of context-free AOGs across modalities.
- A practical search strategy (And-Or fragments) that attempts to mitigate data sparsity and redundancy issues of And-only or Or-only fragment search.
- Derivation (under an unambiguity assumption) of a posterior-gain estimator from sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor/context matrix) is appealing and potentially efficient.
- Empirical results on synthetic image data with known ground-truth grammar are encouraging; real-image perplexity improvements over an AOG baseline are sizable.
- On events, the approach that grounds terminals to atomic actions and learns co-occurrence and followed-by jointly performs best among the proposed variants.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/methodological soundness:
- Strong unambiguity assumption. The posterior gain derivation and efficiency hinge on grammar unambiguity, but real AOGs for images and events are typically ambiguous. There is no measurement of ambiguity in learned grammars, no fallback when ambiguity is present, and no analysis of how violations affect correctness or performance.
- Parameter learning is underspecified. And-rule probabilities are said to be “specified by energy terms on relations,” but the paper does not clearly define the parameterization, estimation procedure, or regularization for these energy terms. Only Or-rule probabilities are given simple frequency estimates; how And-rule energies are learned (or fixed) is critical to likelihood computation and generalization.
- Prior specification is vague and potentially arbitrary. The grammar-size prior uses bit-costs sa and so, but their values, rationale, and sensitivity are not explained. α is fixed to 0.5 without validation; no ablation shows sensitivity to α or encoding choices.
- Search complexity and constraints are unclear. Building n-gram tensors “for different n and relations” suggests a large search space. The algorithm uses greedy/beam search with random restarts, but there is no analysis of runtime scaling, beam sizes, or guarantees; termination and fragment-size constraints are not specified.
- Limited and nonstandard evaluation for events. The datasets are tiny (61 videos; train 55/test 6), with synthetic “subtypes” in the second set. Metrics (purity/inverse purity) are task-specific, and there is no statistical significance analysis. Baselines are limited to two ID-sequence learners; stronger baselines (HMMs/HSMMs, segmental models, neural sequence models, or recent event AOG learners) are missing.
- Generality claims not matched by evidence. The method is advertised as agnostic to domain (including text), but no text experiments are shown. For images, comparisons do not include SPNs or other probabilistic compositional models beyond one AOG baseline; for events, no comparison to modern alternatives.
- Reproducibility gaps. Important practical details (fragment generation constraints, choice of n and relation sets, smoothing for perplexity, handling of context definitions, hyperparameter settings beyond α) are deferred to supplementary or omitted.

Clarity:
- Several key derivations are deferred; the main text does not provide enough information to reproduce results. The description of relations, energy terms, and context construction needs more precision.
- Some figures/tables are hard to interpret without more detail (e.g., Figure 2 matrices/tensors; encodings in the prior).

Originality:
- The And-Or fragment idea is a natural combination, and the sufficient-statistics posterior-gain derivation is interesting, but the overall approach is relatively close in spirit to prior MDL/Bayesian grammar induction and AOG learning. Novelty is moderate.

Significance:
- Potentially useful for multi-modal grammar induction, but the current empirical support is limited in scale, breadth, and baselines. Without stronger validation and ablations, it is hard to assess robustness and impact.

Questions for the authors
1. How is unambiguity enforced or checked during learning? What happens when the grammar becomes ambiguous? Can you quantify ambiguity and its effect on the posterior-gain approximation and performance?
2. Precisely define and learn the And-rule energy terms. Are they fixed templates, MLE/regularized estimates, or learned jointly with structure? How are continuous relation parameters (e.g., spatial/temporal) handled?
3. How are sa and so chosen? Provide sensitivity analyses for α, sa, so, and beam size.
4. What constraints are placed on n and relation types in fragment search? What is the computational complexity and runtime as a function of data size and grammar size?
5. Provide ablations: (a) And-Or vs And-only vs Or-only fragments, (b) with/without context-matrix factor, (c) Viterbi vs full likelihood/EM.
6. For events, compare to stronger baselines (HMM/HSMM/CRF/segmental models, recent AOG event learners) and report statistical significance.
7. For images, compare to SPN structure-learning methods (e.g., Gens & Domingos, ICML’13) and related compositional models; detail smoothing used for perplexity.

Missing/under-cited related work (suggested): SPN structure learning (Gens & Domingos, ICML 2013; Peharz et al.); recent unsupervised PCFG/HDP-PCFG/adaptor grammars for structure induction; modern event parsing/segmentation baselines.

Pros
- Clear motivation for joint induction of composition and reconfiguration.
- Efficient sufficient-statistics-based scoring under stated assumptions.
- Encouraging results on synthetic images and improved perplexity on a real small dataset.
- Potentially useful framework for multi-modal grammar induction.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on untested unambiguity assumption; unclear And-rule parameter learning.
- Prior and hyperparameters insufficiently justified; no sensitivity studies.
- Limited datasets and baselines; small-scale event evaluation with custom metrics.
- Missing ablations and comparisons to stronger, more established methods.
- Reproducibility and clarity gaps in algorithmic details.

Recommendation: Reject
The paper presents interesting ideas, but the methodological assumptions (unambiguity), incomplete parameter-learning specification, limited empirical validation, and missing ablations/comparisons fall short of NeurIPS standards. With more rigorous experiments, clearer parameter learning, sensitivity analyses, and broader comparisons, the work could be much stronger.
