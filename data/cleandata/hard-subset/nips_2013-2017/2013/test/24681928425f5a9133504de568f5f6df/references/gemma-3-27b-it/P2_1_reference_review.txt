## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to unsupervised learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs). Unlike existing methods that either fix the grammar structure or learn it heuristically, the authors propose an iterative algorithm that jointly learns both the structure *and* the parameters of a SAG by inserting new "And-Or fragments." These fragments represent both compositional and reconfiguration relationships simultaneously, aiming for efficiency and robustness, especially in data-scarce scenarios. The approach is formalized with a probabilistic framework, optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar.  Experiments on event and image grammars demonstrate competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The joint learning of grammar structure and parameters is a significant contribution. The introduction of "And-Or fragments" is a clever idea to address the limitations of independently learning compositions and reconfigurations.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The paper provides a clear probabilistic formulation, explicitly optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar. The use of sufficient statistics for efficient computation is a strong point.
* **Generality:** The authors successfully present a generalized formalization of SAGs applicable to various data types (text, images, events), expanding the potential impact of their work.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both event and image grammars are encouraging, demonstrating that the proposed approach achieves competitive or better performance than existing methods, particularly on the more complex event grammar dataset.
* **Clarity (Generally):** The paper is generally well-written and the core ideas are explained clearly. The illustrations (Figures 1 & 2) are particularly helpful in understanding the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Implementation:** While the theoretical framework is clear, the practical implementation seems complex, with many moving parts (n-gram tensors, context matrices, efficient computation of posterior gains). The reliance on greedy/beam search introduces potential sub-optimality.
* **Scalability Concerns:**  The computational cost of learning and using the grammar, especially with larger datasets or more complex structures, isn't thoroughly addressed. While they claim efficiency, a more detailed discussion of the algorithm's time and space complexity would be beneficial. The description of running time (“a few minutes on a desktop”) is vague and needs clarification with dataset sizes.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with existing methods is somewhat limited. Only two previous approaches were compared, and on the image dataset, one was from 2013. A more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art techniques would strengthen the claims.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the choice of the hyperparameter α, though the paper does not discuss a sensitivity analysis.
* **Overly Optimistic Statements:** Some statements regarding the benefits of the approach (e.g., "more efficient and robust" without quantifying these improvements) feel overly strong.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear mathematical formulation.  The empirical results support the claims, although further analysis would strengthen the conclusions. The treatment of the posterior probability and the derivation of sufficient statistics seem well-justified.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, but the explanations of the likelihood gain computation and the algorithm’s practical details could be expanded. More details on the implementation would increase reproducibility.
* **Originality:** The core idea of jointly learning structure and parameters with And-Or fragments is novel and represents a significant advancement over existing techniques. The generalized formulation of SAGs is also a valuable contribution.
* **Significance:** The results are promising, and the potential applications of the learned grammars in various domains (natural language processing, computer vision, event recognition) are significant. However, the broader impact will depend on the scalability and practical usability of the approach.




**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Novel and technically sound approach to unsupervised grammar learning.
* Clear probabilistic framework with efficient computation of posterior gain.
* Competitive empirical results on event and image grammars.
* Potential for broad applicability across different data modalities.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Implementation complexity and potential scalability issues.
* Limited comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
* Lack of sensitivity analysis for hyperparameters.
* Overly optimistic claims without sufficient quantification.


**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. The novelty of the approach, its strong theoretical foundation, and the encouraging empirical results make it a worthwhile addition to NIPS. However, the authors should address the weaknesses related to scalability, comparison with recent work, and hyperparameter sensitivity in a revision.

**Rating:**  **Accept with Minor Revisions** (I lean towards acceptance, but addressing the minor revisions will significantly improve the paper's impact.)
