

# Peer Review: "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

## Summary

This paper proposes an unsupervised learning approach for stochastic And-Or grammars, which are powerful models for representing compositional structures in data (text, images, events). The authors present a unified formalization of these grammars and introduce a novel structure learning algorithm that jointly optimizes grammar structure and parameters by iteratively adding "And-Or fragments" to an initially trivial grammar. This approach differs from prior work that learns compositions and reconfigurations separately (as And-fragments and Or-fragments), which the authors argue leads to less efficient and robust learning. The paper builds upon previous work on stochastic grammars [1, 2, 3, 7, 17, 18] while extending the approach to learn both structure and parameters in a unified manner.

## Strengths

**Quality (Excellent):** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness with a clear theoretical foundation. The authors formalize stochastic And-Or grammars in a data-agnostic way (Section 2), provide a rigorous derivation of the posterior probability optimization (Section 3.3), and introduce sufficient statistics to efficiently compute likelihood gains. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with results on both synthetic and real datasets (Section 4). The comparison against prior work [17, 18] shows consistent improvements in event grammar learning (Table 2) and image grammar learning (Table 3), with statistically significant gains in perplexity. The authors also thoughtfully address limitations, such as the need to modify their algorithm to handle multiple unrelated events per video.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized and generally clearly written. The theoretical concepts (And-Or grammars, fragments) are effectively illustrated with examples (Figures 1-2). The algorithm description is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility, though the supplementary material would be helpful for full implementation details. The experimental setup is clearly described, including the two datasets for event grammars and the synthetic and real image datasets. One minor weakness is that the connection between the "context matrix" and the actual grammar learning procedure could be made clearer.

**Originality (Strong):** The unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments represents a meaningful advance over prior work. Previous approaches [14, 15, 16, 17, 18] either learn And and Or structures separately (which the authors correctly identify as problematic) or rely on heuristics. The paper clearly articulates how their approach avoids the limitations of prior work, particularly in data-scarce scenarios. The formalization of the posterior probability optimization and the use of sufficient statistics for efficient computation are novel contributions that distinguish this work from related NIPS papers on grammar learning.

**Significance (Strong):** The approach has significant potential for applications in multiple domains where compositional structure learning is important (computer vision, natural language processing, activity recognition). The paper demonstrates clear improvements over prior methods in both event and image grammars, with the third method (directly grounding to atomic actions) showing the most promise. The ability to learn grammars without manual specification of rules is a substantial practical advantage. The work advances the state of the art in grammar learning by addressing a key limitation of previous approaches.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity (Minor):** The connection between the context matrix (Figure 2c) and the actual grammar learning procedure is somewhat opaque. A concrete example of how a specific context matrix leads to a specific grammar fragment would improve understanding.

**Quality (Minor):** The paper claims "the algorithm runs reasonably fast: our prototype implementation can finish running within a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples." However, the paper doesn't provide any runtime analysis or comparison to prior methods. For a method claiming to be efficient, it would be valuable to see how its runtime scales with problem size compared to prior work.

**Originality (Minor):** While the unified And-Or fragment approach is novel, the paper could better distinguish itself from related work on sum-product networks [10] and other compositional models. The connection to sum-product networks is mentioned in Section 1 but could be elaborated.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel approach to grammar structure learning that unifies composition and reconfiguration
- Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous posterior probability optimization
- Comprehensive experimental validation on multiple data types (events, images)
- Demonstrated improvements over prior work in both synthetic and real-world settings
- Clear, well-organized presentation of the technical material

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and runtime analysis
- Some connections to related work (sum-product networks) could be strengthened
- The paper doesn't address whether their approach could be extended to non-context-free grammars (a limitation of their formalization)
- The experiments don't include comparison to more recent grammar learning approaches beyond [17, 18]

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of compositional structure learning. The unified approach to learning grammar structure and parameters through And-Or fragments represents a meaningful advance over prior work, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and addresses a problem of significant importance to multiple communities.

The minor weaknesses noted (primarily in clarity and computational analysis) are not substantial enough to outweigh the paper's strong contributions. I recommend acceptance.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a brief runtime analysis showing how the algorithm scales with dataset size compared to prior methods.
2. Expand the discussion of connections to sum-product networks [10], which share conceptual similarities.
3. Include a concrete example illustrating how a specific context matrix leads to the addition of a grammar fragment.
4. Consider adding a brief discussion of potential extensions to non-context-free grammars.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning and is well-suited for publication at NIPS.
