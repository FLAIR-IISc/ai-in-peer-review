This paper tackles the challenging problem of unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs), a powerful formalism for compositional modeling applicable to diverse data types. The core idea—learning both structure *and* parameters of the grammar simultaneously, and doing so with a unified approach to composition and reconfiguration via "And-Or fragments"—is genuinely novel and a strong contribution. While the technical details are dense, the authors present a compelling argument for their method and demonstrate promising results on event and image grammars. This work represents a significant step towards more flexible and automated compositional modeling, going beyond simply optimizing parameters of a pre-defined structure.

The strength of this paper lies in its ambitious goal and the elegant approach to tackling it. The introduction of And-Or fragments is clever, addressing a key limitation of previous structure learning methods. The formalization presented is commendable, providing a framework applicable to various data modalities. The derivation of likelihood gains based on sufficient statistics demonstrates a strong understanding of probabilistic modeling and offers a computationally efficient learning procedure. The experimental results, while not overwhelmingly superior to existing methods, *are* competitive and, importantly, demonstrate the viability of the proposed approach—particularly on the more challenging synthetic event data.

However, the paper is not without weaknesses. The presentation is quite dense, and requires significant effort from the reader to fully grasp the intricacies of the algorithm and derivations. While the authors aim for generality, the experimental evaluation feels somewhat fragmented. The image grammar experiments use a dataset already established in prior work (SZ [17]), which is good, but the benefits of the proposed method aren’t always clearly articulated *relative* to the specific advantages of that prior work. The ablation study comparing the three event grammar learning methods (ID sequences vs. direct application) is helpful, but could be more extensive. The choice of α = 0.5 feels arbitrary; a sensitivity analysis would strengthen the results. Additionally, the discussion on the prior probability (sparsity penalty) feels brief – a more thorough exploration of its impact would be valuable. Furthermore, while the theoretical link to n-gram coherence is interesting, the practical implications of this connection are not fully elaborated. The paper would benefit from visualizing the learned grammars (even snippets) to provide a more intuitive understanding of the structures being discovered.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The unified approach to structure and parameter learning using And-Or fragments is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation and derivations are well-developed.
*   **Potential Impact:** Successful unsupervised grammar learning has broad implications for representation learning and compositional modeling.
*   **Demonstrated Viability:** The experiments demonstrate that the approach *works* and is competitive with existing methods.
*   **Generality:** The formalism is applicable to diverse data types, suggesting broad applicability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is dense and requires significant effort to understand.
*   **Limited Ablation:** The experimental evaluation, while solid, could be more comprehensive.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of hyperparameters (e.g., α) is not thoroughly justified.
*   **Visualization:** Lack of visualization of learned grammars hinders intuitive understanding.



**Overall:** Despite the density and some minor shortcomings in the experimental evaluation, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the approach, its theoretical soundness, and the potential for impact outweigh the weaknesses. This is a bold and ambitious piece of work that pushes the boundaries of unsupervised compositional modeling. I recommend **acceptance**. While improvements in clarity and a more thorough experimental analysis would enhance the paper, the core ideas are strong enough to warrant inclusion in NIPS. The authors have introduced a valuable new perspective on structure learning and laid a foundation for future research in this area.
