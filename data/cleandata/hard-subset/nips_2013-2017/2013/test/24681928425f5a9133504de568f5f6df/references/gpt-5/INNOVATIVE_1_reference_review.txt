Summary
This paper tackles unsupervised structure learning for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), proposing a unified, modality-agnostic formulation and a learning algorithm that jointly induces composition (And) and reconfiguration (Or) through the introduction of And-Or fragments. The learning objective maximizes a sparsity-regularized posterior under Viterbi likelihood, and the authors derive efficient posterior-gain computations via sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor over fragment configurations and a context matrix over fragment-context co-occurrences. The method incrementally inserts fragments using greedy/beam search and updates parses accordingly. Experiments on event parsing and image grammars show competitive or superior results to prior AOG structure-learning approaches.

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself against (i) NLP grammar induction methods (e.g., Bayesian model merging, ADIOS, biclustering), (ii) And-Or grammar learning in vision and events, and (iii) SPNs. The key novelty is learning And and Or jointly via And-Or fragments with an explicit posterior-gain criterion and coherent tensor/matrix statistics, in contrast to prior approaches that fix structure, treat And/Or separately, or rely on heuristics. The work is also related to structure learning in SPNs but differs in the fragment design and posterior analysis.

Strengths
- Novel, unifying learning primitive. Inducing And-Or fragments that simultaneously cover multiple compositions and alternative configurations is an elegant idea that addresses data sparsity, redundancy, and search instability typical in separate And/Or induction.
- Principled objective with efficient surrogate. The posterior-gain derivation under Viterbi likelihood and unambiguity yields a computable criterion based on n-gram tensor and context-matrix coherence. This ties the method to established biclustering/coherence notions and offers intuition about when a fragment is beneficial.
- Cross-domain applicability. The formulation is agnostic to terminal types and relation families, demonstrated on both event sequences (temporal “followed-by” and “co-occurs”) and images (spatial relations). This generality is appealing and rare in grammar induction.
- Empirical improvements. On both synthetic and real datasets, the method outperforms prior AOG learning [Si & Zhu 2013; Si et al. 2011], with clear perplexity gains on real images and better F-measures on the more challenging event dataset variant.
- Good engineering choices. Using Viterbi likelihood/unambiguity bias to stabilize unsupervised induction, a sparsity prior on grammar size, and greedy/beam search with random restarts are sensible and effective in practice.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumption of unambiguity. The posterior-gain computation assumes grammar unambiguity; many real grammars are inherently ambiguous. Please discuss the impact if the learned grammar becomes ambiguous, and whether the gain estimate remains reliable. A small experiment toggling Viterbi vs. full likelihood (on a tiny dataset) or reporting ambiguity rates would help.
- Scalability and search complexity. Searching sub-tensors for high-arity And-rules may be combinatorial. What are practical limits on n (child count) and on the number/types of relations? Any complexity bounds or empirical scaling (e.g., time vs. n, terminals, corpus size)? The “few minutes for 5k samples with >10 terminals” is encouraging but too sparse for judging scalability.
- Prior and coding costs. The prior gain depends on sa and so (bits per RHS element in And/Or rules). How are these chosen in practice? Are results sensitive to α, sa, so? Please provide settings used across experiments and a sensitivity plot or at least commentary.
- Fragment expressivity and guarantee. The claim that any context-free AOG can be constructed by successive And-Or fragments is important; a proof sketch in the main paper (not only supplementary) would bolster confidence. Also clarify whether relations inside And-rules are learned or fixed per experiment; in practice they seem predefined (temporal/spatial types), which limits “agnosticism.”
- Comparisons and ablations. It would strengthen the case to:
  - Compare to SPN structure learning baselines where applicable, given the conceptual proximity.
  - Provide an ablation that attempts separate And-then-Or induction to quantify the benefit of joint fragments.
  - Report beam width and its effect; include variance/error bars across random restarts.
- Evaluation details. For images, perplexity depends on smoothing; please specify the smoothing scheme and ensure fair comparisons. For events, F-measure depends on overlap thresholds; include exact evaluation protocol. Some figures are difficult to parse; adding explicit parse examples and learned fragments would improve clarity.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a clear optimization target and reasonable approximations. The posterior-gain derivation is interesting, though reliant on unambiguity and with some details deferred to the supplement. Experiments are positive but on modest-scale datasets; more thorough baselines/ablations would improve rigor.

Clarity
Generally well-organized. The And-Or fragment, RD, n-gram tensor, and context matrix concepts are valuable but could benefit from a tighter, self-contained derivation and a running toy example with numbers. Define grammar size ‖G‖ precisely. Improve figure legibility.

Originality
High. Joint And-Or fragment induction with an explicit, efficiently computable posterior-gain criterion and cross-modal applicability is a fresh contribution relative to prior AOG/SPN and PCFG induction works.

Significance
Promising direction for unsupervised structure learning of compositional, reconfigurable models across modalities. If scaled and adopted, this could influence grammar-based perception and event understanding.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and elegant And-Or fragment learning
- Principled objective with efficient sufficient statistics
- Cross-domain demonstrations with improved results
- Clear potential to spark follow-up work

Cons against acceptance
- Reliance on unambiguity and limited analysis of its effect
- Limited scalability analysis and ablations
- Some evaluation details and hyperparameters underspecified

Overall recommendation
Accept. The central idea is novel and impactful, and the paper presents a coherent framework with encouraging empirical evidence. While some validations are incomplete and details could be expanded, the contribution opens a valuable line of research in unsupervised grammar induction.

Moderate-high.
