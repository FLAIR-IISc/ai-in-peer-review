

# Peer Review: "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and proposes an unsupervised learning approach that simultaneously learns grammar structure and parameters. The authors address a significant gap in previous work by introducing "And-Or fragments" that jointly learn compositions (via And-nodes) and reconfigurations (via Or-nodes) rather than treating them separately as in prior approaches [14-18]. This is a notable contribution to the field of structured probabilistic modeling, extending work on stochastic grammars for natural language [8,9,11,12] and image modeling [1,3,10,13,17].

## Strengths

The paper's main strengths lie in its theoretical clarity and practical contributions. The authors provide a well-motivated formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type (text, images, events), making the approach broadly applicable. The key innovation—learning And-Or fragments instead of separate And- and Or-fragments—effectively addresses the data scarcity problem inherent in previous approaches. The theoretical derivation of likelihood gain using sufficient statistics (context matrices and n-gram tensors) is elegant and well-justified, enabling efficient computation that avoids re-parsing all training data at each iteration.

The experimental evaluation demonstrates the approach's effectiveness across two distinct domains (event recognition and image parsing), with consistent improvements over prior methods [17,18]. The results on the more challenging synthetic dataset (where actions are divided into subtypes) particularly highlight the robustness of the proposed method. The computational efficiency (processing 5000 samples in minutes) is impressive for an unsupervised structure learning approach.

## Weaknesses

While the paper makes a compelling case for its approach, several concerns merit consideration:

1. **The unambiguity assumption:** The authors rely on Viterbi likelihood and assume grammar unambiguity to simplify the likelihood calculation. This is a significant simplification, as real-world grammars often contain ambiguities. The paper doesn't adequately address how this assumption affects performance on real-world data or provide empirical evidence of the degree of ambiguity in their learned grammars.

2. **Comparison with related work:** The paper compares with [17,18] but overlooks recent advances in grammar learning, particularly those related to sum-product networks [10] and neural grammar models [25]. A more comprehensive comparison with methods that have learned grammars from unstructured data would strengthen the claims about the approach's novelty and superiority.

3. **Computational complexity:** While the paper mentions the algorithm runs quickly, it lacks a detailed complexity analysis. The time complexity with respect to grammar size and data volume isn't quantified, making it difficult to assess scalability to larger problems.

4. **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The paper uses a fixed α=0.5 for all experiments, but doesn't explore how sensitive the results are to this choice. The optimal value of α might vary across domains, and the authors don't provide guidance on hyperparameter selection.

5. **Theoretical limitations:** The paper claims that "using And-Or fragments can avoid or alleviate the problems associated with And-fragments and Or-fragments," but doesn't provide theoretical guarantees about the completeness or convergence properties of the learning procedure.

## Significance and Originality

The paper makes a strong contribution to the literature on unsupervised structure learning. The unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations is both novel and practically valuable. The authors successfully demonstrate the approach's versatility across different data types (images and event sequences), which is a significant strength. The work clearly advances the state of the art over [17,18] and provides a more principled approach than heuristic-based methods [14-16].

## Recommendation

This paper presents a well-motivated, theoretically sound approach to unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars. The unified learning of And-Or fragments represents a meaningful advance over prior work, and the empirical results are convincing across multiple domains. The main concerns are the unambiguity assumption and limited comparison with recent grammar learning methods, but these don't undermine the paper's core contribution.

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with the following suggestions for the authors' consideration:

1. Discuss the impact of the unambiguity assumption in more detail, including potential workarounds for ambiguous grammars.
2. Expand the comparison to include more recent grammar learning approaches.
3. Provide a more detailed complexity analysis and discuss hyperparameter sensitivity.

The paper addresses an important problem in structured representation learning and offers a robust, efficient solution with clear empirical validation. It represents a meaningful contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling and would be valuable to the NeurIPS community.

**Overall Assessment:** Strong contribution to the field with clear theoretical and empirical advances. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's substantial contributions. I recommend acceptance.
