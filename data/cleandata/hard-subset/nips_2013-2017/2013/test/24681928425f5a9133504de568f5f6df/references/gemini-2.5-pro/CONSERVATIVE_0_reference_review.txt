### Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

**Summary**

This paper presents an unsupervised method for learning the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars. The approach is framed as a general one, applicable to various data types like images and event sequences. The core of the method is an iterative, bottom-up algorithm that starts with a trivial grammar (where each training sample is a separate production rule) and progressively refines it. In each step, the algorithm searches for and incorporates an "And-Or fragment" — a new And-node that decomposes into a set of Or-nodes, which in turn generate configurations of existing symbols. The selection of which fragment to add is guided by a greedy search to maximize the posterior probability of the grammar, which is a product of the Viterbi likelihood of the data and a complexity-penalizing prior on the grammar size. The authors claim this unified learning of compositional (And-rules) and reconfigurational (Or-rules) aspects is more robust and efficient than prior work. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated by learning event grammars from video and image grammars from synthetic and real datasets, where it is shown to outperform previous grammar induction techniques.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled Objective Function:** The work is grounded in a clear Bayesian objective: maximizing the posterior probability `P(G|X)`. This provides a methodologically sound foundation for the learning problem, balancing model fit (likelihood) with model complexity (prior), which is crucial for avoiding overfitting in grammar induction.
2.  **Efficient Optimization Heuristic:** The paper derives an efficient way to calculate the gain in posterior probability when adding a new And-Or fragment. By formulating the gain in terms of sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices), the algorithm avoids re-parsing the entire dataset at each step of the search, which makes the greedy search procedure computationally feasible. This is a solid technical contribution.
3.  **Unified Fragment Search:** The proposed "And-Or fragment" is an interesting structural primitive for the search process. The motivation for jointly discovering compositional and alternative structures to overcome data sparsity and ambiguity is plausible, even if it is a heuristic.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Methodological Approximations:** The paper's foundation in maximizing `P(G|X)` is weakened by the use of Viterbi likelihood as a proxy for the true marginal likelihood `P(X|G)`. While the authors cite work suggesting empirical benefits of this approximation, it remains a significant deviation from the established expectation-maximization framework (e.g., the Inside-Outside algorithm) typically used for principled parameter learning in stochastic grammars. This choice simplifies the mathematics but raises questions about the algorithm's convergence properties and the quality of the learned probability distributions. The justification provided is brief and relies on interpreting it as an "unambiguity bias," which warrants a more thorough theoretical or empirical investigation.

2.  **Insufficient Evaluation and Baselines:** The experimental evaluation is the most significant weakness of this paper. While the method is presented as a general learning framework, its performance is only compared against a narrow and dated set of closely related grammar-based models.
    *   For the **event grammar** task, the baselines ([15], [18]) are from 2005 and 2011. The field of activity recognition has advanced substantially, with recurrent neural networks, temporal convolutional networks, and transformers now representing the state of the art. To demonstrate the significance of this work, it is essential to compare it against these established, high-performing models. Without such a comparison, it is impossible to know if this grammar-based approach is a competitive solution for the task or merely an improvement within an outdated paradigm.
    *   For the **image grammar** task, the comparison is primarily against a single, closely related work ([17]). While the improvement in perplexity is substantial, perplexity is an intrinsic measure of model fit and does not necessarily correlate with performance on downstream tasks or the quality of generated samples. The current state of the art in generative image modeling is dominated by GANs, VAEs, and diffusion models. A convincing case for a new generative model at NIPS requires either demonstrating competitive sample quality or highlighting a unique advantage (e.g., superior interpretability, data efficiency, compositional generalization) that these established methods lack. The paper does neither. No generated samples are shown, nor is the interpretability of the learned grammars explored in depth.

3.  **Limited Scope and Scalability:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small-scale problems with discrete, low-dimensional terminal symbols (action IDs, quantized image patches). The claim that the framework is "agnostic to the types of atomic patterns" is only true in a formal sense. The practical feasibility of constructing and searching through n-gram tensors for high-dimensional or continuous data is not addressed or demonstrated. The scalability of the greedy search, especially as the grammar and the vocabulary of symbols grow, remains an open question.

4.  **Incremental Novelty:** The core algorithmic idea—an iterative, greedy search that merges common substructures to build a grammar—is a well-established heuristic in the field of grammar induction (e.g., Stolcke & Omohundro, 1994). The main novel contribution is the specific structure of the "And-Or fragment." While this is a reasonable refinement, it feels more like an incremental improvement upon existing greedy search frameworks rather than a fundamentally new approach to structure learning.

**Conclusion**

This paper tackles the important problem of unsupervised grammar induction with a well-formulated objective and an efficient search algorithm. The idea of using "And-Or fragments" to unify the search for different rule types is a notable contribution.

However, the work is let down by its evaluation. By only comparing to other grammar-induction methods and failing to benchmark against modern, established methods in the respective application domains (activity recognition, generative image modeling), the paper does not provide sufficient evidence of its significance or practical utility. The reliance on a Viterbi approximation also represents a methodological compromise that should be more thoroughly justified. While the technical work is sound within its own context, its failure to engage with the broader state of the art in relevant fields makes it difficult to endorse for a top-tier conference like NIPS. I would be more positive about this submission if the authors provided a compelling comparison to contemporary, non-grammar-based baselines or demonstrated a unique capability of their approach (e.g., generating novel, structured samples of high quality) that is beyond the reach of established models.
