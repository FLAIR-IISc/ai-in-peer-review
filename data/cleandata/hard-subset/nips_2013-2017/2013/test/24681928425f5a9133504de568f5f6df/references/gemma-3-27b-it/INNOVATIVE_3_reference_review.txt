This paper tackles the challenging problem of unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, extending prior work in both natural language processing and vision. The core idea – learning both the grammar *and* its parameters simultaneously, and doing so with a unified approach to composition and reconfiguration via "And-Or fragments" – is genuinely novel and quite bold. This is not merely an incremental improvement; it's a fundamentally different approach to grammar induction. The theoretical grounding, particularly the use of sufficient statistics and the connection to tensor coherence, is strong, and the empirical evaluation, while not extensive, demonstrates promising results on both event and image data.

The strength of this work lies in its ambition and the elegance of its theoretical formulation. Learning grammar structure is notoriously difficult, and the proposed method addresses key limitations of prior approaches – namely, the exponential growth of possible rules and the difficulty of manually specifying compact, expressive grammars. The And-Or fragment concept is clever; by inducing both compositional *and* reconfiguration aspects in a single step, the authors sidestep potential error propagation issues inherent in separate learning phases. The mathematical formulation linking the likelihood gain to tensor/matrix coherence measures provides a solid foundation for efficient learning.

However, the paper isn’t without weaknesses. While the theoretical justification is sound, the experimental validation feels somewhat limited. The comparisons to existing methods, while showing competitive or superior performance, aren’t particularly decisive. The event grammar experiments rely on a somewhat artificial "subtype" modification to create a more challenging dataset, and the benefit of this modification isn't fully elucidated. The image grammar results are more convincing but could be strengthened with a more detailed qualitative analysis of the learned grammars – visualizing the learned structures would provide valuable insights. The discussion of hyperparameter selection (specifically α) is brief. A sensitivity analysis to this parameter could be informative.  The connection to sum-product networks, mentioned in the introduction, isn't fully explored; a more detailed discussion of this relationship would be beneficial. Also, while the authors mention avoiding zero probabilities during perplexity calculations, the specific smoothing technique isn't detailed, which is a minor point.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of unified And-Or fragment learning is genuinely novel and addresses a fundamental challenge in unsupervised grammar induction.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation, connecting the learning process to well-established concepts like sufficient statistics and tensor coherence.
*   **Potential Impact:** If successful, this approach could have a significant impact on various fields, including natural language processing, computer vision, and robotics, by enabling more flexible and robust modeling of compositional data.
*   **Boldness:** The authors tackle a difficult problem with an ambitious solution, which is something NIPS should encourage.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation, while promising, could be more comprehensive and convincing.
*   **Lack of Detailed Qualitative Analysis:** Visualizing the learned grammars (especially for images) would provide valuable insights into their quality and interpretability.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of key hyperparameters (e.g., α) isn't fully explored.




**Overall:**

Despite the limitations in experimental validation, the novelty and theoretical strength of this work are compelling. The authors present a truly innovative approach to unsupervised structure learning that has the potential to significantly advance the field. I believe the paper warrants acceptance. The potential impact of this work justifies overlooking the somewhat incomplete empirical validation. The reviewers should encourage the authors to expand the experimental evaluation and provide more detailed qualitative analyses in future work, but the core contribution is strong enough for acceptance in its current form.
