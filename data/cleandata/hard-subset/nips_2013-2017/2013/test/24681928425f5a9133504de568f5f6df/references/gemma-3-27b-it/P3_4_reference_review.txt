## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning, specifically within the framework of stochastic And-Or grammars. The authors address a significant challenge – learning both the structure *and* parameters of these grammars from unannotated data – and propose a novel approach leveraging And-Or fragments for efficient and robust learning. The theoretical grounding is strong, and the experimental results demonstrate promising performance on both event and image grammars. While the presentation could be streamlined in places, the paper is generally clear and well-motivated, warranting a strong positive recommendation.

**1. Summary & Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of learning stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs) without supervision. SAGs are powerful compositional models used in diverse areas like NLP, computer vision, and event recognition.  Traditionally, learning SAGs involves either manually specifying the grammar structure or learning parameters within a *fixed* structure. The authors argue this fixed-structure approach is limiting, especially with complex data where defining a complete, yet tractable, grammar is difficult. 

Their approach builds on prior work in structure learning for natural language grammars (e.g., Stolcke & Omohundro, 1994) and extends recent attempts at structure learning for SAGs in images and events (Si et al., 2013, Pei et al., 2011). A key innovation is the introduction of “And-Or fragments,” a unified approach to learning both compositional and reconfiguration aspects of the grammar, contrasting with previous methods that treat these separately. They further improve upon existing methods by explicitly optimizing the posterior probability of both structure and parameters, utilizing sufficient statistics for efficiency.  The work also nicely generalizes the SAG framework to be agnostic to the data type (images, events, text), providing a unified formalization.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novel And-Or Fragment Approach:** This is the core strength of the paper. Unifying the learning of compositions and reconfigurations is a clever idea that leads to a more efficient and robust learning process, particularly in data-scarce scenarios.
* **Principled Optimization:**  Explicitly optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar (structure *and* parameters) is a significant improvement over heuristic-based approaches. The use of sufficient statistics for efficient computation is a crucial practical contribution.
* **Unified Framework:** The generalization of the SAG framework to different data types enhances the versatility and broad applicability of the proposed method.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on both event and image grammars demonstrate competitive or superior performance compared to existing approaches. The use of both synthetic and real datasets strengthens the evaluation. The experiments are well-designed and the results are clearly presented.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The formalization of SAGs and the derivation of the likelihood gain formulation are well-presented and rigorous. 
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches and motivates the need for a method that learns both structure and parameters.



**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Presentation and Readability:** While generally clear, the paper can be dense in certain sections (particularly Section 3). The flow of the argumentation could be improved with more concise phrasing and clearer transitions between ideas.  Some figures (e.g., Fig 1) are somewhat cluttered.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions α, but doesn't offer much guidance on how to choose this parameter.  A brief discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity and a potential strategy for selecting optimal values would be beneficial.
* **Scalability:** While the authors claim reasonable runtime, the experiments are limited in scale.  Addressing the scalability of the method to larger datasets and more complex grammars would be a valuable extension. The discussion of computational complexity could be more detailed.
* **Lack of Comparison to Strong Baselines:** While the comparison to Si et al. (2013) and Pei et al. (2011) is useful, more recent and sophisticated unsupervised grammar learning methods (perhaps employing deep learning techniques) would provide a more rigorous benchmark.  
* **Viterbi Likelihood Assumption:** The reliance on Viterbi likelihood introduces a bias towards unambiguous parses. A discussion of the potential impact of this bias and how it might be mitigated could be insightful.




**4. Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments *for* acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated approach to unsupervised structure learning.
* The theoretical contributions are significant, providing a principled framework for learning both structure and parameters.
* The experimental results are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The work has the potential to impact various fields where compositional modeling is important.

**Arguments *against* acceptance:**

* The presentation could be improved for better readability.
* The scalability of the method remains an open question.
* The comparison to stronger baselines is limited.
* The Viterbi likelihood assumption may introduce a bias.

**Overall, the arguments in favor of acceptance significantly outweigh the concerns.**




**5. Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.**  This is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field.  The authors have addressed a challenging problem with a novel and well-grounded approach.  Minor improvements to the presentation and a more thorough scalability analysis could further strengthen the work.
