

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

## Summary
This paper proposes an unsupervised approach for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars, which are powerful models for representing compositional structures in various domains (text, images, events). The authors present a unified formalization of these grammars that is agnostic to the data type, and introduce a learning algorithm that iteratively adds "And-Or fragments" to optimize the grammar's posterior probability. This approach differs from prior work by learning compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules) in a unified manner rather than separately, which the authors claim makes the method more robust to data scarcity. The paper evaluates the approach on event grammar learning (from human activity videos) and image grammar learning (from synthetic and real animal face datasets), showing competitive or superior performance compared to prior methods.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness and Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by formalizing the posterior gain calculation for grammar structure learning using sufficient statistics, which enables efficient greedy search. The derivation of likelihood gain as a product of tensor/matrix coherence measures is elegant and well-justified.

2. **Unified Learning Approach**: The key innovation of learning And-Or fragments (unifying composition and reconfiguration) addresses a genuine limitation in prior work that learned these components separately. The authors convincingly argue that this unification avoids problems like data scarcity for composition learning and grammar redundancy.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The paper provides thorough empirical evaluation on two distinct domains (event sequences and images), with both synthetic and real data. The results demonstrate consistent improvements over previous approaches, particularly on the more challenging second dataset in the event grammar experiments.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The formal definition of stochastic And-Or grammars is precise, and the algorithmic description is thorough with helpful illustrations (Figures 1-3). The supplementary material appears to provide adequate technical details.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: While the paper cites relevant prior work, the comparison with image grammar learning (Table 3) only includes one prior method [17]. The field has seen significant advances since 2013 (e.g., sum-product networks and other deep probabilistic models), and a more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

2. **Ambiguity in Contribution Attribution**: The paper doesn't clearly quantify how much of the performance gain comes from the unified And-Or fragment approach versus other aspects of the method (e.g., the sparsity prior or Viterbi likelihood approximation). Ablation studies would help clarify this.

3. **Computational Complexity**: The paper states the algorithm runs "within a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples," but doesn't discuss how the computational complexity scales with data size or grammar complexity. For larger datasets or more complex grammars, this could be a significant limitation.

4. **Evaluation Metrics**: The metrics used for event grammar evaluation (purity, inverse purity, F-measure) are not standard in the field. While the authors provide definitions, it would be helpful to compare with standard metrics like precision/recall or F1-score to better contextualize the results.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the problem of unsupervised grammar learning, with a novel unified approach that addresses limitations in prior work. The empirical results are promising and well-documented. However, the paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses noted above before acceptance.

**Arguments for acceptance**:
- Novel unified approach to learning grammar structure (And-Or fragments)
- Solid theoretical foundation with efficient gain calculation
- Strong empirical results across two distinct domains
- Clear presentation and organization

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods
- Unclear attribution of performance gains to specific methodological contributions
- Insufficient discussion of computational complexity and scalability
- Non-standard evaluation metrics for event grammar learning

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand the comparison with recent work**: Include comparisons with more recent grammar learning methods (e.g., recent NIPS/ICML papers on grammar induction) to better establish the state-of-the-art.

2. **Add ablation studies**: Include experiments that isolate the effect of the unified And-Or fragment approach versus other components of the method (e.g., sparsity prior, Viterbi approximation).

3. **Clarify computational complexity**: Provide a more detailed analysis of the algorithm's time complexity and discuss scalability to larger datasets or more complex grammars.

4. **Standardize evaluation metrics**: For the event grammar experiments, report standard metrics like precision, recall, and F1-score alongside the authors' metrics to better contextualize the results.

5. **Discuss limitations**: Address potential failure cases of the approach (e.g., when the target grammar has complex dependencies not captured by the And-Or structure) and discuss scenarios where the method might not perform well.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution with promising empirical results. With the suggested improvements to strengthen the comparison with recent work, clarify the contribution, and provide more comprehensive evaluation, this paper would make an excellent NIPS contribution. The current version is good but would benefit from addressing these points before acceptance.
