Summary
The paper studies unsupervised structure learning for stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs), a context-free subclass that generalizes PCFGs and relates to decomposable sum-product networks (SPNs). The authors present a unified formalization agnostic to modality (text, images, events). They propose to learn both structure and parameters from unannotated data by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments: each fragment introduces one And-node whose children are new Or-nodes, and each Or-node expands to a small set of existing terminals/nonterminals. This unifies the search for composition (And) and reconfiguration (Or), in contrast to prior works that treat them separately or fix the rule set.

They optimize a sparsity-regularized posterior using Viterbi likelihood and a size prior. A key technical contribution is an efficient computation of the posterior gain of adding a candidate fragment using sufficient statistics derived from the data: an n-gram tensor counting covered configurations and a context matrix counting configuration–context co-occurrences. Under an unambiguity assumption, the likelihood gain factorizes into two coherence measures of these statistics; the prior gain trades off fragment size against reductions achieved. Search proceeds greedily or with a beam over candidate sub-tensors. Experiments on event grammars (two activity datasets) and image grammars (a synthetic dataset with known ground truth and a real animal-face dataset) show improved F-measure for event detection and lower perplexity (67.5 vs. 129.4) on real images than a recent AOG method.

Relation to prior work
The method builds on grammar induction (Bayesian model merging; ADIOS; iterative biclustering) and AOGs for images/events. Compared to structure learning for AOGs (e.g., Si & Zhu; Si et al.), this work directly optimizes posterior with a sparsity prior and proposes And-Or fragments that jointly learn composition and alternation. The sufficient-statistics view connects to biclustering/tensor coherence. The relation to SPN structure learning is noted but could be further developed; there exist SPN structure learning methods not cited.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The posterior-gain derivation is plausible and the sufficient-statistics trick is elegant; however, it relies on strong assumptions (Viterbi training and grammar unambiguity) that are not deeply analyzed. It is asserted that any context-free AOG can be constructed by composing And-Or fragments; a sketch or proof would strengthen the claim.
- Method details: Some important elements are underspecified in the main text: how “relations” in And-rules are instantiated across modalities; how contexts are defined and extracted; how candidate tensors/sub-tensors are generated and pruned; how unambiguity is enforced or detected; how sa and so (code lengths) are set; and the exact smoothing used for perplexity. Algorithm pseudocode is deferred to the supplement.
- Empirical evaluation: The image experiments are compelling, especially the real-data perplexity improvement. Synthetic image results include F and KL vs. ground truth but lack numerical tables (only a plotted Figure is mentioned). For events, Table 2 with F-measure is referenced but not provided; only qualitative conclusions are included. No error bars or statistical tests are reported. The comparison set is limited (older ID-sequence baselines); ablations (And-only, Or-only, or removing the context term) and sensitivity to α/beam width are missing. Runtime and scaling claims are anecdotal; a complexity analysis would help.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable and well-motivated. The high-level algorithm and figures convey the idea of And-Or fragments. That said, several notational glitches and figure artifacts make parts hard to follow (e.g., Figures 1–2 contain overlapping symbols). The derivations and algorithmic steps are split across the main text and supplementary material, which reduces reproducibility from the main paper. A worked micro-example would aid understanding of the tensor/matrix construction and posterior-gain computation.

Originality
The unified And-Or fragment proposal and the posterior-gain computation via coherence of n-gram tensors and context matrices are original and interesting. Unifying the learning of composition and alternation in AOGs is a meaningful step beyond prior AOG structure-learning approaches. The use of Viterbi likelihood with an explicit sparsity prior is known separately, but their combination here is novel in this context.

Significance
If validated more broadly, the approach could be a useful general-purpose structure learner for compositional, reconfigurable models across modalities. The empirical gains over a recent AOG method on images are sizeable. However, the generality claim would be stronger with additional baselines (e.g., SPN structure learning, modern grammar induction methods) and more thorough analyses. The unambiguity and Viterbi assumptions may limit applicability to ambiguous domains.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear and well-motivated framing of structure learning for AOGs across modalities.
- Novel And-Or fragment mechanism that unifies composition and alternation.
- Efficient posterior-gain computation using sufficient statistics; nice connection to coherence measures.
- Empirical improvements on both synthetic and real datasets; substantial perplexity reduction on real images.
- Practical search strategy with reasonable runtime claims.

Cons for acceptance
- Relies on strong assumptions (Viterbi likelihood; unambiguity) with limited theoretical or empirical justification.
- Missing key experimental details and some results (full event table, error bars, statistical tests).
- Limited baselines; no comparison to SPN structure-learning methods despite stated relation.
- Incomplete clarity on relations, context definition, candidate generation, and coding lengths; reproducibility concerns.
- Some ad-hoc steps (e.g., converting top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes for multi-event videos).

Questions for authors
1. How is unambiguity checked or encouraged during learning? What happens when parses are ambiguous?
2. How sensitive are results to α, sa/so, beam width, and the choice of n (arity) and relation sets?
3. Can you provide ablations isolating the contributions of the n-gram tensor vs. context-matrix terms?
4. How are contexts defined for images and events? Are they local neighborhoods or full surrounding structures?
5. Can you compare against SPN structure-learning baselines and recent grammar induction methods?

Suggestions for improvement
- Include the missing event results table with variance and significance testing; add runtime and scaling plots.
- Provide a formal proof or detailed argument that And-Or fragments suffice to construct any context-free AOG.
- Add ablation studies and sensitivity analyses; compare to SPN structure learning.
- Clarify the encoding-length parameters, context extraction, and candidate sub-tensor mining process; add a small worked example.
- Discuss limitations of the unambiguity and Viterbi assumptions and potential relaxations (e.g., N-best parses, partial E-step).

Recommendation: Weak Accept
The paper presents a thoughtful and potentially impactful approach to unsupervised structure learning for AOGs with solid ideas and promising results, but would benefit from stronger empirical validation and clarity. With the suggested additions, it could be a strong contribution.
