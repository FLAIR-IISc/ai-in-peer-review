Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of stochastic context-free And-Or grammars (AOGs) from unannotated data. AOGs model compositionality via And-rules (decomposition into subparts with relations) and reconfigurability via Or-rules (alternatives), and unify treatments across modalities (text, images, events). The method starts from an initial grammar that exactly generates the training set (one And-rule per sample under S), then iteratively inserts And-Or fragments—each consisting of a new And-node with an And-rule over newly introduced Or-nodes, and Or-rules mapping to existing nodes—to maximize the posterior of the grammar under a sparsity prior. Likelihood is approximated by Viterbi likelihood. The posterior gain for a candidate fragment is computed efficiently under an unambiguity assumption using sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor counting configurations of child choices and a context matrix counting co-occurrences of those configurations with surrounding context. The likelihood gain factorizes into two coherence measures of these statistics, and the prior gain penalizes model size via a code-length proxy. Greedy/beam search with random restarts selects fragments. Experiments on event parsing (indoor activities) and image parsing (synthetic animal-face sketches and a real animal-face dataset) show improved F-measure/KL to ground truth on synthetic data and lower perplexity on real images compared to prior AOG structure learning [17], and competitive or better event parsing F-measure vs. [15,18].

This builds on grammar induction and model merging for language [14–16], Viterbi training and unambiguity regularization for unsupervised grammars [20,21], image/event AOGs [1,3,7,17,18], and is related to SPNs [10]. The key difference from [17,18] is joint induction of compositions and alternatives via And-Or fragments rather than learning them separately.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of context-free AOGs that is modality-agnostic, connecting to SCFGs and decomposable SPNs.
- Principled objective: posterior maximization with sparsity prior; Viterbi likelihood is justified by prior work [20,21].
- Efficient scoring of candidate fragments via sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor and context matrix), avoiding repeated reparsing; closed-form Or-rule probabilities from counts.
- Unified search for compositions and reconfigurations using And-Or fragments addresses data sparsity and redundancy issues of separate And- or Or-fragment searches.
- Empirical gains over strong prior AOG structure-learning baselines on both events and images; synthetic image study includes comparison to the known ground-truth grammar.

Weaknesses
- The unambiguity assumption is central to the likelihood-gain derivation; its validity and impact on learned grammars in ambiguous regimes are not quantified. No ablation without this assumption is provided.
- Search complexity and scalability are not rigorously analyzed. The space of fragments (choice of n, relation types, and candidate sub-tensors) can be large; heuristics and constraints are only briefly described.
- The prior uses code-length parameters (sa, so) and sparsity coefficient α; sensitivity analyses are missing, and α is fixed without justification. It is unclear how sa/so are set across modalities.
- Experimental scope is modest: one event dataset (with a synthetic variant) and one real image dataset. Comparisons to broader grammar-induction baselines (e.g., HDP-PCFGs [9], PCFG induction with Viterbi training [20], SPN structure learning) are absent. For events, non-grammar baselines (HMM/HSMMs) could contextualize significance.
- Some details lack clarity: how relations/energies in And-rules are parameterized and estimated; precise definition of “context” for the context matrix across modalities; smoothing used for perplexity; and how reductions are selected/applied when multiple matches exist.
- The treatment of multi-event videos by converting top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes at the end effectively assumes independence; implications for likelihood accounting and potential double-counting are not analyzed.

Quality
- The optimization criterion and fragment gain decomposition are sound under the stated assumptions, though core derivations are deferred to the supplement. Closed-form Or-rule estimation from reduction counts is reasonable. However, the reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi likelihood is a strong approximation; conditions under which the posterior gain aligns with true likelihood gain are not established.
- Empirical claims are supported by reported metrics, but missing implementation details and lack of runtime/complexity measurements limit reproducibility and assessment of practicality.

Clarity
- The conceptual flow is generally clear, and figures illustrate the iterative insertion of fragments and sufficient statistics (though some figure text appears garbled). The algorithmic framework is well described; pseudocode is in the supplement. More concrete examples of contexts, relation types, and a small end-to-end fragment insertion example would improve clarity.

Originality
- The joint And-Or fragment mechanism for structure induction and the derivation of a posterior-gain surrogate based on tensor/matrix coherence appear novel relative to [17,18] and classic model merging [14–16]. The modality-agnostic formalization is not new per se but is usefully unified.

Significance
- The approach provides a practical and general method to learn compositional and reconfigurable grammars across domains, with demonstrated improvements over prior AOG learners. Broader impact would be strengthened by larger-scale studies and comparisons to alternative hierarchical probabilistic models.

Suggestions for improvement
- Quantify the effect of the unambiguity assumption: measure ambiguity rates in parses, compare to a reparsing-based estimate on smaller subsets, or include unambiguity regularization [21] baselines.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for α, sa, so, and for the choice of n and relation sets; include ablations comparing And-only, Or-only, and And-Or fragment searches.
- Add complexity analysis and empirical runtime scaling with dataset size and grammar size; detail beam width and fragment candidate generation.
- Expand baselines to include HDP/PCFG induction [9,20] and SPN structure learning [10], and non-grammar baselines for events.
- Clarify the definition of context, smoothing for perplexity, and parameterization/learning of relation energies.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, efficient structure learning method that jointly induces composition and alternatives.
- Clear generality across modalities; connections to SCFGs and SPNs.
- Empirical improvements over prior AOG structure learners on both synthetic and real data.

Arguments against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on unverified unambiguity and Viterbi approximations; limited theoretical guarantees.
- Limited experimental breadth and missing comparisons to broader baselines; incomplete analysis of scalability and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Some important implementation details are deferred or unclear, affecting reproducibility.
