Summary
The paper studies unsupervised structure learning for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), a context-free subclass designed to capture compositionality (And) and reconfigurability (Or) across modalities (text, images, events). The key idea is to grow the grammar from a trivial instance by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments—each fragment introduces an And-node whose children are Or-nodes, each expanding to multiple existing nodes—while explicitly maximizing a posterior objective that combines Viterbi likelihood with a sparsity prior on grammar size. To make search efficient, the authors assume unambiguous parses and derive sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor for configurations and a context matrix; posterior gain factorizes into coherence measures on these statistics plus an MDL-like prior term. Greedy/beam search with random restarts selects fragments. Experiments on event datasets (indoor activities) and image datasets (synthetic animal sketch faces and a real animal face set) show improvements over two prior AOG/event methods and one image AOG baseline.

Strengths
- Conceptual unification: a modality-agnostic formalization of context-free AOGs and a single learning framework applicable to events and images.
- Joint structure induction: learning And/Or alternatives together via And-Or fragments is a reasonable design to mitigate data sparsity and redundancy that arise when learning And/Or separately.
- Posterior-driven search: explicit objective (Viterbi likelihood × sparsity prior) rather than purely heuristic merges; the sufficient-statistics derivation is novel within AOG learning and can reduce re-parsing costs.
- Empirical indications: On the presented tasks, the approach outperforms selected prior AOG baselines and appears to produce more compact grammars.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Strong assumptions and approximations: The unambiguity assumption and reliance on Viterbi likelihood are critical to the derivation and claimed efficiency. Many real grammars, especially for images and events, are inherently ambiguous. The impact of violating unambiguity is neither analyzed nor tested. No theoretical guarantees (e.g., consistency, bounds on approximation error, or conditions under which the coherence surrogate aligns with true posterior gain) are provided.
- Objective specification and prior: The prior is a size penalty with constant α and per-symbol code-lengths sa, so. The encoding scheme, its justification, and how these constants are chosen/tuned are under-specified. The posterior gain expression mixes probabilities and counts with ad hoc smoothing in experiments; it is unclear whether likelihoods are true marginals or Viterbi scores at evaluation time.
- Incomplete parameter learning story: And-rules are said to be parameterized by “energy terms on relations,” but the paper only derives Or-rule probabilities from counts. How are the And-rule energies specified/learned, and how do they interact with the posterior gain? For images/events, the set and parameterization of relations (followed-by, co-occurrence, spatial relations) are fixed, but training of their energies is not clearly described.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: Datasets are small; the second event dataset is synthetically manipulated. Comparisons are restricted to older AOG/event works [15,18] and one image AOG [17]. Missing are stronger established baselines for unsupervised structure/segmentation such as HDP-PCFG/adaptor grammars, Bayesian PCFG induction, HSMM/HDP-HSMM for events, or modern PCFG induction methods. For images, perplexity is reported but the computation (marginal vs Viterbi, smoothing) is insufficiently detailed; no qualitative parsing/segmentation comparisons or ablations.
- Missing ablations/sensitivity: No analysis of the benefit of And-Or fragments vs And-only or Or-only; no sensitivity to α, code-lengths, beam width, restart count; no study of the effect of the unambiguity assumption; no reporting of learned grammar size vs performance trade-offs.
- Search and scalability: The search space depends on n-gram tensors and relation types; how candidate fragments are enumerated, pruned, and scored for large vocabularies and higher n is unclear. The reported runtime is for modest settings; there is no complexity analysis or scaling study.
- Clarity and reproducibility: Some derivations are deferred; key formulas and notation (e.g., posterior gain equation, context definition) are hard to parse. The learning/decoding pipelines differ across tasks (e.g., converting to IDs versus action occurrences) and are not fully specified. Important details (smoothing method, parameter initialization, parse algorithm for general AOGs) are missing.

Quality
Technically interesting but not fully convincing. Core claims depend on unproven assumptions, and empirical validation is limited to small-scale comparisons against relatively weak baselines. Important implementation details are omitted, hindering reproducibility.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable but several key parts lack precision (encoding prior, And-rule energies, likelihood vs Viterbi in evaluation). Figures/equations as presented are difficult to follow; the main text frequently refers to the supplement for core derivations.

Originality
Moderate. The joint And-Or fragment idea and the sufficient-statistics formulation are novel within AOG learning, but the overall approach builds on established grammar induction and MDL/posterior-driven structure learning traditions (e.g., model merging, Bayesian PCFGs, SPNs).

Significance
Currently limited. Without stronger baselines, ablations, and robustness analyses, it is hard to assess whether this approach will be adopted. The framework could be useful if validated more rigorously.

Questions for the authors
- How are And-rule energy terms defined and learned? Are they fixed functions of relations or estimated from data?
- How sensitive is the method to the unambiguity assumption? What happens if parses are ambiguous?
- How are sa and so chosen? Does the ranking of fragments change materially with different coding schemes?
- Do you compute true marginal likelihood/perplexity at test time or Viterbi scores? How is smoothing done?
- Can you provide ablations (And-only, Or-only, And-Or) and sensitivity to α and beam width?
- How do you enumerate candidate fragments for large n and relation sets, and what is the computational complexity?

Pros
- Clear motivation for joint And/Or induction; principled posterior objective.
- Potentially general across modalities.
- Some empirical gains over prior AOG methods.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on strong assumptions; limited theory.
- Sparse and non-standard empirical evaluation; missing strong baselines and ablations.
- Important details are under-specified; unclear reproducibility.
- Scalability and robustness not demonstrated.

Recommendation: Reject (weak). Confidence: high.
