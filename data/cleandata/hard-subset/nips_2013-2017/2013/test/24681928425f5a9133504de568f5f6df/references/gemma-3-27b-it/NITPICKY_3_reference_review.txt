This paper proposes an unsupervised learning approach for stochastic And-Or grammars, aiming to simultaneously learn both the grammar structure and its parameters. The approach iteratively inserts new “And-Or fragments” into an initial trivial grammar, optimizing a posterior probability based on sufficient statistics derived from the training data. The authors claim improvements over existing methods in learning both event and image grammars.

While the core idea of learning both structure and parameters simultaneously is promising, and the use of And-Or fragments to unify composition and reconfiguration search is conceptually appealing, the paper suffers from a concerning number of shortcomings, ranging from minor inconsistencies in notation to significant gaps in experimental validation. 

First, the formalization, while aiming for generality, feels unnecessarily verbose. The definitions of Σ, N, S, R, and P are standard for grammars, but the repeated highlighting of their connection to natural language, images, and events doesn't add substantive value and contributes to the paper's length. The distinction between And- and Or-rules is clearly explained, but the constant reiteration of their roles throughout the paper becomes redundant.

Second, the algorithm description lacks crucial details. The crucial step of "greedy search or beam search" for And-Or fragments is glossed over. The authors mention the computation of posterior gain, but the specific implementation details – how the search space is pruned, how beam width is selected, and how the algorithm avoids local optima – remain opaque. The claim of efficient computation based on sufficient statistics is unconvincing without a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity. Specifically, the construction and manipulation of the “context matrix CM” for larger datasets seem computationally expensive, and this is not adequately addressed.

Third, the experimental evaluation is problematic. The event grammar experiments utilize a synthetic dataset modification that, while motivated, seems contrived. The comparison against previous methods ([15, 18]) is limited, and it is unclear why these specific methods were chosen. The reported F-measure improvements, while positive, are not substantial enough to warrant strong claims of superiority. The image grammar experiments, while more compelling, are presented with insufficient detail. The quantization process used for the real dataset is merely referenced to a previous work [17] without explanation. The perplexity metric, while common, doesn’t provide a qualitative understanding of the learned grammars, and visual examples of learned structures would be highly beneficial. The statement "our approach significantly outperforms the competing approach" (Table 3) feels overstated given the limited scope of the experiments. 

Fourth, there are several minor, yet distracting, issues. The notation is inconsistent. For example, the size of the grammar is denoted as “‖G‖”, but its use in the prior probability formulation is not fully clear. The sentence structure in several places is awkward, hindering readability. Figure 1 is difficult to interpret, and the labels are small. The citation style is also inconsistent (e.g., sometimes authors are listed fully, sometimes abbreviated).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The idea of jointly learning structure and parameters for stochastic And-Or grammars is a valid research direction.
* The use of And-Or fragments is a conceptually interesting way to address the composition/reconfiguration tradeoff.
* The approach demonstrates some empirical improvement over existing methods, albeit modest.

**Arguments for rejection:**

* The paper lacks crucial algorithmic details, hindering reproducibility.
* The experimental evaluation is insufficient and lacks rigor.
* The writing is verbose and contains inconsistencies.
* The claimed improvements are not substantial enough to justify the complexity of the approach.
* The paper feels incomplete, more like a proof-of-concept than a fully developed contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the promising core idea, this paper falls short of NIPS standards. The lack of detail, insufficient experimental validation, and minor flaws in presentation overshadow the potential novelty of the work. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should significantly expand the experimental evaluation, provide a more detailed algorithmic description, and address the inconsistencies in notation and writing before resubmission. A more compelling demonstration of the benefits of the And-Or fragment approach, along with a more rigorous comparison to state-of-the-art methods, would be necessary for acceptance.
