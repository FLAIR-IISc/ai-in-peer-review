This paper presents an unsupervised approach to learning stochastic And-Or grammars, aiming to jointly learn both the structure and parameters of the grammar from unannotated data. The core contribution lies in an iterative refinement process using "And-Or fragments," designed to efficiently explore the grammar space and overcome limitations of previous approaches that treat composition and reconfiguration separately. The authors frame the learning as posterior maximization and provide a formulation for efficiently computing the posterior gain. The experimental evaluation includes both synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating competitive or improved performance compared to existing methods in event and image grammar learning.

While the problem addressed is relevant – unsupervised grammar learning is a challenging and valuable pursuit – and the proposed approach appears logically sound, I find myself ultimately skeptical due to a lack of compelling evidence of significant advancement over established techniques. The paper builds upon existing work in natural language processing and image modeling but doesn't convincingly demonstrate a substantial leap forward.

**Strengths:**

*   **Unified Framework:** The proposed And-Or fragment approach is a conceptually appealing way to jointly learn compositional and reconfiguration aspects of the grammar.
*   **Posterior Optimization:** Explicitly optimizing the posterior probability is a principled approach, and the formulation for calculating the posterior gain is a positive contribution.
*   **Theoretical Formulation:** The derivation connecting likelihood gain to tensor/matrix coherence is a potentially useful insight.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The attempt to formulate the stochastic And-Or grammar in a data-agnostic manner is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Improvement:** The experimental results show competitive, but not overwhelmingly superior, performance. The gains, particularly on the synthetic data, don’t justify the complexity of the proposed approach compared to simpler baselines. The improvements on the real image dataset are modest, and the perplexity metric, while standard, doesn’t offer a clear intuitive understanding of the visual quality or interpretability of the learned grammars.
*   **Limited Baselines:** The comparison is primarily against one previous approach ([17]) and a method using IDs ([18]). The paper would be significantly strengthened by including comparisons against more widely used and established techniques for unsupervised structure learning, such as variational autoencoders (VAEs) or generative adversarial networks (GANs) applied to similar tasks. Demonstrating superiority over these methods would build greater confidence in the novelty and significance of the work.
*   **Experimental Detail:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail. For example, the parameter settings for the greedy/beam search are not provided, and the impact of the sparsity prior parameter α is not thoroughly explored. A sensitivity analysis would be helpful.
*   **Complexity and Scalability:** While the authors claim efficiency, the overall algorithm appears complex, and its scalability to larger, more realistic datasets is not demonstrated. The computational cost of forming and evaluating the And-Or fragments could become prohibitive.
*   **Overstated Claims:** Phrases like "significantly outperforms" in the abstract and discussion sections are not fully supported by the presented evidence.

**Specific Concerns:**

*   The discussion of the likelihood gain in terms of tensor coherence (Section 3.3) feels somewhat disconnected from the rest of the paper. The practical benefits of this formulation are not clearly articulated.
*   The modification to handle multiple events per sample (end of Section 4.1) seems like an ad-hoc fix and could introduce biases.
*   The choice of spatial relations in the image grammar experiments seems arbitrary. A justification for this choice would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The approach presents a novel and theoretically grounded framework for unsupervised grammar learning.
*   The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on challenging datasets.
*   The paper addresses an important problem in machine learning with potential applications in diverse domains.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant improvement over existing techniques.
*   The comparisons are limited, and the lack of baselines weakens the claims of novelty and significance.
*   The complexity of the algorithm raises concerns about scalability and practical applicability.
*   The paper relies heavily on theoretical formulations without sufficient empirical validation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While the ideas presented are interesting, the lack of strong empirical evidence and comprehensive comparisons to established methods fails to convince me of its substantial contribution to the field. The paper feels more like a proof-of-concept than a significant advance. I would encourage the authors to conduct more thorough experiments, including stronger baselines and a detailed analysis of scalability and sensitivity to hyperparameters, before resubmitting. A more critical and nuanced discussion of the limitations of their approach would also be beneficial.
