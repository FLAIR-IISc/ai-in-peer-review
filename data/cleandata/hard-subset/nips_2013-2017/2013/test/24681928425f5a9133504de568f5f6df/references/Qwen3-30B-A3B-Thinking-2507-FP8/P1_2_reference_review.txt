

# Peer Review: Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars

## Summary
This paper proposes an unsupervised approach for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars, which are powerful models for representing compositional structures in data like images and event sequences. The authors address a key limitation of previous work, which either manually specifies the grammar structure (challenging and restrictive) or learns only parameters while keeping the structure fixed. Their key innovation is the introduction of "And-Or fragments" that unify the learning of compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules) in a single framework. This approach explicitly optimizes the posterior probability of the grammar and efficiently computes likelihood gains using sufficient statistics. The paper evaluates the approach on event grammars (human activity videos) and image grammars (animal face sketches), showing competitive or superior performance compared to prior methods.

The work builds on prior structure learning approaches for natural language grammars [14,15,16] and extends recent work on And-Or grammars for images [17] and events [18]. It differs from previous work by unifying composition and reconfiguration learning rather than handling them separately, which the authors argue makes the approach more efficient and robust to data scarcity.

## Strengths

**Quality (Excellent):** The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear theoretical justification for the likelihood and prior gains. The derivation of the posterior gain using sufficient statistics is elegant and well-supported. The experimental design is thorough, with careful comparison against relevant baselines on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The authors properly acknowledge limitations (e.g., the grammar unambiguity assumption) and provide detailed experimental results including F-measure and perplexity metrics.

**Clarity (Excellent):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The formal definition of stochastic And-Or grammars (Section 2) is clear and well-motivated, with helpful examples in Table 1 and Figure 1. The algorithm framework (Section 3.2) and the concept of And-Or fragments (Section 3.3) are particularly well-explained with illustrative examples (Figures 2 and 3). The supplementary material appears to provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Originality (Strong):** The unified approach using And-Or fragments represents a meaningful advance over prior work that handles compositions and reconfigurations separately. The paper clearly articulates how this approach differs from previous methods and why it addresses key limitations (e.g., data scarcity issues with separate And/Or fragment learning). The agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars across data types is also a useful contribution.

**Significance (Strong):** Learning compositional structures is a fundamental problem in machine learning, and this work provides a practical, scalable approach with demonstrated success across multiple domains (events and images). The unified learning framework could be broadly applicable to other compositional data domains. The empirical results show clear improvements over prior methods, particularly on more complex datasets.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the paper mentions that the algorithm runs "within a few minutes" on a desktop with 5000 training samples, it doesn't provide a detailed scalability analysis. It would be valuable to know how the approach scales with increasing data size and complexity, which is important for practical applications.

**Assumption of Grammar Ambiguity:** The paper relies on Viterbi likelihood (assuming grammar unambiguity), which may not hold in all real-world scenarios. While the authors note this limitation, a brief discussion of how the approach might be extended to handle ambiguous grammars would strengthen the work.

**Evaluation Depth:** The evaluation on the real image dataset uses perplexity as the sole metric. While perplexity is standard for generative models, additional qualitative analysis of the learned grammars (e.g., visualization of learned structures) would provide deeper insights into the model's capabilities.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of structure learning for compositional models. The unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations using And-Or fragments is novel and well-justified, and the empirical results are compelling. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and makes a significant contribution to the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Clear, well-justified technical contribution
- Strong theoretical foundation with practical implementation
- Comprehensive evaluation across multiple domains
- Novel approach that unifies previous separate learning procedures
- Well-written and clearly organized

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of scalability to very large datasets
- Reliance on the grammar unambiguity assumption
- Evaluation metrics could be more comprehensive for the image dataset

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a technically sound, well-motivated, and significant contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. The authors have made a clear advance over previous work by unifying the learning of compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments, which leads to more efficient and robust learning. The empirical results are thorough and demonstrate the approach's effectiveness across different data domains. The paper is clearly written and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the work. I believe this paper would be of significant interest to the NIPS community, particularly to researchers working on generative models, compositional structure learning, and applications in computer vision and natural language processing.

**Recommendation: Accept**
