## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to unsupervised learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs). Unlike prior work that often relies on manually defined structures or separate learning of composition and reconfiguration, the authors propose an iterative method to learn both the structure *and* parameters of a SAG directly from unannotated data. The core contribution lies in learning “And-Or fragments” – simultaneously inducing compositional and reconfiguration aspects – and optimizing the posterior probability of the entire grammar, leveraging sufficient statistics for efficiency. The work provides a unified formalization of SAGs applicable to various data types (text, images, events) and demonstrates promising empirical results on event and image grammars.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon existing work in stochastic grammars, particularly in natural language processing (e.g., probabilistic context-free grammars) and computer vision (e.g., image grammars). It acknowledges the limitations of manually defined structures [11, 12, 13] and improves upon recent structure learning approaches for And-Or grammars [17, 18] by unifying composition and reconfiguration learning. The approach is related to Bayesian model merging [14, 15, 16] but expands upon these by explicitly optimizing the posterior probability and utilizing a more efficient learning scheme. It also draws connections to sum-product networks [10].

**Strengths:**

* **Unified Framework:** The authors successfully present a unifying formalism for SAGs, applicable across diverse data modalities. This is a significant conceptual step forward.
* **Joint Structure & Parameter Learning:** The simultaneous learning of grammar structure and parameters is a key strength. This avoids the limitations of fixed-structure approaches and allows for more flexible model adaptation.
* **And-Or Fragments:** The introduction of And-Or fragments is a clever approach. It elegantly addresses the challenges of learning composition and reconfiguration concurrently, improving efficiency and robustness.
* **Posterior Optimization:** Explicitly optimizing the posterior probability, coupled with the use of sufficient statistics, provides a principled learning framework.  The derivation of these statistics is a valuable contribution.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on event and image grammars demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, achieving comparable or superior performance to existing methods, especially on the more challenging synthetic event dataset.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear explanation of the methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Scalability Concerns:** While the use of sufficient statistics improves efficiency, the computational complexity of learning, especially for high-dimensional data or complex grammars, isn't thoroughly discussed. The paper mentions the algorithm running reasonably fast on a desktop, but scaling to larger datasets requires further investigation.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The influence of the parameter α (sparsity prior) is not comprehensively explored. A more detailed sensitivity analysis would strengthen the results.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with previous approaches is somewhat limited. While the chosen baselines are relevant, exploring more recent or state-of-the-art methods, where applicable, would provide a more robust evaluation.  Specifically, in the image grammar experiments, comparison with more modern deep learning approaches for scene understanding might be beneficial (though outside the direct scope of the paper).
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of grammar unambiguity, while simplifying the computations, might be restrictive in certain applications. A discussion of the potential impact of this assumption would be useful.
* **Dataset Size:** The event grammar experiments used a relatively small dataset (55 training samples).  Larger-scale evaluation is needed to demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars.
* The proposed method addresses a significant challenge in probabilistic modeling – learning both structure and parameters – in a unified and efficient manner.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on diverse datasets.
* The work has the potential to impact research in areas such as natural language processing, computer vision, and event understanding.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*  Scalability to large datasets and complex scenarios remains a concern.
*  The sensitivity analysis of key parameters (like α) is limited.
*  The comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods is not exhaustive.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised learning of probabilistic grammars. The proposed approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some areas for improvement (scalability analysis, parameter sensitivity, broader comparison), the strengths of the work outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

*   Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the algorithm.
*   Conduct a sensitivity analysis of the parameter α.
*   Consider including a comparison with more recent or state-of-the-art methods, where applicable.
*   Discuss the potential impact of the grammar unambiguity assumption.
*   Consider larger-scale experiments, especially for the event grammar task.
