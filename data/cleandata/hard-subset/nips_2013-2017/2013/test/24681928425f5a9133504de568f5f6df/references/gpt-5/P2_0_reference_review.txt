Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles unsupervised structure learning for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), a grammar formalism that captures compositionality (And) and reconfigurability (Or), with applications across text, images, and events. The key idea is to jointly induce structure and parameters by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments—each consisting of an And-node with multiple child Or-nodes—chosen to maximize the posterior of the grammar under a sparsity prior and a Viterbi-likelihood approximation. The authors derive an efficient scoring of candidate fragments using sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor over configurations and a context matrix over configuration-context co-occurrences. They assume (approximate) unambiguity to avoid re-parsing each time and show that the likelihood gain factorizes into two coherence measures. They evaluate on event parsing datasets (including a variant with subtyped actions) and image parsing datasets (synthetic with ground-truth grammar; real animal faces), reporting improvements over prior AOG structure learning methods [17, 18] and selected NLP grammar induction baselines [15].

Relative to prior grammar induction (Stolcke & Omohundro; Solan et al.; Klein & Manning) and AOG/SPN work (Zhu & Mumford; Zhao & Zhu; Poon & Domingos), the novelty is the unified And-Or fragment search (as opposed to separate And- or Or-fragments), explicit posterior optimization with a length/sparsity prior, and the use of simple sufficient statistics to score fragments without full re-parsing. The paper positions itself appropriately with respect to image/event AOG learning [17, 18] and Viterbi training/unambiguity bias in PCFGs [20, 21].

Quality
- Technical soundness: The framework is plausible and well-motivated. The use of a Viterbi objective and an MDL-style prior is standard and sensible for structure induction. The fragment-scoring machinery (n-gram tensor and context matrix) is an appealing approximation to avoid repeated parsing; the intuition about two kinds of “context-freeness” is compelling. However, key derivations (likelihood gain factorization, coherence link, optimal Or-rule probabilities) are deferred to the supplement; in the main text the equations are hard to parse and contain notation glitches. The unambiguity assumption is strong and its practical validity and impact are not quantified. There is no formal guarantee on search quality (greedy/beam with restarts), nor a complexity analysis of fragment enumeration/tensor construction.
- Empirical support: The synthetic image experiments (with known ground-truth grammar) are a strong point: both F1 and KL divergence improve over [17]. The real image results show sizable perplexity gains over [17]. Event parsing shows competitive or better F-measure than [15, 18], with the “grounded” variant (jointly learning co-occurrence and order) performing best on the more complex dataset. Nonetheless, the event study is small (61 videos, 6 test samples per fold), and the terminal symbols are provided (annotated actions or quantized sketches); robustness to noisy terminals is not examined. Ablations (e.g., And-only, Or-only, fragment size, effect of unambiguity/Viterbi approximation) are missing.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized and explains the high-level algorithm clearly (initial grammar, iterative fragment insertion, posterior-based scoring). Figures help, but some are cluttered and the rendering of equations/indices is inconsistent (e.g., CM[e,c] exponents, repeated symbols). Important definitions (e.g., grammar size encoding lengths sa, so; exact relation parameterization in And-rules) are underspecified, making reproduction difficult. Pseudocode is said to be in the supplement; bringing a concise algorithm box into the main paper would help.

Originality
- The And-Or fragment idea that unifies composition and reconfiguration search is a clear contribution over prior AOG structure induction, which typically alternates or separates And/Or learning. The posterior-based scoring via sufficient statistics and the decomposition into coherence terms is also novel to my knowledge in this context. Related work is cited appropriately, but comparisons to structure learning in sum-product networks (e.g., LearnSPN and its successors) are missing and would strengthen positioning given the stated connection.

Significance
- A general-purpose, practical method for unsupervised structure learning of AOGs is potentially impactful for vision and event understanding. The cross-domain demonstrations support the generality claim. However, the scope of empirical comparisons is limited (mostly to earlier AOG works), and the dependence on relatively clean terminal alphabets reduces practical significance for fully end-to-end settings. With stronger baselines and analysis, this could be a substantial contribution.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for joint And-Or fragment induction; addresses data sparsity and redundancy issues of And-only/Or-only searches.
- Principled objective with sparsity prior; efficient fragment scoring using sufficient statistics.
- Cross-modal applicability (events and images) with solid gains over prior AOG learning methods; synthetic study with ground truth is convincing.
- Sensible practical modifications (e.g., handling multiple events per video, CYK parsing).

Weaknesses
- Heavy reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi approximations; no analysis of their failure modes or empirical effects.
- Insufficient detail on several implementation choices (encoding lengths, relation parameterization, smoothing for perplexity).
- Limited baselines, especially missing comparisons to SPN structure learning and more recent grammar induction methods.
- Search heuristics lack guarantees; no complexity/runtime analysis beyond anecdotal timing.
- Event experiments are small-scale and rely on presegmented atomic actions.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Quantify the impact of the unambiguity assumption: how often are parses ambiguous in practice, and how does performance change if you relax the assumption or approximate re-parsing?
- Provide ablations: And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or fragments; effect of fragment arity n; beam width; Viterbi vs full marginal likelihood.
- Detail encoding length choices (sa, so) and sensitivity to α; clarify And-rule relation parameterization and how energies/probabilities are learned.
- Compare to SPN structure learning algorithms on the image datasets, given the close connection, and to more recent grammar induction baselines in NLP-style settings.
- Clarify smoothing used for perplexity; report standard deviations and statistical significance.
- Release code or provide fuller pseudocode to aid reproducibility.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a well-motivated and potentially useful method with novel elements and promising results, but some theoretical assumptions are strong and empirical evaluation is not yet as comprehensive as expected for NeurIPS. With clarified derivations, stronger baselines/ablations, and more detail for reproducibility, it would make a solid contribution. I lean toward a weak accept contingent on addressing clarity and evaluation breadth.
