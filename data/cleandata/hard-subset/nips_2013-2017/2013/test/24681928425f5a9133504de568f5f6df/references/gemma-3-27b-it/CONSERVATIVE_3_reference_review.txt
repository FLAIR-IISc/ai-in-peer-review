This paper presents an unsupervised learning approach for stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs), aiming to simultaneously learn both the grammar structure and parameters. The authors address a challenging problem, extending previous work in both natural language processing and image modeling. The core idea – learning And-Or fragments in a unified manner – is presented as a key improvement over separate composition and reconfiguration learning. The paper introduces a formalization of SAGs and leverages sufficient statistics for efficient learning.

While the problem is relevant and the motivation is clear, I find the paper lacking in several critical areas, primarily regarding novelty and empirical validation. The extension of previous work, while acknowledged, doesn't appear to represent a substantial breakthrough. The core idea of learning And-Or fragments, while intuitively appealing, feels less like a novel insight and more like a natural extension of existing structure learning techniques. The authors claim this unified approach is more robust to data scarcity, but this claim isn’t convincingly demonstrated.

My main concern lies with the experimental evaluation. The synthetic image experiments show improvement over a single baseline ([17]), which is insufficient. A more comprehensive comparison against established image parsing techniques (e.g., those based on Markov Random Fields or CNNs with structured outputs) is crucial. The real image experiments only report perplexity, a metric that can be misleading and doesn’t directly reflect perceptual quality or semantic understanding. Demonstrating qualitative examples of learned grammars (visualizing the learned structures) would significantly strengthen the evaluation. The event grammar experiments, while comparing to two baselines, are limited by the synthetic nature of the second dataset and the lack of detail regarding the implementation of the comparison methods. The reliance on ID sequences in one configuration of the event learning method appears unnecessarily restrictive, potentially hindering performance and limiting the generalizability of the findings.

The theoretical contribution, while present, is somewhat obscured by the complexity of the formulation. The connection between the likelihood gain calculation and existing coherence measures is mentioned but not fully exploited for insight. The claim of efficiency via sufficient statistics requires a more detailed analysis of computational complexity compared to alternative approaches. Furthermore, the discussion of prior gain feels underdeveloped; the choice of α seems arbitrary and its impact on the results is not thoroughly investigated.

The writing is generally clear, but at times dense and difficult to follow, particularly in section 3. More illustrative examples and a more streamlined presentation of the mathematical derivations would be beneficial. While the authors acknowledge limitations, a more critical self-assessment of the contribution would be appreciated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in unsupervised learning.
*   Presents a unified framework for learning composition and reconfiguration.
*   Formalizes stochastic And-Or grammars in a general setting.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty; the core idea feels incremental.
*   Insufficient empirical validation, lacking comprehensive comparisons to strong baselines.
*   Evaluation metrics (perplexity) are not sufficiently informative.
*   Theoretical contributions require further elaboration and analysis.
*   Writing can be dense and difficult to follow.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. 

While the paper represents a reasonable effort, it doesn't meet the high standards for novelty and empirical rigor expected at NIPS. The limited experimental validation and lack of a compelling demonstration of practical significance make it difficult to justify acceptance. I encourage the authors to significantly expand the experimental evaluation, provide more detailed comparisons to state-of-the-art methods, and clarify the theoretical contributions before resubmitting. A stronger emphasis on demonstrating the advantages of learning And-Or fragments in realistic scenarios is also necessary.
