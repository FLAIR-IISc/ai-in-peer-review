### Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

**Summary:**
This paper proposes an unsupervised method for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs). The core idea is an iterative, bottom-up learning algorithm that starts with a trivial grammar (representing each training sample explicitly) and progressively generalizes it by adding "And-Or fragments." This key contribution, the And-Or fragment, unifies the learning of compositional structures (And-rules) and alternative configurations (Or-rules). The selection of which fragment to add is guided by a Bayesian objective: maximizing the posterior probability of the grammar given the data. This objective incorporates a sparsity prior to encourage compact grammars. The authors derive an efficient method to calculate the gain in posterior probability using sufficient statistics, avoiding costly re-parsing of the entire dataset at each step. The approach is presented as a general framework and evaluated on the tasks of learning event grammars from video and image grammars from real and synthetic data, showing favorable results compared to previous work.

**Strengths:**
*   The central idea of learning via "And-Or fragments" is novel and well-motivated. It provides an elegant solution to the problem of separately and often inefficiently learning compositional and alternative structures, which is a known challenge in grammar induction.
*   The formulation of the learning problem as the maximization of the grammar's posterior probability is principled. The inclusion of a sparsity prior on the grammar size is a standard and appropriate way to control model complexity.
*   The derivation of an efficient update based on sufficient statistics (the n-gram tensor and context matrix) is a significant practical contribution. It makes the search for optimal fragments computationally feasible, which would otherwise be a major bottleneck.
*   The experimental results are strong, particularly on the image grammar learning tasks, where the proposed method shows a substantial improvement in perplexity over the baseline from Si and Zhu [17].

**Weaknesses and Questions:**
Despite the promising core ideas and results, the paper is marred by a significant number of minor flaws, ambiguities, and missing details that collectively undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Precision:**
    *   **Figures:** The figures are of poor quality. Figure 1, which is crucial for understanding the iterative process, is blurry and confusing. The symbols `x` and the numbers are not explained. The diagram appears to use `X` and `Y` as new non-terminals in its illustration, while the caption refers to `N1` and `N2`, creating a disconnect. Figure 2 is similarly low-resolution. Figure 8's "Example quantized images" appear identical to the original images, failing to illustrate the quantization step.
    *   **Definitions:** Key concepts are defined vaguely. The "set of relations" in an And-rule is central to the AOG formalism but is never formally defined, only exemplified in Table 1. Similarly, the "context" used to build the context matrix `CM` in Section 3.3 is described imprecisely as "the surrounding patterns of a configuration." A formal definition is required for reproducibility.
    *   **Equations:** The likelihood gain equation is presented without sufficient derivation or explanation. The term `‖RD‖^n‖RD‖` in the denominator appears to be a typographical error. The connection to "tensor/matrix coherence measures" is asserted but not explained, leaving the reader to consult external references for a core part of the methodology.
    *   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The paper repeatedly uses phrases like "It can be shown that..." (e.g., regarding the algorithm's capability to construct any context-free AOG, and regarding the posterior gain of Or-fragments). Such claims must be substantiated with a proof or a clear citation, ideally within the paper or its supplementary material.

2.  **Methodological Gaps:**
    *   **Assumptions:** The derivation of the efficient posterior gain calculation relies on the assumption of "grammar unambiguity." This is a strong assumption, and its potential impact on the algorithm's performance and correctness when violated is not discussed at all.
    *   **Ad-hoc Modifications:** For the event grammar experiments, the algorithm was modified post-learning by changing top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes to handle videos with multiple events. This feels like an ad-hoc fix that is inconsistent with the principled posterior maximization framework proposed. Does this change not invalidate the objective function that guided the learning process?
    *   **Missing Details for Reproducibility:** Critical details are omitted. The search procedure is described as "greedy search or beam search with random restarts," but no hyperparameters (e.g., beam size, number of restarts) are provided. For the perplexity comparison in Table 3, the paper mentions that the learned distribution was "smoothed," but the specific smoothing method (e.g., Add-k, Kneser-Ney) is not specified. This is a crucial detail, as perplexity is highly sensitive to the smoothing scheme, and it is unclear if the baseline was treated identically.

3.  **Formatting and Presentation:**
    *   The paper exhibits a lack of meticulousness in its presentation. Several references are incompletely or inconsistently formatted (e.g., [1], [2], [9], [22]). The definition of variables `sa` and `so` in the prior gain equation appears after their use. While minor, these issues contribute to a general impression of carelessness.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and powerful idea—the And-Or fragment—for unified grammar structure learning.
*   The Bayesian optimization framework is sound, and the derivation of an efficient update rule is a valuable contribution.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear advance over prior work in at least one domain.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper suffers from a large number of clarity issues, including vague definitions, confusing figures, and poorly explained equations.
*   Key methodological details are missing, and strong assumptions (like unambiguity) are made without discussion, hindering reproducibility and a full assessment of the method's limitations.
*   The overall presentation lacks the polish and precision expected for a top-tier publication, with numerous minor formatting errors and unsubstantiated claims.

**Decision:**
While the core contribution of this paper is significant and the results are promising, the execution and presentation are not yet at the level required for NIPS. The accumulation of minor flaws, ambiguities, and missing technical details is substantial. A reader cannot be expected to guess the details of the context definition, the smoothing method, or the search hyperparameters, nor should they have to trust unsubstantiated claims. I therefore recommend **rejection** for this submission. The paper has strong potential, but it requires a major revision to address the numerous issues raised above before it can be considered a complete and clear scientific contribution.
