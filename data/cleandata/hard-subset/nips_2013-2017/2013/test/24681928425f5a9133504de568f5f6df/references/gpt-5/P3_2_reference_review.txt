Summary
The paper tackles unsupervised structure and parameter learning for context-free stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) across modalities (text, images, events). It proposes a bottom-up, posterior-maximizing procedure that starts from a trivial grammar that memorizes all training samples and iteratively inserts And-Or fragments—each a joint addition of an And-node with child Or-nodes whose productions point to existing nodes. The objective is a sparsity-regularized posterior using Viterbi likelihood. A key technical contribution is an efficient computation of the posterior gain of a candidate fragment from sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor counting covered configurations and a context matrix counting configuration-context co-occurrences. Under an unambiguity assumption, this yields closed-form Or-rule probabilities and a factorized likelihood gain (interpreted via coherence measures) times a prior gain that penalizes fragment size. Greedy/beam search with restarts selects fragments. Experiments on event parsing and image grammars show improved F1 and perplexity over prior AOG structure learners.

Relation to prior work
- Improves on prior AOG structure learning for images/events (e.g., Si & Zhu PAMI 2013; Si et al. ICCV 2011) by learning composition and reconfiguration jointly rather than separately and by optimizing an explicit posterior.
- Extends classic grammar induction/model merging (Stolcke & Omohundro 1994; Solan et al. 2005; Klein & Manning 2004) to AOGs with relations and to non-linguistic domains; the fragment posterior resembles MDL/Bayesian merging criteria.
- Conceptually related to structure learning in SPNs (Poon & Domingos 2011) via alternation of sum and product nodes; novelty is in fragment-level posterior gain and cross-domain grounding of And-relations (temporal/spatial).
- The unambiguity bias via Viterbi likelihood follows findings in unsupervised parsing (Spitkovsky et al. 2010; Tu & Honavar 2012).

Strengths
- Unified formalization of stochastic AOGs that cleanly separates Or (reconfigurations) and And (compositions with typed relations).
- And-Or fragment concept is elegant: it couples composition and reconfiguration, which plausibly improves robustness under data sparsity and reduces redundancy.
- Derivation of a posterior gain computable from sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor/coherence and context matrix/coherence) is insightful and could be broadly useful.
- Practical learning procedure (greedy/beam with restarts) and competitive runtimes; explicit size prior encourages compact grammars.
- Cross-domain experiments (events and images) with nontrivial improvements over published AOG learners; synthetic setup with known ground truth is valuable.

Weaknesses and questions
- Clarity and completeness:
  - Key derivations (likelihood gain factorization, coherence interpretation) and algorithmic details are deferred to the supplement; the main text currently lacks enough information for reproduction (e.g., exact construction of context definitions, tensor extraction, search heuristics).
  - It is unclear how And-rule energies/parameters over relations are learned beyond the induced structure. The paper gives closed-form Or-rule probabilities but does not specify learning/regularization of relation-specific energy terms and their optimization (Viterbi-EM?).
  - The definition and enforcement of “nonoverlapping sub-patterns” and relation constraints (e.g., spatial consistency in images) during parsing and reduction need more detail, especially for 2D.
- Assumptions and complexity:
  - The unambiguity assumption is strong; real AOGs are typically ambiguous. How sensitive are the posterior gain estimates and learned structures to ambiguity? Is there any packed-parse or variational alternative?
  - Search complexity: The space of candidate fragments explodes with n, relation types, and candidate child sets. The text mentions greedy/beam search and random restarts but provides no complexity analysis, pruning strategies, or statistics (e.g., beam width, typical fragment arity n).
  - Initialization with an exact memorization grammar could be very large; what is the memory footprint and how is parsing handled early on?
- Experimental design:
  - Event experiments use ground-truth atomic actions and timings; this removes the noisy detection step where AOGs are most useful. The synthesized “subtypes” experiment is helpful, but evaluation against more realistic detectors or weakly supervised settings would strengthen claims.
  - The modification for multiple events per video (turning top-level Ands into Ors at the end) seems ad hoc; does this change retroactively alter likelihoods/posterior choices? Please clarify.
  - Baselines focus on AOG-specific methods. Given the SPN connection and the use of Viterbi/MDL-like merging, comparisons to modern SPN structure learners and to Bayesian PCFG/HDP-PCFG or adaptor grammars (on a language-like dataset) would better position novelty.
  - For real images, perplexity is used, but the probability model over 2D images is not fully specified (smoothing procedure, parsing ambiguity handling). Without details, it is hard to interpret the magnitude of perplexity gains.
  - Ablations are missing: show the benefit of And-Or fragments vs And-only or Or-only search, effect of fragment arity n, impact of α, sa, so, and beam width.

Quality
Technically promising but incomplete in exposition. The posterior gain formulation and fragment design are plausible and supported by experiments, but key assumptions (unambiguity) and missing algorithmic details limit confidence in generality and reproducibility.

Clarity
Writing is generally clear at a high level, but figures are hard to read (e.g., Figure 1/2 formatting), and several crucial components are only sketched. Please provide pseudocode, complexity discussion, concrete definitions of “context,” and a crisp statement of inference procedures per domain.

Originality
Moderate to high within the AOG literature: jointly inducing And/Or via fragments with a posterior computed from coherence-like sufficient statistics is novel. Relative to broader grammar induction and SPN learning, the work is an interesting adaptation rather than a radical departure.

Significance
Potentially significant for compositional models across vision and events. If made robust to ambiguity and scaled to more realistic settings (noisy detections, richer relations), it could be adopted. Current empirical scope is limited but encouraging.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptual advance (And-Or fragments) with a principled objective.
- Efficient sufficient-statistic-based fragment scoring.
- Cross-domain applicability demonstrated with gains over strong AOG baselines.

Cons against acceptance
- Important derivations and algorithmic details are missing from the main paper; reproducibility concerns.
- Strong unambiguity assumption; unclear robustness.
- Limited baselines and ablations; evaluation settings simplify key difficulties (noisy primitives).

Suggestions
- Add pseudocode, complexity analysis, and a clear definition of context and relation parameter learning.
- Report ablations (fragment types, α sensitivity, beam width), ambiguity analysis, and wall-clock statistics.
- Include comparisons to SPN structure learning and Bayesian grammar induction where applicable.
- For events, test with automatically detected atomic actions; for images, clarify smoothing and parsing ambiguity handling.

Recommendation: Weak accept if the missing details are addressed in the supplement and clarified during rebuttal; otherwise borderline. Confidence: High.
