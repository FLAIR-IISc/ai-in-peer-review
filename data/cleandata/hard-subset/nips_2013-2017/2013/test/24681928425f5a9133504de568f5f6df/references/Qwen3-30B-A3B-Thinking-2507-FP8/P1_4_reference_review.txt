

# Peer Review: Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars

## Summary
This paper proposes an unsupervised approach for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars from unannotated data. The authors formalize stochastic And-Or grammars in a data-agnostic way and present an iterative learning algorithm that adds "And-Or fragments" to the grammar. These fragments unify the learning of compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules), addressing limitations of previous approaches that learned these components separately. The method optimizes the posterior probability of the grammar using sufficient statistics to efficiently compute likelihood and prior gains. The authors evaluate their approach on event grammars (for activity recognition) and image grammars (for scene parsing), showing competitive or better performance than previous methods.

The paper builds on previous work in unsupervised grammar learning [14-18] but makes significant contributions by unifying composition and reconfiguration learning, providing a data-agnostic formalization, and explicitly optimizing posterior probability rather than relying on heuristics.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with clear theoretical foundations. The sufficient statistics approach for efficiently computing likelihood gains is well-justified and significantly improves computational efficiency. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with appropriate baselines, multiple datasets, and metrics (F-measure for event grammars, perplexity for image grammars). The authors acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need to modify the algorithm for videos with multiple unrelated events) and provide appropriate ablation studies (comparing the three methods for applying the approach to event grammars).

**Originality:** The unified "And-Or fragments" approach is a significant conceptual advance over prior work that treats composition and reconfiguration learning separately. The paper clearly articulates how this differs from previous methods [14-18] and why the unification is beneficial (avoids problems with data scarcity, reduces grammar redundancy, and improves robustness). The data-agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars is also a valuable contribution that enables application across multiple domains.

**Significance:** The problem of unsupervised grammar learning is important for pattern recognition across multiple domains. The paper demonstrates practical utility through strong empirical results on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The approach could be applied to other domains beyond those tested (e.g., natural language processing), and the unified learning framework represents a meaningful advancement over previous methods.

## Weaknesses

**Clarity:** While the paper is generally well-written, the paper lacks sufficient detail on how "relations" are specified in And-rules (a critical aspect for the model's expressiveness). The paper mentions different relations for different data types (e.g., "temporal relations" for events, "spatial relations" for images), but doesn't provide concrete examples or mechanisms for defining these relations. The algorithm description in the main paper is somewhat high-level; the supplementary material would be essential for reproducibility.

**Quality:** The experimental results, while strong, could be strengthened with more thorough comparisons. The paper compares with two previous approaches on event grammars, but these approaches are limited to ID sequences (not the direct vector sequences used in the third method). More baselines would strengthen the comparison. The runtime claim ("a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples") lacks context about the specific hardware or dataset complexity.

**Significance:** The paper doesn't adequately address potential scalability limitations. The experiments use relatively small datasets (5000 samples at most), but it's unclear how the approach would scale to larger real-world datasets with millions of samples. The paper also doesn't discuss how the approach might integrate with deep learning architectures, which represent a major trend in representation learning.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions to unsupervised grammar learning. The unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations is novel and well-justified, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over previous methods. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in pattern recognition.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel unified framework for grammar structure learning
- Clear theoretical foundation with efficient computation
- Strong empirical results across multiple domains
- Data-agnostic formalization enabling broader applicability
- Careful evaluation with appropriate baselines

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Some missing details on relation specification in And-rules
- Limited discussion of scalability to very large datasets
- Could provide more thorough comparison with additional baselines

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. Clarify the specification of relations in And-rules with concrete examples.
2. Provide more details on the implementation of the sufficient statistics approach.
3. Include a more detailed discussion of scalability and potential limitations with large datasets.
4. Consider adding a brief comparison with recent deep learning approaches to grammar learning.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. The approach is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated, making it a strong candidate for NIPS.
