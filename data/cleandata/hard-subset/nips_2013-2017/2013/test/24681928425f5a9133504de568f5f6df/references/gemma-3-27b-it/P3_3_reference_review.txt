## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of unsupervised learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs), a powerful formalism for representing compositional and reconfigurable structures in data. The authors propose a novel approach that learns both the structure (grammar rules) and parameters of a SAG simultaneously, moving beyond approaches that fix the structure beforehand. Their method iteratively inserts new "And-Or fragments" into the grammar, optimizing a posterior probability based on sufficient statistics derived from the data.  The key contribution lies in unifying the learning of composition and reconfiguration, improving robustness to data scarcity, and providing a more efficient search procedure. The authors demonstrate empirical success on both event and image grammar learning tasks, achieving comparable or superior performance to existing methods.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon a rich body of work in probabilistic grammar learning, drawing connections to areas like Natural Language Processing (NLP) (stochastic context-free grammars), Sum-Product Networks (SPNs), and recent advances in image and event grammar learning.  They correctly position their work as an extension of earlier methods [14, 15, 16] for structure learning in NLP and improvements upon existing image/event grammar learning techniques [17, 18].  The explicit connection to SPNs is valuable, highlighting the broader context of their work. The reference to biclustering [22] relating to tensor coherence is insightful.

**Strengths:**

* **Unified Structure and Parameter Learning:**  The core contribution of simultaneously learning both grammar structure *and* parameters is significant. Previous work often suffers from the limitations of a fixed grammar, impacting expressiveness.
* **And-Or Fragments:** The introduction of And-Or fragments is a clever idea.  Learning compositions and reconfigurations together is a logical step towards a more robust and efficient learning process, particularly in data-scarce scenarios.  The argument for their advantage over separate And/Or fragment learning is well-reasoned.
* **Principled Optimization:**  The optimization of the posterior probability, combined with the derivation of sufficient statistics, lends a strong theoretical foundation to the approach. This separates it from more heuristic-based methods.
* **Generality:**  The formalism is explicitly designed to be data-agnostic, making it applicable to a broad range of domains, as demonstrated by the event and image grammar experiments.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on both event and image grammars are encouraging, showcasing the effectiveness of the proposed approach, especially on the more complex event grammar task with synthesized subtypes.  Outperforming [17] on the image grammar task is a notable achievement.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the concepts and algorithms.  The figures are helpful in visualizing the learning process and grammar structure.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity:** While the authors mention the use of sufficient statistics to improve efficiency, the computational cost of exploring the space of potential And-Or fragments remains a concern.  A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity (e.g., scaling with grammar size, data size, and fragment complexity) would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper only briefly mentions the hyperparameter α and its setting. A more thorough exploration of its impact on the learned grammar and performance would be beneficial. The sensitivity of the results to other parameters involved in the greedy/beam search process is also unclear.
* **Limited Comparison:** While the comparison with [17, 18] is good, it would be stronger if the authors compared their approach to more recent and sophisticated grammar learning techniques, even if they require supervision.  A discussion of why these other methods were not included would also be useful.
* **Real-World Event Data:**  The event grammar experiments use a relatively controlled dataset.  Applying the method to more complex and noisy real-world event data (e.g., from video surveillance) would provide a more compelling demonstration of its robustness.
* **Prior Choice:** The sparsity prior (penalizing grammar size) is a reasonable choice, but the justification for the specific form of the penalty (α‖G‖) is somewhat limited. A discussion of alternative priors and their potential impact would be beneficial.




**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to unsupervised structure learning of SAGs.
* The And-Or fragment concept is insightful and addresses a key limitation of previous methods.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The work is well-written and clearly explained.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity could be a limitation for large-scale datasets.
* The sensitivity of the method to hyperparameters is not fully explored.
* The comparison to related work is not exhaustive.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised grammar learning. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by strong empirical results. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the concerns outlined above (particularly a more detailed discussion of computational complexity and hyperparameter sensitivity).  Specifically, the authors should provide a more rigorous analysis of the scalability of their algorithm and explore the impact of different hyperparameters on performance.  A broader comparison to recent related work would also strengthen the paper.




**Rating:** 7/10 (Strong Accept)
