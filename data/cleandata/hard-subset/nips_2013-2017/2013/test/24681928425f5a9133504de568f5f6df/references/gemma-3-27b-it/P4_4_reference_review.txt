This paper introduces a method for unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs), extending prior work in natural language processing and image modeling to a more general framework. The core idea is to iteratively insert “And-Or fragments” into an initial trivial grammar, optimizing a posterior probability based on the training data. The authors address the challenge of efficiently learning both grammar structure and parameters simultaneously, proposing a novel fragment-based approach that unifies the search for compositions and reconfigurations. 

The paper builds on a significant body of work in stochastic grammars [1, 8, 10] and unsupervised learning [14, 15, 16, 17, 18].  It positions itself as improving upon previous structure learning approaches, particularly addressing limitations in handling large grammar spaces and the potential for error propagation when learning compositions and reconfigurations separately. The introduction of “And-Or fragments” is framed as a key innovation, aimed at achieving more robust and efficient learning. The authors connect their work to related concepts like sum-product networks and biclustering, demonstrating awareness of the broader research landscape.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality of Formalization:** The authors present a formalization of SAGs applicable to diverse data types (text, images, events), a significant step towards a unified modeling framework.
* **Principled Learning Objective:**  The approach optimizes the posterior probability of the grammar, offering a sound theoretical basis for structure learning.  The use of sufficient statistics for efficient computation is commendable.
* **Novel And-Or Fragments:** The introduction of And-Or fragments addresses a recognized weakness in previous approaches by jointly learning compositions and reconfigurations.  The justification for this design choice is persuasive.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on event and image grammars demonstrate competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods [17, 18]. The use of both synthetic and real datasets adds to the credibility of the evaluation.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and, for the most part, clearly written. The use of diagrams (e.g., Figure 1, Figure 2) effectively illustrates key concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Computational Complexity Discussion:** While the authors mention efficient computation using sufficient statistics, a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm, particularly for large datasets and high-dimensional inputs, would be beneficial. The practical scalability of the approach isn’t fully clear.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the hyperparameter α (sparsity prior) is not thoroughly investigated. A sensitivity analysis exploring the influence of α on the learned grammar structure and performance would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison with related work is somewhat limited. Including more recent and relevant approaches in the experimental evaluation could provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed method's strengths and weaknesses.  For example, newer deep learning-based approaches for compositional modeling are not considered.
* **Dataset Specifics:** The success on the image dataset relies heavily on the quantization process borrowed from [17]. The sensitivity of the results to different quantization schemes or learned feature representations is not discussed.
* **Prior Knowledge Integration:** The paper focuses on purely unsupervised learning. Exploring methods for incorporating prior knowledge, such as domain-specific constraints or expert annotations, could further enhance the performance and robustness of the approach.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to unsupervised structure learning of SAGs.
* The introduction of And-Or fragments is a significant contribution that addresses a known limitation in existing methods.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on benchmark datasets.
* The work is well-written and clearly presents the core ideas and experimental findings.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The computational complexity analysis is lacking, raising concerns about scalability.
* The sensitivity of the approach to hyperparameters is not thoroughly investigated.
* The comparison with related work could be more comprehensive.
* The reliance on specific dataset preprocessing steps (e.g., image quantization) may limit the generalizability of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear focus, a novel contribution, and convincing experimental results. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses.  The approach addresses an important problem in unsupervised learning and offers a promising direction for future research. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should provide a more detailed discussion of computational complexity, conduct a sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameter α, and broaden the comparison with related work.
