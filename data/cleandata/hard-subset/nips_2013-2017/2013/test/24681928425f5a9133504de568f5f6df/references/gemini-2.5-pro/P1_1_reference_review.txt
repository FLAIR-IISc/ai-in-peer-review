### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents an unsupervised algorithm for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs) from unannotated data. The key problem addressed is that previous grammar induction methods often assume a fixed grammar structure, which is impractical for complex domains where the space of possible rules is vast. The authors propose a bottom-up, iterative learning framework that starts with a trivial grammar (one that perfectly explains the training data without generalization) and greedily improves it.

The central contribution is the concept of an "And-Or fragment." Instead of learning compositional rules (And-rules) and alternative rules (Or-rules) separately, the proposed method searches for and incorporates fragments that combine both. An And-Or fragment defines a new composite pattern (an And-node) as a configuration of sub-patterns, where each sub-pattern can itself be one of several alternatives (modeled by Or-nodes). This unified learning of composition and reconfiguration is argued to be more robust and efficient, especially with sparse data. The algorithm selects fragments by optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar, which balances the data likelihood (approximated by the Viterbi likelihood) against a complexity prior that favors smaller grammars. The authors derive an efficient method for calculating the gain in posterior probability based on sufficient statistics collected from the data. The approach is demonstrated on two distinct tasks: learning event grammars from human activity videos and learning image grammars from collections of faces, showing significant improvements over recent, related work.

### **Review**

This is a well-written and technically solid paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of grammar induction and compositional modeling. The proposed approach is elegant, well-motivated, and supported by strong empirical results.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The core problem of learning grammar *structure* from data is fundamental and challenging. The paper's main idea—to jointly learn compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules) through "And-Or fragments"—is a novel and powerful concept. This unification directly addresses a key weakness in prior work (e.g., [17], [18]) where these two learning phases were separated, which can be brittle. This approach represents a clear advance in making grammar induction more automatic and robust. The framework's generality, applicable to different data types like temporal sequences and spatial layouts, further enhances its significance.

*   **Quality:** The technical development is sound. The method is framed within a principled Bayesian objective of maximizing the grammar posterior. A key technical contribution is the derivation of an efficient formula to calculate the posterior gain of adding a candidate fragment. By showing this gain can be computed from sufficient statistics (the n-gram tensor and context matrix), the paper makes the greedy search computationally feasible. The connection drawn between the likelihood gain and tensor/matrix coherence measures provides a nice theoretical intuition for why the method works. The experiments are well-designed and convincing. The method is compared against relevant and recent baselines on multiple datasets, including both synthetic and real-world data, and demonstrates substantial performance gains in terms of both grammar quality (KL-divergence) and predictive power (perplexity, F-measure).

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is laid out convincingly in the introduction. The AOG formalism is explained concisely, and the overall algorithm is illustrated effectively with Figure 1. The central concept of the And-Or fragment is presented with clarity. The experimental sections are also easy to follow, with clear descriptions of the setup and results.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Details of the Search Procedure:** The paper is somewhat vague on the practical details of searching for the best And-Or fragment. It mentions constructing n-gram tensors "for different values of n and different And-rule relations," which sounds computationally intensive. How are candidate relations and n-gram sizes (n) chosen or pruned? A more detailed discussion of the search heuristics and their complexity would strengthen the paper and be crucial for reproducibility. For instance, in the image grammar experiments, how is the vast space of possible relative spatial positions managed during the search?

*   **Reliance on Viterbi Likelihood:** The algorithm optimizes the Viterbi likelihood as a proxy for the true data likelihood. While this is a common practice with some empirical and theoretical justification [20, 21], it is a significant approximation. It biases the learning towards unambiguous grammars, which might not be ideal for domains where ambiguity is inherent. A brief discussion of the potential drawbacks of this choice would make the paper more complete. For example, how might the algorithm perform if multiple parse trees for a single sample have similar probabilities?

*   **Local Optima:** The learning algorithm employs a greedy search (or beam search) to optimize the posterior. This is a heuristic approach susceptible to local optima. While the paper mentions using random restarts, it would be beneficial to include a discussion on the sensitivity of the algorithm to initialization and search parameters. How consistent are the learned grammars across different runs?

*   **Missing Details in Main Paper:** Several crucial details, such as the full derivation of the likelihood gain and the algorithm's pseudocode, are deferred to the supplementary material. While understandable due to space constraints, the likelihood gain derivation is a core technical contribution. Including a more sketched-out version of the derivation or a more detailed intuition in the main text would improve the paper's self-containedness.

### **Arguments for/against acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**

1.  **High Significance:** Addresses the fundamental problem of unsupervised structure learning for a powerful class of compositional models (AOGs).
2.  **Clear Novelty:** The "And-Or fragment" is an elegant and novel idea that effectively unifies the learning of compositional and reconfigurable patterns.
3.  **Strong Technical Quality:** The approach is principled (Bayesian posterior optimization) and includes a solid technical contribution in the efficient computation of the posterior gain.
4.  **Convincing Empirical Validation:** The method is shown to significantly outperform recent, relevant baselines on multiple tasks in different domains, demonstrating its effectiveness and generality.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

1.  **Reproducibility Concerns:** Key details of the search algorithm for finding candidate fragments are underspecified, making it potentially difficult to reproduce the work.
2.  **Approximations Not Fully Discussed:** The implications of using the Viterbi likelihood approximation are not discussed in detail.
3.  **Heuristic Search:** The greedy nature of the optimization procedure is a limitation that could be discussed more thoroughly in the context of local optima.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is an excellent paper. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the core idea and the strong empirical results, far outweigh the weaknesses. The weaknesses are primarily related to clarifying implementation details and discussing limitations, which could be addressed in a final version. The paper presents a clear step forward for unsupervised learning of compositional models and is a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS.
