Summary
The paper tackles unsupervised structure and parameter learning for context-free stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), a compositional and reconfigurable model previously used in NLP, vision, and event understanding. The authors propose to start from a trivial overfitted grammar that exactly generates the training set and iteratively add “And-Or fragments”—each consisting of one new And-node with several new Or-nodes—selected to maximize a posterior objective combining Viterbi likelihood and a sparsity prior over grammar size. They derive an efficient surrogate for the posterior gain by assuming unambiguity and computing sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor over configurations and a context matrix. Greedy/beam search is used to identify fragments. Experiments on event grammars (two indoor activity datasets) and image grammars (a synthetic dataset with known ground-truth grammar and a real animal face dataset) suggest improvements over several prior methods.

Strengths
- Unification: The AOG formalization and fragment-based search are presented as modality-agnostic, with demonstrations on events and images.
- Integrated structure learning: Learning And/Or structure jointly via fragments is sensible and may reduce redundancy relative to alternating And- or Or-only searches.
- Objective-driven: Optimizing a posterior with an explicit size prior is cleaner than heuristic growth rules; the Viterbi-likelihood choice is reasonable in grammar induction.
- Efficient scoring: The use of sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor and context matrix) to approximate likelihood gain is novel and computationally attractive.
- Empirical signals: On synthetic images with ground-truth grammar, the method reports higher F-measure and lower KL; on a real image dataset it reports lower perplexity than [17]; on event data it improves F-measure over ID-based baselines.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Key assumptions not validated: The scoring derivations rely on grammar unambiguity and context-freeness within fragments. There is no empirical assessment of ambiguity in the learned grammars, nor an ablation showing how violations affect performance. This is critical given natural ambiguity in both language-like and visual data.
- Limited and uneven baselines: Event experiments compare primarily against methods that operate on ID sequences, while the proposed approach also uses action-level grounding and additional relations. Absent are stronger modern baselines such as Bayesian PCFG/HDP-PCFG/adaptor grammars, MCMC Bayesian model merging, or structured models like SPNs or hierarchical HMMs on comparable representations. For images, comparisons are limited to [17]; no comparison to alternative compositional models or SPN structure learning.
- Evaluation scope and statistical rigor: Datasets are small; results lack confidence intervals or significance tests; no error bars on synthetic F-measure/KL; no per-category analysis for real images; no diagnostics on overfitting. The perplexity evaluation includes ad hoc smoothing without details, making it hard to assess fairness.
- Heuristic search with few guarantees: The candidate-fragment search space, pruning, and beam settings are under-specified. There is no complexity analysis, no bounds on search error, and no study of sensitivity to beam width or random restarts.
- Hyperparameters and coding lengths: The size prior depends on α and coding costs sa, so, but there is no justification, tuning procedure, or sensitivity analysis. Fixing α=0.5 on synthetic images is arbitrary; for real data these choices can dominate the posterior.
- Ad hoc handling of multi-event videos: Converting top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes at the end to allow multiple events per sample changes the generative story and objectives post hoc. This needs principled modeling (e.g., mixture of independent parses or a top-level repetition operator) and ablation.
- Missing ablations: There is no quantitative evidence that And-Or fragments outperform And-only or Or-only growth when all are scored with the same posterior; no analysis of the role of the context matrix term; no measurement of ambiguity or grammar compactness versus performance.
- Clarity and completeness: Several derivations are deferred to the supplement; some figures are hard to parse and contain artifacts. The claim that any CF AOG can be built by And-Or fragments is plausible but unproven here.

Quality
Technically interesting but several claims hinge on untested assumptions (unambiguity) and choices (coding lengths). Empirical validation is not yet sufficient to establish robustness or superiority over stronger baselines. Important implementation details, sensitivity analyses, and statistical rigor are missing.

Clarity
Generally understandable, but some notational/figure issues and many essential details are in the supplement. Provide full derivations, exact scoring formulae, and rigorous pseudocode. Explain smoothing, parameter settings, and search hyperparameters.

Originality
The idea of jointly growing And/Or structure via fragments with a posterior objective and sufficient-statistics approximation is novel within AOG learning and related to but distinct from Bayesian model merging and SPN structure learning.

Significance
Potentially useful if demonstrated to scale and outperform strong baselines. Current evidence is promising but not conclusive; impact is limited by evaluation scope and assumptions.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Quantify ambiguity of learned grammars; test performance when ambiguity is present; compare Viterbi vs full likelihood.
- Provide complexity analysis and sensitivity to beam width, α, sa, so; report run time vs dataset size and n.
- Add ablations: And-only, Or-only, and full And-Or fragments under identical scoring; remove context term; different n-gram orders.
- Strengthen baselines: HDP-PCFG/adaptor grammars (events), SPN structure learning, Bayesian model merging; for images, alternative compositional models.
- Report statistical significance and variance; include per-category results on the animal dataset.
- Replace the ad hoc top-level conversion with a principled multi-event model.
- Justify the coding-length prior; explore MDL with learned code lengths.

Arguments for acceptance
- Interesting, principled formulation with a clear objective.
- Potentially general across modalities.
- Efficient approximate scoring could make structure search tractable.
- Promising empirical results on both synthetic and real data.

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong unvalidated assumptions underpin scoring; no analysis of ambiguity.
- Limited baselines and small-scale evaluations; lack of statistical rigor.
- Heuristic search without guarantees or thorough sensitivity analysis.
- Several ad hoc modeling choices and missing ablations.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The paper presents a promising idea, but it needs stronger empirical validation against competitive baselines, analysis of the unambiguity assumption, ablations, and clearer methodological details to meet the bar for NeurIPS.
