

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper proposes a novel approach to unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, introducing "And-Or fragments" as the fundamental building blocks for grammar construction. The authors present a unified framework that simultaneously learns grammar structure and parameters by optimizing the posterior probability, rather than using heuristics or separate composition/reconfiguration learning. The key innovation is the unified treatment of compositions (handled by And-rules) and reconfigurations (handled by Or-rules) through And-Or fragments, which the authors demonstrate leads to more efficient and robust learning.

The paper makes several significant contributions: a formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars agnostic to data type, explicit optimization of posterior probability with efficient sufficient statistics, a unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations, and empirical validation across event and image grammars. The authors convincingly argue that previous approaches either learn compositions and reconfigurations separately (leading to error-prone results with scarce data) or use heuristic methods that don't optimize the grammar's posterior probability.

The paper is well-written, with clear definitions (Section 2), a solid theoretical foundation (Section 3), and comprehensive experiments (Section 4). The authors properly contextualize their work against prior research, clearly distinguishing their approach from previous grammar learning methods. The experimental results are strong, showing competitive or better performance than existing approaches on both synthetic and real-world datasets for event and image grammars.

This paper excels in originality—the concept of And-Or fragments as the basic unit for grammar structure learning represents a bold new direction in grammar learning, not just an incremental improvement. The authors successfully demonstrate that this unified approach overcomes limitations of previous methods, particularly when dealing with scarce or ambiguous data. The agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that works across different data types (text, images, events) is a significant contribution to the field.

The paper also has strong quality: the technical approach is sound, the theoretical justification is clear, and the empirical validation is thorough. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the assumption that each training sample contains a single pattern) and address them with a minor algorithmic modification.

While the paper could potentially benefit from more detailed analysis of the learned grammars (beyond perplexity and F-measure) and a more comprehensive comparison to all relevant prior work, these are minor points that don't detract from the core contribution.

This paper addresses an important problem in grammatical modeling with a genuinely novel approach. The And-Or fragments concept is both elegant and powerful, representing a significant step beyond previous work in grammar learning. The authors' unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations is a major conceptual advance that could influence how grammatical models are built for a variety of data types.

I'm particularly impressed by how the authors bridge the gap between theoretical foundation and practical implementation, making their approach both principled and efficient. The demonstration that their approach outperforms previous methods on more complex datasets (where actions are divided into subtypes) shows the practical value of their unified learning framework.

Given the emphasis on novelty in NeurIPS, this paper's core contribution—And-Or fragments as the fundamental unit of grammar structure learning—deserves strong consideration. It's not merely an incremental improvement on existing methods but represents a new way of thinking about grammar structure learning.

**Strengths:**
- Novel concept of And-Or fragments as the basic building block for grammar structure learning
- Unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations
- Strong theoretical foundation with efficient sufficient statistics
- Comprehensive empirical validation across multiple data domains
- Agnostic formalization that works across different data types

**Weaknesses:**
- Limited discussion of scaling to very large datasets
- Slightly limited analysis of learned grammars beyond quantitative metrics
- Could provide more comprehensive comparison to all relevant prior work

**Recommendation:** Accept

This paper presents a genuinely novel and significant contribution to the field of grammatical modeling. The And-Or fragments concept is bold, well-justified, and demonstrates clear advantages over previous approaches. While the paper has minor weaknesses, the core contribution's novelty and significance far outweigh these limitations. The authors have made a substantial advance in unsupervised grammar learning that will likely influence future work in the field.
