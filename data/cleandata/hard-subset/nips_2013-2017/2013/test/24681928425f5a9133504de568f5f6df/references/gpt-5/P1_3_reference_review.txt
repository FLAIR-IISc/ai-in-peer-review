Summary
This paper proposes an unsupervised structure and parameter learning method for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), a context-free extension of SCFGs that also relates to decomposable sum-product networks (SPNs). The authors present a data-agnostic formalization covering terminals, And/Or nonterminals, And-rules that encode compositional relations via energy terms, and Or-rules that encode reconfigurations via probabilities. Learning maximizes a posterior over grammars with a sparsity prior exp(−α‖G‖) and uses Viterbi likelihood as a surrogate for marginal likelihood.

Starting from an initial “memorizing” grammar that exactly generates all training samples, the algorithm iteratively inserts And-Or fragments—each consisting of a new And-node and several new Or-nodes—that can generate a combinatorial family of configurations of existing nodes. This unifies the induction of composition (And) and reconfiguration (Or). To avoid expensive re-parsing, the paper derives an efficient posterior gain computation under an unambiguity assumption, decomposing the likelihood gain into two coherence factors computed from sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor over configurations within the fragment and a context matrix capturing how these configurations appear in surrounding contexts. The prior gain depends on a coding-length model (numbers of bits for And/Or RHS terms). The method uses greedy/beam search with random restarts.

Experiments on event grammars (two datasets of indoor activities) and image grammars (a synthetic animal-face sketch dataset with ground-truth grammar; and a real animal-face dataset) show competitive or superior performance vs. prior AOG structure learning methods for events [Si et al., ICCV’11] and images [Si & Zhu, TPAMI’13], measured by F for event segmentation, F/KL against true grammar on the synthetic images, and perplexity on real images.

Strengths
- Unified, data-agnostic formalism for context-free AOGs clarifies links to SCFGs and SPNs and supports multiple relation types in And-rules (temporal, spatial).
- Novel learning primitive: And-Or fragments jointly introduce compositions and reconfigurations, alleviating data sparsity and redundancy issues seen when learning And- or Or-fragments separately.
- Explicit posterior optimization with a sparsity prior, and a practical, efficient gain computation via sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor and context matrix) under Viterbi/unambiguity assumptions. The link to matrix/tensor coherence is insightful.
- Empirical gains over strong, closely related baselines on both events and images; the method appears reasonably efficient in practice.
- Clear discussion of how Or-rule probabilities are set from reductions, and a principled prior term balancing model compactness and coverage.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Dependence on unambiguity: The gain derivations and efficiency rely on an unambiguity assumption. Ambiguity is common in grammars for vision and events. Please (i) clarify the precise unambiguity condition, (ii) discuss how violations affect correctness/consistency of the gain approximation, and (iii) provide an empirical sensitivity analysis (e.g., adding ambiguous fragments or noise).
- Viterbi likelihood: While motivated by prior work, the method optimizes Viterbi rather than marginal likelihood. It would help to include an ablation comparing Viterbi vs. EM (or annealed Viterbi) for at least one dataset, and to quantify the unambiguity bias [Tu & Honavar 2012] in practice.
- Prior/coding choices: The prior gain depends on sa and so (bits per RHS symbol), but these are unspecified and likely influential. Please define them precisely, justify the coding model (e.g., MDL or universal coding), and show sensitivity to α, sa, so. If tuned, report tuning protocol.
- Fragment search: The space of And-Or fragments is large and constrained by relation types. Details on search (beam width, restarts, limits on n, relation instantiation, pruning) and complexity are missing. Provide pseudocode (promised in the supplement), runtime/scale plots vs. number of samples and terminals, and an ablation comparing And-Or fragments vs. separate And/Or learning.
- Relation specification: The approach assumes a set of relations (temporal “followed-by”, “co-occurring”, spatial relative positions). These are hand-designed and domain-specific. Please clarify how relations are parameterized (energy terms), how many relation templates are considered, and whether relation parameters are learned or fixed.
- Evaluation breadth: Event experiments rely on annotated atomic actions; for the “unsupervised” claim, discuss how the method would fare with automatically discovered (noisy) primitives (beyond the synthetic subtype split). Consider additional baselines such as hierarchical HMMs or HDP-PCFGs on the ID sequences; for images, compare to SPN structure learning (e.g., LearnSPN-style decomposable SPNs) given the stated relationship.
- Reproducibility: Some critical components are deferred to the supplement (derivations, algorithm pseudocode). Figures in the text are hard to read; define “context” precisely (what constitutes context columns), and formalize the n-gram tensor and the coherence measures. Report variance across runs and statistical significance where applicable.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, with a plausible and well-motivated objective and search procedure. However, several key derivations and design choices are relegated to the supplement; the unambiguity assumption and coding prior need clearer justification and empirical support. Experimental comparisons are positive but somewhat narrow.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with useful examples across modalities. Important definitions (context matrix, coding lengths, search hyperparameters) should be made explicit in the main text. Improve figure readability and include algorithm pseudocode.

Originality
The And-Or fragment idea and the posterior gain computation via tensor/matrix coherence appear novel in the AOG literature, going beyond prior structure-learning for AOGs [Si & Zhu 2013; Si et al. 2011] and beyond classic grammar induction (model merging, ADIOS, biclustering). The connection to SPNs is timely.

Significance
Potentially significant for multi-modal grammar induction, offering a scalable, unified mechanism to grow structure with good empirical gains. Wider adoption would benefit from stronger robustness analyses, broader baselines, and clearer reproducibility.

Questions for authors
- What precise unambiguity condition is required for the gain formula? How is ambiguity detected/handled during learning?
- How are sa and so chosen? Are they constants, or do they depend on relation arity or index coding?
- What is the computational complexity per iteration w.r.t. number of terminals, candidate relations, and fragment arity n? What beam sizes were used?
- Can the method learn relation parameters (energy terms) jointly, or are they fixed templates?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled structure-learning primitive (And-Or fragments) with efficient gain computation.
- Unified framework applicable to events and images; solid empirical improvements over close baselines.
- Clear connection to SCFGs and SPNs; contributes to grammar induction literature.

Cons against acceptance
- Heavy reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi likelihood without sufficient analysis.
- Important details (coding prior, search specifics) and derivations are missing from the main paper.
- Limited baselines and datasets; dependence on hand-designed relations.

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on clarifying assumptions (unambiguity, coding prior), adding sensitivity analyses, and strengthening experimental details and baselines. The core idea is interesting and useful, and the empirical gains are promising.
