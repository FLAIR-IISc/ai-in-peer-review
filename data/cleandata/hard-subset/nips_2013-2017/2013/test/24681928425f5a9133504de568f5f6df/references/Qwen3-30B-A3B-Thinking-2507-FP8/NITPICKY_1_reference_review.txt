

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper proposes an unsupervised approach for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars, with applications to event and image modeling. The authors present a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars, introduce a novel approach for learning grammar fragments that unify composition and reconfiguration, and demonstrate empirical results on event and image datasets.

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear contribution by formalizing stochastic And-Or grammars in a way that is agnostic to data type. The proposed And-Or fragments approach addresses a genuine limitation in previous structure learning methods, which often treat composition and reconfiguration separately. The empirical results on event grammars and image grammars show promising performance compared to previous approaches, particularly on the more complex synthetic dataset.

## Major Concerns

1. **Technical Inconsistencies in Key Formulas**: The likelihood gain formula (Section 3.3) contains a critical typographical error. The expression "∏n i=1 ∏mi j=1 ‖RDi(aij)‖‖RDi(aij)‖‖RD‖n‖RD‖" has ‖RDi(aij)‖ appearing twice in the numerator, which is clearly a mistake. This undermines the technical credibility of the paper, as it suggests a lack of careful proofreading. The correct formula likely involves a ratio of counts rather than the duplicated term.

2. **Unjustified Assumptions**: The paper assumes "grammar unambiguity" without justification or discussion of its validity in unsupervised settings. This is a significant assumption that could substantially impact the learning process, yet the authors provide no empirical evidence that their learned grammars are unambiguous, nor do they discuss how violations of this assumption would affect results.

3. **Inconsistent Algorithm Description**: The paper describes a modification to the algorithm for event grammar learning ("change the top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes") that is not mentioned in the main algorithm description (Section 3.2). This is a substantial deviation from the proposed method that requires clear justification but is presented as a minor adjustment.

4. **Problematic Prior Gain Formulation**: The prior gain formula "e−α((nsa+∑n i=1 miso)−‖RD‖(n−1)sa)" contains a potential inconsistency. The term (n-1)sa seems to incorrectly assume that each reduction from n nodes to one node saves (n-1) nodes, but the grammar size reduction should depend on the actual size of the grammar components, not just the number of nodes. The paper fails to clarify this relationship.

5. **Insufficient Experimental Validation**: The event grammar experiments use only 6 test samples (from 61 total videos), which is statistically inadequate for reliable evaluation. The paper also lacks statistical significance tests for performance differences between methods. The image grammar experiments on the synthetic dataset don't specify the exact training set sizes, making replication difficult.

6. **Vague Terminology**: The phrase "deterministic 'concatenating' relations" in Table 1 is imprecise and unhelpful for readers trying to understand the grammatical relations. The paper also frequently uses "grammar size" without defining how it's measured (number of rules? nodes? parameters?).

## Minor Issues

- Figure 1 captions are duplicated in the text, creating confusion about which figure corresponds to which description.
- The paper mentions "we restrict the types of grammar fragments" but never specifies what these restrictions are.
- The description of the n-gram tensor in Figure 2 doesn't clarify what "n" represents (is it the number of nodes?).
- The comparison with previous work lacks specific details about how their approach differs from [17,18] beyond the claims in the introduction.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a potentially interesting approach to structure learning for stochastic And-Or grammars, the numerous technical inconsistencies, unjustified assumptions, and insufficient experimental validation significantly undermine its credibility. The formula errors are particularly concerning as they suggest a lack of rigorous mathematical verification. The paper's claims about the "unified" learning approach and its superiority over previous methods aren't sufficiently supported by the technical details or empirical results.

The paper would benefit from a complete revision to address the mathematical errors, clarify the assumptions, provide more robust experimental validation, and improve the precision of its terminology. As currently written, the technical flaws are too substantial to accept at NIPS, even though the core idea has potential. The paper appears to be a work in progress rather than a complete, rigorously verified contribution.

## Recommendation

Reject. The technical errors and methodological weaknesses are too severe to be overlooked, despite the paper's potentially interesting concept and some promising empirical results. A revised version addressing these issues would be more appropriate for publication.
