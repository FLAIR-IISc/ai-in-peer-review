This paper proposes an unsupervised learning approach for stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs). SAGs are a powerful framework for modeling compositional and reconfigurable structures in various data types like images, events, and natural language. The core contribution lies in jointly learning both the structure and parameters of the grammar from unannotated data, using an iterative process that introduces new ‘And-Or fragments’ to optimize the posterior probability. This approach builds upon prior work in natural language grammar learning and recent advances in learning image/event grammars, aiming to address limitations of existing methods which either rely on predefined structures or separate learning of composition and reconfiguration.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the mathematical formulation of SAGs and the proposed learning algorithm. The motivation is clearly articulated, explaining the challenges of manually specifying grammar structures and the limitations of existing unsupervised approaches. The introduction of And-Or fragments to unify composition and reconfiguration learning is a significant conceptual contribution. The algorithm’s efficiency is addressed through the derivation of sufficient statistics and a clear discussion of the likelihood gain computation. The experimental section provides a comprehensive evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The unified learning of grammar structure and parameters, specifically using And-Or fragments, represents a novel contribution to the field.
*   **Generality:** The formalization of SAGs is presented in a data-agnostic manner, making the approach applicable to diverse data types.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The algorithm is grounded in optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar, with clear derivations for the likelihood gain and prior gain.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on event and image grammars demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach and provide a strong comparison with existing methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear explanations of the key concepts and algorithm steps.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity:** While the paper describes sufficient statistics for efficient computation, the overall algorithm appears complex, potentially making implementation and hyperparameter tuning challenging. More detailed pseudocode or implementation notes would be valuable.
*   **Scalability:** The experiments focus on relatively small datasets. The scalability of the proposed approach to significantly larger and more complex datasets is not fully investigated.
*   **Prior Selection:** The choice of the sparsity prior (α) and its influence on the learned grammar is not extensively discussed. A more detailed analysis of the impact of different prior settings would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Comparison:** While comparisons are made with existing methods, the number of baselines is relatively limited, especially in the image grammar learning experiments. Including more state-of-the-art image grammar learning methods would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Relation Type Assumption:** The experiments assume relative position for image grammars. Exploring other relation types or learning them automatically could broaden the applicability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars.
*   The concept of And-Or fragments offers a significant improvement over existing methods by unifying composition and reconfiguration learning.
*   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on both event and image data.
*   The generality of the SAG formalization makes it applicable to a wide range of data types.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The algorithm’s complexity and potential scalability issues might limit its practical applicability.
*   The limited comparison with existing methods and the lack of detailed analysis of prior selection could weaken the evaluation.
*   The assumption of specific relation types might restrict the generality of the image grammar learning experiments.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a strong contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. The proposed approach is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. While there are some minor weaknesses related to complexity, scalability, and comparison, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, specifically providing more implementation details, exploring the impact of different prior settings, and including a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods.
