

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

## Summary

This paper proposes an unsupervised learning approach for Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAOGs), which are probabilistic models capable of representing compositional and reconfigurable patterns in various data types (e.g., images, events). The authors formalize SAOGs in a data-agnostic manner and present a novel algorithm that simultaneously learns both grammar structure (rules) and parameters from unannotated data. The key innovation is the concept of "And-Or fragments," which unifies the learning of composition (via And-rules) and reconfiguration (via Or-rules) rather than learning them separately as previous approaches did. This approach differs from prior work on structure learning in natural language grammars [14-16] and image/event grammars [17-18] by optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar and using sufficient statistics for efficient computation.

## Strengths

**Technical Soundness (Quality):** The paper provides a clear formalization of SAOGs that extends previous work [1, 7, 10] while maintaining generality across data types. The proposed algorithm is theoretically grounded, with a principled derivation of the likelihood gain using sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices). The authors demonstrate how to compute the posterior gain efficiently without full re-parsing of training data, which is crucial for practical implementation. The experimental evaluation on both event grammars (using human activity videos) and image grammars (using synthetic and real animal face datasets) provides strong empirical support for the approach, showing competitive or better performance than previous methods [17, 18].

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear and significant contribution by introducing And-Or fragments as a unified approach to structure learning, which addresses limitations of previous methods that learn And-rules and Or-rules separately. This is a novel combination of familiar techniques that directly addresses the problem of data scarcity and ambiguity in grammar learning. The paper properly situates itself relative to prior work, clearly differentiating its approach from previous structure learning methods for grammars.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful figures (Figures 1-3) that illustrate the grammar learning process and the concept of And-Or fragments. The algorithm is described with sufficient detail, and the supplementary material provides additional theoretical derivations. The evaluation metrics (F-measure for event grammars, perplexity for image grammars) are appropriate for the tasks, and the experimental setup is well-justified.

**Significance:** The work addresses an important problem in probabilistic modeling—learning complex grammatical structures from unannotated data. The approach has broad applicability across domains (text, images, events), and the empirical results demonstrate tangible improvements over previous methods. The unified learning approach could be particularly valuable for practitioners working with compositional data where manual grammar specification is difficult.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Discussion of Limitations:** The paper does not thoroughly address the limitations of the approach. It would be beneficial to discuss how the method scales with very large datasets or complex grammars, and how sensitive it is to the sparsity parameter α. The authors also do not provide a detailed analysis of when the unambiguity assumption (used in Viterbi likelihood) might fail or lead to suboptimal results.

**Narrow Comparison to Prior Work:** While the paper compares to [17, 18] for image and event grammars, it doesn't adequately compare to more recent approaches in the field. For instance, there are several papers on grammar learning from the past few years that might provide a more comprehensive benchmark.

**Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper does not explore how sensitive the results are to the choice of the sparsity parameter α, which is crucial for balancing grammar complexity and fit. The authors fix α=0.5 without justification, making it difficult to assess the robustness of their approach.

**Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper provides a derivation of the likelihood gain, it lacks a formal analysis of the convergence properties of the algorithm or theoretical guarantees on the quality of the learned grammar.

## Recommendation

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel unified approach to learning SAOG structure using And-Or fragments
- Theoretical foundation with efficient computation using sufficient statistics
- Strong empirical results across two distinct domains (event and image grammars)
- Clear presentation and well-structured paper

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited discussion of limitations and parameter sensitivity
- Narrow comparison to prior work
- Lack of theoretical analysis of algorithm convergence

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling and grammar learning. The technical soundness is high, the approach is novel, and the empirical results are compelling. The weaknesses identified are important but do not undermine the core contribution of the work. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses a fundamental problem in statistical modeling with clear theoretical and practical significance.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limitations and sensitivity analysis.
