**Summary**

This paper presents an unsupervised method for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars. The core problem addressed is that prior methods either rely on a fixed grammar structure, which limits expressiveness, or learn compositional (And-rules) and reconfigurational (Or-rules) aspects separately, which can be inefficient and error-prone, especially with sparse data.

The main contribution is a novel, iterative, bottom-up learning algorithm that optimizes the posterior probability of the grammar, balancing model likelihood with a sparsity-inducing prior. The key innovation is the concept of learning "And-Or fragments" in a single step. An And-Or fragment jointly introduces a new compositional rule (an And-rule) and a set of alternative sub-patterns (via new Or-nodes), unifying the search for composition and reconfiguration. The authors derive an efficient method to calculate the gain in posterior probability for adding a candidate fragment, based on sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) collected from the training data. This general framework is demonstrated on two distinct tasks: learning grammars for human events from video and for object categories from images. The proposed method is shown to outperform relevant prior work on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Core Idea:** The central concept of learning "And-Or fragments" is a significant contribution. It provides a well-motivated and principled way to unify the learning of composition (And-rules) and alternatives (Or-rules). This addresses a clear weakness in prior structure learning approaches that handle these two aspects separately, making the proposed method more robust to data scarcity and potentially leading to more compact and coherent grammars.

2.  **Principled Optimization Framework:** The algorithm is grounded in a clear Bayesian objective: maximizing the posterior probability of the grammar `P(G|X)`. The decomposition of the posterior gain into a likelihood gain and a prior gain is clean. The derivation of an efficient way to compute this gain using sufficient statistics, avoiding repeated parsing of the entire dataset at each step, makes the greedy search computationally feasible.

3.  **Generality and Demonstrated Versatility:** The formalization of And-Or grammars is data-agnostic, allowing for different types of terminals (e.g., words, action primitives, image patches) and relations (e.g., temporal, spatial). The paper successfully demonstrates this generality by applying the same core algorithm to two very different domains—temporal event modeling and spatial image modeling—with strong results in both.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and convincing. The method achieves state-of-the-art or comparable results against relevant baselines ([17], [18]). In particular, the significant reduction in perplexity on the real-world animal faces dataset (67.5 vs 129.4) is a strong piece of evidence for the method's effectiveness. The ablation-like study on the event grammar task (comparing the three methods of application) provides useful insight into the benefits of learning different relation types simultaneously.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity on Key Technical Details:** The derivation of the likelihood gain (Equation in Section 3.3) is central to the paper's technical contribution. However, the formula is presented without much intuition in the main text, and the full derivation is relegated to the supplementary material. The connection to "tensor/matrix coherence" is mentioned but not explained, making it difficult for a reader to fully grasp the mechanics and assumptions behind this crucial calculation without referring to the supplement. A more intuitive explanation in the main paper would significantly improve clarity.

2.  **Scalability and Search Complexity:** The paper describes the search for optimal And-Or fragments as searching within n-gram tensors for sub-tensors with the highest posterior gain. This suggests a potentially massive search space, especially for larger `n` (number of children in an And-rule) and a rich set of possible relations. While the paper states the algorithm is "reasonably fast," it provides little analysis of the computational complexity or practical limitations of this search. How is the search for candidate sub-tensors implemented efficiently? What are the constraints on `n` or the types of relations that can be considered? This is a key practical concern that is not fully addressed.

3.  **Approximations and Heuristics:**
    *   The use of Viterbi likelihood is a key approximation. While justified by prior work, it introduces a bias towards unambiguous grammars. A brief discussion of the potential downsides of this choice would make the paper more complete.
    *   In the event grammar experiments, the method for handling multiple events in a single video (changing top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes at the end) feels like a post-processing heuristic rather than an integrated part of the learning framework. It's unclear how this final step interacts with the posterior optimization performed during learning.

4.  **Comparison to Related Areas:** The paper correctly identifies its relationship to grammar induction in NLP and vision. However, the connection to Sum-Product Networks (SPNs), which is briefly mentioned, could be deepened. And-Or grammars are structurally very similar to SPNs. A discussion comparing this work to structure learning algorithms for SPNs (e.g., LearnSPN by Gens and Domingos) would place the contribution in a broader context and highlight its unique aspects more clearly.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and powerful idea (the And-Or fragment) that elegantly unifies two key parts of grammar induction.
*   The approach is well-founded in a Bayesian optimization framework.
*   It demonstrates impressive empirical performance, significantly outperforming prior work on multiple challenging tasks and datasets.
*   The general framework has the potential for broad impact across different domains where compositional models are applicable.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The clarity of the core technical derivation in the main paper could be improved.
*   Practical concerns about the scalability and complexity of the search procedure are not sufficiently addressed.
*   The use of some heuristics (e.g., handling multiple events) slightly detracts from the principled framework.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This is a high-quality paper with a strong central contribution. The idea of jointly learning compositional and alternative structures via "And-Or fragments" is both novel and effective. The principled optimization framework and the strong, multi-domain empirical results make a compelling case for the method's utility and significance. While there are some weaknesses related to the clarity of technical details and unaddressed questions about scalability, these do not undermine the core contribution. The strengths of this work far outweigh the weaknesses. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in unsupervised structure learning for compositional models.

I recommend **Accept**. This work is likely to be of high interest to the NIPS community and could inspire further research in grammar induction, probabilistic modeling, and structure learning.
