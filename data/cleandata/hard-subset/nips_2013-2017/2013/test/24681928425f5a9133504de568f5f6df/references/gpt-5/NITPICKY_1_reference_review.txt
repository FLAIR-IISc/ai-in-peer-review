Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles unsupervised structure learning for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), proposing to grow a grammar from a trivial initializer by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments (a new And node with several sibling Or nodes) that jointly capture compositions and reconfigurations. The objective is a posterior balancing Viterbi likelihood and an MDL-like sparsity prior exp(−α‖G‖). The authors derive a “posterior gain” criterion using sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor (counts of configurations) and a context matrix (counts of configuration-context co-occurrences), arguing the gain factorizes into two “coherence” terms. They greedily/beam search over candidate fragments and update parses, assuming unambiguity. Experiments on event grammars (two human activity datasets) and image grammars (synthetic and real animal faces) show improvements versus prior AOG structure learning methods [17,18].

This work sits between grammar induction (e.g., PCFG induction and Bayesian model merging [14,15,16], Viterbi EM [20], unambiguity regularization [21]), AOGs for images/events [1,3,7,17,18], and SPN structure learning [10]. The emphasis on joint composition/reconfiguration is reasonable and relates to decomposable SPNs (sum/product nodes). Compared to prior AOG learning that separates And- and Or-structure, the proposed And-Or fragment is a unified primitive. The MDL-like objective echoes classical grammar induction and model merging.

Strengths
- Clear high-level motivation: learning compact, generalizable grammars without enumerating an enormous rule space; unifying composition and reconfiguration.
- Posterior-optimizing framework with a data-dependent sufficient-statistic criterion is appealing and potentially efficient.
- The And-Or fragment idea is natural and addresses failure modes of learning And-only or Or-only fragments.
- Cross-domain applicability is interesting (events and images).
- On reported tasks, improvements over [17,18] are consistent.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality
- Unambiguous grammar assumption: The posterior gain derivation and reuse of Viterbi parses depends on unambiguity, which is a very strong assumption for Or-rich AOGs. No analysis of the bias this introduces, failure modes when ambiguity is unavoidable, or any mechanism to resolve competing parses.
- Parameter learning incomplete: The paper sets Or-rule probabilities by RD counts but does not specify how And-rule “energy terms” (relations) are learned, parameterized, or estimated. Are they fixed/uniform? If learned, where are the estimators/priors? Without And-parameter learning, the “learning both structure and parameters” claim is overstated.
- Prior and coding lengths: The prior uses e−α‖G‖ with “bits” sa and so, yet how sa and so are computed is unspecified. MDL results crucially depend on the coding scheme; different schemes will change the posterior gain and search. No sensitivity analysis on α, sa, so.
- Derivations missing: The key posterior gain expression and the claimed factorization into “coherences” are relegated to supplementary material. The paper uses heavy notation (e.g., products over contexts, RD sets) but the main text contains errors/ambiguities (see below). The claimed universality (“adding only And-Or fragments can construct any context-free AOG”) is stated without proof.
- Computational complexity: Constructing n-gram tensors across relations and searching for best sub-tensors can be combinatorial. Complexity, pruning strategies, beam width, and memory footprint are not provided. The runtime claim (“minutes for 5000 samples”) is anecdotal; no timings, hardware specs, or scaling curves are shown.
- Event experiments are not fully unsupervised: atomic actions and their boundaries are annotated. This deviates from the unsupervised claim and confounds credit assignment between detection and grammar learning. The synthetic second dataset injects random subtypes; realism and implications are unclear.
- Evaluation methodology gaps:
  - Events: Only F-measure with purity/inverse purity on 6 test samples is reported; no variance/confidence intervals or statistical tests; no ablations (e.g., fragment type, unambiguity, Viterbi vs. full likelihood), no sensitivity to α. Baselines restricted to ID-sequence methods [15,18], not to more recent structure learners (e.g., SPN structure learning).
  - Images: Perplexity metric and smoothing are under-specified (which smoothing? effect on risk?). How are image probabilities computed from AOGs with spatial relations? What is the length normalization? The synthetic evaluation uses F and KL-divergence vs. a “true grammar,” but its specification is missing.

Clarity
- Several figures and equations are garbled/unreadable in the draft (e.g., Fig. 1–2 contain overlain numerals “a1 2 3,” repeated “S S,” misaligned symbols; Table 1 cramped). The context matrix/tensor examples are very hard to parse.
- Notation inconsistencies: ‖X‖ is used for cardinality and also appears as a norm; RD and RDi(aij) are introduced abruptly; “context” is never precisely defined (what constitutes the boundary or surrounding for events/images?). Equation for likelihood gain includes terms like ‖RD‖n‖RD‖ which appears erroneous/dimensionality-wise unclear.
- The change “top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes before termination” is a heuristic hack with significant modeling implications but minimal explanation; it also breaks the generative semantics assumed during training.
- Algorithmic details absent: no pseudocode in the paper, no description of beam width, stopping tolerance, or candidate generation, and no proof/sketch that greedy insertion monotonically improves the objective.

Originality
- The And-Or fragment as a joint primitive is a reasonable synthesis, but connections to and differences from SPN structure learning (e.g., LearnSPN, Gens & Domingos 2013; Rooshenas & Lowd 2014) are not discussed. The objective is MDL-like; Bayesian model merging and adaptor grammar literature are insufficiently compared. The “coherence” connection to [22] is interesting but underdeveloped.

Significance
- Potentially useful across modalities, but current evidence is limited and partially supervised. Missing strong baselines (SPN learners, modern grammar induction) and ablations make it hard to assess impact. The unambiguity assumption and incomplete parameter learning may limit adoption.

Formatting and references
- Reference list is inconsistent and incomplete (missing years/pages/venues, capitalization errors; e.g., Poon & Domingos title; missing Gens & Domingos 2013; stray “9,” at end).
- Hyphenation “And‐Or,” “AndOr,” and “And-Or” used inconsistently. Many typography issues: repeated symbols, stray numerals in figures, “Table 1” not legible.

Pros
- Sensible objective combining likelihood and sparsity prior.
- Joint composition/reconfiguration via And-Or fragments.
- Cross-domain demonstration (events, images).
- Empirical improvements over selected AOG baselines.

Cons
- Strong unambiguous-grammar and Viterbi assumptions without analysis.
- And-rule parameter learning unspecified; prior coding unclear.
- Key derivations and claims not in paper; figures/equations error-prone.
- Experimental design lacks ablations, strong baselines, statistical rigor; event setting not fully unsupervised.
- Algorithmic details, complexity, and reproducibility lacking.

Questions for authors
1) How are And-rule energy terms parameterized and estimated? Are relations learned or fixed?  
2) Provide the full derivation of the posterior gain and correct the likelihood gain equation; clarify RD notation and context definition.  
3) What is the coding scheme for sa and so? Sensitivity to α?  
4) Empirically, how often is the unambiguity assumption violated? What happens then?  
5) Complexity: report wall-clock, memory, and scaling vs. n-gram order and number of relations.  
6) Replace the “final And→Or hack” with a principled multi-event model, or justify it properly.  
7) Add modern baselines (SPN structure learners) and ablations.

Recommendation: Reject (confidence: high). The core idea is promising, but the paper has substantial technical, clarity, and experimental shortcomings that need major revision.
