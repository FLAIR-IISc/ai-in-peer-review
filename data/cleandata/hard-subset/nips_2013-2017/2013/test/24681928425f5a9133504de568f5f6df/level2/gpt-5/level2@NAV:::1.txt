This paper proposes a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) and an unsupervised procedure that jointly learns structure and parameters by greedily adding And-Or fragments to maximize a sparsity-regularized posterior (with Viterbi likelihood). The key idea is to search over fragments that simultaneously introduce compositions (And) and reconfigurations (Or), computing posterior gain efficiently via sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) under an unambiguity assumption. Evaluation on event parsing and image modeling shows comparable or improved performance over prior AOG structure learners for events [Si et al. ICCV’11] and images [Si & Zhu PAMI’13], and sensible behavior on a synthetic grammar-identification task. The work relates to Bayesian model merging and iterative biclustering for PCFG induction, and to sum-product networks in its decomposability.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled objective with a compactness prior; Viterbi training is well-motivated and commonly effective in grammar induction.
- And-Or fragments unify the search for compositions and alternatives, plausibly mitigating data sparsity and redundancy seen when learning And/Or separately.
- Efficient posterior-gain computation via sufficient statistics is elegant and practically useful.
- Cross-domain evaluation (events, images) supports the modality-agnostic formalization; improvements over established baselines are non-trivial.

Weaknesses:
- The unambiguity assumption is strong; many AOG applications are inherently ambiguous. The impact on correctness and bias is not quantified.
- Comparisons are somewhat narrow (mostly to [17,18]); it would help to include more recent or stronger grammar induction baselines and end-task metrics (classification/segmentation) beyond perplexity/F-measure.
- Limited analysis of sensitivity to α, fragment arity n, and encoding costs (sa, so); no statistical significance testing.
- Reproducibility details are thin (search beam/greedy settings, initialization, runtime/memory, feature quantization for images).
- The approach still depends on discretization/quantization choices, which may dominate performance on images.

Quality: The derivations for posterior gain and optimal Or-rule parameters are sound given the assumptions; the algorithmic framework is coherent. Empirics support claims but are modest in scale and breadth.

Clarity: Generally well-organized; some notation (e.g., context matrices) is dense and relegated to supplement. Figures could be clearer.

Originality: The And-Or fragment search and unified posterior optimization represent a meaningful advance over prior heuristic structure learners.

Significance: Moderate. The method is broadly applicable and shows measurable gains, but stronger evidence on scalability and ambiguous settings is needed.

Pros for acceptance: principled, novel fragment search; cross-domain applicability; improved results. Cons: strong assumptions; limited baselines/analysis.

Suggestions: (1) Ablations: And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or; effect of n and beam width. (2) Robustness to ambiguity (e.g., entropy regularization, annealed EM). (3) More baselines and significance tests. (4) Report end-task metrics and runtime. (5) Clarify reproducibility and parameter choices.
