

This paper proposes an unsupervised approach for learning the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars, presenting a unified formalization that is agnostic to data type. The authors introduce a novel learning algorithm that iteratively induces grammar fragments by optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar, using And-Or fragments to unify the learning of compositions and reconfigurations.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a clear and formal definition of stochastic And-Or grammars that works across different data types (text, images, events), which is a notable advancement over domain-specific approaches. Second, the proposed learning algorithm explicitly optimizes the posterior probability rather than relying on heuristics, with efficient computation of likelihood and prior gains using sufficient statistics. Third, the use of And-Or fragments to unify composition and reconfiguration learning is theoretically sound and addresses limitations in previous approaches that treated these as separate problems.

The technical quality is strong. The formalization is clear and well-motivated, with careful distinction between And-rules and Or-rules. The derivation of likelihood and prior gains is mathematically rigorous, and the algorithm is well-described with sufficient detail for reproduction. The experiments are well-designed, with appropriate baselines and thorough evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets for event grammars and image grammars. The results demonstrate clear improvements over previous approaches, with the proposed method achieving better performance on both datasets, particularly on the more challenging synthetic dataset where actions were divided into subtypes.

The paper is well-written and organized, with helpful examples and figures (Figures 1-3) that clarify the learning process and algorithmic components. The related work is appropriately cited and discussed, showing how the paper builds upon and differentiates from previous approaches in natural language processing, image grammars, and event grammars.

However, there are several areas for improvement. The paper claims to be data-type agnostic but only evaluates on two domains (events and images), without demonstrating the approach on text data as claimed. This weakens the assertion of broad applicability. The paper mentions that the algorithm was slightly modified to handle videos with multiple unrelated events, but provides no details on this modification or how it affects the generality of the approach. The paper also doesn't thoroughly discuss the trade-offs of using Viterbi likelihood instead of standard likelihood, despite citing [20, 21] as justification.

The computational complexity of the approach isn't fully addressed. While the paper mentions the prototype runs "within a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples," it doesn't provide scaling analysis or discuss how the algorithm would perform on larger datasets or with more complex grammars. The choice of greedy search versus beam search isn't thoroughly justified, and the paper doesn't discuss how these choices might affect the quality of the learned grammars.

The paper claims the third method for event grammar learning (applying the approach directly to vector sequences without restricting the learning order of relations) leads to the best performance, but doesn't fully explain why the ordering matters or provide a comprehensive comparison of the three methods beyond the results. The claim that the division of sitting/standing into subtypes "actually helps" the third method to avoid learning erroneous compositions is interesting but lacks theoretical justification.

The significance of the paper is strong, as it addresses a meaningful problem in unsupervised structure learning with clear applications in multiple domains. The unified formalization and learning approach could potentially bridge different areas of structured data modeling. The empirical results demonstrate a clear advance over previous approaches, particularly on the more challenging dataset.

Overall, this is a strong paper with solid technical contributions, good experimental validation, and clear significance. The minor weaknesses identified are easily fixable in a revised version. The paper advances the state of the art in unsupervised structure learning of grammatical models and makes a valuable contribution to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
