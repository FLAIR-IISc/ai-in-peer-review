This paper presents a novel, unsupervised method for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs). The authors propose a unified, data-agnostic formalization of AOGs and an iterative, bottom-up learning algorithm. Building on prior work in grammar induction, the core contribution is the introduction of "And-Or fragments," which elegantly unifies the learning of compositional structures (And-rules) and reconfigurable alternatives (Or-rules). The algorithm starts from a trivial grammar and greedily adds these fragments to optimize the grammar’s posterior probability, which combines a Viterbi likelihood term with a sparsity prior to encourage compact models.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The concept of the "And-Or fragment" is a significant and novel contribution. It provides a principled solution to the challenging problem of jointly learning composition and reconfiguration, which previous methods often handled separately or heuristically. This unified approach makes the learning process more robust to data scarcity and ambiguity. The framework's data-agnostic design is a major strength, increasing its potential impact across different fields.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The optimization of the grammar's posterior probability provides a solid theoretical foundation. The derivation of the posterior gain using sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) is clever and makes the search for optimal fragments computationally feasible.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the algorithm is explained logically, and the figures effectively illustrate the learning process and the structure of the fragments.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The claims are strongly supported by comprehensive experiments on two distinct and challenging tasks: learning event grammars from video and image grammars from both synthetic and real datasets. The proposed method consistently achieves comparable or significantly better performance than relevant state-of-the-art approaches.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of its limitations. For instance, the greedy search strategy may be susceptible to local optima. A brief analysis of this possibility or the effect of random restarts would be valuable.
*   While the use of Viterbi likelihood is empirically justified, a short discussion on its theoretical implications (e.g., the induced unambiguity bias) would add further depth.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, significant, and well-executed contribution to unsupervised structure learning. The strengths—a principled framework, a key methodological innovation, and strong empirical results—far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The work is likely to be influential and inspire further research into learning compositional models. I strongly recommend acceptance.
