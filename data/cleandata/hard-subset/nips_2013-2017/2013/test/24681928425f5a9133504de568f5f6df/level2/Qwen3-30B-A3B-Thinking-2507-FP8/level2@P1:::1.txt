

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and proposes an unsupervised approach to learning both the structure and parameters of such grammars. The authors address a significant challenge in grammatical inference: learning the grammar structure (i.e., the set of valid rules) from unannotated data, which has typically been difficult due to the exponential search space and the need for manual specification of grammar rules.

The paper's main contribution is a learning algorithm that starts from a trivial initial grammar and iteratively induces And-Or fragments (unified compositions and reconfigurations) to optimize the posterior probability of the grammar. This approach differs from previous work that handles composition (And-fragments) and reconfiguration (Or-fragments) separately, which can be inefficient and error-prone with scarce data. The authors provide a theoretical foundation for their approach by deriving sufficient statistics that enable efficient computation of the posterior gain when adding new grammar fragments.

The paper demonstrates the approach on two different domains: event grammars (learning from activity videos) and image grammars (learning from image datasets). The experimental results show that their method achieves comparable or better performance than previous approaches, particularly on more complex datasets where previous methods struggle.

Strengths:
1. The unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars is well-motivated and clearly presented, making the approach applicable across different data types (images, events, etc.).
2. The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear derivation of sufficient statistics that make the learning process computationally feasible.
3. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, with well-designed experiments on both synthetic and real datasets, and meaningful comparisons to relevant baselines.
4. The approach addresses a practical limitation of previous work (the need for manual grammar specification) and provides a principled way to learn compact, generalizable grammars.
5. The writing is clear and well-organized, with helpful examples (Table 1, Figure 1) that illustrate the concepts.

Weaknesses:
1. While the paper mentions that the approach assumes "a single pattern per sample," this limitation is not thoroughly discussed in relation to the event grammar experiments where videos may contain multiple unrelated events. The authors make a small modification to the algorithm to handle this, but a more detailed analysis of how this affects the grammar learning process would strengthen the paper.
2. The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison of computational complexity with previous approaches. While the authors mention their algorithm runs "within a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples," it would be helpful to understand how the runtime scales with dataset size and complexity.
3. The connection to related work on sum-product networks (SPNs) could be strengthened. The paper mentions SPNs in the introduction but doesn't explicitly discuss how their approach relates to or differs from SPN learning techniques.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. The unified approach to learning both composition and reconfiguration is novel and well-justified, and the experimental results provide convincing evidence of its effectiveness. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the approach is generalizable across different data types, which is a significant advantage over domain-specific methods.

I find the paper to be of high quality, with technically sound contributions that advance the state of the art in grammatical inference. The clarity of presentation makes the technical content accessible to readers outside the immediate field. The originality is well-justified through careful comparison with prior work, and the significance is demonstrated through both theoretical contributions and empirical results.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it presents a principled approach to a challenging problem in unsupervised learning of structured representations. The results are significant enough to be of interest to a broad audience in machine learning, and the method has potential applications in multiple domains beyond those tested in the paper.

My recommendation is to accept this paper. The strengths of the paper clearly outweigh the minor weaknesses, and the contribution is both technically sound and practically relevant. The authors have provided a clear, well-motivated, and well-executed approach to an important problem in the field.
