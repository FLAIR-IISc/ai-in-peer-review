This paper proposes an unsupervised approach to learning stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs), extending previous work on structure and parameter learning. The key contribution lies in a unified formalization of SAGs applicable to diverse data types and an iterative learning algorithm that simultaneously induces compositions and reconfigurations via And-Or fragments, optimizing posterior probability. The work is evaluated on event and image grammars, achieving competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the work within the existing literature on stochastic grammars and unsupervised learning. The formalization of SAGs is presented concisely and is generalized effectively. The algorithmic framework is explained logically, and the introduction of And-Or fragments is a significant improvement over separate composition/reconfiguration learning. The experimental results, while limited in scope, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on two distinct data types.

A significant strength is the unification of composition and reconfiguration learning through And-Or fragments. This addresses a key weakness in prior approaches, especially in data-scarce scenarios. The theoretical grounding of the algorithm, particularly the derivation of the posterior gain based on sufficient statistics and the use of coherence measures, adds credibility and demonstrates a thorough understanding of the underlying principles.  The comparisons to previous work, although not exhaustive, are pertinent and show improvements in performance. The attempt to ground the grammar learning to atomic actions in event grammar learning is also a good design choice and improves performance.

However, several aspects could be strengthened. The discussion of the prior probability P(G) and the parameter α is limited. A more detailed explanation of how α is selected or tuned would be beneficial. While the paper mentions sparsity priors, the specifics of the prior itself aren’t fully elaborated.  The experimental section could benefit from a more comprehensive analysis. The performance gains on the synthetic image dataset are significant, but a discussion of the limitations of this dataset (e.g., its simplicity) would be useful. The real image dataset experiments only report perplexity; including other metrics like segmentation accuracy or qualitative examples of learned grammars would provide a more complete evaluation. The comparisons to existing methods are somewhat limited. Including a more comprehensive set of baselines, or a more detailed comparison to the most relevant recent work, would strengthen the claims of improvement.  Furthermore, the claim that the algorithm terminates when no more grammar fragments can be found that increases the posterior probability could benefit from a discussion of convergence guarantees or potential issues with local optima.  The justification for the choice of And-Or fragments specifically could be further developed – is there a theoretical reason why they are superior to other possible fragment types, or is it primarily empirical?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to jointly learning structure and parameters of SAGs.
*   Unified formalization applicable to different data types.
*   Strong theoretical grounding and efficient computation of posterior gain.
*   Competitive or superior performance on event and image learning tasks.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
*   Insufficient detail on prior specification and hyperparameter tuning.
*   Limited comparison to recent state-of-the-art methods.
*   Lack of discussion of convergence properties.




Overall, this is a solid contribution with a novel approach to a challenging problem. The strengths of the work – the unified framework, theoretical grounding, and initial experimental results – outweigh the weaknesses.  Addressing the points raised above, particularly expanding the experimental evaluation and providing more details on the prior and hyperparameter tuning, would significantly strengthen the paper.  It is recommended for acceptance, contingent on minor revisions addressing these points.
