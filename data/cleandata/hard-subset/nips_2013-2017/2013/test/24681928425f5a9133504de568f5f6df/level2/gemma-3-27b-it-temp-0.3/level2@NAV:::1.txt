## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper presents a novel approach to unsupervised learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs), aiming to learn both the structure and parameters of these grammars from unannotated data. The core contribution lies in a unified formalization of SAGs applicable to diverse data types and an iterative learning algorithm that induces compositions and reconfigurations simultaneously, optimizing posterior grammar probability. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on event and image grammars, achieving comparable or superior performance to existing methods.

The paper builds upon previous work in stochastic grammars, particularly extending structure learning techniques from natural language processing to the domains of images and events. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous approaches, such as the need for manual grammar specification or separate learning of compositions and reconfigurations. Their proposed And-Or fragment learning addresses these issues by jointly optimizing structure and parameters, leveraging sufficient statistics for efficiency. The formalization of SAGs as data-type agnostic is a valuable contribution, increasing the model’s versatility.

The strengths of the paper include a clear problem definition, a well-motivated approach, and a thorough experimental evaluation. The experiments on both event and image grammars demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed method. The comparison against existing approaches, including [17, 18], provides a solid benchmark. The detailed explanation of the likelihood gain computation and the use of tensor coherence measures are particularly commendable.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification in a few areas. While the authors mention sparsity priors, the specific implementation details (e.g., the value of α) could be elaborated upon. The discussion of the algorithm’s computational complexity would also be valuable, particularly concerning scalability to larger datasets and more complex grammars.  Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the limitations of the approach, such as its sensitivity to initialization or potential issues with local optima during the iterative learning process.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and unified approach to SAG learning.
*   Addresses limitations of existing methods.
*   Strong experimental results on diverse datasets.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of computational complexity.
*   Lack of detailed explanation of hyperparameter settings.
*   Insufficient discussion of potential limitations.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed approach represents a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. While some areas could benefit from further elaboration, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above.
