Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) that is agnostic to data modality and an unsupervised method to jointly learn grammar structure and parameters. The method starts from a trivial grammar that exactly generates the training set and iteratively adds And-Or fragments—each containing one And-rule coupled with sibling Or-rules—selected to maximize a MAP objective with a sparsity prior. To make search efficient, the work assumes Viterbi likelihood and local unambiguity to derive a closed-form posterior gain for candidate fragments using sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor over child choices and a context matrix over surrounding patterns; the gain factorizes into tensor/matrix coherence terms and a prior term penalizing fragment size relative to the number of reductions. Experiments on event parsing (two activity datasets) and image modeling (synthetic animal faces with ground-truth grammar; real animal faces) show comparable or better performance than prior approaches to AOG structure learning on images [Si & Zhu 2013] and events [Si et al. 2011], and improvements over older NLP-style structure learning baselines [Stolcke & Omohundro 1994; Solan et al. 2005; Tu & Honavar 2008] where applicable. The framework connects to stochastic grammars and PCFGs [Baker 1979; Klein & Manning 2004], sum-product networks [Poon & Domingos 2011], and prior AOGs for images and events [Zhu & Mumford 2006; Zhao & Zhu NIPS 2011; Ivanov & Bobick 2000; Ryoo & Aggarwal 2006].

Strengths:
- Unified, modality-agnostic formulation of context-free stochastic AOGs, with clear mapping to natural language, image, and event domains.
- Principled objective (MAP with sparsity prior) and a coherent learning story: And-Or fragments capture compositions and reconfigurations jointly, addressing redundancy and data sparsity issues observed when searching for And- or Or-fragments in isolation.
- Efficient evaluation of candidate fragments via sufficient statistics and a factorized posterior gain; the linkage to tensor/matrix coherence provides useful intuition.
- Empirical evidence across two modalities. For events, multiple application variants are explored (IDs vs. grounded actions; ordering of co-occurrence vs. followed-by relations), with the unified approach performing best on the more challenging setting. For images, both synthetic (with precision/recall and KL divergence to a known grammar) and real data (perplexity) show improvements over [Si & Zhu 2013].
- Practicality: beam/greedy search with random restarts; prototype scales to thousands of samples with >10 terminals each.

Weaknesses and points for clarification:
- Dependence on Viterbi training and an unambiguity assumption to compute posterior gains. Ambiguity is common in grammars for real data; discussion of how violations impact estimates, and any mitigation (e.g., beam-based Viterbi or unambiguity regularization [Tu & Honavar 2012]) would strengthen the technical soundness claims.
- The prior relies on coding constants sa and so. The paper would benefit from specifying how these are chosen and sensitivity analyses to α, sa, so. Without this, the strength of the sparsity prior is opaque and could materially affect structure selection.
- Search restricts fragment forms and uses greedy/beam heuristics; while reasonable, an ablation comparing And-Or fragment search to separate And- or Or-fragment search (and to a nonparametric baseline such as HDP-PCFG [Liang et al. 2009]) would better isolate the contribution of the unified fragment design.
- Event experiments rely on annotated atomic actions (and a synthetic subtype split) rather than fully unsupervised discovery of terminals. This is a common assumption in AOG work, but the scope of “unsupervised” should be stated precisely: unsupervised structure and parameter learning given observed terminals and relations. Clarify what supervision is required to define the relation predicates (followed-by, co-occurs, spatial relations).
- Scalability details are limited. Constructing n-gram tensors and context matrices could be costly as n and the number of candidate relations grow. Complexity bounds and memory considerations would help, as would reporting beam width, number of restarts, and candidate counts per iteration.
- Evaluation breadth. Image experiments use perplexity, but not downstream tasks (parsing accuracy, segmentation, detection) where AOGs are often applied; event results focus on F-measure at the temporal level without per-class breakdowns or confidence intervals. Including error bars and additional metrics would increase confidence.

Quality:
Overall technically sound with reasonable approximations, but the reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi estimates should be more thoroughly justified or empirically stress-tested. The posterior gain derivation and sufficient-statistics formulation are compelling; more proof sketches in the main text (not only the supplement) would aid verification.

Clarity:
Generally well-organized and clear. The fragment gain computation, context definition, and the role of sa/so deserve more explicit exposition. Figures would benefit from cleaner captions and font sizes; an algorithm box summarizing one learning iteration would help.

Originality:
The And-Or fragment idea that jointly induces compositions and reconfigurations, coupled with a closed-form posterior-gain estimator via coherence measures, appears novel relative to prior AOG structure learning [Si & Zhu 2013; Si et al. 2011] and classic PCFG induction approaches.

Significance:
A useful step toward practical, modality-agnostic structure learning for AOGs. If robustified against ambiguity and scaled further, the approach could influence structured perception in vision and event understanding, and possibly inspire analogous methods in NLP.

Suggestions for improvement:
Essential:
- Clarify and empirically evaluate the impact of ambiguity on the posterior gain estimates; consider incorporating unambiguity regularization [Tu & Honavar 2012] or a beam-Viterbi variant.
- Specify and analyze sensitivity to α, sa, so; provide guidance or a data-driven scheme for setting them.
- Provide complexity analysis and empirical scaling curves (candidates per iteration, runtime vs. number of terminals and n).

Nice-to-have:
- Ablations: And-Or vs. separate And/Or fragment search; effect of fragment arity n; effect of beam width and restarts.
- Additional baselines (e.g., HDP-PCFG for sequences) and downstream evaluations (segmentation/parse accuracy on images, per-class event metrics).
- Qualitative parses and learned fragments for both domains to illustrate interpretability.

Pros:
- Unified framework; principled MAP objective; efficient sufficient-statistics-based gain.
- Joint induction of compositions and reconfigurations.
- Multi-domain validation with improved results.

Cons:
- Unambiguity/Viterbi assumptions underexplored.
- Hyperparameter/prior specification unclear.
- Limited scalability analysis and evaluation breadth.

Recommendation: Weak accept.
