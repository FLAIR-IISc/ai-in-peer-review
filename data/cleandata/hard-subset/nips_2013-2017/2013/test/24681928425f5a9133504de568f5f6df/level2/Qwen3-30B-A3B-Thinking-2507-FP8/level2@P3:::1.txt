

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and an unsupervised learning approach for simultaneously inferring grammar structure and parameters. The work addresses a significant gap in prior methods that either manually specify grammar structures or learn compositions and reconfigurations separately, which can be inefficient and error-prone with scarce data. The core innovation is the introduction of "And-Or fragments" that systematically unify the learning of both compositional (And) and alternative (Or) structures, enabling efficient optimization of the grammar's posterior probability.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars as data-agnostic is well-motivated and clearly presented, with Table 1 and Figure 1 effectively illustrating how the framework applies to diverse domains (natural language, events, images). The algorithmic contribution—iteratively adding And-Or fragments to optimize the posterior probability—shows technical rigor. The derivation of efficient sufficient statistics for computing likelihood and prior gains (Section 3.3) is particularly strong, avoiding the computational intractability of re-parsing all data at each step. Empirical validation is thorough: experiments on event grammars (using human activity videos) and image grammars (synthetic and real animal faces) demonstrate consistent improvements over prior work [17, 18], with the proposed method outperforming competitors on more complex datasets where prior approaches fail. The ablation study (Table 2) convincingly shows the superiority of grounding grammars to atomic actions and learning relations simultaneously.

The work excels in originality. It clearly distinguishes itself from prior structure learning efforts: [14–16] focus on natural language grammars with separate composition/reconfiguration learning, while [17, 18] address image/event grammars but lack a unified framework. The And-Or fragment concept resolves key limitations (e.g., data scarcity for compositions, redundancy from separate Or-rule learning) and is proven sufficient for constructing any context-free And-Or grammar. The connection to sum-product networks [10] is appropriately contextualized without overclaiming.

Minor weaknesses exist. The scalability discussion is brief; while the paper notes "reasonable speed" for 5,000 samples, it lacks analysis of runtime with larger datasets (e.g., 100k+ samples), which is relevant for real-world applications. The handling of videos containing multiple unrelated events (Section 4.1) via post-hoc top-level Or-node conversion feels slightly ad-hoc; a more principled approach to modeling event sequences would strengthen the method. Additionally, the comparison to SPNs [10] could be expanded—though the paper rightly emphasizes grammatical structure, a brief discussion of how And-Or grammars differ from SPNs in modeling compositional hierarchies would clarify the novelty.

The paper is well-written, with clear organization and sufficient detail for reproducibility. Theoretical claims are well-supported by derivations (supplementary material) and empirical results. The significance is evident: the unified framework enables learning grammars across diverse data modalities without domain-specific re-engineering, addressing a core challenge in probabilistic modeling. The performance gains on event grammars (Table 2) and image grammars (Table 3) demonstrate tangible advances over [17, 18].

The work aligns with NIPS' focus on principled probabilistic modeling and structured representations. It advances the state of the art by providing a theoretically grounded, efficient method for unsupervised structure learning that generalizes beyond prior domain-specific approaches. The minor scalability and event-sequence handling points are reasonable suggestions for revision but do not undermine the paper's core contribution.

In summary, the paper makes a strong contribution to structured probabilistic modeling with clear technical novelty, sound empirical validation, and significant practical implications. The proposed method is both theoretically rigorous and empirically effective, offering a valuable tool for modeling compositional data. The minor suggestions for improvement are not essential to the paper's validity but would enhance its impact.
