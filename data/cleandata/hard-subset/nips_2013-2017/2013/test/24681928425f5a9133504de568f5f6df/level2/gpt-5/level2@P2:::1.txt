Summary
The paper proposes a unified formalization of stochastic context-free And-Or grammars (AOGs) and an unsupervised structure-learning algorithm that jointly induces compositions (And) and reconfigurations (Or) via And-Or fragments. The learning objective maximizes a sparsity-regularized posterior: a Viterbi likelihood over training data coupled with a prior penalizing grammar size. At each iteration, the method proposes candidate And-Or fragments (A → O1…On with Oi → alternatives), evaluates their posterior gain using sufficient statistics (an n-gram tensor for fragment-internal coherence and a context matrix for external coherence), and greedily/with beam search adds the best fragment, performing reductions in the training parses. The approach is instantiated for event grammars (temporal relations: followed-by and co-occurring) and image grammars (spatial relations). On two event datasets and two image datasets (synthetic with known ground truth and a real animal-face dataset), it reports comparable or better accuracy than prior work on events [15,18] and images [17], with improved F-measure and perplexity, respectively.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on stochastic grammars for images and events [1,3,7], unsupervised grammar induction (e.g., Bayesian model merging [14], ADIOS [15], biclustering PCFG [16]), and connections to SPNs [10]. It improves over prior AOG structure learning for images [17] and events [18] by adding And and Or structure jointly via And-Or fragments rather than in separate phases or assuming fixed rule sets. The posterior-optimization perspective with a sparsity prior is also in the spirit of model merging/MDL, but the specific sufficient-statistic-based gain computation and the fragment search are novel in the AOG setting.

Strengths
- Clear, general idea with a unified fragment type that couples composition and reconfiguration, addressing data scarcity and redundancy issues seen when learning And- or Or-parts separately.
- Posterior-based criterion with an explicit size prior, operationalized via efficient sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor and context matrix). The analysis linking likelihood gain to tensor/matrix “coherence” is insightful.
- Empirical evidence across two modalities (events and images), including a synthetic image setup with ground-truth grammar, showing consistent gains over relevant baselines.
- Practicality: simple greedy/beam search with random restarts; runtimes seem reasonable.

Weaknesses and questions
- Unambiguity and Viterbi assumptions: The posterior gain derivation assumes unambiguous parses and uses Viterbi likelihood. These biases can favor unambiguous grammars and may understate uncertainty. How sensitive is performance to ambiguity in the target domain? Are there failure cases where full marginal likelihood (even approximately) changes fragment ranking?
- Parameterization of And-rules and relations: The text states And-rule probabilities are specified by “energy terms on relations,” but the learning of these energies and their role in gain computation are not detailed. In the fragment, A → O1…On appears deterministic; P(A → a1…an) is used in the gain, but only Or-rule probabilities are given in closed form. Please clarify: (i) how relation energies are parameterized/estimated; (ii) how they enter likelihood and the sufficient statistics; (iii) whether relation types are fixed per fragment or learned.
- Prior and coding costs: The prior gain uses sa and so (bits per RHS symbol). How are these chosen? Are results sensitive to α, sa, so? A sensitivity analysis would help.
- Search space and complexity: Constructing n-gram tensors over multiple n and relation types could be expensive. More detail on candidate enumeration, pruning, and time/memory complexity would improve reproducibility and help readers judge scalability beyond the reported setting.
- Generality claim: While the formalization is agnostic to data types, instantiations require specifying relation vocabularies (temporal/spatial). The paper could better articulate what is needed to port the method to a new domain (e.g., text) and why prior NLP grammar induction baselines (e.g., HDP-PCFG [9]) are not compared experimentally.
- Empirical scope and baselines: Event baselines are limited to ID-sequence learners [15,18]; the proposed “method 3” has access to richer co-occurrence structure not available to those baselines. For images, comparing only to [17] leaves out other hierarchical generative models (e.g., SPN structure learning). Consider broader baselines or ablations to isolate the contribution of And-Or fragments vs. And-only or Or-only search.
- Evaluation details: For images, perplexity is computed after smoothing; the smoothing scheme is not specified. For synthetic experiments, details on KL estimation and confidence intervals are missing. For events, statistical significance of F-measure differences would be helpful.
- The claim that And-Or fragments can construct any context-free AOG is plausible; a proof sketch or reference would strengthen the paper.

Quality
The approach is technically sound at a high level, and the sufficient-statistic formulation is elegant. However, key details about And-rule/relations parameter learning, prior coding costs, and search/pruning are underspecified, making it harder to fully verify or reproduce. Empirical results are promising but would benefit from broader baselines and ablations.

Clarity
The paper reads clearly overall, and the high-level algorithm is well-motivated. Some figures appear garbled in the draft; ensure legibility. Please define RD, CM, and the coherence measures precisely in the main text (not only in the supplement), and expand on the role of relation energies and the smoothing used for perplexity.

Originality
Joint And-Or fragment induction with posterior gain computed via tensor/matrix statistics appears novel within AOG learning. The unifying formalization and cross-domain applications are valuable. The general Bayesian/MDL flavor of structure merging is established, but the specific instantiation to AOGs is new.

Significance
A general, practical method for unsupervised AOG structure learning is of interest to the community, with potential applications in vision and activity analysis. The improvements over prior AOG learners suggest the method can advance the state of the art, though stronger empirical breadth and clarity on learning details would increase impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a clear description of relation energy parameterization and learning, and how P(A → a1…an) is computed in practice.
- Include sensitivity analyses for α, sa, so, and study robustness to parse ambiguity.
- Add ablations (And-only, Or-only, And-Or without context coherence) and report their performance and grammar sizes.
- Broaden baselines where feasible (e.g., SPN structure learning for images; more recent grammar induction baselines).
- Detail candidate generation, pruning heuristics, and computational complexity.
- Consider a small NLP experiment to support the modality-agnostic claim.
- Provide proof or a formal argument that And-Or fragments suffice to generate any context-free AOG.

Pros
- Elegant, unified fragment-based structure learning.
- Efficient sufficient-statistic gain computation.
- Empirical improvements on both event and image domains.
- Conceptually connects AOGs with SPNs and MDL-style model merging.

Cons
- Strong unambiguity/Viterbi assumptions; limited analysis of their effects.
- Missing details on relation energy learning and prior coding choices.
- Narrow baselines and limited empirical breadth; few ablations.
- Some clarity/reproducibility gaps.

Overall assessment
A solid and promising contribution to unsupervised structure learning for AOGs with cross-domain evidence. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the learning of relation parameters, enriching experiments with ablations/baselines, and adding complexity/sensitivity analyses.

Reviewer confidence
Moderate. I am familiar with grammar induction and hierarchical generative models, though not all specifics of AOG learning.
