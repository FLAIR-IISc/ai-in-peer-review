

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and proposes an unsupervised approach for learning both the structure and parameters of such grammars. The main claim is that their method, which learns compositions and reconfigurations in a unified manner through "And-Or fragments," achieves better performance than previous approaches on event and image grammars.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical foundation with a formal definition of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type, and provides a rigorous derivation of sufficient statistics for efficient computation of posterior gains. The empirical evaluation on two datasets (event recognition and image parsing) shows significant improvements over previous methods, with compelling results on both synthetic and real-world data. The approach's ability to handle multiple data types through a unified framework is particularly noteworthy.

The paper's key strength lies in its unified learning framework, which addresses a critical limitation in prior work that handled compositions and reconfigurations separately. This leads to more efficient and robust grammar learning, especially with scarce data. The evaluation metrics (F-measure for events, perplexity for images) are appropriate and well-explained.

The paper has minor limitations: the assumption of single-pattern training samples requires modification for real-world data with multiple events per sample, though the authors address this adequately. The comparison with related work could be strengthened by including more recent approaches to grammatical inference.

The contribution is significant as it provides a more general and efficient method for learning structured representations, with clear applicability to multiple domains. The writing is clear and well-organized, with sufficient technical detail for reproducibility. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results robustly support the claims.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of structured probabilistic modeling, with clear technical improvements over existing approaches. The unified framework for learning grammars represents a meaningful advancement in the area, and the results demonstrate practical value for applications requiring compositional structure modeling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the comparison with the most recent grammatical inference literature.
