Summary
The paper proposes a unified, data-agnostic formulation of stochastic context-free And-Or grammars (AOGs) and an unsupervised algorithm that jointly learns both grammar structure and parameters. The key idea is to grow the grammar from a trivial memorizing initialization by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments—each comprising one And-node with multiple child Or-nodes—that together capture both compositionality and reconfigurability. The posterior P(G|X) is optimized, combining a sparsity prior over grammar size and a Viterbi likelihood over the training set. A main technical contribution is an efficient computation of posterior gain for candidate fragments based on sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor (counts of configurations covered by the fragment) and a context matrix (co-occurrence of configurations with their surrounding context). Under an unambiguity assumption, the likelihood gain factorizes into two coherence measures over these statistics; the prior gain penalizes fragment size. Empirically, the method learns event grammars and image grammars, outperforming or matching prior structure-learning approaches for AOGs and related models.

Relation to prior work
The work extends classic unsupervised grammar induction (BMM/MDL, Stolcke & Omohundro; Solan et al.; Tu & Honavar) to the AOG setting used in vision and events (Zhu & Mumford; Zhao & Zhu, NIPS 2011; Si & Zhu 2013). Unlike approaches that fix structure and learn parameters (e.g., EM for PCFGs; SPNs structure fixed or restricted), this paper searches structure via And-Or fragments, akin to joint composition/reconfiguration discovery in AOGs and related to structure learning in SPNs. The sufficient-statistics view and coherence measures connect to biclustering/co-clustering. Compared to prior AOG structure learning for images/events (Si & Zhu; Si et al.), the paper claims improved robustness and efficiency by unifying the search over And and Or alternatives.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The formulation of posterior gain, with Viterbi likelihood and a sparsity prior, is reasonable and aligns with successful practices in grammar induction (Viterbi/contrastive/unambiguity biases). The use of sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor and context matrix) for greedy/beam search is elegant and computationally attractive.
- Assumptions and limitations: The derivation relies on grammar unambiguity and context-freeness. While these biases often help learning, the paper should more clearly quantify when the posterior-gain approximation holds and how violations (ambiguous parses) affect fragment ranking. The encoding prior depends on discrete costs (sa, so); guidance on setting these and sensitivity analyses are limited.
- Empirical evaluation: The event experiments compare against two baselines and show F-measure gains, especially on the more challenging synthetic variant. The image experiments include a synthetic dataset with ground truth and a real dataset where perplexity improves markedly over a prior system. However, statistical significance, ablations (e.g., using only And- or Or-fragments, or removing the prior), and sensitivity to α/beam width are missing. Runtime is reported anecdotally; a more systematic complexity/runtime profile would strengthen the case.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear in defining AOGs, the And-Or fragment, and the learning loop, with helpful figures. The n-gram tensor/context matrix and their role in the posterior gain are explained at a high level; key derivations are deferred to the supplement. It would help to:
- Provide pseudo-code in the main text (not only in the supplement) for the fragment search and posterior-gain computation.
- Specify precisely how “contexts” are defined for images vs. events and how relations (relative positions, temporal “followed-by” and “co-occurring”) are encoded as energy terms.
- Clarify parsing/inference: for events, how CYK is adapted to handle both co-occurrence and followed-by within the same grammar; for images, how spatial relations remain compatible with context-free parsing.

Originality
The unified fragment-based structure learning that jointly induces compositions and reconfigurations is novel relative to prior separate searches for And- and Or-rules. The factorization of posterior gain via coherence of a tensor and a context matrix is a fresh perspective, even if conceptually related to biclustering/coherence measures. The unified, data-agnostic AOG formalization is useful for bridging images and events.

Significance
A general, unsupervised structure-learning method for AOGs is valuable to communities working on scene parsing, event understanding, and hierarchical representation learning. If widely applicable, the approach could reduce manual grammar engineering and improve generalization. The empirical improvements on both event and image datasets are promising, though broader validation and ablations would increase confidence.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually clean unification of structure learning via And-Or fragments; avoids pitfalls of separate And/Or searches.
- Efficient posterior-gain computation from sufficient statistics; practical greedy/beam search.
- Data-agnostic formalization demonstrated on two modalities (temporal events, spatial images).
- Empirical gains over previous AOG structure-learning methods; especially strong perplexity reduction on real images.

Cons and suggestions
- Reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi likelihood is under-examined; need experiments showing robustness when parses are ambiguous.
- Prior/encoding parameters (α, sa, so) and beam width lack sensitivity analysis; include curves showing held-out likelihood vs. α and fragment size penalties.
- Missing ablations: compare against learning only And-fragments or only Or-fragments to substantiate the unification claim; test without the prior; correlate posterior gain with held-out likelihood/perplexity improvements.
- Clarify inference details and contexts definition; give more specifics on relations/energy parametrization and how Or-rule probabilities are estimated in practice beyond the given formula.
- Event experiments: small test splits; report variance/confidence intervals and significance. Discuss the end-of-training switch from top-level And to Or and its effect on evaluation.
- Expand comparisons: for images, include alternative generative compositional models beyond [17]; for events, compare to frequent episode mining or hierarchical motif discovery baselines.

Overall assessment
A solid and potentially impactful contribution to unsupervised grammar structure learning for AOGs, with a principled search objective and practical algorithm. With stronger empirical analysis (ablations, sensitivity, clarity on assumptions/inference), the paper would be very compelling. I lean positive given the novelty and cross-domain results, while encouraging the authors to address the noted issues.
