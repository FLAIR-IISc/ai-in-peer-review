- Presents a unified, data-agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars applicable to images, events, etc.
- Proposes an unsupervised method that learns both grammar structure and parameters, starting from a trivial per-sample grammar.
- Introduces "And-Or fragments" to jointly search for new compositions and reconfigurations, addressing prior approaches that handled them separately.
- Derives sufficient statistics to efficiently compute posterior gain for adding fragments, avoiding full re-parsing at each iteration.
- Empirical evaluation on event grammars (human activity videos) shows competitive or better performance than prior methods, especially on harder datasets.
- Empirical evaluation on image grammars (synthetic and real animal faces) shows significant improvements in F-measure and perplexity over a competing method.
- Strength: clear, well-motivated unified formalization that generalizes across data types.
- Strength: technical novelty in unifying composition and reconfiguration learning via And-Or fragments.
- Strength: sound theoretical derivation enabling efficient posterior computation.
- Strength: well-organized, clearly written paper with helpful examples and figures.
- Weakness: lacks concrete justification or quantitative evidence that separate learning of composition/reconfiguration is problematic.
- Weakness: relatively small datasets (e.g., 61 videos, 5000 image samples) limit generalizability.
- Weakness: missing discussion of computational complexity and scalability to larger datasets.
- Weakness: comparisons to prior work are limited and don’t include the most recent methods.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel, and significant contribution with recommendation for acceptance; meets NIPS criteria (quality, clarity, originality, significance).
