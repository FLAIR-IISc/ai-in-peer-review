Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) that is agnostic to modality (text, images, events), and an unsupervised algorithm that jointly learns grammar structure and parameters from unannotated data. Starting from a trivial grammar that exactly memorizes the training set, the method iteratively inserts And-Or fragments—each comprising one And-rule over several new Or-nodes—selected to maximize the posterior probability under a sparsity prior. Likelihood is approximated with Viterbi likelihood. The posterior gain of a candidate fragment is computed efficiently from sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor over configurations and a context matrix, under an unambiguity assumption. The likelihood gain factorizes into coherence measures of these arrays, and the prior gain balances model growth against reductions. The approach generalizes and integrates ideas from unsupervised natural language grammar induction (e.g., model merging, biclustering) and improves on structure learning for AOGs in images and events that used more heuristic procedures. Empirically, the method is tested on event datasets (indoor activities, plus a variant with split action types) and image datasets (a synthetic animal-face sketch dataset with known ground truth grammar, and a real animal-face dataset). It achieves comparable or better results than prior work [15, 17, 18], notably lower perplexity on real images and better F/KL on synthetic images.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The formalization of context-free AOGs is clear and consistent with prior AOG/SPN literature. The learning criterion is well-defined (posterior under a size-penalizing prior, with Viterbi likelihood). The derivation of posterior gain from sufficient statistics is plausible and potentially impactful; however, key steps (factorization into coherence measures, proof that Or-rule parameters are optimal via reduction counts, and the claim that any CF-AOG can be constructed using And-Or fragments) are deferred to the supplementary material. The unambiguity assumption is central to the gain computation; its implications and robustness are only qualitatively discussed.
- Algorithmic details: The search is greedy/beam with random restarts. While practical, there is limited analysis of computational complexity, memory, and scalability of building n-gram tensors/contexts across relation types. The specification of encoding costs (sa, so) and sensitivity to α are not deeply examined.
- Experiments: The event experiments rely on pre-annotated atomic actions with start/end times; thus “unsupervised” pertains to structure learning given segmented tokens rather than raw video—a reasonable setup but worth stating plainly. The datasets are small (55/6 split), and no statistical significance analysis is provided. The image experiments are stronger: the synthetic study compares to [17] with multiple metrics (F, KL) and shows clear gains; the real dataset perplexity results also show substantial improvement. Details on smoothing and hyperparameter choices are brief. Ablations (e.g., And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or fragments), sensitivity to assumptions, and runtime comparisons are missing.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-organized, defines AOGs and fragments clearly, and uses helpful figures. Some notation is dense, and several central derivations are deferred to the supplement. The practicalities of constructing tensors/matrices, defining “contexts,” and handling multiple relation types could be elaborated. The modifications for multi-event videos (switching top-level And to Or and using CYK) are described but somewhat terse.

Originality:
The joint induction of compositions and reconfigurations via And-Or fragments is a notable contribution beyond prior AOG structure learning [17, 18], which treated them more separately. The use of sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor and context matrix) to approximate posterior gain, and the factorization into coherence measures, appear original. The unified formalization across modalities is incremental but useful. The Viterbi-based objective and sparsity prior are consistent with established grammar induction practices.

Significance:
Learning compact, interpretable, generative grammars across domains is significant for ML, vision, and activity analysis. If the sufficient-statistics-based search scales, this could broaden applicability of AOGs. The empirical gains over strong baselines in images are encouraging; the event results show competitiveness but on modest datasets with strong supervision for atoms. Demonstrating applicability to another modality (e.g., text) would further support the modality-agnostic claim.

Pros:
- Clear, unified formulation of context-free AOGs tied to SPNs and SCFGs.
- Principled objective (posterior with sparsity prior) with efficient posterior-gain estimation from sufficient statistics.
- Joint learning of And/Or via And-Or fragments addresses data scarcity and redundancy issues in prior work.
- Strong improvements on image grammar benchmarks (synthetic and real).
- Practical algorithm with greedy/beam search and plausible runtime claims.

Cons:
- Reliance on unambiguity assumption for gain computation; limited empirical analysis of its impact.
- Key derivations and claims deferred to supplementary; proofs and complexity details missing in the main text.
- Event experiments depend on pre-annotated atomic actions; small test sets; limited statistical analysis.
- Limited ablations (no comparison to And-only/Or-only learning, no sensitivity to α, sa, so).
- The “modality-agnostic” claim is not fully substantiated by experiments beyond images/events.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include (at least briefly) the derivation of the likelihood gain factorization and the constructive argument that And-Or fragments suffice for any CF-AOG.
- Analyze sensitivity to α, sa, so; report memory/time complexity and empirical scaling (n-gram tensor sizes vs sample size and relation vocabularies).
- Provide ablations comparing And-only, Or-only, and And-Or fragment search to validate the joint-learning advantage.
- Evaluate robustness to ambiguity and noise; quantify how violations of unambiguity affect gains and outcomes.
- For events, discuss the generalization from annotated atoms to detector outputs; add experiments with noisy detected actions if possible.
- If feasible, include a small text experiment to buttress the modality-agnostic claim; alternatively, broaden the image/event benchmarks and add significance testing.

Scope:
The work is squarely in NIPS scope (probabilistic models, structure learning, vision/activity applications).

Overall, this is a technically interesting and potentially impactful contribution to unsupervised structure learning of AOGs, with solid empirical evidence in images and competitive results in events. Strengthening theoretical exposition and empirical analysis would further increase confidence.
