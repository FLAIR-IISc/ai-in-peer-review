

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to the type of data being modeled (images, events, etc.), and proposes an unsupervised approach to learn both the structure and parameters of such grammars. The authors address a key limitation of previous work that typically learns compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules) separately, which can be inefficient and error-prone with limited data. Their key innovation is the introduction of "And-Or fragments" that unify the learning of these two aspects, leading to more efficient and robust grammar induction.

The paper builds upon prior work on stochastic grammars for images [1, 3] and events [7, 18], as well as structure learning approaches for natural language grammars [14, 15, 16]. It extends the 2011 NIPS work by Zhao and Zhu on image parsing with stochastic scene grammars [3] and the 2011 ICCV work by Si et al. on event grammars [18], while addressing their limitations regarding grammar structure learning. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their approach, formalizing And-Or grammars as a context-free extension of stochastic context-free grammars.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-motivated claims and thorough empirical evaluation. The authors derive efficient sufficient statistics for computing likelihood and prior gains during grammar learning, which is a significant technical contribution. Their evaluation on event grammars (human activity videos) and image grammars (synthetic and real animal faces) demonstrates competitive or superior performance compared to previous methods. The ablation study (comparing the three different methods of applying their approach) provides valuable insights into the importance of grounding grammars to atomic patterns. The only minor quality concern is the assumption that each training sample consists of a single pattern, which requires modification for videos with multiple unrelated events (though the authors address this adequately).

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized, with a logical progression from problem definition to solution to evaluation. The formalization of And-Or grammars is clear and well-motivated, and the examples in Table 1 and Figure 1 effectively illustrate the concepts. The description of the learning algorithm, particularly the concept of And-Or fragments, is accessible. The figures (Figures 1-3) are helpful for understanding the approach. One minor clarity issue is that some technical details (like the full derivation of likelihood gain) are relegated to supplementary materials, which may hinder reproducibility.

**Originality:** The paper makes a clear contribution by unifying the learning of compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments, which is novel compared to previous approaches that handle these aspects separately. The authors properly contextualize their work against relevant prior NIPS papers (e.g., Zhao & Zhu, 2011; Si et al., 2011) and other work in the field. The approach is sufficiently different from previous methods to be considered a meaningful contribution to the field. The unified formalization of And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type is also an important theoretical contribution.

**Significance:** The work addresses an important problem in unsupervised learning of structured representations. The ability to learn grammars that can model both compositionality and reconfigurability across different domains (images, events) has broad applicability. The empirical results demonstrate that their approach achieves comparable or better performance than previous methods, which suggests practical utility for researchers working on structured prediction tasks. The paper advances the state of the art in structure learning for grammatical models, providing a more efficient and robust alternative to previous approaches.

**Strengths:**
- Clear and unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars applicable across different data types
- Novel use of And-Or fragments for unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations
- Efficient computation of likelihood and prior gains using sufficient statistics
- Thorough empirical evaluation on two different domains with ablation studies
- Strong comparison to relevant baselines

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't fully address how the approach scales to very large datasets or extremely complex grammars
- The assumption of single-pattern samples per training example, while handled with a modification, could be more thoroughly discussed in terms of limitations
- The comparison to sum-product networks (Poon & Domingos, 2011), which are closely related to And-Or grammars, could be more detailed
- The paper doesn't provide enough detail on how the greedy search avoids local optima in the combinatorial search space

**Recommendation:** This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of unsupervised learning of structured representations. The unified approach to grammar learning is theoretically sound and empirically validated across multiple domains. The paper meets all the criteria for high-quality NIPS research. I recommend acceptance.

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**
1. Include a brief discussion of the scaling properties of the approach with dataset size and grammar complexity.
2. Expand the comparison to sum-product networks (which are mentioned in the introduction but not directly compared).
3. Provide more detail on how the greedy search procedure avoids getting stuck in local optima.
4. Consider including a brief discussion of the limitations of the single-pattern assumption in the main text.

Overall, this paper presents a significant advance in the learning of grammatical models, with clear theoretical foundations and compelling empirical results. It makes an important contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning and would be of interest to the NIPS community.
