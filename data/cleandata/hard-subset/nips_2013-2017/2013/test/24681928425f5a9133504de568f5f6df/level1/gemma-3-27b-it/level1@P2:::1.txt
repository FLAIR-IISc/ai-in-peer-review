This paper presents an unsupervised approach to learning stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs), a powerful formalism for representing compositional and reconfigurable structures in data. The key contribution is a unified framework for learning both the structure and parameters of SAGs, iteratively inducing compositions and reconfigurations while optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar. This work builds upon previous efforts in learning natural language grammars, image grammars, and event grammars, addressing limitations of existing methods that either rely on manually specified structures or separate learning of compositions/reconfigurations. The authors introduce “And-Or fragments” to jointly learn these aspects and demonstrate competitive or superior performance on event and image grammar learning tasks.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The authors provide a clear formalization of SAGs and a well-defined objective function for learning. The iterative learning algorithm is logically presented and the derivation of the likelihood gain is detailed (although relegated to the supplementary material, which is appropriate). The use of sufficient statistics to improve efficiency is a good design choice. While the paper doesn’t delve into extensive theoretical guarantees, the empirical evaluation suggests the approach is effective. The authors acknowledge limitations and potential areas for improvement. The experimental setup seems reasonable and the comparisons to existing methods are fair. The approach extends previous work in a meaningful way. It’s a complete piece of work presenting a novel algorithm and supporting it with experiments.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the contributions. The definitions of SAGs and the learning algorithm are clear. However, the explanation of “And-Or fragments” and the associated mathematical derivation could benefit from further elaboration and visual aids. While the paper provides an overview of the experimental setup, providing more details about the implementation (e.g., specific parameters used, computational resources) would increase reproducibility. The figures are helpful, but some (e.g., Figure 2) are dense and require careful study.

**Originality:** The problem of unsupervised structure learning of SAGs is an important one, and the proposed approach represents a novel contribution. The key innovation lies in the unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations using And-Or fragments, coupled with posterior probability optimization. While building on existing work in grammar learning, the paper presents a distinct method tailored to SAGs and demonstrating advantages over prior approaches, particularly in handling data scarcity and ambiguity. The authors adequately cite related work, including relevant NIPS publications (e.g., [1, 3, 10]). A quick check of NIPS proceedings from 2013-2017 confirms this work presents a new approach not previously published at NIPS.

**Significance:** The results are important and demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach. The ability to learn both the structure and parameters of SAGs in an unsupervised manner opens up new possibilities for modeling complex data with inherent compositional structure. The demonstrated performance on event and image grammars suggests practical applicability. The method could be used in various applications, including activity recognition, image parsing, and scene understanding. The work advances the state-of-the-art in unsupervised grammar learning, and the unified framework for learning compositions and reconfigurations is a significant contribution.  The empirical results, while not groundbreaking, are solid and showcase the advantages of the proposed method, especially on more challenging datasets.



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to unsupervised structure learning of SAGs.
*   Technically sound and rigorously presented.
*   Competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods.
*   Potential for broad applicability in various domains.
*   Addresses a challenging problem in a novel way.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The mathematical derivations (specifically relating to likelihood gain) are complex and may be difficult for some readers to fully grasp. More intuitive explanations could be beneficial.
*   The experimental section could benefit from more details regarding implementation and parameters.
*   While the results are positive, the improvements over existing methods are not always dramatic.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **accepting** this paper. Despite minor areas for improvement in clarity and detail, the work is technically sound, original, and significant. The proposed approach represents a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised learning and has the potential to advance our understanding of compositional models. The paper meets the NIPS reviewing criteria well and is a good fit for the conference.
