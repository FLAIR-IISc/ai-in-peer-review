Summary and relation to prior work
The paper formalizes stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs) in a unified, context-free framework covering diverse domains (images, events, text), and proposes an unsupervised method to jointly learn structure and parameters. Starting from a trivial “memorization” grammar that exactly generates the training set, the algorithm iteratively inserts And-Or fragments—each consisting of one new And-node with multiple child Or-nodes—thereby inducing both compositions (And) and reconfigurations (Or) in a single step. A sparsity prior over grammar size encourages compact models; likelihood is approximated by Viterbi likelihood (unambiguity bias). The posterior gain of a candidate fragment is computed efficiently using sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors of within-fragment configurations and a context co-occurrence matrix), yielding factorized “coherence” measures. The approach assumes unambiguity to avoid re-parsing at each step and uses greedy/beam search. Experiments on event parsing (two datasets) and image grammars (synthetic and real) show improvements over prior methods that either fix grammar structure or learn And/Or components separately.

This line builds on stochastic scene grammars for images (Zhu & Mumford ’06; Zhao & Zhu NIPS’11) and event grammars (Si et al. ICCV’11), and relates to unsupervised grammar induction (Baker ’79; Klein & Manning ’04; Stolcke & Omohundro ’94; Tu & Honavar ’08) and decomposable sum-product networks (Poon & Domingos UAI’11). The proposed fragment search is reminiscent of biclustering-based structure discovery and Bayesian model merging, but integrates And/Or jointly for AOGs. It is also closely related to SPN structure learning (e.g., Gens & Domingos, ICML’13) though not cited.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The posterior optimization objective with a sparsity prior is reasonable, and the Viterbi approximation is well-motivated by prior work. The derivation that the posterior gain decomposes into terms based on an n-gram tensor and a context matrix is elegant and practically useful. However, it hinges on a strong unambiguity assumption and on fixed relation types within And-rules. The choice of coding lengths (sa, so) in the prior and their sensitivity is not examined. The search is heuristic (greedy/beam) without guarantees.
- Empirical support: The event experiments (61 videos) compare against two baselines that rely on ID sequences; the proposed methods that operate directly on action vectors show clear gains on the more challenging dataset, particularly when “co-occur” and “followed-by” relations are learned jointly. The image experiments show substantial improvements on a synthetic dataset (F-measure and KL) and lower perplexity on a real dataset than Si & Zhu (2013). Nonetheless, datasets are modest in size; comparisons omit strong alternatives such as HDP-PCFG/adaptor grammars in NLP-like settings, SPN structure learning, hierarchical HMMs/segmental models for events, or more recent structured vision baselines. Runtime and scaling claims are qualitative (“minutes”) without complexity characterization.
- Completeness and honesty: Some derivations are deferred to the supplement; the main text should at least summarize key assumptions and approximations more explicitly (e.g., when the posterior-gain estimator is exact/inexact).

Clarity
The unified AOG definition and the learning narrative are clear, and figures help, though some are crowded. The notion of “relations” in And-rules is domain-agnostic but only instantiated for “followed-by,” “co-occur,” and spatial relations; a succinct formal definition of how relations parameterize energies would aid reproducibility. The coherence measures are referenced but not defined; briefly recalling the measures (or giving intuition) would improve readability. Pseudocode is in the supplement; a high-level algorithm box in the main text would help. Notation could be streamlined (e, c, RD subsets) and Figure 2 could be simplified.

Originality
The central novelty is searching for joint And-Or fragments with a posterior gain computed from sufficient statistics, which unifies composition and reconfiguration induction and mitigates data sparsity and redundancy. While related to Bayesian model merging and biclustering-based PCFG induction, and conceptually to SPN structure learning, the proposed fragment form and posterior-gain estimator for AOGs appear new. The unified, domain-agnostic formalization is a nice contribution.

Significance
AOGs are influential in vision and event understanding; reducing reliance on hand-crafted structures is valuable. If the method scales, it can lower the barrier to deploying AOGs across domains. The improvements over Si & Zhu (2013) on images and over ID-sequence methods for events suggest practical impact. However, broader significance is tempered by the scale and scope of experiments, reliance on pre-quantized terminals and predefined relation types, and the untested robustness of the unambiguity-based gain estimator on larger, more ambiguous corpora (e.g., natural language).

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, unified framework for unsupervised AOG structure and parameter learning.
- And-Or fragments elegantly tie together composition and reconfiguration; well-motivated posterior with sparsity prior.
- Efficient sufficient-statistics-based gain computation; intuitive coherence connection.
- Empirical gains on both events and images; domain-agnostic promise.

Cons for acceptance
- Key assumption of unambiguity for gain estimation; no analysis of its violations or corrective steps.
- Limited baselines (no SPN structure learning, HHMMs, nonparametric PCFGs); modest dataset scales.
- Dependence on predefined relation vocabularies and pre-learned terminals; no learning of relations.
- Search is heuristic; no complexity analysis, convergence, or ablations (e.g., And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or).
- Some clarity issues (definitions of coherence, coding lengths, parameterization of energies).

Suggestions for improvement
- Empirically assess ambiguity: report parse ambiguity rates, compare gain estimates with occasional full re-parsing, or incorporate posterior regularization for unambiguity.
- Add stronger baselines: SPN structure learning (e.g., Gens & Domingos 2013), HDP-PCFG/adaptor grammars (for text-like settings), HHMM/segmental models for events.
- Provide ablations: And-only, Or-only, and And-Or fragment search; effect of beam width; sensitivity to α, sa, so.
- Clarify and justify coding lengths and energy parameterization; report runtime and memory scaling with number of terminals, fragment arity, and data size.
- Broaden evaluation: larger or more diverse datasets; additional metrics (parsing accuracy, interpretability), and qualitative analyses of learned fragments.
- Cite relevant related work on SPN structure learning and MDL/description length criteria in grammar induction.

Overall, this is a solid and timely contribution with a clever learning mechanism and promising cross-domain results. Strengthening empirical comparisons, clarifying assumptions, and expanding evaluations would make it a strong accept. As is, I lean toward accept if space allows.
