

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to the type of data being modeled, and proposes an unsupervised approach to learning both the structure and parameters of such grammars. The authors build on previous work in stochastic grammars for natural language processing [8,9] and extend it to image modeling [1,2,3] and event modeling [4,5,6,7], while connecting to sum-product networks [10]. The key innovation is a unified approach to learning both compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules) through the introduction of "And-Or fragments," which overcomes limitations of previous approaches that treated these aspects separately.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type, enabling a unified learning approach for different domains. Second, it introduces a principled optimization of the posterior probability of the grammar rather than relying on heuristics. Third, it proposes a unified learning mechanism for both compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments, which addresses data scarcity issues and improves robustness. Finally, it demonstrates the approach's effectiveness on both event grammars (from human activity videos) and image grammars (from animal face sketches).

**Strengths:**

*Quality*: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical foundation is strong, with clear derivations of likelihood and prior gains that enable efficient computation. The authors correctly address the challenge of grammar structure learning by optimizing the posterior probability rather than relying on ad hoc heuristics. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, with experiments on both synthetic and real datasets, and clear comparisons to previous methods. The results on the more complex second dataset (with subtype divisions) are particularly convincing, showing the approach's ability to handle realistic data challenges.

*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The unified formalization is presented clearly in Section 2 with helpful examples in Table 1 and Figure 1. The algorithm framework (Section 3.2) and the concept of And-Or fragments (Section 3.3) are explained with sufficient detail, and the figures (particularly Figures 1-3) effectively illustrate key concepts. The supplementary material mentioned for full derivations is a good practice, and the paper provides enough detail for an expert to reproduce the results.

*Originality*: The paper makes several original contributions. The unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars (agnostic to data type) is novel and valuable. The introduction of And-Or fragments as a unified learning mechanism for both compositions and reconfigurations represents a significant conceptual advance over previous approaches [14-18], which handled these aspects separately. The paper properly situates its work within the NIPS proceedings, citing relevant prior work like "Image parsing with stochastic scene grammar" (Zhao & Zhu, NIPS 2011) and "Learning and-or templates for object modeling and recognition" (Si & Zhu, 2013), and clearly distinguishes its contributions from these.

*Significance*: The significance of this work is substantial. The ability to learn grammars from unannotated data in a unified way across different data types (images, events) represents a meaningful advance in unsupervised learning. The results show clear improvements over previous approaches on both event and image grammars, particularly on more challenging datasets. The paper demonstrates that their approach can learn grammars that better capture the underlying structure of the data, with measurable improvements in parsing performance. This work has clear potential for application in computer vision, natural language processing, and activity recognition.

**Weaknesses:**

*The paper does not sufficiently address the scalability of the approach to larger datasets. While the authors mention their prototype implementation runs within minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples, the paper does not discuss how the approach would scale to very large datasets (e.g., millions of images or videos) or how the algorithm would perform on more complex grammars with many nonterminal nodes. This is particularly relevant given the increasing size of modern datasets.

*The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including additional baselines. The paper compares to two previous approaches [15,18] but does not compare to more recent methods that might have emerged since these papers were published (e.g., more recent deep learning approaches to grammar learning). The comparison to [17] is helpful, but a broader comparison would strengthen the significance claim.

*The paper does not thoroughly discuss the limitations of the approach. For example, the authors assume that each training sample consists of a single pattern generated from the target grammar, but they have to modify the algorithm to handle videos with multiple unrelated events. It would be helpful to discuss how this limitation might affect the approach's applicability to other domains or data types.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro: The paper presents a technically sound, well-written, and original contribution that addresses a significant problem in unsupervised learning. The unified approach to learning grammar structure is conceptually novel and the empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over previous methods. The work is well-situated within the NIPS community and has clear potential for impact.

*Con: The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of scalability and limitations, and the experimental evaluation could be strengthened with additional baselines. However, these are minor concerns given the paper's overall quality and contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. The unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars, the principled optimization of the posterior probability, and the novel And-Or fragments approach represent a meaningful advancement over previous work. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and demonstrates clear empirical improvements over prior methods. While there are minor areas for improvement regarding scalability and limitations, these do not detract from the paper's overall quality and significance.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It is a strong contribution to the NIPS community that advances the state of the art in unsupervised structure learning and has the potential to influence future work in grammar-based modeling across multiple domains.
