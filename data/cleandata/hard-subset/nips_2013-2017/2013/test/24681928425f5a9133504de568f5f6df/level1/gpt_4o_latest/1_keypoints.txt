- Presents a unified, data-agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars enabling generalization across text, images, and events.  
- Proposes an And-Or fragment approach that jointly learns compositions and reconfigurations, yielding more compact and robust grammars.  
- Uses explicit posterior optimization for grammar learning rather than heuristic methods, providing a principled approach.  
- Empirically outperforms prior methods on event and image grammar tasks (F-measure, perplexity) with evaluations on synthetic and real datasets.  
- Algorithm shows scalability, handling datasets with thousands of samples within reasonable runtimes.  
- Some technical sections (e.g., derivation of likelihood and prior gains) are dense and need clearer explanations or examples.  
- Evaluation is limited to event and image domains; additional tests (e.g., text, multimodal) are needed to substantiate generality.  
- Assumes context-freeness in grammars, which may limit applicability to context-sensitive data.  
- Lacks comparison to more recent grammar-learning and neural-symbolic methods for broader benchmarking.  
- Argument for acceptance: novel, technically sound unified approach with strong empirical results relevant to probabilistic modeling and unsupervised learning.  
- Argument against acceptance: presentation clarity issues, limited evaluation diversity, and restrictive assumptions (context-freeness, Viterbi likelihood) that may limit applicability.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to improve clarity and broaden evaluation.
