

# Peer Review: Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type and proposes an unsupervised learning approach for both grammar structure and parameters. The authors address a key limitation in prior work where composition (And-rules) and reconfiguration (Or-rules) were learned separately, leading to inefficiency and brittleness. Their core innovation is the use of "And-Or fragments" that jointly optimize both aspects through a unified search procedure.

The paper builds upon the foundational work of Zhu et al. (2011) on image parsing with stochastic scene grammars and extends the structure learning framework from natural language processing (e.g., Stolcke & Omohundro, 1994; Tu & Honavar, 2008) to a more general setting. It also relates to sum-product networks (Poon & Domingos, 2011) as a special case of And-Or grammars. The authors properly contextualize their work within these lines of research, highlighting how their approach differs from previous methods that either restricted grammar structure (e.g., Wang et al., 2012) or learned structure and parameters separately (e.g., Si & Zhu, 2013; Si et al., 2011).

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars is well-motivated and clearly presented. The authors successfully abstract away data-specific details to create a framework applicable across domains (text, images, events).

2. **Algorithmic Innovation:** The And-Or fragments concept is compelling and addresses a genuine limitation in prior approaches. The derivation of sufficient statistics for efficient posterior gain computation is technically sound and represents a significant practical improvement.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both event grammars (using human activity data) and image grammars (synthetic and real animal face datasets) are thorough and well-designed. The approach consistently outperforms prior methods, with particularly strong results on the more complex synthetic dataset where the division of actions into subtypes created greater learning challenges.

4. **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written with clear definitions, illustrative examples (Figures 1-2), and well-organized exposition. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail on the algorithm for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Concerns:** The paper doesn't adequately address scalability to larger datasets. The experiments use relatively modest datasets (5000 samples max), but it's unclear how the approach would scale to the massive datasets common in modern computer vision and NLP applications. The computational complexity of the greedy/beam search isn't thoroughly analyzed.

2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sparsity parameter α is mentioned but not properly evaluated. A sensitivity analysis showing how results vary with α would strengthen the empirical claims and provide practical guidance for users.

3. **Theoretical Guarantees:** The paper relies on greedy search without theoretical guarantees on the quality of the learned grammar. It would be valuable to discuss how the approach might get stuck in local optima and potential mitigation strategies.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work:** While the paper cites relevant prior work, it doesn't sufficiently compare with more recent methods in related domains, particularly the growing literature on structured probabilistic models and neural grammars that have emerged since 2011.

**Significance:**

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of structured probabilistic modeling. The ability to learn grammar structure unsupervised across different domains has clear practical value, as manual grammar specification is often prohibitive. The demonstrated improvements over prior work in both event and image grammars suggest the approach has real-world applicability. The unified framework is particularly valuable for researchers working across multiple domains who would otherwise need to develop separate grammar learning approaches for each.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of probabilistic grammar learning. The theoretical innovation of And-Or fragments is compelling, and the empirical results are convincing. While the paper has some limitations regarding scalability and theoretical guarantees, these are common challenges in structure learning that are acknowledged in the field. The authors have clearly demonstrated the value of their approach through rigorous experiments.

I recommend acceptance. The paper would be significantly strengthened with a brief discussion of scalability concerns and a sensitivity analysis of the α parameter, but these are minor issues for a paper of this quality.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Unified formalization applicable across data domains
- And-Or fragments provide a more efficient and robust learning approach
- Strong empirical results on both event and image grammars
- Clear theoretical derivation of efficient computation
- Well-written and well-organized

*Con:*
- Limited analysis of scalability to large datasets
- Insufficient exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity
- No theoretical guarantees on search procedure
- Limited comparison with very recent work in related areas

The paper advances the state of the art in unsupervised structure learning for grammatical models and has clear potential for application across multiple domains.
