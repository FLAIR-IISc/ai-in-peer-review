### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a general, unsupervised algorithm for learning the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs) from unannotated data. The work begins by providing a unified, data-agnostic formalization of context-free AOGs, which model patterns through compositionality (And-rules) and reconfigurability (Or-rules). The core contribution is a bottom-up learning algorithm that iteratively refines an initial, trivial grammar by adding "And-Or fragments." This key concept unifies the learning of And-rules and Or-rules into a single step, which the authors argue is more robust and efficient than previous methods that learn them separately, such as those by Si & Zhu (2013) for images and Si et al. (2011) for events. The algorithm greedily searches for fragments that maximize the posterior probability of the grammar, which is defined by a Viterbi likelihood term and a complexity prior. A crucial part of the method is an efficient formulation for calculating the posterior gain of adding a fragment using sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices), which avoids costly re-parsing of the data at each iteration. The proposed approach is empirically validated on two distinct tasks: learning event grammars from human activity videos and learning image grammars from synthetic and real animal face datasets. In both cases, the method achieves comparable or significantly better performance than previous state-of-the-art approaches.

### Qualitative Evaluation

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed method is well-founded in a Bayesian framework, optimizing a clear objective function (the grammar's posterior probability). The use of the Viterbi likelihood is a common and reasonable approximation in grammar induction, and the authors correctly cite prior work justifying its use. The central technical contribution—the efficient computation of the posterior gain for adding an And-Or fragment—is well-explained and appears sound, though the full derivation is in the supplementary material. The connection made between the likelihood gain and tensor/matrix coherence measures is insightful. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors test their method in two different domains, using both synthetic and real data. The quantitative results show substantial improvements over relevant and recent baselines, strongly supporting the paper's claims. This is a complete piece of work, presenting a formal framework, a novel algorithm, and strong empirical validation.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. Section 2 provides a concise and general definition of AOGs that is helpful for readers unfamiliar with them. The algorithm is described logically, and Figure 1 provides an excellent illustration of the iterative learning process. The core idea of "And-Or fragments" and the motivation for using them are presented compellingly. The description of how the posterior gain is calculated is dense but provides enough intuition for the reader to grasp the main ideas. The experimental sections are clear about the setup, datasets, and evaluation metrics used. Overall, the paper is easy to follow and provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the contributions.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions. The primary novelty lies in the concept of the "And-Or fragment," which unifies the search for compositional (And) and alternative (Or) structures. This is a significant departure from prior work on AOG structure learning which typically handled these two aspects separately. This unified approach is a conceptually elegant and, as shown by the experiments, practically effective idea. A second valuable contribution is the formulation of a data-agnostic learning framework, whereas previous AOG learning methods were often tailored to a specific domain like images. Finally, while the algorithm builds on general ideas from bottom-up grammar induction (e.g., model merging), the specific instantiation for AOGs and the efficient posterior gain calculation are novel. The related work is well-cited, and the paper does an excellent job of distinguishing its contributions from prior art.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant. AOGs are a highly expressive class of generative models, but their application has been limited by the difficulty of learning their structure from data. This paper provides a principled and effective algorithm that advances the state-of-the-art in this area. The demonstrated performance improvements over recent, specialized methods are substantial. Given its data-agnostic formulation, the framework has the potential to be applied to other domains where compositional structures are prevalent, such as natural language processing or computational biology. The work is likely to be of interest to researchers in generative modeling, computer vision, and machine learning more broadly, and could inspire further research into structure learning for other complex probabilistic models.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The core idea of learning unified "And-Or fragments" is a novel and important contribution to the field of grammar induction and structured generative models.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method is shown to significantly outperform recent, strong baselines on multiple datasets across two different domains, providing clear evidence of its effectiveness.
3.  **Principled Approach:** The algorithm is well-motivated within a Bayesian framework, optimizing a clear posterior probability objective.
4.  **Generality:** The proposed framework is data-agnostic, broadening the potential impact and applicability of AOGs beyond their traditional domains.
5.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Greedy Search:** The algorithm relies on a greedy or beam search, which offers no guarantee of finding a globally optimal grammar structure. While common, a discussion of the potential for getting stuck in poor local minima would have strengthened the paper.
2.  **Dependence on Supplement:** The full derivation of the crucial posterior gain formula is left to the supplementary material, making a full technical verification of this key component difficult from the main paper alone.
3.  **Ad-hoc Modification:** The post-hoc modification made in the event grammar experiments (changing top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes) feels somewhat disconnected from the main learning framework and could be more elegantly integrated.

Despite these minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper in terms of novelty, significance, and empirical performance are overwhelming. This is an excellent paper that represents a clear step forward in learning structured generative models. I strongly recommend acceptance.
