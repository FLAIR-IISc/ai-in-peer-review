The paper introduces an unsupervised method to learn stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs), jointly learning structure and parameters.  
Key contribution: a unified framework that iteratively induces compositions and reconfigurations while optimizing the grammar posterior.  
Introduces "And-Or fragments" to jointly learn compositions and reconfigurations.  
Builds on prior grammar-learning work and addresses limitations of manual structures or separate learning steps.  
Demonstrates competitive or superior performance on event and image grammar tasks.  

Quality: technically sound with clear SAG formalization and a well-defined learning objective.  
Presents a logical iterative learning algorithm; likelihood-gain derivation is provided (in supplement).  
Uses sufficient statistics to improve computational efficiency.  
Limited theoretical guarantees but empirical results support effectiveness; authors acknowledge limitations.  
Experimental setup and comparisons appear reasonable and fair.  

Clarity: generally well-written and organized with an effective introduction and clear definitions.  
Explanation of And-Or fragments and associated math could use more elaboration and visual aids.  
Reproducibility suffers from missing implementation details (parameters, compute resources).  
Some figures (e.g., Figure 2) are dense and require careful study.  

Originality: novel approach to unsupervised SAG structure learning; distinct method for joint composition/reconfiguration learning.  
Approach handles data scarcity and ambiguity better than some prior methods; related work is adequately cited.  

Significance: enables unsupervised learning of both structure and parameters, with applications in activity recognition, image parsing, and scene understanding.  
Advances state-of-the-art in unsupervised grammar learning; empirical gains are solid though not always dramatic.  

Arguments pro: novel and well-motivated; technically rigorous; competitive performance; broad applicability; tackles a challenging problem.  
Arguments con: mathematical derivations are complex and could use more intuition; experimental details lacking for reproducibility; improvements not uniformly large.  

Overall recommendation: accept — technically sound, original, and significant despite minor clarity and detail issues.
