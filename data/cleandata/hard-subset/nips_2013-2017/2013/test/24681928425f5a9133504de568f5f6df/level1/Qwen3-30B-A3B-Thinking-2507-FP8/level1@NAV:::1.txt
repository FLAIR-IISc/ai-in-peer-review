

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and proposes an unsupervised approach to learning both their structure and parameters. The authors address a key limitation in previous work where structure learning for compositions (via And-fragments) and reconfigurations (via Or-fragments) were handled separately, often leading to inefficient or error-prone learning. Their key contribution is introducing And-Or fragments that jointly learn compositions and reconfigurations, optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar through iterative bottom-up learning.

The paper makes a strong connection to prior NIPS work on stochastic grammars for images [1, 3] and events [7], while extending the recent structure learning approaches [17, 18]. The unified formalization (Section 2) is particularly valuable as it abstracts away data type specifics, allowing the same learning framework to apply to different domains. The theoretical foundation (Section 3) is solid, with clear derivation of the posterior gain using sufficient statistics, and the algorithm efficiently computes likelihood and prior gains.

Strengths include: (1) Technical soundness - the approach is rigorously grounded in probability theory and well-validated through experiments; (2) Novelty - the unified And-Or fragment concept represents a significant departure from previous separate handling of compositions and reconfigurations; (3) Significance - the method achieves competitive or better results on both event and image grammars than prior approaches [17, 18], with clear advantages on more complex data; (4) Clarity - the paper is well-organized with helpful examples and figures (Figures 1-2) that effectively illustrate the learning process.

Weaknesses include: (1) The assumption that each training sample contains a single pattern requires modification for multi-event videos, which slightly complicates the application; (2) The empirical evaluation focuses on specific datasets but would benefit from more diverse or larger-scale benchmarks; (3) The computational complexity of the fragment search procedure isn't thoroughly analyzed, though the authors note reasonable runtime on standard hardware.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that advances the state of the art in grammar learning. The unified approach to structure learning represents a meaningful technical improvement with clear practical benefits. The paper is well-written, properly contextualized within the literature, and makes a significant contribution to the field.

Pro: Unified approach to composition and reconfiguration, strong theoretical foundation, competitive empirical results across domains
Con: Limited scalability analysis, minor modification needed for multi-event data, relatively narrow empirical scope
