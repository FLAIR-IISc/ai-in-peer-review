

# Review: Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to the type of data being modeled (images, events, etc.), and proposes an unsupervised approach to learn both the structure and parameters of such grammars. The authors extend previous work on structure learning of natural language grammars [14-16] and recent work on And-Or grammars for images [17] and events [18]. The key innovation is the use of "And-Or fragments" that unify the search for new compositions and reconfigurations in the grammar, addressing a limitation in prior approaches that learned these aspects separately.

The paper begins with a clear formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars as a 5-tuple ⟨Σ, N, S, R, P⟩, distinguishing between And-nodes (representing decompositions into sub-patterns) and Or-nodes (representing alternative configurations). The authors then introduce an unsupervised learning approach that starts from a trivial initial grammar (where each training sample has its own nonterminal node) and iteratively adds new And-Or fragments to optimize the posterior probability of the grammar. The main technical contribution is the derivation of sufficient statistics for efficiently computing the posterior gain of adding an And-Or fragment, which avoids the need for re-parsing all training samples at each iteration.

The paper provides empirical evaluations on two tasks: learning event grammars from human activity videos and image grammars from synthetic and real animal face datasets. For event grammars, the authors compare their approach with two previous methods [15, 18] on two datasets (one with 61 videos and another with synthesized subtypes of actions), showing competitive or better performance, particularly on the more challenging dataset. For image grammars, their approach significantly outperforms the competing method [17] on both synthetic and real datasets, as measured by F-measure and perplexity.

**Strengths:**
1. The unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars is well-motivated and clearly presented, making the approach applicable to different data types.
2. The use of And-Or fragments to unify composition and reconfiguration learning is a significant technical contribution that addresses a clear limitation in prior work.
3. The theoretical derivation of sufficient statistics for efficient posterior gain computation is sound and well-justified.
4. The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over previous methods on both event and image grammar learning tasks.
5. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and includes helpful examples and figures.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for why previous approaches that learn compositions and reconfigurations separately are problematic beyond stating they're "error-prone when data is scarce." Concrete examples or quantitative evidence would strengthen this claim.
2. The dataset sizes used in the experiments are relatively small (e.g., 61 videos total for event grammars, 5000 training samples for images), which limits the generalizability of the results.
3. The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity or scalability to larger datasets, which is important for practical applications.
4. The comparison to previous work is somewhat limited; the paper doesn't compare with the most recent approaches to structure learning of And-Or grammars.

**Pro and Con Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Novel approach to unify composition and reconfiguration learning using And-Or fragments
- Clear theoretical foundation with efficient sufficient statistics derivation
- Demonstrated improvements over previous methods on two different domains
- Unified formalization of And-Or grammars applicable to multiple data types
- Well-written with clear examples and figures

*Con:*
- Limited empirical evaluation on small datasets
- Insufficient justification for the limitations of previous approaches
- Missing discussion of computational complexity and scalability
- Some comparisons to prior work could be more comprehensive

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of unsupervised grammar learning. The unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and the proposed approach to learning both structure and parameters in a unified manner represent a meaningful advancement over previous work. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the empirical results support the claims, particularly on the synthetic and more challenging event grammar dataset.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and addresses a problem of significance in pattern recognition. The approach has potential for application to various domains beyond the two tested in the paper. While the empirical evaluation could be strengthened with larger datasets and more comprehensive comparisons, the technical contributions are substantial.

The paper meets all four key criteria for NIPS: it is technically sound (quality), clearly written (clarity), presents a novel approach to structure learning (originality), and demonstrates significant improvements over previous methods (significance).

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field that would be valuable for the NIPS community. The approach to unified structure learning could influence future work on grammar-based models for pattern recognition.
