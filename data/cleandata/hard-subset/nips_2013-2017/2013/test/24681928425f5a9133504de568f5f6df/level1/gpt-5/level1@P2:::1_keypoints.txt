- Presents a unified, modality-agnostic formalization of context-free stochastic And-Or grammars and an unsupervised algorithm that grows grammars by inserting And-Or fragments from a memorizing initial grammar.  
- Objective combines a sparsity prior on grammar size with a Viterbi likelihood; fragment posterior gain is computed via n-gram tensors and context matrices.  
- Likelihood gain factorizes into two coherence measures; prior gain trades fragment size against enabled reductions.  
- Demonstrates applications to event grammar learning (human activity videos) and image grammar learning (synthetic sketches and real animal faces), showing improvements over prior AOG and unsupervised grammar baselines.  
- Builds on prior AOG, PCFG, Bayesian grammar induction, and SPN work; main novelty is joint And/Or learning via fragments with a principled posterior score.  
- Posterior objective and fragment-level sufficient-statistics heuristic are technically sound and computationally attractive.  
- Strong unambiguity assumption underlies likelihood derivation; paper lacks discussion of failure modes when ambiguity persists.  
- Key claim that successive And-Or fragments can construct any context-free AOG is stated but needs a proof sketch in the main text.  
- Factorization of likelihood gain is intuitive but the derivation and assumptions are not fully shown in the main paper.  
- Experiments show F-measure/KL/perplexity improvements, but event test sets are small (6 videos) and no statistical significance is reported.  
- Limited ablations: missing comparisons to And-only, Or-only, and standard EM vs Viterbi to isolate contributions.  
- Baselines could be broader (e.g., HDP-PCFG, SPN structure learning, recent grammar induction methods).  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity (α, sa/so, beam size) and complexity scaling (n, vocabulary, relation types) are insufficiently analyzed.  
- Paper is generally clear and well organized, but several important derivations and definitions (posterior gain, grammar size, coding constants) are relegated to the supplement.  
- Reproducibility details lacking for event modifications, smoothing for perplexity, and terminal extraction/quantization.  
- Unified And-Or fragment idea and posterior scoring via sufficient statistics are original contributions with cross-domain applicability.  
- Significance is meaningful for structured generative modeling and grammar induction, though tempered by assumptions and scalability concerns.  
- Pros: principled unified And/Or learning, efficient sufficient-statistics scoring, cross-domain validation, practical algorithmic choices.  
- Cons: reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi approximations, key claims deferred to supplement, limited ablations/baselines, unclear scalability and sensitivity.  
- Suggestions: provide proof sketch of fragment expressiveness; quantify sensitivity and runtime complexity; add ablations (And-only/Or-only, EM vs Viterbi, unambiguity bias); report confidence intervals/statistical tests; compare to SPN/HDP-PCFG; clarify smoothing and provide code/parameters.  
- Overall: solid, timely contribution that advances unsupervised AOG structure learning; leans toward acceptance if clarity and evaluation breadth issues are addressed.
