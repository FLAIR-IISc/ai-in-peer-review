- Proposes a modality-agnostic, unified formalization of context-free stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) and an unsupervised algorithm that jointly learns structure and parameters from unannotated data.  
- Learning starts from a memorizing grammar and iteratively inserts And-Or fragments (an And-rule over new Or-nodes) chosen to maximize posterior under a sparsity prior.  
- Uses Viterbi likelihood to approximate data likelihood and computes posterior gain efficiently from sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor over configurations and a context matrix.  
- Central derivation factorizes likelihood gain into coherence measures of these arrays, and prior gain balances model growth; key proofs are deferred to the supplement.  
- Relies on an unambiguity assumption for gain computation; implications and robustness of this assumption are only qualitatively discussed.  
- Algorithmic search is greedy/beam with random restarts; complexity, memory, and scalability (especially building n-gram tensors across relation types) are not deeply analyzed.  
- Encoding costs (sa, so) and sensitivity to sparsity hyperparameter α are specified but not thoroughly examined.  
- Event experiments use pre-annotated atomic action segments (so “unsupervised” is structure learning given tokens), datasets are small (55/6 split), and no statistical significance analysis is provided.  
- Image experiments include a synthetic dataset with ground-truth grammar and a real animal-face dataset; results show clear gains over prior work in synthetic metrics (F/KL) and lower perplexity on real images.  
- Ablation studies (And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or fragments), sensitivity analyses, and runtime comparisons are missing.  
- Paper is well-organized with clear definitions and helpful figures, but notation is dense and several central derivations are in the supplement.  
- Practical details on constructing tensors/matrices, defining “contexts,” and handling multiple relation types need more elaboration.  
- The joint induction of compositions and reconfigurations via And-Or fragments is an original and notable contribution beyond prior AOG structure-learning work.  
- Using sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor and context matrix) and factorizing posterior gain appears novel and potentially impactful.  
- The unified formalization across modalities is incremental but useful; Viterbi objective and sparsity prior align with established grammar induction practices.  
- Significance: learning compact, interpretable generative grammars across domains is important for ML, vision, and activity analysis, and scaling the sufficient-statistics search could broaden AOG applicability.  
- Empirical strengths are strongest in images; event results are competitive but limited by supervision level and dataset size.  
- Pros: clear unified formulation, principled posterior objective, efficient gain estimation from sufficient statistics, joint And/Or learning, and strong image benchmark improvements.  
- Cons: reliance on unambiguity assumption, key derivations in supplement, event experiments depend on annotated atoms and small test sets, limited ablations and sensitivity analysis, and modality-agnostic claim not fully validated.  
- Suggestions: include derivation sketches and constructive proof for fragment sufficiency; analyze sensitivity to α, sa, so; report memory/time complexity and scaling; add ablations and robustness tests to ambiguity/noise; test on noisy detectors or a small text dataset to support modality-agnostic claim.  
- Scope: fits NeurIPS topics (probabilistic models, structure learning, vision/activity applications); overall a technically interesting and potentially impactful contribution that would benefit from stronger theoretical exposition and empirical analysis.
