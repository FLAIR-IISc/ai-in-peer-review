This paper presents a unified, data-agnostic framework for the unsupervised learning of both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs). The authors formulate the problem as maximizing the posterior probability of the grammar, combining a Viterbi likelihood term with a sparsity prior. The core contribution is an iterative, bottom-up learning algorithm that starts with a trivial grammar and progressively introduces "And-Or fragments." This novel mechanism unifies the induction of compositional (And-rules) and reconfigurable (Or-rules) structures, which distinguishes it from prior work in image and event grammar learning [17, 18] that often handles these aspects separately. The method is related to classic grammar induction techniques [14, 15] but extends them to the more general AOG formalism.

**Strengths:**
*   **Quality and Originality:** The paper's main strength lies in its principled approach. The formulation of learning as posterior optimization is sound, and the derivation of an efficient method to calculate the posterior gain of adding an And-Or fragment, based on sufficient statistics, is a significant technical contribution. The central idea of learning via unified And-Or fragments is novel and well-motivated, addressing the data scarcity and redundancy issues associated with learning And-rules and Or-rules separately.
*   **Significance:** The method advances the state of the art in unsupervised structure learning for a powerful and general class of models. The empirical evaluation is strong, demonstrating superior performance against recent, relevant baselines on both event and image modeling tasks. The data-agnostic nature of the framework suggests broad applicability to other domains.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the learning process is effectively illustrated.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity:** While generally clear, Section 3.3 is dense, and the full derivation of the crucial posterior gain formulas is relegated to the supplementary material, making a complete assessment of the technical details difficult from the main paper alone.
*   **Quality:** The search for optimal fragments relies on greedy or beam search, which is a heuristic and may not find the global optimum. The implications of the "grammar unambiguity" assumption used for the gain calculation could also be discussed further.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   The proposed And-Or fragment approach is a novel and elegant solution to a key challenge in grammar induction.
*   The work is technically solid and provides a principled optimization framework.
*   The experiments demonstrate a significant performance improvement over existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The reliance on a heuristic search procedure is a minor limitation.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant contributions in originality, technical depth, and empirical results. It addresses a fundamental problem in unsupervised learning and is likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
