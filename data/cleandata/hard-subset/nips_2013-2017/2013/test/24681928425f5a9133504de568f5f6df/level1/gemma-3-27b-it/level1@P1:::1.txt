This paper presents an unsupervised approach to learning stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs), models useful for representing compositional and reconfigurable structures in data like images and events. The core idea is to iteratively induce compositions and reconfigurations starting from a trivial grammar, optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar. The authors address the challenge of simultaneously learning both the structure and parameters of the SAG, proposing a novel "And-Or fragment" learning strategy that unifies composition and reconfiguration.

This work builds upon a rich history of grammar learning in NLP (e.g., [8, 9, 11, 12, 14, 15, 16]) and its extension to computer vision ([1, 2, 3, 17, 18, 10]). Existing approaches often either fix the grammar structure and learn parameters, or struggle with the exponential search space of possible structures. The proposed approach aims to overcome these limitations with a unified, posterior-probability optimizing learning scheme.  The connection to sum-product networks [10] is noted, but not deeply explored. The work is also related to recent advances in structured prediction and graphical model learning.

**Strengths:**

* **Unified Formalization:** The authors provide a general framework for SAGs applicable to diverse data types, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* **Joint Structure & Parameter Learning:** Addressing both aspects of grammar learning is crucial, and the posterior probability optimization approach is principled.
* **And-Or Fragment Learning:** This is the core technical innovation.  Unifying composition and reconfiguration within a single fragment is a clever idea and potentially more robust than separate approaches. The theoretical justification involving sufficient statistics and efficiency gains is promising.
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments on event and image grammars provide evidence that the approach is competitive or superior to existing methods. The use of both synthetic and real datasets strengthens the evaluation.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Algorithm:** While the authors claim efficiency gains, the algorithm description is quite involved, particularly the details of the likelihood gain calculation and the use of n-gram tensors. It's difficult to assess the practical scalability of the approach without more detailed computational analysis.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the hyperparameter α (sparsity prior) is mentioned but not thoroughly discussed. Its impact on performance and sensitivity to its value should be investigated.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to previous work, particularly in image grammar learning ([17]), could be more comprehensive. Reporting confidence intervals or statistical significance tests would strengthen the conclusions.  The authors mention that [17] only learns from ID sequences – however, more details about the differences in setup (e.g., feature extraction, quantization methods) are warranted.
* **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** Showing examples of learned grammars (e.g., graphical representations) would provide more intuition about the types of structures the approach discovers. The learned grammars are largely presented through quantitative metrics.
* **Connection to SPNs:**  The paper mentions the relationship to sum-product networks (SPNs) but doesn’t fully exploit it. Exploring potential benefits from established SPN techniques (e.g., exact inference, parameter tying) could enrich the work.



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a challenging problem (unsupervised structure learning) with a novel and principled approach.
* The And-Or fragment learning strategy is a significant contribution with a theoretical justification.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the approach’s effectiveness on diverse datasets.
* The unified formalization of SAGs is valuable for broader applicability.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

* The algorithm’s complexity and potential scalability issues are a concern.
* The lack of thorough hyperparameter analysis and a more comprehensive comparison to related work weakens the empirical validation.
* The connection to SPNs feels underdeveloped.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and supported by promising empirical results. While some aspects of the algorithm’s complexity and the evaluation could be strengthened, the overall contribution is valuable to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance, contingent on minor revisions addressing the weaknesses mentioned above, specifically regarding a more thorough discussion of hyperparameters and a more detailed comparison to related work.  Adding some qualitative visualization of the learned grammars would also be beneficial.
