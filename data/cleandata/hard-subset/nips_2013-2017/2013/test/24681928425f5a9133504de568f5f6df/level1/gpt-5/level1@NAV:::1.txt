This paper proposes a unified, data-agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs) and an unsupervised algorithm that jointly learns structure and parameters by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments to maximize a sparsity-regularized posterior. Starting from a trivial grammar that exactly explains the training set, the method searches greedily/with beam search for fragments that increase posterior via a Viterbi likelihood objective and a size prior. A key contribution is scoring candidate fragments using sufficient statistics (an n-gram tensor over child choices and a context matrix), under an unambiguity assumption, which avoids repeated re-parsing. The approach relates to SCFG learning (Stolcke & Omohundro; Klein & Manning), sum-product networks (Poon & Domingos), and prior AOG work for images/events (Zhu et al.; Zhao & Zhu, NIPS 2011), while extending structure learning beyond fixed-rule sets (e.g., [17], [18]) and unifying composition and reconfiguration search. Experiments on event parsing and image grammars (synthetic and real) show competitive or better F-measure and significantly lower perplexity than prior methods.

Quality: The algorithm is technically sound overall, with a clear posterior objective and plausible rationale for Viterbi training and sparsity priors. The derivation of likelihood gain via tensor/matrix coherence is appealing, though it relies on grammar unambiguity and context-freeness; the paper would benefit from empirical checks of ambiguity and discussion of bias introduced by Viterbi training. Experimental comparisons are positive but limited in breadth (few baselines, no significance tests, limited ablation on fragment design and search).

Clarity: The paper is generally well organized and readable. Some notational density (likelihood gain equations, encoding cost parameters sa/so) could be clarified, and the search space and complexity of sub-tensor mining deserve tighter specification. More algorithmic pseudocode in main text would aid reproducibility.

Originality: The unified And-Or fragment induction that couples composition and reconfiguration is novel compared to separate And/Or induction and fixed-structure approaches; the sufficient-statistics formulation is also original in this context.

Significance: Results suggest practical gains and a promising general framework applicable across modalities. Demonstrations cover images and events; inclusion of a language or additional vision benchmark would further support generality.

Pros for acceptance:
- Unified And-Or fragment learning with principled posterior scoring
- Efficient sufficient-statistics-based evaluation; clear connection to coherence measures
- Strong empirical improvements on image grammar perplexity; competitive event parsing

Cons against acceptance:
- Reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi objectives without ablations or diagnostics
- Limited baseline coverage (e.g., HDP/Adaptor grammars, recent SPN/AOG structure learners)
- Incomplete analysis of scalability and search complexity; few sensitivity studies (α, beam width, fragment size)
- Generality claim not tested beyond two domains and hand-crafted terminalization/quantization choices
