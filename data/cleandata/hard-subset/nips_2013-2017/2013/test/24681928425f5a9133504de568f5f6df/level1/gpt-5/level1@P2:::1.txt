Summary and relation to prior work
The paper presents a unified, context-free formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) that is agnostic to the data modality (images, events, text) and an unsupervised algorithm that learns both structure and parameters from unannotated data. The key idea is to grow the grammar from a trivial initial grammar that exactly memorizes the training set by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments—each fragment comprises one And-rule and sibling Or-rules—so as to jointly induce compositions (And) and reconfigurations (Or). The objective is the posterior over grammars, combining a sparsity prior on grammar size with a Viterbi likelihood. To make search efficient, the authors derive a closed-form posterior gain for a candidate fragment using sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor counting covered configurations and a context matrix counting configuration-context co-occurrences. The likelihood gain factorizes into two coherence measures, and the prior gain balances the size of the fragment against the number of reductions it enables. They show applications to event grammar learning from human activity videos and image grammar learning on synthetic sketches (with known ground truth) and a real animal-face dataset, reporting improvements over prior AOG structure learning methods [17, 18] and earlier unsupervised grammar induction baselines [15].

This work builds on the AOG lineage in vision and events [1, 3, 7, 17, 18], connects to PCFG and Bayesian grammar induction [11–16], and is related to SPNs [10]. The main novelty versus [17, 18] is unified learning of And and Or via And-Or fragments with a principled posterior score, versus heuristic or decoupled search.

Quality
Technical soundness: The proposed posterior objective (size prior + Viterbi likelihood) is reasonable and consistent with prior findings that Viterbi training helps in grammar induction [20, 21]. The fragment-level posterior gain using sufficient statistics is elegant and appears to provide a computationally tractable search heuristic. However, several steps rely on assumptions or statements deferred to the supplement:
- The unambiguity assumption used to derive the likelihood gain is strong; AOGs for images/events are typically ambiguous. While the authors motivate it via unambiguity regularization [21], a discussion of failure modes when ambiguity persists would be valuable.
- The claim that “adding only And-Or fragments can construct any context-free And-Or grammar” is important to justify the restricted search space; a formal proof sketch in the main paper would strengthen the work.
- The factorization of likelihood gain into coherence measures is appealing, but the exact derivation and conditions (e.g., independence approximations) are not visible in the main text.

Experimental support: The event experiments compare variants (ID-based vs grounded to atomic actions) and show F-measure improvements, particularly on the harder synthetic-variant dataset. The image experiments include a synthetic setting with access to the true grammar (reporting F-measure and KL) and a real dataset (perplexity). These are appropriate metrics; the comparisons to [17, 18, 15] appear fair. Nonetheless:
- The event test sets are small (6 videos), and statistical significance is not reported.
- There is limited ablation: a comparison to learning with only And-fragments or only Or-fragments would directly support the central claim about the benefits of unified fragments. Similarly, comparing standard EM likelihood versus Viterbi would quantify the impact of that choice.
- More diverse baselines could be considered, e.g., HDP-PCFG variants [9], SPN structure learning, or recent grammar induction methods.
- Hyperparameters (α, encoding costs sa/so, beam size) and sensitivity analyses are minimally discussed.

Complexity: The method relies on constructing n-gram tensors and context matrices. The paper claims practical runtimes (minutes for 5k samples with >10 atoms), but a complexity analysis in terms of n, vocabulary size, and number of relation types would help assess scalability, especially for images where possible relations explode.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and well organized. The unified formalization, algorithm outline, and figures help. That said:
- Important claims and derivations (posterior gain, fragment expressiveness) are only referenced as “can be shown” or relegated to the supplement; including concise statements or lemmas would improve readability and confidence.
- The definition of grammar size and coding constants (sa, so) should be made explicit.
- For the event experiments, the modification for multiple events per video is clever; clarifying when the top-level And becomes Or and its effect on training versus inference would help.
- More detail on smoothing for perplexity and on quantization/terminal extraction would improve reproducibility.

Originality
The unified And-Or fragment proposal for joint structure induction of compositions and reconfigurations is novel relative to prior AOG structure learning, which typically treats And/Or separately. The posterior-based scoring with sufficient statistics and the connection to tensor/matrix coherence are also original contributions in this context. The generality across modalities is a strength.

Significance
Within the structured generative modeling and grammar induction communities, this is a meaningful contribution: it provides a practical, general method for unsupervised AOG structure learning and shows cross-domain applicability with improved empirical results. Broader impact may be tempered by the assumptions (unambiguity, context-free restriction) and by the scalability considerations, but the method enriches the toolbox for interpretable, compositional modeling.

Pros
- Unified, principled approach to learning And and Or structure via And-Or fragments.
- Posterior scoring with efficient sufficient statistics; clear intuition via coherence measures.
- Cross-domain evaluation (events and images); strong synthetic and real-data results vs prior AOG methods.
- Practical algorithm with reasonable runtimes and clear engineering choices (greedy/beam, restarts).

Cons
- Reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi approximations; limited discussion of their impact when violated.
- Key claims/derivations deferred to the supplement; limited theoretical guarantees in main paper.
- Limited ablations and small test sets; lack of broader baselines and significance tests.
- Scalability and sensitivity to α, fragment size, and relation types not thoroughly analyzed.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Provide a proof (or at least a proof sketch) that any context-free AOG can be constructed by successive And-Or fragments.
- Quantify sensitivity to α, sa/so, beam size, and n, and report a complexity analysis.
- Add ablations: And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or fragments; standard likelihood vs Viterbi; with/without unambiguity bias.
- Report confidence intervals or statistical tests on F-measures and perplexities; expand test sets if possible.
- Compare against an SPN structure learner or a nonparametric PCFG variant to broaden context.
- Clarify smoothing for perplexity and provide more reproducibility details (code, parameters).

Overall evaluation
This is a solid and timely contribution to unsupervised structure learning for AOGs, with a clear algorithmic idea, reasonable theory-backed heuristics, and promising empirical results across domains. Despite some approximations and missing theoretical details in the main text, the work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing clarity and evaluation breadth.
