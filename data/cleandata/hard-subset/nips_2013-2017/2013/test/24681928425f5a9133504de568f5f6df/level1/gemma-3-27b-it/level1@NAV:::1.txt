## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper presents a unified framework for unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs), applicable to diverse data types like events and images. The core contribution lies in simultaneously learning both the grammar structure and parameters, overcoming limitations of prior approaches that often rely on pre-defined structures. The authors propose iteratively inducing compositions and reconfigurations via “And-Or fragments” while optimizing posterior probability, a crucial improvement over separate composition/reconfiguration learning. This is a well-motivated problem, as manually specifying grammars is challenging, and SAGs offer a powerful, compact representation for compositional data.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with a good introduction to the problem and related work. The mathematical formulation, while dense, is generally accessible, and the explanation of And-Or fragments is helpful. The algorithm’s efficiency is addressed by leveraging sufficient statistics and formulating the posterior gain effectively. The experimental setup with event and image grammars is reasonable, and the comparison against existing methods demonstrates competitive or superior performance, particularly on the more complex event data. The authors acknowledge limitations and provide supporting details in the supplementary material.

However, the paper suffers from a few weaknesses. While the authors mention related work (e.g., sum-product networks), the connection to recent advances in probabilistic programming and deep generative models (like variational autoencoders or normalizing flows) which also tackle structure learning is not thoroughly explored. This is a missed opportunity to situate the work within a broader context.  Furthermore, the experiments, while showing promising results, lack detailed ablation studies. For example, quantifying the benefit of learning And-Or fragments *jointly* versus separately would strengthen the argument for their approach. Finally, the scaling properties of the algorithm, particularly with respect to the complexity of the data and grammar size, could be discussed further. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to joint structure and parameter learning in SAGs.
*   The And-Or fragment concept offers a principled way to balance exploration and exploitation during learning.
*   Strong experimental results on both event and image data.
*   Clear writing and thorough presentation of the methodology.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of related work in the broader context of deep generative models.
*   Lack of ablation studies to dissect the contributions of key design choices.
*   Insufficient analysis of the algorithm’s scalability.



**Overall Assessment:** I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning and merits acceptance, despite the noted weaknesses. The innovative approach, clear presentation, and promising experimental results outweigh the areas where further exploration would be beneficial. I rate it as a **Strong Accept**.
