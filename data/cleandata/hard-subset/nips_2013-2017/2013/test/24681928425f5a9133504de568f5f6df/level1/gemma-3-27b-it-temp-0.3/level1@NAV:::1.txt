## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper presents a novel approach to unsupervised learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs), aiming to learn both the structure and parameters of these grammars directly from data. SAGs are presented as a powerful tool for modeling compositional and reconfigurable data, applicable to domains like images and events. The core contribution lies in a unified formalization of SAGs and an iterative learning algorithm that induces compositions and reconfigurations simultaneously, optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar. This work builds upon previous research in stochastic grammars (e.g., [8, 9, 10]) and existing approaches to learning image [1, 2, 3] and event grammars [4, 5, 6, 7]. 

The authors clearly identify the limitations of previous methods, which often rely on manually specified structures or separate learning of compositions and reconfigurations. Their proposed "And-Or fragments" offer a compelling solution by jointly learning these aspects, potentially leading to more robust and efficient learning, especially with limited data. The theoretical grounding, including the formulation of the posterior gain and its efficient computation using sufficient statistics, is a significant strength. Empirical evaluation on event and image grammars demonstrates competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods [17, 18].

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The unified approach to learning both structure and parameters of SAGs, along with the introduction of And-Or fragments, is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The optimization of posterior probability and the efficient computation of the gain are well-motivated and presented.
* **Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on diverse datasets.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the concepts are explained clearly, although the mathematical notation could benefit from further clarification in some sections.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity:** The algorithm, while theoretically sound, appears complex and may be difficult to implement and tune. More details on the practical considerations of implementation would be beneficial.
* **Scalability:** The paper doesn't explicitly address the scalability of the approach to very large datasets or complex grammars.
* **Limited Comparison:** While the results are competitive, a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, particularly those utilizing deep learning techniques, would strengthen the claims.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of the hyperparameter α on the learning process is not thoroughly discussed.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a challenging problem with a novel and theoretically grounded approach.
* The empirical results are promising and demonstrate the potential of the proposed method.
* The work contributes to the broader field of probabilistic modeling and structure learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the algorithm and lack of scalability discussion raise concerns about practical applicability.
* The limited comparison with recent deep learning approaches leaves room for doubt regarding the state-of-the-art performance.



**Overall:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While some aspects could be further strengthened, the novelty, theoretical soundness, and promising empirical results warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the scalability concerns and providing a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter sensitivity.
