This paper proposes an unsupervised approach to learning the structure and parameters of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs). The core idea is to iteratively induce compositions and reconfigurations via “And-Or fragments,” optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar. The authors aim for a unified formalization applicable to diverse data types, and demonstrate results on event and image grammars, achieving comparable or better performance than existing methods.

The paper builds upon previous work in stochastic grammars, initially developed for natural language processing, then extended to images and events. It addresses the challenge of manually specifying grammar structure, a common limitation in earlier approaches, by learning both structure and parameters simultaneously. The authors position their work relative to approaches that fix the grammar structure and optimize parameters (e.g., [11, 12, 10, 13]) and those that learn structure but often in a separate manner for compositions and reconfigurations (e.g., [14, 15, 16, 17, 18]). The proposed And-Or fragment concept aims to address the shortcomings of existing methods regarding data scarcity and ambiguity by learning these aspects jointly.  The connection to sum-product networks [10] is also acknowledged, providing further context within the broader field of probabilistic graphical models.

**Strengths:**

*   **Unified Formalization:** The paper successfully presents a general formalization of SAGs applicable to various data modalities.
*   **Joint Structure and Parameter Learning:** The approach tackles a challenging problem by learning both the grammar structure and its parameters concurrently, rather than relying on pre-defined structures.
*   **And-Or Fragment Concept:** The introduction of And-Or fragments is a novel idea that attempts to efficiently and robustly combine composition and reconfiguration learning. The theoretical justification and connection to tensor coherence measures provides a strong foundation.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:**  The experimental results on both event and image grammars demonstrate competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods. The use of both synthetic and real datasets is beneficial.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and clearly presents the methodology and experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Algorithm Description:** While the core ideas are well articulated, the detailed explanation of the algorithm, especially the likelihood gain computation, can be challenging to follow without carefully studying the supplementary material.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The performance appears sensitive to the choice of parameters (e.g., α), and a more thorough exploration of hyperparameter tuning and its impact would be valuable.
*   **Comparison limitations:** The comparison with previous methods [15, 18] is limited by the fact that those methods cannot directly utilize the same features (atomic actions). More comparable baselines, perhaps adapted to work with atomic actions, would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Scalability:** Although the authors mention the runtime is reasonable for the tested dataset sizes, the scalability to much larger datasets and more complex grammars is not discussed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to structure learning in SAGs with a principled optimization objective.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness on multiple datasets, surpassing existing methods in some cases.
*   Clear presentation of the method and experimental setup.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in applying SAGs to real-world problems.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Algorithm description is complex, potentially hindering reproducibility.
*   Hyperparameter sensitivity and limited baseline comparisons raise concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the results.
*   Scalability considerations are not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning in stochastic And-Or grammars. The proposed approach is novel, theoretically sound, and supported by promising empirical results. While there are some weaknesses regarding algorithmic complexity, hyperparameter sensitivity, and scalability, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, focusing on clarifying the algorithmic details, performing a more thorough hyperparameter analysis, and discussing potential scalability limitations. Specifically, including a sensitivity analysis of α, and considering alternative baselines utilizing the same feature set would be beneficial.
