This paper introduces a novel approach to the unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammar structures. A key contribution is the induction of unified AND-OR fragments, which contrasts with previous methods that search for AND and OR operations separately. The proposed algorithm can efficiently compute the value of a new fragment using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on tasks involving the parsing of events and visual objects.

This is a well-written paper that addresses an important problem. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and appears to be technically sound, although the derivations in the supplementary material were not verified for this review.

However, there are a few points that could be clarified to improve the paper's accessibility and impact:

**Technical Clarity:**
While the technical approach is generally well-described, two aspects were unclear and should be explicitly addressed:
1.  **Likelihood Gain Measure:** The paper introduces a "surrogate measure" for the likelihood gain. It should be clarified whether this measure is an exact calculation or an approximation.
2.  **Grammar Arity:** It is not clear if the proposed method can extend to learning fragments with an arity greater than two, or if it is restricted to learning grammars in a "Chomsky normal form." Clarifying the scalability of the approach with respect to rule arity would be valuable.

**Experimental Evaluation:**
My primary concerns relate to the experimental section. The presentation seems to assume significant familiarity with a specific line of prior work, making it difficult for a broader audience to fully grasp the methodology and interpret the results. Specifically, the descriptions of the experimental setup, data representation, and the nature of the learned structures are too condensed.

A significant missed opportunity is the lack of qualitative results. The paper's motivation emphasizes the importance of learning meaningful structure, but the evaluation is purely quantitative. While the reported performance metrics are satisfactory, they are not particularly illuminating on their own. The paper would be far more compelling if it included examples of the interesting grammatical structures discovered by the model. Furthermore, a qualitative comparison showing that the proposed method learns more plausible or insightful structures than competing approaches would greatly strengthen the paper's claims.

**Conclusion and Recommendations:**
In summary, this paper presents a promising and novel contribution to the unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. Its primary weakness lies in the experimental section, which lacks the detail and qualitative examples needed for the results to be fully appreciated by a general audience.

If the paper is accepted, I strongly urge the authors to use the supplementary materials to provide a much more detailed and accessible explanation of their experiments. This should include:
*   Concrete illustrations of the data representation.
*   Visualizations of the learned grammar fragments and parsed outputs.
*   Examples of the specific structures discovered in the event and visual object domains.

Showcasing the qualitative nature of the learned structures would substantially improve the paper's contribution and better justify its claims about structure discovery.
