This paper proposes a new approach to the unguided teaching of AND-OR grammar structure. Unlike previous approaches, the current one motivates integrated AND-OR pieces rather than exploring separate AND and OR operations. The value of the proposed new AND-OR section can be efficiently calculated using a set of sufficient statistics. This approach is evaluated on several tasks, including analyzing phenomena and visual objects.

It is generally a good paper. The problem is significant. The approach is novel and well-motivated. The algorithm seems technically sound, but I was unable to verify the supporting derivations. Some things were unclear to me, which may reflect my own limited time for reading, but I would urge the authors to clarify these points more clearly: First, it was not clear whether the "surrogate measure" of probability gain was an approximate or precise notion. Second, it was not clear whether the approach could extend to learning pieces with more than two - आर्यता, or whether it could only learn grammar in a "Chomsky general form," in a detectable manner.

My main concerns with the paper are focused on the experiments. I had trouble understanding most aspects: exactly what was done, how the examples were represented, and what was learned. I got the impression that the ideal audience for the paper is a reader who has followed, thoroughly studied, and preferably re-applied all the recent visual-grammar papers coming from S.C. Zuu's group. For such a reader, the experimental section would probably be more understandable, and the results would be more useful. But for outsiders, it was less clear. I understand that the small NIPS format has its limitations. But fortunately, NIPS allows for supplementary material, and if the paper is accepted, I would urge the authors to include more details and concrete examples of their experiments in their supplement.

I would especially like to see (or be able to see) examples of any interesting structure discovered in the learned grammar. The paper has a great set about the importance of learning grammar and structure, but then it is a bit disappointing for these expectations that the results are presented purely quantitatively. I agree with the authors that the quantitative results are satisfactory in themselves. But they are not very exciting or illuminating. I would find the paper more compelling if the authors had shown that the interesting structure was learned, and the more interesting structure was learned from this approach, relative to competing approaches. This is an interesting paper on the unguided teaching of AND-OR grammar. While I liked it, I had trouble following the experiments and interpreting the results, due to not being familiar with a lot of prior work (mostly from Zuu's UCLA group) that seemed overwhelming on it. If accepted, the authors should include supplementary material with more details and illustrations of how the experiments work and what interesting structure was learned.
