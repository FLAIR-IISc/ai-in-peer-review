This paper presents a method for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars. These grammars, incorporating both AND and OR rules, provide a powerful framework for representing recursive phenomena across diverse domains, including natural language and image analysis. The authors propose a novel approach to unsupervised structure learning by iteratively introducing new AND-OR fragments and evaluating the resulting changes in model likelihood and prior probability.

The method is evaluated on two tasks: learning event grammars and learning image grammars, achieving results competitive with existing approaches in both cases. 

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a tractable approach to learning stochastic grammars based on AND and OR rules. However, several points could be strengthened.

First, the authors do not discuss the computational scalability of their learning algorithm. It would be valuable to understand how performance degrades as the number of data points increases. Specifically, addressing whether the approach can scale to datasets with thousands of samples would be beneficial. 

Second, given the origins of stochastic grammars in natural language processing, including experiments on standard natural language datasets would significantly strengthen the paper. Demonstrating performance on a benchmark like the Penn Treebank, where previous work by researchers such as Klein and Manning has focused, would provide a valuable comparison point and highlight the potential of this approach in a well-studied domain.

In summary, this paper introduces a promising method for estimating the structure and parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars, demonstrating positive results on event and image grammar learning. Expanding the experimental evaluation, particularly to include natural language data, and discussing computational scalability would further enhance the contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to jointly learning structure and parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars.
*   Sound theoretical foundation, optimizing posterior probability with efficient computation via sufficient statistics.
*   Competitive or superior performance on two challenging tasks (event and image grammars).
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of discussion regarding scalability to large datasets.
*   Absence of experiments on natural language data, a core application area for stochastic grammars.
