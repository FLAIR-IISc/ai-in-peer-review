

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and proposes an unsupervised approach to learning both the structure and parameters of such grammars. The authors address a significant challenge in grammatical modeling: the difficulty of manually specifying grammar structures while maintaining sufficient expressive power. Previous approaches either relied on fixed grammar structures (e.g., [10, 13]) or learned compositions and reconfigurations separately (e.g., [14, 15, 17, 18]), which can lead to suboptimal grammars or inefficient learning. The proposed method differs by inducing unified And-Or fragments that systematically cover multiple compositions, avoiding the limitations of separate And-fragment and Or-fragment learning approaches. This work extends the authors' prior research on structure learning [17, 18] while addressing key limitations in previous natural language grammar learning approaches [14, 15, 16].

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical foundations. The authors formally define stochastic And-Or grammars in a data-type-agnostic manner, providing a clear foundation for their learning approach. The algorithm efficiently computes the posterior gain of candidate fragments using sufficient statistics derived from training data, making the learning process computationally feasible. The theoretical development of likelihood and prior gains (Section 3.3) is well-justified, and the approach's ability to construct any context-free And-Or grammar through And-Or fragments is a significant theoretical contribution.

The paper's strengths lie in its technical soundness, originality, and significance. The approach represents a meaningful advance over previous methods by unifying composition and reconfiguration learning, which addresses the data scarcity problem associated with separate fragment learning. The experimental results on event grammars (Section 4.1) and image grammars (Section 4.2) demonstrate competitive or superior performance compared to prior work, with particularly strong results on the more challenging synthetic event dataset. The paper's significance is enhanced by its applicability to multiple domains (images, events) through a unified framework.

However, several aspects require clarification to strengthen the paper. First, the paper states that the "surrogate measure" of likelihood gain is based on coherence measures, but it's not entirely clear whether this is an exact formulation or an approximation. This distinction is important for understanding the theoretical guarantees of the approach. Second, the paper doesn't address whether the method can extend to fragments with arity greater than 2, which would be valuable for modeling more complex structures. The authors should clarify whether the approach is limited to grammars in "Chomsky normal form" or can handle more general grammatical structures.

The experimental section presents a significant challenge for readers unfamiliar with the Zhu group's prior work. The paper assumes deep familiarity with their visual grammar frameworks (e.g., [1, 3, 7, 17, 18]), which makes the experimental setup difficult to follow for the broader NIPS audience. The authors present quantitative results (Table 2, Table 3) but fail to provide concrete examples of the grammars learned or illustrate the actual structures discovered. The paper's introduction emphasizes the importance of "interesting structure" in grammars, yet the results section presents only numerical metrics without showing how the learned grammars differ from previous approaches or what meaningful patterns they capture. This is a missed opportunity to demonstrate the practical value of the approach.

The clarity of the paper would be significantly improved by including specific examples of learned grammars, particularly for the event and image domains. For instance, showing a small fragment of the learned event grammar that captures a meaningful temporal pattern or demonstrating how the image grammar models compositional structure would greatly enhance understanding. The paper could also benefit from a more detailed explanation of the experimental setup, particularly regarding how the "co-occurring" and "followed-by" relations were defined and implemented.

The paper is technically sound and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of structure learning. The proposed approach is well-motivated, novel, and empirically validated across multiple domains. The main concerns are with clarity and accessibility rather than technical quality. The authors should consider expanding the experimental section with concrete examples of learned structures in the supplementary material, as this would significantly strengthen the paper's impact and accessibility to a broader audience.

To summarize the key points for the program committee:
- Strengths: Novel unified approach to grammar structure learning, theoretically sound, efficient algorithm, strong empirical results across multiple domains.
- Weaknesses: Some theoretical details require clarification (surrogate measure, arity limitations), experimental section lacks concrete examples of learned structures, assumes familiarity with prior work from the Zhu group.

This paper addresses an important problem in structure learning with a technically sound and well-motivated approach. While the experimental presentation could be improved with more concrete examples, the core contribution is significant and represents a meaningful advance over previous work. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should provide additional examples of learned grammars in the supplementary material.
