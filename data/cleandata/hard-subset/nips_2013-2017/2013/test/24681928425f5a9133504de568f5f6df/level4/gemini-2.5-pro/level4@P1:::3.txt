This paper proposes a new method for the unsupervised learning of And-Or grammar structures. The central innovation is the induction of unified And-Or fragments, which contrasts with previous approaches that search for And-rules and Or-rules separately. A key aspect of the method is that the posterior gain from adding a new fragment can be computed efficiently using a set of sufficient statistics. The proposed approach is evaluated on tasks involving parsing events in videos and objects in images.

Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important and challenging problem. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and the algorithm appears technically sound, although I was not able to verify the derivations provided in the supplementary material.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Importance:** The unsupervised induction of grammatical structure from raw data is a fundamental problem in machine learning and AI, with applications across many domains.
*   **Novelty:** The idea of learning unified And-Or fragments, rather than treating compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules) separately, is an elegant and compelling contribution.
*   **Motivation:** The paper clearly motivates why this unified approach may be more robust and efficient, especially in cases of data scarcity.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

My main concerns with the paper relate to the experimental evaluation.

1.  **Clarity of Experiments:** I found it difficult to follow the details of the experimental setup, including the precise data representations, procedures, and the nature of the learned grammars. The presentation seems to assume a high degree of familiarity with a specific line of recent work on visual grammars, much of it from the senior author's research group. While this makes the paper concise, it presents a significant barrier to readers from outside this specific sub-community. The short NIPS format has its limitations, but supplementary materials can be used to address this. If the paper is accepted, I strongly urge the authors to provide a more detailed, self-contained description of the experimental setup in an appendix to improve clarity and reproducibility.

2.  **Lack of Qualitative Results:** The paper’s introduction does an excellent job of motivating the importance of learning compositional structures. However, the results section is purely quantitative. This feels like a missed opportunity. While the quantitative results are satisfactory and demonstrate improved performance over prior work, they are not particularly illuminating on their own. The paper would be significantly more compelling if it included qualitative examples of the grammar structures that were actually discovered. Visualizing some of the learned fragments would provide valuable insight into what the model is capturing and would more directly support the paper's central claims about structure learning. It would be particularly powerful to see a comparison of the structures learned by the proposed method versus those learned by competing approaches.

**Questions for the Authors:**

I have two questions regarding the technical details, which could be clarified in a revised version:

*   The term "surrogate measure" is used in relation to the likelihood gain (Section 3.3). Is this measure an approximation, or is it an exact reformulation under the stated assumption of grammar unambiguity?
*   The examples seem to focus on binary compositions. Is the approach limited to learning fragments with an arity of 2 (i.e., grammars in or reducible to Chomsky normal form), or can it tractably learn rules with higher arity?

In summary, this paper presents a novel and technically interesting contribution to grammar induction. However, its current accessibility and impact are limited by an opaque experimental section and a lack of qualitative results. Addressing these issues by clarifying the experimental setup and, most importantly, showcasing the learned structures would greatly strengthen the paper and make its contributions more tangible and exciting.
