This paper proposes a new approach to unsupervised learning of the structure of AND-OR grammars. Unlike prior work, it induces unified AND-OR fragments rather than searching separately for AND and OR operations. The value of a proposed fragment can be computed efficiently using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on parsing tasks for events and visual objects.

Overall, this is a solid paper. The problem is important, the approach is novel and well-motivated, and the algorithm appears technically sound, though I was not able to verify the supplemental derivations. A few points were unclear to me—some of this may reflect my limited reading time, but I encourage the authors to mark these more clearly. First, is the “surrogate measure” of the likelihood gain an approximation or an exact quantity? Second, can the method be extended to learn fragments with arity greater than 2, or is it restricted to grammars in Chomsky normal form for tractability?

My main concerns are with the experiments. I had difficulty understanding exactly what was done, how the examples were represented, and what was learned. I came away with the impression that the ideal audience is a reader who has closely followed, studied in depth, and preferably re-implemented recent visual-grammar work from S. C. Zhu’s group. For such readers, the experimental section would likely be clearer and the results more immediately useful; for outsiders, it is harder to follow. I recognize the constraints of the NIPS format, but since supplementary material is permitted, if the paper is accepted I strongly urge the authors to provide a detailed supplement with concrete illustrations, data representations, and step-by-step descriptions of the experimental procedures.

I would especially like to see examples of interesting structure discovered in the learned grammars. The paper effectively motivates the importance of grammar and structure learning, but the results are presented purely quantitatively. While I agree that these quantitative results are satisfactory on their own terms, they are not very illuminating. The paper would be more compelling if it demonstrated the learned structures explicitly, and showed that this approach discovers more interesting or meaningful structure than competing methods.

In sum, this is an interesting paper on unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. I liked it, but I struggled to follow the experiments and interpret the results without deep familiarity with prior work (largely from Zhu’s UCLA group). If accepted, the authors should include supplementary material with more details and illustrations of the experimental setups and the structures learned.
