This paper introduces a method for the unsupervised learning of both the structure and parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars. These grammars, which incorporate both compositional (AND) and alternative (OR) rules, are highly expressive and suitable for modeling recursive phenomena in domains like language and vision. The authors propose an elegant learning framework that incrementally introduces new AND-OR fragments to an existing grammar, retaining them only if they improve a scoring function based on data likelihood and a model prior. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on two tasks—learning event grammars from video and image grammars from scenes—where it achieves results that are competitive with prior art.

The primary strength of this paper is its presentation of a principled and seemingly tractable approach to the challenging problem of grammar induction. The incremental, bottom-up strategy of proposing and evaluating new grammatical fragments is intuitive and provides a clear framework for structure learning. The empirical results on event and image grammars are compelling and successfully demonstrate the method's utility on non-trivial problems.

Despite these strengths, the paper could be significantly improved by addressing the following points:

1.  **Scalability and Computational Analysis:** The paper lacks a discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed learning algorithm. While the experiments are successful on the chosen datasets, it is unclear how the method would perform on significantly larger datasets, for instance, with thousands or tens of thousands of data points. What is the computational cost of proposing and evaluating new fragments as the grammar and dataset grow? An analysis of the algorithm's tractability, either theoretical or empirical, would greatly strengthen the paper and clarify its applicability to large-scale problems.

2.  **Absence of Natural Language Experiments:** A significant omission is the lack of experiments on natural language, which is a canonical application domain for grammar induction and arguably the most intuitive use case for the proposed formalism. A large body of previous work on unsupervised grammar induction, such as the influential work by Klein and Manning, has been evaluated on standard benchmarks like the Penn Treebank. Applying the proposed method to such a dataset would provide a crucial point of comparison and a more thorough evaluation of its capabilities relative to established baselines. It would be valuable for the authors to comment on whether their method, in its current form, is applicable to natural language parsing and, if so, what challenges they foresee.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and well-motivated method for the unsupervised induction of stochastic AND-OR grammars. The approach is sound, and the results on event and image data are promising. However, the paper's impact is currently constrained by the lack of analysis on computational scalability and the absence of experiments in the critical domain of natural language. Addressing these points, particularly by demonstrating the method's utility on a standard natural language corpus, would significantly enhance the contribution.
