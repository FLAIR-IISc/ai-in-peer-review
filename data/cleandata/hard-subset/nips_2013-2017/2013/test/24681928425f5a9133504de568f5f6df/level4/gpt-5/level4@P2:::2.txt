This paper proposes an unsupervised method to learn both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs). The authors present a unified, data-agnostic formalization of context-free AOGs and introduce an iterative learning algorithm that grows the grammar by adding And-Or fragments. Each fragment jointly captures compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules), and is selected by optimizing a posterior objective that balances Viterbi likelihood improvements with a sparsity prior on grammar size. The method leverages sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) to efficiently estimate the likelihood gain and sets Or-rule probabilities from observed reductions. Experiments on event and image grammars demonstrate competitive or superior performance to prior approaches.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on unsupervised grammar induction in NLP (e.g., model merging and unsupervised PCFG learning), And-Or models for images/events, and connections to sum-product networks. Unlike approaches that fix grammar structure and only learn parameters, this work performs joint structure and parameter learning with a principled posterior criterion and a unified treatment of And/Or expansions. The And-Or fragment search extends prior image/event AOG learning by coupling composition and reconfiguration, which addresses data scarcity and redundancy issues noted in earlier methods.

Strengths:
- Clear, unified formalization of AOGs applicable across modalities (text, images, events).
- Principled learning objective: posterior maximization with an explicit sparsity prior; Viterbi likelihood is a practical surrogate.
- The And-Or fragment strategy is elegant and addresses key pitfalls of learning And- or Or-only fragments separately; it encourages compact, less redundant grammars.
- Efficient scoring via sufficient statistics reduces repeated parsing, making the search procedure tractable in practice.
- Empirical results on event parsing and image modeling show competitive to better performance than baselines, including lower perplexity on real images.

Weaknesses and questions:
1) Scalability is not fully characterized. While a prototype runs in minutes on 5,000 samples with >10 terminals each, the paper does not provide asymptotic complexity, runtime scaling curves, or memory usage for larger datasets. How does performance vary with the number of terminals, fragment arity, beam width, and grammar ambiguity?
2) Natural language evaluation is missing. Given the method’s stated generality and the close relationship to PCFG learning, an experiment on standard NLP benchmarks (e.g., Penn Treebank for unsupervised parsing, as in Klein & Manning and follow-ups) would be compelling. Even small-scale PTB subsets or synthetic CFG data would help assess performance on linguistic structures, ambiguity, and long-range dependencies.
3) Assumptions and biases: The derivations rely on unambiguity and Viterbi training. How sensitive is the approach to genuine ambiguity in target grammars? Can unambiguity regularization or variational approximations help when Viterbi is a poor proxy?
4) Search details: The paper mentions greedy/beam search with random restarts but gives few details on beam sizes, stopping criteria, or sensitivity to the sparsity hyperparameter α and code-length constants (sa, so). Ablations on these choices and on fragment size/types would strengthen the claims.
5) Clarity of empirical setup: For events, the three application variants differ in important ways (ID mapping vs. grounded actions; ordering constraints). More detail on how “contexts” are defined for temporal relations, how overlapping events are handled, and the effect of the late conversion of top-level And-nodes to Or-nodes would aid reproducibility.

Quality: The approach is technically sound overall, with a coherent objective and plausible approximations. Some theoretical assumptions (unambiguity) and search heuristics should be discussed more explicitly, along with complexity analyses and sensitivity studies.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written. The learning procedure would benefit from more algorithmic detail (pseudocode, hyperparameters, beam widths), clearer definitions of context matrices/tensors for each domain, and expanded figure captions. Reporting runtime and memory for each experiment would improve transparency.

Originality: The unified And-Or fragment induction and posterior-based scoring across domains are novel and thoughtfully motivated. The integration of sufficient statistics for efficient scoring is a practical contribution.

Significance: If scalable, the method could be a useful tool for unsupervised structure learning in multiple modalities. Its ability to induce compact, reconfigurable grammars is valuable, though broader validation—especially on NLP benchmarks—would increase impact.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide complexity analysis and empirical scaling (data size, terminals, fragment arity, beam width).
- Add a natural language experiment (e.g., PTB), or at least synthetic CFG benchmarks demonstrating recovery under ambiguity.
- Include sensitivity analyses for α, sa/so, and Viterbi vs. full likelihood or regularized alternatives.
- Expand experimental details and ablations, and report runtimes.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Principled, unified framework with a clear motivation and practical efficiency.
- Joint induction of compositions and reconfigurations via And-Or fragments is elegant and effective.
- Competitive-to-strong results on two distinct domains.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited evidence of scalability; no NLP experiments despite clear relevance.
- Reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi training without thorough analysis of their impact.
- Insufficient ablations and empirical detail to assess robustness.
