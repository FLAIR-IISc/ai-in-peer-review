This paper proposes a novel approach to unsupervised learning of the structure of AND-OR grammars, differing from previous methods in its induction of unified AND-OR fragments rather than separate searches for AND and OR operations. The value of a proposed new AND-OR fragment can be efficiently computed using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on several tasks, including parsing events and visual objects.

Overall, this is a good paper that tackles an important problem with a well-motivated and novel approach. The algorithm appears to be technically sound, although I did not have the opportunity to thoroughly check the supplemental derivations. However, there were a few points that were unclear to me, which may be due to my limited time for reading, but I would recommend that the authors clarify these points: Firstly, it was unclear whether the "surrogate measure" of the likelihood gain was an approximate or exact notion. Secondly, it was unclear whether the approach could be extended to learning fragments with an arity greater than 2, or whether it could only tractably learn grammars in "Chomsky normal form".

My main concerns with the paper lie in the experimental section. I had difficulty understanding most aspects of the experiments, including the specifics of what was done, how the examples were represented, and what was learned. The paper seems to assume a high level of familiarity with recent visual-grammar papers from S. C. Zhu's group, which may make the experimental section more accessible to readers who are well-versed in this area. However, for readers who are not experts in this field, the experimental section was less clear. While I understand that the short NIPS format has limitations, I would suggest that the authors include more details and concrete illustrations of their experiments in the supplementary materials, should the paper be accepted.

In particular, I would have liked to see examples of any interesting structure discovered in the learned grammars. The paper sets up the importance of grammars and structure learning, but the purely quantitative presentation of the results was somewhat disappointing. While the authors acknowledge that the quantitative results are satisfactory on their own terms, they do not provide much insight or excitement. I would have found the paper more compelling if the authors could demonstrate that interesting structure is learned, and that more interesting structure is learned by this approach relative to competing approaches. 

This is an interesting paper on unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars, but I had trouble following the experiments and interpreting the results, likely due to my unfamiliarity with prior work from Zhu's UCLA group that it builds upon. If accepted, the authors should include supplementary material with more details and illustrations of how the experiments worked and what interesting structure was learned.
