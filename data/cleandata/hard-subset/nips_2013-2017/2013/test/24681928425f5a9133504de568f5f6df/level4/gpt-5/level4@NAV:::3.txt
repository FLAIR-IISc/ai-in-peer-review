This paper introduces a new method for unsupervised learning of the structure of AND-OR grammars. Unlike earlier approaches that search for AND and OR operations separately, this work induces unified AND-OR fragments. The value of a candidate AND-OR fragment can be computed efficiently using sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on several tasks, including parsing events and visual objects.

Overall, this is a strong paper. The problem is important, the approach is novel and well-motivated, and the algorithm appears technically sound, though I was unable to verify the supplementary derivations. A few points were unclear to me—perhaps due to limited reading time—but I encourage the authors to make them more explicit: First, is the “surrogate measure” of likelihood gain an approximation or an exact quantity? Second, can the method extend to learning fragments with arity greater than 2, or is it effectively restricted to grammars in Chomsky normal form?

My main concerns center on the experimental section. I found it difficult to understand exactly what was done, how the examples were represented, and what was learned. My impression is that the ideal audience is readers who have closely followed, deeply studied, and preferably re-implemented recent visual-grammar work from S. C. Zhu’s group. Such readers would likely find the experiments clearer and the outcomes more useful, whereas for outsiders the presentation is less accessible. I appreciate the constraints of the short NIPS format, but since NIPS allows supplementary materials, if the paper is accepted I strongly recommend including much more detail and concrete illustrations of the experiments.

I would especially have liked to see examples of interesting structures discovered in the learned grammars. The paper effectively motivates the importance of grammars and structure learning, but the exclusive focus on quantitative results is somewhat underwhelming relative to that setup. I agree that the quantitative results are satisfactory in their own right, but they are not particularly exciting or illuminating. The paper would be more compelling if it showed that the method learns interesting structures, and that it discovers more compelling structure than competing approaches.

In sum, this is an interesting paper on unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. While I liked it, I found the experiments hard to follow and the results difficult to interpret, in part because I am not thoroughly familiar with much of the prior work (largely from Zhu’s UCLA group) on which it heavily builds. If accepted, the authors should include supplementary material with more details and illustrations of how the experiments were conducted and what interesting structure was learned.
