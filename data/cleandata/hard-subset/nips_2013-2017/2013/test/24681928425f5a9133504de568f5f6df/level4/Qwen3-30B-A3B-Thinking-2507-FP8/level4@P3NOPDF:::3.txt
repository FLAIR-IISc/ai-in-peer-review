

This paper introduces a novel approach for unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammar structures, distinguishing itself by inducing unified AND-OR fragments rather than separately optimizing for AND and OR operations. The method efficiently computes fragment value using sufficient statistics, offering a technically sound framework. The problem is well-motivated, and the core idea represents a meaningful advance over prior work. However, several aspects require clarification to strengthen the paper’s impact and accessibility.

The primary technical concerns involve two ambiguities. First, the paper describes a "surrogate measure" for likelihood gain but does not specify whether this is an approximation or an exact computation. Clarifying this distinction is critical for assessing the method’s theoretical guarantees. Second, the approach’s applicability to fragments with arity greater than two is unclear. The paper implies a focus on Chomsky normal form grammars, but it is uncertain whether the method inherently restricts to binary fragments or can generalize. The authors should explicitly address these points in the main text.

The experimental evaluation presents more significant challenges. While the results on event parsing and visual object recognition appear quantitatively valid, the description assumes deep familiarity with recent work from S. C. Zhu’s group (e.g., specific representations, task formulations, and baseline comparisons). For readers outside this niche, the experimental setup remains opaque: it is unclear how examples were preprocessed, what structural elements were learned, or how the proposed fragments differ from prior grammars. This severely limits the paper’s broader impact. The experimental section reads as if written for an audience already versed in Zhu et al.’s prior publications, rather than the general NIPS community.

Crucially, the paper fails to leverage its strong theoretical foundation to demonstrate *what* structure is actually learned. The introduction compellingly argues for the importance of grammatical structure in unsupervised learning, yet the results are presented solely through quantitative metrics (e.g., parsing accuracy). This omission is a missed opportunity: the most compelling evidence would be concrete examples of novel, interpretable structures discovered by the method—such as hierarchical object decompositions or event sequences—alongside comparisons to competing approaches. Without such examples, the work risks appearing as a technical refinement rather than a substantive advance in structural learning.

While the authors acknowledge the limitations of NIPS’ page constraints, the supplemental materials offer a vital avenue for improvement. If accepted, the authors should include detailed experimental appendices with: (1) step-by-step illustrations of the grammar induction process on small, representative examples; (2) visualizations of learned structures (e.g., parse trees or object part hierarchies); (3) clear comparisons of structural properties between the proposed method and baselines; and (4) a concise glossary of domain-specific terms (e.g., "surrogate measure," "fragments") for broader accessibility. These additions would transform the experimental section from a black box into a transparent, compelling demonstration of the method’s value.

In summary, the paper makes a theoretically grounded contribution to unsupervised grammar learning. Its novelty and technical soundness are strengths, but the current presentation obscures these through ambiguous technical descriptions and an experimental section that fails to deliver on the promise of structural discovery. With the proposed clarifications and supplemental materials, this work could be significantly strengthened. As written, it is technically solid but lacks the interpretability and accessibility needed to resonate beyond a narrow subcommunity. I recommend acceptance *only if* the authors commit to addressing these issues in the final version, particularly through expanded supplementary materials.
