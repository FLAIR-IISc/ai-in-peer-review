This paper proposes a new approach to unsupervised learning of the structure of AND-OR grammars. Unlike prior work, it induces unified AND-OR fragments rather than searching separately for AND and OR operations. The value of a proposed new AND-OR fragment can be computed efficiently using a set of sufficient statistics. The method is evaluated on multiple tasks, including parsing events and visual objects.

Overall, this is a strong paper. The problem is important, the approach is novel and well-motivated, and the algorithm appears technically sound, though I was not able to check the supplemental derivations.

Two points were unclear and should be marked more explicitly:
- Is the “surrogate measure” of the likelihood gain an exact quantity or an approximation?
- Can the approach extend to learning fragments with arity greater than 2, or is it only tractable for grammars effectively constrained to Chomsky normal form?

My main concerns are about the experimental section. I found it difficult to understand exactly what was done, how the examples were represented, and what was learned. The presentation seems to presume a reader who has closely followed—and preferably re-implemented—recent visual-grammar work from S. C. Zhu’s group. For such a reader, the experiments would likely be more accessible and the results more informative; for outsiders, they are hard to parse. I recognize the constraints of the NIPS format, but since supplementary materials are permitted, if the paper is accepted I strongly urge the authors to include much more detail and concrete illustrations of the experimental setup, data representations, and learned outputs.

Relatedly, I would have liked to see qualitative examples of interesting structures discovered by the learned grammars. The paper’s motivation emphasizes the importance of grammar induction and structural learning, but the results are presented purely quantitatively. While those quantitative results appear satisfactory on their own terms, they are neither very illuminating nor especially exciting. The paper would be more compelling if it demonstrated that interesting structure is learned and that this approach uncovers more interesting or richer structure than competing methods.

In sum, this is an interesting paper on unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. I liked it, but I had trouble following the experiments and interpreting the results, particularly without deep familiarity with prior work the paper builds on. If accepted, the authors should provide supplementary material with detailed explanations and illustrations of the experiments and of the structures learned.
