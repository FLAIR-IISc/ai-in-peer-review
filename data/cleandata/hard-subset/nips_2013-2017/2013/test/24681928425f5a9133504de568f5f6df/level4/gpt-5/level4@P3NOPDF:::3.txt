Summary
The paper proposes a new approach for unsupervised learning of the structure of AND-OR grammars. Unlike prior work that searches separately for AND and OR operations, this method induces unified AND-OR fragments and evaluates their contribution efficiently using sufficient statistics. The approach is tested on event parsing and visual object parsing tasks.

Strengths
- Problem importance: Learning structured representations in an unsupervised manner is a central challenge with broad impact.
- Novelty and motivation: The idea of inducing unified AND-OR fragments is conceptually appealing and well-motivated, providing a coherent alternative to piecemeal searches over AND and OR components.
- Technical soundness: The algorithm appears sound from the paper’s description. The notion of computing the value of candidate fragments via sufficient statistics is elegant and suggests good scalability.
- Empirical scope: Evaluation on both events and visual objects indicates cross-domain applicability.

Points needing clarification
- Surrogate likelihood gain: It is unclear whether the “surrogate measure” for likelihood gain is an exact objective (e.g., a tight lower bound or a reformulation) or an approximation. Clarifying this status is important for understanding both theoretical guarantees and practical behavior.
- Fragment arity: Can the method learn fragments with arity greater than two, or is it restricted to binary structures akin to Chomsky normal form? If limited to binary composition, what are the implications for representational capacity and search efficiency? If higher-arity fragments are supported, please describe how tractability is maintained.
- Supplemental derivations: The paper references derivations placed in the supplement. While the main algorithm reads as technically sound, I was not able to verify the supplemental details. A concise summary of key lemmas and assumptions in the main paper would help.

Experimental concerns and requests
The main weaknesses are in experimental clarity and presentation:
- Experimental setup: I found it difficult to reconstruct precisely what was done—how data were represented, what the initial grammar or primitives were (if any), what constraints were imposed, and how search was conducted. The exposition appears to assume close familiarity with prior visual-grammar work, particularly from S. C. Zhu’s group. For readers outside that community, the section is hard to follow.
- Learned structures: The paper motivates grammar induction as a means to capture meaningful structure, yet the results presented are almost entirely quantitative. While the reported numbers are satisfactory on their own terms, they do not convey what was actually learned or why this approach is preferable in terms of interpretability or compositional quality.
- Baselines and comparability: It would help to articulate precisely how competing methods differ in search space, scoring, and representational assumptions, so that performance gaps can be interpreted as stemming from the unified-fragment induction rather than other factors.

Suggestions for improvement (especially for the supplement)
- Provide concrete qualitative examples:
  - Visualizations of learned AND-OR fragments and their composition into full parses.
  - Example parses for representative inputs, annotated to show which fragments fire where.
  - Side-by-side comparisons of fragments learned by this method versus baselines on the same data.
- Make the experiments self-contained:
  - Describe datasets, preprocessing, feature representations, and any supervision or constraints.
  - Detail initialization, search procedure, stopping criteria, and hyperparameters.
  - Report computational costs (runtime, memory) and how sufficient statistics enable the observed efficiency.
- Clarify the objective:
  - Make explicit whether the surrogate likelihood gain is exact, a bound, or a heuristic.
  - If approximate, discuss its bias/variance trade-offs and any empirical effects on search quality.
- Discuss arity and expressiveness:
  - State clearly whether higher-arity fragments can be learned and, if so, how their evaluation remains tractable.
  - If limited to binary forms, justify this choice and discuss practical workarounds (e.g., binarization) and their impact.
- Improve readability for non-specialists:
  - Briefly recap the specific AND-OR grammar formalism used and how it relates to widely known variants.
  - Summarize the key differences from prior approaches that search AND and OR separately, with a concise comparison table or bullet list (could be placed in the supplement).

Overall assessment
This is a strong and interesting paper with a well-motivated contribution and a plausible, efficient algorithmic framework for learning unified AND-OR fragments. The empirical results appear positive, but the paper would be significantly more compelling with clearer, more self-contained experimental descriptions and rich qualitative illustrations of the learned structures. Showing that the method discovers interpretable, nontrivial structure—and more of it relative to baselines—would substantively strengthen the case.

If accepted, I strongly recommend that the authors include detailed supplementary material addressing the above points, especially qualitative visualizations, experimental details sufficient for replication, and clarifications regarding the surrogate objective and fragment arity. These additions would make the work accessible and convincing to a broader audience beyond specialists familiar with prior visual-grammar frameworks.
