This paper proposes a novel approach to unsupervised learning of the structure of AND-OR grammars. Unlike previous methods, the proposed approach induces unified AND-OR fragments, rather than separately searching for AND and OR operations. The value of a proposed new AND-OR fragment can be computed efficiently using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on several tasks, including parsing events and visual objects.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that addresses an important problem. The approach is novel and well-motivated, and the algorithm appears to be technically sound. However, I did not have the opportunity to thoroughly review the supplemental derivations. A few aspects of the paper were unclear to me, which may be due to my limited time for reading, but I would suggest that the authors clarify these points: Firstly, it was not evident whether the "surrogate measure" of the likelihood gain is an approximate or exact notion. Secondly, it was unclear whether the approach can be extended to learning fragments with arity greater than 2, or whether it is limited to tractably learning grammars in "Chomsky normal form".

My primary concerns with the paper pertain to the experimental section. I found it challenging to understand most aspects of the experiments, including the methodology, representation of examples, and the learned results. The paper appears to be geared towards an audience familiar with recent visual-grammar papers from S. C. Zhu's group, and as such, the experimental section may be more comprehensible and the results more meaningful to this audience. However, for readers without this background, the experimental section was less clear. While I acknowledge the limitations of the short NIPS format, I would recommend that the authors include additional details and concrete illustrations of their experiments in the supplementary materials, should the paper be accepted.

In particular, I would have liked to see examples of interesting structures discovered in the learned grammars. The paper sets up the importance of grammars and structure learning, but the presentation of purely quantitative results was somewhat disappointing. Although the authors demonstrate satisfactory quantitative results, they are not particularly exciting or illuminating. I believe the paper would be more compelling if the authors could demonstrate that interesting structures are learned and that more interesting structures are learned using this approach compared to competing methods.

This is an interesting paper on unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. While I appreciated the paper, I had difficulty following the experiments and interpreting the results, largely due to my unfamiliarity with prior work, primarily from Zhu's UCLA group, that it heavily builds upon. If accepted, the authors should include supplementary material with more details and illustrations of how the experiments were conducted and what interesting structures were learned.
