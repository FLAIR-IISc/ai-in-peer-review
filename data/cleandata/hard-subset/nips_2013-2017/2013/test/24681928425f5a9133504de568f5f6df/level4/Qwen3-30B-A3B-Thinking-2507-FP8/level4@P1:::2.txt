

This paper proposes a unified framework for unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, which model recursive patterns with both compositionality (decomposing patterns into sub-patterns) and reconfigurability (multiple alternative configurations). The authors present a novel algorithm that iteratively learns grammar structure and parameters by optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar, starting from a trivial initial grammar. Their key innovation is the use of unified And-Or fragments—simultaneously learning new compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules)—to avoid the limitations of prior approaches that handle these separately. The method is formally grounded in posterior probability optimization and leverages sufficient statistics for efficient computation. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on event grammar learning (using human activity videos) and image grammar learning (on synthetic and real animal face datasets), outperforming prior methods in more complex scenarios.

The paper excels in several key areas. First, it provides a clean, data-type-agnostic formalization of And-Or grammars, enabling application across domains like images and events—a significant step beyond prior domain-specific formulations. Second, the unified learning of structure and parameters via posterior optimization is theoretically sound and avoids the ad-hoc heuristics common in previous structure learning work. Third, the empirical results are well-executed: the method achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on both event and image grammars, with the third experimental method (directly grounding to atomic actions) showing particular promise. The clarity of the algorithmic description and the insightful analysis of why unified fragments outperform separate And/Or fragment learning (e.g., mitigating data scarcity and redundancy) are notable strengths.

However, two critical limitations warrant consideration. First, the paper lacks a thorough analysis of computational scalability. The authors note that their prototype runs "within a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples," but this is insufficient for real-world applications where datasets often contain orders of magnitude more samples (e.g., natural language corpora). The algorithm's complexity with respect to grammar size, sample count, and fragment search space should be explicitly quantified—e.g., providing time complexity bounds or benchmarking on larger datasets—to assess practical viability. Without this, the claim of "reasonable efficiency" remains unsubstantiated.

Second, the absence of experiments on natural language data is a significant gap. Stochastic grammars were originally developed for language, and prior work (e.g., Klein & Manning, 2004; Tu & Honavar, 2008) has extensively applied such methods to language tasks like dependency parsing. The paper’s core contribution—learning grammars for recursive patterns—should be validated on this canonical application. Specifically, the authors should demonstrate whether their approach can learn grammars from standard datasets like the Penn Treebank, and how it compares to established language grammar induction methods. This omission weakens the demonstration of the method's general utility and limits its perceived significance for the NIPS community.

In summary, this is a technically strong paper with a novel and well-motivated approach to grammar learning. The unified And-Or fragment framework is a clear advance over prior structure learning methods, and the empirical results are compelling for event/image domains. However, the lack of scalability analysis and natural language experiments prevents the work from fully realizing its potential impact. Addressing these points would substantially strengthen the paper’s contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with the expectation that the authors address these concerns in the final version.
