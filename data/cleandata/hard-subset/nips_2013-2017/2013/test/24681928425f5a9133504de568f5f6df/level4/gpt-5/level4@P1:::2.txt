Summary
This paper proposes an unsupervised method for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), a context-free formalism that captures compositionality (And-rules) and reconfigurability (Or-rules) and has been applied to images and events. The authors offer a unified, data-agnostic definition of AOGs and introduce a bottom-up learning procedure that starts from a trivial grammar and iteratively inserts And-Or fragments—each comprising an And-node and its child Or-nodes—thereby jointly inducing compositions and alternatives. The learning objective maximizes the posterior P(G|X) under a sparsity prior on grammar size, with likelihood approximated by Viterbi likelihood (leveraging its known unambiguity bias). To make search efficient, the posterior gain from adding a fragment is computed from sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) rather than requiring full reparsing. The approach uses greedy or beam search with random restarts.

The method is related to earlier structure learning for PCFGs (e.g., Stolcke & Omohundro; Solan et al.; Tu & Honavar) and to AOGs for images and events (e.g., Si & Zhu; Pei et al.). Unlike approaches that fix the rule set and only learn parameters, this work learns structure and parameters together and unifies the discovery of compositions and reconfigurations by searching for And-Or fragments instead of separate And- or Or-fragments.

Experiments cover two domains. For event grammars, the authors evaluate three variants: (1) learning from ID sequences derived from framewise action co-occurrence (as in prior work), and (2)–(3) directly from action vectors with both “followed-by” and “co-occurring” relations, with (2) enforcing a particular learning order and (3) learning relations jointly. On two activity datasets, their method is competitive with or better than prior work, with joint learning (method 3) performing best. For image grammars, on a synthetic animal-face sketch dataset the method yields higher F1 and lower KL divergence to the ground-truth grammar than [17]. On a real animal-face dataset, 10-fold cross-validated perplexity improves substantially (e.g., 67.5 vs. 129.4 for [17]). The authors also note a prototype runtime of minutes on a desktop for 5,000 samples with >10 atomic patterns each.

Strengths
- Unified formulation and learning procedure applicable across modalities (text, images, events).
- Principled objective: explicit posterior maximization with a sparsity prior; Viterbi likelihood is a sensible choice in unsupervised grammar induction.
- Elegant And-Or fragment search that integrates composition and reconfiguration, improving robustness under data sparsity and avoiding redundancy.
- Efficient scoring of candidate fragments via sufficient statistics, avoiding repeated full parses.
- Solid empirical improvements on both synthetic and real datasets; flexibility to incorporate different relation types in And-rules.

Weaknesses and questions
1) Scalability and tractability: While the paper mentions a prototype that runs in minutes on 5,000 samples, a fuller analysis of runtime and memory scaling with dataset size, grammar size, fragment arity (n), beam width, and number of relation types would strengthen the claims. How does the cost of constructing and searching n-gram tensors and context matrices grow, and how does performance degrade with more complex relation sets?
2) Natural language evaluation: Since AOGs generalize PCFG-style modeling and the method builds directly on unsupervised grammar induction ideas, a natural testbed is natural language. Can the method learn on standard corpora such as the Penn Treebank (as in Klein & Manning and follow-ups)? Even small-scale or subset experiments would be informative: e.g., inducing unlabeled bracketing or a reduced tagset grammar. If not feasible currently, please discuss the obstacles (e.g., ambiguity assumptions, relation design, search space).

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The approach is technically sound and well-motivated. The posterior gain derivations, the use of sufficient statistics for efficient scoring, and the integration of parameter estimation (rule probabilities) with structure induction are strengths. The use of Viterbi likelihood and sparsity priors is in line with prior evidence. A more detailed complexity analysis and ablations (e.g., effect of And-Or vs. separate And/Or fragment search) would further bolster the case.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-organized, with clear motivation and a coherent algorithmic framework. Some parts are notation- and figure-heavy; providing brief intuition alongside equations (especially for the likelihood gain factorization and the roles of the n-gram tensor vs. context matrix) would aid readability.
- Originality: The And-Or fragment search and the unified posterior optimization across modalities are novel and nontrivial extensions over prior work that either fixed structure or treated And/Or induction separately.
- Significance: The method addresses a central challenge—unsupervised structure learning—in a class of models used across vision and event understanding, with promising results. Demonstrating applicability to natural language or to larger-scale vision/event datasets would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Unified, principled method that advances structure learning for AOGs.
- Conceptually appealing And-Or fragment induction that mitigates data sparsity issues.
- Empirical gains over strong baselines in two domains.
- Practical efficiency via sufficient statistics and greedy/beam search.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited analysis of scalability and computational complexity.
- Absence of natural language experiments on standard benchmarks, despite clear relevance.

Recommendation
Overall, this is a solid and promising contribution to unsupervised grammar induction for And-Or grammars. I lean positive, contingent on clarifying scalability and, if possible, adding or discussing feasibility of natural language experiments (e.g., on PTB) to demonstrate broader applicability.
