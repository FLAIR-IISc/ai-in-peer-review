

This paper introduces a novel method for unsupervised structure and parameter learning in stochastic AND-OR grammars, which model recursive phenomena through AND (conjunction) and OR (disjunction) rules. The authors propose an incremental approach that constructs grammars by adding fragments stepwise, evaluating both likelihood and prior terms to guide learning. The method is theoretically grounded and demonstrates competitive performance on two non-trivial tasks: event grammar learning and image grammar parsing. The clarity of the algorithmic description and the empirical results on these domains represent a solid contribution to the field of probabilistic grammars.

The paper’s core strength lies in its tractable framework for joint structure and parameter estimation—a significant challenge in stochastic grammar learning. By avoiding exhaustive search through the grammar space, the authors achieve a practical solution that outperforms prior methods on the evaluated tasks. The experiments are well-designed, with clear baselines and statistical validation, reinforcing the method’s efficacy in capturing hierarchical structure.

However, two critical gaps limit the paper’s impact. First, the computational tractability and scalability of the algorithm remain unaddressed. The authors do not discuss runtime complexity, memory requirements, or empirical scaling behavior with dataset size. Given that real-world applications (e.g., natural language processing) often involve thousands of examples, this omission is substantial. A brief analysis—such as runtime measurements on increasing dataset sizes or a complexity argument—would strengthen the claim of "tractability" and clarify the method’s practical viability.

Second, the absence of natural language experiments is a notable omission. Natural language is the most prominent application domain for AND-OR grammars, and the paper’s own motivation hinges on this. The authors cite Klein and Manning’s work on PCFGs as a benchmark for grammatical learning, yet they fail to test their method on standard NLP datasets like the Penn Treebank. Without such experiments, the paper cannot demonstrate whether the approach generalizes beyond event/image domains to the core application it targets. For instance:  
- Could the method learn syntactic structures from raw sentences?  
- How does it compare to PCFG-based parsers on standard metrics (e.g., F1 score)?  
- What are the limitations when applied to longer, more complex sentences?  
These questions are essential for establishing the method’s relevance to the broader NLP community.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing these points. For scalability, a short empirical study on synthetic or real-world datasets of varying sizes (e.g., 100–10,000 examples) would suffice. For natural language, even a small-scale experiment on a subset of Penn Treebank (e.g., 500 sentences) would provide crucial validation. The current work’s focus on event and image grammars, while technically sound, risks appearing disconnected from the field’s primary motivation.

Overall, this is a promising technical contribution that advances the state of the art in grammar learning. The method is well-motivated, the experiments are rigorous, and the results are compelling for the tasks presented. With revisions to address scalability and natural language applicability, the paper would make a strong case for publication. As it stands, the lack of these critical experiments prevents the work from fully realizing its potential impact. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to include the proposed analyses.
