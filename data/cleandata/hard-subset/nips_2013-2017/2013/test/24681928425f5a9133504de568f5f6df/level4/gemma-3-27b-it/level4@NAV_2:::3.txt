This paper proposes a novel approach to unsupervised learning of stochastic AND-OR grammars. The core idea is to induce unified AND-OR fragments, rather than separately searching for AND and OR operations, and to efficiently compute the value of a proposed fragment using sufficient statistics. The method is empirically evaluated on event and image parsing tasks.

This is a strong paper addressing an important problem. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and technically sound. The algorithm extends prior work on structure learning of natural language grammars [14, 15, 16] and improves upon recent work on structure learning of AND-OR grammars for images [17] and events [18]. While I wasn't able to verify all supplemental derivations, the overall technical presentation appears rigorous. A few aspects could benefit from further clarification: the nature of the “surrogate measure” for likelihood gain (approximate or exact?) and the limitations on fragment arity (can the method learn beyond binary branching, or is it restricted to Chomsky normal form?). 

My primary concern lies with the experimental presentation. The details regarding the experimental setup, data representation, and learning process are somewhat opaque. Understanding the experiments requires significant familiarity with recent visual grammar work, particularly from the Zhu group. While the NIPS format has length constraints, detailed illustrations and explanations in the supplementary materials would greatly enhance accessibility. 

Specifically, the paper would be strengthened by showcasing examples of the structure discovered by the learned grammars. The quantitative results are solid, but the lack of qualitative insights—demonstrating *what* structure is learned, and how it compares to competing methods—diminishes the impact. The paper sets up a strong motivation for grammar and structure learning, but the results section feels somewhat detached from this initial promise. Illustrating learned structures would significantly bolster the paper’s contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to unsupervised structure learning.
*   Technically sound algorithm with efficient computation of fragment value.
*   Competitive performance on event and image parsing tasks.
*   Addresses a significant problem in unsupervised learning and representation discovery.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental section lacks clarity and detail, making it difficult to fully assess the results.
*   Limited qualitative analysis of the learned grammars.
*   Requires significant background knowledge to fully understand the experimental setup.
