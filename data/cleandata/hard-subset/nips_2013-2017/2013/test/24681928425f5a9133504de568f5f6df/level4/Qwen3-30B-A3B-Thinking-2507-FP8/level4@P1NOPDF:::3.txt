

This paper introduces a novel approach for unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammar structures. Unlike prior methods that search separately for AND and OR operations, the proposed method induces unified AND-OR grammar fragments. The value of these fragments can be efficiently computed using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on parsing events and visual objects.

This is a generally strong paper addressing an important problem. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and technically sound (though I was unable to verify the supplemental derivations). Two points require clarification: first, it remains unclear whether the "surrogate measure" for likelihood gain is approximate or exact; second, the paper does not specify whether the method extends to fragments with arity greater than 2, or is limited to grammars in Chomsky Normal Form.

My primary concerns center on the experimental evaluation. The experimental details are insufficiently explained: the exact methodology, input representations, and learned structures remain unclear. The paper assumes familiarity with recent visual-grammar work from S. C. Zhu's group, making it difficult for outsiders to assess the results. While NIPS constraints are understood, supplementary materials offer an opportunity to address this. I strongly recommend that, if accepted, the authors provide extensive supplementary details—including concrete examples and visualizations—clarifying the experimental setup and outcomes.

Crucially, the paper presents a strong motivation for structure learning via grammars but disappoints in delivering compelling evidence of learned structure. The results are presented purely quantitatively, lacking illustrative examples of meaningful grammatical structures discovered. While the quantitative results are satisfactory on their own terms, they fail to demonstrate the promised value of the approach. I would have found the paper significantly more compelling if the authors had shown: (1) specific, interesting structures learned by their method, and (2) evidence that these structures are superior to those learned by competing approaches. The current presentation of results misses an opportunity to validate the core contribution.

In summary, the paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to unsupervised AND-OR grammar learning. While I found the theoretical contribution compelling, the experimental section requires substantial improvement in clarity and illustrative content to fully support the claims. If the authors address the points above—particularly by providing detailed supplementary material showcasing learned structures—the paper would be a valuable contribution.
