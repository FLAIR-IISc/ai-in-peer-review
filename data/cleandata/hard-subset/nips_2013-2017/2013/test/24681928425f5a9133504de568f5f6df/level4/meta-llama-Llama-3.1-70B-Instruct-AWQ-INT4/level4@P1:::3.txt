This paper proposes a new approach to unsupervised learning of the structure of AND-OR grammars. In contrast to previous approaches, the present one induces unified AND-OR fragments, rather than searching separately for AND and OR operations. The value of a proposed new AND-OR fragment can be computed efficiently using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on several tasks, including parsing events and visual objects.

This is a generally good paper. The problem is important, and the approach is novel and well-motivated. The algorithm appears technically sound, although I was not able to verify the supplemental derivations. A few points were unclear to me, which may reflect my limited time for reading. I would urge the authors to clarify these points: Firstly, it was not clear whether the "surrogate measure" of the likelihood gain was an approximate or an exact notion. Secondly, it was not clear whether the approach could extend to learning fragments with arity more than 2, or whether it could only tractably learn grammars in "Chomsky normal form".

My main concerns with the paper focus on the experiments. I had trouble understanding most aspects of them: exactly what was done, how the examples were represented, and what was learned. The paper seems to be written for a reader who has closely followed, studied, and preferably re-implemented recent visual-grammar papers from S. C. Zhu's group. To such a reader, the experimental section would likely be more understandable, and the results more useful. However, for outsiders, it was less clear. While I understand that the short NIPS format has its limitations, NIPS permits supplementary materials. If the paper is accepted, I would urge the authors to include many more details and concrete illustrations of their experiments in the supplement.

I would especially like to see examples of any interesting structure discovered in the learned grammars. The paper sets up the importance of grammars and structure learning, but it is a bit disappointing to see that the results are presented purely quantitatively. Although I agree with the authors that those quantitative results are satisfactory on their own terms, they are not very exciting or illuminating. I would have found the paper more compelling if the authors could show that interesting structure is learned, and that more interesting structure is learned by this approach relative to competing approaches.

Quality: The paper appears to be technically sound, and the approach is novel and well-motivated. However, I was not able to verify the supplemental derivations.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, but there are some unclear points, particularly in the experimental section. I would urge the authors to clarify these points and provide more details and illustrations in the supplement.

Originality: The approach is novel and well-motivated, and it addresses an important problem.

Significance: The paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field, but I would like to see more evidence that interesting structure is learned and that the approach outperforms competing methods.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper proposes a new approach to unsupervised learning of the structure of AND-OR grammars.
* The approach is novel and well-motivated, and it addresses an important problem.
* The algorithm appears technically sound, and the experimental results are satisfactory.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The experimental section is unclear and lacks details and illustrations.
* It is not clear whether the approach can extend to learning fragments with arity more than 2 or whether it can only tractably learn grammars in "Chomsky normal form".
* The paper does not provide enough evidence that interesting structure is learned and that the approach outperforms competing methods.
