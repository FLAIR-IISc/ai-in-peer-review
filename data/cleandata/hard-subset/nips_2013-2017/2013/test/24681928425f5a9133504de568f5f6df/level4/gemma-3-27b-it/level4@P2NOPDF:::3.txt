This paper introduces a novel approach to unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars, departing from previous methods by inducing unified AND-OR fragments instead of separately learning AND and OR operations. A key benefit of this approach is the efficient computation of fragment values using sufficient statistics. The method is evaluated on tasks involving parsing both events and visual objects.

Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a well-motivated and technically sound approach. While I was unable to fully verify the supplemental derivations, the algorithm appears robust. There were, however, a couple of points that lacked clarity. Specifically, it was unclear whether the “surrogate measure” used to estimate likelihood gain represents an approximation or an exact calculation. Additionally, the paper does not explicitly state whether the method is limited to learning fragments with arity two (Chomsky Normal Form) or if it can be extended to handle higher arities. Addressing these points would enhance understanding.

My primary concerns lie with the experimental section. I found it difficult to fully grasp the details of the experiments – the specific implementation, the data representation, and what precisely was learned. The presentation strongly assumes the reader is deeply familiar with recent work from S. C. Zhu’s group, potentially including re-implementation of their methods, which creates a barrier for those less acquainted with this specific research area. While the NIPS format constraints are understood, the supplementary materials offer an opportunity to provide the necessary context. Should the paper be accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to significantly expand the supplementary information with more detailed explanations and concrete examples of the experimental setup.

I would be particularly interested to see examples of the learned grammars and the structural patterns they capture. The paper effectively establishes the importance of grammar and structure learning, but the results are presented almost exclusively quantitatively. Although the quantitative results are satisfactory in their own right, they lack the intuitive appeal that would come from visualizing learned structures. Demonstrating that the approach learns interesting and meaningful structure, and that this structure is superior to that learned by competing methods, would significantly strengthen the paper. 

In conclusion, this is an interesting and promising contribution to the field of unsupervised AND-OR grammar learning. I found the core ideas compelling, but struggled to fully interpret the experimental results due to a reliance on prior knowledge. With more accessible explanations of the experiments and illustrative examples of the learned structures – ideally in supplementary material – this paper has the potential to be a highly impactful work.
