

This paper proposes a new approach for unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammar structure. Unlike prior methods that search separately for AND and OR operations, the proposed method induces unified AND-OR fragments. The value of a proposed fragment is efficiently computable using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on event parsing and visual object tasks.  

This is a generally strong paper. The problem is important, the approach is novel and well-motivated, and the algorithm appears technically sound (though I could not verify the supplemental derivations). A few points require clarification, which may reflect my limited time reading but should be addressed: (1) It is unclear whether the "surrogate measure" of likelihood gain is approximate or exact; (2) It is unclear whether the approach extends to fragments with arity greater than 2 or is restricted to grammars in "Chomsky normal form."  

My primary concerns involve the experiments. I struggled to follow the experimental methodology, including example representation and specific structures learned. The paper seems targeted at readers intimately familiar with recent visual-grammar work from S. C. Zhu's group; for such readers, the section would likely be clearer and more useful. For outsiders, it lacks sufficient detail. While NIPS constraints are understood, supplementary materials should include more experimental details and concrete illustrations.  

I particularly wish to see examples of interesting structures discovered in the learned grammars. The paper compellingly argues for the importance of grammars and structure learning, but the results—presented solely quantitatively—fail to meet this promise. While the quantitative results are satisfactory on their own terms, they lack excitement or insight. The paper would be more compelling if it demonstrated that this approach learns more interesting structures than competing methods. This is an interesting contribution to unsupervised AND-OR grammar learning, but the experimental section requires significant improvement for broader accessibility. If accepted, the authors should provide supplementary materials detailing the experimental procedures and illustrating key structural discoveries.
