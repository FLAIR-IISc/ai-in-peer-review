This paper introduces a novel approach to unsupervised learning of both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs). SAGs offer a powerful and compact representation for data exhibiting compositionality and reconfigurability, with applications ranging from natural language to images and event modeling. The authors propose an iterative algorithm that builds the grammar by incrementally adding And-Or fragments, optimizing a posterior probability that balances model complexity and data likelihood.

The core idea builds upon existing work in structure learning, particularly for natural language grammars, while extending it to a more general framework applicable to diverse data types. Importantly, the approach unifies the learning of compositional (AND rules) and alternative (OR rules) structures, potentially overcoming limitations of methods that treat these separately. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on event grammar and image grammar learning, achieving competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods.

The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important problem. The proposed approach appears to be a significant step towards more flexible and scalable learning of probabilistic compositional models. The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate the practical utility of the method. 

However, several points could be strengthened. First, the computational complexity and scalability of the algorithm are not thoroughly discussed. While the results are promising, it remains unclear how well this approach will perform with significantly larger datasets (e.g., thousands of data points). Providing some analysis of the computational cost, or at least empirical scaling results, would be valuable. 

Second, given the historical roots of stochastic grammars in natural language processing, the absence of experiments on standard NLP datasets (e.g., the Penn Treebank) is a notable omission.  While the authors acknowledge the applicability of their method to natural language, demonstrating performance on established benchmarks would provide a stronger basis for comparison with existing NLP techniques, such as those developed by Klein and Manning [1, 2].  It would be insightful to understand whether this approach is viable, and if so, how it compares, to established methods in the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The unified approach to learning both structure and parameters of SAGs is a valuable contribution.
*   **Soundness:** The theoretical framework appears well-defined and the optimization procedure is justified.
*   **Empirical Results:** The results on event and image grammars are encouraging and demonstrate the potential of the method.
*   **Generality:** The framework is presented as data-type agnostic, which broadens its potential applicability.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Scalability:** The computational complexity and scalability of the algorithm are not adequately addressed.
*   **Missing NLP Evaluation:** The lack of evaluation on standard NLP datasets limits the assessment of its broader applicability and comparison to existing NLP methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the concerns regarding scalability and the lack of NLP evaluation, I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The proposed approach is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically promising. With some clarifications and potential extensions, this work could have a significant impact. I lean towards acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly providing some discussion of scalability.
