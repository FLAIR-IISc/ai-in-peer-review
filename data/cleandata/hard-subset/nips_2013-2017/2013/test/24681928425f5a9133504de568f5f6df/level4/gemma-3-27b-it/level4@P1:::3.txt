This paper proposes a novel approach to unsupervised learning of the structure of AND-OR grammars. In contrast to previous methods, this work induces unified AND-OR fragments, rather than searching for AND and OR operations separately. The value of a proposed new AND-OR fragment can be computed efficiently using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on event parsing and image modeling tasks.

This is a generally strong paper. The problem is important, and the approach is novel and well-motivated. The algorithm appears technically sound, although I was unable to verify the supplemental derivations. A few aspects could benefit from further clarification. First, it wasn’t clear whether the “surrogate measure” of likelihood gain is an approximation or an exact calculation. Second, it remains unclear whether the approach can extend to learning fragments with arity greater than 2, or whether it is limited to grammars in Chomsky normal form.

My primary concerns lie with the experimental evaluation. I found it challenging to fully understand the details – precisely what was done, how the examples were represented, and what was learned. The experiments seem geared toward readers already intimately familiar with recent visual-grammar work from S.C. Zhu’s group. While the results would likely be more insightful for such an audience, they were less accessible to someone less familiar with the field. Although the NIPS format is concise, the authors should consider providing more details and concrete illustrations in the supplementary material should the paper be accepted.

I would especially appreciate seeing examples of any interesting structure discovered within the learned grammars. The paper effectively sets up the importance of grammar and structure learning, but the presentation of results is purely quantitative, which is somewhat disappointing. While the quantitative results are satisfactory, they lack a compelling narrative. The paper would be more convincing if the authors could demonstrate that the approach learns genuinely interesting structure, and that it learns *more* interesting structure than competing methods.

In summary, this is an interesting paper on unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. While I found the experiments difficult to follow and interpret—partly due to my unfamiliarity with prior work from Zhu's UCLA group—I believe it represents a valuable contribution. If accepted, I recommend the authors expand the supplementary material with more detailed explanations of the experiments and illustrations of the learned structures.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to jointly learning grammar structure and parameters.
*   Efficient computation of fragment value using sufficient statistics.
*   Competitive or superior performance on event and image learning tasks.
*   Theoretically sound and well-motivated approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experiments are difficult to understand without prior knowledge of related work.
*   Lack of qualitative results demonstrating learned structure.
*   Clarification needed regarding the exactness of the likelihood approximation and the arity limitations.
