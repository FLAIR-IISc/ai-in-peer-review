

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to the type of data being modeled (images, events, etc.), along with an unsupervised learning approach for simultaneously learning both the structure and parameters of such grammars. The authors begin with a trivial initial grammar and iteratively induce compositions and reconfigurations by adding And-Or fragments that unify the search for new grammatical structures. This approach differs from previous work that typically learns compositions and reconfigurations separately, which can be problematic when training data is scarce. The paper makes a strong case for the general applicability of their framework across different data types, as evidenced by experiments on event grammars and image grammars.

The main contributions are well-articulated: (1) a data-agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars, (2) explicit optimization of the posterior probability of the grammar structure and parameters, and (3) a unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations that improves efficiency and robustness. The theoretical foundation for the learning algorithm is solid, with clear derivations of likelihood and prior gains, and the sufficient statistics approach makes the optimization computationally feasible.

The paper's experimental evaluation demonstrates promising results. For event grammars, the approach achieves competitive or better performance than previous methods, particularly on the more complex dataset with subtyped actions. For image grammars, the method significantly outperforms the competing approach from Si et al. (2013) in both synthetic and real datasets, as measured by F-measure and perplexity. The authors provide clear comparisons to relevant prior work, and the experimental setup is well-motivated for each application domain.

The paper excels in technical quality and clarity. The formal definition of stochastic And-Or grammars is precise and well-structured, and the algorithm description is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The presentation of the And-Or fragments concept is particularly effective, with Figure 2 providing a clear illustration of how these fragments unify composition and reconfiguration learning. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, with a logical flow from problem definition to algorithm to evaluation.

However, the paper would benefit from several improvements. First, while the authors claim their framework is "agnostic to the type of data," the experimental evaluation is limited to events and images. Adding results on a third data type (e.g., natural language) would strengthen this claim and better demonstrate the framework's generality. Second, the experimental section could be enhanced by comparing with additional relevant methods, particularly Stolcke and Omohundro's approach (reference 14), which the authors mention as a potential comparison point. The current comparisons, while sufficient, could be more comprehensive.

The significance of this work is strong, as grammars are fundamental to pattern recognition across domains, and the ability to learn them unsupervised from raw data is valuable for practitioners. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a more robust and efficient structure learning method that handles both compositionality and reconfigurability in a unified manner. The approach is particularly well-suited for scenarios where the full set of valid grammar rules is too large to specify manually.

The paper is well within NIPS scope, addressing a fundamental problem in representation learning with clear connections to prior work on stochastic grammars and sum-product networks. It makes a solid contribution to the field with both theoretical and empirical merits.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a clear technical contribution to the problem of unsupervised grammar learning. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated across two distinct domains. The main weaknesses are limited experimental scope and the opportunity for additional methodological comparisons. These are relatively minor issues that do not undermine the paper's overall quality and contribution. I recommend acceptance.
