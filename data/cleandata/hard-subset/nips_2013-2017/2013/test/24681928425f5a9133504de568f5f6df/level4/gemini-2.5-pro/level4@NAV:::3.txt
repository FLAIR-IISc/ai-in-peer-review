This paper presents a novel method for the unsupervised induction of AND-OR grammar structures. Departing from prior work that searches for AND and OR operations separately, this approach directly induces unified AND-OR fragments. A key feature is the efficient computation of a candidate fragment's value via a set of sufficient statistics. The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on tasks involving the parsing of events and visual objects.

Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. The technical aspects of the algorithm seem solid, although I did not have the opportunity to verify the derivations provided in the supplementary material. I would ask the authors to clarify two points, which could benefit from greater emphasis in the text: First, is the "surrogate measure" for likelihood gain an exact calculation or an approximation? Second, can the method be extended to learn fragments with an arity greater than two, or is its tractable application limited to grammars in what is effectively a Chomsky normal form?

My primary critique of the paper relates to the experimental evaluation. I found it difficult to grasp the specifics of the experiments, including the precise methodology, the representation of the data, and the nature of the learned outputs. The experimental section seems to assume a deep familiarity with the recent body of work on visual grammars from S. C. Zhu’s group, making it challenging for an outside reader to follow. While acknowledging the constraints of the conference format, I strongly urge the authors to use the supplementary material, should the paper be accepted, to provide comprehensive details and concrete illustrations of their experiments to broaden the paper's accessibility and impact.

I would particularly have liked to see qualitative examples of the interesting structures discovered by the learned grammars. After a compelling motivation centered on the importance of structure learning, the purely quantitative results feel anticlimactic. While the quantitative metrics are satisfactory on their own terms, they offer limited insight and are not particularly illuminating. The paper’s argument would be substantially more persuasive if it demonstrated, through examples, that the learned structures are meaningful and, ideally, richer than those produced by competing methods.

In summary, this is a valuable contribution to the unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. However, my enthusiasm is tempered by the difficulty in following the experimental section, which heavily relies on a specific body of prior work. If accepted, the paper would be greatly improved by the inclusion of supplementary materials that detail the experimental procedures and provide qualitative illustrations of the learned structures.
