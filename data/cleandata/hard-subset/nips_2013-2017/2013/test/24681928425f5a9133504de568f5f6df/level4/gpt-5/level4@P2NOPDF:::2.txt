This paper proposes a method for jointly learning the structure and parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars. These grammars, which contain both AND and OR production rules, can represent recursive phenomena, including natural language and other structured domains. The authors present an unsupervised structure-learning approach that incrementally introduces new AND-OR fragments and evaluates them based on gains in likelihood and prior.

The method is evaluated on two tasks—learning event grammars and learning image grammars—where it achieves results competitive with prior work.

Overall, I found the paper clear and the approach appealing. It offers a practical route to learning stochastic grammars that use AND/OR rules, and the empirical results are encouraging.

My main concerns are:

1) Tractability and scalability: The paper does not analyze the computational complexity of the learning algorithm or report its runtime and memory characteristics. It is unclear how the method scales to larger datasets (e.g., thousands or tens of thousands of examples). Providing complexity bounds, runtime measurements, or scaling experiments would strengthen the contribution.

2) Natural language evaluation: Given that natural language is a primary application for such grammars, I would have liked to see experiments on standard corpora. Is the approach feasible on the Penn Treebank, for instance, where prior work (e.g., Klein and Manning) has established strong baselines? A demonstration on natural language data—or a discussion of practical barriers, such as search space, supervision level, or required priors—would significantly increase the paper’s impact.

In summary, the paper introduces a well-motivated method for learning stochastic AND-OR grammars and shows promising results on two domains. Its contribution would be more compelling with an analysis of scalability and a thorough evaluation on natural language data.
