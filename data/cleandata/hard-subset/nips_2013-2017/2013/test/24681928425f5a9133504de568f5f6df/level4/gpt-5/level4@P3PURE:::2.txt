This paper proposes a method for jointly learning the structure and parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars. These grammars comprise AND and OR rules and can represent a range of recursive phenomena, including natural language and even grammars themselves. The authors introduce a principled approach to unsupervised structure learning by iteratively adding new AND-OR fragments and evaluating them based on gains in likelihood and prior.

The experimental evaluation covers two tasks—learning event grammars and learning image grammars—where the method achieves results that are competitive with prior work. Overall, I found the paper compelling: it presents a seemingly tractable approach to learning stochastic grammars expressible with AND and OR rules, and the empirical results are encouraging.

My main concerns are as follows:
1) Tractability and scalability: The paper does not discuss the computational tractability of the learning algorithm. Will the approach scale to thousands (or more) of datapoints? A discussion of computational complexity, runtime behavior, and memory usage, as well as empirical scaling experiments, would strengthen the contribution.
2) Natural language experiments: Given that natural language is a primary application domain for such grammars, I would have liked to have seen experiments on sentences, for example on the Penn Treebank, which has been a standard benchmark in prior work (e.g., Klein and Manning). Is it feasible to learn on such data using the proposed method? Even a preliminary experiment or a clear discussion of limitations would be valuable.

In summary, this work offers a solid method for estimating both the structure and parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars and demonstrates promising results on event and image grammar tasks. To fully establish its impact, I encourage the authors to provide a more thorough analysis of scalability and to include (or at least substantively discuss) experiments on natural language data.
