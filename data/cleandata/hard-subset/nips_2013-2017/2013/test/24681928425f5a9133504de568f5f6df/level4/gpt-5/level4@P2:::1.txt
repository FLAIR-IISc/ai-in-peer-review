Summary
This paper presents an unsupervised method to learn both the structure and parameters of context-free stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), with a unified formalization that is agnostic to the data type. The approach starts from a trivial grammar that exactly explains the training set and iteratively introduces And-Or fragments—each combining an And-rule with sets of Or-rules—to simultaneously capture compositions and reconfigurations. The algorithm explicitly maximizes a posterior objective with a sparsity prior, using Viterbi likelihood and sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) to efficiently estimate posterior gain under an unambiguity assumption. The method is applied to event parsing and image parsing, showing competitive or improved performance relative to prior work on AOG learning.

Relation to prior work
The framework builds on AOGs used in vision and events (e.g., Zhu and collaborators; Si & Zhu; Pei et al.), and draws from grammar induction in NLP (e.g., EM/Viterbi training, Bayesian model merging). The contribution lies in: (a) a unified AOG formalization across modalities; (b) joint induction of compositions and alternatives via And-Or fragments (rather than separately searching for And- or Or-fragments); and (c) an efficient posterior-gain objective using sufficient statistics. While the paper suggests a generalization of prior structure learning, the scope is in fact a principled specialization to context-free AOGs with Viterbi likelihood and an unambiguity bias, which makes the problem more tractable than general NLP grammar induction.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The posterior maximization with a size prior is clear. The derivations for likelihood and prior gains, and the role of context matrices/tensors, are well motivated. The Viterbi/unambiguity approximations are standard in grammar induction, though they may bias learning.
- Empirical support: Experiments on events and images show consistent gains over selected baselines (notably Si & Zhu-style methods). However, the comparison set is limited. Including Bayesian model merging (Stolcke & Omohundro) and additional NLP-style induction baselines where applicable would strengthen the case. Ablations (e.g., And vs Or vs And-Or fragments; effect of α; beam width) would clarify where the gains come from.
- Reproducibility: The high-level algorithm is clear, but additional details (beam size, restart strategy, encoding costs sa/so, and runtime profiles on larger datasets) would help reproduction.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The unified AOG definition is clear. The figures are helpful but could be cleaner (some labels are hard to read). It would help to:
- Define sa and so earlier and explain their coding scheme.
- Move key parts of the derivation currently in the supplement into the main text, or at least summarize the assumptions and consequences.
- Provide a concise pseudocode in the main paper.

Originality
The use of And-Or fragments to jointly induce compositions and alternatives, together with an efficient posterior-gain computation via sufficient statistics, is a neat and practically useful idea. The unified cross-domain treatment of AOGs is also valuable. The approach is related to but distinct from prior NLP induction and AOG learning.

Significance
Given the breadth of applications for AOGs in vision and event understanding, a practical structure-learning method that works across modalities is useful. While not a breakthrough in theory, the method shows strong empirical performance and may be adopted by practitioners. The contribution would be stronger with broader comparisons and larger-scale experiments.

Pros
- Clear formulation of context-free stochastic AOGs that applies across domains.
- Joint learning of compositions and alternatives via And-Or fragments reduces redundancy and improves robustness.
- Principled posterior objective with a sparsity prior; efficient search using sufficient statistics.
- Solid results on event and image datasets; competitive or better than prior AOG methods.

Cons
- Limited baselines; missing comparison to Bayesian model merging (Stolcke & Omohundro) and other grammar induction approaches where applicable.
- Relies on Viterbi likelihood and unambiguity assumptions; potential bias not deeply analyzed.
- Some implementation details and ablations are missing; scalability beyond the reported sizes is not fully demonstrated.
- The claim of generalization over prior structure learning may be overstated; the contribution is a well-motivated specialization.

Suggestions
- Add comparisons to Stolcke & Omohundro (ref. 14) and, where feasible, other grammar induction baselines on both tasks.
- Provide ablations on the fragment types, α, beam size, and the effect of assuming unambiguity.
- Clarify coding costs (sa, so), provide pseudocode, and improve figure readability.
- Discuss runtime and memory scaling on larger datasets.

Overall assessment
A solid and well-executed paper with a practical and novel take on AOG structure learning. The method is sensible, efficient, and empirically effective, though the evaluation would benefit from broader comparisons and ablations. I lean toward acceptance.
