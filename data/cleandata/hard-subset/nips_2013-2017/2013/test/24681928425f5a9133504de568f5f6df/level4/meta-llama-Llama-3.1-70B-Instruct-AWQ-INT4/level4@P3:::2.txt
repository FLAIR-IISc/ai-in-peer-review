This paper presents a method for learning the structure as well as the parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars. Such grammars contain AND rules and OR rules and can be used to represent several recursive phenomena, including natural language and event grammars. The authors present a nice method for unsupervised structure learning of these grammars by introducing new AND-OR fragments at consecutive steps, and measuring the likelihood and prior gains of their model.

The authors provide a clear and unified formalization of stochastic AND-OR grammars that is agnostic to the type of data being modeled. They also propose an efficient algorithm for learning the structure and parameters of such grammars. The algorithm is based on a greedy search or beam search strategy, and it uses a set of sufficient statistics to efficiently compute the posterior gain of adding a new AND-OR fragment to the grammar.

The authors present experiments on two tasks: learning event grammars and learning image grammars. In both tasks, they achieve results that are competitive with prior art. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in learning stochastic AND-OR grammars from unannotated data.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method and results. The paper also includes a detailed description of the algorithm and its components, which makes it easy to follow and understand.

However, there are a few limitations and potential areas for improvement. One limitation is that the authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of their algorithm. While they mention that their prototype implementation can finish running within a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples, it is not clear how the algorithm will scale to larger datasets. Additionally, the authors do not provide experiments on natural language sentences, which is a significant application area for stochastic AND-OR grammars. It would be interesting to see how the proposed method performs on natural language data, such as the Penn Treebank dataset.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel method for learning stochastic AND-OR grammars, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their contribution. The paper also includes a detailed description of the algorithm and its components, which makes it easy to follow and understand. However, the paper builds on previous work on stochastic AND-OR grammars, and the authors could have provided more context and discussion on how their method relates to previous work in the area.

In terms of significance, the paper presents a method that can be used to learn stochastic AND-OR grammars from unannotated data, which is a significant contribution to the field of machine learning and natural language processing. The method has the potential to be applied to a wide range of tasks, including natural language processing, computer vision, and robotics.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method and results. While there are a few limitations and potential areas for improvement, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of machine learning and natural language processing.
