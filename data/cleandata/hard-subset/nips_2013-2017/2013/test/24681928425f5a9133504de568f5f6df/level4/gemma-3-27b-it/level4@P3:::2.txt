This paper presents a method for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars, a powerful formalism capable of representing compositional and reconfigurable patterns found in diverse data types, including natural language and images. The authors propose an unsupervised learning approach that iteratively introduces new AND-OR fragments into the grammar, optimizing a posterior probability based on likelihood and prior gains. The approach is evaluated on event and image grammar learning tasks, achieving competitive results compared to existing methods.

The paper’s strength lies in its unified framework for learning grammar structure and parameters simultaneously, and its elegant approach to combining compositional (AND) and reconfigurable (OR) aspects in a single learning step. Prior work often treats these as separate problems, potentially leading to suboptimal results. The authors clearly articulate the problem and their contributions, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on two distinct tasks. The connection to related work in stochastic grammars, sum-product networks, and structure learning is well-established, providing appropriate context for the research.

However, the paper would benefit from further discussion of its computational complexity and scalability. While the experimental results are encouraging, it remains unclear how well the approach will scale to larger datasets. Specifically, addressing the question of tractability with thousands of data points would be valuable. Moreover, while the authors demonstrate the applicability to events and images, the natural connection to natural language processing (NLP) – where stochastic grammars originated – is underexplored. Including experiments on a standard NLP dataset like the Penn Treebank, and comparing to existing approaches like those by Klein and Manning [1], would significantly strengthen the paper and demonstrate its broader applicability. The paper mentions the iterative nature of the algorithm, but a more detailed analysis of its runtime behavior and memory requirements would be helpful. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and unified approach to learning stochastic AND-OR grammars.
*   Demonstrated effectiveness on two different data types (events and images).
*   Clear presentation and well-defined contributions.
*   Addresses a challenging problem in unsupervised learning of compositional models.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of discussion regarding scalability to larger datasets.
*   Absence of experiments on natural language data, a key application area for stochastic grammars.
*   Limited analysis of the computational complexity of the learning algorithm.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates promising results. Addressing the concerns regarding scalability and including experiments on natural language data would significantly increase the impact of this work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the aforementioned points.

[1] Klein, D., & Manning, C. D. (2004). Corpus-based induction of syntactic structure: Models of dependency and constituency. *Proceedings of ACL*.
