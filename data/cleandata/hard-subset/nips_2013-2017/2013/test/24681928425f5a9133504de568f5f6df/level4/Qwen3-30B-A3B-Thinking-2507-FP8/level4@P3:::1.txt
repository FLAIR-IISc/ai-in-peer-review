

This paper presents a unified framework for unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, with a focus on learning both the grammar structure and parameters simultaneously. The authors formalize stochastic And-Or grammars in a data-type-agnostic manner, allowing the approach to be applied to diverse domains including images and event sequences. The key innovation lies in their proposed "And-Or fragments" that unify the learning of compositions (via And-nodes) and reconfigurations (via Or-nodes), addressing a limitation in previous approaches that learned these aspects separately.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on grammar learning, building upon previous work on structure learning in natural language processing [14-16] and And-Or grammars for images [17] and events [18]. The authors properly acknowledge these prior works while demonstrating how their unified approach avoids the pitfalls of separate composition and reconfiguration learning, particularly in scenarios with scarce or ambiguous data. The theoretical foundation is sound, with the authors deriving sufficient statistics to efficiently compute the posterior gain during grammar learning.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear examples (Table 1, Figure 1) that effectively illustrate the grammar formalization and learning process. The algorithmic details are sufficiently precise to allow reproduction, with the supplementary material providing additional implementation details. The authors demonstrate the approach on two distinct domains: event grammars (using activity recognition data) and image grammars (using synthetic and real animal face datasets).

The strengths of this paper include:
- A unified approach to structure learning that addresses a genuine limitation in previous work
- A theoretically grounded optimization procedure that explicitly maximizes posterior probability
- Strong empirical results showing comparable or better performance than prior methods
- A clear and accessible presentation of a technically complex approach
- The ability to learn from diverse data types without domain-specific modifications

The main weaknesses include:
- Limited comparison to other structure learning approaches, particularly Stolcke and Omohundro's method [14] which would provide a more meaningful benchmark for the event grammar experiments
- The claim that the approach "generalizes" previous work is somewhat misleading—the approach is a special case that makes the problem tractable rather than a broader generalization
- The paper could more thoroughly discuss the limitations of the approach, such as computational complexity with very large grammars

Regarding the four evaluation criteria:

Quality: The paper is technically sound, with thorough theoretical justification for the posterior optimization approach. The empirical evaluation is well-designed, with appropriate metrics (F-measure, perplexity) and careful experimental setup. The authors acknowledge limitations and provide a clear analysis of why their approach outperforms competitors.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally clear, with well-structured sections and helpful examples. The figures (particularly Figures 1-3) effectively illustrate the key concepts. The writing is precise and accessible to a broad NIPS audience.

Originality: The unified And-Or fragment approach represents a novel way to address the structure learning problem in And-Or grammars. While the basic idea of grammar learning isn't new, the specific approach to unifying composition and reconfiguration learning is novel and well-justified. The paper properly situates itself within the literature, acknowledging prior work while demonstrating the novelty of their contribution.

Significance: The approach has significant potential for applications in domains requiring compositional structure learning, including computer vision and natural language processing. The ability to learn from diverse data types without re-engineering the approach is particularly valuable. The results demonstrate tangible improvements over previous methods, suggesting the approach could be broadly adopted.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field, with a novel approach that addresses a meaningful limitation in previous grammar learning methods. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including comparisons to Stolcke and Omohundro [14], the core contribution is clear and well-supported. The paper successfully demonstrates how their unified approach to structure learning outperforms previous methods in both event and image grammar domains.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the limited comparison to existing structure learning methods by including results from Stolcke and Omohundro's approach, as it represents a more direct comparison to their work than the other methods they currently compare against. Additionally, the paper could better clarify the relationship between their approach and previous work, avoiding the misleading claim that it "generalizes" previous structure learning methods.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, making a clear technical contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling with compositional structures. The method is both theoretically sound and empirically validated, with potential applications across multiple domains. The writing is excellent, and the contribution represents a meaningful advance over previous work in this specific area of grammar learning.
