

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to the type of data being modeled (images, events, etc.) and proposes an unsupervised approach for learning both the structure (grammar rules) and parameters of such grammars. The authors build upon previous work on stochastic grammars in natural language processing [8,9], sum-product networks [10], and And-Or grammars for images [1,3] and events [7]. Their key contribution is a unified approach to learning both compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules) through "And-Or fragments," which addresses limitations of previous methods that treated these as separate problems. The algorithm starts from a trivial initial grammar and iteratively adds new fragments to optimize the posterior probability of the grammar, using efficient computation via sufficient statistics.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-motivated approach. The authors provide a clear formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and derive a principled way to compute the likelihood and prior gains for grammar fragments. The algorithm's efficiency is supported by theoretical derivations and practical implementation details, and the experimental evaluation on event grammars and image grammars shows competitive or better performance compared to previous methods. The paper is well-organized with clear definitions, helpful examples (Table 1), and effective illustrations (Figures 1-3) that make the approach accessible.

The work demonstrates significant originality by presenting a unified framework for learning both grammar structure and parameters, which differs from prior approaches that either manually specify grammar structures [10,13] or learn compositions and reconfigurations separately [17,18]. The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on stochastic grammar learning, particularly in its novel treatment of And-Or fragments. The authors adequately reference related work, though they could more explicitly distinguish their approach from sum-product networks [10], which share conceptual similarities.

The significance of this work is demonstrated through successful application to two distinct domains: event grammars for activity recognition and image grammars for scene modeling. The results show that the approach can effectively learn compositional structures in both domains, suggesting potential for broader application. The experiments are well-designed, with appropriate evaluation metrics (F-measure, perplexity) and careful comparison to prior methods.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. First, the computational complexity and scalability of the algorithm are not sufficiently addressed. The authors mention that their prototype implementation runs within minutes on 5000 samples, but they do not provide details on how the algorithm scales with larger datasets or more complex grammars. This is a critical omission for a method targeting practical applications.

Second, the paper lacks experiments on natural language data, which would be the most obvious application for stochastic And-Or grammars. Previous work in this area (e.g., Klein and Manning [12]) has focused on natural language, making this omission particularly significant. The absence of experiments on standard NLP datasets like Penn Treebank limits the demonstration of the method's broader applicability and potential impact on the field.

Third, while the paper presents a unified approach, the connection to sum-product networks [10] could be more clearly articulated. The authors reference sum-product networks as related work but do not explain how their approach differs or complements this line of research, which would strengthen the paper's contribution.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, though some technical details in the algorithm description (particularly regarding the context matrix and n-gram tensor) could benefit from additional explanation or examples to aid readers in understanding the implementation.

In summary, this paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of stochastic grammar learning with a novel unified approach to structure learning. The empirical results are strong for the two domains tested, but the lack of experiments on natural language data and insufficient discussion of scalability are significant limitations. The work advances the state of the art in grammar learning, but the absence of natural language experiments prevents a full assessment of its broader impact.

Pro-acceptance arguments: Clear formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars applicable to multiple domains; novel unified approach to learning both composition and reconfiguration; strong empirical results on event and image grammars; well-written with effective illustrations.

Con-acceptance arguments: Lack of experiments on natural language data (the most obvious application); insufficient discussion of computational scalability; limited comparison with sum-product networks despite conceptual similarities.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field. While the absence of natural language experiments is a notable weakness, the technical soundness and promising results on the tested domains suggest it would be valuable for the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
