This paper presents an unsupervised method for learning the structure of stochastic And-Or grammars. The central contribution is a novel approach that, unlike prior work which learns compositional rules (And-rules) and alternative configurations (Or-rules) separately, induces unified "And-Or fragments." The learning process is framed as an optimization of the grammar's posterior probability, which combines a Viterbi likelihood term with a sparsity-inducing prior on the grammar's size. Starting from a trivial grammar that exactly represents the training data, the algorithm iteratively uses a greedy or beam search to add the And-Or fragment that yields the largest increase in this posterior objective. A key technical aspect is the efficient computation of this gain using a set of sufficient statistics derived from the data. The general, data-agnostic framework is evaluated on the tasks of learning event grammars from activity videos and image grammars from both synthetic and real object images.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Originality and Significance:**
The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of unsupervised structure learning for generative models. The core idea of learning unified And-Or fragments is novel and well-motivated. It provides a principled way to address the potential shortcomings of learning compositional and reconfigurable structures in separate stages, which can be inefficient and prone to error, especially with sparse data. The proposed framework is presented as a general approach applicable to various data types, which increases its potential significance.

**Quality:**
The technical approach appears sound. The formulation of the learning problem as the maximization of a posterior probability is principled. The derivation of an efficient method for computing the posterior gain, based on sufficient statistics related to n-gram tensors and context matrices, is a strong technical contribution that makes the search-based algorithm feasible. The quantitative experimental results are solid, showing that the proposed method achieves comparable or superior performance to previous approaches on several datasets.

However, there are some areas where the technical presentation could be improved:
1.  In Section 3.3, the link between the likelihood gain and "tensor/matrix coherence measures" is mentioned as a "surrogate measure." It is not entirely clear if the formulated likelihood gain is an exact calculation under the Viterbi likelihood and grammar unambiguity assumptions, or if it is a further approximation. Clarifying this point would strengthen the paper's technical claims.
2.  The paper's examples and framework seem to focus on fragments of a fixed, small arity. It would be helpful to discuss whether the approach can tractably extend to learning fragments with higher arity, or if the search for candidate fragments is restricted in a way that biases the learned grammars towards a structure resembling Chomsky Normal Form.

**Clarity and Reproducibility:**
While the high-level description of the algorithm and the formalization of And-Or fragments are clear, the experimental sections are difficult to follow for a reader not already intimately familiar with the cited prior work, much of which originates from the same research group (e.g., [17], [18]). Key details regarding data representation are sparse. For instance, the exact process of converting videos into sequences of vectors or IDs, or the specifics of the image quantization process, are not detailed enough for an outsider to fully grasp the experimental setup. While page limits are a constraint, NIPS allows for supplementary material, which would be an ideal place to provide these crucial details to ensure the work is understandable and reproducible.

**Evaluation:**
The paper's main weakness lies in its evaluation. The work is motivated by the ability of grammars to capture meaningful, interpretable structure, yet the results are presented purely in terms of quantitative metrics (F-measure, perplexity). While these metrics show the method is effective, they do not provide insight into the quality of the learned structures themselves. The paper would be significantly more compelling if it included qualitative results. For example:
*   What interesting event structures were discovered in the activity video datasets? Can the authors show a learned grammar fragment and explain the compositional event it represents (e.g., "making a phone call" is composed of "sitting," "picking up phone," and "talking")?
*   For the image grammars, what compositional parts of animal faces were learned? Does the learned structure correspond to intuitive concepts like "eye," "nose," or "ear," and does it capture variations in their appearance or configuration?
Showing that the proposed method learns more compact, interpretable, or meaningful structures than competing approaches would provide much stronger evidence for its advantages beyond improved quantitative scores.

**Summary of Arguments:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses the significant and challenging problem of unsupervised grammar induction.
*   Proposes a novel, well-motivated, and principled method for learning unified And-Or fragments.
*   The approach is technically sound, based on optimizing a clear posterior probability objective.
*   Demonstrates strong quantitative performance against prior work across multiple tasks and datasets.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental description lacks sufficient detail, making it difficult for non-specialists to fully understand, evaluate, and reproduce the experiments.
*   The evaluation is entirely quantitative and lacks qualitative examples of the learned structures. This is a missed opportunity to demonstrate the primary benefit of grammar-based models—their interpretability—and to make the paper's contributions more compelling and insightful.

**Recommendation:**
This is a solid paper with a novel and technically sound contribution. However, its impact is limited by the lack of clarity in the experimental section and the absence of qualitative results. I am leaning towards acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to significantly expand on their experiments in supplementary material if the paper is accepted. This should include both a more detailed description of the experimental setup and, crucially, visualizations and interpretations of the learned grammar structures to substantiate the claim that the model discovers meaningful compositional and reconfigurable patterns.
