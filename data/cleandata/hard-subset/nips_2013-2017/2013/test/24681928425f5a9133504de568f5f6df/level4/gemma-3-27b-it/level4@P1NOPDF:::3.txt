This paper introduces a novel approach to unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammar structure. Unlike previous methods that independently search for AND and OR operations, this work induces unified AND-OR fragments. The value of a newly proposed AND-OR fragment is efficiently computed using sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on tasks involving both parsing events and visual objects.

This is a strong paper overall. The problem is important, the approach is novel and well-motivated, and the algorithm appears technically sound, although I was unable to verify the supplemental derivations. A few points lacked clarity, which may be due to my limited reading time, but I encourage the authors to address these more explicitly: First, it’s unclear whether the “surrogate measure” of likelihood gain is an approximation or an exact calculation. Second, it’s not clear whether the approach can extend to learning fragments with arity greater than 2, or whether it’s restricted to tractably learning grammars in Chomsky normal form. 

My primary concerns lie with the experimental section. I found it difficult to understand many aspects of the experiments – precisely what was done, how the examples were represented, and what was learned. The paper seems geared towards readers deeply familiar with recent visual-grammar work from S.C. Zhu’s group, ideally those who have studied and potentially re-implemented it. For such a reader, the experimental section would likely be more understandable and the results more insightful. However, for those outside this specific research area, the experiments are less clear. While I recognize the constraints of the NIPS format, the supplementary materials provision is valuable. If accepted, I strongly suggest the authors significantly expand the supplement with more details and concrete illustrations of their experiments.

I would be particularly interested in seeing examples of any interesting structure discovered in the learned grammars. The paper effectively establishes the importance of grammar and structure learning, but the presentation of results solely as quantitative metrics is somewhat disappointing. While I agree the quantitative results are satisfactory in themselves, they are not particularly exciting or illuminating. I would find the paper more compelling if the authors could demonstrate that interesting structure is learned, and furthermore, that this approach learns *more* interesting structure compared to competing methods. 

In conclusion, this is an interesting paper on the unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. I found it appealing, but struggled to follow the experiments and interpret the results due to my unfamiliarity with much of the relevant prior work, particularly from Zhu’s UCLA group. If accepted, the authors should include significantly more details and illustrations in the supplementary material, explaining how the experiments work and showcasing the interesting structures that were learned.
