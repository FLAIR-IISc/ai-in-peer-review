Summary
The paper proposes an unsupervised method for learning both the structure and parameters of context-free stochastic And-Or grammars from unannotated data. The key idea is to iteratively induce And-Or fragments—each comprising one And-node and several Or-nodes—so as to jointly capture compositionality (And rules) and reconfigurability (Or rules). The objective is a MAP criterion with a sparsity prior on grammar size and a Viterbi (best-parse) approximation to the likelihood. To make greedy/beam search efficient, the authors derive a closed-form posterior gain for adding a fragment under an unambiguity assumption, expressed in terms of sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor over configurations and a context matrix. Experiments on event grammars (human activities) and image grammars (object parts) show comparable or better performance than prior work focused on And-Or templates for images and events.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned between unsupervised grammar induction in NLP (e.g., Baker 1979; Klein & Manning 2004; Stolcke & Omohundro 1994; Liang et al. 2009) and structure learning for And-Or grammars and related models in vision (Zhu & Mumford 2006; Si & Zhu 2013; Si et al. 2011) and sum-product networks (Poon & Domingos 2011). Unlike NLP grammar induction that typically fixes structure and learns parameters (or uses Bayesian merging/splitting), this paper searches over structures but within a restricted And-Or grammar class with explicit relations, and optimizes a MAP objective with a size prior. Compared to prior And-Or structure learners in vision/events that often rely on heuristic merging or separate searches for And vs. Or structure, the proposed And-Or fragment induction unifies these choices and yields an efficient posterior-gain computation from sufficient statistics.

Strengths
- Clear, unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars agnostic to data type, with concrete relational instantiations for images and events.
- Principled MAP objective with a sparsity prior; the Viterbi likelihood choice is reasonable and commonly used in grammar induction.
- Elegant derivation of posterior gain using n-gram tensors and context matrices; this greatly improves search efficiency and provides intuition via coherence measures.
- Unified And-Or fragment induction addresses data sparsity and redundancy issues that arise when learning And and Or structure separately.
- Cross-domain demonstrations (events and images) with solid improvements over representative And-Or learners.

Weaknesses
- Limited baseline comparisons. Methods such as Bayesian Model Merging (Stolcke & Omohundro), CCM (Klein & Manning), or HDP-PCFGs (Liang et al.) could be adapted (e.g., via ID sequences) and would strengthen the case. Similarly, structure learning for SPNs is a relevant baseline.
- The unambiguity assumption and Viterbi approximation are central to the derivation and performance. The paper lacks analysis of when these assumptions hold and how sensitive results are when they are violated.
- The event experiments rely on annotated atomic actions and boundaries; this is closer to semi-supervised structure learning. The “agnostic” claim would be stronger with text experiments or with automatically discovered primitives.
- Missing ablations: (i) And-only vs. Or-only vs. And-Or fragments; (ii) beam width/greedy choice; (iii) effect of α and coding choices (sa, so); (iv) different relation templates and n values.
- Some implementation details are under-specified (e.g., precise definition of contexts; complexity and memory cost of constructing n-gram tensors; smoothing used for perplexity; stopping criterion).
- The modification for multi-event videos (converting top-level And to Or at the end) seems ad hoc; a more principled multi-instance formulation would be preferable.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The posterior-gain derivation and use of sufficient statistics are compelling. However, theoretical guarantees (e.g., consistency or bounds) are not provided, and empirical validation of key design choices (unambiguity, Viterbi, fragment definition) is limited. Results on both domains are promising but comparisons are somewhat narrow.

Clarity
Generally well written and well organized. The figures convey the intuition, though some figure/table references and variable definitions could be tighter. Please clarify: (1) grammar size measure and coding constants sa, so; (2) how contexts are defined for images vs. events; (3) computational complexity and memory footprint of building and scanning n-gram tensors (as a function of dataset size, grammar size, and n); (4) smoothing used for perplexity; (5) the exact train/test splits and variance across runs.

Originality
The unified And-Or fragment search with a tractable posterior-gain computation is a novel and useful contribution beyond prior heuristic structure learners for And-Or grammars. The cross-domain formalization and sufficient-statistics perspective are also original in this context.

Significance
If broadly validated, the approach could influence structure learning for compositional models in vision and activity analysis, and potentially inform grammar induction in other modalities. The current scope is promising but would benefit from stronger baselines and ablations to substantiate generality.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baselines: Bayesian Model Merging (Stolcke & Omohundro), CCM (Klein & Manning), HDP-PCFGs, and SPN structure learning (where applicable), at least on the ID-sequence reduction used in your first method.
- Provide ablations for fragment types, search strategy, α/encoding choices, and the unambiguity/Viterbi assumptions.
- Include runtime and memory metrics and a brief complexity analysis.
- Clarify the context definition and relation templates; report sensitivity to the choice of n.
- For events, discuss the reliance on annotated primitives and explore robustness to noisy detection; for images, analyze sensitivity to quantization.
- Consider adding a small text experiment to support the “agnostic” claim.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, efficient structure-learning method with a clear objective and derivation.
- Unified handling of composition and reconfiguration via And-Or fragments.
- Cross-domain applicability demonstrated with competitive to superior results.
- Potentially impactful for compositional modeling in multiple modalities.

Arguments against acceptance
- Comparisons omit several strong and relevant baselines.
- Key assumptions (unambiguity, Viterbi) not empirically stress-tested.
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses; some ad hoc steps in the event setup.
- No textual experiments despite the generality claim.

Overall evaluation
A solid and thoughtful contribution to structure learning for stochastic And-Or grammars, with an elegant and efficient search criterion and encouraging empirical results. I recommend acceptance if the authors can broaden the comparisons and add targeted ablations/clarifications; otherwise, this is a strong borderline paper.
