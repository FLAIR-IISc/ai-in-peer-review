This paper presents a novel approach to unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammar structures, distinct from previous methods that separately search for AND and OR operations. Instead, it induces unified AND-OR fragments, and the value of a proposed new fragment can be efficiently computed using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is applied to parsing events and visual objects.

Overall, this is a well-written paper addressing an important problem with a well-motivated and technically sound approach. However, I had difficulty verifying the supplemental derivations. A few points were unclear, possibly due to my limited reading time, and I suggest the authors clarify these points: First, the nature of the "surrogate measure" of likelihood gain (approximate or exact) needs further explanation. Second, it is unclear whether the approach can be extended to learn fragments with arity greater than 2 or if it is limited to learning grammars in "Chomsky normal form".

My primary concerns lie in the experimental section, which I found challenging to understand due to a lack of detail on the methodology, data representation, and learned outcomes. The paper appears to cater to an audience familiar with recent visual-grammar papers from S. C. Zhu's group. For readers without this background, the experimental section may benefit from additional details and concrete illustrations, which could be included in supplementary materials if the paper is accepted.

I would have appreciated examples of interesting structures discovered in the learned grammars, as the current quantitative results, while satisfactory, lack excitement and insight. A more compelling presentation would demonstrate the learning of interesting structures and compare the approach to competing methods. This paper offers an interesting contribution to unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars, but its experimental section requires clarification and additional detail to enhance its impact.
