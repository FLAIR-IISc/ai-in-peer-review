Summary
The paper proposes a method for jointly learning the structure and parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars. These grammars combine AND rules (compositional structure) and OR rules (alternative choices), enabling the representation of recursive phenomena across domains, including language and vision. The approach performs unsupervised structure induction by iteratively introducing new AND-OR fragments and scoring them using a balance of likelihood improvement and a prior that penalizes complexity. Experiments on event grammars and image grammars show results that are competitive with prior work.

Strengths
- Clear and appealing framework for unsupervised structure induction in expressive grammars that can capture compositional and alternative structures.
- The fragment-induction strategy, guided by a principled combination of likelihood gain and a prior, is intuitive and potentially widely applicable.
- Empirical results on two distinct domains (events and images) demonstrate promising performance and suggest cross-domain generality.
- The paper addresses both structure and parameter learning, which is often challenging in rich grammar formalisms.

Weaknesses and Questions
- Scalability and tractability are insufficiently addressed. The paper does not quantify the computational complexity of the search over fragments, nor does it provide runtime or memory usage. How does training scale with the number of datapoints, grammar size, and fragment candidates? Will the method handle thousands to tens of thousands of examples?
- The lack of experiments on natural language is a missed opportunity. Since AND-OR grammars are a natural fit for linguistic structure, results on standard NLP benchmarks would significantly strengthen the paper. Is it feasible to train on datasets like the Penn Treebank? How does the approach compare to classical and modern grammar induction methods (e.g., Klein and Manning for PCFG induction), in terms of both accuracy and computational cost?
- Details of the search procedure need clarification. How are candidate fragments generated and pruned? What guarantees or heuristics prevent the search from exploding combinatorially? What are the convergence criteria for the iterative induction process?
- It is unclear how sensitive the method is to initialization, hyperparameters, and the choice of priors. Ablations that remove or vary the prior term, alter fragment sizes, or change the candidate-generation strategy would help explain what drives performance.
- The evaluation focuses on competitiveness with prior art but would benefit from more diagnostic analyses (learning curves, error breakdowns, qualitative examples demonstrating interpretability of learned structures).

Requested clarifications
- Provide theoretical or empirical complexity analysis: expected or worst-case time per iteration, number of candidate fragments considered, and how these scale with data size and grammar depth.
- Report training and inference times, memory usage, and scalability experiments (e.g., performance and runtime as a function of dataset size).
- Describe the fragment proposal mechanism and pruning criteria in detail. Are there mechanisms like beam search, MDL-style penalties, or substructure caching to keep the search tractable?
- Discuss feasibility on natural language: any preliminary results or obstacles (e.g., vocabulary size, non-terminal explosion, sparsity)? Could constraints or weak supervision help?
- Clarify how the prior is chosen and whether it is data-driven or tuned. Include sensitivity analysis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add at least one NLP experiment, even if small-scale (subset of the Penn Treebank, synthetic language with known ground truth, or a constrained tagset/phrase-structure setting). This would demonstrate applicability to the most canonical use case.
- Include scalability studies with larger datasets and grammars, plus wall-clock and memory profiles. If full scaling is not yet feasible, demonstrate partial strategies (e.g., minibatching, parallel candidate evaluation, or stochastic search) and report their effects.
- Provide ablations on fragment induction: limit fragment depth/width, vary prior strength, and compare to fixed-structure baselines to isolate gains from structure learning.
- Offer qualitative visualizations or case studies of learned fragments to highlight interpretability and compositionality.
- Improve reproducibility by detailing hyperparameters, initialization schemes, and stopping criteria.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely contribution that tackles the challenging problem of joint structure and parameter learning for expressive grammars. The cross-domain results are promising, and the fragment-based, likelihood-plus-prior approach is well-motivated. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing scalability head-on and by demonstrating applicability to natural language. With added complexity analysis, runtime/scaling evidence, and at least a targeted NLP experiment or a convincing discussion of feasibility and limitations, I would be enthusiastic about acceptance. As it stands, I am positive about the core ideas and results but would like to see the above issues addressed to solidify the paper’s impact and generality.
