This paper proposes a new approach for learning the structure of AND-OR grammar in an unstructured manner. Unlike previous methods, the current approach creates combined AND-OR fragments instead of searching for separate AND and OR operations. The values of the proposed new AND-OR fragments can be efficiently computed using a set of sufficient statistics. This method has been evaluated on several tasks, such as parsing events and visual objects.

It is generally a good paper. The problem is important. The method is novel and well-inspired. The algorithm seems technically sound, but I was unable to test the supporting claims. A few things were not clear to me, which may only reflect my limited reading time, but I would request the authors to clarify these points more clearly: Firstly, it was not clear whether the "representative measure" of probability gain is an approximate or accurate concept. Secondly, it was not clear whether the method can be extended to learn fragments with more than 2-arity, or whether it can only learn the grammar in "Chomsky's normal form" in a traceable manner.

My main concerns with the paper focus on the tests. I struggled to understand most of them: exactly what was done, how the examples were presented, and what was learned. I got the impression that the paper's ideal audience is a reader who has followed, studied, and possibly re-implemented recent visual-grammar papers from S.C. Zhu's team. To such a reader, the experimental section would likely be more understandable and the results more useful. However, for outsiders, it was less clear. I understand that there are limitations to the small NIPS format. But fortunately, NIPS allows supporting materials, and if the paper is accepted, I would request the authors to include many more details in their supplement and a real picture of their experiments.

I would especially like to see (or be seen) examples of any interesting structures discovered in the learned grammar. The paper has a great setup regarding the importance of learning grammar and structure, but it is somewhat disappointing for these expectations as the results are presented entirely quantitatively. I agree with the authors that those quantitative results are satisfactory on their own terms. However, they are not very exciting or enlightening. I would have found the paper more appealing if the authors could have shown that interesting structures have been learned and that more interesting structures have been learned by this method compared to competitive methods. It is an interesting paper on learning AND-OR grammar in an unstructured manner. Although I liked it, I had trouble following the tests and interpreting the results, as it seemed to be widely made because it was not familiar with many previous works (mostly from Zhu's UCLA group). If accepted, the authors should include more detailed and illustrated supporting materials that show how the tests worked and what interesting structures were learned.
