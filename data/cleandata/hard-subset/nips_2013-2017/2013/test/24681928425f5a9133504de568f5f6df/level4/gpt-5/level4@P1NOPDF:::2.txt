This paper proposes a method for jointly learning the structure and parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars. These grammars combine AND and OR rules and can represent recursive phenomena, including natural language, event grammars, and image grammars. The authors introduce an unsupervised structure-learning approach that incrementally adds new AND-OR fragments and evaluates them using gains in likelihood and prior.

The paper presents experiments on two tasks: learning event grammars and learning image grammars. In both settings, the method achieves results that are competitive with prior work.

Overall, I liked the paper. It presents a promising and seemingly tractable approach to learning stochastic grammars with AND/OR rule structure. My main concerns are as follows:

1) Tractability and scalability are not discussed in sufficient detail. What is the computational complexity of the learning algorithm, and how does it scale with data size and grammar complexity? Will it scale to thousands of datapoints (or more), both in terms of runtime and memory? Some empirical or theoretical analysis here would significantly strengthen the paper.

2) I would have liked to have seen experiments on natural language, which is the most obvious application domain for such grammars. Is it feasible to apply the proposed method to a standard benchmark such as the Penn Treebank, where much prior work (e.g., Klein and Manning) has focused? Even a smaller-scale or ablation study on natural language sentences would help demonstrate applicability and clarify any limitations or necessary adaptations.

In summary, the paper introduces a method for estimating both the structure and parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars and shows solid results on event and image grammar induction. To make the contribution more compelling, I encourage the authors to (a) address the algorithm’s scalability and computational profile, and (b) include, or at least more fully discuss, experiments on natural language data.
