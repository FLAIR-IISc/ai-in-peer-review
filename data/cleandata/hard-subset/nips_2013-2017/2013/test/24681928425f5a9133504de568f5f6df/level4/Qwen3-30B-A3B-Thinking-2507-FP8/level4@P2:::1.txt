

This paper introduces a unified framework for unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, which can model diverse data types including images and event sequences. The authors present a formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to the specific data type, and propose an iterative algorithm that learns both grammar structure and parameters by optimizing the posterior probability. The key innovation lies in using "And-Or fragments" that systematically unify the search for new compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules), addressing a limitation in previous approaches that treated these as separate learning problems.

The paper makes a significant contribution by extending the work on structure learning of natural language grammars [14-16] while improving upon recent approaches for image [17] and event [18] grammars. Previous methods either manually specify grammar structure (which is challenging for complex data) or learn compositions and reconfigurations separately (which is error-prone with scarce data). The proposed approach starts from a trivial initial grammar and iteratively inserts And-Or fragments to optimize the posterior probability, making it more efficient and robust than prior work.

The technical quality of the paper is strong. The formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars is well-motivated and clearly presented. The algorithmic framework is sound, with careful attention to computational efficiency through the use of sufficient statistics to compute posterior gains. The theoretical derivation of likelihood and prior gains (Section 3.3) is thorough, and the authors provide an intuitive explanation of how And-Or fragments alleviate the limitations of previous approaches. The empirical evaluation on two distinct domains (event grammars and image grammars) demonstrates competitive or superior performance compared to baselines, with consistent improvements on more complex datasets.

The paper's clarity is generally excellent. The introduction effectively frames the problem and positions the work within the literature. The formal definitions in Section 2 are precise, and the algorithmic description in Section 3 is well-structured with helpful examples (Figures 1-2). The experimental setup is clearly explained, and the results are presented with appropriate metrics (F-measure for event grammars, perplexity for image grammars). The only minor weakness is that the theoretical analysis in Section 3.3 could benefit from more intuitive explanations to help readers grasp the significance of the coherence measures.

The originality of the work is significant. While the paper builds on previous work on And-Or grammars [1, 3, 7, 17, 18], it makes a clear contribution by unifying the learning of compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments. This approach enables a more compact grammar structure and avoids the limitations of previous methods that learned these components separately. The authors effectively demonstrate how their framework generalizes to different data types without requiring domain-specific adaptations, which is a notable strength compared to previous work.

The significance of this work is substantial. Stochastic And-Or grammars have proven useful for modeling complex patterns in images and events, and this paper addresses a key bottleneck in their application—learning the grammar structure from unannotated data. The ability to learn from diverse data types with a single framework has broad implications for pattern recognition. The empirical results demonstrate clear improvements over previous approaches, particularly on more complex datasets, suggesting this method could be valuable for practitioners in computer vision and activity recognition.

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The main weakness is that the experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including additional comparisons to more recent baselines. Specifically, the method of Stolcke and Omohundro [14] for unsupervised grammar learning could be applied to both event and image grammars as a valuable comparison point, as it represents a state-of-the-art approach for structure learning in natural language processing that could be adapted to these domains. While the current comparisons are sufficient for the paper's claims, adding this comparison would further strengthen the empirical validation.

The paper's main strengths are its clear problem formulation, sound technical approach, and effective empirical validation across two distinct domains. The main weakness is the limited comparison to relevant baselines in the experimental evaluation. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling and grammar learning.

Pro: Unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars applicable to diverse data types; Efficient learning algorithm using And-Or fragments that unifies composition and reconfiguration learning; Strong empirical results on event and image grammars; Clear theoretical foundation with rigorous posterior optimization.

Con: Limited comparison to recent baselines (e.g., Stolcke and Omohundro [14]); Theoretical analysis in Section 3.3 could be more accessible to readers without strong grammatical inference background.
