

This paper proposes a novel approach to unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammar structures. Unlike prior methods that separately search for AND and OR operations, the authors induce unified AND-OR fragments. The value of a proposed fragment can be efficiently computed using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on event parsing and visual object tasks.  

The paper addresses an important problem with a well-motivated, technically sound approach. The algorithm appears rigorous, though I could not verify the supplemental derivations. Two points require clarification: (1) whether the "surrogate measure" for likelihood gain is approximate or exact, and (2) whether the method extends to fragments with arity greater than 2 or is limited to grammars in Chomsky normal form.  

My primary concerns center on the experimental evaluation. The section is difficult to follow for readers unfamiliar with Zhu's group's recent visual-grammar work, which the paper assumes as background. Key details—such as input representation, experimental procedures, and learned structures—are missing. For instance, the paper states that "interesting structure" is learned but presents results purely quantitatively without illustrating discovered grammars or comparing structural properties across approaches. While the quantitative results are satisfactory in isolation, they fail to demonstrate the claimed significance of structural learning. The results would be far more compelling with concrete examples of learned grammars and explicit comparisons of structural quality.  

Given NIPS's constraints, I strongly encourage the authors to include extensive supplementary material detailing experimental protocols, input representations, and visualizations of learned grammars. This would make the work accessible to a broader audience and better justify the method's contribution. The paper is promising but requires greater experimental transparency to fully realize its potential.
