This paper proposes a method for the unsupervised learning of both the structure and parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars. The approach iteratively introduces new AND-OR fragments into the grammar, with the search for an optimal structure guided by improvements in data likelihood and a prior. The authors demonstrate the method's effectiveness on two distinct tasks—learning grammars for events and for images—achieving results that are competitive with previous work.

The primary strength of this work lies in its approach to tackling the challenging problem of grammar induction. By jointly learning the grammar's structure and its parameters in an unsupervised fashion, the authors provide a compelling framework for discovering compositional and recursive patterns in data. The proposed method of incrementally adding grammar fragments appears to be a principled and potentially tractable way to explore the vast search space of possible grammars.

However, the paper could be strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Scalability and Computational Complexity:** A significant concern is the lack of discussion regarding the computational complexity and scalability of the learning algorithm. While the method is presented as tractable, the paper provides no analysis of its performance with respect to the size of the dataset or the complexity of the learned grammar. It is unclear whether the proposed approach would scale to datasets containing thousands or tens of thousands of examples, which is common in many domains. Including an analysis of runtime or experiments on larger datasets would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

2.  **Evaluation on Natural Language Data:** The choice of experimental domains, while demonstrating the method's versatility, omits what is arguably the canonical application for grammar induction: natural language parsing. Given that a primary motivation for stochastic grammars is modeling language, the absence of experiments on standard benchmarks like the Penn Treebank is a notable omission. It would be highly valuable to see how this method compares to established grammar induction techniques in linguistics, such as those developed by Klein and Manning, on a constituency parsing task. Such an evaluation would not only provide a more direct comparison with a large body of previous work but also offer clearer insights into the algorithm's capabilities and limitations in a well-understood and challenging domain.

In summary, this paper presents a novel and promising method for unsupervised grammar induction. While the core idea is elegant and the results on the chosen tasks are competitive, the paper would be substantially improved by a clear analysis of the algorithm's scalability and by an empirical evaluation on a standard natural language processing benchmark.
