

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to the type of data being modeled (e.g., images, events, or natural language), along with an unsupervised approach for learning both the structure and parameters of such grammars. The authors address a significant limitation in prior work where grammar structure learning was either manually specified or handled separately for compositions and reconfigurations. Their key innovation is the introduction of "And-Or fragments," which systematically unify the search for both types of grammar rules in a single learning step. Starting from a trivial initial grammar, their iterative algorithm adds new fragments that optimize the posterior probability of the grammar, leveraging sufficient statistics for efficient computation. The approach builds on previous work in And-Or grammars for images (Zhu et al., NIPS 2011) and events (Si et al., ICCV 2011), but extends it to a more general framework applicable across domains.

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, it provides a clear, data-agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that unifies previous domain-specific formulations. Second, unlike heuristic-based approaches, it explicitly optimizes the posterior probability of the grammar structure and parameters, with an efficient implementation using sufficient statistics. Third, the unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments results in more compact grammars that are more robust to data scarcity compared to prior separate approaches. Finally, the empirical evaluation on event grammars and image grammars demonstrates competitive or superior performance to existing methods, with the third experimental method (which grounds the grammar to atomic actions and learns different relations simultaneously) showing particular promise.

The paper excels in quality, providing a technically sound approach with clear theoretical justification for the posterior optimization procedure. The algorithm is well-motivated and the sufficient statistics derivation is a strong theoretical contribution that enables efficient implementation. The experimental design is appropriate, with careful evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets, and the results are well-presented with appropriate metrics (F-measure for event grammars, perplexity for image grammars). The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-structured exposition, helpful examples, and clear figures illustrating the learning process. The writing is accessible to a broad NIPS audience, though some technical details in the supplementary material could be elaborated in the main text.

The originality of the work is strong. While previous work has explored structure learning of grammars in specific domains, this paper presents a unified framework that works across multiple data types without requiring domain-specific adaptations. The And-Or fragment concept represents a novel approach to grammar learning that addresses key limitations of prior methods, particularly their handling of composition and reconfiguration separately. The paper adequately references related work, including recent NIPS contributions on And-Or grammars (Zhu et al., NIPS 2011; Si et al., ICCV 2011) and natural language grammar learning (Klein & Manning, ACL 2004), though it could better discuss how its approach differs from these.

The significance of the work is clear. Stochastic And-Or grammars have shown promise in modeling complex structures across multiple domains, and this paper provides a more scalable and unified approach to learning these models from unannotated data. The ability to learn grammars that capture both compositionality and reconfigurability without manual specification represents a meaningful advance over prior methods, potentially enabling applications in areas where structured data modeling is crucial but labeled data is scarce.

I have three main concerns regarding the paper. First, the authors do not provide sufficient detail on the algorithm's scalability. The paper states that their prototype implementation runs "within a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples," but this is insufficient to assess whether the method would scale to larger datasets or more complex grammars. A more thorough analysis of computational complexity and performance on larger datasets would strengthen the paper significantly.

Second, while the paper demonstrates effectiveness on event and image grammars, it lacks experiments on natural language data—the most obvious application domain for stochastic grammars. Previous work on unsupervised grammar learning has focused extensively on natural language (e.g., Klein & Manning, ACL 2004; Tu & Honavar, ICGI 2008), and it would be valuable to see how this approach performs on standard NLP benchmarks like the Penn Treebank. Without such experiments, it's difficult to assess the full scope of the method's applicability.

Third, while the unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations is a strength, the paper could better explain why the And-Or fragment approach is superior to learning these components separately. The argument that "the data scarcity problem of And-fragments is alleviated" is somewhat vague—more concrete examples or theoretical justification would strengthen this claim.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of grammar learning. The unified formalization and And-Or fragment approach represent a meaningful advancement over prior work, with solid empirical results demonstrating the method's effectiveness. While the paper would be strengthened by additional experiments on natural language data and more detailed analysis of scalability, these are not critical flaws. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and makes a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic modeling of structured data. I recommend acceptance at NIPS.
