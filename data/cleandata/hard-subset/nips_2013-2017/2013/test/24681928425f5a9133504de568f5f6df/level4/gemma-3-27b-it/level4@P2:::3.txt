This paper introduces a novel approach to unsupervised structure learning of AND-OR grammars. The key contribution lies in inducing unified AND-OR fragments, moving away from the separate learning of AND and OR operations used in previous work. The authors demonstrate an efficient computation of the value of these new fragments utilizing sufficient statistics. The method is evaluated on both event and image parsing tasks.

This is a strong paper addressing an important problem. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and appears technically sound, although a full verification of the supplemental derivations was beyond the scope of this review. While the core ideas are clear, several points could benefit from further clarification. Specifically, the paper would benefit from explicitly stating whether the proposed “surrogate measure” for likelihood gain is an approximation or an exact calculation. Furthermore, it’s unclear if the method is readily extensible to learning fragments with arity greater than two, or if it's implicitly limited to grammars in Chomsky normal form.

My primary concerns relate to the experimental presentation. Understanding the details of the experiments—the specific representations used, the learning process, and the resulting outcomes—proved challenging. The paper seems targeted toward readers deeply familiar with the recent literature on visual grammars, particularly work originating from S.C. Zhu’s group. While this may suffice for experts, it creates a barrier for those less acquainted with this specific research area. The NIPS format limitations are acknowledged, and providing significantly more detailed explanations and concrete illustrations in supplementary materials, should the paper be accepted, would be highly beneficial.

I would be particularly interested in seeing examples of the structure learned by the proposed method. While the paper establishes the importance of grammar and structure learning, the exclusive focus on quantitative results feels somewhat unsatisfying. Although the authors report satisfactory quantitative performance, the results lack intuitive insight. Demonstrating that the approach learns genuinely interesting structural patterns, and that these patterns are superior to those learned by competing methods, would significantly strengthen the paper’s impact.

In summary, this is a promising paper on unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. Despite some difficulties in fully grasping the experimental details and interpreting the results, particularly given my unfamiliarity with prior work from Zhu's lab, the core ideas are compelling. Acceptance is contingent on the authors providing more comprehensive experimental details and illustrative examples in supplementary materials, to increase accessibility and demonstrate the qualitative benefits of the learned structures.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to unsupervised structure learning with theoretical justification.
*   Efficient computation of fragment value via sufficient statistics.
*   Promising experimental results on event and image parsing.
*   Addresses an important problem in probabilistic modeling.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Experimental section lacks sufficient detail and clarity for readers outside the immediate research area.
*   The paper primarily presents quantitative results, without showcasing the qualitative structure learned by the model.
*   Certain aspects of the method (arity limitations, approximation vs. exact calculation) require further clarification.
