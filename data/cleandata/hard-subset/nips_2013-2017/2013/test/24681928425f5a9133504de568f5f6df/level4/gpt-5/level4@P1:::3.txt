Summary and relation to prior work
This paper presents an unsupervised method for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) in a unified, data-agnostic formalism (covering text, images, and events). The key idea is to search over And-Or fragments that simultaneously introduce an And-node and its child Or-nodes, rather than separately inducing And- and Or-rules. The posterior probability of a candidate fragment is optimized with a sparsity prior (penalizing grammar size) and Viterbi likelihood. To make search efficient, the authors derive sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) that allow fast calculation of posterior gains under an unambiguity assumption, with rule probabilities estimated from reduction counts. The method is evaluated on event parsing and image parsing, showing comparable or better performance than prior approaches for event grammars and significant perplexity reductions for image grammars.

The work builds on grammar induction in NLP [11–16], image AOG learning [17], event grammar learning [18], and is conceptually related to SPNs [10]. It leverages Viterbi training and unambiguity biases [20, 21] and adopts coherence measures [22] to factor likelihood gains.

Strengths
- Clear contribution: learning And-Or fragments unifies the induction of compositions and reconfigurations, addressing data sparsity and redundancy issues that arise when learning And- and Or-rules separately.
- Principled objective: explicit posterior optimization with a sparsity prior, and a coherent account of likelihood/prior gains via sufficient statistics.
- Generality: a unified context-free AOG formalization that is agnostic to data type and relation types, demonstrated on both temporal (events) and spatial (images) domains.
- Empirical results: competitive on events and substantially better perplexity on real image data compared to [17].

Weaknesses and questions
- Exactness of likelihood gain: The paper refers to a “surrogate measure” based on tensor/matrix coherence. Is the reported likelihood gain an exact computation under the stated assumptions (Viterbi, unambiguous parses), or an approximation? Please make this explicit.
- Arity and normal form: Can the approach efficiently learn fragments with arity > 2, or is it practically restricted to binary rules (akin to Chomsky normal form)? This affects expressivity and search complexity.
- Assumptions: The unambiguity assumption is crucial to the sufficient-statistics formulation. How sensitive are results to violations of this assumption? Any diagnostics or safeguards?
- Experimental clarity: The event experiments, in particular, were difficult to follow without close familiarity with prior AOG work. The mappings from raw data to terminals, the relation sets used in And-rules (“followed-by,” “co-occurring”), the exact fragment search constraints, and how reductions update top-level rules need more concrete illustrations.
- Qualitative insight: The paper argues for learning rich structure, but presents only quantitative results. Examples of learned fragments/grammars, parse trees for representative events/images, and comparisons of learned structure against baselines would make the contribution much more compelling.
- Reproducibility: Important details are deferred to the supplement (derivations, pseudocode). Please ensure the supplement includes implementation details, hyperparameters (e.g., α = 0.5), search settings (beam width, restarts), encoding costs (sa, so), and data preprocessing.

Assessment by NIPS criteria
- Quality: The method appears technically sound, with a principled objective and efficient computation via sufficient statistics. I could not verify the supplemental derivations; clarity on exact vs approximate likelihood gain would strengthen confidence. The empirical comparisons are appropriate, though more qualitative analysis would help validate the structural claims.
- Clarity: The core idea and algorithmic framework are well motivated, but the event experiments are hard to interpret for readers not steeped in prior AOG work. The paper would benefit from clearer step-by-step examples and visualizations of learned structures.
- Originality: Learning And-Or fragments that unify induction of And- and Or-rules is novel and differentiates this work from prior structure learning in AOGs and from NLP grammar induction methods.
- Significance: A general, efficient approach to unsupervised AOG learning is valuable and likely to influence future work in structured perception. The quantitative gains, especially on image perplexity, are promising; the impact would be higher with convincing qualitative demonstrations of learned structure.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify whether the likelihood gain is exact under the specified assumptions or an approximation; if approximate, characterize the error or conditions under which it is tight.
- State explicitly whether n-ary fragments are supported and how search scales with arity; if restricted to binary, discuss how that affects modeling.
- Provide qualitative examples: visualize several learned And-Or fragments (events and images), show parse trees before/after reductions, and highlight differences from baselines.
- Expand the supplement with full derivations, algorithm pseudocode, parameter settings, dataset preprocessing, and ablations on search strategies (greedy vs beam, restarts).
- Discuss robustness to grammar ambiguity and the effect of using Viterbi likelihood vs full likelihood.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Novel unified fragment-based structure learning for AOGs.
- Principled posterior objective with efficient sufficient-statistics computation.
- Cross-domain applicability and solid quantitative improvements, especially on images.

Cons:
- Experimental section is hard to follow without extensive prior background.
- Lack of qualitative analysis of learned structures limits interpretability.
- Open questions about exactness of likelihood gain and support for higher-arity fragments.

Overall recommendation
This is an interesting and promising paper on unsupervised AOG learning. I liked the core idea and found the quantitative results satisfactory. However, I struggled to follow the experimental setup and to interpret what structures were actually learned. If accepted, the authors should provide a detailed supplement with concrete examples, visualizations, and clarifications on the two key questions (surrogate/exact likelihood gain; support for arity > 2).
