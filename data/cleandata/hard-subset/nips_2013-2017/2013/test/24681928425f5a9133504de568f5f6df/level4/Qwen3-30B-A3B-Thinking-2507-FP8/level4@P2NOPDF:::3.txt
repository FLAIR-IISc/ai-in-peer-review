

This paper introduces a novel approach for unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammar structure, distinguishing itself by inducing unified AND-OR fragments rather than separately optimizing for AND and OR operations. The method efficiently computes fragment value using sufficient statistics, and evaluates its effectiveness on event parsing and visual object tasks. The problem is significant, the approach is well-motivated and technically sound (though supplemental derivations were not verified), and the core idea represents a meaningful advance over prior work.

Two points require clarification to strengthen the paper. First, the paper describes a "surrogate measure" for likelihood gain but does not specify whether this is an approximation or exact. Second, it remains unclear whether the approach generalizes beyond binary fragments (i.e., fragments with arity >2) or is restricted to grammars in Chomsky normal form. These details are critical for assessing the method's scope and limitations.

The primary weakness lies in the experimental section. The description lacks sufficient detail for readers unfamiliar with Zhu’s group’s recent visual-grammar work, making it difficult to understand: (1) the exact experimental setup and data representation; (2) the nature of the learned structures; and (3) how results compare to baselines. The paper assumes an audience deeply versed in the foundational literature from this specific group, which limits its accessibility. While NIPS’ length constraints are acknowledged, supplementary materials offer an ideal opportunity to address this. The authors should include detailed experimental descriptions, concrete visualizations of input data and learned grammars, and step-by-step examples of the learning process.

Furthermore, the paper’s strong theoretical motivation about the importance of structural learning is not fully realized in the results. The quantitative evaluations—while statistically valid—are presented without qualitative insights into the *structure* discovered. The authors miss an opportunity to demonstrate *why* their method produces meaningful grammars by showing examples of interesting, interpretable structures learned (e.g., hierarchical patterns in visual scenes or event sequences). Comparing the *types* of structures learned across methods would significantly strengthen the contribution. Merely reporting accuracy or likelihood gains fails to leverage the paper’s core premise about structural discovery. For instance, illustrating a learned fragment that captures a recurring compositional pattern (e.g., "object part" relationships in images) would better justify the approach’s value than a table of numbers alone.

In summary, this is an interesting paper with a solid theoretical foundation and a promising method. However, the experimental section requires substantial enhancement to be compelling and accessible. The current presentation risks leaving readers—especially those outside Zhu’s research circle—uncertain about the method’s practical utility and the nature of the structures it discovers. If the authors address these concerns in the supplement by providing detailed experimental documentation, visual examples of learned structures, and qualitative comparisons of grammatical patterns, the paper’s impact would be greatly amplified. As it stands, the results, while adequate, do not fully deliver on the promise of structure-based unsupervised learning. The work merits acceptance pending these clarifications, which are feasible within the supplementary material constraints.
