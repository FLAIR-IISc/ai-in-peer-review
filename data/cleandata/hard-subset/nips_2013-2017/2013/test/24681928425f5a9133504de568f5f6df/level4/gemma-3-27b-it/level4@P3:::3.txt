This paper proposes a novel approach to unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or grammars. Unlike previous methods that learn And and Or operations separately, this work introduces a unified framework for inducing And-Or fragments, aiming for more efficient and robust structure learning. The approach leverages sufficient statistics to efficiently compute the value of proposed fragments, and is evaluated on event and image grammar learning tasks.

The paper addresses an important problem in unsupervised learning – discovering both the structure and parameters of complex generative models. The proposed method represents a significant step forward by unifying the search for compositions and reconfigurations within And-Or grammars. The motivation is clear, and the theoretical foundation appears sound, though a complete verification of the supplemental derivations was not possible within the review timeframe.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The unified approach to learning And-Or fragments is a key contribution, potentially addressing limitations of previous separate-learning strategies.
* **Theoretical grounding:** The use of sufficient statistics for efficient computation is a strong point, suggesting scalability and practical applicability.
* **Empirical evaluation:** The experiments on both event and image grammars demonstrate the approach's competitiveness and, in some cases, superiority to existing methods.
* **Clear motivation:** The paper effectively articulates the challenges of unsupervised grammar learning and positions the proposed solution within the broader context of the field.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of experimental details:** The experimental section lacks sufficient detail for readers unfamiliar with the prior work from the Zhu group. More concrete illustrations of data representation, learned structures, and comparison baselines would significantly improve understanding. It feels as though substantial prior knowledge is required to fully appreciate the results.
* **Limited qualitative analysis:** While the quantitative results are promising, the paper would benefit from a qualitative analysis of the learned grammars. Showing examples of discovered structures and comparing them to competing approaches would greatly strengthen the impact of the work.  The focus on purely quantitative results feels somewhat unsatisfying given the paper’s emphasis on the importance of grammar structure.
* **Scalability considerations:** The paper does not explicitly address the scalability of the approach to grammars with higher arity (beyond binary decompositions) or the implications of enforcing a specific grammar form (e.g., Chomsky Normal Form).  Addressing these points would strengthen the theoretical contribution.
* **Ambiguity of “surrogate measure”:** It isn’t immediately clear whether the described “surrogate measure” for likelihood gain is an exact calculation or an approximation. Clarifying this would improve precision.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in unsupervised learning.
* The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on multiple datasets.
* The use of sufficient statistics is a valuable contribution for efficient computation.
* The unification of And and Or fragment learning offers a conceptually elegant and potentially powerful improvement over previous methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The experimental section is difficult to follow for readers not deeply familiar with the related work.
* The lack of qualitative analysis limits the interpretability and impact of the results.
* The scalability limitations to higher-arity fragments are not explicitly addressed.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good paper with a clear contribution to the field of unsupervised learning and stochastic grammars. The technical approach is well-motivated and appears sound. However, the lack of clarity in the experimental details and the absence of qualitative analysis weaken the overall impact. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with the caveat that the authors significantly expand the supplementary material to include detailed explanations of the experiments, concrete examples of learned grammars, and a more thorough discussion of the scalability and limitations of the approach.  Specifically, including visualizations of the learned structures and a clearer explanation of the data representations would greatly enhance the paper’s accessibility and impact.
