This paper presents a new approach to unsupervised learning of the structure of AND-OR grammars. Unlike prior methods, it induces unified AND-OR fragments rather than separately searching for AND and OR operations. The value of a proposed fragment can be computed efficiently using a set of sufficient statistics. The method is evaluated on several tasks, including parsing events and visual objects.

Overall, this is a solid paper. The problem is important, and the approach is novel and well-motivated. The algorithm appears technically sound, although I was not able to verify the supplemental derivations. A few points need clearer exposition: (1) Is the “surrogate measure” of the likelihood gain an exact quantity or an approximation? (2) Can the method extend to fragments with arity greater than two, or is it only tractable for grammars in Chomsky normal form?

My main concerns are with the experiments. I found it difficult to determine exactly what was done, how the examples were represented, and what was learned. The experimental section seems primarily targeted at readers who have closely followed, studied, and perhaps re-implemented recent visual-grammar work from S. C. Zhu’s group; for such readers, the presentation would likely be clearer and the results more informative. For outsiders, it is much less accessible. I recognize the constraints of the short NIPS format, but since NIPS allows supplementary material, if the paper is accepted, the authors should provide substantially more details and concrete illustrations of their experimental setups, representations, and learned outputs.

I would particularly like to see examples of interesting structures discovered by the learned grammars. The paper makes a strong case for the importance of grammar and structure learning, but the results are presented purely quantitatively, which feels like a missed opportunity. While the quantitative performance is satisfactory on its own terms, it is neither very exciting nor especially illuminating. The paper would be more compelling if it demonstrated that the method discovers interesting structure—and more of it relative to competing approaches.

In sum, this is an interesting paper on unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. I liked it, but I struggled to follow the experiments and interpret the results, in part because I am not deeply familiar with the prior work (largely from Zhu’s UCLA group) on which it heavily builds. If accepted, the authors should include supplementary material with more details and illustrations explaining how the experiments were conducted, how data were represented, and what meaningful structures were learned.
