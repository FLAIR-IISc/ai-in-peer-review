

This paper proposes a novel unsupervised approach for learning the structure of AND-OR grammars. Unlike prior work, it induces unified AND-OR fragments instead of separately searching for AND and OR operations. The value of a proposed fragment is efficiently computed using sufficient statistics. The method is evaluated on parsing events and visual objects.  

This is a strong paper. The problem is important, the approach is novel and well-motivated, and the algorithm appears technically sound (though I was unable to verify the supplemental derivations). Two points require clarification: first, it is unclear whether the "surrogate measure" of likelihood gain is approximate or exact; second, it is unclear whether the approach extends to fragments with arity greater than 2 or is limited to grammars in Chomsky normal form.  

My primary concerns involve the experiments. I struggled to understand the methodology: how examples were represented, what was learned, and the exact experimental procedure. The paper assumes familiarity with recent visual-grammar work from S. C. Zhu's group, making the experimental section opaque to outsiders. While NIPS constraints are acknowledged, supplementary materials should include detailed explanations and concrete illustrations of the experiments.  

Most critically, the paper fails to showcase interesting structures discovered in the learned grammars. Despite a compelling motivation for structural learning, the results are presented purely quantitatively without illustrative examples. While the quantitative results are satisfactory, they lack excitement and fail to demonstrate why the learned structures are meaningful or superior to alternatives. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including examples of discovered structures and comparative analysis of structural quality. This is an interesting contribution, but its impact would be greatly enhanced with supplementary details and structural examples.
