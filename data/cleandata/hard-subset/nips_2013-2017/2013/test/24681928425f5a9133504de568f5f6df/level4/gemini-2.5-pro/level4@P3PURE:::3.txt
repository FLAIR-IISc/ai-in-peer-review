This paper proposes a new method for the unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammar structures. In contrast to prior work that searches for AND and OR productions separately, the proposed approach induces unified AND-OR fragments. The value of a candidate fragment is computed efficiently using a set of sufficient statistics. The method is evaluated on tasks involving parsing events and visual objects.

This is a well-motivated paper that addresses the important problem of grammar induction. The core idea of learning unified fragments is novel, and the algorithm appears to be technically sound, although I was unable to verify the derivations in the supplementary material.

There were a few points that could be clarified to improve the paper's accessibility:
1.  Is the "surrogate measure" of the likelihood gain an approximation, or is it an exact calculation?
2.  Can the approach be extended to learn fragments with an arity greater than two, or is it fundamentally limited to learning grammars in a structure analogous to Chomsky normal form?

My main concern, however, lies with the experimental evaluation. I found it difficult to understand the experimental setup, the representation of the examples, and the specific structures that were learned. The presentation seems to assume that the reader is intimately familiar with the recent body of work on visual grammars from the authors' research group. While this may make the section clear to a specialized audience, it is opaque to outsiders.

Given the constraints of the conference format, a more detailed explanation in the main paper may not be feasible. However, if the paper is accepted, I would strongly urge the authors to use the supplementary materials to provide a much more thorough description of the experiments, including concrete illustrations.

Furthermore, the paper's motivation emphasizes the importance of learning meaningful grammatical structure, yet the evaluation is presented in purely quantitative terms. While the quantitative results are satisfactory, they are not particularly illuminating and do not fully deliver on the paper's premise. The work would be far more compelling if the authors showcased examples of the interesting structures discovered by their learned grammars. Demonstrating that the proposed method learns more meaningful or insightful structures compared to competing approaches would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

In summary, this is an interesting paper with a novel approach to unsupervised grammar learning. Its primary weakness is an experimental section that is difficult to interpret for a general audience. If accepted, the authors should be required to provide supplementary material with significantly more detail on their experiments and, crucially, to include qualitative results that illustrate the kinds of structures their model learns.
