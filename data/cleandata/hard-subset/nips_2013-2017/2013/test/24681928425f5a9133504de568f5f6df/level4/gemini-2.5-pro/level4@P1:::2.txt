This paper presents a method for the unsupervised learning of both the structure and the parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars. These grammars serve as powerful generative models for compositional and reconfigurable patterns, with applications in domains like images and events. The authors propose a unified framework that learns from unannotated data using an iterative, bottom-up algorithm. Starting from a trivial grammar that simply memorizes the training data, the method progressively introduces new "And-Or fragments." This core contribution is a novel structure that jointly introduces a compositional And-rule and several reconfigurable Or-rules, unifying a search process that previous methods often handled separately. The decision to add a particular fragment is guided by a principled objective: maximizing the posterior probability of the grammar. The authors derive an efficient method to calculate the gain in this objective, based on sufficient statistics from the data. The general applicability of the framework is demonstrated through experiments on learning event grammars from video and image grammars from both synthetic and real-world datasets.

**Strengths**

*   The central idea of learning via "And-Or fragments" is novel and well-motivated. Unifying the search for compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules) is an elegant approach that directly addresses potential issues of data scarcity and search inefficiency that can arise when these structures are learned independently.
*   The overall method is technically sound and principled, framed as an optimization of the grammar's posterior probability. The derivation of the likelihood and prior gains provides an efficient, data-driven heuristic to guide the greedy search for new grammar fragments.
*   The paper provides a general, data-agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars, which effectively highlights the potential for the framework to be applied to a wide variety of data types beyond those explored in the experiments.
*   The empirical results presented are strong. On both the event grammar and image grammar learning tasks, the proposed method achieves performance that is competitive with or superior to prior, more specialized approaches, successfully demonstrating its effectiveness.

**Weaknesses**

*   The scalability of the learning algorithm could be analyzed more thoroughly. While the authors report fast runtimes on their datasets (a few minutes for 5000 samples), the paper would be strengthened by a more formal discussion of the algorithm's computational complexity. It is not entirely clear how the search for optimal And-Or fragments scales with factors such as the size of the terminal vocabulary, the length of the input samples, or the complexity of the target grammar. Such an analysis would help readers better understand the practical limitations of the approach on larger-scale problems.
*   A significant weakness is the absence of experiments on natural language data. Given that stochastic context-free grammars have their roots in natural language processing, this domain represents a crucial and standard benchmark for grammar induction algorithms. The authors motivate their work by referencing structure learning in natural language grammars, which makes this omission particularly noticeable. Evaluating the method on a standard dataset like the Penn Treebank would provide a valuable comparison against seminal work in the field (e.g., Klein and Manning, 2004) and would substantially strengthen the paper's claims of generality.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a well-written paper presenting a solid contribution to unsupervised structure learning and grammar induction. The core idea of learning with And-Or fragments is technically sound, novel, and effectively addresses key challenges in the field. The strengths lie in the principled optimization framework and the strong empirical results on vision-related tasks. The primary weaknesses are the lack of a formal complexity analysis and the omission of experiments in the natural language domain, which feels like a missed opportunity for a more comprehensive evaluation. Despite these points, the paper introduces a valuable and interesting approach that is likely to be useful for researchers working on structured probabilistic models.
