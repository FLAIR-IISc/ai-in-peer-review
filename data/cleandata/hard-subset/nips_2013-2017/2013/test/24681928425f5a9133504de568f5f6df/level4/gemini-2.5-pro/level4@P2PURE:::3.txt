This paper proposes a novel approach to the unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammar structures. In contrast to previous methods that search for AND and OR operations separately, this work induces unified AND-OR fragments. The value of a new fragment can be computed efficiently using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on tasks involving parsing events and visual objects.

Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. The algorithm appears to be technically sound, although I was unable to verify the derivations in the supplementary material.

A few points could be clarified to strengthen the presentation:
1.  It is not specified whether the "surrogate measure" of the likelihood gain is an approximation or an exact quantity.
2.  The paper should clarify if the approach can be extended to learn fragments with an arity greater than two, or if it is restricted to learning grammars in a form analogous to Chomsky normal form.

My main concern is with the experimental section, which I found difficult to understand in terms of the exact procedures, data representations, and the nature of the learned structures. The presentation seems to assume that the reader is intimately familiar with recent visual-grammar publications, particularly from S.C. Zhu's group, which makes the results less accessible and useful for a broader audience. While I appreciate the page limitations of the NIPS format, I strongly urge the authors to use the supplementary materials to provide significantly more detail and concrete illustrations of their experiments if the paper is accepted.

Furthermore, the paper's motivation centers on the importance of learning grammars and discovering structure, yet the evaluation is purely quantitative. While the quantitative results are satisfactory, they are not particularly illuminating and do not fully deliver on the paper's premise. The paper would be far more compelling if it presented examples of the interesting structures discovered in the learned grammars. Demonstrating that this approach learns more meaningful structures compared to competing methods would provide much stronger evidence of its advantages.
