This paper introduces an unsupervised method for learning the structure of AND-OR grammars. Unlike prior work that separately searches for AND and OR operations, the proposed approach induces unified AND-OR fragments. The score of a candidate fragment can be computed efficiently from sufficient statistics. The method is evaluated on event parsing and visual object parsing.

Overall, this is a solid paper addressing an important problem. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and appears technically sound. I could not, however, verify the supplemental derivations. A few points need clearer exposition:
- It is unclear whether the “surrogate measure” for likelihood gain is an exact computation or an approximation.
- It is also unclear whether the method can learn fragments of arity greater than two, or whether it is effectively limited to binary structures (i.e., Chomsky normal form) for tractability.

My primary concerns are with the experimental section. I found it difficult to reconstruct exactly what was done: how the data were represented, what model components were learned, and the precise evaluation protocol. The presentation seems tailored to readers who have closely followed and perhaps re-implemented recent visual-grammar work from S. C. Zhu’s group. Such readers may find the experiments straightforward, but for others the details are hard to parse. I recognize the constraints of the NIPS format, but NIPS does allow supplementary materials. If the paper is accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to provide a comprehensive supplement with:
- Clear descriptions of datasets, preprocessing, and feature representations;
- Precise definitions of the learned fragments and grammar components;
- Details of the search procedure, objective(s), and hyperparameters;
- Decoding/inference procedures, evaluation metrics, and ablations;
- Concrete visualizations of learned fragments, parse trees, and example parses.

I would especially like to see qualitative examples of the structures the model discovers. The paper convincingly motivates the importance of grammar induction, but the results are presented almost entirely as quantitative scores. While the reported numbers are satisfactory, they are not very illuminating. Demonstrating that the method recovers interesting and interpretable structures—and that these are richer or more accurate than those produced by competing approaches—would significantly strengthen the paper.

In summary, this is an interesting contribution to unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. I liked the core idea and the technical approach, but I struggled to follow the experiments and interpret the results without deep familiarity with related prior work. If accepted, the authors should include a detailed and illustrated supplementary document that clarifies the experimental setup and showcases representative learned structures. Additionally, please clarify whether the likelihood “surrogate” is exact or approximate, and whether the method extends beyond binary-arity fragments.
