This paper proposes a novel approach to unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. Unlike prior work that independently searches for AND and OR operations, this paper induces unified AND-OR fragments. The value of a proposed new fragment is computed efficiently using sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on tasks involving parsing both events and visual objects.

This is a generally strong paper addressing an important problem with a novel and well-motivated technique. The algorithm appears technically sound, though I was unable to fully verify the supplemental derivations. A few points lacked clarity, which may be due to time constraints on my part, but I encourage the authors to address them explicitly: First, it is unclear whether the “surrogate measure” of likelihood gain represents an approximation or an exact calculation. Second, the paper does not specify whether the approach can extend to learning fragments with arity greater than 2, or if it is limited to tractably learning grammars in Chomsky normal form. 

My primary concerns lie with the experimental section. I found it difficult to understand the details of the experiments – specifically, what was done, how the examples were represented, and what was ultimately learned. The presentation strongly suggests that the intended audience is well-versed in the recent visual-grammar literature from S. C. Zhu’s group, potentially having even re-implemented it. For readers familiar with that body of work, the experimental section may be more accessible and the results more informative. However, for those outside this immediate area, it lacks sufficient clarity. While I acknowledge the constraints of the NIPS short-paper format, the allowance for supplementary materials provides an opportunity to address this. If accepted, I strongly recommend the authors expand the supplement with significantly more detail and concrete illustrations of their experimental setup.

I would be particularly interested in seeing examples of any interesting structural patterns discovered within the learned grammars. The paper effectively establishes the importance of grammars and structure learning, but the presentation of results is almost exclusively quantitative, which is somewhat disappointing given these expectations. While the quantitative results are satisfactory in their own right, they are not particularly illuminating. The paper would be more compelling if the authors could demonstrate that their approach learns genuinely interesting structure, and that this structure is richer or more insightful than that learned by competing methods. 

Overall, this is an interesting paper on the unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. I found it promising, but struggled to follow the experiments and interpret the results due to my unfamiliarity with much of the relevant prior work (primarily originating from Zhu’s UCLA group). Should the paper be accepted, I recommend the authors provide supplementary material detailing the experimental procedures and illustrating the learned structures.
