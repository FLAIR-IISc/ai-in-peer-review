This paper proposes a novel approach to unsupervised learning of the structure of AND-OR grammars, differing from previous methods by inducing unified AND-OR fragments rather than separately searching for AND and OR operations. The value of a proposed new AND-OR fragment can be efficiently computed using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on several tasks, including parsing events and visual objects.

Overall, this is a well-written paper addressing an important problem. The approach is novel and well-motivated, and the algorithm appears technically sound, although I was unable to verify the supplemental derivations. However, a few aspects of the paper were unclear, and I would recommend that the authors clarify these points. Specifically, it was not evident whether the "surrogate measure" of likelihood gain is an approximate or exact notion. Additionally, it was unclear whether the approach can be extended to learn fragments with an arity greater than 2 or if it is limited to learning grammars in "Chomsky normal form".

My primary concerns lie with the experimental section. I had difficulty understanding the specifics of the experiments, including the representation of examples and what was learned. The paper appears to be targeted towards readers familiar with recent visual-grammar papers from S. C. Zhu's group. While the experimental section may be clear to such readers, it may be less accessible to those without prior knowledge in this area. Given the limitations of the NIPS format, I suggest that the authors include supplementary materials providing more details and concrete illustrations of their experiments if the paper is accepted.

In particular, I would have liked to see examples of interesting structures discovered in the learned grammars. The paper effectively sets up the importance of grammars and structure learning, but the purely quantitative presentation of the results is somewhat disappointing. While the authors acknowledge that the quantitative results are satisfactory, they lack excitement and insight. I believe the paper would be more compelling if the authors could demonstrate that interesting structures are learned and that their approach outperforms competing methods in this regard.
