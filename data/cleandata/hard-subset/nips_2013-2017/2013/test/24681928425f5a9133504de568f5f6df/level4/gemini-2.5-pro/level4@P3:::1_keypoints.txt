- Proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars by iteratively refining a trivial grammar using induced "And-Or fragments."  
- And-Or fragments jointly model composition (And-rules) and alternatives/reconfiguration (Or-rules), unifying structure induction.  
- Learning framed as maximizing posterior probability combining a Viterbi likelihood term and a sparsity prior to favor compact grammars.  
- Efficient posterior-gain computation derived using sufficient statistics to evaluate candidate fragment additions.  
- Data-agnostic grammar formulation demonstrated on two modalities: event grammars from human activity videos and image grammars from animal face sketches.  
- Empirical results show performance comparable to or better than recent domain-specific methods, especially on complex datasets.  

- Novel and principled framework: unified induction of And-Or fragments is well-motivated and addresses compositionality and reconfiguration together.  
- Clear Bayesian optimization objective with sound derivation of posterior gain guiding greedy fragment search.  
- General and broadly applicable approach, validated across temporal and spatial domains.  
- Strong empirical validation showing competitive or superior results to recent specialized methods.  

- Claim of "generalizing" previous work may overstate scope; method is a specific, tractable search strategy within the broader grammar-induction problem.  
- Limited experimental baselines: lacks comparison to classic grammar-induction methods (e.g., Stolcke & Omohundro Bayesian model merging).  
- Search procedure uses greedy/beam search with random restarts, which is prone to local optima; discussion and analysis of sensitivity is brief.  

- For acceptance: novel, technically sound method with data-agnostic formulation and strong empirical evidence across domains.  
- Against acceptance: narrow experimental comparisons (missing classic baselines) and limited analysis of search limitations.  

- Overall assessment: technically solid, well-written, original, and significant contribution likely to influence structured-model research; weaknesses are minor and addressable.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
