This paper proposes an unsupervised method to learn both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), presented in a unified, data-type-agnostic formalism. The authors start from a trivial grammar that exactly reproduces the training samples and iteratively insert And-Or fragments—each combining an And-rule with several sibling Or-rules—that jointly induce compositions and reconfigurations. The learning objective maximizes the posterior of the grammar under a sparsity prior on grammar size, with a Viterbi (best-parse) likelihood that encourages unambiguous parses. To make search efficient, the method assumes unambiguity to derive sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) that factor the likelihood gain into coherence measures, and uses greedy or beam search over candidate fragments. Experiments on event grammars and image grammars show performance comparable to or better than prior methods [17, 18].

Relation to prior work: The paper situates AOGs relative to PCFGs, SPNs, and prior AOGs for images and events. The learning strategy extends ideas from grammar induction in NLP (e.g., Bayesian model merging [14], ADIOS [15], iterative biclustering [16]) but, crucially, learns a restricted, context-free subclass with a specific fragment type (And-Or fragments) and Viterbi training. While the paper suggests generalization over prior structure learning, the contribution is more accurately a tractable specialization that unifies the search for composition and reconfiguration within AOGs. This is a meaningful and useful restriction, but it is not broader than the structure learning problem typically addressed in NLP.

Strengths:
- Clear, unified formalization of context-free stochastic AOGs, independent of domain.
- A practical structure-learning algorithm that integrates composition and reconfiguration via And-Or fragments, mitigating data sparsity and redundancy relative to separately learning And- and Or-fragments.
- Principled objective (posterior with sparsity prior) and an efficient approximation via sufficient statistics and Viterbi likelihood.
- Empirical results on two domains (events and images) showing competitive or superior performance to recent AOG learners [17, 18].
- Sensible engineering choices (e.g., fragment-level search, reductions, simple termination) leading to reasonable runtime.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Claim of generality is overstated. The method addresses a specific, context-free AOG subclass with an unambiguity assumption and Viterbi training. This specialization makes the problem tractable but is not more general than standard NLP grammar induction.
- Limited baselines. A broader set of comparisons would strengthen the case, especially applying Stolcke and Omohundro’s Bayesian model merging [14] to both tasks. Other relevant NLP induction baselines (e.g., EM-trained PCFGs [11], Klein & Manning [12]) could be adapted to the event sequence setting.
- The unambiguity assumption and reliance on Viterbi likelihood may bias learning toward compact but potentially brittle grammars; a discussion of failure modes or ambiguity handling would help.
- Some implementation details are under-specified for reproducibility: encoding lengths (sa, so), beam width, sensitivity to α, and the exact construction of contexts in the context matrix.
- The “data-type-agnostic” claim would be more convincing with a textual benchmark, even small-scale, given the connections to PCFG induction.

Quality:
- The method is technically sound and well-motivated. The posterior gain decomposition, use of sufficient statistics, and explicit prior are coherent.
- The empirical section is positive but would benefit from stronger baselines and ablations (e.g., And-Or fragments vs. And-only or Or-only searches; impact of Viterbi vs. standard likelihood; effect of the unambiguity assumption).

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized. The learning process and figures are helpful.
- Suggested clarifications: precisely define the encoding for grammar size (sa, so); detail the construction of contexts and n-gram tensors; describe beam search parameters and termination; discuss computational complexity.

Originality:
- The idea of learning And-Or fragments that jointly induce composition and reconfiguration within AOGs is novel within the AOG literature and a practical adaptation of classic grammar induction strategies.
- The sufficient-statistics view and coherence-based interpretation are interesting, though grounded in established ideas.

Significance:
- Useful for practitioners modeling compositional, reconfigurable structures in images and events; likely to be adopted as a more robust AOG learner.
- Broader impact would be enhanced by stronger cross-domain validation and comparisons to classic NLP induction methods.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add comparisons to Bayesian model merging [14] on both datasets; where feasible, include EM-PCFG [11] or Klein & Manning [12] variants adapted to the event sequences.
- Provide ablations: (i) remove Or from fragments; (ii) replace Viterbi likelihood with standard likelihood; (iii) vary α, beam width.
- Justify and test the unambiguity assumption (e.g., a controlled experiment on ambiguous data).
- Report runtime and complexity as a function of training size and fragment arity; clarify how contexts are defined for the event and image tasks.
- If possible, include a small text corpus demonstration to support the data-agnostic claim.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid, well-motivated algorithm that unifies structure induction of composition and reconfiguration.
- Clear formalization, practical search strategy, and positive empirical results across two domains.
- Likely to be of interest to communities working with AOGs, SPNs, and compositional models.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Overstated generality relative to NLP grammar induction; specialized setting limits broader claims.
- Insufficient comparative evaluation against classic grammar induction methods (especially [14]).
- Some assumptions (unambiguity, Viterbi training) and implementation details are not fully justified or analyzed.

Overall, this is a strong and useful contribution on learning the structure of a particular, practically important class of grammars. With expanded comparisons and clarifications, it would be a solid addition to the program.
