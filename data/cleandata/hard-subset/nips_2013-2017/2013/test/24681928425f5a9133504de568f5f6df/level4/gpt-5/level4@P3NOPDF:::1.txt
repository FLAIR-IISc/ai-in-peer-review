This paper proposes a method for learning the structure of stochastic And-Or grammars. While the authors present the approach as a generalization of prior structure-learning work, it is more accurately a specialized instance of the broader grammar induction problem common in NLP. That specialization is not a drawback—in fact, it appears to be what makes the problem tractable—and the paper’s contribution is meaningful within this targeted setting. The algorithm is well-motivated, technically sound, and performs competitively against existing grammar induction methods.

Strengths
- Clear and principled formulation: The restriction to stochastic And-Or grammars yields a well-defined search space that enables efficient learning without sacrificing modeling utility for the targeted domains.
- Sensible algorithmic design: The learning procedure is coherent, and the paper provides enough detail to understand the steps and their rationale.
- Empirical effectiveness: Within the scope of the reported experiments, the method compares favorably to several existing approaches for inducing grammars.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement
- Overstated generality: The paper should avoid framing the contribution as a generalization of the broader structure learning problem in NLP. It is better positioned as an effective and tractable solution for a specific subclass of grammars.
- Limited experimental comparisons: The empirical evaluation would be stronger with additional, well-established baselines. In particular, Bayesian model merging methods such as Stolcke and Omohundro (ref. 14) appear applicable to both tasks studied here and would provide a valuable comparison point. Including this baseline would substantially improve the credibility of the empirical claims.
- Ablation and analysis: The paper would benefit from ablations clarifying which components of the method drive the gains (e.g., specific scoring functions, search heuristics, or constraints). Additionally, more discussion of computational complexity, runtime, and scalability would help readers assess practical utility.
- Sensitivity and robustness: Reporting sensitivity to hyperparameters, initialization, and data size/noise would strengthen the evaluation and demonstrate robustness across settings.

Suggestions
- Reframe the claims to emphasize that the key insight is the tractable specialization to stochastic And-Or grammars, rather than a generalization over existing grammar induction frameworks.
- Add comparisons to Stolcke and Omohundro’s Bayesian model merging and any other widely used grammar induction baselines that are feasible to adapt, reporting both accuracy/quality metrics and computational cost.
- Provide ablations and error analyses to illustrate where the method succeeds or fails and to quantify the contributions of individual components.
- Include runtime and scalability results (e.g., as a function of grammar size and data volume) and basic sensitivity analyses to increase confidence in real-world applicability.

Overall assessment
This is a solid paper that offers a well-justified and effective approach to learning the structure of a particular class of grammars. The methodological contribution is clear, and the results are promising. The main limitation is the scope of the empirical evaluation and the tendency to overstate generality. With a more balanced positioning and stronger experimental comparisons—particularly to Bayesian model merging—the paper would make a compelling contribution. As it stands, it remains a worthwhile and credible piece of work, especially if the authors can include the suggested baseline and analyses.
