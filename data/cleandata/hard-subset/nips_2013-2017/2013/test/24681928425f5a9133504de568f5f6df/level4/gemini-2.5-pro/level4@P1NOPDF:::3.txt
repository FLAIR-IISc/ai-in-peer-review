This paper proposes a novel approach for the unsupervised structure learning of AND-OR grammars. In contrast to prior work that searches for AND and OR operations separately, the proposed method induces unified AND-OR fragments. A key contribution is an efficient method for computing the value of a proposed fragment using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on tasks involving the parsing of events and visual objects.

Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. The algorithm appears to be technically sound, although I was not able to verify the derivations in the supplementary material. I have a few questions that I hope the authors can clarify:

1.  Is the "surrogate measure" for the likelihood gain an approximation, or is it an exact calculation? The paper should be more explicit on this point.
2.  Can the proposed method be extended to learn fragments with an arity greater than two? Or is it fundamentally limited to learning grammars in Chomsky normal form?

My primary concern with the paper lies in the experimental evaluation. I found it difficult to understand several key aspects of the experiments, including the precise methodology, the representation of the data, and the nature of the learned grammars. The experimental section appears to assume a high degree of familiarity with a specific body of prior work, particularly from S. C. Zhu's group, making it challenging for a broader audience to follow the evaluation and interpret its significance. While I understand the space constraints of the NIPS format, the authors could significantly improve the paper's accessibility by providing a more detailed supplementary document. If the paper is accepted, I strongly urge the authors to include more details and concrete illustrations of their experimental setup.

Furthermore, the paper would be significantly more compelling if it included qualitative results. The introduction provides a strong motivation for learning grammatical structure, but the evaluation focuses exclusively on quantitative metrics. While these metrics are satisfactory on their own terms, they do not provide much insight into the structures being learned. Showcasing examples of interesting grammatical rules discovered by the model would be highly illuminating. A qualitative comparison demonstrating that this approach learns more meaningful or interpretable structures than competing methods would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution.

In summary, this is an interesting paper with a promising technical approach. However, its contribution is diminished by an opaque experimental section and a lack of qualitative results. I would be more enthusiastic about the paper if the authors were to provide a more detailed explanation of their experiments and demonstrate the kinds of interesting structures their method is capable of learning.
