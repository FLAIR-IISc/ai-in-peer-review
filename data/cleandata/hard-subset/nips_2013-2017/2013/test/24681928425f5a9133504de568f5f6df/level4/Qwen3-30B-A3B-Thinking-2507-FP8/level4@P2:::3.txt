

This paper presents a unified framework for unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, which can model diverse data types including images and events. The authors extend previous work on structure learning by introducing a novel approach that simultaneously learns grammar structure and parameters through iterative insertion of unified And-Or fragments, rather than handling composition (And-rules) and reconfiguration (Or-rules) separately. The method optimizes the posterior probability of the grammar, with likelihood gains computed efficiently using sufficient statistics derived from training data. The paper evaluates the approach on event grammars (from human activity videos) and image grammars (from synthetic and real datasets), demonstrating competitive or superior performance compared to prior methods.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a data-agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars, (2) a unified learning framework that explicitly optimizes both structure and parameters, and (3) empirical validation showing the approach's effectiveness across multiple domains.

Strengths:
- The problem is important and well-motivated, as grammars provide a powerful framework for modeling compositionality and reconfigurability in diverse data types. The authors effectively connect their work to prior NIPS contributions on stochastic grammars for images [1,3] and events [7].
- The approach is technically sound and novel. The unified And-Or fragment method addresses a key limitation of prior work that handles And and Or operations separately, particularly in data-scarce scenarios. The efficient computation of likelihood gains using sufficient statistics is a significant technical contribution.
- The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with strong quantitative results on both event and image grammars. The comparison with previous methods [17,18] is well-justified and demonstrates the approach's effectiveness.
- The paper is generally well-written with clear explanations of the grammar formalism and learning framework, making it accessible to the NIPS audience.

Weaknesses:
- The paper would benefit significantly from concrete examples of the grammars actually learned. While the results are quantitatively satisfactory, the experimental section lacks illustrative examples of the grammatical structures discovered, which would better demonstrate the approach's value and distinguish it from competing methods.
- Some technical details require clarification. The "surrogate measure" of likelihood gain needs explicit specification of whether it's an exact calculation or approximation. The paper also doesn't fully address whether the approach can handle fragments with arity greater than 2, which would impact its applicability to more complex grammatical structures beyond Chomsky normal form.
- The experimental section assumes familiarity with Zhu's group's prior work, making it challenging for readers outside this specific research community to fully appreciate the contributions. More detailed explanations of the data representations and learning procedures would improve accessibility.

Quality: The technical approach is sound, with a well-motivated optimization procedure. The efficient computation of likelihood gains using sufficient statistics is particularly strong. The experimental results support the claims, though the lack of illustrative examples of learned grammars slightly limits the depth of the evaluation.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear, but the experimental section would benefit from more detailed explanations of the data representations and learning procedures. The connection to prior NIPS work could be strengthened by more explicit contrast with specific methods from recent proceedings.

Originality: The unified approach to learning And-Or fragments represents a meaningful advance over previous methods that handle composition and reconfiguration separately. The data-agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars is also novel and valuable.

Significance: The problem of unsupervised grammar learning is important for modeling structured data, and the authors' approach offers a promising solution with demonstrated effectiveness across multiple domains. The significance would be enhanced with more concrete examples of the learned grammatical structures and their interpretability.

Recommendation: I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of grammar-based modeling, with a novel approach that addresses a meaningful problem. The main improvements needed are to provide more illustrative examples of the learned grammars and clarify the technical points about the likelihood gain calculation and grammar complexity.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel unified approach to grammar structure learning
- Strong empirical results across multiple domains
- Theoretical foundation with efficient computation
- Clear problem statement and motivation

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of illustrative examples of learned grammars
- Some technical details unclear (surrogate measure, arity >2)
- Experimental section assumes familiarity with prior Zhu group work

This is a high-quality paper with significant contributions to the field. With minor revisions to address the clarity concerns and provide more concrete examples of the learned grammars, it would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
