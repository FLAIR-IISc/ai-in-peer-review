- Proposes a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters.  
- Addresses the challenge of manual grammar specification by inducing unified And-Or fragments that cover multiple compositions.  
- Differs from prior work by jointly learning composition and reconfiguration, avoiding separate And-/Or-fragment learning limitations.  
- Extends the authors' prior structure-learning work and overcomes limitations of earlier natural language grammar learning methods.  
- Provides a data-type-agnostic formal definition of stochastic And-Or grammars, giving a clear theoretical foundation.  
- Presents an efficient algorithm that computes posterior gain of candidate fragments using sufficient statistics from training data.  
- Theoretical development of likelihood and prior gains is well-justified; claims ability to construct any context-free And-Or grammar via fragments.  
- Empirically strong: competitive or superior results on event and image grammars, especially on a challenging synthetic event dataset.  
- Major clarity question: whether the "surrogate measure" of likelihood gain is exact or an approximation, affecting theoretical guarantees.  
- Unclear if the method extends to fragments of arity >2 or is limited to Chomsky-normal-form–like grammars; authors should clarify.  
- Experimental presentation assumes deep familiarity with the Zhu group's frameworks, making setup hard to follow for a broad audience.  
- Lacks concrete examples or visualizations of learned grammars and insufficient detail on how "co-occurring" and "followed-by" relations were defined/implemented.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel, and significant; recommend acceptance contingent on adding concrete learned-structure examples (e.g., in supplementary material).
