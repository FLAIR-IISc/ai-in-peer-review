This paper proposes a novel approach to unsupervised learning of the structure of AND-OR grammars, differing from previous methods by inducing unified AND-OR fragments rather than searching for AND and OR operations separately. The value of a proposed new AND-OR fragment can be computed efficiently using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on several tasks, including parsing events and visual objects.

The paper tackles an important problem, presents a well-motivated approach, and appears to be technically sound. However, I was unable to verify the supplemental derivations due to time constraints. A few aspects of the paper were unclear, which may be attributed to my limited reading time, but I would recommend that the authors clarify these points: Firstly, it was not evident whether the "surrogate measure" of the likelihood gain was an approximate or exact notion. Secondly, it was unclear whether the approach could be extended to learning fragments with arity greater than 2 or if it was limited to tractably learning grammars in "Chomsky normal form".

My primary concerns lie with the experimental section. I had difficulty understanding the methodology, representation of examples, and the learned outcomes. The paper seems to be geared towards an audience familiar with recent visual-grammar papers from S. C. Zhu's group. While the experimental section may be clear to such readers, it may be less accessible to outsiders. Given the constraints of the NIPS format, I suggest that the authors include supplementary materials to provide more details and concrete illustrations of their experiments if the paper is accepted.

I would have appreciated seeing examples of interesting structures discovered in the learned grammars. The paper effectively sets up the importance of grammars and structure learning, but the purely quantitative presentation of results is somewhat disappointing. Although the authors' quantitative results are satisfactory in their own right, they lack excitement and insight. The paper would have been more compelling if it demonstrated the learning of interesting structures and compared these to competing approaches.

In conclusion, this is an interesting paper on unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. While I found it engaging, I had trouble following the experiments and interpreting the results due to my unfamiliarity with prior work, primarily from Zhu's UCLA group. If accepted, the authors should provide supplementary materials with more details and illustrations of the experimental methodology and the interesting structures learned.
