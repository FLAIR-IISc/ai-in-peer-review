This paper presents an unsupervised method for learning the structure of stochastic And-Or grammars. Unlike prior work that searches for And- and Or-structure separately, the proposed method induces unified And-Or fragments and evaluates their contribution efficiently via sufficient statistics. The framework is instantiated on multiple domains, including event parsing and image/object parsing.

Overall, this is a solid submission. The problem is important, and the proposed approach is original and well motivated. The method appears technically reasonable, though I was unable to verify the supplementary derivations. A few conceptual points could be clarified: (1) whether the “surrogate” objective for likelihood gain is an exact formulation or an approximation; and (2) whether the approach supports learning higher-arity fragments in practice, or if tractability effectively limits it to a Chomsky-normal-form-style setting.

My main reservations concern the experimental section. I found it difficult to follow exactly what was done—how inputs were represented, how the proposed fragments mapped to data, and what, concretely, was learned. The presentation seems tuned to readers deeply familiar with earlier visual-grammar work (much of it from S. C. Zhu’s group); such readers will likely find the setup and comparisons straightforward, but it is less accessible to broader NeurIPS audiences. The venue allows supplementary material, and if accepted, the paper would benefit from a much more detailed supplement including step-by-step descriptions and concrete examples for each experiment.

I would particularly like to see qualitative illustrations of non-trivial structure discovered by the learned grammars. The motivation emphasizes structural modeling, but the results are almost entirely quantitative. While the reported metrics are credible and satisfactory, they are not especially illuminating. The paper would be stronger if it showcased interesting induced structures and demonstrated that this approach recovers richer or more faithful structure than competing methods.

In sum, this is a noteworthy contribution on unsupervised induction of And-Or grammars. I liked the core idea and framing but struggled with the experimental narrative and interpretation without a deep prior investment in the related literature. If accepted, the authors should provide a comprehensive supplement with richer explanations and visualizations of the experimental procedures and the structures learned.
