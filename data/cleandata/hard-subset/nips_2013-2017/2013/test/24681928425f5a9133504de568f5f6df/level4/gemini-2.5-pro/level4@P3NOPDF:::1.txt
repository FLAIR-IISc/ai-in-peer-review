This paper introduces an algorithm for learning the structure of stochastic And-Or grammars from data. The core contribution is a sensible and well-motivated approach that demonstrates promising results on the tasks presented. The decision to focus on a particular class of grammars, which makes the learning problem tractable, is a pragmatic and well-justified choice that leads to a clear and focused contribution. Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a valuable method for grammar induction.

However, there are two main areas where the paper could be significantly improved: the positioning of the contribution and the scope of the experimental evaluation.

First, the paper's framing of the work as a "generalization" of previous structure learning methods may be misleading. It appears the proposed method addresses a specialized, albeit important, subclass of the broader grammar induction problem. This specialization is precisely what makes the problem tractable and is, in fact, a strength of the work. I would recommend the authors revise this claim to more accurately position their contribution. Highlighting the method as an effective approach for a specific, well-defined class of grammars, rather than a general solution to the structure learning problem faced in fields like NLP, would be more precise and would not detract from the paper's merits.

Second, the experimental evaluation, while demonstrating the method's promise, could be strengthened by including additional comparisons. The set of baseline methods is somewhat limited. A particularly relevant and seemingly absent comparison is the state-merging method proposed by Stolcke and Omohondro (1994, reference [14]). This is a classic and powerful baseline for grammar induction that appears applicable to the tasks presented in the paper. Its inclusion would provide a more comprehensive and valuable benchmark for assessing the proposed algorithm's performance and would make the empirical claims of the paper more convincing.

In summary, this is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed algorithm is well-founded, and the results are promising. The work would be strengthened by refining its claims regarding generalization and by expanding the experimental evaluation to include stronger, more established baselines. Despite these points for improvement, the paper represents a clear and noteworthy advance.
