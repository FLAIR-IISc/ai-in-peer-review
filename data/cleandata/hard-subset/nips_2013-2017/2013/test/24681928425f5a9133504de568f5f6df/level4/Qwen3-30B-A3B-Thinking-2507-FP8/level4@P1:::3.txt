

This paper introduces a novel unsupervised approach for learning the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars, with a focus on unifying the induction of compositional (And) and reconfigurable (Or) fragments. The core contribution lies in optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar through iterative insertion of And-Or fragments—unified units that simultaneously model both composition and reconfiguration—rather than handling them separately as in prior work. The authors derive efficient sufficient statistics to compute the posterior gain of candidate fragments, enabling scalable learning. Empirical validation on event and image grammars demonstrates competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods.

**Strengths:**  
The paper addresses a significant gap in grammar learning literature by unifying structure and parameter optimization within a single framework. The formalization of And-Or grammars as data-agnostic models is well-motivated and enables cross-domain applicability (e.g., events, images). The technical approach is sound: the posterior optimization strategy avoids heuristic dependencies, and the derivation of sufficient statistics for efficient gain computation is a notable contribution. The experimental scope is appropriate, covering two distinct domains (event parsing and image grammar learning) with both synthetic and real datasets. Results consistently outperform prior methods on quantitative metrics (e.g., F-measure for event parsing, perplexity for image grammars), and the ablation study on the three method variants (ID sequences vs. direct vector application) effectively demonstrates the advantage of grounding grammars to atomic patterns.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**  
1. **Clarity of Key Technical Claims:** The paper asserts that the "surrogate measure" for likelihood gain is derived from coherence measures of n-gram tensors and context matrices (Section 3.3). However, it remains unclear whether this factorization provides an *exact* or *approximate* measure of the posterior gain. The authors should explicitly clarify this—e.g., by stating whether the factorization is mathematically exact under the unambiguity assumption or serves as a practical proxy. This ambiguity affects confidence in the optimization’s theoretical guarantees.  

2. **Scope Limitations:** The method’s tractability for fragments beyond binary (arity-2) relations is not addressed. The paper implies learning in "Chomsky normal form" (e.g., Figure 2(a) shows ternary And-rules), but it is unclear whether the approach can efficiently handle higher-arity compositions or if this constraint is inherent to the sufficient statistics derivation. A brief discussion of this limitation would strengthen the contribution.  

3. **Experimental Transparency:** While the quantitative results are convincing, the experimental details lack sufficient detail for reproducibility or broader interpretation. For instance:  
   - The event grammar experiments (Section 4.1) rely on atomic action annotations but omit concrete examples of the *learned grammars* (e.g., a visualization of a fragment capturing "sitting → [subtypes A/B/C] + [co-occurring drinking]"). The paper’s strong motivation for structural learning is undermined by the absence of such illustrations.  
   - The image grammar section (Section 4.2) uses quantized images (Figure 8), but the relation types (e.g., "relative positions") are not concretely defined. How do these relations differ from those in prior work (e.g., [17])? Providing one example of a learned relation (e.g., "patch X is above patch Y with offset 0.2") would clarify the model’s expressiveness.  
   These gaps make the results feel "quantitatively solid but conceptually thin" for readers outside Zhu’s research group. The supplementary material should include such visualizations and concrete relation examples.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a strong technical contribution with clear novelty and solid empirical validation. The unified And-Or fragment approach is a meaningful advancement over prior separable induction methods, and the computational efficiency via sufficient statistics is well-justified. The paper’s main weakness is insufficient detail in the experimental presentation, which limits accessibility for readers unfamiliar with the Zhu group’s prior work. However, this is a solvable issue via supplementary materials.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept**. The paper makes a significant, well-executed contribution to unsupervised structure learning for stochastic grammars. The technical merits outweigh the experimental transparency concerns, which can be addressed through minor revisions. The authors should include in the supplement:  
1. Visual examples of learned grammars (e.g., event grammar fragments, image relation patterns),  
2. Clarification on the exactness of the likelihood gain factorization,  
3. Discussion of higher-arity fragment feasibility.  
These additions would substantially strengthen the paper’s impact and accessibility.
