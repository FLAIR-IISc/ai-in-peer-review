- Proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of stochastic context-free And-Or grammars by iteratively inducing And-Or fragments.
- Key idea: induce fragments (one And-node + several Or-nodes) to capture compositionality and reconfigurability, using a MAP objective with a sparsity prior and Viterbi (best-parse) likelihood.
- Derives a closed-form posterior-gain for fragment addition under an unambiguity assumption, computable from an n-gram tensor and a context matrix for efficient greedy/beam search.
- Evaluated on event (human activity) and image (object parts) grammars, showing comparable or improved performance over representative And-Or learners.
- Positions work between NLP grammar induction and vision/SPN structure learning, differing by searching structures within a restricted And-Or class and using sufficient-statistics-based posterior gain.
- Strength: unified, data-agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars with concrete relational instantiations for images and events.
- Strength: principled MAP objective and reasonable Viterbi approximation common in grammar induction.
- Strength: elegant sufficient-statistics derivation (n-gram tensors and context matrices) that improves search efficiency and intuition via coherence measures.
- Strength: unified And-Or fragment induction mitigates data sparsity and redundancy from separate And/Or learning.
- Strength: cross-domain demonstrations show solid improvements over existing And-Or learners.
- Weakness: limited baseline comparisons—missing Bayesian Model Merging, CCM, HDP-PCFGs, and relevant SPN structure learners.
- Weakness: heavy reliance on unambiguity assumption and Viterbi approximation without analysis of when they hold or sensitivity to violations.
- Weakness: event experiments use annotated atomic actions/boundaries, making the setup closer to semi-supervised learning and weakening the “agnostic” claim.
- Weakness: missing critical ablations (And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or fragments; beam width/greedy choices; α and coding choices; relation templates and n).
- Weakness: under-specified implementation details (context definitions, complexity/memory of n-gram tensors, smoothing for perplexity, stopping criteria).
- Weakness: ad hoc modification for multi-event videos (converting top-level And to Or) rather than a principled multi-instance model.
- Quality: technically sound with compelling derivation, but lacks theoretical guarantees and empirical validation of key design choices.
- Clarity: generally well written, but needs tighter definitions/references for grammar size/coding constants, contexts, complexity, smoothing, and train/test details.
- Originality: novel unified And-Or fragment search and sufficient-statistics perspective beyond prior heuristic learners.
- Significance: potentially impactful for compositional modeling across modalities if broader validation is provided.
- Suggested improvements: add strong baselines (BMM, CCM, HDP-PCFG, SPN), perform ablations, report runtime/memory and complexity, clarify context/templates, test robustness to noisy primitives/quantization, and consider a small text experiment.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled and efficient structure-learning method, unified composition/reconfiguration, cross-domain applicability, and promising empirical results.
- Arguments against acceptance: omitted strong baselines, untested core assumptions, limited ablations and sensitivity analyses, and ad hoc experimental steps.
- Overall: solid, thoughtful contribution recommended for acceptance if authors broaden comparisons and add targeted ablations/clarifications; otherwise a strong borderline paper.
