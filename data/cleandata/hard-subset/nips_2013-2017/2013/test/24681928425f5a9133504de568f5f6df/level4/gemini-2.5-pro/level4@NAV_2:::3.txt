This paper presents an approach for the unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or grammar structures. The authors introduce a unified formalization of these grammars, designed to be agnostic to the data type being modeled. The core contribution is a learning algorithm that, starting from a trivial grammar, iteratively induces "And-Or fragments" to optimize the grammar's posterior probability. This method unifies the search for compositional structures (And-rules) and reconfigurable alternatives (Or-rules), which previous work often handled separately. The value of adding a new fragment is calculated efficiently using a set of derived sufficient statistics. The proposed approach is evaluated on the tasks of learning event grammars from video data and image grammars from both synthetic and real image datasets.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of unsupervised grammar induction. The central idea of inducing unified And-Or fragments is novel and well-motivated. It provides an elegant solution to potential issues in prior work, such as data scarcity or error propagation that can arise when learning compositional and alternative structures in separate steps.
*   **Quality:** The learning framework appears technically sound. The optimization of the grammar's posterior probability provides a principled objective function. A key technical strength is the efficient computation of the posterior gain for candidate fragments, which is formulated based on sufficient statistics derived from the training data. The unified, data-agnostic grammar formalization is also a valuable contribution that increases the potential applicability of the work.
*   **Performance:** The quantitative results on both event and image grammar learning tasks are strong, showing performance that is comparable or superior to previous approaches.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity of the Method:** The technical presentation is mostly clear, but a couple of points could be clarified.
    1.  It is not explicitly stated whether the likelihood gain calculation, described as a "surrogate measure" involving tensor coherence, is an exact formulation under the Viterbi assumption or an approximation.
    2.  The paper does not discuss limitations on the arity of the learned And-rules. It is unclear if the approach can tractably learn rules with more than two children or if it is effectively restricted to a form analogous to Chomsky normal form.
*   **Clarity of Experiments:** My main concern is the lack of detail in the experimental section. For a reader not intimately familiar with the specific body of prior work cited (e.g., [17, 18, 23]), it is difficult to fully understand the experimental setup, how data was represented, and precisely what was learned. The description would benefit from more self-contained explanations of the preprocessing steps and methodologies. Given the page limits, a detailed supplementary document would be an excellent way to address this.
*   **Evaluation and Significance:** While the quantitative results are satisfactory, the evaluation is a missed opportunity to showcase the paper's main strength: structure learning. The paper is motivated by the importance of learning compositional and reconfigurable structures, but the results are presented purely through aggregate metrics like F-measure and perplexity. The paper would be far more compelling and illuminating if it included qualitative examples of the *interesting structures* discovered by the learned grammars for both the event and image tasks. Showing that the method learns interpretable and meaningful grammar rules would provide much stronger evidence of its success and would better demonstrate its significance beyond incremental performance gains.

In summary, this is a good paper with a novel, well-motivated, and technically sound approach to an important problem. The primary weaknesses are the lack of clarity in the experimental description and an evaluation that relies solely on quantitative metrics without providing qualitative insight into the learned structures. Should the paper be accepted, I strongly urge the authors to include a supplementary appendix with more experimental details and, most importantly, concrete examples and illustrations of the learned grammar structures.
