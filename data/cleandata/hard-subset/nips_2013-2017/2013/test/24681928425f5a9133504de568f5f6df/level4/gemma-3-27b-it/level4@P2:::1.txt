This paper introduces a method for unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, addressing both structural and parameter learning. The authors frame their work as a generalization of existing structure learning techniques, arguing that focusing on And-Or grammars allows for a tractable problem formulation. While the claim of generalization may be overstated – the proposed method addresses a specific instance of structure learning rather than a broader class – the approach represents a valuable contribution to the field. The core algorithm, iteratively inducing compositions and reconfigurations via posterior probability optimization, appears sound and demonstrates competitive performance against existing grammar induction methods.

The paper effectively builds upon prior work in stochastic grammars, citing relevant contributions from natural language processing (e.g., [8, 9]) and computer vision [1, 3, 7]. It correctly identifies the challenge of manually specifying grammar structures and proposes a data-driven approach to overcome this limitation. The novel contribution of unifying the search for compositions and reconfigurations through “And-Or fragments” is clearly articulated and appears to be a key aspect of the algorithm's success. The theoretical justification, leveraging sufficient statistics and a sparsity prior, is also well-presented.

However, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened. While the results on event and image grammars are promising, the comparison to related work feels somewhat limited. Specifically, the approach of Stolcke and Omohundro [14], a foundational work in unsupervised grammar induction, would provide a valuable benchmark. Applying this method (or others from the NLP literature) to both the event and image grammar tasks would further demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach and contextualize its contributions. Additionally, a more detailed ablation study exploring the impact of different design choices (e.g., the choice of the sparsity parameter α) would be beneficial.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly defines the problem of unsupervised structure learning for stochastic And-Or grammars.
*   **Novel Approach:** The introduction of And-Or fragments for unified composition and reconfiguration learning is a significant contribution.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The use of posterior probability optimization and sufficient statistics provides a solid theoretical foundation.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the competitiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to related work could be more comprehensive, particularly regarding established methods from the NLP literature (e.g., Stolcke & Omohundro [14]).
*   **Overstated Generalization:** The claim that the method “generalizes” previous work should be toned down; it addresses a specific instance of the broader structure learning problem.
*   **Lack of Ablation Study:** Further investigation into the sensitivity of the method to parameter choices would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to an important problem.
*   The theoretical foundation and experimental results are compelling.
*   The work has the potential to advance the field of probabilistic modeling and structure learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The limited comparison to related work weakens the claim of superiority.
*   The overstated generalization claim could be misleading.
*   The lack of an ablation study leaves some questions unanswered.

**Overall:**

This is a solid paper presenting a method for learning the structure of a particular class of grammars. Despite some minor weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of the work justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns regarding the comparison to related work and the claim of generalization.
