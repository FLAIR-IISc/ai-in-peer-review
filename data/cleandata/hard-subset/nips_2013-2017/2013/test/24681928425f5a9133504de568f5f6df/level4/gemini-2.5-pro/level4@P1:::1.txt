This paper presents an unsupervised method for learning the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars from unannotated data. The core idea is to begin with a trivial grammar that explicitly represents each training sample and then iteratively generalize it by introducing new "And-Or fragments" in a bottom-up fashion. This search process is guided by an objective to maximize the posterior probability of the grammar, which effectively balances data likelihood with a complexity penalty (a prior favoring smaller grammars). The authors demonstrate the versatility of their data-agnostic framework by applying it successfully to two different domains: learning event grammars from videos and image grammars from visual data.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper's primary contribution is a novel and principled approach to grammar structure learning. The introduction of "And-Or fragments" as the fundamental unit for search is a clever and effective strategy. It unifies the discovery of compositional structures (And-nodes) and alternative reconfigurations (Or-nodes), addressing a key challenge in grammar induction where these two aspects are often handled separately.
*   The algorithm is well-motivated by a clear Bayesian objective function—optimizing the grammar's posterior probability. The derivation of the posterior gain, which can be computed efficiently from sufficient statistics, makes the search process practical.
*   The empirical evaluation is solid, demonstrating that the proposed method achieves comparable or superior performance to previous, more specialized approaches on two distinct and challenging tasks. The consistent outperformance on the image grammar tasks is particularly convincing.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The paper's positioning could be refined. The authors suggest their approach "generalizes" previous work on structure learning. However, the core contribution could also be framed as a well-chosen and powerful *specialization* of the grammar induction problem. By restricting the search to discovering complete And-Or fragments, the method makes the otherwise intractable problem of structure learning computationally feasible and robust. This is a significant strength in its own right, and the paper would be stronger if it were framed as such. I would suggest the authors consider toning down claims of generality relative to the broader structure learning problems faced in NLP and instead emphasize how this structured search is a key contribution for enabling induction in these complex visual and event domains.
*   The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including more comparisons to other relevant methods. In particular, given the method's connection to classic grammar induction techniques, a comparison to the well-known Bayesian model merging approach of Stolcke and Omohundro (reference [14]) would be particularly insightful. Their method is conceptually related and appears applicable to the discrete sequence representations used in the event grammar experiments. This would provide a valuable comparison point from the computational linguistics literature.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a solid paper that presents a sensible and effective algorithm for learning the structure of a particular, yet powerful, class of grammars. The technical approach is sound, the core ideas are original, and the experimental results are convincing. The work is significant as it provides a unified framework that performs well in multiple domains. With minor revisions to the paper's positioning and potentially the inclusion of a key baseline from the grammar induction literature, this will be a strong contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
