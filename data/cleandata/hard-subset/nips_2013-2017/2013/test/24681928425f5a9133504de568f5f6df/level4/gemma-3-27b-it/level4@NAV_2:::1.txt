This paper presents a method for learning the structure of stochastic And-Or grammars. The authors argue that their approach provides a unified formalization applicable to various data types, and propose an unsupervised learning method optimizing both grammar structure and parameters. This work builds upon previous research in structure learning, notably extending approaches from natural language processing [14, 15, 16] and image grammars [17, 18]. The core idea of iteratively inducing compositions and reconfigurations via And-Or fragments is well-motivated and addresses limitations of prior methods that learn these components separately.

The strengths of the paper lie in its principled approach to structure learning, explicitly optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar. The introduction of And-Or fragments represents a novel contribution, aiming to improve efficiency and robustness compared to learning compositions and reconfigurations independently. The empirical evaluation demonstrates competitive or superior performance on event and image grammar learning tasks. The clarity of the presentation is generally good, with sufficient detail provided to understand the core concepts and algorithm.

However, the paper could be strengthened by a more comprehensive comparison to related work. While the authors acknowledge prior efforts, the inclusion of methods like Stolcke and Omohondro [14] would offer valuable context and demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach more convincingly. Additionally, while the authors claim generality across data types, the experiments focus on event and image grammars. Expanding the empirical evaluation to include, for example, a natural language processing task, would further validate this claim. While the discussion of the likelihood gain formulation and its connection to tensor/matrix coherence measures is interesting, it could benefit from more intuitive explanation for readers less familiar with these concepts. 

Arguments for acceptance:
* Novel and principled approach to unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars.
* Well-motivated use of And-Or fragments to unify composition and reconfiguration learning.
* Competitive empirical performance on event and image grammar learning tasks.
* Clear and generally well-written presentation.

Arguments against acceptance:
* Limited comparison to some relevant prior work (e.g., [14]).
* Empirical evaluation primarily focuses on event and image grammars, potentially limiting the demonstrated generality.
* The detailed mathematical formulation of the likelihood gain could be more accessible.

Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a valuable contribution to the field of probabilistic grammar learning. While some improvements to the experimental evaluation and presentation would be beneficial, the technical soundness, originality, and significance of the work support acceptance.
