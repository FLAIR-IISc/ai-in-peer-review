This paper presents an unsupervised method for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs). Unlike prior approaches that search separately for And- and Or-rules, the authors induce joint And-Or fragments, thereby modeling composition and reconfiguration in a unified step. Candidate fragments are scored via an efficient computation based on sufficient statistics and a Viterbi likelihood objective with a sparsity prior on grammar size. The method is evaluated on event parsing and image grammar learning, where it reports competitive or better quantitative results than previous work.

Quality: The approach is well-motivated and appears technically sound. I was not able to check the supplemental derivations, so I cannot fully verify the likelihood gain formulation. Two points require clarification: (1) Is the “surrogate measure” for likelihood gain exact under the stated assumptions (e.g., grammar unambiguity, Viterbi training), or is it an approximation? Please state this explicitly and delineate any assumptions. (2) Can the search induce fragments with arity greater than two, or is it effectively restricted to Chomsky-normal productions for tractability? A brief discussion of computational trade-offs for higher arity would help.

Clarity: The experimental section is difficult to follow without deep familiarity with the prior AOG literature (particularly pipelines from Zhu’s group). It was often unclear how examples were represented, which relations were allowed in And-rules, and precisely what structures were learned. For a broader NIPS audience, the paper would benefit from a more self-contained description of the data preprocessing, terminal/relational choices, and the learning/search setup (e.g., beam widths, stopping criteria, and hyperparameters). The paper would also be stronger with qualitative illustrations of learned fragments and parse trees, highlighting interpretable structure discovered by the method.

Originality: Joint induction of And-Or fragments, with an efficient sufficient-statistics-based scoring, is a notable contribution relative to prior AOG structure learning that treats And- and Or-expansions separately. The unified treatment plausibly addresses data scarcity and redundancy issues observed in earlier approaches.

Significance: If broadly applicable, the method could provide a practical route to inducing compact, reusable grammars for images and events. The quantitative results are encouraging, but the impact would be clearer with qualitative evidence of what structure is recovered and how it differs from structures learned by competing methods.

Suggestions for improvement (especially for the supplementary material if accepted):
- Clarify whether the likelihood gain factorization is exact or approximate, enumerate assumptions (e.g., unambiguity), and discuss sensitivity if they are violated.
- Specify supported arities and whether non-binary compositions are handled directly or via binarization; report any empirical effects on speed and quality.
- Provide a self-contained experimental protocol: how terminals are defined, what relations are permitted in And-rules for each domain, how contexts are constructed, and how smoothing is performed for perplexity.
- Report search details (candidate generation, beam size, random restarts), stopping criteria, and runtime.
- Include qualitative examples: learned And-Or fragments, example parses, and side-by-side comparisons with baselines to demonstrate more informative or compact structure.
- Where possible, include ablations (e.g., And-only, Or-only, or separate induction) to substantiate the benefits of joint fragments.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important problem (unsupervised structure learning for expressive grammars).
- Novel unified fragment induction with an efficient scoring scheme.
- Competitive or superior quantitative performance on both image and event domains.
- Potential for broad applicability across modalities.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Experimental section is hard to reproduce and to interpret without substantial prior familiarity; presentation limits accessibility.
- Lack of qualitative analysis makes it difficult to assess whether the learned structures are genuinely more informative or compact.
- Key technical points (exactness of the likelihood gain, support for higher-arity productions) are not clearly stated.

Overall: This is a solid and promising paper on unsupervised AOG learning. I found it interesting, but had difficulty following the experiments and interpreting the results without substantial background. If accepted, please include a more self-contained supplement with detailed protocols and qualitative illustrations of the learned structures.
