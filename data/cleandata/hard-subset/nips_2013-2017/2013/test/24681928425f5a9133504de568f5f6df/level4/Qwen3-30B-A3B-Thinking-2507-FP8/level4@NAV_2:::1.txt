

This paper introduces a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars applicable across data types (text, images, events) and proposes an unsupervised structure-learning approach that optimizes grammar posterior probability through iterative And-Or fragment induction. The work builds upon prior NIPS contributions in grammatical inference (e.g., Stolcke and Omohundro, 1994; Si et al., 2011) while addressing limitations in existing image grammar learning (Si and Zhu, 2013; Si et al., 2011). The key innovation lies in unifying composition and reconfiguration learning via And-Or fragments, avoiding the error-prone separate handling of And-rules and Or-rules in previous methods.  

Strengths:  
- **Originality**: The formalization is genuinely data-type agnostic (Table 1), enabling cross-domain grammar learning—a significant advance over domain-specific prior work (Zhu and Mumford, 2006; Zhao and Zhu, 2011).  
- **Quality**: The posterior optimization framework is theoretically sound, with efficient sufficient statistics derivation (Section 3.3). Empirical results on event grammars (Table 2) and image grammars (Table 3) demonstrate clear superiority over competing approaches (SZ, 2013; Si et al., 2011), with robust performance even on ambiguous data (e.g., subtype-divided actions in Dataset 2).  
- **Significance**: The method enables unsupervised grammar learning for previously intractable domains (e.g., event parsing without manual annotation), with measurable gains in purity/F-measure and perplexity.  

Weaknesses:  
- **Experimental scope**: The comparison to Stolcke and Omohundro (1994), a foundational grammar structure-learning method applicable to both event and image domains, is missing. This would provide a more rigorous baseline than the current limited comparisons (e.g., only ID-sequence methods in Table 2).  
- **Generality claim**: While the formalization is agnostic to atomic patterns, the algorithm’s efficiency relies on restricting grammar fragment types (Section 3.3), which somewhat limits its applicability to highly complex grammars.  

Pro: Unified learning of compositions/reconfigurations avoids prior fragmentation issues; strong empirical validation across two domains.  
Con: Missing comparison to a key prior grammar structure-learning method (Stolcke and Omohundro, 1994); generality constrained by fragment-type restrictions.  

The paper makes a well-executed contribution to grammatical inference, with clear technical advances over recent NIPS work (e.g., Si et al., 2011). Adding the Stolcke and Omohundro comparison would significantly strengthen the evaluation. Overall, the method is technically sound, well-motivated, and demonstrates meaningful progress for the field.
