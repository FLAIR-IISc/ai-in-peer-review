This paper introduces a novel approach to unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammar structure. Unlike prior work that separately induces AND and OR fragments, this method learns unified fragments, enabling efficient computation of their value using sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on both event parsing and visual object recognition tasks.

Overall, this is a strong paper addressing an important problem with a well-motivated and technically sound contribution. While the algorithm’s technical correctness appears convincing (though a full verification of the supplemental derivations was beyond the scope of this review), a few points require clarification. First, the paper would benefit from explicitly stating whether the “surrogate measure” for likelihood gain represents an approximation or an exact calculation. Second, it remains unclear if the method extends to fragments with arity greater than two, or if it is inherently limited to learning grammars in Chomsky Normal Form. Addressing these points would significantly improve clarity.

My primary concerns relate to the experimental evaluation. I found it difficult to fully grasp the experimental methodology, data representation, and the precise nature of what was learned. The experiments appear to assume a high degree of familiarity with a specific body of recent work – particularly visual grammar research from S.C. Zhu’s group – to the extent that a thorough understanding of the paper's contributions requires intimate knowledge of that prior work, ideally including reimplementation experience. While the NIPS format understandably constrains detail, the supplementary materials offer an opportunity to address this. Should the paper be accepted, I strongly encourage the authors to substantially expand the supplement with detailed explanations and concrete illustrations of the experimental setup and results.

Specifically, the paper would be greatly strengthened by the inclusion of examples showcasing the learned grammar structure. While the paper effectively establishes the importance of grammar learning, the exclusive reliance on quantitative results feels somewhat underwhelming. The quantitative results are demonstrably satisfactory according to the authors’ evaluation, but they lack intuitive appeal. Demonstrating that the approach learns *interesting* structure, and that this structure is demonstrably superior to that learned by competing methods, would make the paper substantially more compelling. 

In summary, this is an interesting and promising paper on unsupervised AND-OR grammar learning. However, my ability to fully appreciate the experimental results and contributions was hampered by a reliance on specialized prior knowledge. Expanding the supplementary material to provide greater experimental detail and illustrative examples of the learned structures would significantly enhance the paper’s impact and accessibility.
