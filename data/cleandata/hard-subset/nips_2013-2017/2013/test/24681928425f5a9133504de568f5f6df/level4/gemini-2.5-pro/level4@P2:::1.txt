This paper presents an unsupervised method for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars from unannotated data. The core idea is to start with a trivial grammar that explicitly represents each training sample and then iteratively generalize it by introducing new grammar fragments. The objective is to maximize the posterior probability of the grammar, which combines the data likelihood with a sparsity prior that penalizes grammar size.

The main contribution is the introduction of "And-Or fragments" as the unit of structure learning. An And-Or fragment jointly introduces a new compositional rule (an And-rule) and a set of alternative sub-configurations (Or-rules). The authors argue that this unified approach is more robust to data scarcity and ambiguity than prior methods that learn compositional and alternative structures separately. The framework is formalized in a data-agnostic way, and its effectiveness is demonstrated on two distinct tasks: learning event grammars from video activity data and learning image grammars from collections of faces. In both cases, the proposed method achieves performance comparable or superior to specialized previous approaches.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Principled Learning Framework:** The central idea of iteratively inducing "And-Or fragments" to optimize the grammar's posterior probability is elegant and well-motivated. Unifying the search for compositional (And) and reconfigurable (Or) structures is a significant conceptual contribution that directly addresses practical challenges in grammar induction, such as data scarcity.

2.  **Efficient Optimization:** The paper provides a clear and technically sound derivation for calculating the gain in posterior probability when adding a new fragment. Formulating this gain in terms of sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) allows for an efficient search over candidate fragments, which is crucial for the algorithm's practicality.

3.  **Generality and Broad Applicability:** The authors present a unified formalization of And-Or grammars that is not tied to a specific data modality. This is a notable strength, and the successful application of the *same* learning algorithm to both temporal event sequences and spatial image data convincingly demonstrates the framework's flexibility and potential for broad impact.

4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is solid. The method is shown to outperform recent, specialized approaches for learning event grammars [18] and image grammars [17], particularly on more complex or challenging datasets. The ablation-like comparison of the three methods for event grammar learning provides useful insight into the benefits of learning relations directly from atomic actions.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Positioning with Respect to Prior Work:** The paper claims to present a "unified formalization" and "extend" previous work. While the framework is indeed unified in its application across domains, the learning algorithm itself could be viewed as a clever and effective *specialization* of the general grammar induction problem. For instance, the search is constrained to adding specific "And-Or fragments." Framing the work as a tractable and powerful specialization of a notoriously difficult problem is a strong position. I would suggest the authors refine their claims to more clearly delineate how their work specializes the search strategy compared to more general grammar induction methods from the Natural Language Processing (NLP) literature, which makes the problem tractable and effective.

2.  **Limited Experimental Comparisons:** The experimental comparisons are primarily against closely related works, often from the same research group ([17], [18]). While these are relevant baselines, the paper would be significantly strengthened by comparison to more established, classic grammar induction algorithms. In particular, the Bayesian model merging approach of Stolcke and Omohundro [14] is a highly influential method for learning probabilistic context-free grammars. Since the proposed method operates on sequences of symbols in the event task, applying this baseline seems feasible and would provide a very valuable comparison point for the community.

3.  **Clarity on the Search Procedure:** The paper states that greedy or beam search is used to find the best And-Or fragments but provides limited detail on how candidate fragments are proposed and evaluated. For reproducibility, it would be beneficial to elaborate on this search. For example, how are candidate n-grams and their corresponding contexts identified? Is the search exhaustive up to a certain `n`? Providing pseudocode or a more detailed description of the search step within the main algorithm would improve the clarity and completeness of the work.

**Summary of Arguments:**

**Pro-acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated approach for learning And-Or grammars by inducing unified "And-Or fragments."
*   The method is technically sound, based on optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar with an efficient computation of the gain.
*   Demonstrates impressive generality by successfully applying the same algorithm to both event and image domains.
*   Achieves strong empirical results, outperforming relevant recent baselines on several datasets.

**Con-acceptance (or points to address):**
*   The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including comparisons to classic and more diverse grammar induction baselines, such as Stolcke and Omohundro [14].
*   The paper's positioning relative to the broader field of grammar induction could be clarified; framing it as a tractable specialization would be more precise.
*   Algorithmic details regarding the search for candidate fragments could be more explicit to improve reproducibility.

Overall, this is a solid and valuable contribution. The proposed method for unified structure learning is elegant, and the results are convincing. The paper is well-written and presents a complete piece of work. Addressing the points above, particularly by broadening the experimental comparison, would further strengthen this commendable paper.
