This paper proposes a new approach to the unguided teaching of the structure of AND-OR grammars. Unlike previous approaches, the current one motivates integrated AND-OR fragments rather than searching for separate AND and OR operations. The value of the proposed new AND-OR fragment can be efficiently calculated using a set of sufficient statistics. This approach is evaluated on several tasks, including parsing events and visual objects.

It's generally a good paper. The problem is significant. The approach is novel and well-motivated. The algorithm seems technically sound, but I couldn't verify the complementary derivations. Some things were unclear to me, which may reflect my own limited time for reading, but I would urge the authors to clarify these points more explicitly: First, it wasn't clear whether the "surrogate measure" of probability gain was an approximate or exact notion. Second, it wasn't clear whether the approach could extend to learning fragments with more than two - आर्यता, or whether it could only learn grammars in a "Chomsky normal form" in a traceable way.

My main concerns with the paper are focused on the experiments. I had trouble understanding most of the aspects: exactly what was done, how the examples were represented, and what was learned. I got the impression that the ideal audience for the paper is a reader who has followed, thoroughly studied, and preferably re-implemented all the recent visual-grammar papers coming from S.C. Zhu's group. For such a reader, the experimental section would probably be more understandable, and the results would be more useful. But for outsiders, it was less clear. I understand that the small NIPS format has its limitations. But fortunately, NIPS allows complementary material, and if the paper is accepted, I would urge the authors to include more details and concrete examples of their experiments in their complement.

I would especially like to see (or be able to see) examples of any interesting structure discovered in the learned grammars. The paper has a great set about the importance of learning grammars and structure, but then it is a bit disappointing for these expectations that the results are presented purely quantitatively. I agree with the authors that the quantitative results are satisfactory in themselves. But they are not very exciting or illuminating. I would find the paper more compelling if the authors had shown that the interesting structure is learned, and that the more interesting structure is learned by this approach relative to competing approaches. It is an interesting paper on the unguided teaching of AND-OR grammars. While I liked it, I had trouble following the experiments and interpreting the results, due to not being familiar with a lot of prior work (mostly from Zhu's UCLA group) that seemed to weigh heavily on it. If accepted, the authors should include complementary material with more details and illustrations of how the experiments work and what interesting structure was learned.
