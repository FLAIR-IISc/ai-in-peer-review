

This paper introduces a method for learning the structure of stochastic And-Or grammars. The authors claim their approach "generalizes" prior work on structure learning, but it actually represents a constrained special case that enables tractable computation. This is a critical clarification: the problem addressed is not more general than standard NLP structure learning challenges, but rather a specific variant that simplifies the task. The authors correctly acknowledge this limitation, and their method nonetheless constitutes a valuable contribution to the field.

The core algorithm is well-motivated and empirically validated. The authors demonstrate strong performance against existing grammar induction techniques on standard benchmarks, with clear evidence that their approach outperforms competing methods. The theoretical foundation is sound, and the exposition is sufficiently detailed to support reproducibility. This work advances the state of the art by providing a practical solution to a computationally challenging problem within a well-defined subclass of grammars.

The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. While the comparisons presented are meaningful, they omit a key baseline: the method of Stolcke and Omohondro (reference 14), which is directly applicable to the grammars and tasks under consideration. This omission limits the contextualization of the results. Including this comparison would provide a more rigorous assessment of the proposed method's relative strengths and help establish its position within the broader landscape of grammar induction techniques. The authors should address this gap to strengthen their empirical claims.

The paper is well-written, logically structured, and makes a clear technical contribution. The focus on a tractable subclass of grammars is both principled and practically significant, offering a viable path for real-world applications where full generality is computationally infeasible. The authors successfully balance theoretical rigor with empirical validation, and their results align with the problem's constraints.

In summary, this is a solid paper that advances grammar induction through a focused, computationally efficient approach. The method is technically sound, the experiments are largely convincing, and the contribution is meaningful within the field. With the addition of the suggested baseline comparison, the work would be even more compelling. As it stands, it represents a worthy contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions to address the experimental evaluation.
