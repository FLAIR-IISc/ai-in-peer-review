This paper introduces a novel approach for the unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammar structures. In contrast to previous methods that search for AND and OR operations separately, this work proposes to induce unified AND-OR fragments. A key contribution is an efficient method for computing the value of a proposed new fragment using a set of sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on tasks involving the parsing of events and visual objects.

This is a well-written paper that addresses an important and challenging problem. The proposed approach is novel, well-motivated, and appears to be technically sound, although I was not able to verify the derivations provided in the supplementary material.

The paper could be strengthened by clarifying a few key points:

1.  It was not entirely clear whether the "surrogate measure" of the likelihood gain is an exact calculation or an approximation. Clarifying this point is important for understanding the theoretical guarantees of the learning algorithm.

2.  The discussion should specify whether the approach can extend to learning grammar fragments with an arity greater than two, or if it is fundamentally limited to learning grammars in a structure analogous to Chomsky normal form. This has significant implications for the expressiveness of the grammars that can be learned.

My primary concern with the paper lies in the experimental evaluation. I found it difficult to understand the specifics of the experiments, including the exact procedures followed, the representation of the examples, and the precise nature of what was learned. The presentation seems to assume significant prior knowledge of recent work in visual grammars, particularly from the senior author's research group. For a reader not intimately familiar with this line of work, the experimental section is difficult to parse, and the results are hard to place in a broader context.

While the space limitations of the NIPS format are understandable, this is a case where supplementary materials are essential. If the paper is accepted, I would strongly urge the authors to include a detailed appendix that provides more concrete illustrations of the experiments. This should include examples of the input data, details on the feature representations, and a clearer walkthrough of the experimental pipeline.

Furthermore, a significant missed opportunity is the lack of qualitative results that illustrate the discovered grammatical structures. The paper's introduction builds a strong case for the importance of learning grammars and discovering structure, but the evaluation then relies exclusively on quantitative metrics. While the reported quantitative results are satisfactory on their own terms, they are not particularly exciting or illuminating. They demonstrate *that* the method performs well, but not *what* it has learned.

The paper would be far more compelling if the authors could showcase examples of interesting structures discovered by their algorithm. Visualizing portions of the learned grammars—and ideally, comparing them to the structures learned by competing approaches—would provide crucial insight into the method's strengths and more directly validate the paper's core claims. Such qualitative evidence is often as important as quantitative benchmarks in the field of unsupervised structure learning.

In summary, this is an interesting paper with a novel contribution to the unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammars. However, its accessibility and impact are currently limited by an opaque experimental section and a lack of qualitative results. I would encourage the authors to significantly expand upon their experiments with detailed explanations and examples in the supplementary material to make their good work more understandable and convincing to a wider audience.
