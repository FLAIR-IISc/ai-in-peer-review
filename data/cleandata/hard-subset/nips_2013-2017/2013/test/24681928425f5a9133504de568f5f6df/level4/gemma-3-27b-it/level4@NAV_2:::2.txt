This paper presents a method for learning the structure and parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars, which compactly represent compositionality and reconfigurability and have applications in areas like natural language processing and image modeling. The authors propose an unsupervised approach that iteratively introduces new AND-OR fragments to refine the grammar, optimizing for posterior probability through consideration of both likelihood and prior gains. 

The paper is well-motivated, placing the work within the context of existing research on stochastic grammars [8, 9] and sum-product networks [10], and clearly articulates the challenges of unsupervised grammar induction, particularly regarding structure learning [11, 12, 13]. The proposed method builds upon previous work on structure learning for both natural language [14, 15, 16] and images/events [17, 18], and offers a unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations. The key contributions—a data-agnostic formalization, posterior probability optimization, unified learning of compositions/reconfigurations, and empirical validation—are clearly stated.

The strengths of the paper lie in its principled approach to structure learning and the elegant formulation of the likelihood gain based on sufficient statistics and tensor/matrix coherence [22]. The authors convincingly argue for the benefits of simultaneously learning AND and OR fragments, and the empirical results on event and image grammars demonstrate competitive performance. The connection to existing work in Bayesian model merging [19] and the discussion of potential scalability issues are appreciated.

However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of computational complexity. While the algorithm is reported to run reasonably fast on the tested datasets, a more detailed analysis of its scalability with increasing data size and grammar complexity would strengthen the work. Furthermore, given the historical roots of stochastic grammars in natural language processing [8, 9], it would be valuable to see experiments on a standard natural language dataset, such as the Penn Treebank, and a comparison to existing methods specifically designed for parsing natural language [11, 12]. This would help to assess the generalizability of the proposed approach and provide a stronger benchmark against established techniques.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to unsupervised structure learning of stochastic AND-OR grammars.
*   The theoretical formulation, particularly the efficient computation of the likelihood gain, is well-motivated and clearly explained.
*   The empirical results demonstrate competitive performance on challenging tasks.
*   The work addresses a significant problem in probabilistic modeling and offers a potentially valuable tool for various applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The discussion of computational complexity and scalability is limited.
*   The lack of experiments on standard natural language datasets weakens the assessment of generalizability.
*   The presentation, while clear, could benefit from more detailed explanations of certain technical aspects.



Overall, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While the limitations mentioned above warrant consideration, the strengths of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the scalability and natural language evaluation points.
