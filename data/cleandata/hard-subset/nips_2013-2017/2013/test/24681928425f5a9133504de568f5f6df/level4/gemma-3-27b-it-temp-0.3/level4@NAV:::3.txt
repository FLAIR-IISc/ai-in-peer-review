This paper introduces a novel approach to unsupervised learning of AND-OR grammar structure, differing from prior work by inducing unified AND-OR fragments instead of separately learning AND and OR components. The proposed method efficiently computes the value of new fragments using sufficient statistics. The approach is evaluated on tasks involving parsing events and visual objects.

Overall, this is a strong paper addressing an important problem with a well-motivated and technically sound approach. While I was unable to fully verify the supplemental derivations, the core algorithm appears robust. I do have a few clarifying questions: First, it would be helpful to explicitly state whether the “surrogate measure” for likelihood gain is an approximation or an exact calculation. Second, the paper doesn’t clearly indicate whether the method generalizes to fragments with arity greater than 2, or if it is limited to learning grammars in Chomsky normal form.

My primary concerns relate to the experimental evaluation. The details of the experiments – the specific procedures, example representations, and learned outcomes – were difficult to follow. The presentation assumes a high degree of familiarity with recent visual-grammar research, particularly from the S. C. Zhu group, which may limit accessibility for readers outside that specific area of expertise. While acknowledging the constraints of the NIPS format, I strongly encourage the authors to significantly expand the supplementary materials with detailed explanations and concrete illustrations of the experimental setup and results, should the paper be accepted.

Specifically, I would be very interested to see examples of the structural patterns discovered by the learned grammars. The paper effectively establishes the importance of grammar and structure learning, but the exclusive focus on quantitative results feels somewhat unsatisfying. While these quantitative results are valid, they lack a qualitative component that would demonstrate the learned structure and its advantages over competing methods. 

In conclusion, this is a promising paper on unsupervised AND-OR grammar learning. However, my understanding of the experiments and interpretation of the results were hampered by a reliance on specialized prior work. Expanding the supplementary materials to include more detailed experimental descriptions and illustrative examples of learned structure would substantially strengthen the paper.
