- Proposes an unsupervised, iterative bottom-up method that starts from a trivial grammar and progressively adds "And-Or fragments" to learn both structure and parameters.
- Introduces a unified fragment structure that jointly creates compositional And-rules and reconfigurable Or-rules, merging searches previously handled separately.
- Fragment addition is guided by a principled objective: maximizing the posterior probability of the grammar.
- Derives an efficient method to compute the gain in the posterior using sufficient statistics from the data.
- Provides a general, data-agnostic formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars, highlighting broad applicability beyond tested domains.
- Demonstrates strong empirical results on event grammars from video and image grammars on synthetic and real datasets, outperforming or matching specialized baselines.
- Technically sound and well-motivated; the And-Or fragment idea is novel and addresses data scarcity and search inefficiency concerns.
- Lacks a formal computational complexity analysis; scalability with vocabulary size, sample length, and grammar complexity is unclear.
- Omits experiments on natural language data (e.g., Penn Treebank), missing a standard benchmark and comparison to seminal grammar induction work.
- Overall: solid, well-written contribution likely useful for structured probabilistic model research despite the noted evaluation and scalability gaps.
