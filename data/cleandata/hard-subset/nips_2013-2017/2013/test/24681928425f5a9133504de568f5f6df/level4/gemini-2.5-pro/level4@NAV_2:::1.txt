This paper presents an unsupervised method for learning both the structure and the parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars from unannotated data. The core contribution is a bottom-up algorithm that begins with a trivial grammar capable of generating only the training samples. It then iteratively generalizes the grammar by inducing "And-Or fragments," which serve to unify the learning of compositional structure (And-rules) and reconfigurational alternatives (Or-rules). The selection of which fragment to add is guided by a principled objective: maximizing the posterior probability of the grammar, which incorporates both a Viterbi likelihood term and a sparsity-inducing prior on the grammar's size. The authors derive an efficient way to compute the posterior gain, making the search tractable. This general framework is evaluated on two distinct problems: learning event grammars from video and image grammars from object datasets. This work relates to and extends prior work on grammar induction in natural language processing [14, 15, 16] as well as more recent applications of And-Or grammars in computer vision [17, 18].

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Originality and Approach:** The primary strength of this paper is the novel idea of learning "And-Or fragments" to jointly induce compositions and reconfigurations. Many previous structure learning approaches handle these two aspects separately, which can be less efficient and robust. The proposed unified approach is sensible, elegant, and well-motivated.
*   **Technical Quality:** The work is technically sound. It frames the learning problem in a principled Bayesian framework, optimizing the grammar's posterior probability. The derivation of the posterior gain, which enables an efficient greedy or beam search over candidate fragments, is a solid technical contribution. The connection of this gain to tensor and matrix coherence measures is insightful.
*   **Significance and Generality:** The method provides a powerful, domain-general tool for learning compositional models. The successful application to two very different data types (temporal event sequences and spatial image patches) convincingly demonstrates the approach's flexibility and supports the claim of a data-agnostic formalization. The strong empirical results, which show performance comparable or superior to specialized state-of-the-art methods, highlight the significance of the contribution.

**Weaknesses / Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Experimental Comparisons:** The main weakness lies in the choice of baselines. While the comparisons to recent, domain-specific methods [17, 18] are valuable, the evaluation would be significantly stronger with the inclusion of more foundational grammar induction algorithms. For instance, the classic Bayesian model merging approach of Stolcke and Omohundro [14] seems applicable to the discrete sequence data used in the event grammar task and would provide a more comprehensive comparison point from the wider grammar learning literature.
*   **Clarity of Positioning:** The paper's positioning could be slightly refined. While it builds upon ideas from NLP, framing the work as a general extension of grammar learning might be an overstatement. The method's strength lies in its specialization to the particular structure of And-Or grammars, which makes the problem tractable. Clarifying that this is a powerful, specialized method for this grammar class, rather than a general-purpose grammar induction algorithm, would be more precise.

Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a novel and well-executed method for a challenging and important problem. The core idea is strong, the technical development is sound, and the experiments are convincing. The primary area for improvement is a more comprehensive experimental comparison with established grammar induction baselines.
