

This paper introduces a method for unsupervised learning of both structure and parameters in stochastic AND-OR grammars, which model recursive phenomena like natural language and image structures through AND (composition) and OR (choice) rules. The authors propose a novel approach that incrementally constructs the grammar by introducing new AND-OR fragments at each step, evaluating both likelihood improvements and prior-based gains to guide the learning process. The method demonstrates competitive performance on two tasks: learning event grammars and image grammars, achieving results comparable to state-of-the-art methods in these domains.

The paper presents a valuable contribution by offering a computationally feasible framework for learning complex grammatical structures, addressing a key challenge in unsupervised grammar induction. The incremental fragment construction strategy is well-motivated and effectively balances model complexity with empirical fit.

However, two significant limitations warrant consideration. First, the authors provide insufficient detail on the computational tractability of their algorithm. Crucially, it remains unclear whether the method scales efficiently to large datasets—specifically, whether it can handle thousands of data points within reasonable time and memory constraints. Without empirical evidence or complexity analysis (e.g., time complexity as a function of data size), the practical applicability of the approach for real-world applications remains uncertain. A brief discussion of scalability, perhaps including runtime benchmarks on progressively larger datasets, would substantially strengthen the paper.

Second, the absence of experiments on natural language data is a notable gap, given that natural language is the most prominent application domain for such grammars. The authors reference prior work by Klein and Manning on the Penn Treebank, yet the paper fails to demonstrate whether their method can be applied to standard NLP tasks. It is unclear whether the approach can effectively learn grammatical structures from syntactic trees in corpora like Penn Treebank, or if the model’s complexity or learning dynamics pose fundamental barriers. Including even preliminary experiments on a small-scale natural language dataset would significantly bolster the paper’s relevance and demonstrate the generality of the proposed framework beyond the two presented tasks.

In summary, the paper offers a promising and theoretically sound method for learning AND-OR grammars with competitive empirical results on non-linguistic tasks. Addressing the scalability concerns and extending the evaluation to natural language data—particularly using a benchmark like Penn Treebank—would substantially enhance the paper’s impact and convince reviewers of its broader applicability. The current work is strong but would benefit from these additional validations to fully establish its utility for the intended domain.
