Summary and relation to prior work
This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) and an unsupervised procedure to learn both structure and parameters from unannotated data. The key idea is to learn And-Or fragments—each comprising a new And-node with multiple sibling Or-nodes—so that compositions (And) and reconfigurations (Or) are induced jointly rather than in two decoupled stages. The posterior over grammars is optimized with a sparsity prior, and likelihood is approximated by the Viterbi likelihood. The authors further derive sufficient statistics (an n-gram tensor over fragment configurations and a context matrix) that allow efficient evaluation of a fragment’s posterior gain under an unambiguity assumption. Experiments span event grammar learning from video-derived action streams and image grammar learning on synthetic and real datasets, showing performance comparable to or better than recent baselines.

The work relates to several threads: (i) structure and parameter learning for probabilistic grammars in NLP (e.g., Bayesian model merging, dependency/PCFG induction, Viterbi training/regularization); (ii) And-Or templates/grammars in computer vision for images and events; and (iii) connections to decomposable sum-product networks. The main methodological novelty is the unified And-Or fragment search with an explicit posterior objective and sufficient statistics, which extends structure-learning ideas from NLP to the more general, data-agnostic AOG setting.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The learning objective and search procedure are clearly defined. The sufficient-statistics formulation for posterior gain is compelling, but the main text relies on a supplementary derivation and an unambiguity assumption. Please make explicit whether the “surrogate” likelihood gain is exact under the unambiguity assumption (and only approximate otherwise), and clarify where approximations enter (Viterbi likelihood, unambiguity, any independence assumptions in the coherence factorization).
- Scope of fragments: It appears fragments allow arbitrary arity n (not limited to binary/Chomsky normal form), which is important when relations are not concatenative. Please state explicitly the supported arities and whether learning is tractable for n>2 in practice (both in the search over sub-tensors and in parsing).
- Relations and parameterization: The approach is agnostic to relation types in And-rules, but performance and tractability depend critically on how relations are defined and parameterized (e.g., temporal order, co-occurrence, spatial offset). More detail on how relations are encoded, scored, and constrained would improve reproducibility.
- Search and complexity: The method uses greedy/beam search with random restarts over candidate fragments. A brief complexity analysis, pruning criteria, and run-time/memory statistics for each dataset would help assess scalability and robustness to local optima.
- Experiments: The quantitative results are promising. However, much of the setup (preprocessing, featureization, exact terminal inventories, relation sets, smoothing for perplexity, and CYK settings) is hard to reconstruct from the current text. The paper would benefit from a more thorough experimental exposition in the supplementary material, including qualitative illustrations of learned structures.

Clarity
The exposition of the model and algorithm is generally clear, but several important points are under-specified:
- Clarify “surrogate measure” vs “exact likelihood gain,” and be explicit about the conditions (e.g., unambiguity) under which the posterior gain formula is valid.
- Provide definitions and examples for the n-gram tensor and context matrix on both event and image domains, with small concrete instances.
- Summarize the key steps of the search procedure (candidate generation, scoring, acceptance) and termination criteria in the main text.
- For the event experiments, clearly describe the three variants (IDs vs direct action vectors; enforced order of co-occurrence vs followed-by; grounding to atomic actions), with diagrams of learned event structures and example parses.
- For the image experiments, show qualitative parses or templates learned for each animal category, and at least one compare-and-contrast example against the competing method.

Originality
- The unified formalization of context-free AOGs and the proposal to learn And and Or components jointly via And-Or fragments are novel and well-motivated.
- Casting fragment selection as posterior optimization with a sparsity prior and deriving sufficient statistics to evaluate gains efficiently extend and synthesize ideas from PCFG induction, SPNs, and AOG learning in a novel way.

Significance
- The ability to induce compact, interpretable structured models across modalities (events and images) is important. The method yields improvements over prior approaches in both domains (e.g., lower perplexity on real images; better F-measure on the more difficult event dataset).
- The main limitation to broader impact is clarity and reproducibility; more accessible experimental details and qualitative demonstrations would substantially increase the paper’s utility to researchers outside the immediate subcommunity.

Specific questions for the authors
- Is the likelihood gain expression exact under grammar unambiguity and Viterbi training, or still approximate? What is the empirical impact of violations of unambiguity?
- What arities n are explored in practice? How do runtime and accuracy scale with n and with the size of the candidate pool?
- How are And-rule relations parameterized and scored across domains? Are relation sets fixed a priori or learned?
- What smoothing is applied when computing perplexity on images?
- How sensitive are results to the sparsity hyperparameter α and to encoding costs sa, so?
- Could you show qualitative examples: learned event hierarchies, image part decompositions, and parse trees?

Pros
- Clear, principled objective with sparsity prior; uses Viterbi likelihood and sufficient statistics to make search practical.
- Unified fragment learning that couples compositionality and reconfigurability, reducing redundancy and data scarcity issues.
- Domain-agnostic formalism demonstrated on two distinct modalities with favorable results.
- Potentially interpretable models that can support parsing-based tasks.

Cons
- Reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi approximations is not fully justified or ablated.
- Experimental section is difficult to follow without substantial prior familiarity; limited qualitative insight into learned structure.
- Search heuristics and computational complexity are under-documented.
- Dependence on manually specified relation sets may limit portability.

Suggestions for improvement (most can go to the supplement)
- Provide a clear derivation of the posterior gain, with conditions for exactness, and an ablation on the unambiguity assumption.
- Include qualitative visualizations of learned fragments and parses for both domains.
- Detail preprocessing, terminal inventories, relation definitions, smoothing, and evaluation protocols.
- Report runtime/memory, beam sizes, restarts, and sensitivity to α; include ablations comparing And-only, Or-only, and And-Or fragment learning.
- If feasible, release code or pseudocode detailed enough to reproduce the experiments.

Overall assessment
A well-motivated and technically interesting contribution to unsupervised structure learning for AOGs, with solid quantitative results. I recommend acceptance conditional on improving clarity and providing richer qualitative evidence of learned structure in the supplementary material.
