- Proposes a unified method for unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or fragments, unlike prior separate-learning approaches.  
- Uses sufficient statistics to efficiently compute fragment values, suggesting scalability and practical applicability.  
- Evaluated empirically on event and image grammar tasks, showing competitive and sometimes superior performance.  
- Strong motivation and sound theoretical foundation, though supplemental derivations were not fully verified by the reviewer.  
- Novelty: unifies composition and reconfiguration search in And-Or grammars, addressing prior limitations.  
- Experimental details are insufficiently clear for readers unfamiliar with prior Zhu group work.  
- Lacks qualitative analysis and examples of learned grammars, limiting interpretability despite good quantitative results.  
- Does not address scalability to higher-arity fragments or implications of enforcing specific grammar forms (e.g., CNF).  
- Ambiguity around whether the “surrogate measure” for likelihood gain is exact or approximate.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically sound, efficient computation via sufficient statistics, and strong empirical results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: unclear experimental presentation, limited qualitative analysis, and unaddressed scalability concerns.  
- Overall assessment: good paper with clear contribution but weakened by experimental clarity and lack of qualitative/scale discussion.  
- Recommendation: accept conditional on expanded supplementary material with detailed experiment explanations, visualizations of learned grammars, and discussion of scalability/limitations.
