### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes an unsupervised method for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars from unannotated data. The key idea is to start with a trivial grammar that generates only the training samples and iteratively refine it. The refinement process involves searching for and incorporating "And-Or fragments," which simultaneously model composition (through an And-rule) and reconfiguration/alternatives (through a set of Or-rules). This unified approach to inducing compositional and reconfigurable structures is the main contribution. The learning algorithm is framed as an optimization problem that seeks to maximize the posterior probability of the grammar, which combines a Viterbi likelihood term with a sparsity prior to favor compact grammars. The authors derive an efficient method for calculating the gain in posterior probability for adding a given fragment, based on sufficient statistics collected from the training data. The data-agnostic formulation of the grammar and the learning algorithm is demonstrated on two distinct tasks: learning event grammars from human activity videos and learning image grammars from animal face sketches. The experimental results show that the proposed method achieves performance comparable to or better than previous domain-specific approaches.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled and Novel Learning Framework:** The core contribution—the induction of "And-Or fragments"—is a novel and well-motivated approach to structure learning. By unifying the search for compositional (And) and alternative (Or) rules, the method elegantly addresses a key challenge in grammar induction. This unified search is more robust to data scarcity and ambiguity compared to methods that learn these components separately.

2.  **Clear Optimization Objective:** The algorithm is grounded in a clear Bayesian objective: maximizing the posterior probability of the grammar. The derivation of the posterior gain, factorized into likelihood and prior gains, is technically sound and provides an efficient way to guide the greedy search for new grammar fragments. This places the work on a more solid theoretical footing than purely heuristic-based approaches.

3.  **Generality and Broad Applicability:** The formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars is deliberately data-agnostic, allowing the same learning machinery to be applied to different modalities. The successful application to both temporal event data and spatial image data is a strong demonstration of the method's flexibility and significance.

4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments are well-executed and show that the proposed approach is competitive with or superior to recent, specialized methods ([17], [18]) in both the event and image domains. The improved performance, especially on the more complex datasets (the synthesized event data and the real image data), validates the effectiveness of the proposed learning strategy.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Positioning of the Contribution:** The paper suggests its approach "generalizes" previous work. A more accurate description might be that it proposes a specific, structured, and tractable search strategy within the broader grammar induction problem. While this structured approach is a clear strength and a key contribution, framing it as a strict generalization of the entire field (e.g., structure learning in NLP) may be an overstatement. The contribution is significant on its own merit as a specialized and effective learning algorithm.

2.  **Limited Experimental Baselines:** The experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including comparisons to more foundational grammar induction algorithms. While the comparisons to recent, closely related work ([17], [18]) are valuable, a key baseline from the literature is the Bayesian model merging approach of Stolcke and Omohundro [14]. Their method is a classic in the field and its principles could likely be adapted to these tasks. Comparing against such a well-established method would provide better context for the performance of the proposed algorithm and further solidify its contribution.

3.  **Search Procedure:** The paper states that greedy search or beam search with random restarts is used to find optimal And-Or fragments. This type of search is susceptible to local optima. A more detailed discussion of the search space's complexity and the potential limitations of a greedy strategy would be beneficial. For instance, an analysis of how sensitive the final learned grammar is to the random restarts or the beam width would add depth to the evaluation.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated method for learning And-Or grammars by inducing unified "And-Or fragments."
*   The approach is technically sound, based on maximizing a well-defined posterior probability objective.
*   The data-agnostic formulation is a significant strength, demonstrated convincingly on two different and challenging domains (events and images).
*   The empirical results are strong, showing clear improvements over relevant recent work.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   The experimental comparison is somewhat narrow, missing important baselines from the classic grammar induction literature (e.g., Stolcke and Omohundro [14]).
*   The discussion of the greedy search procedure and its limitations (e.g., local optima) is brief.

### Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is a solid and well-written paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning.

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the optimization problem and the derivation of the posterior gain are clear and correct. The experimental methodology is appropriate and supports the claims.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The core idea of "And-Or fragments" is explained effectively with illustrations, and the algorithm is described in sufficient detail.
*   **Originality:** The primary contribution—the unified induction of And-rules and Or-rules via And-Or fragments—is novel and addresses a significant challenge in grammar learning.
*   **Significance:** The results are important. By providing a general framework that works well on different data types, the paper is likely to be influential for researchers working on structured models in computer vision, activity recognition, and potentially other fields.

The weaknesses, primarily concerning the scope of experimental baselines and the discussion of the search strategy, are relatively minor and do not undermine the core contribution. They represent opportunities for future work. The paper presents a sensible, novel, and empirically successful algorithm for a difficult problem.

**Recommendation: Accept**
