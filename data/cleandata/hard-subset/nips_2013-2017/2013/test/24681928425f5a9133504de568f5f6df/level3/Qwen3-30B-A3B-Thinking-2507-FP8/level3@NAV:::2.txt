

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic AND-OR grammars and proposes an unsupervised learning approach that jointly optimizes grammar structure and parameters. The authors extend previous work on structure learning by introducing AND-OR fragments that simultaneously model compositions (via AND-rules) and reconfigurations (via OR-rules) in a unified manner. The method starts from a trivial initial grammar and iteratively introduces new fragments to maximize the posterior probability of the grammar, using efficient sufficient statistics to compute likelihood and prior gains.

The paper's strengths include a technically sound approach with a clear theoretical foundation, well-organized presentation, and convincing empirical results on event and image grammars. The unified treatment of AND-OR fragments represents a meaningful advancement over prior work that handled compositions and reconfigurations separately [14-18]. The formalization is agnostic to data type, suggesting broad applicability beyond the evaluated domains.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses. The most critical omission is the lack of natural language experiments, which is particularly surprising given the paper's claim about modeling recursive phenomena like natural language. The authors should have compared against established NLP benchmarks like Penn Treebank and prior work by Klein & Manning [11,12]. Furthermore, the paper fails to address algorithmic tractability or scalability to larger datasets (e.g., thousands of datapoints), despite the authors' claim of "tractable" learning. The experiments with 5000 samples (as mentioned in Section 3.3) are insufficient to demonstrate scalability to real-world problems.

The significance of the work is limited by these omissions, as the primary application domain for stochastic grammars (natural language processing) remains unaddressed. While the event and image grammar results are competitive, they don't demonstrate the claimed advantages for recursive phenomena.

Overall, this is a technically solid paper with a novel methodological contribution, but its significance is undermined by the lack of natural language experiments and scalability analysis. The work would be substantially strengthened by addressing these limitations. I recommend rejection due to the critical omission of natural language experiments and insufficient scalability discussion, which are essential for a paper claiming to advance stochastic grammar learning for recursive phenomena.
