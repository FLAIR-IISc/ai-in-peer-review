## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper proposes a method for unsupervised learning of both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars, aiming to model compositional and reconfigurable data. The authors present a unified formalization of these grammars and an iterative learning approach that introduces And-Or fragments based on posterior probability optimization. They demonstrate competitive results on event and image grammar learning tasks. 

The work builds upon existing research in stochastic grammars, particularly extending ideas from natural language processing (e.g., Klein & Manning, cited) and recent work on learning image grammars (e.g., Zhu & Mumford, cited). The motivation for jointly learning structure and parameters is well-articulated, addressing limitations of previous approaches that either fixed the grammar structure or struggled with scalability. The introduction of And-Or fragments as a unified way to induce compositions and reconfigurations is a novel contribution. 

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear presentation of the formalization and the proposed learning algorithm. The experimental evaluation, while limited, demonstrates the approach’s effectiveness on the chosen tasks. The theoretical justification for the likelihood gain computation, leveraging sufficient statistics and coherence measures, is a positive aspect.

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses. The most critical is the lack of discussion regarding algorithmic tractability and scalability. While the authors mention running the prototype on datasets with “5000 training samples,” there’s no analysis of computational complexity or memory requirements, making it unclear how well this approach would scale to larger, more realistic datasets (e.g., thousands of datapoints as noted by the reviewer).  

More importantly, the absence of experiments on natural language data is a major omission. Given the roots of stochastic grammars in NLP, and the explicit comparison to Klein & Manning, evaluating the method on a standard benchmark like the Penn Treebank is crucial. Without this, it’s difficult to assess the true potential of the approach and its competitiveness with established NLP techniques. The experiments on event and image grammars, while valuable, don’t fully justify the claim of a “tractable way to learn stochastic grammars” for complex, recursive phenomena like natural language. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to jointly learning structure and parameters of And-Or grammars.
*   Clear presentation and theoretical justification.
*   Competitive results on event and image grammar learning.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of scalability analysis and discussion of algorithmic tractability.
*   Missing experiments on natural language data, a core application area for stochastic grammars.
*   Limited experimental evaluation overall.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend **rejection** at this time. While the paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution, the lack of scalability analysis and the absence of natural language experiments significantly limit its impact and prevent a strong assessment of its practical utility. Addressing these concerns would substantially strengthen the paper.
