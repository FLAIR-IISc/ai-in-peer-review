

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to the type of data being modeled (text, images, events), along with an unsupervised learning approach for learning both grammar structure and parameters. The authors address the important problem of grammar structure learning, which is typically difficult to specify manually. The paper extends prior work on stochastic grammars for natural language [8, 9], sum-product networks [10], and And-Or grammars for images [1, 3] and events [7].

The key innovation is the proposal to learn unified AND-OR fragments rather than separate AND and OR searches. Previous approaches would learn new compositions (via AND-fragments) and reconfigurations (via OR-fragments) in separate steps, which could be error-prone with scarce data. The authors instead propose a unified approach that simultaneously learns both aspects through AND-OR fragments, which can represent multiple compositions and configurations within a single fragment. This approach is theoretically grounded through the efficient computation of fragment value using sufficient statistics, which allows the algorithm to avoid re-parsing all training samples when evaluating potential fragments.

The paper is well-motivated and the algorithm is technically sound. The authors present a clear problem definition, formalization of the grammar, and detailed learning framework. The empirical evaluation on event parsing and image parsing tasks shows competitive or better performance than previous approaches, which demonstrates the practical value of the method.

The main strengths of the paper are:
- The unified approach to grammar structure learning is novel and well-justified
- The formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars as data-agnostic is valuable for future work
- The efficient computation of fragment value using sufficient statistics is a significant technical contribution
- The experimental results across two distinct domains (event parsing and image parsing) provide strong evidence of the method's effectiveness

However, the paper has several important weaknesses that need to be addressed:

1. **Experimental clarity**: The experimental section is difficult to understand. It's unclear what was done, how examples were represented, and what was learned. The paper assumes familiarity with prior work from S. C. Zhu's group, making it inaccessible to outsiders. For instance, the description of the "third method" for event grammars is particularly confusing without visual examples of the grammars learned.

2. **Theoretical clarity**: The reviewer could not verify supplemental derivations due to lack of clarity. It's unclear whether the "surrogate measure" of likelihood gain is exact or an approximation. The paper states the likelihood gain can be computed efficiently using sufficient statistics, but the derivation is not sufficiently clear for verification.

3. **Scope limitations**: It's unclear whether the method extends to fragments with arity >2 or is limited to Chomsky normal form (which would restrict the expressive power of the learned grammars). This is a significant limitation for practical applications.

4. **Lack of qualitative examples**: The paper presents largely quantitative results without showing qualitative examples of interesting structures discovered by the method. The reviewer would like to see concrete examples of structures that were learned, as the current results don't illustrate what makes this approach better than competing methods.

If the authors can address these concerns by providing:
- More detailed experimental descriptions and concrete illustrations of learned grammars
- Clearer theoretical derivations and clarification of the likelihood gain measure
- Discussion of the method's limitations regarding fragment arity
- Qualitative examples of interesting structures discovered

then this paper would be a strong contribution. The current version is technically sound but lacks sufficient clarity and detail to fully convince reviewers of its value and limitations.

In terms of the NIPS review criteria:
- **Quality**: The approach is technically sound, but some theoretical aspects are unclear. The experimental results are convincing but lack sufficient detail for reproducibility.
- **Clarity**: The paper is well-written but assumes familiarity with prior work that isn't accessible to outsiders. More explanation and examples are needed.
- **Originality**: The unified AND-OR fragment approach is novel compared to previous separate AND/OR search methods.
- **Significance**: The problem is important, and the method advances the state of the art for grammar learning, but the significance would be stronger with more qualitative insights.

Overall, this is a promising approach to grammar structure learning with a strong technical foundation. However, the current version requires significant clarification in the experimental presentation and theoretical exposition to be fully convincing. The paper would benefit from extensive supplementary material with more experimental details and concrete illustrations of the grammars learned. If these concerns are addressed in the camera-ready version, I would strongly support acceptance.
