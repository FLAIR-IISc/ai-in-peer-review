

This paper presents an unsupervised approach for learning stochastic And-Or grammars by inducing unified And-Or fragments rather than separate And- or Or-searches. The authors propose a formalization of And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data types and develop an algorithm that iteratively optimizes the posterior probability of the grammar structure and parameters. The key innovation lies in the unified representation of compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments, with efficient computation of likelihood gains using sufficient statistics. The method is evaluated on event parsing and image parsing tasks, showing competitive or better performance than prior approaches.

The paper addresses an important problem in structure learning with a technically sound approach that builds on prior work by Zhu's group and sum-product networks. The proposed unified fragment representation is novel and well-motivated, offering a more efficient and robust alternative to previous approaches that handle compositions and reconfigurations separately. The algorithm's efficiency through sufficient statistics is particularly noteworthy.

However, several concerns require clarification. The paper's experimental section is difficult to follow without familiarity with Zhu's prior work, making the evaluation inaccessible to many readers. The authors do not clearly explain how examples were represented or what specific structures were learned. The "surrogate measure" for likelihood gain appears to be an approximation rather than exact, but this is not explicitly stated. It's also unclear whether the method extends beyond Chomsky normal form (arity >2 fragments) or is limited to this constraint. The supplementary derivations are insufficient for verification, and the paper lacks qualitative examples of discovered structures—relying solely on quantitative metrics that don't illuminate the method's strengths.

The significance of the work is promising, but the lack of clear experimental details and qualitative results weakens the contribution. While the approach appears technically sound, the paper would benefit from more transparent experimental reporting, clearer derivations, and concrete illustrations of learned structures. The authors should address these concerns in revision, particularly by providing extensive supplementary material with detailed experimental procedures and visual examples of the grammatical structures discovered.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and potentially valuable approach, but requires significant clarification to demonstrate its full contribution to the field. The technical soundness and novelty are strong points, but the current presentation undermines these strengths. I recommend major revision to address the clarity and experimental transparency issues before acceptance.
