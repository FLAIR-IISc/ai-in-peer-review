Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) from unannotated data. The authors give a unified, context-free formalization of AOGs applicable to multiple modalities (images and events) and introduce an iterative, bottom-up structure learning procedure. Starting from a trivial grammar that memorizes the training set, the algorithm repeatedly adds And-Or fragments—each consisting of one new And node and multiple new Or nodes—to increase the posterior P(G|X). The objective uses a sparsity prior on grammar size and a Viterbi approximation to the likelihood. A key technical contribution is an efficient scoring of candidate fragments via sufficient statistics: n-gram tensors of configurations and a context matrix, yielding a factorized posterior gain (likelihood coherence terms times a prior term). Search is performed greedily or with a beam.

The work connects to and aims to “generalize” prior structure-learning approaches for PCFGs and AOGs (e.g., Bayesian Model Merging [14], Klein & Manning [12], Solan et al. [15], image/event AOG learning [17, 18]) and to sum-product networks [10]. In practice, the method restricts to context-free AOGs and to a specific fragment class (And node with n Or children), which makes search and scoring tractable.

Experiments demonstrate event grammar induction on activity videos and image grammar induction on synthetic and real datasets, showing comparable or better performance than prior AOG-specific learners ([17, 18]) under the reported setups.

Strengths
- Sensible, tractable structure learning formulation: The And-Or fragment abstraction unifies learning compositions and reconfigurations, alleviating data sparsity and redundancy issues common when learning And and Or rules separately.
- Principled objective and efficient scoring: Explicit posterior optimization with a size prior and Viterbi likelihood; clever use of sufficient statistics (configuration tensors and context matrices) to avoid full re-parsing during search.
- Competitive empirical results: On both event and image domains, the method performs well relative to prior AOG learners and shows practical runtimes.
- Clear connection to established formalisms (PCFGs, SPNs) and prior AOG work, with a unified definition that is modality-agnostic.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope of the “generalization” claim: While the formalization is unified, the learning algorithm targets a special case—context-free AOGs and a specific And-Or fragment family, plus assumptions of unambiguity for scoring and Viterbi training. This is a reasonable and necessary restriction for tractability, but the paper should temper claims of generalization and more clearly delineate what is and is not covered relative to [12, 14, 15, 17, 18].
- Baselines are narrow: Comparisons are primarily to AOG-specific methods ([17, 18]). To assess the structure learning component more broadly, the paper should compare to general-purpose grammar induction baselines, especially Bayesian Model Merging (BMM) [14], which is a natural and historically important baseline for unsupervised structure learning. Additional informative comparisons include Klein & Manning [12], Solan et al. [15], and HDP-PCFG-style approaches [9] on appropriate sequence data. Even if direct application to images requires adaptation, event-sequence experiments can include these baselines.
- Assumptions and their impact: The posterior gain derivation assumes grammar unambiguity and uses Viterbi likelihood. The paper would benefit from:
  - An empirical study of how ambiguity affects the scoring heuristic and final quality (e.g., synthetic ambiguous grammars).
  - An ablation comparing Viterbi vs. full likelihood (where feasible) and the contribution of the two coherence factors and the size prior.
- Details and clarity: Some choices are underspecified, hindering reproducibility and interpretation.
  - How are sa and so (encoding costs) chosen and how sensitive are results to them and α?
  - How are “contexts” defined in the context matrix for events and images?
  - Search details (beam width, restart strategy) and time/space complexity as a function of n and grammar size.
  - A small textual PCFG experiment would further support the modality-agnostic claim.
- Evaluation breadth: More datasets and metrics would strengthen the case (e.g., error analyses, grammar compactness vs. performance, robustness to noise, and sensitivity to the maximum fragment arity n).

Quality
Overall technically sound, with a coherent objective and plausible derivations under stated assumptions. The algorithm design is sensible and the implementation appears practical. The empirical methodology is reasonable but would be much stronger with broader baselines (notably [14]) and ablations clarifying where the gains come from.

Clarity
Generally well-organized and readable. The And-Or fragment idea and sufficient-statistics scoring are explained clearly. Please clarify the encoding prior (sa, so), unambiguity assumption, context construction, and search settings. Including pseudocode (referenced in the supplement) and releasing code would help reproducibility.

Originality
Moderate. The unification of And/Or search via fragments and the efficient posterior-gain computation are nice ideas. However, the approach is close in spirit to established grammar merging/induction methods (e.g., [14, 12]) adapted to the AOG setting, with additional assumptions to ensure tractability.

Significance
Solid contribution for learning the structure of a particular, practically useful subclass of AOGs. Likely to be of interest to researchers in grammar induction and structured models for vision and events. Impact would increase with stronger cross-paradigm baselines and demonstrations beyond vision/events.

Questions for the authors
- Is the unambiguity assumption used only for efficient scoring, or is it required for correctness? How does performance change on ambiguous targets?
- How are sa and so set? Are they fixed constants or data/grammar dependent? Please report sensitivity to α, sa, so.
- What is the maximum fragment arity n used in experiments, and how does runtime scale with n and beam width?
- Can you provide comparisons to BMM [14] and other PCFG induction baselines on the event-sequence setting?

Recommended additional comparisons
- Stolcke and Omohundro (1994) [14] as a core baseline for structure learning.
- Klein & Manning (2004) [12], Solan et al. (2005) [15], and HDP-PCFG variants [9] where applicable.

Pros
- Principled, tractable structure-learning method for AOGs with good empirical performance.
- Unified treatment of And/Or via fragments; efficient scoring via sufficient statistics.
- Clear formalization and practical applicability to images and events.

Cons
- Overstated generalization claim; method targets a special, context-free subclass with additional assumptions.
- Limited baselines and missing comparison to canonical structure learning methods (notably [14]).
- Some key implementation and modeling details are under-specified; limited ablation.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. This is a solid, well-motivated contribution for learning the structure of a particular, useful class of grammars. Strengthening the experimental section—especially by adding comparisons to Stolcke & Omohundro [14] and ablations—would substantially improve the paper.
