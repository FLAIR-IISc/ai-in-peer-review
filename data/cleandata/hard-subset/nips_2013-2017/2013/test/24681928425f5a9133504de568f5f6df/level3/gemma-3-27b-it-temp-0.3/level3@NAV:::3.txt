## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper presents a novel unsupervised approach to learning stochastic And-Or grammars, focusing on inducing unified AND-OR fragments rather than separate composition and reconfiguration searches. The motivation is strong: existing methods often struggle with scalability or require manual specification of grammar structure. The proposed method, leveraging sufficient statistics for efficient fragment valuation, is a technically sound contribution to the field of compositional modeling, with potential applications in areas like event parsing and visual object parsing. The problem addressed is important, and the approach demonstrates originality by unifying the search for compositions and reconfigurations. 

The core idea of learning AND-OR fragments in a unified manner is compelling and addresses a key limitation of previous approaches. The theoretical justification, particularly the efficient computation of fragment value using sufficient statistics, appears solid. The experimental evaluation on event and image parsing tasks provides initial evidence of the method's effectiveness, achieving comparable or better performance than existing techniques. 

However, significant clarity issues hinder a full assessment. While the paper outlines the algorithm, the supplemental derivations are difficult to verify, and clearer explanations are needed. The nature of the “surrogate measure” of likelihood gain is unclear – is it an exact calculation or an approximation, and what are the implications of either? Furthermore, the paper doesn’t explicitly state whether the method is limited to Chomsky Normal Form (CNF) or can handle fragments with arity greater than 2, which impacts its expressiveness.

The most significant weakness lies in the presentation of the experiments. The descriptions of the tasks, data representation, and what was actually *learned* are vague. The paper heavily relies on familiarity with prior work from S.C. Zhu’s group, making it inaccessible to researchers outside that specific lineage.  The results are largely quantitative, lacking illustrative examples of the discovered structure. Including qualitative examples of interesting fragments learned by the algorithm would significantly improve understanding.  It would also be valuable to demonstrate how this approach learns *more interesting* or *different* structures compared to competing methods – simply achieving comparable performance isn't sufficient to justify the increased complexity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and technically sound approach to unsupervised grammar learning.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in compositional modeling.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance on benchmark tasks.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in derivations and explanations.
*   Unclear limitations regarding fragment arity and approximation accuracy.
*   Poorly presented experiments, hindering understanding and assessment.
*   Insufficient qualitative analysis of learned structures.



**Recommendation:** I recommend **rejection** in its current form. While the core idea is strong, the lack of clarity and the inadequate experimental presentation prevent a confident assessment of its contribution. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly with extensive supplementary material detailing the experiments and providing concrete examples, would significantly strengthen the paper.
