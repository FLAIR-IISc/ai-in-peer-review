- Proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars, agnostic to data type (images, events).  
- Introduces a unified formalization of And-Or grammars that represents compositionality (And) and reconfigurability (Or).  
- Core contribution: iterative grammar expansion by adding "And-Or fragments" that jointly learn compositions and reconfigurations via posterior maximization.  
- Formalism is general across domains, addressing prior domain-specific grammar limitations.  
- Builds on and extends prior grammar induction work (Stolcke & Omohundro; Tu & Honavar) and recent image/event grammar methods.  
- Argues previous methods either separate composition/reconfiguration learning or rely on heuristics, which can fail with scarce data.  
- And-Or fragments systematically cover multiple compositions and learn related Or-rules in a unified framework.  
- Technical quality is strong: clear formalization, helpful cross-domain examples (Table 1), and well-motivated algorithm.  
- Derives efficient sufficient statistics to compute posterior gain during expansion, avoiding full re-parsing and reducing computation.  
- Empirical evaluation on human activity videos and image datasets (synthetic and real animal faces) shows competitive or superior performance.  
- Outperforms state-of-the-art image grammar learner (Si & Zhu, 2013) with significant perplexity improvements.  
- Algorithm is efficient in practice (runs within minutes on standard hardware with 5,000 samples) and better suited to data-scarce scenarios.  
- Overstates generality: method applies to a specific class (context-free And-Or grammars) and should acknowledge this limitation more clearly.  
- Experimental gap: lacks comparison to Stolcke & Omohundro (2000) baseline and explicit comparisons showing benefit of unified vs. separate learning of compositions/reconfigurations.  
- Clarity is generally good with clear definitions and examples, though some sufficient-statistics details could be more intuitive; important derivations are in the supplement.  
- Significance: practical and efficient contribution for unsupervised grammar learning across domains, valuable for vision and activity recognition.  
- Originality: moderate—sensible extension of prior work but the unified And-Or fragment approach is novel and well-executed.  
- Recommendation: accept — solid technical contribution and thorough evaluation, with suggested improvements to experimental comparisons and clarity about scope.
