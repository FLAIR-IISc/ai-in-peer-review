This paper proposes an unsupervised method for jointly learning the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs). The key idea is to iteratively introduce And-Or fragments—each combining an And-rule with sibling Or-rules—chosen to maximize posterior gain computed from sufficient statistics (an n-gram tensor and a context matrix) under a Viterbi-likelihood objective and a sparsity prior. The framework is presented as data-agnostic, with experiments on learning event grammars and image grammars showing competitive or superior results to prior AOG learners (e.g., Si & Zhu) and related image/event parsing work. The paper positions AOGs relative to SCFGs, sum-product networks, and earlier structure-learning methods (e.g., Bayesian model merging; Klein & Manning; Tu & Honavar).

Quality: The proposed fragment-based posterior objective is technically interesting and the use of sufficient statistics is clever. However, claims of tractability and applicability to recursive phenomena are not substantiated by an analysis of algorithmic complexity or large-scale experiments. The reliance on Viterbi training and an unambiguity assumption is pragmatic but could bias learning; there is no convergence or approximation-quality discussion. Empirically, the image/event results are solid, but the absence of natural language experiments (e.g., Penn Treebank) and comparisons to established grammar induction baselines (e.g., Klein & Manning 2004; HDP-PCFGs; adaptor grammars) weakens the support for the broader claims.

Clarity: Generally well written and well organized. The learning objective, fragment definition, and sufficient statistics are clearly described. It would help to quantify the beam/greedy search space, report runtime/memory vs. dataset size, and specify how inference scales with grammar size.

Originality: Joint induction of And-Or fragments that unify composition and reconfiguration is a notable and novel contribution relative to separate And/Or searches. The unified formalization across modalities is also valuable.

Significance: If scalable, the approach could impact structure learning in vision, events, and language. As presented, significance is limited by scalability questions and lack of NLP validation.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel fragment-based joint structure learning with principled objective.
- Clear formalization connecting AOGs to SCFGs/SPNs.
- Competitive results on two non-text domains.

Cons against acceptance:
- No complexity analysis; unclear scalability to thousands of sequences/images or longer inputs.
- No natural language experiments; missing comparisons to PCFG induction baselines (e.g., PTB vs. Klein & Manning, Liang et al., adaptor grammars).
- Limited ablations (e.g., And-only vs. Or-only vs. And-Or fragments; beam size sensitivity).

Suggestions: Add complexity and scaling analysis; report wall-clock and memory vs. data size. Include PTB experiments with standard unsupervised baselines. Provide ablations and sensitivity studies, and discuss handling of ambiguity/recursion depth.
