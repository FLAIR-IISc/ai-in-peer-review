This paper presents a novel unsupervised method for learning the structure of stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs), a powerful model for representing compositional and reconfigurable patterns. The core contribution is a unified, bottom-up learning framework that induces "And-Or fragments" rather than performing separate searches for compositional (And-rules) and alternative (Or-rules) structures. This approach is well-motivated, as it aims to be more robust and efficient. The authors derive an efficient method to compute the posterior gain of adding a new fragment using sufficient statistics, making the search tractable. This work builds upon a long line of research in grammar induction for vision and events, a topic of continued interest at NIPS, but distinguishes itself with its unified learning primitive.

The paper has several significant strengths. The problem of unsupervised structure learning is important and challenging. The proposed approach of inducing unified fragments is elegant and novel, providing a principled method to jointly discover compositionality and alternation. The algorithm appears technically sound, and the derivation of an efficient objective for the greedy search is a strong technical contribution.

However, the paper has major weaknesses, primarily concerning clarity and the experimental evaluation. The experiments are extremely difficult to follow for a reader not already deeply familiar with the cited prior work from the same research group. The paper lacks crucial details on data representation, the specific learning process, and what was ultimately learned, making it impossible to fully assess the empirical claims or reproduce the results.

Furthermore, several technical points require clarification. It is unclear whether the "surrogate measure" of likelihood gain is an exact calculation under the Viterbi assumption or a further approximation. The paper should also clarify if the method is restricted to fragments of arity two or can be extended to higher-arity rules. The derivations in the supplement were difficult to verify and would benefit from a clearer exposition.

To be convincing, the paper needs to show not just that it achieves better quantitative scores, but that it learns more meaningful structure. The evaluation is currently missing qualitative examples, such as visualizations of learned grammar fragments or parse trees for sample images and events. These would provide invaluable insight into the model's capabilities.

In summary, while the core idea is strong and the approach is technically interesting, the paper is not ready for acceptance due to the severe lack of clarity in its experimental section. If the authors were to substantially revise this section, provide the requested clarifications, and include compelling qualitative results, it would be a very strong contribution.
