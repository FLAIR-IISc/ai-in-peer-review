- Proposes a unified, bottom-up unsupervised method that induces "And-Or fragments" to learn stochastic AOG structure instead of separate searches for And/Or rules.  
- Derives an efficient computation of posterior gain using sufficient statistics to make the fragment search tractable.  
- Builds on prior grammar induction work but distinguishes itself by the unified learning primitive for jointly discovering compositionality and alternation.  
- Strength: tackles an important, challenging problem with an elegant, novel, and principled approach.  
- Strength: algorithm appears technically sound and the efficient objective for greedy search is a strong technical contribution.  
- Major weakness: experimental section lacks clarity and is hard to follow for readers unfamiliar with the authors' prior work.  
- Major weakness: crucial experimental details are missing (data representation, specific learning process, what was actually learned), preventing assessment or reproducibility.  
- Technical clarifications required: whether the surrogate likelihood gain is exact under the Viterbi assumption or an additional approximation, and whether fragments are limited to arity two or extensible to higher arity.  
- Supplement derivations are difficult to verify and need clearer exposition.  
- Needs qualitative results (visualizations of learned fragments/parse trees) to demonstrate meaningful learned structure, not just improved quantitative scores.  
- Recommendation: not ready for acceptance in current form; would be strong if experimental clarity, requested technical clarifications, and compelling qualitative evaluations are added.
