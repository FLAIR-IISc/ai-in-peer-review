- Proposes an unsupervised method to learn structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs) applicable to images and events.
- Iteratively adds And-Or fragments (one And-node + sibling Or-nodes) to maximize grammar posterior under a sparsity prior.
- Uses Viterbi (max) likelihood with an unambiguity assumption to approximate likelihood and speed learning.
- Computes posterior gain efficiently via sufficient statistics from an n-gram tensor over child choices and a context co-occurrence matrix.
- Claims to unify learning of compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules) and to be more robust/efficient than separate searches.
- Experiments on image and event datasets show comparable or better F-measure and perplexity than prior SAG work.
- Method is positioned relative to And-Or grammar, SPN, and grammar induction literature but focuses on a specific context-free subclass.
- Technical derivations (fragment-level posterior gain, use of sufficient statistics) are sensible and well-motivated.
- The unambiguity assumption is practical but an approximation whose impact is not quantified.
- The claim that And-Or fragments can construct any context-free And-Or grammar is plausible but needs a formal justification or pointer.
- Empirics are positive but baselines are limited; missing comparisons to Bayesian Model Merging, Klein & Manning PCFG, DP-PCFG, and SPN structure learners.
- Missing ablations (And-only/Or-only search, context term, Viterbi vs full likelihood, beam width, hyperparameter sensitivity) hinder attribution of gains.
- Efficiency is promising (minutes for 5k samples), but more detailed complexity and memory analysis of tensor/matrix construction and search is needed.
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized, with useful figures, but some definitions and details are missing or need emphasis.
- Clarify grammar size and coding constants (sa, so), choice and sensitivity of α, and modality-specific context definitions.
- Provide pseudocode/flowchart for the search procedure, termination criterion, and handling/diagnosis of unambiguity violations.
- Improve figure legibility (small fonts in Figs. 1–3).
- Novelty lies in joint And-Or fragment learning and an efficient sufficient-statistics-based posterior-gain objective across modalities.
- The “generalization” claim is overstated; the method is a principled specialization to a tractable subclass.
- Significance: cross-modal, unsupervised grammar learning is impactful and promising for compositional vision and activity understanding.
- Pros: unified modality-agnostic formalization, principled posterior objective, efficient scoring, conceptually clean fragment search, positive cross-domain results.
- Cons: misleading generalization claim, limited baselines, missing ablations, and brief implementation/theoretical details.
- Suggested improvements: add stronger baselines (Stolcke & Omohundro, Klein & Manning, DP-PCFG, SPN learners), run ablations, clarify context and hyperparameter choices, provide a proof sketch about fragment expressivity, report ambiguity rates, and release code/data.
- Overall evaluation: solid, practically relevant contribution with promising results; would be stronger with expanded baselines and ablation studies — reviewer leans positive.
