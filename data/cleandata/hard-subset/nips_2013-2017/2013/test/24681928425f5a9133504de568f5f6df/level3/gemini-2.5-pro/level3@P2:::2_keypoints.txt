- Proposes a unified unsupervised method to learn stochastic And-Or grammars by starting from a memorizing grammar and iteratively adding "And-Or fragments" under a MAP objective (Viterbi likelihood + complexity prior).
- Main contribution is the "And-Or fragment" concept enabling joint induction of compositional (AND) and alternative (OR) rules, avoiding separate error-prone steps.
- Derives a posterior gain for adding fragments using efficiently computable sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor and context matrix), making greedy search practical.
- Claims data-agnostic formulation and validates it on two modalities: temporal event grammars from video and spatial object grammars from images.
- Empirical results show comparable or superior performance to prior specialized methods in the tested domains.
- Strength: original, principled approach with a clear MAP objective and joint learning of AND/OR structure.
- Strength: technical soundness via derivations that connect learning to measurable data properties (tensor coherence).
- Strength: demonstrated generality across different modalities, suggesting wide applicability.
- Strength: paper is clear and well-organized with intuitive explanations and helpful figures.
- Weakness: no experiments on natural language data despite strong motivation and connections to NLP grammar induction literature.
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of computational complexity and scalability; search space may be combinatorial and runtime claims are under-supported.
- Weakness: limited experimental scale (e.g., 61 videos, 320 images); lacks large-scale evaluations.
- Request: explain the decision not to evaluate on canonical natural language parsing tasks (e.g., Penn Treebank) and compare to NLP baselines.
- Request: provide detailed complexity/runtime analysis showing how performance scales with vocabulary size, sequence length, and dataset size.
- Request: analyze sensitivity to initialization and randomness in greedy/beam search (variance across runs).
- Request: discuss limitations of using Viterbi likelihood approximation, especially for highly ambiguous ground-truth grammars.
- Overall: novel and promising framework with strong contributions, but weakened by missing NLP evaluation and limited scalability analysis; addressing these would substantially strengthen the paper.
