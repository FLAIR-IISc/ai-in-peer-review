This paper proposes a novel unsupervised method to learn stochastic And-Or grammars by inducing unified And-Or fragments rather than separate AND/OR searches. The authors provide a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to the type of data being modeled. They also present an algorithm framework for learning both the structure and parameters of such grammars.

The main contribution of this paper is the introduction of And-Or fragments, which unify the search for new compositions and reconfigurations in the grammar. The authors show that by adding only And-Or fragments, their algorithm is still capable of constructing any context-free And-Or grammar. They also propose an efficient method to compute the posterior gain of adding an And-Or fragment, which is based on a set of sufficient statistics of the training data.

The authors evaluate their approach on tasks including event parsing and visual object parsing. They demonstrate that their method is competitive with previous approaches on event parsing and outperforms them on visual object parsing.

The reviewer finds the problem important, the approach novel and well-motivated, and the algorithm technically sound. However, there are several concerns that need to be addressed. Firstly, the reviewer could not verify the supplemental derivations and requests clearer derivations/clarifications. Secondly, it is unclear whether the "surrogate measure" of likelihood gain is exact or an approximation. Thirdly, it is unclear whether the method extends to fragments with arity >2 or is limited to Chomsky normal form.

The major concern is that the experiments are hard to understand—what was done, how examples were represented, and what was learned are not clear. The experimental presentation assumes familiarity with prior work (S. C. Zhu’s group), making it inaccessible to outsiders. If accepted, the paper should include extensive supplementary material with more experimental details and concrete illustrations.

The reviewer would like to see qualitative examples of interesting structure discovered; current results are largely quantitative and not very illuminating. It would be helpful to demonstrate that this approach learns more interesting structure than competing methods.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the algorithm is well-motivated. However, there are some concerns regarding the clarity of the derivations and the experimental presentation.

Clarity: The paper is well-written, but the experimental presentation is not clear. The reviewer had to spend a lot of time trying to understand what was done and how examples were represented.

Originality: The approach is novel and well-motivated. The introduction of And-Or fragments is a significant contribution to the field.

Significance: The problem is important, and the approach has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of grammar learning.
