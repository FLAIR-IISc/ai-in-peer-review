

**Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"**

This paper presents a unified approach for unsupervised learning of both structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars. The authors propose an iterative algorithm that starts from a trivial grammar and adds "And-Or fragments" - unified structures that simultaneously represent compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules) - to optimize the posterior probability of the grammar. This work extends previous approaches to structure learning of natural language grammars [14-16] and image grammars [17, 18] by unifying the learning of And and Or rules in a single framework.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to the type of data being modeled, enabling application to diverse domains like images, events, and potentially natural language. Second, the authors explicitly optimize the posterior probability of the grammar rather than relying on heuristics, with efficient computation of likelihood and prior gains using sufficient statistics. Third, the unified And-Or fragment approach addresses limitations of previous methods that learn And and Or rules separately, which can be error-prone with scarce data.

The empirical evaluation demonstrates the approach's effectiveness on two domains: event grammars (learning temporal structures from human activity videos) and image grammars (modeling spatial structures of animal faces). The results show competitive or better performance compared to previous methods on both synthetic and real datasets. The authors also provide a clear explanation of the learning process through well-designed illustrations.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the technical content accessible. The experimental design is sound for the domains studied, and the results are presented with appropriate metrics (F-measure for event grammars, perplexity for image grammars).

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that limit its impact. Most notably, the authors fail to address algorithmic tractability or scalability. The largest experiment uses 5,000 samples (each with >10 atomic patterns), but the paper does not provide analysis of how the algorithm scales to larger datasets (e.g., 10,000+ samples) or discuss computational complexity. This is particularly concerning for a method claiming to provide "a tractable way to learn stochastic grammars."

The most critical omission is the lack of experiments on natural language data, which is the traditional application domain for stochastic grammars. The paper claims to provide a tractable method for modeling "recursive phenomena (e.g., natural language)" but does not validate this claim on natural language datasets like Penn Treebank. The authors should compare their method to established natural language grammar learning approaches (e.g., Klein & Manning [12]) to demonstrate its value in this important application area.

The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of related work in natural language processing. While it cites some relevant work [8, 9, 14-16], it does not engage with the extensive literature on unsupervised learning of natural language grammars. For instance, the work of Klein & Manning [12] on unsupervised learning of probabilistic context-free grammars would be a natural point of comparison.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Conduct experiments on natural language datasets (Penn Treebank or similar)
2. Provide scalability analysis for larger datasets
3. Include comparisons to relevant natural language grammar learning approaches
4. Discuss the computational complexity of their algorithm in more detail

In terms of review criteria:

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound approach with good empirical results for the domains studied. However, the lack of scalability analysis and natural language experiments limits the strength of the claims.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and the technical content is accessible with good illustrations.

**Originality**: The unified And-Or fragment approach represents a novel combination of techniques for grammar learning, though the core idea of structure learning for grammars is not entirely new.

**Significance**: The work has significance for grammar learning in multiple domains, but the limited scope (no natural language experiments) and lack of scalability analysis reduce its potential impact.

**Pro acceptance arguments**: Novel unified approach to grammar structure learning; good empirical results on two non-trivial domains; clear formalization of And-Or grammars.

**Con acceptance arguments**: No natural language experiments or comparisons; no scalability analysis for larger datasets; limited discussion of related work in natural language processing.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a good approach, but the lack of natural language experiments and scalability analysis prevents it from making a strong case for its claimed generality and tractability. I recommend a weak acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address these limitations in the camera-ready version.
