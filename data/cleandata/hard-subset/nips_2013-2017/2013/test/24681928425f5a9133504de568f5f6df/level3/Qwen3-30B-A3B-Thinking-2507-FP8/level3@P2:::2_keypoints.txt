- Presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and an unsupervised algorithm that jointly optimizes grammar structure and parameters.  
- Introduces "And-Or fragments" to unify learning of compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules), addressing prior separation.  
- Iterative algorithm starts from a trivial grammar and adds fragments that maximize posterior probability using sufficient statistics for efficiency.  
- Evaluated on event grammars (human activity videos) and image grammars (synthetic and real animal faces) with competitive results.  
- Unified formalization across data types (text, images, events) is a significant contribution, increasing applicability.  
- Explicit posterior optimization is a clear technical advance over heuristic-based structure learning methods.  
- And-Or fragment design mitigates data scarcity and fragile search problems, yielding consistent empirical gains.  
- Algorithmic efficiency justified theoretically and empirically (training completes in minutes on 5,000 samples).  
- Presentation is clear with well-chosen examples and a logically structured algorithm.  
- Major weakness: no experiments on natural language data (e.g., Penn Treebank) despite claims about modeling recursive phenomena.  
- Scalability analysis is underdeveloped; no complexity discussion or evaluation on large-scale datasets (e.g., 100k+ samples).  
- Experiments lack ablation studies (e.g., comparing unified fragments vs separate And/Or learning) and deeper failure-case analysis.  
- Novelty is incremental—builds on Zhu’s And-Or grammars and sum-product networks—limiting transformative impact.  
- Recommendation: technically sound but weakened by missing NLP experiments and scalability analysis; reject pending major revisions to address these gaps.
