This paper presents a novel unsupervised method for jointly learning the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars. The core idea is to iteratively build a grammar by adding "And-Or fragments" that unify the learning of compositional (AND-rules) and reconfigurable (OR-rules) structures. This process greedily optimizes the posterior probability of the grammar, balancing data likelihood with a sparsity-inducing prior on grammar size. The work builds upon a rich history of grammar induction, extending ideas from Bayesian model merging (e.g., Stolcke & Omohundro, 1994) to the more general And-Or grammar formalism, which has seen success in computer vision (e.g., Zhu & Mumford, 2006).

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality:** The proposed "And-Or fragment" is a novel and elegant concept for structure learning. By jointly inducing compositions and reconfigurations, the approach appears more robust to data scarcity and ambiguity than methods that learn these components separately.
*   **Quality:** The method is technically sound, based on a clear optimization of the grammar's posterior probability. The experimental results presented on event and image grammar learning are strong, demonstrating competitive or superior performance against previous domain-specific approaches. The paper is well-written and the proposed framework is clearly explained.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Quality & Significance:** The paper's claims of tractability and scalability are not sufficiently supported. While the authors state the algorithm is "reasonably fast," there is no formal analysis of its computational complexity or empirical evaluation of how it scales with dataset size or grammar complexity. This is a critical omission for assessing its practical utility on larger problems.
*   **Significance:** A more significant weakness is the experimental scope. The paper claims to offer a tractable way to learn grammars for recursive phenomena, with natural language being the canonical example. However, it completely omits experiments on standard natural language datasets (e.g., the Penn Treebank). This makes it impossible to compare against a large body of influential prior work in NLP grammar induction, such as Klein & Manning (2004), and significantly weakens the paper's claims of generality.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a promising and technically sound approach, its contributions are undermined by insufficient evaluation.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The unified learning of And-Or fragments is a novel and valuable contribution to the grammar induction literature. The results on vision-related tasks are compelling.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Claims of tractability are unsubstantiated, and the absence of experiments on natural language data makes the claimed generality of the method difficult to evaluate. I would recommend strengthening the paper with a thorough complexity analysis and, crucially, experiments on a standard NLP benchmark before publication.
