Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an unsupervised method to jointly learn the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), within a unified, context-free formalization that is agnostic to data type (images, events, language). Starting from a trivial grammar that exactly reproduces the training set, the method iteratively introduces And-Or fragments—each comprising one And-rule and multiple sibling Or-rules—and greedily selects the fragment that maximizes the posterior (likelihood times a sparsity prior). Likelihood is approximated with Viterbi likelihood; prior penalizes grammar size. To score fragments efficiently, the paper derives sufficient statistics in the form of n-gram tensors (counts of configurations covered by the fragment) and context matrices (counts of configuration-context co-occurrences), yielding closed-form likelihood gains under an unambiguity assumption, as well as optimal Or-rule probabilities. The authors evaluate on two domains—event grammars and image grammars—showing competitive or superior performance to prior work on event modeling [Si et al., ICCV’11] and image grammars [Si & Zhu, PAMI’13], with reported fast runtimes on modest datasets.

The work connects to unsupervised grammar induction in NLP (e.g., Bayesian model merging, Stolcke & Omohundro’94; Klein & Manning’04; Tu & Honavar’08), stochastic scene grammars (Zhao & Zhu, NIPS’11), and sum-product networks (Poon & Domingos, UAI’11). The key novelty is learning And and Or structure jointly via And-Or fragments, which aims to mitigate data sparsity and redundancy issues that arise when And- and Or-structure are induced separately.

Strengths
- Clear modeling contribution: a unified AOG formalization and a principled posterior maximization objective with a sparsity prior.
- Joint structure learning via And-Or fragments is well-motivated and practically useful; the sufficient-statistics-based scoring is elegant and yields closed-form Or-rule estimates.
- Viterbi likelihood and unambiguity regularization are known to help in unsupervised grammar induction; the paper leverages them coherently.
- Empirical evidence on two non-text domains (events and images) shows competitive or improved results vs. strong domain baselines; perplexity and F-measure/KL metrics are appropriate for those settings.
- The framework claims to be data-type agnostic and to model recursive phenomena, which is attractive for broader applicability.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Algorithmic tractability and scalability are not rigorously addressed. The search over And-Or fragments (n-gram tensor construction, sub-tensor search, beam search) lacks complexity analysis. Empirically, datasets are relatively small (61 videos; 320 images). Claims of tractability for recursive grammars and “thousands of datapoints” would be much more convincing with scaling curves (time/memory) and larger benchmarks.
- Lack of natural language experiments. The paper motivates grammar learning for language and claims to model recursion, but provides no experiments on text. Even small-scale PTB experiments (WSJ10, as in Klein & Manning’04), child-directed speech, or synthetic Dyck languages would substantiate the recursion and domain-agnostic claims. Comparisons to classic unsupervised PCFG induction baselines (Klein & Manning’04; EM with PCFG; Bayesian model merging) are missing.
- Reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi likelihood: while practical, these assumptions can bias the method toward unambiguous grammars—potentially problematic for natural language. The paper would benefit from analyzing failure modes when ambiguity is inherent, and from comparing Viterbi vs. full likelihood (or posterior-regularized EM).
- Limited ablations. The paper motivates And-Or fragments vs. And-only or Or-only fragments; an ablation comparing these alternatives (and varying n, beam width, α, encoding costs sa, so) would strengthen the case.
- Some technical details are deferred to the supplement. The definitions of coherence measures, the precise sub-tensor search procedure, and the encoding-size constants (sa, so) deserve more space in the main text for reproducibility.

Quality
- The approach is technically sound overall, with a clear objective and derivations for posterior gain under stated assumptions. However, theoretical guarantees about consistency or bounds on search error are absent, and time/space complexity is not analyzed.
- Empirical support is solid for the chosen domains but is narrow in scope; more extensive experiments (scale, domains, ambiguity) are needed to back the broader claims.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. The learning loop is explained with intuitive figures. To improve: define the coherence measures precisely in the main text; specify search space constraints (n, relation templates), beam width, and stopping criterion; explain the choice and sensitivity of α, sa, so; and quantify “minutes” of runtime with corpus size and hardware.

Originality
- The unification of And/Or induction via And-Or fragments and the sufficient-statistics formulation for scoring is a novel and interesting combination relative to prior AOG and PCFG induction work.

Significance
- If scalable and domain-agnostic, the method could be impactful for structured perception (vision/video) and potentially NLP. As presented, significance is limited by the absence of language experiments and scalability evidence. Demonstrating performance on large datasets and recursive phenomena would substantially elevate impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a complexity analysis and empirical scaling study (e.g., up to 50k–100k samples; vocabulary/terminal growth; n and relation template settings). Include wall-clock and peak memory vs. data and grammar size.
- Add natural language experiments: WSJ10 unsupervised constituency induction (standard train/test), synthetic Dyck languages to probe recursion, and comparisons to Klein & Manning (2004), EM-PCFG, and Bayesian model merging. Report F1/bracketing scores.
- Ablations: And-only, Or-only, and And-Or variants; effect of Viterbi vs. EM; effect of unambiguity regularization; fragment size (n), beam width; impact of α, sa, so.
- Analyze ambiguity: test on data with known ambiguity; report parse multiplicity and how the method copes.
- Clarify and formalize the coherence measures and the sub-tensor search procedure; provide pseudocode in the main text and release code if possible.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually neat joint structure-learning mechanism with principled scoring.
- Promising empirical results on two non-text domains; method is broadly applicable.
- Potential to bridge grammar induction across modalities.

Arguments against acceptance
- No evidence on natural language despite explicit claims; recursion claim remains untested.
- Missing complexity analysis and convincing scalability experiments.
- Limited ablations and sensitivity analyses; reliance on unambiguity may limit applicability.

Overall: a solid and interesting contribution with clear potential, but it needs stronger experimental breadth (especially on natural language) and a more thorough treatment of tractability/scalability to meet the bar.
