This paper presents a novel, unsupervised approach for learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars. The core idea is to iteratively build a compact grammar by adding "And-Or fragments" that best increase the posterior probability of the grammar given the training data. This method unifies the search for compositional structures (And-rules) and reconfigurable alternatives (Or-rules), which previous methods often handled separately. The authors propose a general, data-agnostic formalization of And-Or grammars and derive an efficient method to calculate the posterior gain of adding a candidate fragment using sufficient statistics. The approach is demonstrated on the tasks of learning event grammars from video and image grammars from object datasets.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The problem of unsupervised structure learning for rich, compositional models is a central challenge in machine learning. The proposed approach of learning unified And-Or fragments is a novel and elegant contribution to this area. It addresses a key limitation of prior work, where separate learning of compositions and alternatives can be inefficient and error-prone, especially with sparse data. The formulation within a principled Bayesian framework that optimizes the grammar's posterior probability is also a significant strength.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The algorithm appears technically sound. A key contribution is the derivation of an efficient objective function for scoring candidate fragments. Formulating the likelihood gain in terms of sufficient statistics (the n-gram tensor and context matrix) makes the greedy/beam search procedure computationally feasible. The unified formalization of And-Or grammars is clear and helps position the work as a general-purpose structure learning algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Clarity of Technical Details:** While the high-level idea is clear, some crucial technical details are ambiguous.
    *   The paper states that the Viterbi likelihood is used and that the posterior gain calculation is made efficient "by assuming grammar unambiguity". It is unclear whether the resulting "likelihood gain" formula is an exact calculation of the Viterbi likelihood gain under this assumption, or if it is a further approximation or "surrogate measure". This distinction is important for understanding the precise nature of the objective function being optimized. A more detailed derivation in the supplementary material would be beneficial.
    *   The paper does not specify whether the learned And-rules are restricted to a specific arity (e.g., binary, as in Chomsky Normal Form). This is an important detail regarding the expressive power of the learned grammars and the scope of the search procedure.

2.  **Experimental Evaluation and Presentation (Major Concern):** The experimental sections are very difficult for an outside reader to understand and evaluate. The presentation seems to assume significant prior familiarity with the authors' previous work and the specific datasets used.
    *   **Lack of Detail:** The descriptions of data preprocessing and representation are insufficient. For instance, in the event grammar experiments, the process of converting videos into sequences of IDs or vectors is described too briefly to be fully understood. Similarly, in the image grammar experiments, the quantization process and the nature of the "atomic patterns" are not explained adequately.
    *   **Lack of Qualitative Results:** The evaluation is almost exclusively quantitative (F-measure, perplexity). While the results show that the proposed method outperforms baselines, they provide very little insight into the quality of the learned structures. The paper would be significantly more impactful if it included qualitative examples. For instance, visualizing a few learned And-Or fragments for a "making a phone call" event or a "cat face" would make the contribution much more tangible and convincing. Showing example parse trees for test images/videos would also help demonstrate what the model is capturing.
    *   **Accessibility:** The reliance on concepts and methods from prior work (e.g., [17], [18]) without sufficient explanation makes the experimental validation inaccessible to a broader audience. A strong paper should be largely self-contained.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

To strengthen the paper, I strongly recommend the authors:
*   Clarify the derivation of the likelihood gain and its relationship to the true Viterbi likelihood.
*   Explicitly state any constraints on the structure of the learned grammar rules (e.g., arity).
*   Thoroughly revise the experimental sections to be self-contained. Provide clear, step-by-step descriptions of the data representation and experimental setup.
*   Most importantly, include qualitative results that showcase the learned structures. Visualizations of grammar rules and example parses would provide crucial evidence that the model is learning meaningful, interpretable compositional representations, which is a primary motivation for using grammars. These could be included in supplementary material if space is an issue.

In summary, the paper introduces a well-motivated and technically interesting approach to a significant problem. However, its potential impact is currently hindered by a lack of clarity in some technical aspects and, more critically, by an experimental section that is difficult to understand and fails to provide qualitative insight into the learned models. Addressing these issues, particularly by adding concrete examples of the learned structures, would make this a much stronger contribution.
