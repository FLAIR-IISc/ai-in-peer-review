

This paper proposes a unified approach for unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, which are powerful models for representing compositionality and reconfigurability in various data types. The authors present a formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to the specific data domain, and introduce an iterative structure learning algorithm that simultaneously learns grammar structure and parameters by optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar. Starting from a trivial initial grammar, the approach incrementally inserts new And-Or fragments (unifying composition and reconfiguration learning) and updates grammar rules to maximize the posterior probability.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides a general formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that can model diverse data types (text, images, events), extending previous work on image grammars [1,3] and event grammars [7]. Second, the authors explicitly optimize the posterior probability of both structure and parameters, rather than relying on heuristics as in some prior approaches. Third, by learning compositions and reconfigurations in a unified manner through And-Or fragments, the method avoids the limitations of previous approaches that learn these aspects separately, leading to more efficient and robust grammar learning. Finally, the empirical evaluation demonstrates competitive or superior performance on both event grammars (using activity recognition datasets) and image grammars (using synthetic and real animal face datasets) compared to existing methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough explanation of the grammatical formalism and learning algorithm. The key technical contribution lies in the formulation of likelihood and prior gains for And-Or fragments, which enables efficient greedy search for optimal grammar extensions. The derivation of sufficient statistics for efficient computation of these gains is particularly strong, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the approach's effectiveness.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. Most significantly, the authors do not address algorithmic tractability or scalability, which is crucial for real-world applications. The paper states that their implementation runs "within a few minutes on a desktop with 5000 training samples," but this is insufficient for many practical applications where datasets may contain thousands or millions of samples. The computational complexity of the algorithm is not analyzed, and there is no discussion of how the method would scale to larger datasets or more complex grammars.

Perhaps the most serious omission is the lack of experiments on natural language data. Given that stochastic grammars have traditionally been used for natural language processing, the authors should have evaluated their approach on standard NLP benchmarks like the Penn Treebank. The paper cites Klein & Manning [12] as prior work on unsupervised grammar learning, but fails to compare with their approach or apply the method to natural language data. This omission is particularly striking given the authors' claim that their formalization is "agnostic to the type of the data being modeled."

The paper also lacks adequate comparison to recent work on grammatical inference. While the authors compare to [17,18] for event grammars and [17] for image grammars, they do not discuss how their approach relates to or improves upon the significant body of work on unsupervised grammar learning in natural language processing. For example, the paper would benefit from comparing with the work of Tu & Honavar [16] or Stolcke & Omohundro [14], which are directly relevant to the problem.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the lack of natural language experiments and scalability analysis. While the approach is promising for event and image grammars, the authors overstate the potential for broader applicability to "recursive phenomena" like natural language without providing evidence. The paper's claim that "stochastic And-Or grammars are powerful models of patterns that combine compositionality and reconfigurability" is well-supported for images and events, but not for natural language.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Include experiments on natural language data (e.g., Penn Treebank) and compare with standard NLP grammar learning approaches.
2. Provide a more detailed analysis of computational complexity and scalability.
3. Discuss how the method relates to and improves upon existing grammar learning work in natural language processing.

Pro: Unified formalization of And-Or grammars, general to multiple data types.
Pro: Explicit optimization of posterior probability, rather than heuristic approaches.
Pro: Unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments.
Pro: Strong empirical results on event and image grammars.

Con: No experiments on natural language data despite claims of generality.
Con: Inadequate discussion of scalability and computational complexity.
Con: Limited comparison to relevant prior work in natural language processing.
Con: Overstated claims about applicability to natural language without evidence.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a novel approach to grammar learning that addresses important limitations of previous work. The method is well-motivated and the experimental results are promising for the specific domains evaluated. However, the lack of natural language experiments and scalability analysis significantly limits the paper's significance and broader impact. The paper would be substantially stronger with these additions, particularly given NIPS' focus on fundamental advances in machine learning. I recommend rejection due to these critical omissions, though the work itself is promising and could be substantially improved for future submission.
