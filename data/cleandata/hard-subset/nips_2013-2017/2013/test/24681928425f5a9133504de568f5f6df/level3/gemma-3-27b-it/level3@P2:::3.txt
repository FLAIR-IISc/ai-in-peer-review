This paper presents a novel unsupervised approach to learning Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs), a powerful framework for representing compositional and reconfigurable structures in data. The authors address the challenging problem of simultaneously learning both the structure and parameters of SAGs, moving beyond previous approaches that often fix the grammar structure or learn compositions and reconfigurations separately. The key contribution lies in the introduction of “And-Or fragments” – a unified mechanism for inducing both compositional and reconfigurable elements, coupled with an efficient algorithm for optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar based on sufficient statistics. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and tackles an important problem with potential applications across various domains.

The paper effectively summarizes prior work in stochastic grammars, referencing contributions from natural language processing (NLP), image modeling, and event recognition. It correctly positions SAGs as a generalization of stochastic context-free grammars and sum-product networks. The formulation of the problem, as maximizing the posterior probability of the grammar given the data, is standard and appropriate. The proposed algorithm builds upon existing structure learning techniques in NLP but significantly extends them to handle the unified And-Or fragment learning, addressing a crucial limitation of previous work.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The concept of learning And-Or fragments in a unified manner is a significant contribution. It offers a principled approach to balance compositionality and reconfigurability during grammar induction.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed algorithm appears technically well-grounded. The use of sufficient statistics to efficiently compute the posterior gain is a clever optimization. The connection to tensor/matrix coherence measures provides a useful theoretical interpretation.
* **Problem Importance:** Learning compositional models from unannotated data is a core challenge in machine learning. SAGs offer a promising framework for tackling this problem, and the unsupervised learning setting is particularly valuable.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches and convincingly demonstrates the advantages of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Derivation Clarity:** The derivations in the supplementary material regarding the likelihood gain calculation are difficult to follow. Providing more detailed and step-by-step derivations would significantly improve the paper's accessibility. It’s unclear whether the presented “surrogate measure” is an exact computation or an approximation – this needs clarification.
* **Scalability & Generalization:** The paper doesn't explicitly address the scalability of the approach to grammars with higher arity (fragments with more than two children). Is the method limited to Chomsky Normal Form-like structures? A discussion of these limitations would be beneficial.
* **Experimental Presentation:** The experimental section is the weakest aspect of the paper. The descriptions are too concise and assume a strong prior understanding of the research group’s previous work (specifically, S.-C. Zhu’s group). This makes it difficult for reviewers unfamiliar with this specific literature to fully appreciate the experimental setup and results. What exactly was *learned* in each experiment isn’t adequately explained.
* **Lack of Qualitative Results:** While quantitative results are presented, the paper lacks compelling qualitative examples of the structures discovered by the algorithm. Showing examples of the learned grammars, perhaps visualized in a simplified form, would greatly enhance the paper’s impact and provide valuable insights.
* **Comparative Structure Analysis:** The paper demonstrates competitive performance, but doesn’t convincingly show that the *structure* learned by the proposed method is qualitatively superior to that of competing approaches. Demonstrating this aspect would strengthen the claim of novelty.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach to a challenging problem.
* The proposed algorithm is efficient and well-motivated.
* The experimental results, while requiring better presentation, demonstrate competitive performance.
* The work has the potential to significantly advance the field of compositional modeling.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The experimental section is difficult to understand without prior knowledge of the relevant literature.
* The derivations are not clearly presented and require further clarification.
* The lack of qualitative results and comparative structure analysis weakens the overall impact of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to NIPS. The core idea is strong and the technical details appear solid. However, the current presentation, particularly the experimental section, needs significant improvement. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with major revisions**. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Expand the Experimental Section:** Provide detailed descriptions of the experimental setup, data representation, and learned models. Include concrete examples of the input data and the resulting learned grammar structures.
2.  **Clarify Derivations:** Provide clearer and more detailed derivations of the key equations, especially regarding the likelihood gain calculation.
3.  **Provide Qualitative Results:** Include visualizations of the learned grammars and examples of how they are used to parse data.
4.  **Address Scalability and Generalization:** Discuss the limitations of the approach regarding grammar arity and potential extensions to more complex structures.
5.  **Provide extensive supplementary material** detailing experimental configurations and learned structures.



If these revisions are addressed, I am confident that this paper will be a strong addition to the NIPS proceedings.
