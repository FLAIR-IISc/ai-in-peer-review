- Addresses unsupervised structure learning for stochastic And-Or grammars with a domain-agnostic approach that learns both structure and parameters from unannotated data.
- Proposes an iterative bottom-up algorithm starting from a trivial grammar and progressively adding new grammar fragments to maximize posterior probability.
- Introduces "And-Or fragments" that unify learning of compositional (And) and alternative (Or) structures.
- Derives an efficient posterior-gain computation for candidate fragments using sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices).
- Uses greedy or beam search over fragments enabled by the efficient gain computation.
- Evaluated on event grammar learning from videos and image grammar learning on synthetic and real datasets, showing comparable or superior performance to recent specialized methods.
- Technically sound with a principled posterior objective that includes a grammar-size penalty to encourage compactness and generalization.
- The posterior-gain derivation is a key strength and appears correct, enabling efficient search.
- Writing and organization are clear; Figure 1 effectively illustrates the bottom-up process.
- Positioning relative to prior work could be clearer: the method is a powerful specialization to And-Or grammars rather than a broad generalization of Bayesian model merging.
- Original contribution is the unified And-Or fragment learning and the tensor/matrix-based formulation for gain, which is novel and insightful.
- Significant because restricting search to specific fragments makes the problem tractable while retaining strong empirical performance and domain-agnostic applicability.
- Experimental evaluation is solid but could be strengthened by comparing to more general, classic grammar induction methods.
- Recommended to include a baseline comparison to Bayesian model merging (Stolcke & Omohundro) to better contextualize trade-offs between specialization and general induction.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-motivated algorithm; unified fragment idea addresses data sparsity; principled objective and efficient derivation; strong empirical results across tasks.
- Arguments against acceptance: overstated positioning as a generalization of prior work; lacking comparison to classic, more general grammar induction baselines.
