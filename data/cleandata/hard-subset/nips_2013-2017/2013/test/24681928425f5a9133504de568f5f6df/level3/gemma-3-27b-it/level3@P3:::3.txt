This paper proposes a novel unsupervised approach to learning stochastic And-Or grammars, addressing a challenging problem in structured prediction. The core idea of inducing unified AND-OR fragments, rather than searching for AND and OR structures separately, is a significant contribution. The theoretical motivation, particularly the efficient computation of fragment values using sufficient statistics, is strong and well-aligned with prior work in probabilistic grammar induction (e.g., Stolcke & Omohundro, 1994; Klein & Manning, 2004). The problem is important, as stochastic And-Or grammars provide a powerful framework for modeling compositional and reconfigurable data, demonstrated in applications like event parsing and visual object parsing (Zhu et al., 2006; Ivanov & Bobick, 2000). The approach appears technically sound, building on existing techniques while introducing a unifying framework.

However, despite the strong theoretical foundation and promising approach, several critical issues hinder a clear assessment of the paper's contribution and its readiness for publication. My primary concern revolves around the lack of clarity in the experimental presentation. While the authors state they achieve “comparable or better performance,” it's remarkably difficult to understand *what* was done, *how* the examples were represented, and *what* specifically the method learned. The presentation heavily relies on prior work from S.C. Zhu’s group, making it inaccessible to reviewers unfamiliar with that specific lineage of research. 

Several specific points require clarification. First, the nature of the “surrogate measure” of likelihood gain is unclear. Is it an exact calculation, or an approximation? The paper claims efficiency, but the degree of approximation (if any) should be explicitly stated. Second, the authors should clarify whether their method extends to fragments with arity >2 (more than two child nodes in an AND-rule). Is it inherently limited to Chomsky Normal Form, or can it handle more complex structures? This limitation, if present, should be acknowledged and discussed. 

Furthermore, the supplemental derivations, while mentioned as being provided, were not easily verifiable, necessitating clearer and more detailed explanations. A step-by-step breakdown of the crucial equations would significantly improve understanding. 

Crucially, the results are overwhelmingly quantitative. While tables of F-measures and perplexity are provided, they lack illustrative examples. Qualitative examples of interesting structures discovered by the algorithm – visualizations of learned grammars for both event and image parsing – are sorely missing.  Showing *what* the learned grammar represents would provide invaluable insight into the method's effectiveness.  Finally, a direct comparison demonstrating that this approach learns *more interesting* or *better generalizing* structure than competing methods (beyond simply achieving slightly better numbers) is needed. For instance, could the learned grammars be used to successfully parse novel examples not seen during training?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The unified AND-OR fragment approach is a novel and potentially significant contribution to unsupervised grammar learning.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical foundations appear solid, and the proposed method builds logically on existing work.
*   **Importance:** The problem of unsupervised structure learning for stochastic And-Or grammars is relevant and has applications in various domains.
*   **Promising Results:** The quantitative results suggest that the approach is competitive with existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper lacks clarity in its experimental presentation, making it difficult to assess the results.
*   **Accessibility:** The reliance on prior work from a specific research group hinders understanding for reviewers unfamiliar with that work.
*   **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The absence of qualitative examples limits the interpretability of the results.
*   **Unclear Approximations:** The exactness of the likelihood gain measure is not clearly stated.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising approach to unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars. However, the lack of clarity in the experimental presentation and the absence of qualitative analysis significantly detract from its impact. If accepted, the authors *must* include extensive supplementary material detailing the experimental setup, concrete illustrations of learned structures, and a thorough discussion of the surrogate likelihood measure.  Demonstrating the method's ability to learn genuinely insightful structures and generalizing beyond the training set would strengthen the paper substantially. Currently, the paper falls short of NIPS standards due to these deficiencies, but possesses the potential to be a valuable contribution with significant revisions.
