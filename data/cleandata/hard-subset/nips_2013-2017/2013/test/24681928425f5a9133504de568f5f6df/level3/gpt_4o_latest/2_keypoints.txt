- Summary: Proposes an unsupervised, data-type-agnostic method to learn structure and parameters of stochastic AND-OR grammars by iteratively adding fragments and optimizing posterior; evaluated on event and image grammars with strong results.

- Strength 1 (Unified Framework): Formalizes stochastic AND-OR grammars independently of data type, enabling broad applicability across domains.

- Strength 2 (Efficient Learning): Iterative fragment addition unifies learning of compositions and reconfigurations, improving robustness to data scarcity and ambiguity.

- Strength 3 (Empirical Validation): Competitive on event grammars and significantly outperforms prior work on image grammars (improved F-measure, KL, perplexity); experiments are well-designed.

- Strength 4 (Theoretical Contribution): Derives sufficient statistics for efficient computation of posterior gains, reducing computational overhead.

- Weakness 1 (Scalability): Scalability to large, real-world datasets is not evaluated and remains unclear.

- Weakness 2 (Limited Scope): Experiments are limited to events and images; no natural language evaluation to support generality claims.

- Weakness 3 (Clarity): Some technical derivations (likelihood and prior gains) need clearer exposition or additional examples.

- Pro Argument: Tackles an important unsupervised grammar-learning problem with a novel, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated approach.

- Pro Argument: The unified framework and efficient algorithm represent a meaningful advance over prior methods.

- Con Argument: Lack of natural language experiments weakens the paper's claim of general applicability.

- Con Argument: Absence of discussion on scalability raises concerns about practical deployment on large datasets.

- Recommendation: Recommend acceptance with minor revisions contingent on clarifying scalability concerns and expanding discussion/applicability to natural language.
