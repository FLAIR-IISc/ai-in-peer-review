- Proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments that capture composition and reconfiguration.

- Formalizes a context-free subclass of AOGs and optimizes a sparsity-regularized Viterbi posterior.

- Key technical contribution: a closed-form posterior-gain criterion (under an unambiguity assumption) that factors into coherence measures of an n-gram tensor and a context matrix, enabling efficient greedy/beam search.

- Empirically learns event and image grammars and outperforms prior And-Or grammar learners in several settings.

- Related to PCFG structure learning and model-merging (e.g., Stolcke & Omohundro), unsupervised grammar induction, and sum-product networks; the method is actually a restricted special case rather than a full generalization.

- Algorithm is sensible and well-motivated; posterior-gain derivation is clear given assumptions, but Viterbi training and unambiguity bias introduce approximation error that should be discussed.

- Experiments are promising but would benefit from stronger baselines and ablation studies.

- Paper is generally clear and organized; needs more pseudocode and detail on search/complexity, fragment parameterization, and the role of hyperparameters (α, sa, so) for reproducibility.

- Originality: unifying And/Or search into a single fragment with an efficiently computable posterior gain is a nice idea, though conceptually related to prior model-merging/fragment induction.

- Significance: solid contribution for structure learning in a practical class of grammars with positive multimodal results; broader impact would be clearer with additional baselines and domains.

- Suggested additions: comparisons to Stolcke & Omohundro (1994), PCFG/HDP-PCFG induction, EM/Viterbi PCFG learners; ablations (And-only, Or-only, And-Or), sensitivity to α/beam width, and variance/confidence intervals.

- Also suggested: report parsing speed and training time, and clarify the unambiguity assumption and potential failure modes.

- Arguments for acceptance: tractable, sensible algorithm with a clear objective; unifies composition and reconfiguration; good empirical performance across modalities.

- Arguments against acceptance: overstated generalization claim, missing key baselines and ablations, reliance on Viterbi/unambiguity without robustness analysis.

- Overall recommendation: weak accept.
