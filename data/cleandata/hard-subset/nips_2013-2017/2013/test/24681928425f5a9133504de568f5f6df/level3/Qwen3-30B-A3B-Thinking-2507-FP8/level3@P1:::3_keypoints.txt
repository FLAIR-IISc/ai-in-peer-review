- Proposes an unsupervised method to learn structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars, agnostic to data type (images, events).
- Main contribution: unified "And-Or fragments" search that jointly covers compositions and reconfigurations instead of learning them separately.
- The approach is grounded in posterior optimization; likelihood gain computed efficiently via sufficient statistics.
- Algorithm: start from trivial grammar and iteratively add And-Or fragments that maximize posterior probability.
- Demonstrated on event grammars (human activity datasets) and image grammars (synthetic and real animal faces).
- Empirical results comparable or better than prior methods, especially on complex datasets where multiple relations are learned simultaneously.
- Strength: well-motivated problem for modeling compositional and reconfigurable patterns across domains.
- Strength: technically sound with clear theoretical foundation for posterior optimization.
- Strength: novel unified fragment search that avoids separate composition/reconfiguration limitations.
- Weakness: derivation of likelihood gain (Eq. 3) and surrogate vs. exact likelihood relationship is unclear—uncertain if factorization is exact or approximate.
- Weakness: unclear scope regarding arity—claim of constructing any context-free grammar vs. possible limitation to Chomsky normal form (arity ≤2).
- Weakness: experimental section assumes familiarity with Zhu's group's representations (e.g., "atomic actions," "quantized images"), making experiments hard to follow for outsiders.
- Weakness: lacks qualitative examples of learned grammars; results are mainly quantitative (F-measure, perplexity).
- Significance: moderate—interesting unified approach but incremental in probabilistic modeling; interpretability/usefulness of learned grammars not clearly demonstrated.
- Suggested improvements: clarify likelihood derivations and approximations; specify scope re: arity; provide accessible experimental descriptions; add qualitative grammar examples.
- Overall: technically promising with good empirical results but requires clarifications and additional material before strong acceptance.
