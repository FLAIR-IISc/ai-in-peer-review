

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper presents a method for unsupervised learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, which are powerful models for representing compositionality and reconfigurability in data. The authors propose an iterative approach that starts from a trivial grammar and gradually adds And-Or fragments to optimize the posterior probability of the grammar. The key innovation lies in the unified learning of both compositions (via And-nodes) and reconfigurations (via Or-nodes) through a single type of grammar fragment (And-Or fragments), rather than handling them separately as previous approaches did.

The paper builds on prior work in stochastic grammars for natural language processing [8, 9] and sum-product networks [10], as well as recent work on And-Or grammars for images [17] and events [18]. While the authors claim to "generalize" previous structure learning work, their approach is actually a special case of more general structure learning methods, specifically restricting to a particular class of grammar fragments that makes the problem tractable. This restriction is reasonable and necessary for practical learning, as the authors correctly note that learning general grammars would be computationally intractable.

The paper's main strengths include:
- A clear formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type (text, images, events)
- A sensible algorithm that efficiently computes the posterior gain of adding grammar fragments using sufficient statistics
- Strong empirical results on both event grammars (from human activity videos) and image grammars (from animal face sketches), showing competitive or better performance than prior approaches
- The unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations that avoids the pitfalls of separate learning approaches

The paper's main weakness is its limited experimental evaluation. While the authors compare to [17, 18] for image and event grammars, they do not include a comparison to the seminal work by Stolcke and Omohundro [14] on probabilistic grammar induction, which would provide a valuable baseline for structure learning. The authors should have included this comparison as it represents a fundamental approach to grammar induction that predates their work and establishes a standard for the field.

The quality of the paper is high. The technical approach is sound, with clear theoretical derivations of the likelihood and prior gains. The empirical evaluation is well-designed for the specific tasks (event and image grammars), though it would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the restriction to context-free grammars and the need for a sparsity prior.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The formalization of And-Or grammars is presented logically, and the algorithm is explained with helpful figures (Figure 1 and Figure 2). The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproduction.

The originality is moderate. While the unified learning of compositions and reconfigurations via And-Or fragments is a sensible contribution, the authors do not adequately distinguish their work from existing grammar induction approaches. The claim that their method "generalizes" previous work is misleading, as they are actually restricting to a special case that makes the problem tractable. The paper would benefit from a clearer statement of how their approach differs from previous structure learning methods, particularly Stolcke and Omohundro [14].

The significance of the work is moderate. The method provides a practical solution for learning a specific class of grammars that can be applied to different data types (images, events), but the restricted class of grammars limits its applicability to more complex structures. The unified learning approach is a useful contribution to the field of grammar induction, but it does not represent a breakthrough in the broader context of unsupervised structure learning.

In summary, this paper makes a solid contribution to the specific problem of learning the structure of stochastic And-Or grammars. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and performs well on the evaluated tasks. The restriction to a tractable special case is reasonable, and the unified learning approach is a valuable improvement over previous methods that handled compositions and reconfigurations separately.

**Recommendation:** Accept. While the paper's claim of "generalization" is overstated, the approach is a valuable contribution to the specific problem it addresses. The experimental evaluation could be strengthened with comparisons to Stolcke and Omohundro [14], but the current results are sufficient to demonstrate the method's effectiveness. This paper would be a useful addition to the NIPS proceedings as it provides a clear, practical approach to an important problem in unsupervised structure learning.
