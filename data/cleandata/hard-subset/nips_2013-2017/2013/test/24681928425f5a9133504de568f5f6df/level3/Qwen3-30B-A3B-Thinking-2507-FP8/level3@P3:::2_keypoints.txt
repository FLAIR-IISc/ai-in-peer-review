- Presents a unified iterative algorithm to unsupervisedly learn both structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars by adding And-Or fragments to optimize posterior probability.  
- Formalizes stochastic And-Or grammars in a data-agnostic way, enabling application to images, events, and potentially language.  
- Explicitly optimizes posterior probability using efficient likelihood and prior gain computations with sufficient statistics.  
- And-Or fragment representation unifies learning of composition (And) and reconfiguration (Or), addressing limitations of separate learning approaches.  
- Empirical evaluation on event grammars (human activity videos) and image grammars (animal faces) shows competitive or superior performance on synthetic and real datasets.  
- Experiments use appropriate metrics (F-measure for events, perplexity for images) and provide clear illustrative explanations of the learning process.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes technical content accessible.  
- Novelty: unified And-Or fragment approach is a meaningful combination of existing grammar-learning ideas.  
- Main weakness: lacks analysis of algorithmic tractability and scalability to larger datasets (no complexity analysis or experiments beyond 5,000 samples).  
- Critical omission: no experiments on natural language data despite claims about modeling recursive phenomena like language.  
- Missing comparisons to established unsupervised grammar learning methods in NLP (e.g., Klein & Manning) and insufficient discussion of relevant NLP literature.  
- Recommendations to authors: add natural language experiments (e.g., Penn Treebank), provide scalability analysis, compare to NLP grammar-learning baselines, and detail computational complexity.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound with good domain-specific empirical results, but limited claims by missing scalability and language validation.  
- Significance: promising cross-domain potential, but impact diminished by lack of NLP validation and scalability evidence.  
- Pro arguments: novel unified approach, solid empirical results in two domains, clear formalization.  
- Con arguments: no NLP experiments/comparisons, no scalability analysis, limited NLP-related discussion.  
- Overall recommendation: weak accept, contingent on addressing scalability and natural language evaluation in the final version.
