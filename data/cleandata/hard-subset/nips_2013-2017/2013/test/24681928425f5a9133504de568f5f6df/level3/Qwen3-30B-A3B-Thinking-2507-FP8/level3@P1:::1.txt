

This paper proposes an unsupervised approach to learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars, which represent compositionality and reconfigurability in data patterns. The authors present a unified formalization of these grammars that is agnostic to the data type (e.g., images, events), addressing a limitation in previous work where grammars were often restricted to specific domains. The core contribution is a method that learns grammatical structure by iteratively adding "And-Or fragments" to an initial trivial grammar, which unifies the learning of compositions (via And-rules) and reconfigurations (via Or-rules) in a single optimization framework that maximizes the posterior probability of the grammar.

The paper builds on previous work on structure learning of grammars, particularly extending the natural language grammar induction approaches of Stolcke and Omohundro (2000) and Tu and Honavar (2008), while improving upon recent applications to images and events. The authors make a compelling argument that previous approaches either learn compositions and reconfigurations separately (which can be error-prone with scarce data) or rely on heuristics rather than optimizing the posterior probability. Their proposed And-Or fragments address these issues by systematically covering multiple compositions and learning related Or-rules in a unified manner.

The technical quality of the paper is strong. The formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars is clear and general, with Table 1 providing excellent examples across different domains. The algorithmic approach is well-motivated, with the authors deriving efficient sufficient statistics for computing the posterior gain during grammar expansion, which avoids the computational burden of full re-parsing. The experimental evaluation on event grammars (using human activity videos) and image grammars (using synthetic and real animal face datasets) demonstrates competitive or superior performance compared to prior approaches. Notably, the method outperforms the state-of-the-art image grammar learning approach (Si and Zhu, 2013) on both synthetic and real datasets, with significant improvements in perplexity.

The paper's main strength lies in its unified approach to structure learning, which provides a more robust and efficient way to learn grammars compared to previous methods that handle compositions and reconfigurations separately. The algorithm's efficiency is demonstrated by the implementation running within minutes on standard hardware with 5,000 training samples. The paper also makes a good case for why their approach is more suitable for data-scarce scenarios, as the unified learning of And-Or fragments reduces the risk of learning incomplete grammars.

However, the paper has some notable weaknesses. The claim of "generalizing" previous structure learning work is somewhat overstated - the method actually applies to a specific class of grammars (context-free And-Or grammars) rather than providing a general solution to structure learning. While this restriction makes the problem tractable, the paper should more clearly acknowledge this limitation. Additionally, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including comparisons to Stolcke and Omohundro (2000), which represents a fundamental baseline in unsupervised grammar induction that the authors cite but do not compare against. The paper's claim that their method "unifies the search for new compositions and reconfigurations" would be more convincing with explicit comparisons showing how this approach outperforms separate learning of compositions and reconfigurations.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with clear definitions, well-structured sections, and helpful examples (Figures 1-2). The writing is accessible to NIPS readers familiar with grammatical models, though some of the technical details about the sufficient statistics could be made more intuitive. The supplementary material appears to contain important derivations that would strengthen the main paper.

In terms of significance, the work makes a solid contribution to the field of grammatical models, providing a practical and efficient approach for learning the structure of And-Or grammars across different data types. The ability to learn from unannotated data is particularly valuable for applications in computer vision and activity recognition, where manual grammar specification is often infeasible. While the approach is limited to context-free grammars, the authors provide a clear and useful solution to a well-defined problem.

The paper meets NIPS quality standards with sound technical content and thorough evaluation. The originality is moderate - it's a sensible extension of previous work rather than a radical new concept - but the unified approach to structure learning is novel and well-executed. The significance is demonstrated through empirical results that show clear improvements over existing methods in two different domains.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that advances the state of the art in unsupervised structure learning for grammatical models. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful problem with practical applications. While the experimental evaluation could be enhanced by comparing to the Stolcke and Omohundro baseline, the results presented are sufficient to demonstrate the approach's value. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the work is a solid contribution to a specific but important class of grammatical models.
