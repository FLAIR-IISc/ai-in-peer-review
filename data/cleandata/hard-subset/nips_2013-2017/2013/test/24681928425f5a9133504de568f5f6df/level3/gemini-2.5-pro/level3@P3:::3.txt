### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel, unsupervised method for learning the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars. The work builds upon previous research in grammar induction, particularly from the computer vision community (e.g., Zhu et al.), but proposes a significant departure from prior techniques. The central contribution is a bottom-up learning algorithm that induces unified "And-Or fragments" rather than searching for compositional And-rules and reconfigurable Or-rules in separate, potentially error-prone steps. The algorithm starts with a trivial grammar that overfits the training data and iteratively introduces new fragments to maximize the grammar's posterior probability, which combines a Viterbi likelihood term with a sparsity prior on grammar size. A key technical contribution is an efficient method for computing the posterior gain of adding a fragment by using a set of pre-computed sufficient statistics. The authors demonstrate the versatility of their domain-agnostic approach by applying it to two different tasks: learning event grammars from human activity videos and learning image grammars from animal face datasets.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The problem of unsupervised structure learning for compositional models is highly important and challenging. The paper's core idea of learning unified And-Or fragments is novel and well-motivated. It provides an elegant solution to the data scarcity and search complexity issues that arise when attempting to learn compositional and alternative structures separately. This unified approach represents a significant conceptual advance over prior work.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proposed framework is principled, framed as a search to optimize the grammar's posterior probability. The algorithm appears technically sound, and the derivation of an efficient method to calculate the posterior gain for a candidate fragment is a strong contribution that makes the greedy search procedure practical.

3.  **Broad Applicability:** The formalization of the And-Or grammar is general and agnostic to the data modality. The successful application to both temporal event parsing and spatial image parsing effectively demonstrates the potential breadth and impact of this work.

**Weaknesses (Reasons for Rejection / Areas for Improvement):**

1.  **Clarity and Reproducibility of Experiments (Major Concern):** The primary weakness of this paper is the experimental section. It is written in a way that is largely inaccessible to readers who are not already deeply familiar with the specific formalisms and experimental setups of prior work from the authors' group (e.g., [17], [18]). Key details about data representation, the definition of relations, and the search process are omitted. For instance:
    *   In the event grammar experiments, it is unclear how videos are converted into sequences of binary vectors or how the "co-occurring" and "followed-by" relations are concretely defined and used to propose candidate fragments.
    *   In the image grammar experiments, the description of image quantization, the nature of the "atomic patterns," and how "relative positions" are encoded as relations is too brief to allow for replication.

2.  **Lack of Qualitative Insight:** The experimental results are presented almost exclusively through quantitative metrics like F-measure and perplexity. While these show the method performs well, they offer very little insight into the quality or interpretability of the structures being learned. The paper would be far more compelling if it included visualizations of the learned grammar rules (e.g., showing the parts that constitute a learned "eye" fragment in the image domain) or diagrams of parsed examples. This would help demonstrate that the algorithm is discovering meaningful, hierarchical structure, which is the central promise of grammar-based models.

3.  **Clarity of Technical Details:** There are several points in the technical description that require clarification:
    *   The paper states that the formulation for likelihood gain provides a "surrogate measure." It is not clear if this measure is an exact calculation of the change in Viterbi likelihood under the model's assumptions, or if it is a further approximation. This distinction is important for understanding the guarantees of the optimization procedure.
    *   The paper's examples seem to focus on fragments with an arity of 2 or 3. It is unclear if the method is restricted to a form analogous to Chomsky Normal Form or if it can learn And-rules with arbitrary arity (A → O1...On).

### **Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   **Derivations:** I found it difficult to verify the derivations presented in the supplementary material for the likelihood gain. I would strongly encourage the authors to provide a clearer, more detailed, step-by-step derivation to improve transparency and allow the community to build upon this technical result.

*   **Qualitative Evaluation:** Could you provide some qualitative examples of the most interesting or high-scoring And-Or fragments discovered in your experiments? For the animal faces dataset, it would be extremely valuable to see visualizations of the terminal nodes that are grouped under learned Or-nodes, and how these are composed by And-nodes. This would provide strong evidence that your method learns more meaningful structures than competing approaches, beyond what perplexity scores can show.

*   **Experimental Details:** If this paper is accepted, it is crucial that the authors provide extensive supplementary material with complete details on the experimental setup. This should include pseudo-code for the fragment search, precise definitions of the data representations and relational predicates used for both the event and image domains, and values for all hyperparameters. This is essential for the work's reproducibility and long-term impact.

In summary, this paper introduces a novel and technically sound approach to the important problem of unsupervised grammar induction. The core idea of learning unified And-Or fragments is a significant contribution. However, the paper is severely hampered by a lack of clarity in its experimental description and a failure to provide qualitative results that would give insight into the learned structures. While I lean towards acceptance due to the strength of the core ideas, my recommendation is conditional on the authors' ability and commitment to thoroughly address these clarity and reproducibility issues in the final version and in extensive supplementary materials.
