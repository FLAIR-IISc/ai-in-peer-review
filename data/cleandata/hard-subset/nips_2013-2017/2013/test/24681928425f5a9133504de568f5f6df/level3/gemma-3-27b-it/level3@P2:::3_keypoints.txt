- Novel unsupervised method for learning Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs) that jointly infers grammar structure and parameters.  
- Introduces “And-Or fragments” as a unified mechanism to induce both compositional and reconfigurable elements.  
- Proposes an efficient algorithm that optimizes the grammar posterior using sufficient statistics.  
- Well-motivated and technically grounded, extending existing structure-learning techniques from NLP.  
- Correctly positions SAGs as a generalization of stochastic context-free grammars and sum-product networks; uses a standard posterior formulation.  
- Strength: Unified And-Or fragment learning is a significant, principled novelty balancing compositionality and reconfigurability.  
- Strength: Use of sufficient statistics for efficient posterior gain computation; theoretical link to tensor/matrix coherence.  
- Strength: Addresses an important problem—learning compositional models from unannotated data—in a valuable unsupervised setting.  
- Strength: Clear articulation of limitations in prior work and motivation for the new approach.  
- Weakness: Derivations in the supplement (likelihood gain) are hard to follow; unclear if the surrogate measure is exact or approximate.  
- Weakness: Scalability and generalization to higher-arity fragments are not discussed; potential restriction to CNF-like structures unsettled.  
- Weakness: Experimental section is too concise, assumes prior knowledge, and fails to clearly state what was learned in each experiment.  
- Weakness: Lacks qualitative examples or visualizations of learned grammars to illustrate discovered structures.  
- Weakness: Does not convincingly demonstrate that the learned structures are qualitatively superior to competing methods.  
- Argument for acceptance: Technically sound, novel, efficient approach with competitive results and potential field impact.  
- Argument against acceptance: Poorly presented experiments and unclear derivations; missing qualitative and comparative structure analyses.  
- Overall assessment: Promising and potentially valuable contribution, but presentation—especially experiments and derivations—needs significant improvement.  
- Recommendation: Accept with major revisions—expand experimental details, clarify derivations, add qualitative visualizations, address scalability/generalization, and provide extensive supplementary material.
