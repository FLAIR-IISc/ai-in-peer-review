- Proposes a novel unsupervised method that induces unified AND-OR fragments instead of separately searching for AND and OR structures.  
- Theoretical motivation is strong, with efficient fragment value computation using sufficient statistics and ties to prior grammar induction work.  
- Problem is important: stochastic And-Or grammars model compositional, reconfigurable data with applications in event and visual parsing.  
- Approach appears technically sound and builds logically on existing techniques.  
- Quantitative results suggest competitive or better performance than baselines.  
- Major concern: experimental presentation is unclear, making it hard to understand what was done and what was learned.  
- Paper relies heavily on prior work from a specific research lineage, reducing accessibility for unfamiliar reviewers.  
- The surrogate measure of likelihood gain is not clearly defined—unclear whether it’s exact or an approximation and to what degree.  
- Unclear whether the method handles fragments with arity >2 or is limited to Chomsky Normal Form; this limitation needs discussion.  
- Supplemental derivations are not easily verifiable; crucial equations require clearer, step-by-step explanation.  
- Results are overwhelmingly quantitative and lack illustrative, qualitative examples of learned grammars.  
- Missing visualizations or examples showing what the learned structures represent for event and image parsing.  
- Need direct comparisons demonstrating that learned grammars generalize or capture more interesting structure than competing methods.  
- Required revisions if accepted: extensive supplementary material, concrete illustrations of learned structures, and thorough discussion of the surrogate likelihood measure.  
- Overall: promising and potentially significant contribution, but currently lacks clarity and qualitative validation; not ready for acceptance without substantial revisions.
