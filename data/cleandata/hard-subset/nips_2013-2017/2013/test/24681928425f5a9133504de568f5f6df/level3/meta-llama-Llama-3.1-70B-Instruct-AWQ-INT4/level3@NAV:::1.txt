This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and proposes an unsupervised approach to learning the structures and parameters of such grammars. The authors claim that their approach generalizes previous structure learning work, but upon closer inspection, it appears to be a special case. Nevertheless, this restriction makes the problem tractable, and the basic algorithm is sensible and performs well compared to other grammar induction methods.

The paper's main contribution is the development of an unsupervised approach to learning stochastic And-Or grammars, which are capable of modeling both compositionality and reconfigurability. The authors propose an iterative bottom-up algorithm that starts from a trivial initial grammar and adds new intermediate nonterminal nodes to generalize the grammar and increase its posterior probability.

The experimental evaluation is thorough, with applications to learning event grammars and image grammars. The results show that the proposed approach is competitive with previous approaches on the first dataset and outperforms them on the more complicated second dataset. The authors also demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a real dataset of animal faces.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The experimental evaluation could be improved with more comparisons to other methods, particularly the Stolcke and Omohundro approach (ref. 14), which would provide a valuable baseline. Additionally, the authors could provide more analysis on the advantages and disadvantages of using And-Or fragments versus And-fragments and Or-fragments.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is also well-written, well-organized, and provides enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques, and it is clear how this work differs from previous contributions. The authors adequately reference related work, and the paper provides a unique theoretical and pragmatic approach to learning stochastic And-Or grammars.

In terms of significance, the results are important, and other people (practitioners or researchers) are likely to use these ideas or build on them. The paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research and advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and proposes an unsupervised approach to learning the structures and parameters of such grammars.
* The experimental evaluation is thorough, with applications to learning event grammars and image grammars.
* The results show that the proposed approach is competitive with previous approaches on the first dataset and outperforms them on the more complicated second dataset.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The experimental evaluation could be improved with more comparisons to other methods.
* The authors could provide more analysis on the advantages and disadvantages of using And-Or fragments versus And-fragments and Or-fragments.

Overall, I recommend acceptance of this paper, as it presents a solid contribution to the field of learning stochastic And-Or grammars.
