This paper presents an unsupervised method for learning the structure of stochastic context-free And-Or grammars. The core idea is an iterative, bottom-up algorithm that begins with a trivial grammar that simply enumerates the training examples. In each step, the algorithm searches for an "And-Or fragment"—a small subgraph containing both a compositional And-rule and several alternative Or-rules—to add to the current grammar. The selection of the best fragment is guided by maximizing the posterior probability of the grammar, which effectively balances the data likelihood with a complexity penalty that encourages smaller grammars. This approach is positioned as a general framework applicable to different data types, and its effectiveness is demonstrated on the tasks of learning event grammars from activity videos and image grammars from collections of faces. The method relates to a long line of work on grammar induction, particularly search-based methods that modify an existing grammar to improve a scoring function. It aims to improve upon recent methods for learning visual grammars by unifying the search for compositional (And) and reconfigurable (Or) structures.

**Strengths:**

*   **Principled and Sensible Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm is well-motivated. Framing structure learning as a search process that optimizes a Bayesian posterior objective is a solid, principled approach. The iterative, bottom-up procedure is intuitive and appears effective.
*   **Novel Search Operator:** The central idea of using "And-Or fragments" as the atomic unit of search is a valuable contribution. This unified operator for inducing both compositionality and alternatives is elegant and, as the authors argue, may be more robust to data scarcity than learning And-rules and Or-rules in separate steps.
*   **Tractable Approach to a Hard Problem:** While the authors claim their method "generalizes" previous work, it is perhaps more accurately described as a clever specialization that makes the difficult problem of structure learning tractable for this grammar formalism. This is a strength. By restricting the search to the addition of these specific fragments, the method defines a workable path through an otherwise enormous search space. This is a reasonable and pragmatic design choice.
*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experiments show that the proposed method performs well, achieving comparable or superior results to the specific, recent baselines chosen for comparison in both the event and image domains.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Characterization of Contribution:** The paper's claim to "generalize" or "extend" previous structure learning work (e.g., [14]) should be clarified. The proposed method appears to be a specific instance of a search-based grammar induction algorithm, rather than a strict generalization of frameworks like Bayesian model merging. A more precise discussion of how this work fits into the broader context of classic grammar induction literature would strengthen the paper. For example, framing the work as an effective adaptation of search-based principles to the specific structure of And-Or grammars would be more accurate.
*   **Limited Experimental Baselines:** The experimental evaluation, while positive, is narrow. Comparisons are made only to very recent and closely related work ([17], [18]). To demonstrate a significant advance in the state of the art of grammar induction, it is important to compare against more established and general-purpose baselines. A particularly relevant and valuable comparison would be to the Bayesian model merging algorithm of Stolcke and Omohondro (Ref. [14]). While this would require adapting the baseline to the input data format, such a comparison would provide a much stronger statement about the performance and advantages of the proposed And-Or fragment approach relative to foundational methods in the field.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed piece of work that makes a valuable contribution to the learning of compositional models. The algorithm is clear, the motivation is sound, and the results are promising. The primary weaknesses lie in the need for a more nuanced positioning relative to prior art and a more comprehensive set of experimental comparisons. Despite these points, the paper presents a useful and effective algorithm for learning the structure of a specific but increasingly important class of grammars. It is a worthy contribution to the field.
