This paper proposes an unsupervised method for learning both structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) by iteratively inserting And-Or fragments that jointly capture composition and reconfiguration. The authors formalize a context-free subclass of AOGs and optimize a sparsity-regularized Viterbi posterior. A key technical contribution is a closed-form posterior-gain criterion (under an unambiguity assumption) that factors into coherence measures of an n-gram tensor and a context matrix, enabling efficient greedy/beam search. Empirically, the method learns event and image grammars and outperforms prior And-Or grammar learners in several settings.

Relation to prior work: The approach is closely related to structure learning for PCFGs and model-merging methods (e.g., Stolcke & Omohundro, 1994), to unsupervised NL grammar induction (e.g., Klein & Manning, 2004; Spitkovsky et al., 2010), and to sum-product networks. Despite the claim to “generalize” earlier structure learning, the proposed method is in fact a special case—restricted to a context-free AOG subclass with specific fragment templates. This restriction, however, is reasonable and makes the search tractable while retaining practical expressivity for the targeted domains.

Quality: The algorithm is sensible, well-motivated, and the posterior-gain derivation is clear given the assumptions. Use of Viterbi training and an unambiguity bias is standard but should be discussed as a source of approximation error. Experiments are promising, though the evaluation would benefit from stronger baselines and ablations.

Clarity: Generally clear and well-organized. Pseudocode and more detail on search/complexity, fragment parameterization, and the role of α, sa, so would improve reproducibility.

Originality: Combining And/Or search into a unified And-Or fragment with an efficiently computable posterior gain is a nice idea, though conceptually related to Bayesian model merging and fragment induction.

Significance: A solid contribution for structure learning in a particular, useful class of grammars, with positive results on images and events. Broader impact would be clearer with additional baselines and domains.

Suggestions:
- Include comparisons to Stolcke & Omohundro (1994; ref. 14) as a core baseline, and to PCFG/HDP-PCFG induction and EM/Viterbi PCFG learners.
- Provide ablations (And-only, Or-only, And-Or), sensitivity to α/beam width, and variance/confidence intervals.
- Report parsing speed and training time; clarify unambiguity assumption and failure modes.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Sensible, tractable algorithm with a clear objective.
- Unifies composition and reconfiguration; good empirical performance.
- Applicable across modalities (images, events).

Arguments against acceptance:
- Overstated generalization claim; method targets a special case.
- Missing key baselines (notably Stolcke & Omohundro) and limited ablations.
- Reliance on Viterbi/unambiguity without robustness analysis.

Overall: Weak accept.
