This paper presents a novel approach to unsupervised structure learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs), aiming to simultaneously learn both the grammar’s structure and its parameters. The core idea is to iteratively refine a grammar starting from a trivial initial state by introducing new ‘And-Or fragments’, effectively combining compositional and reconfiguration learning. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their method on event and image grammars, achieving comparable or superior performance to existing techniques.

The work builds upon a significant body of research in stochastic grammars, initially prevalent in natural language processing (NLP) [8, 9] and increasingly applied to visual data [1, 2, 3] and event modeling [4, 5, 6, 7].  Existing approaches often either manually define the grammar structure or focus on parameter learning with a fixed structure [11, 12, 13].  The authors address the limitations of these approaches, particularly the challenges of defining a compact and expressive grammar structure in complex domains like image parsing where the possible rules can be exponentially large.  Their approach distinguishes itself from previous structure learning attempts [14, 15, 16, 17, 18] by optimizing the *posterior probability* of both grammar structure and parameters, and crucially, by learning compositions and reconfigurations in a unified manner with 'And-Or fragments'. This is a strong point as it tackles the potential error-proneness of separate learning stages, particularly with limited or ambiguous data.  The proposed formalization of SAGs, designed to be data-type agnostic, is a valuable contribution.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The unification of composition and reconfiguration learning through And-Or fragments appears well-motivated and potentially more robust than existing methods. The optimization of the posterior probability provides a principled framework for learning, and the derivation of sufficient statistics for efficient computation is a notable technical accomplishment. The empirical results, while limited in scope, demonstrate promising performance on event and image grammar learning. The clarity of the mathematical formulation and the detailed explanation of the algorithm are commendable.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the authors mention algorithmic tractability, the scalability of the proposed approach is not thoroughly investigated. The paper lacks any discussion of computational complexity, and it's unclear how the method would perform with significantly larger datasets (e.g., thousands of samples). This is a crucial aspect given the iterative nature of the algorithm and the potential for exponential growth in the grammar size.  A significant omission is the lack of evaluation on natural language data.  Given the historical roots of stochastic grammars in NLP, a comparison with established methods on standard NLP datasets such as the Penn Treebank [11, 12] is essential to demonstrate the broader applicability and competitiveness of the proposed approach. The authors acknowledge related work in NLP but do not position their method within that landscape with empirical comparisons.  Furthermore, the experiments, while demonstrating the effectiveness on the chosen datasets, feel somewhat limited. More extensive experimentation with different dataset sizes, parameter settings, and comparison with a wider range of baselines would strengthen the conclusions. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to joint structure and parameter learning of SAGs.
*   Principled optimization framework based on posterior probability.
*   Unified learning of composition and reconfiguration.
*   Promising empirical results on event and image grammars.
*   Data-type agnostic formulation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of scalability analysis and discussion of computational complexity.
*   Absence of experiments on natural language datasets, a crucial domain for stochastic grammars.
*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
*   Missing comparisons to a broader range of baseline methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and conceptually interesting contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. The proposed method addresses a significant challenge in learning stochastic grammars, and the initial empirical results are encouraging. However, the lack of scalability analysis and the absence of NLP experiments raise concerns about the broader applicability and impact of the work.  I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon the authors addressing the scalability concerns and ideally including at least a small-scale evaluation on a natural language dataset. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and demonstrate its potential to contribute meaningfully to the field.
