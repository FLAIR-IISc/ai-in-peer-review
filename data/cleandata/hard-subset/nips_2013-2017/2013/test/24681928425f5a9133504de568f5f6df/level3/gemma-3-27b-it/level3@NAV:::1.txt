## Review: Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars

This paper presents a method for unsupervised learning of the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs). The authors propose an iterative approach that starts with a trivial grammar and progressively adds “And-Or fragments” – combinations of compositional and reconfigurable rules – optimizing a posterior probability based on the training data. The work aims to provide a generalized framework applicable to diverse data types, demonstrated through experiments on event and image grammars.

The paper builds upon existing work in grammar induction, specifically referencing techniques from natural language processing (NLP) and previous approaches to learning SAGs for images and events. The contribution lies in the unified treatment of composition and reconfiguration through the proposed And-Or fragments, and the explicit optimization of the grammar’s posterior probability. While the authors claim to “generalize” prior structure learning work, this generalization appears limited, as the method largely focuses on a specific iterative addition of fragments. This isn't necessarily a weakness; by focusing on this particular approach, the authors have created a tractable algorithm.

The core algorithm is sensibly designed and the experimental results demonstrate competitive performance, often outperforming previous methods, particularly on a more complex event dataset. The experimental evaluation, however, could be strengthened. The authors acknowledge this, and the recommendation to compare against Stolcke and Omohondro (ref. 14) is a valuable one. This baseline represents a significant earlier work in grammar induction and would provide a more robust point of comparison. Additional comparisons to more recent deep learning-based methods for scene or event understanding could also be beneficial, even if they don’t directly use a grammar-based approach.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of unsupervised SAG structure learning.
*   **Sensible Algorithm:** The iterative fragment-based approach appears well-reasoned and the use of posterior probability optimization is appropriate.
*   **Competitive Performance:** Experimental results demonstrate the approach's effectiveness on event and image data.
*   **Unified Framework:** The And-Or fragment concept provides a unified way to handle compositionality and reconfigurability.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Generalization:** The claim of generalization is overstated; the method focuses on a specific structure learning strategy.
*   **Insufficient Baselines:** The experimental evaluation would benefit from comparisons to stronger and more recent baselines, notably Stolcke and Omohondro.
*   **Algorithm Complexity:** While tractable, the algorithm's efficiency for very large datasets or complex data types could be a concern.

**Overall:**

This is a solid contribution to the field of probabilistic grammar induction. The focused approach allows for a tractable and effective algorithm, demonstrating good performance on relevant tasks. Addressing the concerns regarding baseline comparisons would further strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to incorporate the suggested baseline and provide a more nuanced discussion of the method’s generalizability.
