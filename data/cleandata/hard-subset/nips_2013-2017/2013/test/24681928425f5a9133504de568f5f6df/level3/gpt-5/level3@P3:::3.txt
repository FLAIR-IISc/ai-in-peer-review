Summary
This paper proposes an unsupervised structure-learning algorithm for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) that operates across data modalities (events and images). The core idea is to induce unified And-Or fragments—each containing an And node whose children are Or nodes—rather than alternating between separate searches for compositions (And rules) and reconfigurations (Or rules). The method optimizes a posterior objective combining Viterbi likelihood and a sparsity prior. A key technical contribution is that the posterior gain of inserting a candidate fragment can be evaluated efficiently from sufficient statistics—an n-gram tensor over fragment configurations and a context co-occurrence matrix—thus enabling greedy or beam search. Experiments on event parsing and visual object parsing show competitive or improved performance relative to prior work.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on and connects several lines:
- Unsupervised grammar induction in NLP (e.g., Stolcke & Omohundro; Klein & Manning; Tu & Honavar) by explicitly optimizing a probabilistic objective and using Viterbi likelihood/unambiguity bias.
- Structure learning for image/event AOGs from S. C. Zhu’s group (e.g., Si & Zhu; Si et al.), improving on separate And/Or induction by unifying them via fragments.
- Links to decomposable sum-product networks and stochastic context-free grammars.
The paper’s main novelty is the unified And-Or fragment induction and the sufficient-statistics-based posterior gain computation that obviates repeated re-parsing.

Evaluation by review criteria

Quality
- The problem is important and the approach is technically sound in high level. The posterior objective, sparsity prior, and Viterbi training are reasonable and grounded in prior evidence.
- The sufficient-statistics derivation (likelihood gain factorization into tensor/matrix coherence) and the unambiguity assumption are crucial but not fully verifiable from the main text; several steps are deferred to the supplement and are hard to follow. It is also unclear whether the “surrogate measure” is exact under stated assumptions or an approximation. Clarification is needed on: counting conventions, contexts, and when duplicates are merged to change Viterbi likelihood.
- Complexity and scalability claims are plausible but would benefit from explicit bounds for constructing tensors/contexts and for candidate-fragment enumeration as a function of alphabet size, n, and beam width.

Clarity
- The method section is generally well-motivated, but the derivations and the experimental section are difficult to follow without deep familiarity with previous AOG work. It is hard to discern what exactly is being learned (rules, parameters, relations) and how examples are represented in each setting (IDs vs. action vectors; image quantization and relations).
- The paper would benefit from a concrete, self-contained running example of fragment induction (with counts, context definitions, and the resulting probabilities), and from qualitative illustrations of learned structures and parses.

Originality
- Unified And-Or fragment induction, and computing fragment value via sufficient statistics, are novel and interesting advances over separate And/Or searches and heuristic structure growing used in prior AOG work.
- The cross-domain formalization of context-free AOGs is a useful unification.

Significance
- If made reproducible and accessible, the approach could influence structure learning in AOGs and related generative models. Results on both events and images are promising, though mostly quantitative. Demonstrating qualitatively “more interesting structure” than baselines would strengthen impact.

Major strengths
- Well-motivated, unified method that addresses known pitfalls of separate And/Or searches.
- Efficient posterior-gain evaluation via sufficient statistics; avoids expensive re-parsing in the inner loop.
- Cross-domain applicability demonstrated on events and images with improved quantitative results.

Major weaknesses and requests for clarification
- Derivations: The likelihood gain expression and its factorization into coherence terms need a clearer, self-contained derivation in the main text or a substantially expanded supplement. Please state explicitly:
  - Assumptions required (Viterbi learning, unambiguity, independence) and where they are used.
  - Whether the “surrogate measure” equals the exact Viterbi-likelihood gain under these assumptions, or is an approximation; if approximate, characterize the bias and when it is tight.
  - Exact definitions of the “context” for the context matrix, how contexts are extracted, and how co-occurrences are counted.
  - How merging identical reduced samples changes the Viterbi likelihood and how ties are handled.
- Arity/general form: The text suggests A → O1…On is allowed, but it is unclear whether the implementation supports n > 2, whether search is limited to Chomsky Normal Form (binary rules), and how n is selected. Please clarify:
  - Are candidate fragments restricted to binary And rules in practice?
  - If conversion to CNF is used, how are Or siblings preserved, and what is the effect on the posterior?
  - Any empirical analysis of learned arities.
- Experiments are hard to follow:
  - Events: Precisely specify how frames are converted to action vectors, how sequences are merged into IDs (Method 1), and how the “co-occurring” and “followed-by” relations are instantiated in And rules (Methods 2/3). Describe the grammar change “right before termination” to Or at the top, and the parsing procedure (CYK) with enough detail to reproduce.
  - Images: Provide the quantization pipeline, terminal vocabulary size, relation parametrization (relative positions binning and energy terms), and smoothing used in perplexity computation.
  - Report hyperparameters (α, sa, so, beam width, n bounds), sensitivity analysis, and runtime/memory statistics for tensor/context construction.
- Qualitative results are missing. Please show:
  - Example learned And-Or fragments (graphs) for both domains, with counts, probabilities, and a few parses of held-out examples.
  - A side-by-side comparison with structures learned by competing methods to substantiate the claim of “more interesting structure.”
- Scope of “unsupervised”: Event experiments use annotated atomic actions; clarify that supervision exists at the primitive level and the learning is unsupervised for higher-level structure.
- Baselines/ablations: Add comparisons to (i) separate And-only and Or-only searches, (ii) model merging baselines, and (iii) an ablation that replaces the sufficient-statistics gain with re-parsing-based gain to validate the surrogate.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and principled approach to AOG structure learning with cross-domain applicability.
- Technical idea of unified fragments plus efficient gain via sufficient statistics is elegant and potentially impactful.
- Promising quantitative results in two modalities.

Cons for acceptance
- Key derivations and assumptions are not clearly presented; unclear if the surrogate gain is exact.
- Experimental presentation is difficult to parse; reproducibility and accessibility to non-experts are limited.
- Lack of qualitative evidence of learned structure and limited ablations.

Suggestions for revision (if accepted, include in an expanded supplement)
- Full derivations with assumptions; clear statement on exactness vs approximation of the surrogate.
- Detailed algorithm pseudocode, candidate-fragment generation, complexity analysis, and sensitivity to α, beam width, n.
- Complete, self-contained experimental details with concrete illustrations of learned grammars and parses; release code and data processing scripts if possible.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The problem is important, the approach is novel and technically interesting, and the results are promising. However, clarity issues—especially around derivations and experimental presentation—need to be addressed to make the contribution accessible and reproducible. With the requested clarifications and qualitative evidence, this would be a strong paper.
