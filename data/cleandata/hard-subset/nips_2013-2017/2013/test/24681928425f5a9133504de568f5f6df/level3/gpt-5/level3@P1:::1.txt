This paper proposes an unsupervised method to learn both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) from unannotated data. The authors give a unified, context-free formalization of AOGs (connecting to decomposable sum-product networks [10]) that is agnostic to modality, and introduce a bottom-up structure learning procedure that incrementally inserts And-Or fragments to maximize a sparsity-regularized posterior. A key design choice is to learn compositions (And rules) and reconfigurations (Or choices) jointly via And-Or fragments, rather than in separate phases, alleviating brittleness under data sparsity and reducing redundancy. The algorithm uses Viterbi likelihood (unambiguity-biased) and an MDL-style prior, derives sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) so that posterior gains can be computed efficiently without full re-parsing, and assigns optimal Or-rule probabilities from the counts. The approach is evaluated on (i) event grammars for human activity videos (with both ID-sequences and action-occurrence sequences) and (ii) image grammars for animal faces, showing comparable or better performance than prior work [15, 17, 18].

Relation to prior work: The framework builds on AOGs in vision and events [1, 3, 7, 17, 18], PCFG induction and model merging [11, 12, 14–16], and connects to SPNs [10]. The main novelty is the use of And-Or fragments with efficient posterior gain computation grounded in coherence measures [22], and a unified treatment across modalities. While the paper positions the formalization as “unified” and “agnostic,” the learning setting is a context-free subclass with unambiguity assumptions and decomposability constraints, which makes the problem tractable but also narrows the claim of generality. Within these constraints, the method advances practical structure learning for AOGs.

Quality: The algorithm is sensible, well-motivated, and technically sound given the stated assumptions. The posterior gain derivation (likelihood gain via context-free coherence plus MDL prior) is plausible and computationally attractive; using Viterbi likelihood is supported by prior evidence [20, 21]. The experiments demonstrate improvements over relevant AOG baselines [17, 18] and competitiveness with a biclustering-based PCFG approach [15]. However:
- The reliance on grammar unambiguity and Viterbi training is a strong modeling assumption; some discussion of failure modes for ambiguous grammars or noisy data would help.
- The method depends on pre-specified atomic patterns and relation types (e.g., temporal “followed-by” and “co-occurs,” spatial relations); end-to-end sensitivity to these design choices is not analyzed.
- Experimental scope is somewhat limited. More comparisons would strengthen the case (see below), and ablations (e.g., And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or fragments, effect of α, beam width) would be informative.
- Complexity/scalability beyond a few thousand samples and tens of terminals is not rigorously profiled.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized. The unified AOG definition and learning loop are described clearly, and figures convey the iterative insertion of fragments. Some notation (RD, CM, coherence factors) is dense and relegates key details to the supplement; moving a concise pseudocode and a worked example of the sufficient-statistics computation into the main text would improve readability. A brief, explicit statement of the unambiguity assumption’s role and limits would also help.

Originality: The joint induction of compositions and reconfigurations via And-Or fragments with an efficient posterior gain estimator is a novel and useful algorithmic contribution. The cross-modal formalization is incremental but valuable for unifying prior AOG variants.

Significance: For communities working on structured generative models for images and events, this is a solid step toward practical, unsupervised structure learning. The method appears robust in low-data regimes and yields compact grammars. Its reliance on context-free, decomposable structures limits direct applicability to highly context-sensitive domains, but within vision and activity parsing it is likely to be adopted and extended.

Experimental evaluation: The event experiments cover both simpler and synthetic-harder settings, with three training strategies. The image experiments include synthetic data with ground-truth evaluation (F1, KL divergence) and a real dataset with perplexity. To strengthen the empirical case:
- Include a baseline based on Bayesian Model Merging (Stolcke & Omohundro [14]) adapted to the AOG/PCFG setting; this is a classic, strong comparator for structure induction.
- Compare against Klein & Manning [12] (or modern PCFG/DMV variants) on a small textual dataset to substantiate the modality-agnostic claim, or clarify the scope to images/events.
- Add ablations on fragment types and the effect of Viterbi vs full likelihood.
- Report runtime, memory, and scaling with number of terminals and sample length; include sensitivity to α, sa/so.

Pros:
- Clear, principled objective (posterior over structure and parameters) with efficient sufficient-statistics-based search.
- Unifies learning of And and Or structure via And-Or fragments; reduces redundancy and improves robustness.
- Competitive to superior results on both event and image grammars; good performance under data scarcity.
- Cross-modal formalization and practical algorithm likely to be useful to practitioners.

Cons:
- “Unified/general” claim tempered by a context-free, decomposable, unambiguous subclass; broader generality is not demonstrated.
- Limited comparisons; missing a key baseline (Bayesian model merging [14]) and ablations.
- Dependence on predefined terminals and relation vocabularies; no end-to-end evaluation.
- Some derivations and algorithmic details are in the supplement; clarity could be improved in the main text.

Recommendation: Accept. A solid, well-executed contribution to unsupervised structure learning for And-Or grammars, with a practical algorithm and promising results. Strengthening the empirical comparisons (especially to [14]) and clarifying assumptions would further improve the paper.
