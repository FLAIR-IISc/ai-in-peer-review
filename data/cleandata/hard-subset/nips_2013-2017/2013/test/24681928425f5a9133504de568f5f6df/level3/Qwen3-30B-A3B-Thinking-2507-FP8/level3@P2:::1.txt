

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to the type of data being modeled (images, events, etc.), along with an unsupervised approach to learning both the structure and parameters of such grammars. The authors build upon previous work in stochastic grammars for natural language processing [8,9], image modeling [1,3], and event recognition [7], while extending the recent work on structure learning of And-Or grammars for images [17] and events [18]. The key insight is that the authors propose to learn compositions and reconfigurations in a unified manner through "And-Or fragments," avoiding the separate handling of And-fragments and Or-fragments used in prior approaches.

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars applicable across different data types; (2) an explicit optimization of the posterior probability of the grammar structure and parameters, rather than relying on heuristics; (3) a unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations; and (4) empirical validation on event grammars and image grammars showing competitive or better performance than previous approaches.

**Strengths:**
The paper is technically sound with a clear algorithmic approach that efficiently computes sufficient statistics to evaluate grammar fragments. The unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations (via And-Or fragments) is particularly elegant and addresses a genuine limitation of prior work that handled these aspects separately. The experimental results on both event grammars and image grammars are convincing, with the proposed method outperforming previous approaches on more complex datasets. The formalization is general enough to apply to different types of data while maintaining computational tractability.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's claim of "generalization" over previous work is overstated. The method is actually a special case of a broader class of grammars, and the restriction to this special case is what makes the problem tractable. The authors would strengthen their contribution by more clearly articulating how their approach differs from and improves upon previous structure learning methods, particularly Stolcke and Omohundro [14] which is a valuable baseline for grammar structure learning. The experimental evaluation would be significantly improved by comparing against this baseline and other relevant approaches, as the current comparisons are limited to [17] and [18].

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed method compared to previous approaches. While the authors note that their algorithm runs within minutes on a desktop for 5000 samples, they don't provide a thorough comparison of computational efficiency.

**Quality:**
The technical content is strong, with a well-motivated approach that addresses a clear limitation in prior work. The experimental evaluation is generally sound but could be more comprehensive. The claims are well-supported by the empirical results, though the comparison to relevant prior work is insufficient.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to methodology to experiments. The unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars is particularly well-explained. The figures illustrating the learning process (Figures 1 and 2) are helpful in understanding the approach.

**Originality:**
The unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments represents a genuine novelty in grammar structure learning. However, the paper's claim of generalization is misleading, as the method operates within a restricted class of grammars. The approach is a special case of a broader class, though a useful one for the applications considered.

**Significance:**
The paper makes a solid contribution to the problem of learning grammars for structured data, with potential applications in computer vision, natural language processing, and activity recognition. The ability to learn grammar structures from unannotated data is valuable for many real-world applications. The approach could potentially be extended to other structured prediction problems, though the paper doesn't explore this wider applicability.

**Recommendation:**
This is a solid contribution to the field of grammar-based modeling. While the claim of generalization is overstated, the unified approach to learning grammar structure is novel and effective. The paper would be strengthened by more comprehensive experimental comparisons (particularly with Stolcke and Omohundro [14]) and a clearer articulation of how the method relates to previous work. The experimental results are convincing, and the technical approach is sound.

**Pro:**
- Unified approach to learning compositions and reconfigurations
- Explicit optimization of posterior probability
- Good empirical results on event and image grammars
- Clear formalization applicable to multiple data types

**Con:**
- Overstated claim of generalization (method is a special case)
- Insufficient comparison to relevant prior work (particularly Stolcke and Omohundro [14])
- Limited discussion of computational complexity

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to grammar structure learning. With minor revisions to address the experimental comparisons and clarify the relationship to previous work, it would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
