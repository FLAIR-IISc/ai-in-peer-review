This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and proposes an unsupervised approach to learning the structures as well as the parameters of such grammars. The authors extend previous work on structure learning of natural language grammars and improve upon recent work on structure learning of And-Or grammars of images and events. The proposed approach starts from a trivial initial grammar and iteratively induces compositions and reconfigurations in a unified manner, optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar.

The paper relates to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere, particularly in the areas of stochastic grammars, natural language processing, and computer vision. The authors provide a thorough review of related work and clearly explain how their approach differs from and improves upon existing methods.

The strengths of the paper include the clear and concise presentation of the proposed approach, the thorough experimental evaluation, and the comparison to previous methods. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in learning event grammars and image grammars, achieving comparable or better performance than previous approaches.

One of the main weaknesses of the paper is the limited scope of the experimental evaluation. While the authors compare their approach to a few previous methods, they do not provide a comprehensive comparison to other grammar induction methods. Specifically, the reviewer recommends comparing to Stolcke and Omohondro (ref. 14) as a valuable baseline.

Another weakness is the claim that the proposed approach "generalizes" previous structure learning work. However, the reviewer notes that the approach is actually a special case, and the restriction to this special case makes the problem tractable. While this is a reasonable simplification, it should be clearly stated as such.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow and understand.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide sufficient information for the expert reader to reproduce the results. The paper is well-organized, and the figures and tables are helpful in illustrating the proposed approach and the experimental results.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to learning the structure of stochastic And-Or grammars. While the idea of using And-Or fragments is not new, the authors' formulation and optimization of the posterior probability of the grammar are original contributions.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses an important problem in the field of stochastic grammars and provides a new approach to learning the structure of such grammars. The results are important and have the potential to impact the field of computer vision and natural language processing.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to learning the structure of stochastic And-Or grammars.
* The experimental evaluation demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The paper is well-written, and the authors provide sufficient information for the expert reader to reproduce the results.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The experimental evaluation is limited in scope and does not provide a comprehensive comparison to other grammar induction methods.
* The claim that the proposed approach "generalizes" previous structure learning work is misleading.
* The paper does not provide a clear discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of the proposed approach.

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of stochastic grammars, and the paper is well-written and well-organized. While there are some weaknesses in the experimental evaluation and the presentation of the approach, the paper is a valuable addition to the field and deserves to be accepted.
