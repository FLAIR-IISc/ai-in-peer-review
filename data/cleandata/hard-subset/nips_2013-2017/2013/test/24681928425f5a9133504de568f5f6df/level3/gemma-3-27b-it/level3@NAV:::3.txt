## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper proposes a novel unsupervised approach for learning stochastic And-Or grammars by inducing unified AND-OR fragments, a departure from previous methods that learn compositions and reconfigurations separately. The core idea—efficiently computing fragment values using sufficient statistics—is well-motivated, and the method is technically sound, building upon existing work in grammar induction. The application to both event parsing and visual object parsing demonstrates the versatility of the proposed framework, and the problem addressed is certainly important within the broader context of compositional representation learning.

The strengths of this work lie in its novel approach to structure learning and the theoretical grounding provided. The unification of AND and OR fragment induction is a clever idea that promises to improve robustness in data-scarce scenarios.  The use of sufficient statistics for efficient computation is a significant contribution, and the extension of previous work on natural language grammars to other data types is commendable.

However, despite the clear potential, several critical weaknesses hinder a strong recommendation for acceptance. Primarily, the experimental presentation is severely lacking. It’s exceptionally difficult to understand *what* was actually done. The paper assumes a strong familiarity with the work of S.C. Zhu’s group, making it inaccessible to researchers outside that immediate circle. Details about data representation, parameter settings, and the specifics of the experimental setup are missing, preventing any attempt at replication or meaningful comparison. 

Furthermore, while the paper claims comparable or better performance, the results are largely quantitative and lack illustrative examples. Qualitative visualizations of the discovered structures – what kinds of compositions and reconfigurations *were* actually learned – are desperately needed to demonstrate the method’s effectiveness.  It’s also unclear if the “surrogate measure” used for likelihood gain is an exact calculation or an approximation, and the limitations regarding arity (whether the method is restricted to Chomsky Normal Form) should be explicitly addressed. 

Finally, a crucial piece missing is a demonstration of how this approach learns *more interesting* structure than competing methods. Simply achieving comparable performance isn’t sufficient; the novelty of the fragment-based approach needs to be convincingly demonstrated through a more comprehensive comparative analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Novel approach, technically sound, well-motivated, potential for significant impact.
**Arguments against Acceptance:** Poor experimental presentation, lack of clarity, missing details, unconvincing experimental results.

**Recommendation:**  Reject.  While the core idea is promising, the lack of experimental clarity and insufficient validation preclude acceptance in its current form.  A revised version with significantly expanded supplementary material, concrete examples of learned structures, and a more rigorous experimental comparison is strongly recommended for future consideration.
