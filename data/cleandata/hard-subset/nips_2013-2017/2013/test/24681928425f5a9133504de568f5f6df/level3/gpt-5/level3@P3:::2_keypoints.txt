- Proposes an unsupervised method to jointly learn structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) using And-Or fragments and a posterior (likelihood × sparsity prior) objective.  
- Starts from a trivial grammar reproducing the training set and greedily adds And-Or fragments that maximize posterior gain.  
- Approximates likelihood with Viterbi likelihood and uses an unambiguity assumption to obtain closed-form gains and optimal Or-rule probabilities.  
- Efficient scoring via sufficient statistics: n-gram tensors and context matrices for fragment coverage and co-occurrence counts.  
- Evaluated on event and image domains, showing competitive or superior performance to prior domain baselines with modest runtimes.  
- Connects to unsupervised grammar induction (PCFG/Bayesian merging), stochastic scene grammars, and sum-product networks.  
- Main novelty: jointly learning And and Or structure via And-Or fragments to mitigate sparsity/redundancy from separate induction.  
- Strength: clear unified AOG formalization and principled posterior objective with sparsity prior.  
- Strength: motivated and practical joint structure learning; elegant sufficient-statistics scoring and closed-form Or-rule estimates.  
- Strength: coherent leverage of Viterbi likelihood and unambiguity regularization known to help unsupervised induction.  
- Strength: solid empirical evidence on two non-text domains with appropriate metrics (perplexity, F-measure, KL).  
- Claim of domain-agnostic, recursive modeling is attractive for broader applicability.  
- Weakness: lacks rigorous tractability/scalability analysis; search and tensor operations complexity not analyzed.  
- Weakness: empirical datasets are small (61 videos, 320 images); scalability claims (“thousands of datapoints”) unsupported without scaling curves.  
- Weakness: no natural language experiments despite language and recursion claims; missing comparisons to PCFG/EM/Bayesian baselines.  
- Weakness: reliance on unambiguity and Viterbi likelihood may bias against inherently ambiguous data like natural language.  
- Weakness: limited ablations (And-only vs Or-only, Viterbi vs full likelihood, hyperparameter sensitivity) and deferred technical details to the supplement.  
- Quality: technically sound derivations but missing theoretical guarantees and complexity bounds; empirical support is narrow in scope.  
- Clarity: generally well-written, but coherence measures, sub-tensor search, encoding constants, and some hyperparameter choices should be in main text.  
- Originality: novel unification of And/Or induction via And-Or fragments and sufficient-statistics scoring relative to prior AOG/PCFG work.  
- Significance: potentially impactful for structured perception and NLP if scalability and language performance are demonstrated.  
- Suggestions: add complexity analysis and empirical scaling studies (time/memory up to tens of thousands of samples).  
- Suggestions: include natural language experiments (WSJ10, Dyck languages) and comparisons to Klein & Manning (2004), EM-PCFG, Bayesian merging.  
- Suggestions: perform ablations (And-only/Or-only, Viterbi vs EM, unambiguity effects, fragment size, beam width, α/sa/so).  
- Suggestions: analyze ambiguity handling, formalize coherence measures and sub-tensor search, provide pseudocode and release code.  
- Arguments for acceptance: conceptually neat joint structure learning with principled scoring and promising cross-domain results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing language experiments, scalability evidence, and thorough ablations; reliance on unambiguity limits applicability.  
- Overall: solid and interesting contribution with clear potential, but needs broader experiments (especially NLP) and stronger scalability/ablation evidence to fully justify claims.
