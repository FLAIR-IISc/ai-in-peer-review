## Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper proposes a method for unsupervised learning of both the structure and parameters of Stochastic And-Or Grammars (SAGs). The authors address the challenge of learning compositional and reconfigurable models from unlabeled data, a problem relevant to areas like natural language processing, image understanding, and event recognition. They present a unified formalization of SAGs and an iterative learning approach that induces AND-OR fragments to optimize a posterior probability of the grammar. The work builds upon previous research in both natural language grammar induction and more recent applications of SAGs to visual data. 

The core contribution lies in the simultaneous learning of structure and parameters, and the use of AND-OR fragments to improve efficiency and robustness compared to approaches that learn compositions and reconfigurations separately. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on event and image grammar learning tasks, achieving competitive or superior results to existing methods. The presented likelihood gain formulation, based on sufficient statistics, is a clever approach to making the learning process more tractable. 

However, the paper suffers from several limitations. The most significant is the lack of discussion around algorithmic tractability and scalability. While the authors mention the efficiency of their approach, it remains unclear how it performs as the number of data points increases – a critical consideration for real-world applications. The experiments, though demonstrating good results on the chosen tasks, lack a crucial comparison to established methods on standard benchmarks like the Penn Treebank for natural language processing. This omission significantly weakens the claim of advancing the state of the art, particularly given the historical roots of SAGs in NLP.

Further, the experimental section could be strengthened with a more thorough ablation study – examining the impact of different design choices (e.g., the sparsity prior coefficient α) and a more detailed analysis of the learned grammars. While the results are promising, more experiments, specifically on more complex and larger datasets including standard NLP benchmarks, are needed to convincingly demonstrate the generalizability and scalability of the proposed method. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to jointly learning structure and parameters of SAGs.
*   The AND-OR fragment induction strategy appears promising for efficient learning.
*   Competitive results on event and image grammar learning.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of scalability analysis and discussion of algorithmic tractability.
*   Missing experiments on standard NLP datasets and comparisons to relevant prior work.
*   Limited ablation study and detailed analysis of learned grammars.



**Overall:** While this paper presents a technically sound and potentially valuable contribution, the lack of scalability analysis and the absence of standard benchmark comparisons are significant weaknesses. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address these issues in a revised version. Addressing the scalability concerns and including experiments on established NLP datasets are crucial for elevating this work to a level suitable for NIPS.
