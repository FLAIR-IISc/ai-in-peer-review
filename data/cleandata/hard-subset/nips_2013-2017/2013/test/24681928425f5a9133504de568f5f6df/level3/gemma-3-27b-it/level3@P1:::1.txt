This paper presents an unsupervised approach to learning both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars, a powerful formalism for representing compositional and reconfigurable patterns found in various data types like images and events. The authors address the challenge of automatic grammar induction from unannotated data, a common problem in applying these grammars to real-world scenarios. They propose an iterative algorithm that starts with a trivial grammar and incrementally adds fragments, optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar at each step. A key contribution is the unified treatment of compositions and reconfigurations through the introduction of “And-Or fragments,” which the authors argue improves efficiency and robustness compared to methods that learn these separately. The approach is evaluated on event and image grammar learning tasks, demonstrating competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods.

This work builds upon a rich history of research in stochastic grammar learning. Stochastic grammars have long been used in natural language processing (e.g., [8, 9]) and have seen increasing application in computer vision and event modeling (e.g., [1, 3, 4, 7]). The authors appropriately position their work within this context, noting the connection to sum-product networks [10] and acknowledging prior work in unsupervised grammar induction. Existing approaches often rely on manually specifying the grammar structure or optimizing parameters within a fixed structure (e.g., [11, 12, 13]). This paper distinguishes itself by tackling the more challenging problem of *structure learning* – discovering the grammar itself from data.  Previous work on structure learning has included Bayesian model merging [14] and iterative biclustering [16]. While the authors claim their approach generalizes previous structure learning work, it is more accurate to characterize it as a specialized and effective application tailored to And-Or grammars, particularly leveraging the compositionality inherent in the framework.

The strengths of this paper are considerable. The proposed algorithm is technically sound and logically presented. The use of a posterior probability optimization framework is a strong design choice, allowing for a principled approach to structure learning. The introduction of And-Or fragments is a clever idea that appears to address practical limitations of learning compositions and reconfigurations in isolation, especially when data is scarce. The experimental results, while not overwhelmingly conclusive, demonstrate that the approach achieves competitive or superior performance on the tested datasets. The clarity of the presentation is also commendable, making the core ideas accessible. The authors clearly explain the mathematical foundations and algorithm details, providing sufficient information for replication.  

However, there are also some weaknesses.  The experimental evaluation, while solid, could be strengthened by a more comprehensive comparison with existing methods.  In particular, the authors should consider including a comparison to Stolcke and Omohondro’s work [14] on inducing probabilistic grammars by Bayesian model merging. This is a well-established baseline in the field and would provide a more thorough assessment of the proposed approach's novelty and effectiveness. While the authors justify their focus on And-Or grammars, a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential drawbacks of this specific formalism would be beneficial.  For example, it is unclear how the approach would scale to more complex datasets or grammars with a very large branching factor.  The assumptions underlying the approximation of the likelihood with the Viterbi likelihood could also be discussed in more detail.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-motivated approach to unsupervised grammar induction.
*   Novel introduction of And-Or fragments simplifies and improves the learning process.
*   Competitive performance on event and image grammar learning tasks.
*   Clear and well-written paper.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited comparison to existing structure learning methods, specifically missing a key baseline from Stolcke and Omohondro [14].
*   Potential scalability issues for more complex datasets or grammars are not addressed.
*   Assumptions behind the Viterbi likelihood approximation could be further clarified.

**Overall:**

This is a solid contribution to the field of probabilistic grammar learning. The proposed approach is innovative, technically sound, and demonstrates promising results. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened with a more comprehensive comparison to existing methods, the authors have presented a valuable and well-articulated piece of work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the inclusion of the Stolcke and Omohondro baseline and a brief discussion of the potential limitations of the approach.
