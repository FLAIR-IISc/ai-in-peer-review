

This paper proposes an unsupervised approach for learning the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars, presenting a unified formalization that is agnostic to the data type being modeled (images, events, etc.). The authors address the important problem of grammar structure learning, which has been largely neglected in prior work that either manually specifies grammar structures or learns compositions and reconfigurations separately. Previous work on learning natural language grammars [14, 15, 16] and image grammars [17] has struggled with either the combinatorial complexity of grammar rules or the difficulty of learning compositions and reconfigurations in separate steps.

The main contribution is the introduction of "And-Or fragments" that unify the search for new compositions and reconfigurations. Unlike previous approaches that learn And-fragments (for compositions) and Or-fragments (for reconfigurations) separately, the authors propose a single search space for And-Or fragments that systematically covers multiple compositions and reconfigurations. This approach is theoretically grounded in optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar, with the likelihood gain efficiently computed using sufficient statistics derived from training data. The algorithm starts from a trivial initial grammar and iteratively improves it by adding And-Or fragments that maximize the posterior probability.

The paper demonstrates the approach on event grammars (using human activity datasets) and image grammars (using synthetic and real animal face datasets). The results show comparable or better performance than previous approaches, particularly on more complex datasets where the ability to learn multiple relations simultaneously proves advantageous.

Strengths of the paper include:
- The problem is well-motivated and important for modeling compositional and reconfigurable patterns across domains.
- The approach is technically sound, with a clear theoretical foundation for the posterior optimization.
- The unified And-Or fragment search is novel and avoids the limitations of separate composition and reconfiguration learning.
- The empirical results are promising, particularly on the more complex event dataset where the method outperforms competitors.

However, several weaknesses require clarification before the paper can be accepted:

First, the derivations of the likelihood gain formula (Equation 3) and the relationship between the "surrogate measure" and the exact likelihood gain are unclear. The paper states the likelihood gain "can be factorized as the product of two tensor/matrix coherence measures" but does not explicitly clarify whether this is an exact derivation or an approximation. This is a significant concern for theoretical soundness.

Second, the paper is unclear about the scope of the approach. The authors state that "by adding only And-Or fragments, our algorithm is still capable of constructing any context-free And-Or grammar," but it's unclear whether the method extends beyond Chomsky normal form (i.e., fragments with arity >2). This limitation would significantly impact the expressive power of the learned grammars.

Third, the experimental section is difficult to follow without prior knowledge of Zhu's group's previous work. The paper assumes familiarity with their specific representations for events and images (e.g., "atomic actions" and "quantized images"), making it hard for readers unfamiliar with this line of work to understand what was learned or how the experiments were conducted. The paper would benefit from more detailed descriptions of the data representations and grammatical structures learned.

Fourth, the paper lacks qualitative examples of the grammars learned. The results are primarily quantitative (F-measure, perplexity), but the authors do not provide concrete examples of the grammatical structures discovered, which would help readers understand the value of the approach. A few illustrative examples of learned grammars would significantly strengthen the paper.

The paper's significance is moderate. While the problem of grammar learning is important, the paper's contribution appears incremental in the context of the broader field of probabilistic modeling. The unified approach is interesting, but the experimental results don't clearly demonstrate that the learned grammars are more interpretable or useful than those learned by competing approaches.

The paper would be significantly improved by:
1. Providing clearer derivations of the likelihood gain formula with explicit statements about approximations
2. Clarifying the scope of the method (arity >2 vs. Chomsky normal form)
3. Including more detailed experimental descriptions accessible to readers outside Zhu's group
4. Adding qualitative examples of grammars learned from the datasets

The paper presents a technically sound approach to an important problem with promising empirical results. However, the unclear theoretical derivations, limited experimental accessibility, and lack of qualitative examples are significant concerns. If the authors provide the requested clarifications and additional material in the final version, this would be a strong candidate for acceptance. As is, the paper's contribution is valuable but not yet fully convincing due to the ambiguities noted above.
