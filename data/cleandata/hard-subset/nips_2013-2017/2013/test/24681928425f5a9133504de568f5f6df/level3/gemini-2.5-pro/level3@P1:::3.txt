This paper presents an unsupervised method for learning the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars. The core idea is to iteratively build a grammar from a trivial initial state by proposing and adding "And-Or fragments". This unified approach to inducing compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules) is contrasted with prior work that learns these structures separately. The learning objective is to maximize the posterior probability of the grammar, which combines a Viterbi likelihood term with a sparsity-inducing prior on the grammar's size. A key technical contribution is an efficient method for calculating the posterior gain of adding a candidate fragment, which relies on a set of sufficient statistics derived from the training data. The proposed framework is general and is evaluated on two distinct domains: learning event grammars from video and learning image grammars from both synthetic and real images. The work builds upon previous research in grammar induction, particularly from natural language processing (e.g., Stolcke & Omohundro, 1994) and more recent work on And-Or grammars for vision from the authors' research group (e.g., Si & Zhu, 2013).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The problem of unsupervised structure learning is of fundamental importance in machine learning. The proposed approach of learning unified And-Or fragments is novel and well-motivated. It elegantly addresses the challenges of data scarcity and search complexity that arise when trying to learn compositional and alternative structures separately. The general, data-agnostic formalization of And-Or grammars is also a valuable contribution, increasing the potential impact of the work.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The overall framework is technically sound. Optimizing the grammar's posterior probability provides a solid theoretical foundation. The derivation of an efficient method to calculate the gain from adding a new fragment, based on tensor and matrix coherence measures, appears to be a strong technical result that makes the greedy search procedure computationally feasible.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Clarity on Technical Details:**
    *   The derivation of the likelihood gain is a central part of the contribution but is not sufficiently explained in the main paper. While details are likely in the supplement, the main text should provide more intuition. Specifically, the paper states the likelihood gain is formulated "by assuming grammar unambiguity," but later refers to the tensor/matrix coherence measures as a "surrogate measure." Is the derived formula for likelihood gain an exact computation under the unambiguity assumption, or is it an approximation? This is a critical point that needs clarification.
    *   It is unclear if the method is restricted to fragments of a certain arity (e.g., binary compositions akin to Chomsky Normal Form) or if it can handle more general And-rules. This detail about the scope of the method should be stated explicitly.

2.  **Clarity of Experiments (Major Concern):** The experimental section is extremely difficult to understand for a reader not already intimately familiar with the authors' previous work. This significantly hinders the evaluation of the paper's contributions.
    *   **Data Representation:** It is not clear how the raw data (videos, images) is converted into the sequences of terminal symbols that the algorithm operates on. For the event grammars, the description of the three different methods is confusing. For the image grammars, the paper states it follows the method from [17] to "quantize the images and learn the atomic patterns," but this is not self-contained. A reader should not have to consult another paper to understand the experimental setup.
    *   **Lack of Qualitative Results:** The paper claims to learn "structure," but provides almost no examples of the structures it learns. The results are presented purely through quantitative metrics (F-measure, perplexity). To be convincing, the paper should include visualizations of learned grammar fragments for both the event and image domains. What interesting compositional event structures were discovered? What do the learned And-Or templates for animal faces look like? Showing that the learned structures are more meaningful or interpretable than those from competing methods would be a much stronger argument for the superiority of this approach than perplexity scores alone.
    *   **Inaccessibility:** The experimental presentation seems tailored to a very specific audience (likely those who follow S. C. Zhu's group's work closely). For a broad conference like NIPS, the paper must be accessible to a general machine learning audience.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper presents a novel and promising approach to a difficult and important problem. The core idea of learning unified And-Or fragments is elegant and the technical approach seems sound. However, the paper is severely hampered by a lack of clarity, particularly in the experimental section. The contributions are difficult to assess because it is unclear what was actually done in the experiments and what was learned. The absence of qualitative results makes it impossible to judge whether the method truly discovers meaningful structure.

While the core ideas have merit, the paper in its current form is not ready for publication. I lean towards rejection, but would be willing to reconsider if the authors can make substantial revisions to address the clarity issues.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   Revise the experimental section to be completely self-contained. Clearly describe the data preprocessing, the representation of terminals, and the nature of the relations in the And-rules for each experiment.
*   Provide concrete, qualitative examples of the learned grammar fragments in the main paper. Show the interesting structures that the algorithm discovers and contrast them with baseline methods.
*   Clarify the technical details regarding the likelihood gain calculation and the scope/limitations of the types of fragments that can be learned.
*   If the paper is accepted, extensive supplementary material with even more experimental details, derivations, and examples of learned structures will be essential for reproducibility and for the community to fully appreciate the contribution.
