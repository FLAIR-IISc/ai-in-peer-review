Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes an unsupervised method to jointly learn both the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs), within a unified, context-free formalization that is agnostic to the data domain. The key technical idea is to iteratively introduce And-Or fragments—each comprising one new And-node and multiple sibling Or-nodes—chosen to maximize posterior probability under a sparsity prior. The method approximates the data likelihood with Viterbi likelihood, and derives a closed-form posterior-gain objective that factors into a prior gain (favoring compact grammars) and a likelihood gain computable from sufficient statistics (an n-gram tensor over fragment configurations and a context matrix), under an unambiguity assumption. Search proceeds greedily or with beam search over candidate fragments; rule probabilities for new Or-nodes are set from the sufficient statistics.

The authors position their approach as a unification and extension of structure learning for PCFGs and AOGs, building on Bayesian model merging (Stolcke & Omohundro, 1994), unsupervised grammar induction (e.g., Klein & Manning, 2004; Tu & Honavar, 2008), and AOGs/SPNs (Zhu & Mumford, 2006; Poon & Domingos, 2011; Si & Zhu, 2013). Unlike prior AOG structure learning for images or events that learn And- and Or-structure in separate phases, this work learns them jointly via And-Or fragments, arguing improved robustness and compactness.

Empirically, the method is evaluated on two domains: event grammars from human activity videos and image grammars for animal face sketches. On events, three variants are compared, including a direct grounding to atomic actions with both “co-occurring” and “followed-by” relations learned jointly; F-measure is competitive with or better than prior ID-sequence-based approaches. On images, the method outperforms a prior And-Or template learner (Si & Zhu, 2013) on synthetic data (F-measure, KL) and achieves substantially lower perplexity on a real dataset.

Strengths
- Clear, unified formalization of context-free stochastic AOGs that highlights their relation to SCFGs and SPNs.
- Joint structure learning via And-Or fragments is well-motivated and addresses data sparsity and redundancy seen when learning And/Or structure separately.
- Derivation of a posterior-gain objective that can be computed from sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor and context matrix) is elegant and potentially practical.
- Solid empirical improvements on image grammar learning (synthetic and real) and competitive results on event parsing; broader applicability across domains is promising.
- The paper is careful to connect with Viterbi training literature and unambiguity bias in unsupervised grammar learning.

Weaknesses
- Scalability and complexity are insufficiently analyzed. Constructing and searching n-gram tensors and context matrices for many relations/fragment arities could be costly; only small-scale timing (minutes for ~5000 samples, ~10 terminals/sample) is reported.
- The generality claim (agnostic to data type, applicable to natural language) is not supported by experiments on language, nor by comparisons to standard unsupervised parsing baselines (e.g., Klein & Manning, 2004; Spitkovsky et al., 2010).
- The unambiguity assumption underlying the sufficient-statistics derivation is strong; the impact of violations on accuracy and computations is not studied.
- Limited ablations. It’s unclear how much each design choice (And-Or vs And-only fragments, sparsity prior α, beam width, fragment arity, context definition) contributes.
- Event experiments use small test sets; significance tests or variance are not reported. Some evaluation choices (e.g., converting to Or at termination, CYK details) could be elaborated.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with a coherent probabilistic objective and reasonable heuristics (Viterbi likelihood, sparsity prior). The sufficient-statistics formulation is a nice contribution. However, formal complexity analysis and empirical scalability characterization are missing. The unambiguity assumption deserves empirical stress tests. Experimental comparisons are appropriate in vision/event settings but omit critical NLP baselines given the stated scope. More thorough error analysis and ablations would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The AOG formalism and And-Or fragments are clearly defined. The posterior gain decomposition is explained, though key derivations are deferred to the supplement. Some details could be clarified: precise construction of contexts, fragment candidate generation and pruning, complexity of n-gram tensor mining, smoothing used for perplexity, and runtime/memory profiles.

Originality
The joint And-Or fragment induction and the sufficient-statistics factorization of the posterior gain are original and useful. The work thoughtfully integrates ideas from PCFG induction, SPNs, and AOGs. While related to Bayesian model merging and prior AOG learning, the unification and practical learning framework appear novel.

Significance
If scalable, the approach could have impact across multiple modalities where compositionality and reconfigurability matter. The image results show strong gains; the event results are competitive. However, the lack of NLP experiments and limited scalability analysis temper the breadth of the claimed generality. Demonstrating performance on standard unsupervised parsing benchmarks would substantially increase significance for the NIPS community.

Questions for the authors
- What is the computational complexity per iteration in terms of number of terminals, current nonterminals, fragment arity n, and number of relation types? What are memory costs for the n-gram tensors and context matrices?
- How are candidate fragments generated and pruned? What beam sizes and n values are used in the experiments?
- How sensitive are results to α, sa, so, and to the unambiguity assumption? What happens when parses are ambiguous?
- Can you report runtimes on the image and event datasets and show scaling beyond 5k samples or with larger vocabularies?
- Any results on natural language (e.g., WSJ PTB) or synthetic recursive languages to substantiate the generality claim?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments on natural language (unsupervised constituency/PCFG induction) with comparisons to Klein & Manning (2004), Spitkovsky et al. (2010), Tu & Honavar (2008).
- Provide complexity analysis and scaling experiments; include runtime/memory and beam/arity sensitivity curves.
- Include ablations: And-only vs And-Or fragments; remove sparsity prior; vary α; alternative context definitions.
- Analyze robustness to ambiguity; quantify how often the unambiguity assumption is violated and its effect.
- Report statistical significance and error breakdowns; add non-grammar baselines (e.g., SPNs without explicit structure learning) where appropriate.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and elegant joint And-Or fragment learning with a principled posterior-gain objective.
- Strong improvements on image grammar learning; competitive event grammar results.
- Clear, unified formulation linking AOGs to SCFGs and SPNs; potentially broad applicability.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing NLP experiments despite generality claims; lacks comparison to standard unsupervised parsing baselines.
- Scalability and complexity not convincingly addressed; limited empirical scale.
- Strong unambiguity assumption not empirically validated; limited ablations.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper contains solid ideas and promising results in vision/event domains, but falls short on scalability analysis and breadth of evaluation, particularly for natural language where the approach is positioned to apply. Addressing the questions and suggested experiments would likely move this to an accept.
