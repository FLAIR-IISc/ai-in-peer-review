This paper proposes an unsupervised method for learning stochastic And-Or grammars by inducing unified And-Or fragments rather than alternating separate searches for And- and Or-structures. The approach formalizes a context-free And-Or grammar that is data-type agnostic and optimizes a sparsity-regularized posterior, using Viterbi likelihood. A key technical idea is to score candidate And-Or fragments efficiently via sufficient statistics: n-gram tensors over child combinations and context matrices over surrounding patterns, enabling greedy/beam search. The method is evaluated on event parsing and visual object parsing, showing competitive-to-better quantitative performance than prior work, including And-Or templates in vision and earlier event grammars (e.g., Si et al.; Zhao & Zhu, NIPS 2011). The formulation also connects to unsupervised grammar induction (Stolcke & Omohundro; Klein & Manning) and to SPNs (Poon & Domingos).

Quality: The learning objective and search strategy are well-motivated, and the fragment-level posterior gain computation is technically sound in spirit. However, I could not verify the supplemental derivations: the factorization of likelihood gain and the role of the “coherence” quantities need clearer, step-by-step proofs and explicit assumptions. It is unclear whether the “surrogate measure” of likelihood gain is exact under unambiguity/Viterbi or an approximation used only for ranking; please state this precisely and quantify the approximation error if any.

Clarity: The experimental section is very hard to follow without prior familiarity with S. C. Zhu’s pipeline. Critical details are missing: how examples are represented across domains, how contexts are constructed, what concrete fragments were learned, and end-to-end parsing procedures. Please include extensive supplementary material with full data preprocessing, feature/quantization steps, grammar fragments learned, parse trees, and qualitative examples.

Originality: Inducing unified And-Or fragments with efficient sufficient-statistics scoring is novel and addresses limitations of separate And/Or searches.

Significance: If made reproducible, the method could be impactful across modalities.

Requests/concerns:
- Clarify whether the likelihood-gain expression is exact or approximate; define “surrogate” precisely.
- State all assumptions (e.g., unambiguity, Viterbi training) used in derivations and discuss robustness if violated.
- Does the method support And-rules with arity > 2 or require CNF binarization? If binarized, how are fragments and statistics adapted?
- Provide complexity analysis of fragment search and memory cost of tensors/contexts.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Important problem; elegant, unified fragment induction.
- Efficient scoring via sufficient statistics; promising cross-domain results.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Experimental presentation is opaque; limited qualitative insight.
- Derivations and scope (exactness, arity) are insufficiently clarified.
- Limited evidence that learned structure is more interesting/expressive than baselines; add qualitative comparisons.
