## Review of Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars

This paper presents a novel unsupervised approach to learning the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs). The authors address a key challenge in applying SAGs – the difficulty of manually specifying grammar structures – by proposing an iterative learning algorithm that induces compositions and reconfigurations simultaneously through the discovery of “And-Or fragments.” The approach aims to optimize the posterior probability of the grammar and is evaluated on both event parsing and image grammar learning tasks. 

SAGs have seen increasing use in modeling compositional and reconfigurable data, drawing connections to work in natural language processing (NLP) [8, 9] and image modeling [1, 2, 3]. The motivation for unsupervised learning is clear, as manual grammar specification is a significant bottleneck.  The paper correctly identifies existing strategies focusing either on fixed grammar structures optimized for parameters [11, 12, 13] or separate learning of compositions and reconfigurations [17, 18].  The proposed unified approach to fragment learning is a significant contribution, addressing the limitations of prior work.

**Strengths:**

The paper's primary strength lies in its technical novelty.  The idea of learning both structure and parameters concurrently, and unifying the search for compositions and reconfigurations within the And-Or fragment concept, is a strong contribution. The formulation of a computationally efficient method for evaluating the posterior gain of adding these fragments, leveraging sufficient statistics, is also noteworthy.  The authors demonstrate a sound theoretical understanding of the problem and present a well-motivated algorithm. The problem addressed is important; the ability to automatically learn the structure of these grammars would broaden their applicability.  Finally, the empirical results demonstrate competitive performance on the chosen tasks, exceeding prior work in some cases.

**Weaknesses:**

While technically sound, the paper suffers from clarity issues, particularly concerning the experimental setup and results. The descriptions of the experiments on event grammars and image grammars are insufficient for a reader unfamiliar with the related work from S.-C. Zhu’s group to fully understand the methodology and interpret the results. What specific representations were used for event data beyond “ID sequences”? How were images quantized and what specifically did the learned atomic patterns represent? A more detailed explanation of these aspects is crucial. 

The supplementary material is mentioned as providing derivations, but the reviewer found these difficult to verify. Clearer, more detailed derivations would significantly strengthen the paper. Moreover, it's unclear whether the “surrogate measure” for likelihood gain is an exact computation or an approximation, and clarification on this point is needed.  

Further, the paper doesn’t explicitly address the scalability of the approach to fragments with arity greater than two, limiting its expressiveness potentially. Is the approach intrinsically tied to a Chomsky normal form grammar? This limitation should be explicitly acknowledged.

The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating quantitative improvements, lacks qualitative insights.  The paper would be greatly enhanced by including examples of learned grammar structures – visualizations of the discovered compositions and reconfigurations. Currently, the results are largely numerical and provide little intuition into *what* the algorithm is actually learning.  A key question is whether the proposed method learns more *interesting* or insightful structures compared to existing approaches, and this requires qualitative analysis.

**Originality:**

The unification of composition and reconfiguration learning through And-Or fragments is a novel contribution.  While building on previous work in structure learning [14, 15, 16], the specific formulation and algorithmic approach presented here represent a significant advancement.

**Significance:**

Successful unsupervised learning of SAG structures would significantly broaden their applicability to various domains, reducing the reliance on costly manual specification.  The improved performance on event and image grammar learning tasks suggests the potential for real-world impact.  The efficient computation of posterior gain is also a significant contribution that could facilitate the application of this approach to larger datasets and more complex models.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and well-motivated approach to unsupervised structure learning.
*   Technically sound with a clear formulation and efficient computation.
*   Demonstrated competitive performance on relevant tasks.
*   Addresses a critical bottleneck in the application of SAGs.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in experimental descriptions and results.
*   Supplemental material requires further clarification and detailed derivations.
*   Limited qualitative analysis of learned structures.
*   Scalability to higher-arity fragments is unclear.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. While the technical aspects are strong, the lack of clarity and insufficient experimental detail hinder a full assessment of its impact. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with major revisions**. The authors must significantly improve the clarity of the experimental sections, provide more detailed derivations in the supplementary material, and include qualitative examples of the learned grammar structures. Addressing these concerns would substantially strengthen the paper and demonstrate the full potential of their approach.  A more thorough discussion of the limitations of the method and potential avenues for future work would also be beneficial.
