This paper presents a novel unsupervised method for learning the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars. The core idea is to start with a trivial initial grammar that perfectly explains the training data and then iteratively introduce more general structures. This is achieved by inducing "And-Or fragments" in a bottom-up fashion, a process designed to greedily optimize the posterior probability of the grammar. The authors frame this work as an extension of prior research on grammar induction in both natural language processing and computer vision, with the key contribution being a unified approach to learning compositional (And-rules) and reconfigurable (Or-rules) aspects of the grammar.

The paper’s main strength lies in its sensible and well-motivated algorithm. The unified learning of And-Or fragments is an elegant approach that addresses the challenge of discovering both compositionality and alternation simultaneously. While the paper claims to generalize previous structure learning work, it appears to be more of a special case. However, this restriction is a pragmatic and reasonable choice, as it makes an otherwise difficult problem tractable and leads to a robust algorithm. The empirical evaluation demonstrates strong performance on both event and image grammar learning tasks when compared against recent, related methods.

The primary weakness is the limited scope of the experimental evaluation, which could be improved with more extensive comparisons. The claim of outperforming previous approaches would be significantly strengthened by including more established baselines from the grammar induction literature. Specifically, the Bayesian model merging approach of Stolcke and Omohondro (1994, ref. 14) would be a highly relevant and valuable point of comparison. Furthermore, the authors should revise their claims of "generalizing" prior work to more accurately position their method as a powerful technique for a specific but important class of grammars.

In summary, this paper makes a solid contribution by presenting an effective and principled algorithm for unsupervised structure learning of a particular class of stochastic grammars. The methodology is sound and the results are promising. While the positioning and experimental comparisons could be improved, the work is a valuable advancement for the field.
