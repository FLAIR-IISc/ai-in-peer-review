Summary
This paper proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs), with a unified, data-agnostic formalization that covers images and events. The central idea is to iteratively add And-Or fragments—each comprising one new And-node and several sibling Or-nodes—to maximize the posterior of the grammar under a sparsity prior. Likelihood is approximated with Viterbi likelihood (unambiguity bias), and the posterior gain of a candidate fragment is computed efficiently using sufficient statistics derived from an n-gram tensor over child choices and a context matrix capturing co-occurrence with surrounding structure. The authors argue this unifies learning of compositions (And-rules) and reconfigurations (Or-rules) and improves robustness and efficiency versus learning And- and Or-fragments separately. Experiments on event parsing and image grammar learning show comparable or better performance than prior work (e.g., [17, 18]) on F-measure and perplexity.

The work is positioned relative to: And-Or grammars in vision and events [1,3,7,17,18], SPNs [10], and grammar induction in NLP [11,12,14–16]. While the paper claims to “generalize” prior structure-learning methods, the method focuses on a specific context-free subclass of SAGs with an unambiguity assumption in scoring. I view this restriction as a reasonable specialization that makes the learning problem tractable and implementable across modalities.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The fragment-level posterior gain derivation (likelihood via Viterbi + size prior) and the use of sufficient statistics are sensible and well-motivated. The unambiguity assumption to avoid repeated reparsing is clearly stated and practically useful, though it should be emphasized that it is an approximation whose impact is not quantified. The claim that And-Or fragments suffice to construct any context-free And-Or grammar is plausible; a brief formal justification or pointer would strengthen the paper.
- Empirics: The image experiments include both synthetic (with known grammar) and a real dataset; the event experiments evaluate detection quality via F-measure. Results are positive overall. That said, baselines are limited: for structure learning, a stronger set (e.g., Bayesian Model Merging [14], Klein & Manning 2004 [12], and related PCFG/DP-PCFG approaches [9]) would better contextualize performance; for models closely related to SPNs, structure-learning baselines would also be informative. Ablations (e.g., And-Or vs And-only/Or-only search; with/without context coherence; full likelihood vs Viterbi; effect of beam width; sensitivity to α, sa, so) are missing but would help attribute gains.
- Efficiency: The approach appears practical (minutes for 5k samples), which is a strong point; more detailed complexity and memory analysis of the tensor/matrix construction and search would be helpful.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with an accessible unified formalization and helpful figures. A few clarifications would improve readability:
- Precisely define grammar size and the coding constants sa, so; explain how they are chosen and sensitivity to α.
- More details on “context” across modalities (how context windows are defined for images vs events).
- Provide pseudocode in the main text (or a concise flowchart) for the search procedure and termination criterion.
- Expand on the unambiguity assumption and its practical check/violation handling.
- Improve figure legibility (small fonts in Figs. 1–3).

Originality
The main novelty lies in (i) learning with And-Or fragments to jointly induce composition and reconfiguration, and (ii) an efficient posterior-gain objective using coherence-like statistics over n-gram tensors and contexts. While related to classical merging/biclustering/MDL-based grammar induction [14–16] and to SPN structure learning [10], the formulation for SAGs across modalities and the particular sufficient-statistics-based scoring is a useful and, to my knowledge, novel combination. The claim of “generalization” over prior structure-learning work seems overstated; this is better framed as a principled specialization to a tractable subclass.

Significance
Learning structured, reconfigurable grammars directly from unannotated images and events is impactful, and the method’s ability to operate across modalities is appealing. The empirical gains over prior SAG learning methods are promising. With stronger baselines and ablations, the contribution could influence practical grammar induction for compositional vision and activity understanding, and inform SPN/grammar hybrid structure learning.

Pros
- Unified, modality-agnostic formalization of context-free SAGs.
- Principled objective (posterior with sparsity prior) and efficient scoring via sufficient statistics.
- And-Or fragment search unifies composition and reconfiguration; conceptually clean and pragmatic.
- Positive results on both event and image domains; practical runtime.

Cons
- “Generalization” claim is misleading; method targets a restricted subclass and relies on unambiguity.
- Baselines are limited; missing comparison to Bayesian model merging [14] and classic PCFG induction [12], as well as DP-PCFG [9].
- No ablations to isolate contributions (fragments vs separate And/Or search; Viterbi vs full likelihood).
- Some implementation details (α, sa/so, context definition, complexity) and theoretical justifications are brief.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add comparisons to Stolcke & Omohundro (1994) [14] and Klein & Manning (2004) [12]; where applicable, include DP-PCFG [9] and SPN structure-learning baselines.
- Provide ablations: (a) And-only and Or-only fragment searches; (b) remove context coherence term; (c) Viterbi vs sum over parses; (d) beam width and random restart sensitivity; (e) α, sa, so sensitivity.
- Clarify context definitions per modality; report the effect of different context scopes.
- Include a short proof/sketch that And-Or fragments can construct any context-free And-Or grammar.
- Report ambiguity rates in learned grammars and any heuristics to resolve conflicts.
- Release code and processed datasets to support reproducibility.

Overall evaluation
A solid contribution to structure learning for a particular, practically relevant class of grammars. The approach is sensible and effective, and the cross-modal demonstrations are valuable. Strengthening baselines and adding ablations would substantially improve the paper and bolster its claims. I lean positive.
