This paper presents a novel approach to unsupervised structure learning of stochastic And-Or grammars, a powerful formalism for modeling compositional and reconfigurable data. The authors address a key challenge in utilizing these grammars – the difficulty of manually specifying their structure – by proposing an iterative algorithm that jointly learns both the grammar structure and its parameters. The method introduces And-Or fragments, optimizing posterior probability using a tractable formulation based on sufficient statistics. Empirical evaluation on event and image grammars demonstrates competitive performance compared to existing approaches.

The work builds upon previous research in stochastic grammars, extending ideas from natural language processing [8, 9] and sum-product networks [10] to more general data types [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7]. It acknowledges existing methods that learn parameters with fixed structures [11, 12, 13] and contrasts itself with those attempting structure learning [14, 15, 16, 17, 18]. The unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars, independent of the data type, is a notable contribution, allowing application to diverse domains. The introduction of And-Or fragments, learning compositions and reconfigurations simultaneously, represents a significant improvement over methods that treat these processes separately.

The strengths of the paper lie in its theoretically sound approach, particularly the posterior probability optimization and the efficient computation of likelihood gains using sufficient statistics. The formalization provides a general framework for learning hierarchical structures in data. The experiments, while limited in scope, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method in two different domains. The connection to existing work in natural language processing, specifically the concept of unambiguous grammars [20, 21], is well-articulated.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The most significant concern is the lack of discussion regarding algorithmic tractability and scalability. While the authors mention efficiency improvements, the practical limits of the approach concerning the number of training samples or the complexity of the learned grammars remain unclear. The paper does not address how the algorithm performs with thousands of datapoints, a realistic scenario in many applications.

Furthermore, the evaluation primarily focuses on event and image grammars. Given the historical roots of stochastic grammars in natural language processing, the absence of experiments on standard NLP datasets like the Penn Treebank is a major oversight. Comparisons to established methods for unsupervised parsing, such as the work of Klein and Manning [12], are crucial to establish the significance of this contribution within the NLP community. The choice of evaluation metrics, while appropriate for the chosen datasets, may not fully capture the nuances of grammar learning.

The presentation could also be improved. While the technical details are generally clear, the connection between the mathematical formulations and the intuitive understanding of the algorithm is sometimes lacking. Figure 1, illustrating the learning process, is helpful, but could benefit from clearer labeling and explanations.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to jointly learning structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars.
*   Tractable formulation for posterior probability optimization.
*   Competitive results on event and image grammar learning.
*   Unified formalization applicable to diverse data types.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of discussion regarding scalability and algorithmic tractability.
*   Absence of experiments on natural language datasets.
*   Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
*   Presentation could be clearer in connecting theory to intuition.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a technically sound and potentially significant contribution to the field of unsupervised structure learning. However, the lack of scalability discussion and the absence of NLP experiments raise concerns about its practical impact and broader applicability. I recommend a **weak accept**, contingent on the authors addressing the scalability issue and including a brief experiment on a standard natural language dataset in a revised version. Further clarification on the computational complexity and potential optimizations would greatly strengthen the paper.
