Summary
The paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or Grammars (AOGs) and an unsupervised algorithm to learn both grammar structure and parameters from unannotated data. The central idea is to search over And-Or fragments—small subgrammars that jointly introduce a new And-node, its child Or-nodes, and their alternative expansions—rather than alternating or decoupling searches for And-only or Or-only extensions. The learning objective is the posterior P(G|X) under a sparsity prior on grammar size, with the likelihood approximated by the Viterbi likelihood. The key technical contribution is an efficient computation of the posterior gain of a candidate fragment using sufficient statistics derived from the data: an n-gram tensor over configurations and a context matrix. Under an unambiguity assumption, the likelihood gain factorizes into two “coherence” terms, and the prior gain reflects a trade-off between fragment size and the amount of data it explains (via reductions). Experiments on event parsing and image grammar induction show competitive or superior performance to prior work, including improved F-measure for events and lower perplexity on a real image dataset compared to [17].

Relation to prior work
The paper sits at the intersection of grammar induction (e.g., PCFG learning from unannotated corpora [11,12,14,15,16]) and AOGs for images and events [1,3,7,17,18], and connects to structure learning in sum-product networks [10]. Compared to prior AOG structure learning [17,18], it proposes a unified And-Or fragment search motivated by posterior optimization, rather than heuristic or separate And/Or expansions. In NLP, it is conceptually related to Bayesian model merging [14] and iterative biclustering for PCFGs [16], but extends to multimodal relations (spatial/temporal) and the AOG formalism. The use of Viterbi likelihood and unambiguity bias echoes [20,21].

Strengths
- Clear and well-motivated formulation of And-Or fragments that unifies composition and reconfiguration, addressing data scarcity and redundancy issues that arise when learning And- or Or-fragments separately.
- Posterior-driven search with an explicit sparsity prior; efficient gain computation via sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor and context matrix), making greedy/beam search practical.
- Unified formalization of stochastic AOGs agnostic to data type; demonstrated on both events and images.
- Empirical results show competitive or better performance than prior AOG structure learning, including a large perplexity improvement on real images.

Weaknesses
- Clarity of the experimental section is limited: representations, relations, and what was actually learned are hard to reconstruct without strong familiarity with prior AOG work. Many choices appear in the supplement or are assumed known.
- The status of the “surrogate” likelihood gain is unclear: is the factorized gain exact under the unambiguity assumption, or an approximation? The derivation is not shown.
- Ambiguity assumptions and their practical impact are under-explained; robustness to ambiguity/noise is unclear.
- The scope of fragments is not fully specified: can arity be greater than 2 and are arbitrary relation types per And-rule allowed? Is conversion to CNF required?
- Limited qualitative analysis: few concrete examples of learned fragments/parses; hard to judge the interpretability and “interestingness” of discovered structure.
- Evaluation lacks ablations (e.g., And-only vs Or-only vs And-Or fragments, with/without unambiguity bias) and additional baselines; some datasets are small and the event setup introduces synthetic variants.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The posterior objective, fragment design, and sufficient-statistics-based gain computation are well motivated. However, key derivations are deferred: the exact conditions under which the likelihood gain factorization holds, and how the optimal Or-rule probabilities follow, should be made explicit. Experimental methodology seems careful, but documentation is insufficient for full reproducibility.

Clarity
Core ideas are clearly stated, but the paper assumes familiarity with prior AOG pipelines (feature quantization, relation specification, parsing). The experimental section should be self-contained. Figure explanations and notation (e.g., RD, CM, sa/so) could be streamlined and defined earlier. More qualitative figures of learned grammars and parses would greatly aid accessibility.

Originality
High. The And-Or fragment search and the derivation of an efficient posterior gain based on coherence of configuration/context statistics are novel within AOG learning. The unification across data types under a single learning framework is appealing.

Significance
Potentially significant. If made accessible and reproducible, this approach could become a standard tool for unsupervised structure induction in AOGs and related graphical grammars. The empirical gains on both synthetic and real data support impact, though broader comparisons and qualitative evidence would strengthen the case.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, principled approach to AOG structure learning with efficient search.
- Unifies composition and reconfiguration; addresses known pitfalls of separate And/Or induction.
- Demonstrated improvements on both image and event domains.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient experimental detail and qualitative illustrations reduce accessibility and verifiability.
- Key derivations and assumptions (unambiguity, exactness of gain) not fully specified in the main text.
- Limited ablations and baselines; small test sets in events.

Questions for the authors
- Is the presented likelihood gain formula exact under the stated unambiguity assumption, or an approximation? Please provide a clear main-text derivation or theorem with conditions.
- What is the precise definition of “unambiguity” used, and how sensitive is performance when data induce ambiguous parses? Any strategy to relax this assumption?
- Can fragments have arbitrary arity and mixed relation types per And-rule? Do you require CNF-style binarization at any stage?
- How are relation types specified and scored in practice (e.g., spatial offsets, temporal Allen relations)? Are they learned or discretized a priori?
- How are sa and so chosen; how sensitive are results to α, beam width, and encoding choices?
- What is the computational complexity per iteration and total runtime as a function of n, fragment size, and dataset size?

Suggestions for improvement (especially for camera-ready)
- Add a detailed experimental appendix (or expanded main text): data preprocessing, terminal/feature definitions, relation parameterization, parsing/inference algorithms, hyperparameters, runtime, and code pointers.
- Provide multiple qualitative examples: learned And-Or fragments, parse trees of images/events, and side-by-side comparisons with baselines.
- Include ablations (And-only, Or-only, And-Or without coherence factorization, with/without unambiguity bias) and sensitivity analyses.
- Clarify and, if possible, move essential derivations from the supplement into the paper; explicitly state assumptions under which the gain factorization holds.
- Improve figure readability and define notation (RD, CM, sa, so) upon first use.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on substantially improved clarity and experimental documentation. The core ideas are novel and promising, the method appears technically sound, and the reported results are encouraging, but the paper needs to be more accessible to readers outside the AOG literature.

Reviewer confidence
Medium.
