This paper proposes an unsupervised structure-learning method for stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) that jointly induces compositions and reconfigurations via “And-Or fragments.” The authors first give a unified, modality-agnostic formalization of context-free AOGs that covers images, events, and text. Learning proceeds from a trivial grammar that exactly reproduces the training set; at each iteration, the algorithm adds an And-Or fragment A → O1…On with sibling Or-nodes Oi → {aij}, and updates parses to maximize a sparsity-regularized posterior. A Viterbi likelihood (unambiguity bias) replaces the marginal likelihood. The key technical contribution is an efficient computation of posterior gain for candidate fragments using sufficient statistics: an n-gram tensor that counts configurations covered by the fragment and a context matrix that counts co-occurrences of those configurations with their contexts. Under an unambiguity assumption, the likelihood gain factorizes into two coherence measures over these statistics; the prior gain penalizes fragment size and rewards reductions. Empirically, the method is evaluated on event parsing (two video datasets) and image grammars (synthetic and real animal-face datasets), with comparable or better performance than prior AOG structure-learning methods [17, 18].

Relation to prior work: The formulation connects AOGs to SCFGs [8, 9] and decomposable SPNs [10]. Compared with prior structure learning in NLP (Bayesian model merging [14], ADIOS [15], biclustering [16]) that typically separates rule induction and parameter estimation, this work directly targets posterior improvement and couples And/Or induction. In vision, it extends And-Or learning for images/events [17, 18] by learning unified fragments rather than alternating between composition and reconfiguration. The use of Viterbi training echoes findings in unsupervised syntax [20, 21].

Strengths
- Novel unified fragment induction: Learning And- and Or-structure jointly in a single fragment addresses data sparsity and redundancy that often plague separate searches. This is a clear conceptual advance over prior AOG learning.
- Principled objective and efficient scoring: A sparsity prior over grammar size and a closed-form posterior gain computable from sufficient statistics (n-gram tensor and context matrix) make the search tractable and interpretable.
- Cross-modal generality: One formalism and one algorithm applied to temporal events and spatial images is compelling and aligns with the community’s interest in structured, compositional generative models.
- Empirical signal: On events, the approach is competitive or better than sequence-based baselines; on images, perplexity/F-measure improvements over [17] are substantial.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Derivations and “surrogate” likelihood gain: The paper states that the likelihood gain “can be factorized” and “entails” optimal Or-rule probabilities, but details are deferred. It is unclear whether the factorization is exact under stated assumptions (Viterbi + unambiguity) or an approximation. Please include full derivations in the main paper or a clearly referenced supplement, with explicit assumptions and error bounds if any approximation is used.
- Scope of unambiguity assumption: How strong is the assumption of grammar unambiguity in practice? What happens when parses are mildly ambiguous? A small ablation varying ambiguity (e.g., by adding/removing competing rules) would help calibrate robustness.
- Fragment arity and normal form: In theory, fragments allow A → O1…On. In practice, do you restrict to binary (CNF) for tractability, or can n > 2 be learned efficiently? How do runtime and memory scale with n and with the size of Or-option sets mi? Please specify the fragment-generation space and beam settings used.
- Experimental clarity and accessibility: The event experiments, especially Methods 2/3, are difficult to follow without familiarity with prior pipelines from [18] and related work. The paper should clearly and self-containedly describe:
  - How atomic actions are represented (binary vectors, durations, overlap handling), how “co-occurrence” vs “followed-by” relations are encoded, and how contexts are defined for the statistics.
  - The exact preprocessing/quantization for images, how spatial relations are discretized (bins for relative position/scale/rotation), and how smoothing for perplexity is done.
  - Splits, hyperparameters (α, sa, so), beam width, random restart counts, and runtime/memory footprints.
- Qualitative insight: The paper is light on qualitative illustrations of learned structure. Showing a few learned And-Or fragments, parses of event sequences, and image part hierarchies would make the contribution much more concrete and persuasive.
- Comparisons and ablations: It would strengthen the case to include:
  - An ablation that learns only And-fragments or only Or-fragments to quantify the benefit of unified fragments.
  - A comparison against a strong SCFG/SPN structure learner, if applicable, to highlight advantages unique to AOGs.
  - Sensitivity to α and to the choice of relation sets.

Quality
- Overall technically sound and well-motivated. The posterior objective and sufficient-statistics-based gain are appealing. However, important derivations are not presently verifiable, and key assumptions (unambiguity; Viterbi approximation) deserve empirical stress tests.

Clarity
- The core idea is clear, but exposition needs to be more self-contained. Many experimental details are implicit or rely on readers’ familiarity with earlier AOG work. Figures would benefit from clearer captions and concrete examples grounded in actual data.

Originality
- The unified And-Or fragment induction and the posterior-gain computation via tensor/matrix coherence are original and differentiate this work from prior grammar-induction and AOG-learning methods.

Significance
- High potential impact: a general, efficient approach to unsupervised structure learning for compositional, reconfigurable models across modalities. The quantitative gains on images are notable; with improved presentation and qualitative evidence, the work could influence future grammar-based modeling in vision and activity understanding.

Suggestions for the authors
- Provide full derivations of the likelihood/prior gain, conditions for exactness, and complexity analysis.
- Add detailed, self-contained descriptions of data representations, relation parameterizations, and evaluation protocols.
- Include qualitative visualizations of learned fragments and parses.
- Report ablations (And-only, Or-only, unified; with/without unambiguity assumption) and sensitivity to α, beam size.
- Clarify how sufficient statistics are collected/updated incrementally and memory usage for high-arity fragments.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually novel and broadly applicable approach to AOG structure learning.
- Principled objective with efficient scoring; promising empirical results across two modalities.
- Addresses a long-standing challenge (joint composition/reconfiguration induction) with a clean mechanism.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient derivational detail; ambiguity about exactness of the likelihood-gain factorization.
- Experimental section is hard to reproduce and understand without prior familiarity; limited qualitative insight.
- Limited analysis of assumptions (unambiguity, Viterbi) and fragment-arity scalability.

Overall: I lean toward acceptance if the authors substantially expand the derivations and clarify the experiments with concrete, accessible examples and ablations.
