

# Review of "Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars"

This paper presents a unified formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars and proposes an unsupervised approach to learn both the structure and parameters of such grammars. The authors address the important problem of grammar induction in a unified manner across different data types (images, events), extending previous work on structure learning of natural language grammars [14-16] and recent work on image/event grammars [17-18]. The key innovation is learning "And-Or fragments" that systematically cover multiple compositions and reconfigurations, avoiding the separate search for And-fragments and Or-fragments used in prior approaches.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound in its core proposal. The authors present a formalization of stochastic And-Or grammars that is agnostic to data type, which is valuable for unifying different modeling approaches. The proposed method efficiently computes the posterior gain of adding grammar fragments using sufficient statistics, making the learning process computationally feasible. The empirical evaluation demonstrates competitive or superior performance on event grammar and image grammar learning tasks compared to previous approaches. However, the paper would benefit from clearer derivations of the likelihood gain formula (Section 3.3), particularly regarding whether the "surrogate measure" is an exact calculation or approximation. The paper also does not clarify whether the method extends to fragments with arity greater than 2 or is limited to Chomsky normal form, which is important for understanding the approach's generality.

**Clarity:** The paper has significant clarity issues, particularly in the experimental sections. The description of how event data was represented (Section 4.1) is difficult to follow without familiarity with Zhu's group's prior work. For instance, it's unclear how "vector sequences" were processed, what "context" meant in the context matrix (Figure 2), or how the grammar learned from raw vectors differs from the ID sequence approach. Similarly, the image grammar experiments (Section 4.2) lack concrete examples of the learned structures, making it hard to assess whether the approach discovers meaningful patterns. The paper would greatly benefit from more detailed experimental setup descriptions and qualitative examples of learned grammars.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by proposing a unified approach to learning both compositions and reconfigurations through And-Or fragments, rather than separate searches. This addresses a limitation in prior work where separate searches for And-fragments and Or-fragments can be error-prone with scarce data. The paper clearly differentiates its approach from previous work on structure learning, particularly in the introduction and contributions section.

**Significance:** The problem of unsupervised grammar learning is important for modeling compositional data across domains. The approach's ability to learn from different data types (images, events) with a unified framework is significant. The empirical results demonstrate practical utility, though the lack of qualitative examples makes it difficult to assess whether the learned structures provide meaningful insights beyond quantitative performance metrics.

**Strengths:** The unified approach to learning grammar structure is well-motivated and technically sound. The efficient computation of fragment value using sufficient statistics is a valuable contribution. The paper successfully demonstrates the approach on two different domains (event parsing and image parsing) with competitive results.

**Weaknesses:** The experimental presentation is opaque to readers unfamiliar with the authors' prior work. The paper lacks concrete examples of the grammars learned, making it hard to assess the quality of the discovered structures. The theoretical derivations need clarification, particularly regarding the likelihood gain calculation. The paper would benefit from more detailed experimental setup descriptions and qualitative analysis of the learned grammars.

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:* The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach. The unified framework for learning grammar structure is a significant contribution that could benefit multiple domains. The technical approach is sound, and the empirical results are promising.

*Con:* The paper's clarity issues and lack of qualitative examples significantly limit its accessibility and impact. Without more concrete illustrations of the learned grammars, it's difficult to assess whether the approach discovers meaningful structure beyond improving quantitative metrics. The unclear theoretical derivations and lack of discussion about the method's limitations (e.g., arity constraints) are concerning.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The paper has significant potential but requires substantial improvements to the experimental presentation and theoretical clarity. The authors should provide more detailed experimental descriptions, concrete examples of the grammars learned (including visualizations), and clarifications of the theoretical derivations. Supplemental material should include complete experimental details and qualitative examples of discovered structures to make the work accessible to a broader audience. With these revisions, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
