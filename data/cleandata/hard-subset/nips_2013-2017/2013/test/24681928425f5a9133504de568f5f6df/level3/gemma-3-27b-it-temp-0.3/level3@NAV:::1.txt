## Review of “Unsupervised Structure Learning of Stochastic And-Or Grammars”

This paper presents a method for unsupervised learning of the structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (SAGs). The authors propose an iterative approach that begins with a trivial grammar and progressively induces compositions and reconfigurations, optimizing the posterior probability of the grammar at each step. The core contribution lies in the unified learning of And-Or fragments, aiming for a more efficient and robust learning process compared to methods that treat compositions and reconfigurations separately. 

The work builds upon previous research in both natural language processing (NLP) and computer vision, extending structure learning techniques to the more general framework of SAGs. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, referencing relevant papers on grammar induction and probabilistic modeling. The proposed algorithm is sensible, leveraging a sparsity prior to encourage compact grammars and utilizing sufficient statistics for efficient posterior gain computation. 

A key strength of this paper is the reasonable approach of focusing on a specific class of grammars to achieve tractability. While the authors claim to “generalize” previous work, it’s accurate to characterize this as a special case – a limitation they acknowledge. This specialization allows for a more focused and effective algorithm. The experimental results on both event and image grammars demonstrate competitive or superior performance compared to existing methods.

However, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened. While the results are promising, the comparison to other methods is somewhat limited. Specifically, the authors should include a comparison to Stolcke and Omohondro (ref. 14), a foundational work in Bayesian grammar induction, as suggested. This would provide a more comprehensive benchmark and better contextualize the performance of the proposed approach. Further analysis of the learned grammar structures, beyond just quantitative metrics, would also be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-motivated approach to a challenging problem.
*   Sensible algorithm with a clear theoretical basis.
*   Competitive performance on relevant datasets.
*   Addresses a relevant problem in probabilistic modeling and structured prediction.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The claim of “generalization” is overstated.
*   Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, particularly with respect to baseline comparisons.



**Overall:** This is a solid contribution to the field of learning the structure of a particular class of grammars. The paper is well-written and clearly presents a technically sound approach. Addressing the concerns regarding the experimental evaluation would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
