- Proposes an unsupervised method to learn both structure and parameters of stochastic And-Or grammars (AOGs) from unannotated data.
- Introduces a unified, context-free formalization of AOGs (connecting to decomposable sum-product networks) that is modality-agnostic in scope.
- Presents a bottom-up structure learning procedure that incrementally inserts And-Or fragments to maximize a sparsity-regularized posterior.
- Key design: jointly learns compositions (And rules) and reconfigurations (Or choices) via And-Or fragments to reduce redundancy and improve robustness under data sparsity.
- Uses Viterbi likelihood (unambiguity-biased) and an MDL-style prior; derives posterior gain formula combining likelihood gain and prior.
- Computes efficient sufficient statistics (n-gram tensors and context matrices) so posterior gains can be evaluated without full re-parsing.
- Optimally assigns Or-rule probabilities directly from counts.
- Evaluated on event grammars for human activity videos and image grammars for animal faces, showing comparable or better performance than prior work.
- Builds on prior AOG, PCFG induction, model merging, and SPN literature; main novelty is And-Or fragments with efficient posterior gain computation.
- The “unified/agnostic” claim is narrowed by restricting to a context-free, unambiguous, decomposable subclass, which limits generality.
- Algorithm is sensible and technically sound under stated assumptions; posterior gain derivation and Viterbi use are plausible and computationally attractive.
- Main limitations: strong modeling assumption of grammar unambiguity and reliance on Viterbi training—failure modes under ambiguity/noisy data are not explored.
- Method depends on pre-specified atomic patterns and relation types (temporal/spatial); end-to-end sensitivity to these design choices is not analyzed.
- Experimental scope is limited: missing important baselines (e.g., Bayesian Model Merging) and modality-diverse comparisons (e.g., Klein & Manning), and lacks key ablations.
- Scalability/complexity beyond a few thousand samples and tens of terminals is not rigorously profiled; runtime/memory scaling absent.
- Clarity is generally good, but notation (RD, CM, coherence factors) is dense and many derivations are relegated to the supplement; main text needs pseudocode and a worked example.
- Originality: joint induction of And and Or via fragments with efficient posterior gain estimation is novel and useful; cross-modal formalization is incremental but helpful.
- Significance: solid contribution for structured generative models in vision and activity parsing, likely to be adopted, but limited by context-free/decomposable assumptions.
- Pros summarized: principled posterior objective, efficient sufficient-statistics-based search, unified And-Or fragment learning, competitive empirical performance in low-data regimes.
- Cons summarized: restricted subclass limits generality, limited comparisons and ablations, dependence on predefined terminals/relations, and some key details hidden in supplement.
- Recommendation: Accept, with suggested improvements in empirical comparisons (especially [14]), ablations, scalability analysis, and clearer statement of assumptions.
