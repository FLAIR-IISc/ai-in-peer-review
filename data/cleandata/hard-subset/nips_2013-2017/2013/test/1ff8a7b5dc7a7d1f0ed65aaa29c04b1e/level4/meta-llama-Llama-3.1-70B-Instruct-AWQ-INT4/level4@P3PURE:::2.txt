REPLY TO REBUTTAL: 

I mostly agree with the suggestion to focus on a single task. However, I still believe it would be worth exploring the use of truncated backpropagation with a smaller number of steps (e.g., 30 steps) to assess its performance. Although it is less likely to learn to balance parentheses, it may still achieve competitive entropies at a lower computational cost.

The paper explores the effectiveness of deep recurrent neural networks, leading to several interesting conclusions. When the number of parameters is held constant, deeper recurrent neural networks outperform standard RNNs. Notably, the analysis reveals that deeper networks tend to capture more "long-term information." Additionally, the paper demonstrates that plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can successfully train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, contradicting the previous assumption that this task required Hessian-free (HF) optimization.

However, the paper's claim to introduce the deep RNN architecture is not accurate, as this concept has been explored in prior work (e.g., http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf). While it is acceptable to focus on analysis rather than introducing new architectures, the paper should not claim to have pioneered this approach.

The analysis, although meaningful, would be more compelling if it were applied to multiple problems, including speech tasks. This would help to verify that the findings are not an artifact of the specific problem being addressed.

Furthermore, the HF experiments may have been unnecessarily expensive, as the truncated backpropagation approach (introduced in the 1990s) has been successfully employed by Mikolov (see his PhD thesis) to train RNNs for language modeling. It is likely that truncated BPTT would yield similar results in this context, and confirming this would be a valuable addition to the paper.

This work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs, with two primary results: (1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs when the number of parameters is fixed on character-level language modeling, and (2) the deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure. Although the results are intriguing, the paper would be strengthened by including a similar analysis on a speech task to demonstrate the generalizability of these findings.
