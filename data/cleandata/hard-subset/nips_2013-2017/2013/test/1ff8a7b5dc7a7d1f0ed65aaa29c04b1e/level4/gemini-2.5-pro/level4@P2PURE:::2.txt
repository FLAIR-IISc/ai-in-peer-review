This paper presents an analysis of deep Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and offers several interesting findings. The authors demonstrate that, when the number of parameters is held constant, deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs on a character-level language modeling task. Furthermore, their analysis suggests that deeper layers in the network capture more long-term information. A notable result is that plain Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT) can successfully train these models to balance parentheses, a task previously thought to require more complex methods like Hessian-Free (HF) optimization.

However, the paper has a few weaknesses that should be addressed:

First, the claim of introducing the deep RNN architecture is inaccurate. This architecture has appeared in prior work (e.g., Graves, ICASSP 2013, and likely earlier). The authors should revise this claim and instead frame the paper's contribution as a valuable analysis of an existing architecture. Focusing on analysis rather than architectural novelty is an entirely valid contribution.

Second, the paper's conclusions, while meaningful, are based on experiments on a single task. This limited scope raises the possibility that the findings are an artifact of the specific problem domain. The analysis would be substantially strengthened by demonstrating similar results on other tasks, such as in speech processing, to establish the generality of the claims.

Finally, the reliance on HF optimization for experiments seems computationally excessive without comparison to a more standard baseline. The truncated BPTT algorithm, introduced in the 1990s and successfully used by Mikolov to train excellent RNN language models, is a more common and efficient alternative. It is highly probable that truncated BPTT would also perform well here. The paper would be more convincing if it included a comparison against this method to contextualize the performance and computational cost of the HF approach.
