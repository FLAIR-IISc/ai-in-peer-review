This paper investigates the properties and performance of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), an architecture where multiple RNN layers are stacked hierarchically. The authors apply DRNNs to the task of character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus, training them with standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and backpropagation through time (BPTT). The core contributions are an empirical demonstration that DRNNs can outperform standard RNNs with a comparable number of parameters, and an in-depth analysis suggesting that the hierarchical structure allows different layers to learn representations at different time scales. The paper shows that this architecture, trained with a relatively simple algorithm, achieves state-of-the-art results that were previously reported using more complex, second-order optimization methods like Hessian-Free (HF).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates impressive performance on a difficult, large-scale benchmark. Achieving a bits-per-character (BPC) score competitive with the state-of-the-art established by Hessian-Free optimization, but using only SGD, is a significant and valuable result. This finding contributes to the broader discussion on the trade-offs between first- and second-order optimization methods for large neural networks.

2.  **Insightful Analysis of Temporal Hierarchy:** The primary strength of the paper is not just the performance, but the analysis of the network's internal dynamics. The experiments designed to measure the effective time scales of different layers (e.g., the typo perturbation and context switch tests) provide compelling evidence for the central hypothesis: stacking RNN layers creates a temporal hierarchy where deeper layers capture longer-range dependencies. This is a valuable insight into *why* deep architectures are effective for sequential data.

3.  **Demonstration of Long-Term Dependency Learning:** The analysis of parenthesis balancing is particularly effective. Showing that the model learns to maintain a state representing an open parenthesis over hundreds of characters—a sequence length longer than what it was explicitly trained on—is a strong demonstration of its ability to capture complex, long-range structural information. The finding that this can be achieved with standard BPTT is noteworthy, as it was previously thought that such tasks required more specialized architectures or training algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Overstated Novelty of the Architecture:** The paper frames the DRNN as a novel contribution. However, this architecture of stacked recurrent layers has been explored before. For instance, Graves et al. used stacked bi-directional LSTMs for speech recognition in their 2013 ICASSP paper, which is even cited by the authors (reference [8]). The contribution here is not the invention of the architecture, but rather its thorough analysis and successful application to character-level language modeling with a simple training algorithm. The paper's framing should be adjusted to reflect this, positioning the work as a crucial analysis and empirical validation rather than the introduction of a new model.

2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The conclusions about the general utility of DRNNs are based entirely on a single task. While character-level language modeling is a challenging benchmark, the findings regarding the emergent temporal hierarchy may be an artifact of this specific problem. To strengthen the claim that this is a general property of DRNNs, the analysis would be much more convincing if replicated in a different domain, such as speech recognition or music generation, where temporal hierarchies are also prevalent.

3.  **Incomplete Comparison of Training Methods:** The paper makes a strong point by comparing the performance of its SGD-trained models to the more computationally expensive HF-trained models from prior work. However, it omits a highly relevant and widely used baseline: truncated backpropagation through time (BPTT). As shown in work by Mikolov and others, RNN language models can be trained very effectively using truncated BPTT (e.g., unrolling for 30-50 steps), which is significantly more computationally efficient than the full BPTT over 200 steps used here. While full BPTT is likely necessary for the parenthesis-balancing task, it is possible that truncated BPTT could achieve a competitive BPC score at a fraction of the training cost. Including this comparison is essential for a complete picture of the trade-offs between different training strategies for RNNs.

**Summary and Suggestions:**

This is a well-executed study with valuable insights into the behavior of deep recurrent networks. The strengths lie in the strong empirical results and the detailed analysis that supports the hypothesis of an emergent temporal hierarchy.

The main weaknesses are the overstatement of the architecture's novelty and the limited experimental scope. I recommend that the authors revise the paper to:
*   Clearly position their work as an in-depth *analysis* of the DRNN architecture, properly contextualizing it with prior work that has used similar models.
*   Acknowledge that the findings are currently limited to character-level language modeling and discuss how they might generalize.
*   Include a discussion or, ideally, an experiment comparing their full BPTT approach with the more common and efficient truncated BPTT baseline to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of training methods.

Despite these points, the analysis is solid and the results are significant. The paper makes a clear contribution to our understanding of deep models for sequential data and is a worthy addition to the NIPS proceedings.
