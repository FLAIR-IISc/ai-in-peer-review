- Older data may receive more processing in deeper networks; building deep RNNs (or inserting extra nonlinear layers between timesteps) is plausible for short-term performance—have you tried that?  
- How critical is gradient normalization, why does it “avoid the problem of bifurcations,” and could it disrupt SGD’s averaging behavior? (See ICML 2013 U. of Montreal paper on gradient truncation/RNN optimization.)  
- Compare and contrast with prior Wikipedia compression work (e.g., Mikolov et al.).  
- Of the experiments probing layer roles, the text generation results (Table 2) are the most convincing.  
- Interpreting “brain surgery” layer ablations is difficult due to complex interdependencies between layers.  
- Major gap: insufficient evidence that higher layers truly capture more abstract/long-term properties versus simply relocating units to the first layer; post-training emergence is suggestive but not conclusive.  
- Parameter mismatch: the regular RNN uses fewer parameters, which may skew comparisons—tests should match parameter/unit counts (e.g., deeper DRNN vs. equivalent-unit RNN).  
- Figure 2 should report benchmark performance across DRNN depths trained from scratch, using wider layers to compensate for parameter/unit differences.  
- Minor: define DRNN-AO and IO in the main text rather than only in Figure 1.  
- Overall: the paper shows strong performance for hybrid deep-recurrent networks, presents largely convincing experiments, is honest about limitations, and is well-written; exploring deep temporal architectures is valuable.
