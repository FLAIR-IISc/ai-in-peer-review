The authors propose a new deep architecture that combines the hierarchical nature of deep learning with time-series modeling from HMMs or recurrent neural networks. The proposed training algorithm builds the network layer-by-layer using supervised (pre-)training for the next-character prediction purpose. Experiments show that after training a very large network for approximately 10 days, the network's performance on the Wikipedia dataset published by Hinton et al. is better than previous work. The authors then proceed to analyze and discuss in detail how the network performs its task. For example, long-term dependencies are modeled in higher layers, and the correspondence between opening and closing brackets is modeled as "pseudo-stable attracter-like states."

This paper is of good quality. It is well-written, the experiments are well-established, and the figures well support the conclusions. My main issue with the paper is the experiments. The comparison is only made on one dataset, which has not been extensively worked on. The direct comparison is only with other RNN-related models, perhaps not with more general hierarchical HMMs, etc., which are capable of solving similar tasks (Table 1 lists other approaches, but on a different corpus, which makes them incomparable).

The paper's contribution is more in analyzing the operation of the network (which is quite well done) than in what it achieves, but that is not the paper's purpose. A new deep architecture is proposed, which combines the hierarchical features of deep learning with time-series modeling. The paper is well-written, the model is well-analyzed, but the results are somewhat inconclusive.
