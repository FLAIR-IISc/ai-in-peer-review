This paper introduces a Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN), a hybrid architecture that combines properties of deep and recurrent networks, and demonstrates its effectiveness on text prediction and compression tasks. A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to a series of experiments arguing that higher-level layers in the DRNN learn to represent more abstract and long-term structures in the data. The paper is well-written, easy to read, and commendably honest about its own potential shortcomings. Exploring these kinds of deep temporal networks and gaining insight into their function is a worthwhile endeavor. While the experiments are convincing, I have a few reservations and suggestions for additional experiments that could strengthen the paper's claims.

**Strengths and Main Contributions**

The paper is well-motivated by the insight that in a standard RNN, older data benefits from more layers of processing than newer data. Proposing a deep RNN architecture is a sensible and interesting approach to address this imbalance. The experiments suggesting that different layers learn hierarchical representations operating on different time-scales are intriguing, particularly the text generation results in Table 2, which provide persuasive evidence for this phenomenon.

**Questions and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparisons:** My main concern is that the empirical comparisons may not be entirely fair. The standard RNN baseline appears to have significantly fewer parameters than the DRNNs it is compared against. The paper's claims would be substantially strengthened by comparing the DRNNs to a standard RNN with an equivalent number of parameters (i.e., a wider hidden layer) or, ideally, an equivalent number of units. For example, comparing a 2-layer DRNN to a single-layer RNN with the same total unit count would provide a more controlled assessment of the benefits of depth.

2.  **Demonstrating the Value of Depth:** The biggest missing piece of the empirical puzzle is whether the higher layers are more effective at processing abstract, long-term properties specifically *because* of their depth. Do they benefit from the preceding levels of processing in a non-trivial way? While the results show that higher layers tend to assume these roles after training, this is incomplete evidence. A crucial experiment would be to demonstrate that moving these units to the first layer (i.e., creating a wider, shallower network) results in worse performance on long-term dependencies.

3.  **Alternative Architectures and Ablations:**
    *   As an alternative to stacking recurrent layers, have the authors considered adding extra layers of non-linearities between each timestep within a single recurrent layer? It would be interesting to see how this compares to the proposed architecture.
    *   Instead of Figure 2, it would be more informative to see how DRNNs of various depths perform on the benchmarks when trained from scratch. To control for model capacity, shallower models could be designed with wider layers to match the parameter or unit counts of their deeper counterparts.

4.  **Clarity on Gradient Normalization:** Could the authors elaborate on the importance of the gradient normalization procedure? Specifically, how does it "avoid the problem of bifurcations"? It would be beneficial to discuss and cite relevant recent work on training RNNs, such as the ICML 2013 paper by Pascanu et al. from the University of Montreal, which analyzes gradient truncation. Furthermore, does normalizing the gradient magnitude in this way potentially interfere with the implicit averaging behavior of SGD, which relies on gradient scale?

5.  **Additional Baselines:** For the Wikipedia compression tasks, the comparisons could be made more comprehensive by including results from other relevant work, such as that by Mikolov et al.

6.  **Interpretation of Experiments:** The text generation experiment (Table 2) is the paper's most persuasive evidence for hierarchical processing. However, as the authors commendably point out, interpreting the effects of this kind of "brain surgery" on RNNs is challenging due to the complex interdependencies that may have developed between layers during training. This caveat should be kept in mind when drawing strong conclusions.

**Minor Point**

*   The terms DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO should be defined in the main text, not just in the caption for Figure 1.
