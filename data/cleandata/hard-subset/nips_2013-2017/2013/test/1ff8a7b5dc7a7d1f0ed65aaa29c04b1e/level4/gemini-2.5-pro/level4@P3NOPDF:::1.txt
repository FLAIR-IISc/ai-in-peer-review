The authors propose a new deep architecture that combines the hierarchical feature learning of deep networks with the time-series modeling capabilities of models like HMMs or Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). The proposed training algorithm constructs the network in a layer-by-layer fashion, using a supervised pre-training scheme based on a next-letter prediction objective. The experiments demonstrate that after extensive training (approximately 10 days), a large-scale version of this network achieves improved performance on a Wikipedia dataset previously used by Hinton et al., surpassing prior RNN-based approaches. Following this, the authors dedicate a significant portion of the paper to analyzing the network's internal mechanisms, providing insights into how it solves its task. For example, they show that long-term dependencies are modeled in higher layers and that phenomena like matching opening and closing parentheses are handled by what they describe as a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state."

The paper is of good quality. It is well-written, the model is presented clearly, the experiments are designed thoughtfully, and the figures effectively support the authors' findings and analysis. The deep dive into the network's learned representations is particularly insightful and valuable to the community.

My primary concern with the paper, however, is the limited scope of the experimental evaluation. The empirical claims of the paper rest on performance on a single dataset, which does not appear to be a widely adopted benchmark for this task. This makes it difficult to gauge the generalizability and robustness of the proposed architecture.

Furthermore, the comparative analysis is constrained. Direct performance comparisons are only made against other RNN-related models. While the paper acknowledges other relevant model classes, such as hierarchical HMMs which can solve similar tasks, it does not provide a direct empirical comparison. Table 1 lists results from other approaches, but these are on a different corpus, rendering them incomparable and preventing a clear assessment of where the proposed model stands in the broader landscape of sequential data modeling.

In its current form, the paper's main contribution seems to be more in the excellent analysis of *how* the proposed network operates, rather than in *what* it achieves in terms of state-of-the-art performance. While this analysis is a significant strength, the paper is framed around the proposal of a novel architecture with superior performance. The current experimental results, being confined to a single dataset and a narrow set of competitors, make this central claim somewhat inconclusive.

In summary, this is a well-written paper presenting a novel architecture with a particularly strong and insightful analysis of the model's internal workings. However, the empirical results, while promising, are not sufficient to fully substantiate the claims of state-of-the-art performance. The impact of the work would be substantially increased by a more comprehensive experimental evaluation on multiple standard benchmark datasets with a broader set of comparison models.
