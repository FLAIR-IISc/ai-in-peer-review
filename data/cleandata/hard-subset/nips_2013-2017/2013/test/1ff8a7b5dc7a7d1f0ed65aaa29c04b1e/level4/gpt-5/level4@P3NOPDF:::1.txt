Summary
The paper introduces a deep architecture that integrates hierarchical feature learning with time-series modeling, drawing on ideas from HMMs and recurrent neural networks. The model is trained layer by layer via supervised pretraining on a next-character prediction objective. On a Wikipedia dataset previously used by Hinton et al., the approach improves over prior reported results after training very large networks for roughly 10 days. The authors also provide a thoughtful analysis of the learned dynamics, showing that higher layers capture longer-term dependencies and that structures such as matched parentheses are represented through pseudo-stable, attractor-like states.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for combining hierarchical representations with temporal modeling.
- Well-written paper with informative figures and a careful presentation.
- Insightful analyses that illuminate how different layers encode dependencies, including qualitative diagnostics of long-range structure.
- Demonstrated performance improvement on the Hinton et al. Wikipedia dataset.
- The layer-wise training strategy is described coherently and seems practically useful for very deep temporal models.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope: evaluation is conducted on a single dataset that does not appear to be a common benchmark, making it difficult to assess generality.
- Baseline comparisons are restricted primarily to RNN-related models; there is no direct comparison to other structured sequence models (e.g., hierarchical or segmental HMMs) that target similar phenomena.
- Table 1 aggregates results from different corpora, which are not directly comparable to the reported results on the Wikipedia dataset.
- The paper’s most compelling contribution is the analysis of internal mechanisms; the performance gains, while promising, are not sufficiently contextualized to establish clear state-of-the-art progress.
- Training cost is very high (∼10 days for large networks), and the paper does not adequately discuss efficiency, scalability, or the trade-off between depth, capacity, and accuracy.

Suggestions for improvement
- Broaden the empirical evaluation to include at least one or two standard benchmarks for character- or word-level prediction, enabling direct, apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Include baselines beyond RNNs, such as hierarchical HMMs or other structured probabilistic sequence models that can capture multiscale dependencies, as well as strong n-gram or neural language model baselines where appropriate.
- Report comprehensive training details (hardware, parallelism, number of parameters, wall-clock vs. epochs) and provide learning curves. This would allow readers to gauge the efficiency and reproducibility of the approach.
- Add ablations to isolate the contribution of depth, layer-wise pretraining, and any architectural components. For example, compare:
  - With vs. without layer-wise supervised pretraining.
  - Shallow vs. deep versions at fixed parameter budgets.
  - End-to-end fine-tuning vs. frozen lower layers after pretraining.
- Where possible, perform multiple runs with different random seeds and report variance to assess stability.
- Clarify how the model handles very long contexts in practice (e.g., truncation, resets, or memory mechanisms) and whether the observed attractor-like states generalize across sequences or are corpus-specific artifacts.
- Provide quantitative analyses linking the layer-wise diagnostics to measurable performance (e.g., do layers that track parentheses more reliably also reduce specific error types?).

Questions for the authors
- After layer-wise pretraining, do you perform end-to-end fine-tuning of all layers? If so, how much does it improve performance?
- How sensitive is the model to depth, hidden size, and the choice of pretraining objective? Are there regimes where shallower models with similar parameter counts close the gap?
- Can you include direct comparisons to hierarchical/segmental HMMs or closely related structured models on the same dataset?
- What are the exact training resources used (GPUs/CPUs, memory), and what is the throughput? Could the training time be reduced without sacrificing accuracy?
- How robust are the “attractor-like” states across different domains or datasets?

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed paper with strong qualitative insights into the internal dynamics of a deep temporal architecture. The writing and analyses are clear and compelling. However, the experimental validation is too narrow to convincingly establish the model’s empirical advantages. Strengthening the evaluation with additional datasets and stronger, directly comparable baselines would significantly improve the paper. As it stands, the work’s primary contribution is the careful analysis of how the model operates rather than unequivocal advances in performance. With a broader and more rigorous empirical study, this could become a strong contribution.
