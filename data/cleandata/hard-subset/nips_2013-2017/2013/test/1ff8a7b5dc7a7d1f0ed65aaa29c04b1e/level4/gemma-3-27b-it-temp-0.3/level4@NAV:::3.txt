I appreciate the observation regarding the differing levels of processing applied to older versus newer data, and the proposed use of a deep RNN to improve short-term performance is a sensible approach. Considering additional nonlinearities between timesteps would also be a worthwhile exploration – have you investigated this?

The importance of gradient normalization and its effect on preventing bifurcations warrants further clarification. The work by researchers at the University of Montreal presented at ICML 2013 on gradient truncation and RNN optimization may offer relevant insights. Furthermore, could this gradient normalization potentially disrupt the averaging effect of SGD?

When contextualizing these results within the existing literature on Wikipedia compression, a comparison to the work of Mikolov et al. would be beneficial.

Of the experiments designed to analyze layer-specific roles concerning timescale and abstraction, the text generation results (Table 2) are the most compelling. However, as the authors acknowledge (line 241), interpreting the impact of these layer manipulations on RNNs is complicated by potential interdependencies developed between layer outputs.

A key empirical question remains: do higher layers genuinely excel at processing abstract, long-term text properties compared to simply relocating those units to the first layer? Observing these roles emerge post-training is suggestive, but doesn’t definitively prove they benefit from the preceding processing stages.

I noted that the standard RNN appears to have fewer parameters in these experiments (2767^2 > 2119^2, considering recurrent and interlayer weights), potentially creating an uneven comparison. A stronger demonstration would involve showing that deeper RNNs outperform a standard RNN with an equivalent number of parameters, or ideally, the same number of units – for example, a two-layer DRNN versus a standard RNN with comparable unit counts. This would significantly bolster the paper’s claims.

Additionally, instead of Figure 2, presenting the performance of DRNNs with varying depths on the benchmarks, trained from scratch and potentially utilizing wider layers to balance parameter/unit counts, would be more informative.

Minor points:

- Please define DRNN-AO and IO within the text, not just in Figure 1. 
This paper investigates DRNNs, a hybrid deep and recurrent architecture, demonstrating strong performance in text prediction/compression. A significant portion of the work focuses on experiments supporting the hypothesis that higher layers represent more abstract, long-term data structures. While these experiments are largely persuasive, I have some reservations and suggest additional investigations. I believe exploring these deep temporal networks and understanding their post-training behavior is valuable. The paper is well-written, intellectually honest, and thoroughly addresses its own potential limitations – a quality I particularly appreciate.
