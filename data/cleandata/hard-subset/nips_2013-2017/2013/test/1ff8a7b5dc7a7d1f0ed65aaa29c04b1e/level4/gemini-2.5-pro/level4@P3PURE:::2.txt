REPLY TO REBUTTAL:
I mostly agree with the authors' comments regarding the single-task limitation. It would still be worthwhile, however, to try truncated backpropagation with, for example, 30 steps. While this approach is less likely to learn to balance parentheses (a point that would be good to verify), it may still achieve competitive entropies at a fraction of the computational cost.

This paper investigates the usefulness of deep Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and reaches several interesting conclusions. The primary finding is that when holding the number of parameters constant, deeper RNNs outperform standard, shallower RNNs on a character-level language modeling task. Other interesting results include an analysis showing that deeper networks capture more long-term information and the demonstration that plain Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT) can train these models to balance parentheses, a task previously thought to require more advanced optimizers like Hessian-Free (HF).

However, the paper has a few weaknesses that should be addressed.

First, the paper claims to introduce the deep RNN, but this architecture has been used before (for example, in Graves et al., ICASSP 2013, and likely much earlier). The paper should not claim to have introduced this architecture. It is perfectly acceptable for a paper to focus on an analysis of an existing architecture rather than introducing a new one.

Second, the analysis, while meaningful, would be significantly more compelling if it were demonstrated on several problems, perhaps including a speech-related task. As it stands, the findings may be an artifact of the single problem domain explored. Demonstrating similar results on a different task would greatly strengthen the paper's conclusions.

Finally, the experiments using Hessian-Free (HF) optimization were likely unnecessarily expensive. Truncated BPTT, introduced in the 1990s and used successfully by Mikolov to train excellent RNN language models, is a strong and efficient baseline. It is likely that truncated BPTT would also perform well here, and it would be beneficial to obtain a confirmation of this. Such a comparison would provide a more practical and relevant baseline for the community.
