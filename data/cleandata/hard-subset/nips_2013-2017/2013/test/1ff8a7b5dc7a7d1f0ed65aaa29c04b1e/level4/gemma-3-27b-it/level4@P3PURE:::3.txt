This paper introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), a hybrid of deep and recurrent neural networks, and demonstrates their effectiveness for text prediction and compression compared to existing approaches. A significant portion of the work focuses on experiments designed to support the claim that higher layers process and represent more abstract and long-term structures in the data. These experiments are generally persuasive, but I have several reservations and suggest a few additional investigations. Overall, I find this work worthwhile, offering valuable insight into deep temporal networks and their functionality post-training. The paper is also well-written, intellectually honest, and thorough in addressing its potential limitations, which I particularly appreciate.

I find the core idea – that older data may benefit from more layers of processing than newer data – compelling. Building a deep RNN appears to be a reasonable approach to improve performance on shorter-term dependencies, and may offer other advantages as well. A natural extension to consider would be the addition of extra layers of nonlinearities between each timestep; have the authors explored this?

The importance of gradient normalization and its role in “avoiding bifurcations” requires further clarification. The authors should elaborate on this mechanism. A recent ICML 2013 paper from researchers at the University of Montreal investigates gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs, which may be relevant here. Furthermore, could normalizing the gradient in this manner potentially disrupt the averaging behavior typically observed with SGD?

Regarding prior work on Wikipedia compression tasks, the authors should compare their results to those of Mikolov et al. 

Of the various analyses of layer roles concerning timescale and abstraction, the text generation experiment (Table 2) is the most convincing. However, as the authors acknowledge (line 241), interpreting the effects of such “brain surgery” on RNNs is problematic due to the complex interdependencies that likely develop between layer outputs.

A crucial missing empirical component is demonstrating whether the higher layers genuinely process more abstract and long-term text properties *better* than if those units were placed in the first layer. While the observed emergence of these roles after training is a positive sign, it does not definitively prove that the extra levels of preceding processing contribute in a nontrivial way.

I observed that the regular RNN used in the experiments appears to have fewer parameters than the DRNNs (2767^2 x 2 > 2119^2 x 2, considering both recurrent and interlayer weight matrices). This makes the comparison somewhat unfair. A more compelling evaluation would involve comparing deeper RNNs to a standard RNN with an equivalent number of parameters, or ideally, the same number of units. For example, a two-layer DRNN versus a standard RNN with the same total number of units, mitigating the parameter count advantage of the DRNN. This would significantly strengthen the paper’s claims.

Additionally, replacing Figure 2 with a visualization of DRNN performance across varying depths, when trained from scratch, would be beneficial. This could be complemented by employing wider layers in the deeper networks to compensate for differences in parameter or unit counts.

Minor comments:

- The abbreviations DRNN-AO and IO should be defined in the text, not solely in Figure 1.
