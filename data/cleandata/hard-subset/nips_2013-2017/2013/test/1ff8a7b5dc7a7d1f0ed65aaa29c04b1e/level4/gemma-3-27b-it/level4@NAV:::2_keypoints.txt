I concur with the authors’ response about the single-task assessment.

Explore truncated backpropagation (e.g., 30 steps) — it may lower compute cost and possibly match entropies, though its efficacy on parenthesis balancing should be verified.

Paper shows deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs when parameter count is held constant.

Analysis finds deeper networks retain greater long-term information in higher layers.

Plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, contrary to claims that HF was necessary.

The claim of introducing “deep RNN” is inaccurate—prior work (e.g., Graves 2013) exists; the paper should emphasize analysis rather than novelty of the architecture.

Extend evaluation to other tasks (e.g., speech) to determine whether findings generalize beyond the current problem.

HF experiments may be unnecessarily expensive; truncated BPTT (used since the 1990s and by Mikolov) is likely a cheaper, effective alternative and should be tested.

Overall key results to highlight: depth improves performance at fixed parameters, deeper layers capture longer-range structure, and truncated BPTT can enable learning of parenthesis balancing.
