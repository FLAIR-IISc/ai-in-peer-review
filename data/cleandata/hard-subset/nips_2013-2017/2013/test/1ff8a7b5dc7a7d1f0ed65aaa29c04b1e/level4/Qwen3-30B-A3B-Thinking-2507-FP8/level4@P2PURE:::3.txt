

I appreciate the insight regarding the arbitrariness of older data benefiting from potentially more layers of processing compared to newer data. Constructing a deep RNN appears reasonable for improving performance on shorter-term tasks, alongside other potential advantages. An additional straightforward approach would be to insert extra nonlinear layers between timesteps—have you explored this?  

The significance of gradient normalization and its role in "avoiding the problem of bifurcations" require further clarification. A recent ICML 2013 paper by researchers from the University of Montreal addresses gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs, which may be relevant here. Additionally, does normalizing gradients in this manner disrupt the "averaging" behavior inherent to SGD?  

Regarding prior work on Wikipedia compression tasks, comparisons to Mikolov et al.'s results would be valuable. Among the experiments examining layer roles in time-scale and abstraction, the text generation experiment (Table 2) is the most persuasive. However, as noted by the authors on line 241, interpreting the effects of "brain surgery" on RNNs may be problematic due to complex interdependencies between layer outputs.  

The most significant gap in the empirical analysis is whether higher layers genuinely process more abstract, long-term text properties than if those units were relocated to the first layer. The observation that higher layers adopt these roles post-training is suggestive but insufficient evidence; it does not confirm they benefit nontrivially from preceding processing levels.  

I note that the standard RNN uses fewer parameters in these experiments (2767² × 5 > 2119², counting both recurrent and inter-layer weight matrices). This comparison may be unfair. A stronger validation would involve deeper RNNs outperforming a standard RNN with identical parameter counts or unit counts—e.g., a 2-layer DRNN versus a standard RNN with the same number of units. This would eliminate the parameter-count bias and significantly strengthen the paper's claims.  

Additionally, Figure 2 would be more informative if replaced with results showing how various DRNN depths perform on benchmarks when trained from scratch, potentially with wider layers than shallower counterparts to account for parameter/unit differences.  

Minor: Define DRNN-AO and IO in the main text, not solely in Figure 1.  

This paper examines a hybrid deep-recurrent architecture (DRNNs) for text prediction/compression, demonstrating strong performance relative to existing methods. A substantial portion of the work focuses on experiments arguing that higher layers process more abstract, long-term data structures. While these experiments are largely convincing, the reservations above warrant addressing. The research on deep temporal networks is valuable, and the paper is well-written, intellectually honest, and thoroughly acknowledges its limitations.
