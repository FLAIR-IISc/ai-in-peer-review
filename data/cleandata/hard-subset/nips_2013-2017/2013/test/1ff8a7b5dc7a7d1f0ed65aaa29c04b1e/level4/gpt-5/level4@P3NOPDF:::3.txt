This paper investigates deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs)—stacked recurrent layers with recurrence at each depth—for character-level text prediction/compression. The central idea that earlier timesteps may effectively undergo more processing than more recent inputs is compelling, and building depth in the recurrent transition appears to help with shorter-term modeling while potentially enabling richer temporal abstractions. The paper is clearly written, and I appreciate the authors’ candor in discussing limitations.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for deepening the recurrent transition and an architecture that is straightforward to implement.
- Strong empirical results on text prediction/compression, with analyses aimed at understanding the roles of different layers.
- The text generation ablation (Table 2) is the most persuasive evidence of differentiated temporal/abstraction roles across layers.

Main questions and concerns
1) Architecture/design alternatives. A natural baseline to consider is adding extra nonlinear layers within each timestep’s transition (i.e., making the per-step transition function deeper) without stacking recurrent layers. Have you tried inserting additional nonlinearities between timesteps as an alternative to stacked recurrence? This could help tease apart “transition depth” from “recurrent depth.”

2) Gradient normalization and “bifurcations.” Please clarify:
- How critical is gradient normalization to your results? Ablations quantifying performance with/without normalization would help.
- What precisely do you mean by “avoid the problem of bifurcations,” and what empirical signal indicates you are avoiding them?
- How does your approach relate to ICML 2013 work from U. Montreal (e.g., Pascanu, Mikolov, Bengio) on exploding/vanishing gradients and gradient clipping/truncation in RNNs? A brief conceptual and empirical comparison would strengthen the paper.
- Could gradient normalization interfere with the averaging behavior of SGD (e.g., by distorting the noise scale)? Some discussion or a small experiment (variance of updates, convergence behavior) would be useful.

3) Fairness of comparisons and parameter counts. As configured, the DRNN and the single-layer RNN do not appear to be matched for parameter budget, which makes it hard to isolate the effect of depth. Please provide controlled comparisons where:
- Model families are matched on total parameter count, and ideally also on number of hidden units.
- For example, compare a 2-layer DRNN to a single-layer RNN with the same number of units, and also to a single-layer RNN widened to match the DRNN’s parameter budget. If the DRNN still outperforms, it would substantially strengthen the claims.

4) Depth vs. width trade-offs from scratch. Instead of (or in addition to) Figure 2, it would be more informative to show performance as a function of depth for DRNNs trained from scratch, with shallower models widened to roughly equalize parameter counts. This would make the depth contribution more compelling.

5) Evidence for long-term/abstract processing in higher layers. While the text generation ablation is suggestive, interpreting “brain surgery” on RNNs is tricky due to interdependencies between layers (as you note). The key missing piece is a controlled test of whether higher layers truly capture longer-term or more abstract properties beyond what could be achieved by reallocating capacity to the first layer. Suggestions:
- Unit relocation/shuffling: Move some higher-layer units (or their capacity) to the first layer and retrain to equal total parameters. Does performance or the qualitative division of labor change?
- Probing horizons: Quantify each layer’s effective temporal horizon (e.g., layer-specific ablations conditioned on context length, or mutual information between layer activations and distant past tokens).
- Layer freezing: Train the first layer(s), then freeze them and train upper layers, vs. the reverse, to test whether upper layers leverage processed features in a nontrivial way.
- Surrogate tasks: Add probes for long-range properties (e.g., bracket/quote closure, paragraph boundaries) read out from each layer’s states.

6) Relation to prior work on Wikipedia compression. Please compare more directly to results by Mikolov et al. on similar Wikipedia prediction/compression tasks. Even if datasets or preprocessing differ, a normalization or qualitative discussion would help position your contributions.

Clarifications and presentation
- Please define DRNN-AO and IO in the main text (not only in Figure 1).
- Provide training details for gradient normalization (thresholds, norms used, where applied) to ensure reproducibility.
- If possible, report variance across runs for key results, as RNN training can be sensitive to initialization and optimization noise.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and timely paper that advances our understanding of deep temporal architectures and offers promising empirical results. The architectural idea is well motivated, and the qualitative analyses are thoughtful. However, the central claims about the benefits of depth for modeling multiple time scales would be much more convincing with controlled comparisons that fix parameter budgets/units and with additional experiments that disentangle depth-driven abstraction from simple capacity allocation.

With the additions suggested above—especially (i) controlled parameter-matched baselines, (ii) experiments varying depth vs. width from scratch, (iii) deeper analysis of gradient normalization and its relation to prior work, and (iv) clearer evidence that higher layers capture genuinely longer-term/abstract dependencies—I would view the paper as significantly stronger. Even in its current form, it is a valuable step toward understanding deep recurrent structures, but the empirical case for depth per se needs tighter controls to fully substantiate the claims.
