The paper proposes a new deep architecture that combines hierarchical representations from deep learning with time-series modeling in the spirit of HMMs and recurrent neural networks. The training procedure builds the network layer by layer using supervised pretraining on a next-letter prediction objective. In experiments, the authors train very large networks for roughly 10 days and report improved performance over previous work on a Wikipedia dataset introduced by Hinton et al. The paper also provides a detailed analysis of how the network approaches its task, showing, for example, that long-term dependencies emerge in higher layers and that the correspondence between opening and closing parentheses manifests as a “pseudo-stable, attractor-like” state.

Overall, the paper is of good quality: it is clearly written, the experimental setup is sound, and the figures effectively support the claims. My main concern is the experimental evaluation. The comparison is conducted on a single dataset that does not appear to have an extensive body of prior work, and direct comparisons are limited to other RNN-related models. More conventional baselines such as hierarchical HMMs, which are capable of handling similar sequence-structure phenomena, are not included. Although Table 1 lists other approaches, those results are on a different corpus and therefore are not directly comparable.

In sum, the paper’s primary contribution lies in its insightful analysis of how the proposed network operates—an analysis that is executed well—rather than in what it empirically achieves. However, this emphasis does not fully align with the paper’s stated aim of introducing a new deep architecture that combines hierarchical features with time-series modeling. While the paper is well-written and the model is carefully analyzed, the empirical results are somewhat inconclusive.
