

The paper investigates deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), where each layer is a recurrent network receiving the previous layer's hidden state. It demonstrates that DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks in character-level language modeling using only stochastic gradient descent (SGD), while providing analysis of emergent time scales and long-term dependency handling. The work builds on prior NIPS contributions: Graves et al. (2013) used stacked bidirectional LSTMs for speech recognition (NIPS 2013), and El Hihi & Bengio (1996) proposed hierarchical RNNs for long-term dependencies (NIPS 1996), though the current paper focuses on analysis rather than performance gains.  

Strengths: The paper provides compelling evidence that DRNNs with fixed parameter count outperform standard RNNs on character-level language modeling (BPC 1.47 vs. 1.63), and successfully demonstrates that DRNNs can learn to balance parentheses (previously thought requiring Hessian-free optimization). The time-scale analysis—showing deeper layers retain longer context (e.g., top-layer output dominates closing-parenthesis prediction)—is novel and well-supported. The use of layer-wise ablation (e.g., disabling bottom-layer output degrades text quality) adds valuable interpretability.  

Weaknesses: The architecture is not new; Graves et al. (2013) already employed stacked bidirectional LSTMs for speech (NIPS 2013), and the DRNN structure described is functionally identical to their "DRNN-1O" setup. The paper overstates novelty by claiming to "introduce" the architecture. Further, the analysis is limited to character-level language modeling; extending it to speech tasks (e.g., TIMIT) would strengthen significance by demonstrating broader applicability. The comparison to Hessian-free training is misleading—truncated BPTT (used successfully by Mikolov for language modeling) could achieve competitive results at lower cost, as noted in the rebuttal.  

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding DRNNs’ temporal hierarchy, but the novelty claim and narrow scope limit its impact. A revision clarifying prior work and adding a speech task analysis would significantly strengthen the submission.
