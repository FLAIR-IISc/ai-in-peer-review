

This paper investigates the utility of deep recurrent neural networks (RNNs) through a focused analysis, yielding several compelling insights. The core findings—that deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs when parameter counts are fixed, deeper layers encode more long-term dependencies, and plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to solve the parentheses-balancing task—represent meaningful contributions to the understanding of RNN dynamics. The analysis of long-range information in deeper layers, particularly via information-theoretic measures, is especially well-executed and merits attention. However, the paper’s claims and scope require significant refinement to strengthen its impact and accuracy.  

**Major Concerns:**  
1. **Overstated Novelty:** The paper claims to "introduce" deep RNNs as a novel architecture, which is inaccurate. Deep RNNs have been extensively used since at least Graves et al. (ICASSP 2013), and earlier variants (e.g., stacked RNNs in Graves 2008) predate this work. The authors should reframe their contribution as an *analysis* of deep RNNs rather than an architectural innovation. This misattribution undermines the paper’s credibility and misleads readers about the novelty of the core framework.  

2. **Limited Scope of Validation:** The analysis is confined to character-level language modeling (LM), which may not generalize. To avoid the risk of findings being problem-specific artifacts, the authors must extend their experiments to a distinct task—ideally one with well-established long-range dependencies, such as speech recognition or machine translation. For instance, demonstrating that deeper layers capture more contextual information in a speech task would validate the broader relevance of their insights. Without this, the results could be attributed to peculiarities of the LM setup (e.g., predictable character transitions).  

3. **Inefficient Experimental Design:** The paper’s reliance on hierarchical feedback (HF) for the parentheses task is unnecessarily costly. Truncated BPTT, a standard technique since the 1990s (e.g., Williams & Zipser, 1989), was successfully employed by Mikolov (2012) to train highly effective language models. Given that truncated BPTT typically achieves comparable performance at a fraction of the computational cost, the authors should replace HF with a truncated BPTT baseline. Crucially, they should verify whether truncated BPTT *also* solves the parentheses task (as the paper’s claim about "plain BPTT" is likely conflated with truncated approaches). The current HF implementation appears to be a red herring; a truncated BPTT comparison would be far more insightful and efficient.  

**Minor Improvements:**  
- The rebuttal’s suggestion to test truncated BPTT with 30 steps is valid but should be framed as a critical experiment, not a minor "worth trying." The authors must report whether truncated BPTT (with 30 steps) achieves competitive entropy while avoiding the pitfalls of full BPTT (e.g., vanishing gradients in long sequences). If it succeeds, it would further undermine the need for HF and align the work with established practices.  
- The paper’s claim that "plain BPTT" trains RNNs to balance parentheses requires clarification. Standard BPTT struggles with long sequences due to vanishing gradients; the authors likely mean *truncated* BPTT (as in the parentheses task). This ambiguity should be resolved explicitly to avoid confusion.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
The paper’s core analysis—on deeper RNNs’ parameter efficiency and information encoding—is valuable and well-executed for the character LM task. However, the misattribution of novelty, narrow scope, and inefficient HF approach significantly weaken its contribution. Addressing these issues would transform this into a stronger, more generalizable study.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Reject.** The paper’s current form does not meet NIPS standards for novelty or rigor. The architectural claim is factually incorrect, the validation is insufficiently broad, and the experimental design overlooks established, efficient alternatives (truncated BPTT). While the insights on deep RNNs are interesting, they are overshadowed by these critical flaws. If the authors revise to:  
(i) Correctly frame the work as an *analysis* (not a novel architecture),  
(ii) Extend experiments to a speech or translation task, and  
(iii) Replace HF with a truncated BPTT baseline for the parentheses task,  
the paper could become a strong contribution. As-is, it risks misleading the community about both the novelty of the architecture and the generality of the findings.  

**Key Revisions Needed:**  
- Remove all claims of introducing deep RNNs; cite Graves (2013) and earlier work.  
- Add a speech task (e.g., TIMIT) to validate the "long-range information" finding.  
- Replace HF experiments with truncated BPTT (30 steps) for parentheses and LM, reporting entropy and training cost.  
- Clarify whether "plain BPTT" refers to truncated BPTT in the parentheses section.  

This work has potential but requires substantial revision to avoid being dismissed as a narrow, mischaracterized analysis. The proposed extensions are feasible within the scope of the paper and would significantly elevate its contribution.
