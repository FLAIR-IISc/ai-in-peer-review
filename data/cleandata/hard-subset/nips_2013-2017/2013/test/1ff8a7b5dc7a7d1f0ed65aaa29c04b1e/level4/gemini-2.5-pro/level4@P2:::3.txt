### Summary

This paper introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), an architecture that stacks multiple RNN layers to form a temporal processing hierarchy. The central motivation is that standard RNNs apply the same single-layer transformation at each timestep, which may be insufficient for complex, hierarchical processing of inputs and arbitrarily favors older data with more processing steps. The authors argue that by stacking RNNs, where each layer receives the hidden state of the layer below as input, the model can more naturally capture the multi-timescale structure inherent in sequential data.

The paper applies this architecture to the task of character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. The proposed DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on this benchmark, notably using only standard stochastic gradient descent (SGD). A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to a series of analytical experiments that aim to demonstrate that different layers in the DRNN learn to operate on different time scales and represent different levels of abstraction in the text.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and intellectually honest about its potential limitations. The topic of deep temporal models is highly relevant, and the work provides valuable insights into their function.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Strong Motivation and Novelty:** The critique of standard RNNs—that older data receives more processing than newer data for no compelling reason—is insightful. The proposed DRNN architecture is a logical and well-motivated approach to introduce hierarchical processing capabilities at each timestep.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark is a significant result. It is particularly noteworthy that this was accomplished with simple SGD, while previous work required more complex second-order methods like Hessian-free optimization.
3.  **Insightful Analysis:** The experiments designed to probe the internal dynamics of the trained networks are a major strength. The text generation examples (Table 2), the typo-perturbation analysis (Figure 3), and the investigation of long-term dependencies via parenthesis closing (Figure 4) provide compelling, albeit qualitative, evidence that the layers develop a functional hierarchy.
4.  **Clarity and Honesty:** The paper is clearly written and transparent about its methodology. The authors' acknowledgment of the interpretability challenges in their analysis (e.g., complex interdependencies between layers) is commendable.

**Arguments for Rejection / Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Potentially Unfair Experimental Comparison:** A primary concern is whether the comparison between the DRNNs and the standard RNN baseline is entirely fair. The total number of trainable parameters should be carefully matched. By my calculation, the parameter count for the baseline RNN (2119 units) seems significantly lower than for the DRNNs (~4.9M parameters). A standard RNN has recurrent and input weights, roughly `2119^2 + 2119*Nin`, while a 5-layer DRNN-AO has `5 * (706^2 + 706*706)`, ignoring output and bias terms. This suggests the DRNNs have a substantial advantage in parameter count. The paper's claims would be much stronger if the DRNNs were compared against a standard RNN with a similar number of total parameters (i.e., a much wider single-layer RNN).
2.  **Isolating the Benefit of Depth:** The paper provides evidence that higher layers learn longer-term, more abstract features. However, it does not conclusively demonstrate that the hierarchical *structure* is the cause. An alternative hypothesis is that any sufficiently large network will use different subsets of its units for these different roles. A crucial missing experiment would be to compare a DRNN against a standard (wider) RNN with a similar number of *units*. For example, comparing a 2-layer DRNN with 1000 units/layer to a standard RNN with 2000 units. This would help disentangle the benefits of depth from the benefits of simply having more neurons.
3.  **Alternative Experimental Visualization:** Instead of Figure 2, which shows the effect of removing a layer's contribution post-training, it would be more informative to see a plot of final test performance for DRNNs of varying depths (e.g., 1 to 5 layers) trained from scratch. To ensure a fair comparison, the layer widths could be adjusted so that each network has a comparable number of parameters or units. This would more directly answer the question of how depth impacts performance.
4.  **Details on Training:** The gradient normalization trick is critical to the training success but is not fully explained.
    *   Could the authors elaborate on its importance and the claim that it "avoids the problem of bifurcations"?
    *   How does this relate to other recent work on stabilizing RNN training, such as the gradient clipping methods explored by Pascanu et al. (ICML 2013)?
    *   Does normalizing the gradient vector in this manner risk interfering with the stochastic averaging properties of SGD?

**Missing References:**
The results on this Wikipedia dataset should also be contextualized with other relevant work, for example by Mikolov et al., who have also published results on large-scale language modeling tasks.

**Questions for the Authors:**
*   Have you considered alternative ways to introduce depth, for instance, by adding more feed-forward layers of non-linearity between each recurrent time step?

**Minor Issues:**
*   The acronyms DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO are used before they are explicitly defined in the text (they are first defined in the Figure 1 caption). Please define them in Section 2.2.

### Conclusion

This is a well-executed and interesting paper that presents strong results and insightful analysis on an important class of models. The primary weakness lies in the experimental comparison to the baseline, which may not be properly controlled for the number of parameters. Addressing this issue with additional, more controlled experiments would significantly strengthen the paper's central claims. Despite this reservation, the paper's novel analysis and strong performance make it a valuable contribution to the field.
