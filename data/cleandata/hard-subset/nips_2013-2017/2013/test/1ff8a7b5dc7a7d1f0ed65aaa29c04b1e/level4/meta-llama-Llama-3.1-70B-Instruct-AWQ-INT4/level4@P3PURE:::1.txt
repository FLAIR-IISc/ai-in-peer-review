The authors propose a novel deep architecture that integrates the hierarchical representation of deep learning with time-series modeling, akin to Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) or recurrent neural networks. The proposed training algorithm constructs the network layer-by-layer using supervised (pre-)training with a next-letter prediction objective. The experiments demonstrate that, after training very large networks for approximately 10 days, the network performance on a Wikipedia dataset published by Hinton et al. surpasses that of previous work. The authors then delve into an analysis and discussion of the network's approach to its task, providing insights into how it models long-term dependencies in higher layers and captures correspondence between opening and closing parentheses as a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state".

The paper is of good quality, featuring clear writing, well-designed experiments, and figures that effectively support the findings. However, my primary concern lies with the experimental setup. The comparison is limited to a single dataset, which appears to be understudied. Furthermore, the direct comparison is only made with other RNN-related models, omitting potentially more common hierarchical HMMs and other approaches that can tackle similar tasks (although Table 1 lists alternative methods, they are evaluated on a different corpus, rendering them incomparable).

The paper's contribution lies more in the analysis of how the network operates, which is examined in considerable depth, rather than in its achievements. While the paper aims to introduce a new deep architecture that combines hierarchical features of deep learning with time-series modeling, the results are somewhat inconclusive. Nevertheless, the paper is well-written, and the model is thoroughly analyzed, which somewhat compensates for the limitations in the experimental results.
