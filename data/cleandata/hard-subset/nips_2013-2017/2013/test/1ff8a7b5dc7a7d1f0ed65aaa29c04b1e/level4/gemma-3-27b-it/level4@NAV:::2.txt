I largely concur with the authors’ response regarding the single-task assessment.

It would nonetheless be worthwhile to explore truncated backpropagation, for instance, with a length of 30 steps. While it may be less effective at learning parenthesis balancing (though this should be verified), it could potentially achieve comparable entropies with significantly reduced computational cost.

This work investigates the capabilities of deep recurrent neural networks and arrives at notable conclusions: specifically, that increasing depth enhances performance relative to standard RNNs when parameter count is held constant. Further interesting findings include an analysis revealing greater “long-term information” retention in deeper networks, and the demonstration that plain Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT) can successfully train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses – a capability previously attributed to Hierarchical Feedback (HF). 

However, the claim of introducing the “deep RNN” is inaccurate, as this architecture has prior art, such as that presented in Graves (2013) [http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf], and likely even earlier work. The paper should therefore refrain from presenting this as a novel contribution, and instead emphasize its analytical focus.

The analysis, while valuable, would be strengthened by extension to additional tasks, perhaps within the domain of speech processing. This would help to determine whether the observed findings are specific to the current problem or represent a more general phenomenon. 

Finally, the HF experiments may have been prohibitively expensive, given the established success of truncated backpropagation – introduced in the 1990s and effectively employed by Mikolov (as detailed in his PhD thesis) for training high-performing RNN language models. It is plausible that truncated BPTT would yield good results here as well, and confirmation of this would be beneficial. 

In summary, this paper presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs. The key results demonstrate that: 1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs in character-level language modelling when parameter count is fixed; 2) deeper layers exhibit enhanced long-range structure; and 3) truncated BPTT can facilitate learning to balance parentheses in character-level RNNs. While the results are interesting, the paper would be considerably strengthened by a comparable analysis performed on a speech task – for example, to validate the superiority of deeper RNNs and to further explore the presence of long-range information in their deeper layers.
