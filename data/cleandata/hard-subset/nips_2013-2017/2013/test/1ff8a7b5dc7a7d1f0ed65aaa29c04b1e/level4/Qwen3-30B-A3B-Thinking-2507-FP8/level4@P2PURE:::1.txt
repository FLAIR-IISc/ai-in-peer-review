

The authors propose a novel deep architecture that integrates hierarchical representations from deep learning with time-series modeling approaches used in HMMs or RNNs. Their training algorithm constructs the network layer-by-layer via supervised pre-training on a next-letter prediction objective. Experiments demonstrate that after training extremely large networks for approximately 10 days, the model achieves improved performance on the Wikipedia dataset introduced by Hinton et al., surpassing prior work. The authors subsequently analyze the network's operational mechanisms, such as how higher layers model long-term dependencies and how correspondence between opening and closing parentheses is represented as a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state."

The paper is of good quality: it is well-written, experiments are well-designed, and figures effectively support the findings. My primary concern lies with the experimental evaluation. The comparison is limited to a single dataset with minimal prior work, and direct comparisons are restricted to other RNN-based models without benchmarking against more conventional hierarchical HMMs or similar approaches capable of addressing analogous tasks (Table 1 lists alternative methods, but on a different corpus, rendering them incomparable). 

The paper's stated contribution centers on proposing a new deep architecture combining hierarchical deep learning features with time-series modeling. However, the paper's strongest contribution is its analysis of *how* the network operates (which is thorough and well-executed), rather than its achieved results. This misalignment between the paper's stated focus and its actual contribution renders the results somewhat inconclusive.
