This paper introduces and analyzes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), an architecture that stacks multiple RNN layers to form a temporal hierarchy. The core idea is that each layer processes the sequence of hidden states from the layer below, allowing the network to naturally capture information at different time scales. The authors apply this architecture to the challenging task of character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus. They demonstrate that a 5-layer DRNN, trained with simple Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD), achieves state-of-the-art performance, matching results previously obtained only with more complex Hessian-free optimization methods. A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to a series of experiments designed to analyze the emergent properties of the DRNN, arguing that higher layers learn to represent more abstract and longer-term dependencies in the text.

This work builds upon prior research into hierarchical RNNs (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio, 1996) and more recent applications of deep/stacked RNNs (e.g., Graves et al., 2013). While the architecture itself is not entirely novel, this paper's primary contributions are the state-of-the-art empirical results on a large-scale, real-world task using a simple training algorithm, and a more in-depth analysis of the functional hierarchy that emerges within the network's layers.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates impressive performance on a difficult benchmark. Achieving results comparable to the state-of-the-art (Sutskever et al., 2011) using standard SGD is a significant finding, suggesting that this architecture may be easier to train than other complex RNN variants.

2.  **In-depth Analysis:** The authors go beyond simply reporting performance metrics and conduct a series of thoughtful experiments to understand the model's behavior. The analyses of emergent time scales (Fig. 3), parenthesis closing, and particularly the qualitative text generation examples (Table 2) provide compelling evidence that the different layers specialize, with higher layers capturing longer-range dependencies.

3.  **Clarity and Honesty:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors are commendably transparent about the potential limitations of their analysis, for instance, acknowledging the difficulty of interpreting "ablation" experiments where individual layers are removed post-training due to complex interdependencies.

4.  **Significance:** The paper validates the effectiveness of a simple and intuitive idea—stacking RNNs—for hierarchical temporal processing. The motivation that in a standard RNN, older data passes through more processing steps than newer data is insightful, and the proposed deep architecture is a logical solution. This work provides a solid foundation for further research into deep temporal models.

**Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Baseline Comparison and Parameter Count:** The most significant weakness is the comparison to the standard RNN baseline. The DRNN models have approximately 4.9 million parameters, while the standard RNN has significantly fewer (~2119^2 ≈ 4.5 million). However, the paper's comparison between a 5-layer DRNN-1O (727 units/layer) and the standard RNN (2119 units) seems to miscalculate the DRNN's parameter count. A 5-layer DRNN has 5 recurrent matrices and 4 inter-layer matrices. For the DRNN-1O, this would be roughly 5 * 727^2 + 4 * 727^2, which is far more than 4.9 million parameters. Even if the calculation in the paper is correct via some unstated simplification, the fundamental point remains: the comparison is between a deep, narrow model and a shallow, wide one with a different parameter count.

    To make a truly convincing case for the benefits of depth, the authors should compare the DRNN against a standard RNN with a comparable number of *parameters* (i.e., a much wider single-layer RNN). This would help disentangle whether the performance gain comes from the hierarchical structure or simply from the increased model capacity.

2.  **Analysis of Depth:** The experiments demonstrate that higher layers in a trained DRNN take on longer-term roles, but they do not conclusively prove that the hierarchical structure is *necessary* for this to happen. A more direct investigation into the contribution of depth would be highly valuable. For example, instead of Figure 2, a plot showing the final BPC performance of DRNNs with varying depths (e.g., 2, 3, 4, 5 layers), perhaps normalized for the same total parameter count, would provide much stronger evidence for the paper's central claim.

3.  **Justification of Training Method:** The gradient normalization technique is described as a heuristic "trick." While effective, this section could be strengthened by citing and relating it to more formal contemporary work on training RNNs, such as the paper on gradient clipping by Pascanu et al. (ICML 2013). A brief discussion of why this normalization is necessary (e.g., exploding gradients) and how it might affect the convergence properties of SGD would improve the technical quality.

4.  **Related Work:** While the paper covers key works, the comparison could be broadened by including results from other contemporary RNN-based language models, for example, the work by Mikolov et al., to provide a more complete picture of the state of the art at the time.

**Minor Points:**

*   The terms DRNN-AO and DRNN-1O are used before being formally defined in the main text (they are only explained in the caption for Figure 1). It would improve clarity to define them in Section 2.2.
*   The authors suggest that another approach would be to add extra layers of non-linearities between each timestep in a standard RNN. It would be interesting to hear their thoughts on how that might compare to the proposed architecture.

**Conclusion:**

This is a very good paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field. The investigation into deep recurrent architectures is timely, the results are strong, and the analysis is insightful. My primary reservation concerns the fairness of the experimental comparison to the standard RNN baseline. If the authors can provide a more controlled comparison that demonstrates the superiority of depth over a shallow but equally large model, it would significantly strengthen their claims. Despite this, the paper is well-executed and provides compelling evidence for the utility of DRNNs, making it a solid candidate for acceptance.
