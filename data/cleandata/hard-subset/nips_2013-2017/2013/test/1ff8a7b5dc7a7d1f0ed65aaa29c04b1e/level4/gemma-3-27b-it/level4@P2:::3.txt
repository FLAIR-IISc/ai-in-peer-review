This paper investigates deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), a hybrid of deep and recurrent architectures, demonstrating their effectiveness for text prediction and compression. The authors argue that DRNNs, with recurrent connections at each layer, naturally capture temporal hierarchies present in data, addressing a limitation of standard recurrent neural networks. The experiments show competitive performance on a Wikipedia text corpus, and analyze how different layers contribute to modeling varying timescales within the data. The paper is well-written, intellectually honest, and thoroughly discusses potential shortcomings.

The core idea – questioning the necessity of numerous processing steps for older data in RNNs and proposing a hierarchical approach – is insightful. Building a deep RNN seems a reasonable way to improve processing of both short and long-term dependencies. One potential extension would be to explore adding nonlinearities between each timestep, and evaluating its effect. 

The impact of gradient normalization and its relation to “avoiding bifurcations” warrants further discussion. The authors mention a related ICML 2013 paper from the University of Montreal on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs, and referencing it would strengthen the analysis. A consideration is whether gradient normalization impacts the averaging behavior inherent in stochastic gradient descent.

The paper would benefit from comparison to related work by Mikolov et al. on similar Wikipedia compression tasks, providing a more comprehensive benchmark. 

Among the experiments designed to understand layer roles, the text generation experiment (Table 2) is particularly compelling. However, the authors acknowledge the difficulty in interpreting the effects of ablating layer contributions due to potential interdependencies, a valid concern.

A significant missing element is demonstrating whether higher layers genuinely process more abstract and long-term properties than if those units were placed in the first layer. Showing that these layers *benefit* from preceding layers – beyond simply assuming these roles post-training – would significantly strengthen the paper’s claims.

The comparison to the standard RNN is potentially skewed due to differing parameter counts. The regular RNN appears to have fewer parameters (considering recurrent and inter-layer weights), potentially favoring the DRNNs. A more convincing comparison would involve DRNNs outperforming a standard RNN with an equivalent number of parameters or units. For instance, comparing a 2-layer DRNN to a standard RNN with the same number of units would provide a fairer assessment.

Instead of Figure 2, presenting a benchmark comparison of DRNNs with varying depths, trained from scratch (potentially with wider layers to balance parameter counts), would offer valuable insight.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The DRNN architecture represents a reasonable and potentially impactful combination of deep and recurrent networks.
*   **Performance:** The DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art results for recurrent networks on a challenging text compression task.
*   **Analysis:** The paper provides insightful analysis of layer behavior and emergent timescales within the network.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
*   **Honesty:** The authors openly acknowledge limitations and potential pitfalls.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Parameter count discrepancy:** The comparison with the standard RNN is potentially unfair due to differing parameter counts.
*   **Missing Ablation Study:** Lack of a demonstration that higher layers genuinely benefit from deeper processing.
*   **Limited comparison:** The lack of comparison to recent work by Mikolov et al. 
*   **Incomplete Experimental Validation:** The lack of a comprehensive study of depth vs performance with balanced parameters. 

**Minor points:**

*   Define DRNN-AO and IO in the text, not solely in Figure 1.



Overall, this is a worthwhile contribution to the field. Addressing the concerns regarding the comparison to standard RNNs and providing further evidence for the benefits of hierarchical processing would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
