

This paper proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), a hierarchical architecture where each layer is a recurrent network that receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. The authors argue that this structure naturally captures temporal hierarchies in time series data, which common RNNs fail to do explicitly. They demonstrate state-of-the-art performance in character-level language modeling on Wikipedia text using simple SGD training, and provide analysis of emergent time scales across layers.

The work builds upon prior research on hierarchical RNNs (e.g., [3,5,6]) and stacked LSTMs [8], but makes a distinct contribution by explicitly analyzing how the hierarchical architecture creates different time scales at different layers. The paper relates well to NIPS 2013 work on hierarchical RNNs [5] and deep learning for language modeling [19], but could better contextualize its contribution relative to more recent NIPS papers on RNNs and deep architectures.

Strengths:
1. The paper presents a compelling argument for why hierarchical RNNs may better capture temporal structures in time series data, with a clear theoretical foundation about how different layers develop different time scales.
2. The experimental design is thorough, with careful analysis of layer contributions through both quantitative metrics (BPC) and qualitative text generation.
3. The analysis of temporal dynamics through typo-perturbation experiments (Figure 3) provides convincing evidence of emergent time-scale hierarchies.
4. The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the architecture and experimental setup, and demonstrates intellectual honesty about the limitations of their approach.

Weaknesses:
1. The comparison between DRNNs and standard RNNs is potentially unfair due to parameter count differences. The standard RNN has 2119 units while the DRNN-1O has 727 units per layer across 5 layers. As the authors note, the DRNN has approximately 4.9 million parameters compared to the RNN's approximately 2.7 million, which may artificially favor the DRNN. A fairer comparison would use a standard RNN with similar total parameters or similar number of units.

2. The "brain surgery" experiments (removing layer outputs) have limited interpretability due to complex interdependencies between layers, as the authors themselves acknowledge. The paper would be strengthened by a more principled analysis of layer contributions, perhaps using techniques like layer-wise relevance propagation.

3. The paper could better situate itself within recent work on text prediction. The authors mention Mikolov et al.'s work on language modeling [19] but do not compare their results to more recent NIPS papers on text prediction (e.g., [18, 21]).

4. The gradient normalization technique is not sufficiently justified. The authors state it "avoids the problem of bifurcations" but provide no theoretical or empirical evidence for this claim. The connection to recent ICML 2013 work on gradient truncation [2] would strengthen this methodological choice.

5. The paper claims DRNNs reach "state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks" but does not sufficiently demonstrate how this compares to other recent RNN approaches that have used more advanced training algorithms (e.g., Hessian-free optimization [15, 16]).

Pro arguments for acceptance:
- The paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to understanding how hierarchical architectures can capture temporal hierarchies
- The experimental analysis of time-scale emergence is novel and well-executed
- The paper is well-written and transparent about its limitations
- The DRNN architecture could inspire future work on hierarchical temporal modeling

Con arguments against acceptance:
- The parameter count comparison between DRNNs and standard RNNs is unfair
- The interpretation of layer contributions is limited by complex interdependencies
- The paper does not sufficiently contextualize its results within recent NIPS literature on language modeling
- The gradient normalization method requires better justification

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to understanding how hierarchical architectures can better capture temporal structures in time series data. The empirical analysis of emergent time scales is particularly compelling. However, the unfair parameter comparison and limited justification of the gradient normalization technique are significant concerns that should be addressed before acceptance. The paper would be substantially improved by a more careful comparison with standard RNNs of comparable size, and by better situating the work within recent NIPS literature on language modeling.

The authors have made a thoughtful contribution to the field, and with the suggested revisions, this paper would be an excellent addition to NIPS. As it stands, the paper is well-written, technically sound, and demonstrates a significant advance in understanding hierarchical temporal processing, but the parameter comparison issue prevents it from being fully convincing.
