This paper explores the concept of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) for text prediction and compression tasks. The authors propose that the arbitrariness of older data benefiting from more layers of processing compared to newer data can be addressed by building a deep RNN. This approach seems reasonable, as it can potentially improve performance on shorter-term tasks and offer other benefits.

One potential extension to this work could be the addition of extra layers of nonlinearities between each time step. It would be interesting to know if the authors have experimented with this approach.

The use of gradient normalization in this paper is also noteworthy. However, it is unclear how important this component is to the overall performance of the DRNN. The authors claim that this technique avoids the problem of bifurcations, but further explanation is needed. Additionally, a recent paper from ICML 2013 on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs may be relevant to this work. Furthermore, normalizing the gradient in this way may potentially disrupt the averaging behavior of stochastic gradient descent (SGD), which could have unintended consequences.

In terms of previous results on Wikipedia compression tasks, the authors may want to compare their work to that of Mikolov et al.

The experiments designed to examine the roles played by each layer in terms of time scale and abstraction are well-conceived, but the text generation experiment (as shown in Table 2) is the most persuasive. However, as the authors themselves point out, interpreting the effects of "brain surgery" on RNNs can be problematic due to the complex interdependencies between the outputs of various layers.

A significant missing piece of the empirical puzzle in this paper is the question of whether the higher layers are actually better at processing more abstract and long-term properties of the text than if the units were moved to the first layer. The fact that the higher layers take on these roles after training is good but incomplete evidence that this is the case.

The comparison between the regular RNN and the DRNN may be unfair, as the regular RNN has fewer parameters (2767^2 * 5 > 2119^2, considering both recurrent and inter-layer weight matrices). A more convincing comparison would be between a standard RNN with the same number of parameters or units as the DRNN. For example, a 2-layer DRNN versus a standard RNN with the same number of units would strengthen the paper's claims.

Instead of Figure 2, it would be more informative to see how well various depths of DRNN perform on the benchmarks when trained from scratch, possibly with wider layers to make up for the difference in the number of parameters and/or units.

Minor suggestions include defining DRNN-AO and IO in the text, not just in Figure 1.

Overall, this paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors demonstrate intellectual honesty and thoroughness in discussing the potential problems and shortcomings of their work. The concept of deep temporal networks is worth exploring, and this paper provides valuable insights into how they function after training.
