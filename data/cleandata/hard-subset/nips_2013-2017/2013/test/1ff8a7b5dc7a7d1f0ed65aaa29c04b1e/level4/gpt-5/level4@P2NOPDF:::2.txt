REPLY TO REBUTTAL:
- I largely agree with the concern about evaluating on a single task.
- It would still be worthwhile to try truncated backpropagation through time (e.g., 30 steps). While it may be less likely to learn parenthesis balancing—this should be checked—it could achieve competitive entropies at a fraction of the cost.

This paper investigates the usefulness of deep recurrent neural networks (stacked RNNs) and presents an informative analysis. The main findings are: (1) with a fixed parameter budget, deeper RNNs outperform standard single-layer RNNs on character-level language modeling; (2) deeper layers capture more long-range information; and (3) plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, a result previously thought to require HF.

However, the paper claims to introduce deep RNNs, which is not accurate. Similar architectures have been used previously (e.g., Graves, ICASSP 2013) and likely earlier. The manuscript should not claim architectural novelty; it is perfectly fine to focus on analysis and empirical insights instead.

While the analysis is meaningful, its generality is unclear because it is demonstrated on a single problem. Replicating the findings on additional tasks—ideally including a speech task—would strengthen the conclusions and reduce the risk that the results are dataset-specific artifacts. Showing that deeper RNNs also outperform shallower ones on speech, and that higher layers encode longer-range structure there as well, would notably increase the paper’s impact.

Regarding optimization, the HF experiments may be unnecessarily expensive. Truncated BPTT, introduced in the 1990s and used effectively by Mikolov (see his PhD thesis) for strong language models, is a plausible and much cheaper alternative. Please consider including truncated BPTT baselines (e.g., 30-step truncation), report their compute/performance trade-offs, and test whether they can match entropy results even if they fail to learn strict parenthesis balancing. Verifying this would provide a more complete and practically relevant comparison.

Overall, this is an interesting and generally well-executed analysis of deep RNNs with clear, useful conclusions. The paper would be significantly stronger if it (a) corrected the novelty claim, (b) validated the findings on at least one additional domain such as speech, and (c) included truncated BPTT as a baseline to contextualize the cost and necessity of HF.
