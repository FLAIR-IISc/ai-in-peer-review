I appreciate the authors' observation that older data can benefit from more layers of processing compared to newer data, and their proposed solution of building a deep RNN to improve performance on shorter-term tasks. Adding extra layers of nonlinearities between each time step is another potential approach, and I would be interested to know if the authors have explored this option.

I am curious about the importance of gradient normalization in the authors' approach and how it helps to "avoid the problem of bifurcations." A recent paper from ICML 2013 by researchers from the University of Montreal on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs may be relevant to this discussion. Additionally, I wonder whether normalizing the gradient in this way could potentially disrupt the "averaging" behavior of stochastic gradient descent (SGD).

In terms of previous work on Wikipedia compression tasks, the authors may want to compare their results to those of Mikolov et al.

Among the experiments designed to examine the roles of each layer in terms of time-scale and abstraction, I find the text generation experiment (as shown in Table 2) to be the most persuasive. However, as the authors themselves note in the paragraph on line 241, interpreting the effects of "brain surgery" on RNNs can be problematic due to the complex interdependencies between the outputs of different layers.

In my opinion, the biggest missing piece of the empirical puzzle in this paper is the question of whether the higher layers are actually better at processing more abstract and long-term properties of the text than if the units were moved to the first layer. That is, are they benefiting from the extra levels of processing that precede them in a non-trivial way? While it is encouraging that the higher layers take on seemingly more abstract and longer-term roles after training, this is incomplete evidence that they are truly processing more abstract information.

I notice that the regular RNN has fewer parameters in these experiments (since 2767^2  5 > 2119^2, counting both recurrent and inter-layer weight matrices), which may make the comparison unfair. A more convincing demonstration would be to show that the deeper RNNs outperform a standard RNN with the same number of parameters or units. For example, comparing a 2-layer DRNN to a standard RNN with the same number of units, where the difference in the number of parameters does not unfairly favor the RNN, would strengthen the paper's claims.

Instead of Figure 2, it would be more informative to see how well various depths of DRNN perform on the benchmarks when trained from scratch, possibly with wider layers to compensate for the difference in the number of parameters and/or units.

Minor points:

* The authors should define DRNN-AO and IO in the text, not just in Figure 1.

This paper explores a hybrid of deep and recurrent neural networks called DRNNs, which combine the strengths of deep networks with recurrent connections that operate through time. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this architecture for text prediction and compression, outperforming existing approaches. A significant portion of the paper is devoted to a series of experiments designed to argue that higher-level layers process and represent more abstract and long-term structures in the data. While these experiments are convincing, I have some reservations, as discussed below.

I believe it is worthwhile to investigate these deep temporal networks and gain insight into their behavior after training. The paper is well-written, easy to read, and commendably honest and thorough in discussing its potential problems and limitations.
