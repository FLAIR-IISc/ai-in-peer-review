This paper presents a novel deep learning architecture that integrates hierarchical feature learning with time-series modeling techniques commonly found in Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs). The proposed model is trained layer-by-layer using supervised pre-training with a next-letter prediction objective. The authors demonstrate that, after an extended training period (approximately 10 days) on a large-scale dataset, their network achieves improved performance on a Wikipedia text corpus previously introduced by Hinton et al. Notably, the paper also includes a detailed analysis of the network’s internal representations and behaviors, revealing insights into how it approaches the task of sequential prediction. For example, the authors observe that long-term dependencies are modeled in higher layers, and demonstrate how balanced structures like parentheses are represented as “pseudo-stable attractor-like states.”

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented. The experimental setup is robust, and the figures effectively illustrate and support the reported findings. However, my primary concern lies with the limited scope of the experimental evaluation. The evaluation is confined to a single dataset, which, while challenging, appears to lack a substantial body of existing comparative work. Furthermore, the direct comparisons are primarily restricted to other RNN-based models, omitting comparisons with potentially relevant hierarchical HMMs and other models capable of addressing similar sequential prediction tasks. While Table 1 lists alternative approaches, these are evaluated on a different corpus, rendering a direct and meaningful comparison difficult.

The paper’s principal contribution resides more in its insightful analysis of *how* the network functions, rather than in achieving state-of-the-art results on a broad range of benchmarks. The analysis is indeed well-executed and provides valuable understanding of the model's learned representations. However, the paper initially positions itself as presenting a novel architecture with performance improvements, and the experimental results, while positive, don't fully substantiate this claim given the limited comparative landscape. 

Specifically, the lack of comparison to non-RNN sequential models is a weakness. It’s unclear whether the performance gains are attributable to the specific architectural choices (combining deep learning and time-series modeling) or simply to the scale of the network and the pre-training procedure. Demonstrating superiority over more established baselines – even if requiring adaptation to the same dataset – would significantly strengthen the paper’s contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   Novel and interesting architectural combination.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Robust experimental setup.
*   Insightful analysis of the network’s internal representations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation on a single dataset.
*   Lack of comparison to relevant hierarchical HMMs and other sequential models.
*   The primary contribution lies in analysis rather than substantial performance gains.
*   Extremely long training time (10 days) may be a practical limitation.



**Overall:**

This is a solid paper with a compelling idea and a well-executed analysis. However, the limited scope of the experiments and the relatively modest performance gains—when considered in isolation—prevent it from being a particularly strong contribution. I would recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding comparative evaluation. Specifically, I recommend adding comparisons to at least a few relevant hierarchical HMMs or similar models on the same dataset, or providing a more compelling justification for why such comparisons are not feasible. Additionally, a discussion of the practicality of the 10-day training time would be valuable.
