Response to rebuttal
- I agree with the concern about evaluating on only one task; adding a second domain (e.g., speech) would strengthen the generality of the claims.
- Please consider reporting results with truncated BPTT (e.g., 30 steps). While it may not learn long-range constructs such as balanced parentheses as reliably, it could achieve competitive BPC at a fraction of the cost; verifying this would be valuable.

Summary
This paper studies stacked recurrent architectures in which each layer is a recurrent neural network that consumes the previous layer’s hidden state. Two output variants are considered: only the top layer connected to output (DRNN-1O) and all layers connected (DRNN-AO). Models are trained with SGD and BPTT on a large Wikipedia character-level language modeling task with ~4.9M parameters. The authors report state-of-the-art performance among recurrent models with comparable parameter counts, previously achieved with multiplicative RNNs trained via Hessian-free optimization. They analyze emergent time scales across layers using perturbation experiments, ablate layer-wise output contributions, and demonstrate generative behavior including long-range patterns such as closing parentheses.

Relation to prior work
Stacked/Hierarchical RNNs have significant precedent (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández, Graves & Schmidhuber; and stacked bidirectional LSTMs for TIMIT by Graves et al.). The paper’s strength lies in analysis and empirical evidence rather than architectural novelty. The manuscript should temper claims of introducing the “deep RNN” and cite earlier work more explicitly.

Strengths
- Clear empirical finding: with a fixed parameter budget, deeper recurrent architectures outperform a single-layer RNN on character-level LM.
- Simple training with SGD/BPTT, including a practical layer-wise training schedule for DRNN-1O, achieves results comparable to HF-trained models.
- Insightful analyses: layer ablations, typo-perturbation and context-switch tests that reveal emergent temporal hierarchies; qualitative generation illustrating short- and long-range structure; targeted probe on parentheses.
- The observation that layers naturally specialize to different time scales is compelling and useful to practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Scope: all results are on a single task/dataset. It is unclear whether conclusions hold across domains (e.g., speech, other text corpora).
- Baselines: no comparison to LSTMs or gated variants, which are standard for modeling long-range dependencies; no truncated BPTT baseline (known to work well for language modeling).
- Confounds in training: DRNN-1O uses incremental layer-wise training while DRNN-AO and RNN do not; this makes it harder to attribute gains purely to depth vs training procedure.
- Limited ablations on depth/width trade-offs under a fixed parameter budget; no report of variance across random seeds.
- Novelty is primarily empirical/analytical rather than architectural.

Quality
The experiments are generally sound: large dataset, appropriate metric (BPC), careful training descriptions (gradient normalization, BPTT window, curriculum for DRNN-1O). The perturbation analyses are well-motivated and averaged over many instances. Some claims should be qualified: the architecture is not new; the comparison to HF-trained models would benefit from reporting total data passes and compute. Including additional baselines (LSTM, truncated BPTT) would strengthen the technical case.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well-organized. Suggestions:
- Clarify the gradient “normalization” (is it rescaling to unit norm or clipping to a threshold?).
- State the exact BPTT truncation (250 steps with first 50 steps discarded in backprop) and justify this choice.
- Discuss the 95-character vocabulary vs. the 86 used in prior work and any impact on BPC comparability.
- Separate clearly the effects of output-at-all-layers from the incremental training schedule.

Originality
Architectural novelty is limited; similar stacked recurrent structures have appeared previously. The contribution is the systematic analysis demonstrating learned temporal hierarchies and that plain SGD/BPTT can match HF-trained recurrent models on this task.

Significance
Despite limited novelty, the results are practically significant: they support using depth in RNNs under a parameter budget, show that SGD suffices on large-scale char LM, and provide useful diagnostic analyses. Broader impact would increase with additional tasks and stronger baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a truncated BPTT baseline (e.g., 30–50 steps) and report BPC and qualitative long-range behavior (e.g., parentheses).
- Include LSTM (single-layer and stacked) comparisons under the same parameter budget and training budget.
- Control for training procedure: evaluate DRNN-AO with and without the incremental schedule; test DRNN-1O trained end-to-end.
- Explore depth/width trade-offs with fixed parameters and report sensitivity/variance across seeds.
- Consider a second domain (e.g., TIMIT/WSJ phoneme or word tasks) to test whether the temporal hierarchy findings generalize.
- Report compute (GPU-days) and effective epochs for fairer comparison to HF results.
- Release code/hyperparameters to aid reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear empirical benefit of depth with fixed parameter budget.
- Strong, insightful analyses of emergent temporal hierarchies.
- Demonstrates that SGD/BPTT can achieve results comparable to HF on large-scale char LM.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty; stacked RNNs are not new.
- Single-task evaluation; missing key baselines (LSTM, truncated BPTT).
- Training procedural differences confound attribution of gains.

Overall
An informative empirical/analytical paper on deep recurrent networks for character-level modeling. I lean toward acceptance if the authors adjust novelty claims and, ideally, add at least one strong baseline (e.g., truncated BPTT or LSTM) or a second task. Otherwise, it remains a valuable analysis paper with practical takeaways.
