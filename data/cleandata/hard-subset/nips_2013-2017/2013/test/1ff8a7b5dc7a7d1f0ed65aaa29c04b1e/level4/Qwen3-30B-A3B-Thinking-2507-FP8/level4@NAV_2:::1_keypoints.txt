- Proposes a deep recurrent neural network (DRNN) stacking recurrent layers where each layer takes the hidden state of the layer below, creating a temporal hierarchy.  
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art character-level language modeling on Wikipedia (BPC ≈ 1.51) trained with plain SGD rather than Hessian-free optimization.  
- Provides detailed analysis showing emergent time scales: lower layers capture short-term character statistics, upper layers capture long-term structure (e.g., parentheses matching).  
- Experimental design is strong: clear motivation, layer-wise ablation studies, typo-perturbation tests, and insightful parenthesis-closing analysis.  
- Writing and figures are clear and effectively support claims about layer-specific roles.  
- Parenthesis analysis reveals "pseudo-stable attractor-like" behavior, giving mechanistic insight into hierarchy.  
- Main weakness is narrow evaluation scope: experiments use only the Wikipedia corpus.  
- Lacks comparisons to established hierarchical sequence models (e.g., HMMs, deep HMMs) and other relevant benchmarks.  
- Uses non-comparable baselines (e.g., PAQ compression), weakening the "state-of-the-art" claim for recurrent models.  
- Missing broader validation: no ablations on non-language tasks or scalability tests to demonstrate generality.  
- Pro acceptance points: novel architectural analysis of time-scale emergence, solid empirical validation of layer roles, and demonstration that hierarchical temporal processing emerges with simple training.  
- Con acceptance points: limited dataset scope, insufficient comparison to prior hierarchical models, and results appear incremental rather than a clear field advance.  
- Overall assessment: technically sound, well-written, and original in perspective, but its significance is constrained by the narrow evaluation and comparative context.
