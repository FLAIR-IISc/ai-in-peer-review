I appreciate the authors’ insight that older data may benefit from more layers of processing than newer data, and that a deep RNN offers a reasonable approach to modeling shorter-term dependencies alongside other potential benefits. It would also be worthwhile to investigate adding further nonlinearities between timesteps – have the authors considered this?

The importance of gradient normalization and its mechanism for avoiding bifurcations warrants further explanation. The recent ICML 2013 work from the University of Montreal on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs may be relevant here. Furthermore, could normalizing gradients in this manner disrupt the averaging effect of SGD?

Regarding prior work on Wikipedia compression, a comparison with the contributions of Mikolov et al. would be beneficial.

Of the experiments designed to probe the roles of different layers with respect to timescale and abstraction, the text generation results (Table 2) are the most compelling. However, as the authors acknowledge (line 241), interpreting the effects of layer manipulation in RNNs is challenging due to the complex interdependencies that likely develop between layers.

A key empirical question remaining is whether the higher layers demonstrably process abstract and long-term text properties *better* than if those units were placed in the first layer. Simply observing that these roles emerge in training provides incomplete evidence that they are benefiting from the preceding layers’ processing.

I observed that the regular RNN has fewer parameters in these experiments (2767^2 * 2 > 2119^2 * 2, considering recurrent and interlayer weights), which may introduce unfairness to the comparison. A stronger evaluation would demonstrate that deeper RNNs outperform a standard RNN with a comparable number of parameters, or, ideally, the same number of units. For example, comparing a 2-layer DRNN to a standard RNN with an equivalent number of units would help isolate the benefits of depth. This would significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions.

Additionally, a figure illustrating the performance of DRNNs with varying depths trained from scratch – perhaps with wider layers in shallower models to control for parameter/unit count – would be more informative than Figure 2. 

Minor points:
- Please define DRNN-AO and IO within the text, and not just in Figure 1. This paper investigates DRNNs, a hybrid of deep and recurrent networks featuring recurrent connections at each layer, demonstrating strong performance on text prediction and compression tasks. The authors present experiments intended to show that higher layers represent more abstract and long-term structures, which are generally convincing, though I have some reservations detailed below and suggest additional experiments. Overall, this is a worthwhile investigation into deep temporal networks, offering insight into their functionality. The paper is well-written, intellectually honest, and thoroughly addresses its own potential limitations, which I particularly appreciate.
