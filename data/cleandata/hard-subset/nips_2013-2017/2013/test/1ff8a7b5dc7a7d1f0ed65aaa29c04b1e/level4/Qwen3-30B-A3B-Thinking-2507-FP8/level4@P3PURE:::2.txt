

Reply to Rebuttal:  
I largely agree with the comment regarding the single-task nature of the work. However, it would still be valuable to test truncated backpropagation with, for example, 30 steps. While this approach is less likely to learn to balance parentheses (though this should be verified), it may achieve competitive entropies at a significantly reduced computational cost.  

The paper investigates the utility of deep recurrent neural networks and presents several interesting findings. When holding the number of parameters constant, deeper recurrent neural networks outperform standard RNNs. The analysis further reveals that deeper networks contain more "long-term information," and demonstrates that plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses—a task previously thought to require hierarchical feedback (HF).  

However, the paper claims to introduce the "deep RNN" architecture, which has been used before (e.g., Graves, 2013, and likely earlier). The paper should not claim to have introduced this architecture; it is acceptable to focus on analysis without proposing a novel architecture.  

The analysis, while meaningful, would be significantly strengthened by applying it to multiple tasks (e.g., speech). Without such validation, the findings risk being artifacts of the specific character-level language modeling task.  

Additionally, the HF experiments appear unnecessarily expensive. Truncated backpropagation (introduced in the 1990s) was successfully employed by Mikolov (in his PhD thesis) to train RNNs as excellent language models. It is highly probable that truncated BPTT would also perform well here, and the paper would benefit from confirming this.  

In summary, the work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs, with three main results: (1) deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs with fixed parameter counts on character-level language modeling; (2) deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure; and (3) plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses. To substantially strengthen the paper, it should extend its analysis to additional tasks (e.g., speech) to confirm the generality of its findings.
