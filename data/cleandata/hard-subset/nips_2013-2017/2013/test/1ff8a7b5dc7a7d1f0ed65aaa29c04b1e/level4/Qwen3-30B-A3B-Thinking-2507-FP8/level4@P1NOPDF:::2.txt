

REPLY TO REBUTTAL:  
I largely agree with the point regarding the single-task focus. However, it would be worthwhile to test truncated backpropagation with a modest step count (e.g., 30 steps). While this approach is less likely to learn to balance parentheses (though this should be verified), it may achieve competitive performance at significantly lower computational cost.  

This paper investigates the utility of deep recurrent neural networks (RNNs), presenting several compelling findings. First, when parameter counts are held constant, deeper RNNs consistently outperform standard RNNs on character-level language modeling. Second, the authors demonstrate that deeper network layers retain more long-term contextual information. Third, they show that standard backpropagation through time (BPTT) can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses—a task previously believed to require hierarchical feedback (HF).  

However, the paper claims to "introduce" the deep RNN architecture, which is inaccurate. Deep RNNs were already employed in prior work, such as Graves (2013), and likely earlier. The authors should revise the text to clarify that this work focuses on analysis rather than architectural innovation. It is acceptable to prioritize analysis over introducing new architectures.  

The analysis is valuable, but its broader implications would be strengthened by validation across multiple tasks. Testing on speech recognition or other domains (e.g., to confirm deeper RNNs’ performance gains and their deeper layers’ enhanced long-range information retention) would mitigate concerns that the results are specific to the character-level language modeling task. Without this, the findings risk appearing as artifacts of the problem choice.  

Additionally, the HF experiments appear unnecessarily costly. Truncated BPTT—a technique established in the 1990s and successfully applied by Mikolov in his PhD thesis for high-performance language modeling—would likely suffice for the parentheses task. Confirming this would be a useful validation of the approach and further reduce computational overhead.  

In summary, the paper offers an interesting analysis of deep RNNs, with three key contributions: (1) deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs under fixed parameter budgets on character-level LM; (2) deeper layers encode more long-range structure; and (3) standard BPTT can solve the parentheses task without HF. To elevate the work, the authors should: (a) remove the claim of architectural novelty, (b) extend the analysis to at least one additional task (e.g., speech), and (c) replace HF with truncated BPTT in the parentheses experiments to demonstrate cost-effectiveness. The core insights are promising, but broader validation is essential for impact.
