This paper introduces a novel deep architecture that integrates the hierarchical structure of deep learning models with the principles of time-series modeling, drawing inspiration from HMMs and recurrent neural networks. The proposed training methodology constructs the network in a layer-wise fashion, utilizing a supervised pre-training phase with a next-character prediction objective. The experiments show that after extensive training (approximately 10 days) of large-scale models, the network achieves improved performance over existing methods on the Wikipedia dataset by Hinton et al. Subsequently, the authors provide a detailed analysis of the model's internal mechanics, illustrating how higher layers learn long-term dependencies and how structures like matching parentheses are represented by a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state."

The manuscript is of high quality. The writing is clear, the experimental design is sound, and the accompanying figures effectively illustrate the paper's claims. My main concern, however, pertains to the experiments. The evaluation is confined to a single dataset which has not been widely benchmarked. Moreover, direct comparisons are made only against other RNN-based models, omitting other relevant baselines such as hierarchical HMMs that can solve similar tasks. While Table 1 lists other approaches, their results are on a different corpus, which makes a direct comparison impossible.

The contribution of this work seems to lie more in the insightful analysis of *how* the network functions—which is executed very well—than in the performance it achieves. This emphasis appears to diverge from the paper's stated goal of proposing a new high-performing architecture. In summary, the paper is well-written and presents a commendable analysis of the model, but the empirical results are somewhat inconclusive due to the limited experimental scope.
