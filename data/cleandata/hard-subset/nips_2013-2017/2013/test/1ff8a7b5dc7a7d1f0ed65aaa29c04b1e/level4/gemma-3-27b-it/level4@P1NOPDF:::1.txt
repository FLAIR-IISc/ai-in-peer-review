The authors propose a novel deep learning architecture that integrates the hierarchical structure of deep learning with time-series modeling techniques commonly found in Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) or recurrent neural networks. Their training algorithm constructs the network layer-by-layer, utilizing supervised (pre-)training with a next-letter prediction objective. The experiments demonstrate that, after training very large networks for approximately 10 days, performance on a Wikipedia dataset (originally published by Hinton et al.) improves over existing approaches. The authors then provide a detailed analysis of how the network tackles this task, highlighting, for instance, that long-term dependencies are modeled in higher layers and that correspondence between opening and closing parentheses is represented as a “pseudo-stable attractor-like state.”

Overall, this is a high-quality paper. It is well-written, the experiments are well-designed, and the figures effectively support the findings. My primary concern lies with the experimental evaluation. The comparison is limited to a single dataset, which doesn’t appear to be widely used in the literature. Furthermore, direct comparisons are primarily with other RNN-based models, neglecting potentially relevant hierarchical HMMs and similar approaches capable of addressing the same task. While Table 1 lists other methods, they are evaluated on a different corpus, rendering direct comparison impossible.

The paper’s contribution is more focused on *how* the network operates – an analysis conducted quite effectively – than on achieving state-of-the-art results, although the paper initially sets out to propose a new architecture. The proposed deep architecture successfully combines hierarchical feature learning with time-series modeling. The paper is well-written, the model is analyzed thoroughly, but the results are somewhat inconclusive as a result of the limited experimental scope.
