Response to rebuttal
- I agree with the concern that the paper evaluates only a single task; this limits how broadly the conclusions can be generalized.
- It would be worthwhile to try truncated backpropagation through time (e.g., 30 steps). While such truncation may be less likely to learn perfect parenthesis balancing, it could still achieve competitive entropies at a fraction of the computational cost; verifying this would be valuable.

Summary
This paper analyzes deep recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and reports three main findings on character-level language modeling: (1) when the total parameter budget is held constant, deeper RNNs outperform standard (single-layer) RNNs; (2) deeper layers retain more long-range information than shallower layers; and (3) standard BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, a task previously believed to require Hessian-free (HF) optimization.

Strengths
- Clear, focused empirical question: Does depth help when parameter count is fixed? The answer appears to be yes on the tested benchmark.
- Informative analysis of temporal structure: the evidence that deeper layers carry longer-range information is interesting and aligns with intuitions about hierarchical temporal abstraction.
- Clarifying result on optimization: showing that plain BPTT suffices for parenthesis balancing helps dispel a common assumption about the necessity of HF.
- The work is well-motivated and, taken at face value, provides actionable guidance for practitioners choosing architectures under a parameter budget.

Concerns and suggestions
- Novelty/positioning: The paper should not claim to introduce “deep RNNs.” Stacked/ multilayer RNNs have prior art (e.g., Graves, ICASSP 2013, and likely earlier). It is perfectly fine to frame the contribution as an empirical and analytical study rather than an architectural innovation, but the claims should be adjusted accordingly and prior work properly acknowledged.
- Single-task scope: All key conclusions are drawn from character-level language modeling on one dataset. This raises the possibility that the findings are task- or dataset-specific. The paper would be much stronger if at least one additional domain were included—for example, a speech recognition task—both to test whether deeper RNNs still win under a fixed parameter budget and to examine whether deeper layers again exhibit longer-range temporal structure.
- Optimization and computational cost: The HF experiments appear unnecessarily expensive given the availability and demonstrated success of truncated BPTT in RNN language modeling (e.g., Mikolov’s PhD thesis). Since the authors show that full BPTT can already learn parenthesis balancing, it would be valuable to:
  - Evaluate truncated BPTT with a reasonable window (e.g., 30 steps) to quantify the accuracy–efficiency trade-off.
  - Report whether truncated BPTT reaches competitive entropies and at what wall-clock cost.
  - Clarify whether the parenthesis-balancing result persists (perhaps partially) under truncation.
  This would provide a more practically relevant picture of training efficiency.
- Generality of the “long-term information” analysis: The method used to assess long-range information is compelling, but it would be more convincing if replicated across multiple datasets or modalities. Even within character-level modeling, testing on a second corpus would help guard against dataset-specific artifacts.
- Experimental clarity: Since the core comparison hinges on a fixed parameter budget, please ensure and document that other confounds are controlled (e.g., identical regularization, comparable initialization, and training budgets across depths). Reporting variance across multiple seeds would help assess robustness.

Minor comments
- Please explicitly acknowledge prior deep/stacked RNN work (e.g., Graves, ICASSP 2013) and potentially earlier sources, and revise the introduction to avoid claiming architectural novelty.
- Provide more detail on training settings for HF versus BPTT (compute budget, convergence criteria), so readers can judge the cost-effectiveness of each.
- If possible, include qualitative probes (e.g., unit visualizations) to illustrate the kinds of long-range patterns captured at deeper layers.

Overall assessment
This is a well-executed and useful empirical study of deep RNNs. The finding that deeper RNNs are preferable under a fixed parameter budget, coupled with evidence that deeper layers capture longer-range structure, provides clear insight for model design. The clarification that standard BPTT can learn parenthesis balancing is also valuable.

However, the paper’s impact would be significantly strengthened by (1) reframing the contribution to emphasize analysis rather than architectural novelty and by properly crediting prior stacked RNN work; (2) broadening the empirical scope beyond a single character-level language modeling task; and (3) incorporating a cost-aware comparison that includes truncated BPTT. With these adjustments, the work would offer both stronger evidence for its conclusions and more practical guidance for the community.
