This paper introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), a hybrid of deep and recurrent architectures, and demonstrates their effectiveness on text prediction and compression tasks. The central argument is that deeper layers within the DRNN learn to represent more abstract and long-term dependencies in the data, benefitting from increased processing depth compared to shallower layers handling shorter-term information. The paper is well-written, intellectually honest in acknowledging its limitations, and offers valuable insight into the functioning of deep temporal networks. However, I have several suggestions that, if addressed, would significantly strengthen the paper.

I find the core intuition—that older data potentially benefits from more layers of processing—compelling, and the use of deep RNNs to model both short and long-term dependencies a reasonable approach. A natural extension, which the authors should consider, is to explore the inclusion of additional non-linearities between each timestep. Has this been investigated?

The role of gradient normalization is a key component of the proposed method. The authors state that it avoids “bifurcations,” but the explanation lacks sufficient detail. A comparison to recent work on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs, such as the ICML 2013 paper from researchers at the University of Montreal, would be beneficial. Furthermore, the authors should address the potential impact of gradient normalization on the averaging behavior of Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD). Could this normalization interfere with the benefits of SGD?

When comparing to prior work on Wikipedia compression, the authors should consider including a comparison to Mikolov et al.’s work in this area, providing a more comprehensive benchmark.

Of the various ablation studies designed to assess the function of each layer, the text generation results (Table 2) are the most persuasive. However, the authors themselves rightly caution (lines 240-241) that interpreting the effects of these “brain surgery” interventions on RNNs is problematic due to complex interdependencies between layer outputs. This inherent difficulty must be more thoroughly discussed.

My primary concern relates to establishing a causal link between depth and abstraction. While the paper demonstrates that higher layers *appear* to process more abstract and long-term features, it doesn’t definitively prove that they do so *because* of their greater depth. A crucial experiment would involve testing whether relocating units from higher layers to the first layer diminishes their ability to capture these longer-term dependencies. This would directly address whether they benefit from the preceding layers in a non-trivial way. Demonstrating this benefit is essential to support the paper’s central claim.

The experimental setup raises a potential issue of fairness. The regular RNN used for comparison appears to have fewer parameters (2767^2 * 2 > 2119^2 * 2). This parameter difference could unfairly advantage the DRNNs. A stronger comparison would involve evaluating DRNNs against a standard RNN with the *same* number of parameters or, ideally, the same number of units. For example, a two-layer DRNN could be compared to a standard RNN with an equivalent number of units. Such a comparison would provide more compelling evidence for the benefits of depth.

Regarding Figure 2, it would be more informative to see the performance of DRNNs with varying depths when trained from scratch. Perhaps wider layers could be used in the deeper networks to compensate for differences in parameter count, allowing for a more direct comparison of architectural effectiveness. 

Minor points:

*   The acronyms DRNN-AO and IO are used in Figure 1 but not explicitly defined in the text. This should be rectified for clarity.



Overall, this is a promising paper that presents a novel architecture and insightful experiments. Addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly demonstrating a causal link between depth and abstraction and ensuring a fair comparison to standard RNNs, would significantly strengthen the contribution and impact of this work.
