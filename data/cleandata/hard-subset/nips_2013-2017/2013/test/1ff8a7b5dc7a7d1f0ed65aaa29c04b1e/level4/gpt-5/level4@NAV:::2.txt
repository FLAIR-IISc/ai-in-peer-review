Review- REPLY TO REBUTTAL:
- I largely agree with the comment about evaluating on a single task.
- It would still be worthwhile to try truncated backpropagation with, for example, 30 steps. It may be less likely to learn to balance parentheses (though that should be verified), but it could still achieve competitive entropies at a fraction of the cost.

This paper examines the utility of deep recurrent neural networks.

It draws several interesting conclusions: when the parameter count is held fixed, deeper recurrent networks outperform standard RNNs. Other notable observations include evidence that deeper models retain more “long-term information,” and that plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, whereas this was previously thought to require HF.

However, the paper claims to introduce deep RNNs, which have appeared before—for example, in http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf, and likely earlier—so it should not claim to have introduced this architecture. It is fine not to propose a new architecture and instead focus on analysis.

The analysis, while meaningful, would be more compelling if demonstrated on multiple problems, perhaps including a speech task. Otherwise, the findings may be specific to the chosen problem.

Finally, the HF experiments were likely more costly than necessary, since the truncated backpropagation approach (introduced in the 1990s) was successfully used by Mikolov (see his PhD thesis) to train RNNs that are excellent language models. It is therefore plausible that truncated BPTT would work well here too, and it would be good to confirm this.

This work provides an interesting analysis of deep RNNs. The two main results are: 1) with a fixed parameter budget on character-level language modeling, deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs; 2) deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure; and 3) truncated BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses. The results are interesting, but since this is purely an analysis, the paper would be much stronger with a comparable study on a speech task (e.g., showing that deeper RNNs perform better and that their deeper layers capture more long-range information).
