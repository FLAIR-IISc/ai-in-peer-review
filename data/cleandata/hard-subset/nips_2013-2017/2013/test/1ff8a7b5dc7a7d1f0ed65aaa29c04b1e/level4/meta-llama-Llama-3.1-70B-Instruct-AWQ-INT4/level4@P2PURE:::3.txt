I appreciate the authors' observation about the arbitrariness of older data benefiting from more layers of processing compared to newer data, and their proposal to address this issue using a deep RNN is reasonable. Furthermore, another potential approach to explore would be to incorporate additional layers of nonlinearities between each timestep.

I am curious about the importance of gradient normalization in this work and how it helps "avoid the problem of bifurcations." A recent ICML 2013 paper by researchers from the University of Montreal on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs may be relevant to this discussion. Additionally, I wonder whether normalizing the gradient in this manner could potentially disrupt the "averaging" behavior of stochastic gradient descent (SGD).

In terms of previous work on Wikipedia compression tasks, the authors may want to consider comparing their results to those of Mikolov et al.

Among the various experiments designed to examine the roles of each layer in terms of time-scale and abstraction, I find the text generation experiment (as shown in Table 2) to be the most persuasive. However, as the authors themselves note in the paragraph on line 241, interpreting the effects of this kind of "brain surgery" on RNNs can be problematic due to the complex interdependencies that may have developed between the outputs of the various layers.

A significant missing piece of the empirical puzzle in this paper is whether the higher layers are actually better at processing more abstract and long-term properties of the text than if the units were moved to the first layer. In other words, are they benefiting from the extra levels of processing that precede them in a nontrivial way? While it is encouraging that they take on these seemingly more abstract and longer-term roles after training, this is incomplete evidence that this is the case.

I notice that the regular RNN seems to have fewer parameters in these experiments, since 2767^2 * 5 > 2119^2 (counting both recurrent and inter-layer weight matrices). This comparison may be unfair, and a more convincing demonstration would be if the deeper RNNs outperformed a standard RNN with the same number of parameters, or even better, the same number of units. For example, comparing a 2-layer DRNN to a standard RNN with the same number of units would strengthen the paper's claims, as the difference in the number of parameters would not unfairly favor the RNN.

Instead of Figure 2, it would be more informative to see how well various depths of DRNN performed on the benchmarks when trained from scratch, possibly with wider layers than their deeper counterparts to compensate for the difference in the number of parameters and/or units.

Minor points:

- The authors should define DRNN-AO and IO in the text, not just in Figure 1.

This paper explores a hybrid of deep and recurrent neural networks called DRNNs, which are like deep networks but with recurrent connections at each layer that operate through time. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this architecture for text prediction/compression compared to existing approaches. A significant portion of the paper is devoted to a series of experiments designed to argue that the higher-level layers process and represent more abstract and long-term structures in the data. While these experiments are convincing, I have some reservations, as elaborated below, and would like to see additional experiments.

I believe it is worthwhile to investigate these kinds of deep temporal networks and gain insight into their functioning after training. The paper is well-written, easy to read, and commendably intellectually honest and thorough about its potential problems and shortcomings, which I appreciate.
