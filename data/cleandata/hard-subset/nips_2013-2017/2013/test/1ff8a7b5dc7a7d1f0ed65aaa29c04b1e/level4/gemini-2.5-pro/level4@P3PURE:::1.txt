The authors propose a novel deep architecture that combines the hierarchical structure of deep learning with the time-series modeling capabilities of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) or Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). The proposed training algorithm constructs the network layer-by-layer, employing a supervised pre-training strategy based on a next-letter prediction objective. Experiments conducted on a Wikipedia dataset from Hinton et al. show that the proposed model, after extensive training of approximately 10 days, achieves improved performance over prior work on this dataset. The paper further provides a detailed analysis of the network's internal mechanisms, illustrating, for example, that higher layers capture long-term dependencies while the correspondence between opening and closing parentheses is represented by a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state."

Overall, the paper is of high quality: it is well-written, the experimental setup is sound, and the figures effectively support the authors' findings. However, my primary concern lies with the experimental evaluation. The performance comparison is limited to a single dataset which appears to have been studied by relatively few other researchers. Consequently, the direct comparison is restricted to other RNN-based models, omitting potentially relevant baselines such as hierarchical HMMs that are also capable of solving similar tasks. While the authors do list other approaches in Table 1, these results are on a different corpus, rendering them incomparable and limiting the context of the current work's performance.

The main contribution of this work seems to lie less in the demonstrated performance of the proposed architecture and more in the insightful analysis of *how* the network functions. While this analysis is a strength of the paper, it appears misaligned with the primary stated goal of proposing a new, high-performing deep architecture. In summary, while the paper introduces an interesting model and presents a thorough analysis, the limited experimental validation makes the performance claims somewhat inconclusive.
