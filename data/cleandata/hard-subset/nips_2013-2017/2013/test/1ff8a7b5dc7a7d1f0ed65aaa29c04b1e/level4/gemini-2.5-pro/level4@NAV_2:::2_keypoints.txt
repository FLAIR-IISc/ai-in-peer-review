- Paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (stacked RNNs) and argues they induce a temporal hierarchy, evaluated on large Wikipedia character-level language modeling with in-depth internal-dynamics analysis.
- Empirical result: for a fixed parameter budget, a deep RNN outperforms a shallow RNN and achieves state-of-the-art results using simple SGD.
- Analysis result: higher layers encode longer time scales and different layers contribute distinctly to predictions, offering insight into why DRNNs work.
- Important finding: standard backpropagation-through-time lets DRNNs learn long-range dependencies (e.g., balancing parentheses over hundreds of characters), challenging the necessity of Hessian-Free optimization.
- Clarity issue: stacked RNNs are not novel—authors should reframe the contribution to emphasize analysis and demonstration of emergent temporal hierarchy rather than presenting the architecture as new.
- Generality concern: experiments are limited to one task (character-level language modeling); validation on other domains (e.g., speech recognition) is needed to confirm broader applicability.
- Missing baseline: paper omits comparison to truncated BPTT, a common efficient alternative; include it to determine if full BPTT is essential and whether truncated BPTT can handle long-range tasks like parenthesis balancing.
