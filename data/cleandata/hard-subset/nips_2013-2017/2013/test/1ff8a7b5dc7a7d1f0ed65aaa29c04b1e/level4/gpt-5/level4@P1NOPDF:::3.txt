I like the point about the arbitrariness whereby older data can benefit from many more layers of processing than newer data. Building a deep RNN seems like a reasonable way to improve shorter-term modeling while also providing other benefits. Another obvious extension would be to add extra nonlinear layers within the transition between time steps (i.e., make the per-step transition deeper). Have you tried this?

How important was gradient normalization in practice, and why does it “avoid the problem of bifurcations”? There is a relevant ICML 2013 paper from the University of Montreal on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs that may bear on this. Also, could normalizing gradients in this way interfere with the “averaging” behavior of SGD?

With respect to prior results on Wikipedia compression/prediction, you should also compare against work by Mikolov et al.

Among your experiments probing the roles of different layers (time scale and potential “abstraction”), the text-generation analysis in Table 2 is the most persuasive. However, as you note (around line 241), interpreting “brain-surgery”-style interventions in RNNs is difficult because of the complex interdependencies that can arise between layers’ outputs.

For me, the biggest missing empirical piece is whether higher layers are truly better at capturing more abstract, longer-term properties than if the same units were placed in the first layer. In other words, do they benefit in a nontrivial way from the additional levels of processing that precede them? The fact that higher layers appear to take on seemingly more abstract and longer-term roles after training is encouraging but, on its own, incomplete evidence.

I also worry about fairness in the comparisons. The plain RNN appears to have fewer parameters in your setup (e.g., counting both recurrent and inter-layer matrices gives something like 2*767^2*5 > 2*119^2), which could bias results. A stronger claim would be to show that deeper RNNs outperform a standard single-layer RNN matched for parameter count—or, even better, matched for the number of units. For example, compare a 2-layer DRNN to a single-layer RNN with the same number of units, ensuring that differences in parameter count do not bias the comparison.

Similarly, instead of Figure 2, it would be more informative to report performance across different DRNN depths trained from scratch, possibly using wider shallow layers to roughly equalize the number of parameters and/or units. This would clarify the contribution of depth per se.

Overall, this paper studies a hybrid of deep and recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), which resemble deep architectures but include recurrent connections at each layer operating through time. The authors demonstrate that this architecture performs very well on text prediction/compression compared to existing approaches. A substantial portion of the paper presents experiments arguing that higher layers represent more abstract, longer-term structure. These are quite convincing, though I have the reservations noted above and would welcome a couple more targeted experiments to resolve them. I think it is valuable to study deep temporal networks and to understand how they function after training. The paper is clearly written and, importantly, intellectually honest and thorough about potential limitations—something I especially appreciate.
