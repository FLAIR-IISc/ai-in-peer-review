

The paper investigates deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), where each layer is a recurrent network processing the hidden state of the previous layer. The authors argue this architecture naturally captures temporal hierarchies in time series, demonstrating state-of-the-art character-level language modeling performance (1.472 BPC on Wikipedia) with simple SGD, and analyzing emergent time scales via perturbation experiments. Key prior work includes Graves et al. (ICASSP 2013) on stacked bidirectional LSTMs for speech, and El Hihi & Bengio (NIPS 1996) on hierarchical RNNs. The paper correctly notes that DRNNs have been used before but claims novelty in *analysis* rather than architecture.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Quality & Clarity:** The analysis of time-scale emergence via typo perturbations (Figure 3) is technically sound and compelling. The demonstration that deeper layers exhibit longer memory (e.g., top layers retaining parentheses context for 200+ characters) provides strong empirical evidence for the core hypothesis. Experiments are well-designed, with clear ablation studies (e.g., disabling layer outputs in Figure 2, parentheses closing tests in Figure 4). The writing is precise, and the training methodology (SGD with gradient normalization) is thoroughly described.  
2. **Significance:** The results resolve a key question: plain SGD *can* train RNNs to model long-term dependencies (parentheses) without Hessian-free methods, contradicting prior assumptions. This has practical implications for training efficiency. The BPC results (1.472 vs. prior 1.49 using HF) are competitive and demonstrate DRNNs’ effectiveness for character-level tasks.  
3. **Originality (in analysis):** While the DRNN architecture itself is not new (Graves 2013), the *systematic analysis* of emergent time scales across layers—validated via multiple experiments (perturbation, text generation, parentheses)—is novel and valuable for understanding hierarchical RNNs.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Originality (in claim):** The paper incorrectly states it "introduces" DRNNs (Section 1), but Graves et al. (ICASSP 2013) used identical stacked bidirectional LSTM architectures for speech recognition. The authors should reframe this as "revisiting and analyzing" DRNNs, not introducing them. This undermines the novelty claim.  
2. **Significance (scope):** The analysis is confined to character-level language modeling. To demonstrate broader significance, the authors should extend experiments to a *different domain* (e.g., speech or video) where temporal hierarchies are well-established. For instance, testing DRNNs on TIMIT phoneme recognition would validate whether deeper layers capture syllable-level structure (as hypothesized), rather than being specific to text. Without this, the findings risk being task-specific artifacts.  
3. **Methodology gap:** The paper omits comparison to *truncated BPTT* (a standard technique for RNNs since the 1990s, e.g., Mikolov 2012 PhD thesis), which achieved state-of-the-art language modeling with minimal computational cost. The authors claim SGD suffices for DRNNs, but without showing truncated BPTT’s performance (which Mikolov used successfully), the efficiency claim lacks context. The rebuttal note rightly suggests testing this—it would strengthen the paper significantly.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pro:*  
- Strong empirical validation of hierarchical time scales via multiple experiments.  
- Demonstrates SGD alone can achieve HF-level results for long-term dependencies (parentheses task).  
- Well-executed analysis of layer contributions (Figures 2–4).  
- Clear, reproducible methodology.  

*Con:*  
- Overstated novelty (architecture not new; must correct framing).  
- Limited to one domain (text); no evidence of generalizability.  
- Omission of truncated BPTT comparison weakens the efficiency claim.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Weak Accept.** The paper makes a valuable contribution to *understanding* DRNNs through rigorous analysis, but the originality claim is inaccurate, and the narrow scope limits its significance. With minor revisions:  
1. Remove "introduces" and reframe as analysis of existing architecture.  
2. Add a brief comparison to truncated BPTT (e.g., "We tested truncated BPTT with 30 steps; BPC rose to 1.52, confirming DRNNs’ advantage requires full BPTT").  
3. Include *one* additional experiment (e.g., on TIMIT) to demonstrate cross-domain validity.  

Without these, the work remains a solid but incremental contribution to language modeling, not a transformative advance for NIPS. The core insights about time-scale emergence are compelling, but the paper’s current framing risks misleading readers about its novelty. Addressing these points would elevate it to a strong contribution.
