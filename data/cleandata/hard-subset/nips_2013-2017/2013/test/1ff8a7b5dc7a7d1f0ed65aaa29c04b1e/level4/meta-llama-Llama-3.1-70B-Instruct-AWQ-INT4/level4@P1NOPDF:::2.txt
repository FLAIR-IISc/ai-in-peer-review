REPLY TO REBUTTAL: 

I mostly agree with the comment regarding the single task. However, I still think it would be worthwhile to try truncated backpropagation with a larger number of steps, such as 30. Although it is less likely to learn to balance parentheses (which should be verified), it may still achieve competitive entropies at a significantly lower computational cost.

The paper explores the effectiveness of deep recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and reaches several interesting conclusions. Notably, when the number of parameters is held constant, deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs. Another interesting finding is the analysis demonstrating that deeper networks retain more "long-term information." Furthermore, the paper shows that plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, a task previously thought to be achievable only with Hessian-free (HF) optimization.

However, the paper claims to introduce the deep RNN architecture, which is not accurate. Deep RNNs have been used in prior work, such as in http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf, and likely in earlier studies as well. The paper should not claim to have introduced this architecture. Instead, it should focus on the analysis and acknowledge that it builds upon existing work.

While the analysis is meaningful, it would be more compelling if it were demonstrated on multiple problems, including those in speech processing. Without such validation, the findings may be specific to the character-level language modeling task and not generalizable. 

Finally, the HF experiments may have been unnecessarily expensive, as truncated backpropagation, introduced in the 1990s, was successfully used by Mikolov (see his PhD thesis) to train RNNs as excellent language models. It is likely that truncated BPTT would yield similar results in this case, and confirming this would be a valuable addition to the paper.

This work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs, with two primary results: (1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs on character-level language modeling when the number of parameters is fixed, and (2) deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure. Additionally, the paper shows that plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses. Although these results are interesting, the paper would be significantly stronger if it included a similar analysis on a speech task, such as demonstrating that deeper RNNs perform better and their deeper layers retain more long-range information.
