Post-rebuttal note: I appreciate the authors’ response and agree with the concern about evaluating only one task. I still recommend adding a simple truncated BPTT baseline (e.g., 30 steps): although it may be less likely to learn long-range structures such as balanced parentheses, it could achieve competitive entropy at a fraction of the cost, and would help clarify the necessity of long unrolls for this dataset.

Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies “deep recurrent neural networks” (DRNNs) formed by stacking recurrent layers, with two output variants: output from the top layer only (DRNN-1O) or from all layers (DRNN-AO). The authors train these models with SGD and gradient norm normalization, and for DRNN-1O use incremental layer-wise training to ensure each layer directly connects to the output during part of training. On Wikipedia character-level language modeling (∼4.9M parameters), DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance among recurrent models trained with SGD and match prior Hessian-free (HF) results for similar parameter budgets. The paper analyzes emergent time scales across layers using perturbation and context-switch experiments, ablations of layer outputs, and qualitative generation (including long-range phenomena such as closing parentheses).

Stacked recurrent architectures are not novel per se: hierarchical/stacked RNNs date back at least to El Hihi & Bengio (NIPS 1996) and Fernández/Graves/Schmidhuber (IJCAI 2007), and stacked (bi-directional) LSTMs have been applied to speech (e.g., Graves et al., ICASSP 2013). The contribution here is a careful empirical study of stacked tanh-RNNs on character-level modeling, a comparison of output-connection schemes, and an analysis of emergent time scales. The results are also positioned against multiplicative RNNs trained with HF (Sutskever et al., 2011).

Strengths:
- Clear empirical finding that, at fixed parameter count, deeper RNNs outperform a single-layer RNN on this task; both DRNN variants are competitive with HF-trained models of similar size.
- Well-executed diagnostics: per-layer ablations, typo and context-switch perturbations, and qualitative generation demonstrate plausible temporal hierarchies and long-range effects (e.g., tracking parentheses over hundreds of characters).
- Demonstration that plain SGD with BPTT (and gradient normalization) suffices to learn long-range dependencies such as closing parentheses—previously shown mainly with HF—adds practical value.
- The training protocol for DRNN-1O (incremental layer-wise with full-network updates) is simple and reproducible.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Novelty: The architecture should not be presented as new; stacked recurrent networks (including stacked LSTMs) are established. Please reframe the contribution as an analysis and empirical study of DRNNs.
- Scope: All results are on one dataset and one task (character-level modeling). To support broader claims about temporal hierarchies, it would be valuable to include at least one additional domain (e.g., TIMIT phoneme recognition or a speech/sequence labeling task), showing both performance and time-scale analysis.
- Baselines: Modern recurrent baselines are missing. Including a stacked LSTM (or GRU), and a multiplicative RNN trained with SGD, would clarify whether the observed benefits are due to depth per se or gating/multiplicative dynamics. A comparison to Mikolov-style truncated BPTT (commonly used in language modeling) would contextualize training cost and the necessity of long unrolls.
- Training confound: The markedly long time scales in intermediate layers of DRNN-1O may be driven by the incremental training schedule (layers trained while directly connected to outputs). Please disentangle architecture from training procedure by: (a) training DRNN-1O without incremental output reattachment, and/or (b) training DRNN-AO with the same incremental schedule, and repeating the time-scale analysis.
- Evaluation details: The character set differs slightly from [19]. Although the authors argue this does not affect cross-entropy meaningfully, reporting BPC on the exact 86-character set and clarifying total effective “epochs” would make comparisons more direct. Reporting variance across runs would strengthen claims.
- Efficiency: Training takes ∼10 days on a single GPU. A TBPTT baseline (e.g., 30–50 steps) would quantify any accuracy–efficiency trade-off and test whether long unrolls are required for the observed gains.

Quality:
- Technically sound overall, with reasonable training heuristics (gradient normalization, linear LR decay). The time-scale analyses are insightful. Conclusions about architectural benefits would be stronger with additional baselines and control experiments to isolate the effect of the incremental training scheme.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized. Please tone down claims of architectural novelty; expand the related work section to more fully cover stacked RNN/LSTM and hierarchical RNN literature; and explicitly describe unroll lengths and TBPTT vs. full BPTT.

Originality:
- Moderate. The main contribution is the empirical and analytical study of depth in RNNs and output-connection strategies, rather than a new architecture.

Significance:
- Moderate. The results reinforce that depth helps in RNNs and show that SGD-trained DRNNs can match HF-trained models on this task. The time-scale analysis is likely to be useful to practitioners. Broader impact would increase with additional tasks and baselines.

Pros:
- Strong empirical performance for stacked RNNs with simple training.
- Careful and informative analysis of emergent time scales.
- Demonstrates learning of long-range dependencies (parentheses) with SGD/BPTT.

Cons:
- Architectural novelty is limited; related work coverage should reflect this.
- Single-task evaluation; missing key baselines (stacked LSTM/GRU, TBPTT).
- Training schedule may confound conclusions about time scales.

Recommendation:
Borderline accept. If the authors (i) clearly reposition the paper as an analysis, (ii) add at least a lightweight TBPTT baseline, and (iii) strengthen related work and, ideally, include one additional task or a stacked LSTM baseline, this would be a solid accept. Even without all additions, the analysis and empirical findings are valuable to the community.
