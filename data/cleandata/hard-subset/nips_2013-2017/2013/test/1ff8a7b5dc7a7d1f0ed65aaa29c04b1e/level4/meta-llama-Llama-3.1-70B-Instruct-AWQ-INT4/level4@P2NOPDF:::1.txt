The authors propose a novel deep architecture that integrates the hierarchical representation of deep learning with time-series modeling, akin to Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) or recurrent neural networks. The training algorithm constructs the network layer-by-layer using supervised pre-training with a next-letter prediction objective. The experiments demonstrate that, after training large networks for approximately 10 days, the network performance on the Wikipedia dataset published by Hinton et al. surpasses previous work. The authors then delve into the analysis and discussion of how the network approaches its task, revealing insightful findings, such as the modeling of long-term dependencies in higher layers and the representation of correspondence between opening and closing parentheses as a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state".

The paper is well-written, with well-designed experiments and figures that effectively support the findings. However, my primary concern lies in the experimental evaluation. The comparison is limited to a single dataset, which appears to have received relatively little attention in the literature. Furthermore, the comparison is restricted to other RNN-related models, neglecting more common hierarchical HMMs that can tackle similar tasks. Although Table 1 lists other approaches, they are evaluated on a different corpus, rendering them incomparable.

While the paper excels in its analysis of how the network operates, its primary contribution is not in the results achieved but rather in the in-depth examination of the network's behavior. The paper's main objective is to propose a new deep architecture that combines hierarchical features of deep learning with time-series modeling. In this regard, the paper is well-written, and the model is thoroughly analyzed, but the results are somewhat inconclusive.
