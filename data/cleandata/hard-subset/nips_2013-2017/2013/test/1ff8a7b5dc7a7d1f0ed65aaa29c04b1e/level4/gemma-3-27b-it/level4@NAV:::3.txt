I appreciate the observation regarding the differing processing applied to older versus newer data, and the use of a deep RNN to address short-term dependencies seems well-motivated, potentially offering additional benefits. Considering extra nonlinearities between timesteps could also be a productive avenue for exploration – have you investigated this?

The importance of gradient normalization and its effect on bifurcation avoidance warrants further clarification. Relevant work on gradient truncation and RNN optimization by researchers at the University of Montreal (ICML 2013) may offer useful context. Furthermore, could this normalization method potentially disrupt the averaging effect of SGD?

When comparing to prior work on Wikipedia compression, it would be valuable to discuss the approach by Mikolov et al. and its relationship to the results presented here.

Of the experiments designed to assess layer-specific roles in terms of timescale and abstraction, the text generation results (Table 2) are most compelling. However, I agree with the authors’ caution (line 241) regarding interpreting the impact of layer manipulation due to complex interdependencies within the RNN.

A key remaining empirical question is whether higher layers demonstrably process more abstract, long-term text properties compared to units placed in the initial layer. Simply observing such roles emerge post-training isn’t conclusive evidence of nontrivial benefit from preceding processing levels.

I noted a potential imbalance in the parameter count between the regular RNN and the deeper RNNs (2767^2 > 2119^2, considering both recurrent and interlayer weights). A more equitable comparison would involve deeper RNNs outperforming a standard RNN with a comparable number of parameters or units – for instance, a two-layer DRNN versus a standard RNN with an equivalent number of units. This would substantially strengthen the paper’s conclusions. 

Instead of Figure 2, presenting results from DRNNs of varying depths trained from scratch – potentially employing wider layers to account for parameter/unit differences – would be more informative.

This paper investigates DRNNs, a hybrid deep and recurrent architecture, demonstrating strong performance in text prediction/compression. The core argument centers on the claim that higher layers capture more abstract and long-term structures. While the experiments are generally persuasive, I have some reservations and would like to see additional analysis. Investigating these deep temporal networks and understanding their function is a worthwhile endeavor. The paper is well-written, intellectually honest, and thoroughly addresses potential limitations—a quality I particularly value.
