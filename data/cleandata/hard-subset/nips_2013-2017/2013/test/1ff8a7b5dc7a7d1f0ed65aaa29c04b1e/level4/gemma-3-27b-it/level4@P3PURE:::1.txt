The authors propose a novel deep learning architecture that integrates the hierarchical representations of deep learning with time-series modeling techniques commonly found in Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) and recurrent neural networks. Their training algorithm constructs the network layer-by-layer via supervised (pre-)training using a next-letter prediction objective. Experiments demonstrate that, after approximately 10 days of training on very large networks, performance on a Wikipedia dataset (previously published by Hinton et al.) improves upon existing results. The authors further analyze and discuss details of the network’s operation, highlighting, for example, that long-term dependencies are modeled in higher layers and that the correspondence between opening and closing parentheses is represented as a “pseudo-stable attractor-like state.”

This is a well-written and high-quality paper with well-designed experiments and figures that effectively support the findings. However, my primary concern lies with the scope of the experimental evaluation. The comparison is limited to a single dataset, which appears to have received relatively little attention from the broader research community. Furthermore, direct comparisons are primarily with other RNN-based models, lacking a comparison with potentially relevant hierarchical HMMs and similar approaches capable of tackling comparable tasks. While Table 1 lists other approaches, the use of a different corpus renders them incomparable. 

The paper’s contribution resides more in its insightful analysis of *how* the network functions (an analysis conducted with commendable thoroughness) than in the magnitude of its achieved performance, though the paper explicitly aims to present a novel architecture and demonstrate its effectiveness. In summary, while a new deep architecture combining hierarchical feature learning with time-series modeling is proposed and thoroughly analyzed, the results remain somewhat inconclusive due to the limited experimental scope.
