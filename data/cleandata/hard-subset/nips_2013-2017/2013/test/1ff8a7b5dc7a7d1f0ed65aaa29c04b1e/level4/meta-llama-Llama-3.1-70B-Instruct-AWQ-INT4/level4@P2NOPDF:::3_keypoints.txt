- Authors’ core idea: older data may need more processing layers; proposing/deploying a deep RNN (DRNN) to address this is insightful.

- Suggestion: explore adding extra nonlinearities between time steps as an alternative architectural approach and report whether this was tried.

- Method detail needed: provide a clearer explanation of gradient normalization’s role and specifically how it helps “avoid bifurcations.”

- Relevant literature: consider citing/relating to the ICML 2013 U. of Montreal work on gradient truncation and RNN optimization.

- Concern: gradient normalization might interfere with SGD’s implicit averaging—investigate and discuss this risk.

- Related work gap: compare results to Mikolov et al. on Wikipedia compression tasks.

- Experimental strength: the text generation experiment (Table 2) is the most compelling empirical result.

- Interpretation caveat: “brain surgery” analyses are hard to interpret because of complex interdependencies between layer outputs.

- Missing evidence: need experiments showing higher layers truly process more abstract/long-term properties than if those units were in the first layer (i.e., non-trivial benefit from preceding processing).

- Fair comparison issue: regular RNN had fewer parameters—compare DRNN to standard RNNs with matched parameter counts or units (e.g., 2-layer DRNN vs single-layer RNN with same units).

- Suggested alternative figure/analysis: replace Figure 2 with plots of performance versus DRNN depth trained from scratch, using wider layers to equalize parameter counts.

- Minor/formatting: define DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO in the text, not only in Figure 1.

- Overall appraisal: experiments on layer roles are well-conceived but need additional validation; paper is well-written, honest about limitations, and worth pursuing.
