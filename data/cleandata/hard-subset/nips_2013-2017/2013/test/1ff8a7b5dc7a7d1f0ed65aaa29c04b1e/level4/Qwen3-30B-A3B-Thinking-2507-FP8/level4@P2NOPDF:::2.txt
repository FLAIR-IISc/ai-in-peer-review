

REPLY TO REBUTTAL:  
I largely concur with the author's rebuttal regarding the single-task focus. However, it would be worthwhile to test truncated backpropagation (with, e.g., 30 steps) to assess computational efficiency. While this approach may not replicate the parentheses-balancing capability (which relies on full backpropagation), it could achieve competitive entropy scores at significantly lower cost, warranting verification.  

This paper investigates the utility of deep recurrent neural networks (RNNs), presenting three key findings: (1) deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs when parameter counts are fixed, on character-level language modeling; (2) deeper layers encode more long-range structural information; and (3) standard backpropagation through time (BPTT) enables character-level RNNs to balance parentheses—a capability previously attributed only to Hessian-Free (HF) optimization.  

The analysis is compelling, but the paper’s claim to "introduce" the deep RNN architecture is inaccurate. This architecture was explicitly used in Graves et al. (ICASSP 2013), and likely predates it. While the paper need not introduce novel architectures, it should avoid implying novelty in the model design. The core contribution—empirical analysis of depth effects—remains valuable, but the phrasing should reflect prior work.  

The analysis would be significantly strengthened by extending it to a diverse task, such as speech recognition. Current results are limited to character-level language modeling, raising the risk that findings reflect task-specific artifacts rather than general properties of deep RNNs. A speech task would better demonstrate whether deeper networks consistently improve performance and exhibit enhanced long-range information retention.  

Furthermore, the HF experiments appear computationally excessive. Truncated BPTT—a well-established technique since the 1990s—was successfully employed by Mikolov (PhD thesis, 2010) to train highly effective language models. Given this precedent, truncated BPTT should be tested as a more efficient alternative to HF for the parentheses task. Confirming its efficacy would not only validate the authors’ claims but also provide a practical benchmark for future work.  

In summary, the paper offers an interesting analysis of deep RNNs, but its impact would be substantially enhanced by:  
1) Correcting the architecture novelty claim,  
2) Expanding the analysis to at least one additional task (e.g., speech), and  
3) Comparing HF with truncated BPTT for efficiency.  

Without these adjustments, the work risks being perceived as a narrow case study rather than a generalizable contribution. The core insights about depth and long-range information are promising, but their broader relevance hinges on empirical validation beyond character-level text.
