- Notes that standard RNNs give earlier tokens effectively more processing, creating an arbitrary depth bias over time; deep RNNs could strengthen short-term modeling and offer other benefits.  
- Suggests trying additional nonlinear layers within each time step (between recurrent updates) and asks for comparison results.  
- Requests clarification on the role and impact of gradient normalization: its criticality, how it “avoids bifurcations,” relation to ICML 2013 work on gradient truncation/optimization, and whether it biases SGD gradient estimates.  
- Recommends comparing results on related benchmarks (e.g., Wikipedia compression) with Mikolov et al.’s work.  
- Finds text generation results (Table 2) the most persuasive but warns that “brain surgery” interventions may be confounded by complex interdependencies, complicating causal interpretation.  
- Identifies a major missing experiment: direct evidence that higher layers capture more abstract/longer-term properties beyond what the same number of units in the first layer could achieve.  
- Raises concern about parameter-count fairness: standard RNNs may use fewer parameters than deep models, potentially biasing comparisons, and asks for comparisons with equal parameter or equal-unit baselines.  
- Suggests more informative ablations: report performance of different DRNN depths trained from scratch, and compare with wider shallow models matched for parameters/units to isolate depth effects.  
- Minor request: define DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO in the main text rather than only in Figure 1.  
- Overall assessment: paper is clearly written, presents strong text prediction/compression results and thoughtful experiments on layer roles, but would be strengthened by the requested controls and clarifications.
