The authors propose a novel deep architecture that integrates the hierarchical structure of deep learning with time-series modeling techniques, reminiscent of Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) or recurrent neural networks. The training algorithm constructs the network layer-by-layer, utilizing a supervised pre-training approach with a next-letter prediction objective. The experiments demonstrate that, after training large networks for approximately 10 days, the network's performance on the Wikipedia dataset published by Hinton et al. surpasses that of previous work. The authors then delve into a detailed analysis of the network's behavior, revealing insights into how it tackles its task. Notably, they show that long-term dependencies are modeled in higher layers, and the correspondence between opening and closing parentheses is captured by a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state."

The paper is of good quality, featuring clear writing, well-designed experiments, and figures that effectively support the findings. However, my primary concern lies in the experimental evaluation. The comparison is limited to a single dataset, which appears to have received relatively little attention in the literature. Furthermore, the direct comparison is only made with other RNN-related models, neglecting more common hierarchical HMMs and similar approaches that can tackle comparable tasks (Table 1 lists other approaches, but on a different corpus, rendering them incomparable).

While the paper sets out to propose a new deep architecture, combining hierarchical features of deep learning with time-series modeling, its primary contribution lies in the analysis of how the network operates. This analysis is conducted quite thoroughly, but the results are somewhat inconclusive in terms of the network's performance. The paper's strength lies in its well-written presentation, thorough model analysis, and the novelty of the proposed architecture. Nevertheless, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened by incorporating a more diverse range of datasets and baseline comparisons.
