- Notes the arbitrariness that older inputs receive more processing and suggests deep RNNs (or inserting extra nonlinear layers between timesteps) as a way to improve short-term modeling; asks if this was tried.

- Questions the role and importance of gradient normalization, asks why it “avoids the problem of bifurcations,” and whether it could interfere with SGD’s averaging; points to a 2013 ICML paper on gradient truncation/optimization as relevant.

- Recommends comparing against prior work on Wikipedia compression, specifically Mikolov et al.

- Finds the text generation results (Table 2) the most persuasive evidence for layer roles, but warns that interpreting layer “surgery” is problematic due to complex interdependencies.

- Identifies the key missing experiment: whether higher layers truly gain nontrivial benefits in abstraction/long-term processing from preceding layers versus simply being placed in the first layer.

- Raises a fairness concern: the standard RNN appears to have fewer parameters than the DRNN in the experiments, so comparisons may be biased in favor of the DRNN.

- Suggests a stronger comparison: show deeper RNNs outperform a standard RNN with the same number of parameters or, preferably, the same number of units (e.g., compare 2-layer DRNN to standard RNN with matched units).

- Recommends replacing (or supplementing) Figure 2 with results showing DRNNs of various depths trained from scratch, with shallower models widened to compensate for parameter/unit differences.

- Minor request: define DRNN-AO and IO in the text rather than only in Figure 1.

- Overall appraisal: the DRNN hybrid is promising for text prediction/compression; experiments are mostly convincing and the paper is clear and honest about limitations, but a few additional experiments are needed.
