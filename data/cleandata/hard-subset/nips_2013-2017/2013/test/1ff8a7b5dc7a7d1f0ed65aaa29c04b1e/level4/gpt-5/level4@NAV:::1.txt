The authors introduce a new deep architecture that merges the hierarchical representations of deep learning with time-series modeling familiar from HMMs or recurrent neural networks. The training procedure constructs the network incrementally, layer by layer, using supervised (pre-)training on a next-letter prediction objective. The experiments show that, after training very large models for roughly 10 days, performance on the Wikipedia dataset published by Hinton et al. surpasses previous results. The authors then analyze and discuss how the network tackles the task. For instance, long-range dependencies are captured in higher layers, and the correspondence between opening and closing parentheses appears as a “pseudo-stable attractor-like state.”

The paper is of solid quality. It is clearly written, the experimental setup is sound, and the figures effectively support the claims. My principal concern is the experimental evaluation. The comparison is limited to a single dataset that has seen relatively little prior work. Direct comparisons are restricted to RNN-related models rather than including more standard hierarchical HMMs that can address similar problems (Table 1 mentions other approaches, but on a different corpus, making them not directly comparable).

The paper’s contribution lies more in elucidating how the network operates (which is examined thoroughly) than in the magnitude of its empirical advances, which is not quite what the paper sets out to emphasize. A new deep architecture is proposed that combines hierarchical deep learning with time-series modeling. The writing is strong and the model is well analyzed, but the empirical results remain somewhat inconclusive.
