The authors propose a new deep architecture that combines the hierarchical representations of deep learning with time-series modeling akin to HMMs or recurrent neural networks. The training algorithm builds the network layer by layer using supervised pretraining with a next-letter prediction objective. In experiments, very large networks trained for about 10 days improve over prior work on a Wikipedia dataset published by Hinton et al. The paper also analyzes how the network approaches its task; for instance, higher layers capture long-term dependencies, and the correspondence between opening and closing parentheses is modeled as a “pseudo-stable, attractor-like” state.

The paper is of good quality: it is well written, the experiments are well designed, and the figures clearly support the findings. My main concern is the experimental evaluation. The comparisons are conducted on a single dataset that appears to have relatively little prior work, and the direct comparisons are only to RNN-related models. More classical baselines—such as hierarchical HMMs and related sequence models capable of addressing similar tasks—are not included. Although Table 1 lists other approaches, they are evaluated on a different corpus, making those results not directly comparable.

Overall, the paper’s primary contribution lies more in its careful analysis of how the network operates (which is done well) than in its demonstrated performance. While this analysis is valuable, it is not fully aligned with the paper’s stated aim of establishing a new deep architecture through strong empirical results. The paper is well written and the model is thoroughly analyzed, but the empirical results are somewhat inconclusive.
