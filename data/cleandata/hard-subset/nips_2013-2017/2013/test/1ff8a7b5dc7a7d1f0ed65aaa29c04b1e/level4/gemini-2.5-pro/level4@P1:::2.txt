This paper investigates the properties and performance of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) on the task of character-level language modeling. The authors' core contribution is an in-depth analysis of this architecture. Their central finding is that, for a fixed parameter budget, DRNNs outperform standard shallow RNNs. The analysis provides evidence that the hierarchical structure of DRNNs allows different layers to operate on different time scales, with deeper layers learning to capture longer-term dependencies. A notable result is that these networks can be trained with simple stochastic gradient descent and backpropagation through time (BPTT) to handle complex, long-range dependencies like balancing parentheses, a capability previously associated with more advanced second-order optimization methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates that DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art performance on a challenging character-level language modeling task, rivaling results previously obtained only with the more complex Hessian-free (HF) optimization algorithm. Showing that this can be accomplished with standard BPTT is a valuable practical contribution.

2.  **Insightful Analysis:** Beyond simply reporting performance metrics, the paper provides a meaningful analysis of the network's internal dynamics. The experiments showing that different layers capture different time scales and contribute differently to the final prediction offer valuable insight into why the deep architecture is effective. The specific analysis of how the network handles parentheses is a compelling demonstration of its ability to learn non-trivial long-range structure.

3.  **Clear Motivation and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly motivated. The authors effectively argue why a hierarchical temporal structure is desirable for tasks like language modeling and provide experiments that directly support their hypotheses.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Originality of the Architecture:** A major concern is the claim of originality regarding the DRNN architecture. While the authors provide a novel analysis, the architecture itself is not new and has been used in prior work (e.g., Graves et al., ICASSP 2013). The authors should reframe their contribution not as the introduction of a novel architecture, but as a valuable and detailed analysis of an existing one. Focusing on the analysis is a perfectly valid contribution, but the current framing is misleading.

2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The analysis, while insightful, is confined to a single task (character-level language modeling on Wikipedia). This makes it difficult to assess the generality of the conclusions. The observed benefits of a temporal hierarchy could be an artifact of this specific problem domain. The paper would be significantly stronger if the authors could demonstrate similar results and properties on a task from a different domain, such as speech recognition.

3.  **Training Methodology and Baselines:** The paper focuses on full BPTT, which is computationally expensive for long sequences. A more common and efficient baseline for training RNN language models is truncated BPTT (TBPTT), which was successfully used by Mikolov and others to achieve excellent results. It is highly probable that TBPTT would perform well on this task at a fraction of the computational cost of full BPTT. Including a comparison with TBPTT would provide a more complete and practical assessment of training methods for these models. This would help clarify whether full BPTT is truly necessary to learn the long-range dependencies observed, or if a more pragmatic truncated approach would suffice.

### Summary

This paper presents an interesting and thorough analysis of deep recurrent neural networks. The key strengths are the strong empirical results and the insightful analysis that sheds light on the internal workings of the model. However, the paper is weakened by its overstated claims of architectural novelty and the narrow focus on a single task. To improve the work, the authors should properly situate the architecture within existing literature, and ideally, provide results on a second task to demonstrate the generality of their findings. Additionally, a comparison with the more standard truncated BPTT training method would strengthen the experimental evaluation.
