This paper explores the concept of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), which are essentially a combination of deep neural networks (DNNs) and recurrent neural networks (RNNs). The authors argue that common RNNs do not explicitly accommodate temporal hierarchies in time series data, and propose a hierarchical architecture to address this limitation. The DRNN architecture consists of multiple layers, each of which is a recurrent neural network that receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of DRNNs on a character-level language modeling task, achieving state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on a Wikipedia text corpus. They also provide an analysis of the different emergent time scales in the DRNNs, showing that the higher layers are capable of capturing longer-term dependencies in the data.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear explanation of the proposed architecture and its advantages over traditional RNNs. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the experimental results, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

One potential weakness of the paper is the lack of a direct comparison between DRNNs and traditional RNNs with the same number of parameters. The authors note that the regular RNN has fewer parameters than the DRNNs, which may make the comparison unfair. A more convincing comparison would be to train a standard RNN with the same number of parameters as the DRNNs, or to use a 2-layer DRNN versus a standard RNN with the same number of units.

Additionally, the authors could provide more insight into the role of the higher layers in processing more abstract and long-term properties of the text. While the text generation experiment is persuasive, it may be problematic to interpret the effect of "brain surgery" on RNNs due to the complex interdependencies between the outputs of the various layers.

Overall, the paper presents a compelling case for the use of DRNNs in text prediction and compression tasks, and provides a thorough analysis of the experimental results. However, some additional experiments and comparisons would strengthen the paper's claims and provide a more complete understanding of the benefits and limitations of DRNNs.
