The authors present a novel deep learning architecture integrating hierarchical deep learning principles with time-series modeling techniques common in Hidden Markov Models and recurrent neural networks. Their training methodology involves a layer-wise, supervised pre-training approach utilizing a next-letter prediction objective. Results demonstrate performance gains on a large-scale Wikipedia dataset (as previously published by Hinton et al.) after an extensive training period of approximately ten days, surpassing prior results. The paper further includes a detailed analysis of the network’s behavior, highlighting how long-term dependencies are captured in deeper layers and illustrating the modeling of structural elements like parenthesis matching through “pseudo-stable attractor-like states.”

The paper is generally strong, exhibiting clear writing and well-designed experiments with supportive figures. However, the experimental evaluation is a primary concern. The study focuses solely on a single dataset, which appears relatively unexplored, and comparisons are largely limited to recurrent neural network-based models. A broader comparison against alternative approaches capable of addressing similar tasks – such as hierarchical Hidden Markov Models – would strengthen the work. While Table 1 acknowledges other relevant methods, their evaluation on a different corpus renders direct comparison difficult. 

The paper’s primary contribution lies in its insightful analysis of *how* the network functions, a component executed effectively, rather than a substantial advancement in performance. Although this detailed analysis is valuable, it deviates from the paper’s stated goal of introducing a new architecture with improved capabilities. In summary, the proposed deep architecture is well-presented and analyzed, but the experimental results remain somewhat inconclusive due to the limited scope of evaluation.
