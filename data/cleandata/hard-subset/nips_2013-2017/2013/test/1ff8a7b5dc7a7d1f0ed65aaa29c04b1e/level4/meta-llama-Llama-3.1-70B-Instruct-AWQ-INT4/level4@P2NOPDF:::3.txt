I appreciate the authors' insight that older data may benefit from more layers of processing compared to newer data, and their proposed solution of using a deep RNN to address this issue. Adding extra layers of nonlinearities between each time step is another potential approach to explore. I would be interested in knowing whether the authors have investigated this possibility.

The use of gradient normalization is an important aspect of the proposed method, and I would like to see a more detailed explanation of its significance. Specifically, I am curious about how gradient normalization helps to "avoid the problem of bifurcations." A recent ICML 2013 paper by researchers from the University of Montreal on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs may be relevant to this discussion. Furthermore, I wonder whether normalizing the gradient in this way could potentially disrupt the "averaging" behavior of stochastic gradient descent (SGD).

In terms of related work, the authors may want to compare their results to those of Mikolov et al. on Wikipedia compression tasks.

The experiments designed to examine the roles of each layer in terms of time-scale and abstraction are well-conceived, but I find the text generation experiment (Table 2) to be the most compelling. However, as the authors themselves acknowledge, interpreting the effects of "brain surgery" on RNNs can be problematic due to the complex interdependencies between the outputs of different layers.

A crucial piece of evidence missing from the empirical results is whether the higher layers are actually better at processing abstract and long-term properties of the text than if the units were placed in the first layer. In other words, do the higher layers benefit from the extra levels of processing that precede them in a non-trivial way? While the results suggest that the higher layers take on more abstract and longer-term roles after training, this is incomplete evidence for the authors' claims.

I notice that the regular RNN has fewer parameters in the experiments, which may make the comparison unfair. A more convincing comparison would be between a deeper RNN and a standard RNN with the same number of parameters or units. For example, comparing a 2-layer DRNN to a standard RNN with the same number of units would strengthen the paper's claims.

Instead of Figure 2, it would be more informative to show how well different depths of DRNN perform on the benchmarks when trained from scratch, possibly with wider layers to compensate for the difference in the number of parameters and/or units.

Minor points:

* The authors should define DRNN-AO and IO in the text, not just in Figure 1.
* The paper explores a hybrid of deep and recurrent neural networks called DRNNs, which are like deep networks but with recurrent connections at each layer that operate through time. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this architecture for text prediction and compression compared to existing approaches.
* A significant portion of the paper is devoted to experiments arguing that higher-level layers process more abstract and long-term structures in the data. While these experiments are convincing, I have some reservations and would like to see additional experiments.
* I believe it is worthwhile to investigate these types of deep temporal networks and gain insight into their functionality after training. The paper is well-written, easy to read, and intellectually honest about its potential problems and shortcomings, which is appreciated.
