I appreciate the observation that in standard RNNs older inputs arbitrarily pass through more “layers” of computation than newer inputs. Stacking recurrent layers seems like a sensible way to give the model additional nonlinear processing for short-term signals while also enabling other benefits. A straightforward alternative baseline would be to insert extra nonlinear transformations between consecutive time steps (i.e., additional feedforward layers within each step). Have you explored this variant?

How critical was the gradient normalization in practice, and in what precise sense does it “avoid bifurcations”? There is a recent ICML 2013 paper from the University of Montreal on gradient truncation/clipping and optimization in RNNs that seems relevant. Also, could scaling the gradient in this way interfere with the stochastic averaging properties of SGD?

For prior results on Wikipedia-style compression tasks, you might also consider comparisons to work by Mikolov et al.

Among the experiments aimed at probing layer-wise roles in terms of temporal scale and potential “abstraction,” the text generation study (Table 2) is the most compelling to me. That said, as you note around line 241, interpreting the effects of this kind of ablation “brain surgery” is tricky because of the complex interdependencies that can form among the outputs of different layers.

The largest empirical gap, in my view, is establishing whether higher layers truly handle more abstract and longer-term structure better than if those same units were placed in the first layer—i.e., whether they are deriving nontrivial benefit from the additional processing below them. The fact that upper layers appear to assume more abstract/longer-term roles after training is encouraging but not conclusive evidence on this point.

It also looks like the plain RNN has fewer parameters in your setup, since 2767^2 × 5 > 2119^2 (I’m counting both recurrent and inter-layer weight matrices, hence the multiplication by 2), so the comparison may be somewhat unfavorable. A stronger case would be made by showing that deeper RNNs outperform a standard RNN with the same parameter budget—or better yet, the same number of units. For example, compare a 2-layer DRNN to a single-layer RNN with the same number of units, where parameter-count differences wouldn’t bias the comparison so strongly toward the RNN. This would substantially bolster the claims.

Relatedly, instead of Figure 2, I would have liked to see results across multiple depths trained from scratch on the benchmark, possibly widening shallower models to match the number of parameters and/or units.

This paper studies a hybrid of deep and recurrent architectures—DRNNs—which resemble deep networks but include recurrent connections at each layer operating through time. The authors demonstrate that this architecture performs very well on text prediction/compression relative to existing approaches. Much of the paper presents experiments arguing that higher layers capture more abstract, longer-term structure; these are fairly persuasive, though I have the reservations noted above and would welcome a couple of additional experiments. Overall, it is valuable to investigate deep temporal models and to analyze their post-training behavior. The manuscript is clearly written and, commendably, candid about potential shortcomings and confounds.
