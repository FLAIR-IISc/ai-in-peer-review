I appreciate the observation that, in standard RNNs, earlier tokens can effectively undergo more processing than recent ones, creating an arbitrary depth bias over time. Building a deep RNN seems a reasonable way to strengthen short-term modeling while potentially bringing other benefits. Another natural extension would be to insert additional nonlinear layers within each time step (i.e., between recurrent updates). Have you tried this, and if so, how does it compare?

Could you clarify the role and impact of gradient normalization? How critical is it to your results, and why does it “avoid the problem of bifurcations”? There is related work from ICML 2013 (U. Montreal) on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs that may be relevant; a comparison or discussion would help. Also, does this kind of gradient normalization interfere with the averaging properties of SGD or introduce bias in the gradient estimates?

On related benchmarks such as Wikipedia compression, it would be useful to compare with results by Mikolov et al.

Among the experiments probing the different roles of each layer over time-scale and abstraction, the text generation results (Table 2) are the most persuasive. That said, as you note around line 241, interpreting “brain surgery” interventions can be tricky because of complex interdependencies between layers’ outputs, which may confound causal conclusions.

The biggest missing empirical piece, in my view, is direct evidence that higher layers genuinely capture more abstract, longer-term properties than the same number of units would if placed in the first layer. In other words, do higher layers benefit in a nontrivial way from the extra levels of processing that precede them, beyond what a single-layer allocation of those units could achieve? Observing that higher layers appear to take on these roles after training is suggestive but not conclusive.

I am also concerned about parameter-count fairness. The standard RNN seems to use fewer parameters than the deep models (e.g., 2767^2 × 5 > 2119^2; counting both recurrent and inter-layer matrices roughly doubles these totals), which could bias the comparison. A more convincing evaluation would show deeper RNNs outperforming a standard RNN with the same total number of parameters—or, more stringently, with the same number of units. For example, compare a two-layer DRNN against a single-layer RNN with the same number of units, so that parameter count does not unduly favor the standard RNN. Evidence along these lines would substantially strengthen the paper’s claims.

Relatedly, instead of Figure 2, it would be more informative to report how different DRNN depths perform on benchmarks when trained from scratch, potentially using wider shallow models to match parameter counts and/or units with their deeper counterparts. Such ablations would clarify the contribution of depth per se.
