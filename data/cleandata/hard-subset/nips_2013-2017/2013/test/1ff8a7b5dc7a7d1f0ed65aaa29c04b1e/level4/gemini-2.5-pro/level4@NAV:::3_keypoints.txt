- Authors identify an asymmetry in standard RNNs (earlier timesteps processed by more layers) and propose DRNN to mitigate it and better model short-term dependencies.
- Question: have the authors tried adding additional nonlinear layers between timesteps (in addition to between deep layers)?
- The paper needs stronger motivation and empirical analysis for gradient normalization: its impact on final performance and the claim it "avoids the problem of bifurcations."
- Discuss relation of the normalization to recent work on gradient truncation/optimization in RNNs (e.g., ICML 2013 University of Montreal paper) and whether it may interfere with SGD averaging.
- For the Wikipedia compression tasks, results should be compared to prior benchmarks such as Mikolov et al.
- The experiments probing layer roles are insightful; text generation (Table 2) gives persuasive evidence of layer specialization, but interpretation is hard due to interdependencies formed during training.
- A central open question: is hierarchical position necessary for higher-layer specialization, or could a sufficiently wide single-layer RNN achieve similar effects?
- Recommend an experiment showing higher layers benefit nontrivially from preceding levels rather than from simple capacity increase.
- Current baseline comparison is confounded by parameter mismatches; DRNN should be compared to standard RNNs with matched parameter counts or matched numbers of units.
- Instead of Figure 2, present benchmark performance for DRNNs of varying depths trained from scratch, controlling for total parameters/units by widening shallower models.
- Minor: explicitly define DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO in the main text.
- Overall: DRNN is promising and effective on text prediction/compression; paper is well-written and experiments are compelling but need additional controls to substantiate claims.
