This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) that stack recurrent layers to induce a temporal hierarchy. Each layer is a recurrent network that receives the hidden state of the previous layer, and the authors consider two output variants: output from the top layer only (DRNN-1O) and output from all layers (DRNN-AO). Training uses SGD with gradient normalization and a simple layer-wise supervised pretraining schedule for DRNN-1O. Experiments focus on character-level language modeling on a large Wikipedia corpus (about 1.4B characters), with networks of roughly 4.9M parameters trained for ~10 days on a single GPU. The DRNNs achieve strong bits-per-character (BPC) results—reported as improving over prior work—and are competitive with multiplicative RNNs trained via Hessian-free optimization. The paper then analyzes emergent time scales across layers, the impact of ablating layer outputs, generative samples, sensitivity to typos and context switches, and long-range dependencies such as closing parentheses, which are argued to behave like a “pseudo-stable attractor-like state.”

Relation to prior work: The architecture is closely related to earlier hierarchical/stacked recurrent models (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández, Graves & Schmidhuber; Graves et al. on stacked LSTMs), as well as to multiscale designs that explicitly enforce different time steps. The main novelty here lies less in proposing a new architecture than in systematically analyzing the temporal behavior of stacked recurrent layers trained with SGD on a large corpus.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation for stacking RNN layers to capture temporal hierarchies; the architecture and training setup are well-described.
- Careful experimental protocol and strong engineering: large models, extensive training, and well-presented figures that support the claims.
- Insightful analyses: layer ablations, typo/context switch perturbations, and the parentheses experiment convincingly demonstrate emergent multi-timescale behavior and long-range interactions.
- The simple training strategy (SGD with gradient normalization and layer-wise supervised pretraining) achieves competitive or improved performance compared to more complex second-order methods on this corpus.

Weaknesses:
- Empirical evaluation is limited to a single dataset, with only three model instantiations due to training cost. This makes it hard to assess robustness and generality.
- Direct comparisons are limited to other RNN-style models on the same dataset; Table 1 includes non-RNN methods but on different corpora, making those numbers not directly comparable.
- The comparison set omits strong baselines such as LSTMs (stacked or otherwise) and hierarchical/multiscale models (e.g., hierarchical HMMs) evaluated under the same conditions.
- The architectural contribution is incremental relative to prior stacked/ hierarchical RNN work; the most original part is the analysis. This is somewhat at odds with the framing of the contribution as a new architecture.
- Limited exploration of design choices (e.g., number of layers, per-layer sizes, output placement), variance across runs, or sensitivity to hyperparameters.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, methods are standard and appropriate, and claims are mostly supported by experiments. The training details are clear. However, the evidence base is narrow (one corpus, few model variants), and the performance claims would be stronger with broader comparisons and additional datasets.

Clarity: The manuscript is well written and organized. Figures are informative and directly support the conclusions. Minor suggestions: clarify the dataset composition and any preprocessing; report training budget in terms of total tokens processed; include variance across initializations if feasible.

Originality: Stacked RNNs are not new; the incremental training schedule and the comprehensive temporal analyses are the most novel aspects. Related work is cited, but the distinction from prior stacked LSTM results could be sharpened.

Significance: The analytical insights into emergent time scales and long-range dependencies are valuable and likely to inform future work. The empirical gains, while promising, are limited by the scope of evaluation and the comparison set.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add results on at least one additional standard benchmark (e.g., text8/enwik8, Penn Treebank) and provide parameter-matched comparisons to LSTMs and other multiscale/hierarchical models.
- Include a non-RNN baseline (e.g., hierarchical HMM or strong n-gram/KN smoothing) on the same corpus for context.
- Expand ablations: effect of per-layer output connections (AO vs 1O) under matched training; varying number of layers/units; training without layer-wise pretraining.
- Report variability across runs and provide learning curves; if possible, release code/scripts for reproducibility.
- Discuss how vocabulary choices (95 vs 86 characters) affect BPC and comparability.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, careful analysis of how depth in RNNs induces multi-timescale behavior.
- Competitive performance with a simple training scheme.
- Well-written and likely to be of interest to the NIPS community studying sequence models.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical scope: one dataset, few models, and narrow baselines.
- Architectural novelty is modest relative to prior stacked/hierarchical RNN work.
- Cross-paper performance comparisons are not apples-to-apples in several cases, weakening the strength of the empirical claims.

Overall assessment: A well-executed and clearly presented paper whose primary contribution is the analysis of deep recurrent architectures and their emergent temporal hierarchies. The results are promising but somewhat inconclusive due to limited comparisons and datasets. Strengthening the empirical evaluation would substantially improve the paper’s impact.
