

The authors propose a novel deep architecture that integrates the hierarchical structure of deep learning with time-series modeling techniques from HMMs or recurrent neural networks. The training algorithm constructs the network layer-by-layer via supervised pre-training using a next-letter prediction objective. Experiments demonstrate that after approximately 10 days of training on very large networks, the model achieves improved performance on the Wikipedia dataset introduced by Hinton et al., surpassing prior work. The authors further analyze the network's operational dynamics, revealing that higher layers model long-term dependencies and that the correspondence between opening and closing parentheses is captured through a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state."  

The paper is of high quality, well-written, and presents well-designed experiments with figures that effectively support the findings. However, the primary concern lies in the experimental evaluation. The comparison is conducted solely on the Wikipedia dataset, which has seen limited prior work, and the benchmarking is restricted to RNN-related models without comparison to more established hierarchical approaches like HMMs—methods capable of addressing similar tasks. Table 1 lists alternative approaches, but they are evaluated on a different corpus, rendering them incomparable.  

The paper's stated contribution centers on proposing a new architecture combining hierarchical deep learning with time-series modeling, yet the core value emerges from the insightful analysis of *how* the network operates. This analytical focus contrasts with the paper's explicit emphasis on the architecture's performance, resulting in a mismatch between the claimed contribution and the demonstrated results. While the model is well-analyzed and the writing is clear, the results remain somewhat inconclusive due to the narrow experimental scope.
