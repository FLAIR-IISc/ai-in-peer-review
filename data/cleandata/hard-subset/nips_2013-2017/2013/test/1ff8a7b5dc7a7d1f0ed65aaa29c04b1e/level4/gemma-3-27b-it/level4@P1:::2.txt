This work investigates the usefulness of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs). The paper reaches interesting conclusions: when holding the number of parameters constant, deeper recurrent neural networks outperform standard RNNs. Other interesting findings include an analysis showing that the deeper networks capture more long-term dependencies, and the result that plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses – a feat previously thought to require more advanced methods like Hessian-free optimization.

While the paper does not introduce the deep RNN architecture – it has been used previously, for example, in Graves et al. (ICASSP 2013) and likely earlier – it effectively focuses on an analysis of its properties. This is a valid approach, and the analysis is meaningful. However, it would be strengthened by applying it to a broader range of problems, such as a speech task. This would help demonstrate whether the findings are specific to character-level language modeling or generalize to other temporal data.

The authors acknowledge the computational cost of Hessian-free (HF) training and refrain from including those experiments. It would be worthwhile to explore truncated backpropagation through time (TBPTT) with a reasonable truncation length (e.g., 30 steps). While TBPTT might not achieve the same performance in balancing parentheses, it could offer competitive entropies at a significantly lower computational cost. The success of TBPTT in training RNN language models, demonstrated by Mikolov (PhD thesis), suggests this is a promising direction.

**Strengths:**

*   Demonstrates improved performance with deeper RNNs compared to standard RNNs, given a fixed parameter budget.
*   Provides analysis suggesting that deeper layers learn to capture longer-range dependencies in the data.
*   Shows that standard BPTT can achieve surprisingly good performance on a challenging task (parenthesis balancing), comparable to methods previously thought to be superior.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The DRNN architecture is not novel; prior work already exists (e.g., Graves et al., 2013). The paper should avoid claiming novelty in the architecture itself.
*   The analysis is limited to a single task (character-level language modeling). Applying the analysis to other tasks (e.g., speech recognition) would strengthen the findings.
*   The absence of TBPTT results leaves open the question of whether a simpler training method could achieve comparable performance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a sound analysis of DRNNs, providing insights into their behavior and potential benefits.
*   The results are clearly presented and supported by experimental evidence.
*   The findings contribute to a better understanding of deep recurrent networks and their ability to model long-term dependencies.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of architectural novelty diminishes the paper's overall impact.
*   The limited scope of the analysis (single task) raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
*   The absence of TBPTT results is a missed opportunity to explore a potentially effective and computationally efficient training method.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper with interesting findings. While it doesn’t introduce a new architecture, the analysis of DRNNs provides valuable insights. Addressing the weaknesses – specifically, expanding the analysis to other tasks and exploring TBPTT – would significantly strengthen the paper. I believe that, with minor revisions addressing these points, this work would be a valuable contribution to the NIPS proceedings.
