The authors propose a novel deep architecture that integrates the hierarchical structure of deep learning with the temporal modeling capabilities of recurrent neural networks. The proposed approach involves layer-by-layer training using supervised learning for a next-character prediction task. Experiments demonstrate that, after extensive training (approximately 10 days) of large networks, the model achieves state-of-the-art performance on a Wikipedia-based text corpus previously used by Hinton et al. The paper further presents an analysis of the network’s behavior, revealing insights into how it tackles the task – for instance, highlighting the role of higher layers in modeling long-term dependencies and characterizing the handling of parentheses as a “pseudo-stable attractor-like state”.

This paper is of good quality, exhibiting clear writing and well-designed experiments supported by informative figures. A primary concern, however, lies with the experimental evaluation. The comparison is limited to a single dataset, which appears to have a relatively narrow scope of existing work. The direct comparison is largely restricted to RNN-based models, overlooking potentially relevant hierarchical models like Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) which address similar tasks. While Table 1 lists other approaches, discrepancies in the corpora used render direct comparison difficult.

The paper’s main contribution resides in the analysis of *how* the network operates—an analysis conducted thoroughly—rather than a groundbreaking performance achievement, although this isn’t necessarily a flaw given the paper’s stated goals. A new deep architecture is proposed, successfully combining the hierarchical benefits of deep learning with time-series modeling. The model is well-analyzed, but the results, while promising, remain somewhat limited in scope. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel architecture combining deep learning and recurrent neural networks.
*   Thorough analysis of the learned representations and temporal dynamics within the network.
*   State-of-the-art performance on a challenging character-level language modeling task.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of the methods and results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation with a focus on a single dataset.
*   Lack of comparison to alternative hierarchical models (e.g., HMMs) commonly used for sequential data.
*   The performance gain, while notable, is incremental rather than transformative.
*   The extensive training time (10 days) may limit reproducibility and accessibility.
