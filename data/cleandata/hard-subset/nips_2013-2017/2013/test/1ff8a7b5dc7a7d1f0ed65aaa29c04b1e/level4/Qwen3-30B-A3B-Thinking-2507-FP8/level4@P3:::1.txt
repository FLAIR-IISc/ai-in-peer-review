

This paper proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), a hierarchical architecture where each layer is an RNN processing the hidden state of the layer below. The authors argue that this structure inherently creates multiple temporal scales, enabling better modeling of time-series data with multi-scale dependencies. They validate their approach on character-level language modeling using a Wikipedia corpus, demonstrating state-of-the-art RNN performance with simple SGD training (no Hessian-free optimization), and provide detailed analysis of emergent time scales through experiments on typo perturbations, context switching, and parenthesis-closing tasks.  

**Strengths**  
The paper excels in clarity and methodological rigor. The DRNN architecture is well-motivated, with clear equations (Section 2.1) and intuitive schematic diagrams (Fig. 1). The experimental design is thorough: they compare DRNN-1O (output only at top layer) and DRNN-AO (outputs at all layers), analyze layer contributions via ablation (Fig. 2), and validate temporal hierarchies through perturbation experiments (Fig. 3). The analysis of how higher layers model long-term dependencies (e.g., maintaining parenthesis-closing probability over hundreds of characters, Fig. 4) is particularly insightful and well-supported. The paper also thoughtfully addresses training challenges (Section 2.3) and demonstrates that DRNNs achieve competitive results (BPC ≈ 1.47) with SGD alone, contrasting with prior work requiring Hessian-free methods [19]. The writing is precise, figures are informative, and the manuscript provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.  

**Weaknesses**  
The primary limitation lies in **significance and scope**. The claimed "state-of-the-art" performance is context-specific: the comparison (Table 1) relies on a single dataset (Wikipedia text) and only includes RNN-based baselines (e.g., [19]’s Hessian-free RNN), omitting standard hierarchical models like HMMs or hierarchical LSTMs [5,6] that have been applied to language modeling. For instance, [5] used hierarchical RNNs for speech (not text), and [6] focused on structured sequence labeling—neither directly comparable to the authors’ task. The inclusion of PAQ compression [14] as a benchmark is misleading, as it is a lossless compression algorithm, not a language model. Consequently, the paper fails to demonstrate *why* DRNNs are superior to existing hierarchical approaches for the task, undermining the significance claim.  

The **originality** is also overstated. The DRNN architecture builds directly on Graves et al. [8] (2013), who used stacked bidirectional LSTMs for TIMIT phoneme recognition. While [8] prioritized performance over analysis, this paper focuses on *analyzing* the architecture’s temporal dynamics—yet the title and abstract frame it as a *new architecture*, not a *new analysis*. This misalignment weakens the contribution. The core innovation is the temporal-scale analysis, not the model itself; the paper should explicitly position this as an analytical contribution to existing DRNN work [8], rather than a novel model.  

**Quality and Clarity**  
The technical quality is strong: experiments are well-designed, with careful ablation studies (e.g., layer removal in Fig. 2) and robust metrics (BPC). The training procedure (Section 2.3) is transparent, and the SGD-based approach is a practical advantage. However, the single-dataset focus limits generalizability. The authors acknowledge this by noting "the networks are so large that there is very little difference in performance for different initializations" (Section 3.1), but this doesn’t address whether DRNNs generalize beyond Wikipedia text.  

**Recommendation**  
This paper is a solid technical contribution with valuable insights into temporal hierarchies in RNNs, but its significance is constrained by narrow evaluation. It would be a strong fit for NIPS if the authors:  
1. Expanded comparisons to standard hierarchical models (e.g., HMMs, hierarchical LSTMs) on the same dataset,  
2. Clarified that the *analysis*—not the architecture—is the primary contribution,  
3. Discussed limitations (e.g., DRNNs’ scalability to longer sequences, lack of ablation on non-language tasks).  

As written, the paper overstates its novelty and underdelivers on significance. The analysis (Fig. 3–4) is compelling evidence for *how* DRNNs operate, but the paper’s framing as a new model obscures this. Given NIPS’ emphasis on advancing the field, the current version lacks sufficient evidence that DRNNs offer a *practical* or *broadly applicable* improvement over existing methods.  

**Pro/Con Summary**  
*Pro*:  
- Clear, well-structured analysis of temporal hierarchies via rigorous experiments.  
- Practical SGD training approach (no Hessian-free needed).  
- Strong theoretical grounding in time-scale emergence.  
*Con*:  
- Limited dataset/baseline scope undermines significance claims.  
- Architecture is incremental (builds on [8]), not novel.  
- Overstated "state-of-the-art" due to non-comparable baselines.  

**Verdict**: *Borderline. Accept if revisions address scope/originality gaps; otherwise, reject.* The analysis is valuable, but the paper must better articulate its *analytical* contribution versus the *architectural* one to align with NIPS’ focus on impactful scientific advances.
