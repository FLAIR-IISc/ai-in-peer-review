- Paper proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs) and shows they improve text prediction and compression, with generally persuasive experiments.
- Experiments suggesting higher layers capture more abstract, long-term structures are promising but need further validation.
- Core idea that older data benefits from more layers is compelling; ask whether extra nonlinear layers between timesteps were explored.
- The role of gradient normalization in “avoiding bifurcations” needs clearer explanation and connection to related work (e.g., ICML 2013 on gradient truncation/optimization).
- Question whether gradient normalization might disrupt SGD’s averaging behavior.
- Results should be compared to prior Wikipedia compression work (Mikolov et al.).
- Text generation (Table 2) is the most convincing analysis, but layer “surgery” interpretations are problematic due to inter-layer dependencies.
- Missing empirical test showing higher layers actually perform abstraction/long-term processing better than if those units were in the first layer.
- Comparisons are unfair because the baseline RNN has fewer parameters; recommend matching parameter or unit counts for fair evaluation (e.g., two-layer DRNN vs. single RNN with same total units).
- Replace Figure 2 with DRNN performance across varying depths trained from scratch and consider widening layers to equalize parameters/units.
- Minor: define abbreviations DRNN-AO and IO in the main text rather than only in Figure 1.
