This paper introduces a deep recurrent neural network (DRNN) architecture, which stacks multiple RNN layers hierarchically. Each layer receives the hidden state of the layer below it as input, thereby combining the temporal modeling capabilities of RNNs with the hierarchical feature processing characteristic of deep networks. The authors hypothesize that this structure allows the network to naturally capture temporal hierarchies at different time scales.

The DRNN is evaluated on a large-scale character-level language modeling task using a Wikipedia corpus. The results demonstrate that the proposed model achieves state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on this dataset, rivaling models trained with more complex second-order optimization methods (like Hessian-free) using only standard stochastic gradient descent. Beyond performance, a significant portion of the paper is dedicated to analyzing the trained models. The authors provide experimental evidence that different layers learn to operate on different time scales, with higher layers capturing longer-term dependencies. They also present a compelling case study on how the model learns to handle long-range structures, such as matching parentheses, which they describe as a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state."

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The proposed architecture is explained well, and the figures effectively illustrate both the model and the experimental findings.
2.  **In-depth Analysis:** The main strength of the paper lies in its detailed analysis of the network's internal dynamics. The experiments designed to probe the time scales of different layers and the investigation into handling parentheses provide valuable insights into *how* these deep models function. This type of analysis is often missing in papers that focus solely on performance benchmarks.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed DRNN architecture is a natural and well-motivated extension of existing models. Achieving state-of-the-art results on a challenging benchmark with a relatively simple training procedure (SGD with gradient clipping) is a significant and impressive achievement.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The empirical validation is confined to a single, large-scale dataset for character-level language modeling. While the results are strong, the generality of the architecture's benefits remains unproven. It would be more convincing to see its performance on other sequential tasks (e.g., speech recognition, time-series prediction) where temporal hierarchies are also known to be important.
2.  **Narrow Comparison:** The comparison is primarily with other RNN variants. While this is appropriate for claiming state-of-the-art within that family of models, a broader discussion or comparison with other types of hierarchical sequence models could have strengthened the paper's claims of novelty and significance. Furthermore, as the review draft notes, Table 1 includes results from other approaches on different corpora, which are not directly comparable and could be misleading to readers.
3.  **Framing of Contribution:** The paper presents itself primarily as a proposal for a new architecture with superior performance. However, its most significant contribution arguably lies in the detailed analysis of the learned representations. The framing of the paper could be adjusted to better highlight this analytical contribution, which is a strength in itself, rather than focusing purely on the architectural novelty.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that proposes a well-motivated deep recurrent architecture and demonstrates its effectiveness on a difficult task. The analysis of the model's behavior is particularly insightful. The main weaknesses are the reliance on a single dataset and a somewhat narrow set of comparison models, which makes it difficult to assess the broader impact of the proposed architecture. Nevertheless, the strong results and detailed analysis make it a valuable contribution to the field.
