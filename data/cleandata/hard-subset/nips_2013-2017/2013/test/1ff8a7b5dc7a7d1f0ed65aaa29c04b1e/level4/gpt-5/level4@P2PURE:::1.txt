The authors introduce a novel deep architecture that merges hierarchical representations from deep learning with time-series modeling in the spirit of HMMs and recurrent neural networks. The proposed training procedure constructs the network layer by layer, using supervised pretraining with a next-letter prediction objective. In experiments, very large networks trained for roughly 10 days achieve improved performance over prior work on the Wikipedia dataset released by Hinton et al. The paper then offers a detailed analysis of how the model tackles the task: higher layers capture long-range dependencies, and the relationship between opening and closing parentheses appears as a pseudo-stable, attractor-like state.

Overall, the paper is of good quality: it is well written, the experimental setup is sound, and the figures effectively support the findings. My primary concern lies with the empirical evaluation. The comparisons are limited to a single dataset that appears to have relatively little prior work, and direct comparisons are only made against other RNN-type models—not against more common alternatives such as hierarchical HMMs that can handle similar tasks. Although Table 1 lists additional approaches, they are reported on a different corpus and thus are not directly comparable.

The paper’s main contribution lies more in elucidating how the network operates (analyzed thoroughly and convincingly) than in demonstrating what it achieves, which does not fully align with the stated objective of proposing a new deep architecture that combines hierarchical deep learning with time-series modeling. The paper is well written and the model is carefully analyzed, but the empirical results remain somewhat inconclusive.
