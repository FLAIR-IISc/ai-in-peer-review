**Summary**

This paper introduces the Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN), an architecture that stacks multiple RNN layers hierarchically. The motivation is that standard RNNs, despite being deep in time, only apply a single layer of non-linear processing to the input at each timestep. This may be insufficient for complex data that requires hierarchical processing. In a DRNN, the output of each layer becomes the input sequence for the next, allowing the network to build up representations at different levels and timescales. The authors demonstrate that a 5-layer DRNN achieves state-of-the-art performance on a large-scale character-level language modeling benchmark (Wikipedia), matching results previously obtained with more complex second-order training methods. A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to analyzing the trained models, presenting evidence that higher layers learn to operate on longer timescales and capture more abstract properties of the text, such as tracking parentheses over long distances.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Simple Architecture:** The paper's core motivation is compelling. The critique that standard RNNs may lack sufficient processing depth at each timestep is insightful, and the proposed DRNN architecture is a simple, intuitive, and natural solution.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The DRNN achieves excellent performance on a challenging benchmark, matching the state of the art for recurrent networks using only simple stochastic gradient descent (SGD). This suggests that the architectural prior is well-suited to the task and can simplify the optimization challenge.
3.  **Thoughtful Analysis:** The authors go beyond simply reporting performance and conduct a series of experiments to understand *how* the DRNN works. The analyses of emergent timescales, context recovery, and parenthesis tracking provide valuable insights into the model's behavior and support the hypothesis that a temporal hierarchy is learned.
4.  **Clarity and Honesty:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The authors are also commendably forthright about the potential limitations of their analysis, such as the difficulty of interpreting "ablation" experiments on highly interdependent trained models.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors**

While the analysis is intriguing, the central empirical claim—that the hierarchical structure itself provides a distinct advantage—could be substantiated more rigorously.

1.  **Controlled Comparisons:** The primary missing piece is a conclusive demonstration that the hierarchical processing of the DRNN is more effective than simply using a larger, single-layer RNN. The current comparison is against a standard RNN with a similar number of *parameters*. While this is a fair comparison, it doesn't fully isolate the benefit of depth. A key question remains: are the higher layers of the DRNN learning more abstract, long-term features because of their position in the hierarchy, or simply because they exist? Could these same capabilities be learned if their corresponding units were simply added to the first layer, making it wider? A more convincing comparison might be against a standard RNN with the same total number of hidden *units* (which would have many more parameters). A controlled experiment comparing, for instance, a 2-layer DRNN to a single-layer RNN with twice the units per layer could help disentangle the effects of depth from raw capacity.

2.  **Effect of Depth:** Following the point above, a valuable experiment would be to analyze the effect of network depth directly. Instead of the layer-removal experiment in Figure 2, it would be more informative to see performance on the benchmark for DRNNs of varying depths (e.g., 2, 3, 4, and 5 layers), trained from scratch. To ensure a fair comparison, the layer widths could be adjusted to keep the total parameter count constant across these different architectures. This would provide direct evidence for the benefits of adding more layers.

3.  **Training Method Details:** The use of gradient normalization is mentioned as a key trick to stabilize training. Could you elaborate on its importance? How does performance degrade without it? This technique is similar to gradient clipping, which was recently analyzed in "On the difficulty of training Recurrent Neural Networks" (Pascanu et al., ICML 2013). It would be beneficial to discuss the connection and cite this relevant work. Additionally, does normalizing the gradient magnitude to 1 at every step interfere with the implicit averaging that SGD performs over mini-batches of varying gradient norms?

4.  **Alternative Architectures:** The proposed DRNN adds depth by stacking layers that operate sequentially in time. An alternative approach to increasing processing depth would be to add more non-linear layers *within* a single timestep, between the input, the recurrent state, and the output. Have you considered or experimented with such an architecture?

5.  **Missing Comparisons:** For Wikipedia text compression, it would be useful to also compare against the results from Mikolov et al.'s work on RNN language models, as they represent strong, relevant baselines in this area.

**Minor Issues**

*   The acronyms DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO are defined in the caption for Figure 1 but should also be explicitly defined in the main body of the text for clarity.

**Conclusion**

This is a well-written and interesting paper that explores a simple yet powerful extension to the RNN architecture. The strong results and insightful analysis make a good case for the utility of DRNNs. The main weakness is the lack of more controlled experiments to definitively prove that the hierarchical structure, and not just the parameterization, is the source of the performance gains. Addressing the points above, particularly by adding comparisons between DRNNs of varying depths, would significantly strengthen the paper's claims. Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution by highlighting the importance of architectural choices in deep temporal models and providing a compelling direction for future research.
