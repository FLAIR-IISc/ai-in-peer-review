- Paper investigates DRNNs for text prediction and finds higher layers capture more abstract, long-term structure.
- Core insight: older data benefits from deeper processing; DRNN architecture is a reasonable approach for short-term dependencies.
- Gradient normalization claim needs deeper justification; "avoids bifurcations" is unclear and may conflict with SGD averaging/bias convergence.
- Cite and compare to relevant work on gradient truncation/optimization (e.g., Gulcehre et al., ICML 2013).
- Must compare against prior Wikipedia compression baselines (e.g., Mikolov et al.) to contextualize empirical claims.
- Text generation analysis (Table 2) is the most persuasive experiment but has causal interpretation limits due to complex inter-layer dependencies.
- Major untested hypothesis: unclear if higher layers inherently leverage depth for abstraction or simply adopt roles after training due to architecture.
- Recommend experiments that reposition units across layers or use single-layer RNNs with matched parameter counts to test whether depth, not capacity, drives abstraction.
- Figure 2 depth comparisons are limited; need DRNNs trained from scratch across multiple depths with layer widths adjusted to match total parameters.
- Minor: define DRNN-AO and IO in the main text and tighten the description of DRNNs as deep networks with recurrent connections at each layer.
- Overall: well-written and valuable, but parameter fairness and causal mechanism tests are critical omissions to address before strong acceptance.
