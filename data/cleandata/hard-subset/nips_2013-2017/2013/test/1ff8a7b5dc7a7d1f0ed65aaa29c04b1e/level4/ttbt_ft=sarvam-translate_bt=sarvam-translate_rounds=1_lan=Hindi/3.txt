Among the various experiments designed to investigate the different roles played by each layer in terms of time-frame and perhaps "abstractness," what I find most convincing is the experiment involving text generation (as shown in Table 2). However, as the authors themselves state in the paragraph at line 241, explaining the impact of such "brain surgery" on RNNs can be problematic because complex interdependencies can develop between the outputs of the different layers.

For me, the biggest missing piece of the empirical puzzle in this paper is the question of whether higher layers are actually better at processing more abstract and long-term properties of text, rather than simply moving units to the first layer. That is, are they benefiting from additional levels of processing that propel them forward in a non-trivial way? That they adopt these seemingly more abstract and long-term roles after training is good, but incomplete evidence that it is.

I noticed that these experiments have fewer parameters in the regular RNN because 2767^2  5 > 2119^2 (I'm counting both the recurrent and inter-layer weight matrices, so multiplying by 2), so the comparison might be a little unfair. Minor:
- You should define DRNN-AO and IO somewhere in the text, not just in Figure 1. This paper looks at a hybrid of deep and recurrent neural networks called DRNN, which are like deep networks but with recurrent connections at each layer that work through time. The authors have shown how such an architecture can work very well for text prediction/compression compared to existing approaches. A large part of this paper is dedicated to a series of experiments designed to argue that higher-level layers are processing/representing more abstract and long-term structures in the data. These experiments are quite convincing, but I have some objections, as detailed in my full review below, and I would like to see some more experiments. I think it's worth looking at such deep temporal networks and getting an idea of how they work after training. The paper is also easy to read, and seems quite intellectually honest and thorough about its potential problems and shortcomings, which I particularly appreciate.
