This paper investigates the properties and performance of Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), an architecture constructed by stacking multiple RNN layers, on the task of character-level language modeling. The authors' central hypothesis is that this hierarchical structure is well-suited for modeling time series with dependencies across multiple time scales. The paper presents several key findings: first, that a multi-layer DRNN outperforms a standard single-layer RNN with a comparable number of parameters, achieving state-of-the-art results on a large Wikipedia corpus using only stochastic gradient descent (SGD). Second, through a series of empirical analyses, the paper demonstrates that a temporal hierarchy emerges within the network, where deeper layers learn to operate on longer time scales and capture longer-term dependencies. Finally, the authors show that their model, trained with full backpropagation through time (BPTT), can successfully learn very long-range dependencies, such as balancing parentheses over hundreds of characters, a task previously associated with more powerful second-order optimization methods. While the DRNN architecture has been used previously (e.g., by Graves et al., 2013), this work provides a novel and in-depth analysis of its internal dynamics.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Strong Empirical Performance with Simple Methods:** The paper achieves state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on a challenging and widely used benchmark. The fact that this is accomplished with a relatively simple training algorithm (SGD with gradient normalization) is a significant result, highlighting the power of the deep architecture itself rather than a complex optimization procedure.
*   **Insightful and Novel Analysis:** The primary contribution of this paper is its thorough analysis of the DRNN's behavior. The experiments designed to measure the effective time scales of different layers (Figure 3) and the specific investigation into long-term dependencies like parenthesis balancing (Figure 4, Table 2) provide valuable insights into *why* and *how* these deep architectures succeed on complex sequential data. This goes beyond simply reporting a performance score and contributes to a better scientific understanding of these models.
*   **Clarity and Quality of the Work:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and presents its methods and results effectively. The authors provide sufficient detail about the model architecture, training procedure, and experimental setup to support their claims and facilitate reproducibility. The analyses are well-motivated and provide a complete piece of work.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

*   **Positioning and Originality of the Architecture:** The paper's introduction could be interpreted as claiming the DRNN architecture as a novel contribution. However, stacked or hierarchical RNNs have been explored in prior work (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio, 1996; Graves et al., 2013). While the authors do cite some of this work, they should more explicitly frame their contribution as a novel and deep *analysis* of this known architecture, rather than the introduction of the architecture itself. This is a matter of positioning but is crucial for accurately representing the paper's novelty.
*   **Limited Scope and Generalizability of Findings:** The analysis, while comprehensive for the chosen task, is confined to a single domain: character-level language modeling. The strong conclusion that DRNNs inherently foster a temporal hierarchy would be significantly more convincing if supported by evidence from other sequential domains where such hierarchies are prominent, such as speech recognition or music modeling. As it stands, it is unclear whether the observed emergent properties are general to DRNNs or an artifact of the specific statistical structure of natural language text.
*   **Incomplete Comparison of Training Methods:** The authors contrast their SGD-based training with the more complex Hessian-Free (HF) optimization method. However, a more practical and highly relevant baseline is missing: truncated backpropagation through time (BPTT). As demonstrated by Mikolov and others, truncated BPTT is highly effective for training state-of-the-art RNN language models and is far less computationally demanding than full BPTT over long sequences. It would be valuable to include a comparison with truncated BPTT (e.g., with 30-50 steps). This baseline might achieve competitive bits-per-character scores at a fraction of the computational cost, even if it is less likely to solve the extremely long-range parenthesis task. Such a comparison would provide a more complete picture of the performance-cost trade-offs.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a valuable and well-executed analysis of Deep RNNs. The empirical results are strong, and the investigation into the learned temporal hierarchies represents a significant scientific contribution. The main weaknesses are the potential overstatement of architectural novelty and the limited scope of the analysis (a single task), which limits the generality of the conclusions. Despite these points, the quality of the analysis and the significance of the findings make this a strong submission for NIPS.
