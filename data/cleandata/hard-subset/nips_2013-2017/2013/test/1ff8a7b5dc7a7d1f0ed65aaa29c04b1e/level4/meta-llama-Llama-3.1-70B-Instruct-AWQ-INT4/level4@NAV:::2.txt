REPLY TO REBUTTAL:

I generally concur with the notion of focusing on a single task.

It would be worthwhile to explore truncated backpropagation with a smaller number of steps (e.g., 30 steps), as it may achieve competitive performance at a lower cost, despite being less likely to capture long-range dependencies like parentheses balancing.

The paper examines the effectiveness of deep recurrent neural networks and arrives at several intriguing conclusions. Notably, it demonstrates that deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs when the number of parameters is held constant. Furthermore, the analysis reveals that deeper networks tend to capture more long-term information, and that plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can successfully train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, a task previously thought to be achievable only with Hessian-free optimization.

However, the paper's claim to introduce the deep RNN architecture is not accurate, as similar architectures have been explored in prior work (e.g., http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf). The authors should revise their claim and focus on the analysis instead.

While the analysis is meaningful, its significance would be enhanced by demonstrating its applicability to multiple problems, such as speech tasks. This would help to establish the generality of the findings and mitigate the concern that they may be specific to the chosen problem.

Finally, the Hessian-free experiments may have been unnecessarily expensive, as truncated backpropagation, a technique introduced in the 1990s and successfully employed by Mikolov in his PhD thesis, could have achieved similar results. It would be beneficial to confirm this hypothesis and explore the potential of truncated BPTT in this context.

This work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs, yielding three key results: (1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs on character-level language modeling when the number of parameters is fixed; (2) deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure; and (3) plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses. To strengthen the paper, the authors should consider extending their analysis to speech tasks, demonstrating the superiority of deeper RNNs and the presence of long-range information in their deeper layers.
