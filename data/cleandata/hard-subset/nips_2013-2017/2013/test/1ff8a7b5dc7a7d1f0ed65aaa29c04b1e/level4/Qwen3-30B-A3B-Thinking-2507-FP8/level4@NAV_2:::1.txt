

The authors propose a deep recurrent neural network (DRNN) architecture that stacks recurrent layers, where each layer receives the hidden state of the layer below as input, creating a temporal hierarchy for processing time series. This builds on prior work like Graves et al. (NIPS 2013) who used stacked bidirectional LSTMs for speech recognition but focused on performance over architectural analysis. The paper demonstrates DRNNs achieve state-of-the-art character-level language modeling on the Wikipedia corpus (BPC ≈ 1.51) using only SGD—previously requiring Hessian-free optimization—while providing detailed analysis of emergent time scales (e.g., bottom layers model short-term dependencies like character statistics, top layers capture long-term structures like parentheses matching).  

**Strengths**: The paper excels in clarity and analysis. The architecture is well-motivated, the experimental design (including layer-wise ablation for output contributions and typo-perturbation tests) rigorously demonstrates hierarchical time scales, and the analysis of parenthesis closing (showing "pseudo-stable attractor-like" behavior) is particularly insightful. The writing is precise, and the figures effectively support claims about layer-specific roles.  

**Weaknesses**: The contribution’s significance is limited by narrow evaluation scope. The primary comparison (Table 1) uses only the Wikipedia corpus, with no benchmarks against hierarchical models like HMMs or deep HMMs (e.g., from NIPS 2012–2014) that solve similar sequence tasks. The paper cites non-comparable baselines (e.g., PAQ compression), weakening the claim of "state-of-the-art" for recurrent networks. While the architectural analysis is strong, the results lack broader validation—e.g., no ablation on non-language tasks or scalability tests.  

**Pro/Con Acceptance**:  
*Pro*: Novel architectural analysis of time-scale emergence in RNNs, thorough empirical validation of layer-specific roles, and clear demonstration that DRNNs learn hierarchical temporal processing without complex training.  
*Con*: Limited dataset scope (only Wikipedia), insufficient comparison to established hierarchical sequence models (e.g., deep HMMs), and results do not clearly advance the field beyond incremental performance gains over prior RNNs.  

The paper is technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), and offers a fresh perspective on RNN hierarchy (Originality), but its significance (Significance) is constrained by the narrow evaluation. While the analysis is compelling, the lack of comparative context against hierarchical models in the field diminishes the impact.
