The authors present a novel deep learning architecture that integrates the hierarchical structure of deep networks with time-series modeling techniques commonly found in Hidden Markov Models and recurrent neural networks. Their training algorithm constructs the network incrementally through supervised, layer-wise pre-training using a next-letter prediction objective. The experimental results demonstrate performance improvements on a Wikipedia dataset (originally published by Hinton et al.) after training very large networks for an extended period of approximately ten days. The authors further analyze the network’s behavior, providing insights into how it approaches the task; for instance, they observe that higher layers model long-term dependencies and that balanced parentheses are represented as a “pseudo-stable attractor-like state.”

This is a high-quality paper, well-written and supported by clear figures and well-designed experiments. However, my primary concern lies with the limited scope of the experimental evaluation. The comparison is conducted on a single dataset, which appears to have received relatively little prior attention. Furthermore, the direct comparisons are limited to other RNN-based models, neglecting potentially relevant alternatives such as hierarchical Hidden Markov Models – methods capable of tackling similar tasks. While Table 1 acknowledges other approaches, their evaluation is performed on a different corpus, rendering direct comparison impossible. 

The paper’s contribution primarily resides in its detailed analysis of *how* the network functions, an analysis which is performed effectively. However, this focus on mechanistic understanding differs from the paper’s stated goal of presenting a new architecture with improved performance. The proposed deep architecture successfully combines hierarchical feature learning with time-series modeling, and the model is well-analyzed, but the results remain somewhat inconclusive due to the limited experimental validation.
