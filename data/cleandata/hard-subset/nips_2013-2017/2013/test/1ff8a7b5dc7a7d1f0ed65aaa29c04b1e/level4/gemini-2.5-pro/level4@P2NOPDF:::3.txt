This paper introduces a Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN) architecture, which combines the temporal processing of RNNs with the hierarchical feature extraction of deep networks. The authors apply this model to text prediction and compression tasks, demonstrating strong performance compared to existing methods. A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to a series of experiments designed to argue that higher layers in the DRNN learn more abstract and long-term dependencies in the data.

The paper is well-written, intellectually honest in acknowledging its own limitations, and presents a compelling motivation. The core idea—that in a standard RNN, older inputs benefit from more levels of processing than recent ones, and that a deep architecture can mitigate this—is insightful. The experiments designed to probe the function of each layer, particularly the text generation results in Table 2, provide persuasive initial evidence that the layers indeed specialize in different temporal scales and levels of abstraction.

While the work is promising, I have several concerns and suggestions, primarily regarding the experimental validation of the architecture.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Fairness of Experimental Comparison:** My primary concern lies with the empirical evaluation. The baseline standard RNN appears to have significantly fewer parameters than the proposed DRNN models. This disparity in model capacity makes it difficult to conclude that the performance gains are due to the deep architecture itself, rather than simply a larger parameter count. The paper's claims would be substantially strengthened by comparing the DRNN against a standard (single-layer) RNN with a comparable number of parameters or, ideally, a similar number of hidden units (which would involve a much wider single layer). A direct comparison between a multi-layer DRNN and a parameter-matched wide RNN is crucial for validating the architectural benefits of depth.

2.  **Demonstrating the Value of Depth:** This leads to a more fundamental question that the paper raises but does not fully resolve: do the higher layers learn long-term, abstract features *because* they are deep, or is this an emergent property that would also appear in a sufficiently large, shallow network? The current experiments show a correlation between depth and function, but a stronger causal link has not been established. The most significant missing piece of the empirical puzzle is whether the higher-layer units truly benefit from the preceding layers' processing in a non-trivial way. A compelling experiment would be to demonstrate that these units perform their function better at the top of the hierarchy than they would if they were simply added to the first layer.

3.  **Systematic Study of Depth:** It would be more informative to see a systematic study of performance as a function of depth. For example, instead of the analysis presented in Figure 2, I would recommend showing results from DRNNs of various depths (e.g., 1 to N layers) trained from scratch on the benchmark tasks. To ensure a fair comparison across depths, the layer widths could be adjusted to maintain a consistent total number of parameters or units. This would provide a clearer picture of the trade-offs between depth and width in this architecture.

**Questions and Additional Suggestions:**

1.  **Gradient Normalization:** The paper mentions using gradient normalization to "avoid the problem of bifurcations." Could the authors elaborate on the importance of this technique for their results? How precisely does it prevent bifurcations? A recent ICML 2013 paper from the University of Montreal on gradient clipping and optimization in RNNs may be relevant here. Additionally, does normalizing the gradient norm to a fixed value interfere with the adaptive step-sizing implicit in SGD's averaging behavior?

2.  **Alternative Architectures:** As another way to add depth and non-linearity, have the authors considered adding hidden layers *between* timesteps within a single recurrent layer? It would be interesting to know how this compares to the proposed vertically-stacked architecture.

3.  **Missing Comparison:** For the Wikipedia compression tasks, the results should be compared to relevant prior work, such as that by Mikolov et al., to better contextualize the performance.

4.  **Interpretation of Analyses:** Regarding the "brain surgery" experiments designed to isolate layer functions, I appreciate the authors' own acknowledgement (line 241) that interpreting such interventions is difficult due to the complex, learned interdependencies between layers. This is a crucial point of caution that rightly highlights the challenge of interpreting these complex models.

**Minor Points:**

*   The terms DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO are used in Figure 1 but are not defined in the main text. They should be explicitly defined for clarity.

In summary, this is a thought-provoking paper that explores an interesting and important class of models. The analysis of the learned representations is a valuable contribution. However, to be a strong candidate for publication, the central claims about the benefits of depth need to be supported by more rigorous and fair empirical comparisons against appropriately sized baseline models.
