I largely concur with the authors’ response regarding the single-task focus.

However, exploring truncated backpropagation, for instance with a length of 30 steps, would still be valuable. While it might be less effective at learning parenthesis balancing, verifying this and assessing its performance in terms of entropy at a reduced computational cost would be worthwhile.

This work investigates the capabilities of deep recurrent neural networks, arriving at notable conclusions: maintaining a constant parameter count, deeper RNNs surpass standard RNNs in performance; a compelling analysis reveals that deeper networks capture more “long-term information”; and the study demonstrates that standard BPTT can successfully train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses – a feat previously attributed to hierarchical reinforcement learning. 

The claim of introducing the “deep RNN” is inaccurate, as this architecture has been previously utilized, for example, in Graves et al. (2013) [http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf], and likely earlier. The paper’s strength lies in its analysis, and it is perfectly acceptable to focus on analysis without introducing novel architectures.

While the analysis is insightful, its impact would be amplified by extending it to additional tasks, such as a speech-based problem. This would help to determine whether the observed findings are specific to the current task or represent a more general phenomenon. 

Furthermore, the full hierarchical reinforcement learning experiments appear computationally expensive. Given the success of truncated backpropagation – introduced in the 1990s and effectively employed by Mikolov (as detailed in his PhD thesis) for training high-performing RNN language models – it is plausible that truncated BPTT would yield good results here as well. Confirmation of this would be beneficial. 

In summary, this work offers an interesting analysis of deep RNNs. The key findings are: 1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs with a fixed parameter budget in character-level language modeling; 2) deeper layers demonstrate greater long-range dependency capture; and 3) truncated BPTT can effectively train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses. While the results are interesting, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a similar analysis applied to a speech task, demonstrating the benefits of deeper RNNs and the presence of long-range information in their deeper layers.
