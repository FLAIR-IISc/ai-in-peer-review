

The paper presents an interesting exploration of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) for text prediction, with compelling evidence that higher layers process more abstract, long-term structures. The core insight—that older data benefits from deeper processing—resonates well, and the proposed DRNN architecture appears a reasonable approach for handling shorter-term dependencies. However, several critical points warrant clarification and additional validation to strengthen the claims.

First, the role of gradient normalization requires deeper justification. The authors state it "avoids the problem of bifurcations," but this term is unclear without context. A relevant ICML 2013 paper by Gulcehre et al. (University of Montreal) examines gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs, which may offer useful parallels. Crucially, normalizing gradients could disrupt SGD’s inherent averaging behavior, potentially biasing convergence. The authors should explicitly address this risk.

Second, the comparison to prior work on Wikipedia compression (e.g., Mikolov et al.) is essential for contextualizing results. Without this benchmark, the paper’s claims about DRNN superiority lack full empirical grounding.

The most persuasive experiment is the text generation analysis (Table 2), though the authors rightly acknowledge interpretational challenges: "brain surgery" interventions (e.g., layer disconnection) may misattribute effects due to complex inter-layer dependencies. This caveat is well noted but underscores a key limitation in causal inference.

**The paper’s most significant gap is the untested hypothesis: Do higher layers *necessarily* leverage deeper processing for abstraction, or do they merely adopt roles *after* training due to architectural constraints?** The observation that higher layers "appear" more abstract is suggestive but insufficient. To prove the mechanism, the authors must demonstrate that *repositioning* units from higher to lower layers (e.g., moving top-layer units to the first layer) degrades long-term abstraction, while keeping the same parameter count. Current experiments only compare DRNNs to standard RNNs with fewer parameters (2767²×5 vs. 2119²), which unfairly advantages the DRNN. A fairer comparison would use a standard RNN with identical total parameters or units (e.g., a 2-layer DRNN vs. a single-layer RNN with matching width). This would decisively test whether depth *itself* enables better abstraction or merely increases capacity.

Furthermore, Figure 2’s comparison of DRNN depths is limited. To better validate depth’s role, the authors should show results for DRNNs trained from scratch across multiple depths, with wider layers (to match parameter counts) rather than fixed width. This would clarify whether depth, not just capacity, drives performance gains.

Minor points: Define DRNN-AO and IO in the main text (not just Figure 1). The description of DRNNs as "deep networks with recurrent connections at each layer" is accurate but could be tightened for clarity.

Overall, the paper is well-written, intellectually honest, and offers valuable insights into deep temporal networks. The experimental design is thorough, but the parameter fairness issue and untested causal mechanism represent critical omissions. Addressing these would significantly bolster the contribution. The work is a worthwhile step toward understanding how depth shapes temporal representations, and the authors’ self-awareness about limitations is commendable. With the suggested revisions, this could become a strong NIPS contribution.
