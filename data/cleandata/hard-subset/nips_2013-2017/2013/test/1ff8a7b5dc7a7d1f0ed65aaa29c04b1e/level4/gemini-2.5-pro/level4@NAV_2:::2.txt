This paper investigates the properties of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), an architecture where multiple RNN layers are stacked hierarchically, with each layer receiving the hidden state of the layer below as its input. The central hypothesis is that this structure facilitates a temporal hierarchy, allowing the network to process information across multiple time scales. The authors evaluate this architecture on a character-level language modeling task using a large Wikipedia corpus, providing an in-depth analysis of the network's internal dynamics. This work builds upon previous uses of deep or stacked RNNs, such as in speech recognition (Graves et al., 2013), but focuses specifically on analyzing the emergent hierarchical properties and their benefits for a complex sequential modeling task.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper provides strong empirical evidence that, for a fixed number of parameters, a deep RNN outperforms a standard (shallow) RNN on the character-level language modeling task, achieving state-of-the-art results with a relatively simple stochastic gradient descent training procedure.
*   The analysis of the network's internal dynamics is a key contribution. The experiments demonstrating that higher layers learn longer time scales and that different layers contribute distinctively to the final prediction provide valuable insight into *why* this architecture is effective.
*   A particularly interesting finding is that the DRNN, trained with standard backpropagation through time, can successfully learn long-range dependencies, such as balancing parentheses over hundreds of characters. This challenges previous assumptions that such tasks required more powerful optimization methods like Hessian-Free optimization.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity of Contribution:** The paper's introduction could be interpreted as claiming the DRNN architecture itself as a novel contribution. However, stacked RNNs have been utilized in prior work (e.g., Graves et al., ICASSP 2013). The authors should carefully reframe their contribution to emphasize that the novelty lies in the detailed analysis and demonstration of the emergent temporal hierarchy, rather than the architecture itself.
*   **Generality of Findings:** The analysis is compelling but is confined to a single task (character-level language modeling). The conclusions about the benefits of DRNNs would be significantly strengthened if they were validated on a problem from a different domain, such as speech recognition, to show that the observed properties are not an artifact of the specific task.
*   **Comparison of Training Methods:** The paper contrasts its training method with Hessian-Free optimization but overlooks a more common and computationally efficient baseline: truncated backpropagation through time (BPTT). As shown in prior work on RNN language models (e.g., Mikolov's thesis), truncated BPTT is highly effective. Including a comparison would clarify whether full BPTT is necessary to achieve the reported performance or if a less expensive, truncated approach would suffice. It would be particularly interesting to see if truncated BPTT can also solve the parenthesis balancing task.
