This paper investigates a deep recurrent neural network (DRNN), a hybrid of deep and recurrent neural networks, where each layer incorporates recurrent connections operating through time. The authors demonstrate strong performance on text prediction/compression, achieving state-of-the-art results for recurrent networks on a Wikipedia corpus. A significant portion of the paper focuses on experiments designed to understand how different layers learn to represent varying levels of abstraction and long-term dependencies in the data. The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and exhibits commendable intellectual honesty in acknowledging potential limitations.

The core idea – questioning the necessity of extensive memory depth in traditional RNNs and proposing a hierarchical approach – is insightful. The observation that older data doesn't necessarily *require* more processing steps than newer data is a compelling motivation for exploring hierarchical architectures. This work builds upon previous efforts in hierarchical RNNs [3, 5, 6, 8], but provides a more in-depth analysis of the resulting time scales and layer functionality. The approach of stacking RNNs to create a hierarchy is a natural extension of deep learning principles to the temporal domain. 

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The DRNN achieves state-of-the-art performance on a challenging text prediction task, surpassing existing recurrent network approaches, even those utilizing more advanced training algorithms [19].
* **Novel Architecture:** The DRNN architecture provides a principled way to incorporate hierarchical processing into recurrent networks, offering a potential advantage over traditional RNNs for tasks with multi-scale temporal dependencies.
* **Detailed Analysis:** The authors conduct a series of experiments to investigate the role of different layers, including analysis of time scales (Figure 3) and the impact of removing layer contributions during text generation (Table 2). The text generation experiment, while acknowledging its limitations, is particularly persuasive.
* **Clear Writing & Honesty:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors are candid about potential weaknesses and avenues for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Gradient Normalization:** The importance of gradient normalization is not fully explained. The authors mention avoiding "bifurcations" without providing sufficient detail. The connection to the work on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs [ICML 2013, U of Montreal researchers] should be discussed.  The potential impact of gradient normalization on the averaging behavior of SGD also warrants consideration.
* **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to the work of Mikolov et al. on language modeling and related compression tasks.
* **Abstraction vs. Layer Position:** While the experiments suggest that higher layers capture more abstract and long-term properties, a crucial piece of evidence is missing: demonstrating that these properties are a *result* of the layer's position in the hierarchy, and not simply due to the units themselves. It’s unclear whether the same units, if moved to an earlier layer, would exhibit similar behavior.
* **Parameter Count Disparity:** The comparison to the standard RNN suffers from a disparity in parameter counts. The RNN has fewer parameters than the DRNNs, potentially biasing the results. A fairer comparison would involve either matching the number of parameters or, better yet, the number of units.  Exploring the performance of different DRNN depths with appropriately sized layers (e.g., wider layers in shallower DRNNs to maintain parameter count) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Missing Ablation Study:**  Instead of Figure 2 showing the performance *reduction* by removing layer contributions, a more informative experiment would be to train DRNNs of varying depths from scratch and compare their performance on the benchmark tasks.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and well-motivated architecture (DRNN) that achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging task.
* The detailed analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of deep recurrent networks and the emergence of hierarchical time scales.
* The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature and are forthcoming about the limitations of their work.
* The paper offers a significant contribution to the field of recurrent neural networks and has the potential to inspire further research.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a direct comparison with a standard RNN with a comparable number of parameters weakens the claim of superior performance.
* The absence of an experiment demonstrating that the learned abstractions are a function of layer position, and not simply inherent to the units, leaves a crucial question unanswered.
* The explanation of gradient normalization is insufficient and needs further elaboration.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel approach and compelling results. While the concerns regarding parameter count and the abstraction experiment are valid, they do not fundamentally undermine the contribution of the work. I believe the paper warrants acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses in a response or future revision.  I rate this paper as **Strong Accept** pending minor revisions.
