The authors propose a novel deep architecture that integrates the hierarchical representation of deep learning with time-series modeling, drawing inspiration from Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) and recurrent neural networks. The training algorithm constructs the network layer-by-layer, utilizing supervised pre-training with a next-letter prediction objective. The experimental results demonstrate improved performance on a Wikipedia dataset published by Hinton et al. after training large networks for approximately 10 days, surpassing previous work. The authors then delve into an analysis of the network's task-solving approach, revealing insights such as the modeling of long-term dependencies in higher layers and the representation of correspondence between opening and closing parentheses as a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state".

The paper is well-written, with well-designed experiments and figures that effectively support the findings. However, my primary concern lies in the experimental evaluation. The comparison is limited to a single dataset, which appears to have received relatively little attention in the literature. Furthermore, the comparison is only made with other RNN-related models, omitting potentially more common hierarchical HMMs and other approaches that can tackle similar tasks (although Table 1 lists alternative methods, they are evaluated on a different corpus, rendering them incomparable). 

The paper's contribution lies more in its analysis of how the network operates, which is examined in depth, rather than in its achievements. While the paper aims to propose a new deep architecture combining hierarchical features of deep learning with time-series modeling, the results are somewhat inconclusive. The paper's strengths lie in its clear writing, thorough model analysis, and well-structured experiments, but the findings could be more comprehensive.
