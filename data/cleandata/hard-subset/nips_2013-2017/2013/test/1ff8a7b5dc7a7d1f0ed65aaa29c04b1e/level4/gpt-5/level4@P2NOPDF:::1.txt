This paper introduces a deep architecture that fuses hierarchical feature learning with time-series modeling in the spirit of HMMs and recurrent neural networks. The training procedure builds the network layer by layer using supervised pretraining on a next-character prediction task. Experiments show that, after roughly 10 days of training very large models, performance on the Wikipedia dataset used by Hinton et al. surpasses prior results. The authors then offer a detailed analysis of the model’s behavior, demonstrating, for example, that higher layers capture long-range dependencies and that matching parentheses are represented through quasi-stable, attractor-like dynamics.

Overall, the paper is of high quality. It is clearly written, the experimental setup is sound, and the figures effectively support the claims. My main concern lies with the empirical evaluation. The comparison is limited to a single dataset that has seen relatively little prior use, which constrains the scope of the conclusions. Direct comparisons are made only to other RNN-style approaches, rather than to more classical hierarchical HMMs or related probabilistic sequence models that could address similar tasks. Although Table 1 cites additional methods, they are evaluated on a different corpus, making the results not directly comparable.

In my view, the paper’s primary contribution is the insightful analysis of how the network solves the problem, which is indeed thorough and informative. However, the paper is positioned as presenting a new deep architecture that integrates hierarchical learning with temporal modeling, and the empirical results, while promising, are not sufficiently broad to definitively substantiate the claimed advantages. The writing is strong and the internal analyses are compelling, but the results remain somewhat inconclusive given the limited benchmark coverage and baseline diversity.
