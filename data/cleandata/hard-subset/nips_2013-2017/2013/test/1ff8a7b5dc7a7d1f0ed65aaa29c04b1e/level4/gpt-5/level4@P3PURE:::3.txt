This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs)—hybrids of deep and recurrent architectures in which each layer has recurrent connections operating through time—and demonstrates strong text prediction/compression performance compared to existing approaches. A large part of the paper presents experiments aimed at showing that higher layers capture more abstract, longer-term structure. These experiments are generally convincing, though I have reservations noted below and would like to see a couple of additional comparisons. The topic—deep temporal networks and how their learned representations function—is important, and the paper is clearly written and intellectually honest about potential issues and limitations, which I especially appreciate.

I particularly like the observation that, in standard RNNs, older inputs can receive many more layers of processing than newer inputs in an arbitrary way. Building a deeper RNN is a reasonable way to improve shorter-term modeling while bringing other potential benefits. Along those lines, another straightforward extension would be to insert additional nonlinear transformations between successive timesteps (i.e., per-time-step depth). Have you tried this?

How important was gradient normalization in your results, and by what mechanism does it “avoid the problem of bifurcations”? There is a recent ICML 2013 paper from U. Montreal on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs that may be relevant here. Also, does normalizing gradients in this manner risk interfering with the “averaging” behavior of SGD?

Regarding prior work on Wikipedia compression tasks, it would be useful to compare against results by Mikolov et al.

Of the experiments probing the roles of different layers in terms of time scale and possible abstraction, the text generation experiment (Table 2) is the most persuasive. However, as you note around line 241, interpreting the effects of this kind of “brain surgery” on RNNs is tricky because of complex interdependencies among layers’ outputs that can arise during training.

For me, the biggest missing empirical piece is whether higher layers are actually better at capturing more abstract, long-term properties than if the same units were housed in the first layer—i.e., whether they benefit in a nontrivial way from the additional levels of processing that precede them. The fact that higher layers appear to assume longer-term or more abstract roles after training is suggestive but incomplete evidence for such a claim.

On experimental fairness, the parameter counts across models appear mismatched. For example, under a rough count, comparing something like 2767^2 to 5 × 2119^2 (and, in both cases, counting both recurrent and inter-layer weight matrices, effectively doubling each term) suggests the comparison might be unfair. A more convincing evaluation would match models by total parameters or, better yet, by the number of units. For instance, compare a two-layer DRNN against a standard single-layer RNN with the same number of units, arranged so that differences in parameter count do not unduly favor the RNN. Demonstrating improvements under such matched settings would substantially strengthen the paper’s claims.

Relatedly, instead of (or in addition to) Figure 2, it would be more informative to report how various DRNN depths perform on the benchmarks when trained from scratch, possibly widening shallower models to compensate for differences in parameter counts and/or units.
