The authors propose a new deep architecture that combines deep learning classification with time-series modeling obtained from HMMs or recurrent neural networks. The proposed training algorithm uses supervised (pre-) training to build a layer-by-layer network for the purpose of predicting the next character. Experiments show that after training very large networks for about 10 days, the network's performance improved compared to previous work on Wikipedia datasets published by Hinton et al. The authors then provide a detailed analysis and discussion of how the network performs its task. For example, long-term dependencies are modeled at a high level, the relationship between the opening and closing of parentheses is modeled as a "pseudo-stable attracter-like state."

The paper is of good quality. It is well-written, the experiments are well-established, and the figures support the results well. My main problem with the paper is the experiments. The comparison is only done on one dataset, where it does not seem that much work has been done. Direct comparison is only with other RNN-related models, possibly not with more general hierarchical HMMs, etc., which are capable of solving similar tasks (Table 1 lists other methods, but in a different corpus, which makes them incomparable).

The paper's contribution is more in the analysis of how the network works (which is quite well analyzed) than in what it achieves, but not what the paper intended to do. A new deep architecture has been proposed, which combines the features of deep learning classification with time-series modeling. The paper is well-written, the model is well-analyzed, but the results are somewhat unclear.
