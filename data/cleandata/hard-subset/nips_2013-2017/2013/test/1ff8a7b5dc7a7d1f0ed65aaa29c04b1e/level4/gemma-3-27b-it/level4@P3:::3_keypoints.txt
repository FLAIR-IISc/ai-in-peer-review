- Core idea: Proposes a Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN) that stacks recurrent layers to form a hierarchical temporal processing architecture, challenging the need for deep memory in standard RNNs.  
- Empirical result: Achieves state-of-the-art performance on Wikipedia text prediction/compression, outperforming prior recurrent approaches.  
- Novelty: DRNN offers a principled way to incorporate hierarchical, multi-scale temporal dependencies into recurrent networks.  
- Analysis: Provides detailed experiments probing layer roles and time scales (Figure 3) and layer-removal effects on text generation (Table 2).  
- Writing: Paper is clearly written and candid about limitations and future directions.  
- Weakness — gradient normalization: Explanation of gradient normalization and its relation to bifurcations, gradient truncation, and SGD averaging is insufficient.  
- Weakness — related work comparison: Needs a more thorough comparison to Mikolov et al. and other language-modeling/compression work.  
- Weakness — abstraction vs. layer position: Lacks an experiment showing abstractions arise due to layer position rather than intrinsic unit properties.  
- Weakness — parameter count disparity: Comparison to standard RNNs is biased by unequal parameter counts; should match parameters or units and test varying depths with adjusted widths.  
- Weakness — missing ablation: Should train DRNNs of varying depths from scratch instead of only measuring performance reduction by removing layer contributions.  
- Argument for acceptance: Novel, well-motivated architecture with strong results and insightful analysis that advances understanding of deep recurrent networks.  
- Argument against acceptance: Key methodological gaps (parameter-matched baselines, positional-abstraction evidence, gradient-normalization explanation) weaken the claims.  
- Overall assessment: Strong paper deserving acceptance (rated Strong Accept) conditional on addressing the noted weaknesses in revision.
