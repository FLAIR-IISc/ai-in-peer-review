This paper investigates the properties of deep Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and presents several interesting findings. The core conclusions are: 1) when controlling for the number of parameters, deep RNNs outperform their shallower counterparts on character-level language modeling; 2) an analysis reveals that deeper layers in the network capture more "long-term information"; and 3) contrary to previous assumptions that required methods like Hessian-Free (HF) optimization, standard Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT) is sufficient to train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses.

While the analysis is valuable, the paper has several areas that require significant improvement:

First, the manuscript claims to introduce the deep RNN architecture. This claim is inaccurate, as stacked or deep RNNs have been utilized in prior work, for instance, in Graves et al. (ICASSP 2013) and likely even earlier. The authors should revise their contribution to focus on the novelty of their *analysis* of these models, not the introduction of the architecture itself. Presenting a rigorous analysis of an existing architecture is a valid and welcome contribution.

Second, the experimental validation is confined to a single problem domain. While the results are meaningful, their generality is limited. The findings could potentially be an artifact of the specific character-level language modeling task. To strengthen the paper's conclusions, the analysis should be replicated on at least one other distinct problem, such as a speech recognition task. This would help demonstrate that the observed benefits of depth and the properties of the hidden layers are more fundamental characteristics of the models.

Finally, the comparison of training methods is incomplete. The authors show that BPTT can succeed where it was previously thought HF was necessary, but they omit a crucial and widely used baseline: truncated Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT). This approach has been successfully used to train high-performing RNN language models (e.g., Mikolov's thesis) and is far more computationally efficient than full BPTT or HF. A thorough comparison should include truncated BPTT. It would be particularly insightful to evaluate its performance; for example, using a truncation of 30 steps might not solve the parenthesis balancing task, but it could still achieve competitive entropy scores at a substantially lower computational cost, which would be an important result to report.
