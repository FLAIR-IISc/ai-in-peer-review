This paper presents an analysis of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) and their application to character-level language modeling. The authors propose a hierarchical architecture, where each layer is a recurrent neural network that receives the hidden state of the previous layer as input. This allows for hierarchical processing of time series data and captures the structure of time series in a more natural way.

The paper builds upon previous work on recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and deep neural networks (DNNs). The authors argue that common RNNs do not explicitly accommodate temporal hierarchies, and most research has focused on training algorithms rather than the basic architecture. They also mention that some hierarchical architectures employing RNNs have been proposed in the past, but these have been limited to artificial tasks.

The authors' main contribution is the analysis of the DRNN architecture and its application to character-level language modeling. They show that DRNNs can reach state-of-the-art performance for recurrent networks on a Wikipedia-based text corpus, even when trained with simple stochastic gradient descent (SGD). They also provide an analysis of the different emergent time scales in the DRNNs.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their architecture and experiments. The results are interesting, and the analysis of the emergent time scales in the DRNNs is particularly insightful. However, I do have some concerns about the novelty of the architecture and the generality of the results.

Firstly, the authors claim to introduce the deep RNN architecture, but this architecture has been used before in other papers. For example, Graves et al. (2013) proposed a similar architecture for speech recognition. Therefore, the authors should not claim to have introduced this architecture.

Secondly, the analysis is limited to a single task, namely character-level language modeling. While the results are interesting, it is unclear whether they generalize to other tasks, such as speech recognition or image classification. To strengthen the paper, the authors could provide a similar analysis on another task, such as speech recognition.

Finally, the authors mention that they used plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) to train the DRNNs, but they do not compare their results to other training methods, such as truncated BPTT or Hessian-free optimization. Truncated BPTT, in particular, has been shown to be effective for training RNNs, and it would be interesting to see how it compares to plain BPTT on this task.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their architecture and experiments. The results are interesting, and the analysis of the emergent time scales in the DRNNs is particularly insightful. However, I do have some concerns about the novelty of the architecture and the generality of the results.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their architecture and experiments. The results are presented in a clear and concise manner, and the figures and tables are well-organized and easy to understand.

In terms of originality, the paper does not introduce a new architecture, but rather provides an analysis of an existing architecture. However, the analysis is insightful, and the results are interesting.

In terms of significance, the paper provides a thorough analysis of the DRNN architecture and its application to character-level language modeling.
