This paper introduces a novel deep architecture that integrates the hierarchical structure of deep learning with time-series modeling techniques, such as Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) or Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). The proposed training algorithm constructs the network layer-by-layer, employing a supervised pre-training scheme based on a next-character prediction objective. On a Wikipedia dataset previously used by Hinton et al., the experiments show that a very large version of the network, after approximately 10 days of training, achieves improved performance over prior work. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the network's internal mechanisms, demonstrating, for example, that higher layers learn long-term dependencies and that matching parentheses are modeled via a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state."

The paper is well-written, the experiments are methodologically sound, and the figures effectively support the findings. My primary concern, however, lies with the experimental evaluation. The empirical comparison is confined to a single dataset that does not appear to be a widely-used benchmark. Furthermore, the direct performance comparison is limited to other RNN-based models, excluding other relevant model classes like hierarchical HMMs that can solve similar tasks. While Table 1 lists results from other approaches, they are on a different corpus, which prevents a direct comparison.

Ultimately, the paper's main contribution lies more in its insightful analysis of *how* the network operates—which is very well done—than in its performance achievements. This emphasis on analysis seems to diverge from the paper's stated goal of proposing a new state-of-the-art architecture. In summary, while the paper presents a novel model and a compelling analysis of its behavior, the empirical results are inconclusive due to the limited scope of the experiments.
