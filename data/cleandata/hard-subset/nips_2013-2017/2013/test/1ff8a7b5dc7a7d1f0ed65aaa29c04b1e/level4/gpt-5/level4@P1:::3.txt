Summary
This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs): stacked recurrent layers in which each layer receives the previous layer’s hidden state as input. Conceptually, they combine deep hierarchies with temporal recurrence, aiming to induce multiple time scales and hierarchical processing of sequences. The authors evaluate DRNNs on character-level language modeling (Wikipedia), showing state-of-the-art performance for recurrent models using only SGD, and provide analyses suggesting emergent temporal hierarchies across layers. A substantial part of the paper investigates whether higher layers capture more abstract and longer-term structure. These analyses are interesting and often convincing, and the paper is clearly written and commendably candid about limitations.

Strengths and main comments
- I appreciate the argument about the arbitrariness of older inputs effectively receiving more “processing” in standard RNNs simply because they pass through more unrolled steps, without an architectural rationale that older inputs need more computation than newer ones. Stacking recurrent layers is a reasonable architectural response that could improve shorter-term processing while allowing distinct time scales to emerge.
- An obvious baseline to consider is adding extra nonlinear layers between timesteps (i.e., deeper transition functions per time step without stacking separate recurrent layers). Have you tried architectures that add additional nonlinear transformations within a single time step of a standard RNN?
- The text generation ablation (Table 2) is, to me, the most persuasive piece of evidence about layer roles: removing the bottom-layer output contribution quickly destabilizes text, while removing the top-layer contribution yields sequences with reasonable local statistics but lacking longer-range coherence. As you note (line ~241), interpreting “brain surgery” ablations is tricky because layer outputs can be highly interdependent (e.g., compensatory biases, synergies). Still, this experiment provides meaningful qualitative support for the intended hierarchy.
- The temporal-scale analyses (typo perturbations, context switches) are useful. The different time constants learned across layers—especially the short time scales in lower layers and longer ones in higher layers for DRNN-AO—align with the architectural hypothesis. Your observation that DRNNs recover more slowly from context switches than a single-layer RNN suggests longer effective context use.

Methodology and optimization questions
- How crucial was gradient normalization in practice, and by how much did it affect stability and final performance? The claim that it “avoids the problem of bifurcations” could use elaboration: what specific bifurcations or parameter jumps were observed without normalization? There is a recent ICML 2013 paper from the Montreal group on gradient truncation/optimization in RNNs that seems relevant; please discuss how your approach relates and whether similar stability issues arise here.
- Does normalizing gradients in this way interfere with the averaging behavior of SGD (e.g., step-size adaptation relative to gradient magnitude)? Some discussion or empirical evidence would strengthen the case for this heuristic.
- In terms of prior work on Wikipedia compression/text prediction, it would be helpful to compare against relevant results by Mikolov et al., in addition to MRNN and PAQ.

Fairness of comparisons and suggested experiments
- The standard RNN baseline appears to have substantially fewer parameters than the DRNNs (e.g., 2119^2 vs. multiple stacked layers, counting both recurrent and inter-layer matrices). This may make the comparison unfair. A stronger case would be to show DRNNs outperform:
  1) a single-layer RNN with the same number of total parameters, and ideally
  2) a single-layer RNN with the same number of units (to decouple depth from capacity).
  For instance, compare a 2-layer DRNN to a single-layer RNN with the same unit count, where parameter count differences do not disadvantage the RNN so heavily that the comparison becomes moot.
- Relatedly, instead of Figure 2, it would be more compelling to report benchmark performance for various DRNN depths trained from scratch, possibly widening shallower models to match either total parameter count or number of units. This would directly test whether depth per se is the source of the gains, beyond capacity.

Overall assessment
- The paper makes a solid case that stacked recurrent architectures can achieve strong results with simple SGD and that a temporal hierarchy emerges across layers. The analyses are thoughtful, and the text generation ablations are particularly illustrative. My main reservation is the fairness of the baseline and the missing experiment that directly tests whether higher layers truly benefit from preceding layers in a nontrivial way (e.g., moving those units to the bottom layer and comparing). Addressing this would significantly strengthen the claim that higher layers capture more abstract, longer-term properties because of depth, not just capacity or training dynamics.

Minor comments
- Define DRNN-AO and DRNN-1O explicitly in the text, not only in Figure 1.
- Please include a comparison to Mikolov’s Wikipedia language modeling/compression results where applicable.
