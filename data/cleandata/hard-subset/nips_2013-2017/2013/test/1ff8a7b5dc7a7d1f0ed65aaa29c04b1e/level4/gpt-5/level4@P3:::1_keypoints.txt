- Studies stacked deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) with two output variants: DRNN-1O (top-layer output) and DRNN-AO (all-layer outputs).
- Trains models with SGD using gradient normalization and a linear learning-rate schedule; DRNN-1O uses incremental layer-wise supervised training while optimizing full network.
- Evaluated on large Wikipedia character-level language modeling; BPC comparable to best reported RNN results (MRNNs trained with Hessian-free) at similar parameter counts.
- Provides in-depth analyses: layer-output ablations, perturbation/typo tests, context-switch tests, generative experiments, and parentheses long-distance dependency tests.
- Finds evidence of emergent hierarchical temporal scales, with higher layers capturing longer-term structure.
- Demonstrates that simple SGD with gradient normalization can train large stacked RNNs to competitive performance without second-order methods.
- Architecture, training setup, and hyperparameters are clearly presented with supportive figures.
- Relation to prior work: builds on hierarchical/staked RNN literature (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio, stacked LSTMs), differing mainly by using SGD and providing systematic time-scale analyses.
- Strength: thorough empirical and interpretive analyses beyond headline performance.
- Strength: convincing evidence for layer-dependent time scales and differences between DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO tied to training procedures.
- Strength: practical relevance in showing competitive performance with simpler optimization.
- Weakness: limited experimental scope—single dataset, few model variants, and no variance estimates across seeds.
- Weakness: narrow baseline coverage—no LSTM baseline on same corpus and missing non-neural baselines, complicating Table 1 interpretation.
- Weakness: architectural novelty is incremental; main novelty lies in analysis rather than architecture.
- Weakness: several training choices lack justification or ablation (output weight init, gradient normalization vs clipping, incremental pretraining, equal layer widths).
- Weakness: comparability concerns due to different character vocabularies (95 vs. 86 chars) relative to prior work.
- Quality: technically sound and careful analyses, but limited baselines and dataset coverage constrain claims of state-of-the-art.
- Clarity: well written and organized; requests clarity on gradient normalization vs clipping, validation protocol, and vocabulary impact.
- Originality: moderate — prior hierarchical RNN work exists; contribution mainly empirical/analytical.
- Significance: analysis is valuable and practically informative, but impact limited by constrained comparisons and single dataset.
- Key suggestions: add LSTM baseline, ablations for gradient normalization and incremental training, report error bars, reconcile vocab differences, and test on an additional corpus.
- Pros for acceptance: insightful analyses, competitive results with simple training, clear writing and reproducibility.
- Cons for acceptance: limited baselines/single dataset, incremental novelty, and some ad hoc/unablated training choices.
- Overall assessment: solid, insightful paper focused on analysis of stacked RNNs; borderline to weak accept pending stronger baselines and comparability.
