

The paper presents an interesting exploration of deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) for text prediction/compression, with a compelling argument regarding the arbitrariness of older data benefiting from deeper processing compared to newer data. The authors' proposal to build a deep RNN for improved short-term modeling is reasonable and well-motivated. However, the authors should consider whether adding extra layers of nonlinearities between timesteps (beyond the standard DRNN architecture) could further enhance performance, as this is a straightforward extension worth investigating.

The role of gradient normalization requires further clarification. Specifically, why does the proposed normalization "avoid the problem of bifurcations," and how does it interact with the averaging behavior of SGD? A relevant recent ICML 2013 paper by researchers from the University of Montreal on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs may provide useful context for this discussion.

For benchmarking, the authors should compare their results to Mikolov et al.'s prior work on Wikipedia compression tasks, as this establishes a critical baseline for the field.

Among the experiments examining layer roles in time-scale and abstraction, the text generation results (Table 2) are the most persuasive. However, as acknowledged by the authors on line 241, interpreting the effects of "brain surgery" (e.g., layer removal) is problematic due to complex interdependencies developed between layer outputs during training.

The most significant omission in the empirical analysis is the lack of evidence that higher layers genuinely benefit from their position in the network hierarchy. Merely observing that higher layers process more abstract/long-term properties after training is insufficient; the authors must demonstrate that these layers would not achieve equivalent performance if relocated to earlier positions. This would establish whether the benefit stems from the nontrivial sequence of processing rather than emergent properties.

The parameter count comparison between the standard RNN (2767² × 5 parameters, counting both recurrent and inter-layer weight matrices) and the DRNN (2119² parameters) appears unfair, as the standard RNN has significantly more capacity. A more convincing comparison would use DRNNs with the same total number of parameters or the same number of units (e.g., a 2-layer DRNN versus a standard RNN with identical unit count). This would eliminate the advantage of the standard RNN's larger parameter space and strengthen the paper's claims.

Additionally, Figure 2 would be more informative if it showed performance across multiple DRNN depths trained from scratch, with wider layers compensating for parameter differences relative to shallower models.

Minor: Define DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO in the main text, not solely in Figure 1. The paper's focus on DRNNs—a hybrid of deep and recurrent networks with recurrent connections at each layer—provides valuable insight into their functioning for text prediction. While the series of experiments arguing for higher layers' role in processing abstract/long-term structures is largely convincing, the above reservations necessitate additional analysis. The work is intellectually honest, thoroughly addresses limitations, and is well-written, making it a worthwhile contribution to the field.
