I appreciate the point about how older data might benefit from more layers of processing than newer data. Building a deep RNN seems like a reasonable approach to improve performance on shorter-term dependencies, and potentially offers other benefits as well. Another straightforward extension would be to add more nonlinearities between each timestep – have you considered this?

How crucial were the gradient normalizations, and could you elaborate on why this prevents “bifurcations”? A recent ICML 2013 paper from researchers at the University of Montreal explores gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs, which might be relevant to your work. Furthermore, could normalizing the gradient in this way potentially disrupt the averaging behavior of SGD? 

Regarding previous results on Wikipedia compression tasks, the work by Mikolov et al. is also worth comparing against. 

Of the experiments designed to investigate the different roles of each layer – specifically in terms of timescale and abstraction – I find the text generation experiment (Table 2) most persuasive. However, as the authors acknowledge (lines 240-241), interpreting the effects of this type of “brain surgery” on RNNs can be problematic due to the complex interdependencies that likely develop between layer outputs.

For me, the biggest empirical question remaining is whether the higher layers genuinely process more abstract and long-term properties of the text better than if those units were positioned in the first layer. Demonstrating that they *take on* these roles after training is a good start, but isn't conclusive evidence that they benefit from the preceding layers in a meaningful way.

I noticed that the regular RNN appears to have fewer parameters in these experiments (2767^2 * 2 > 2119^2 * 2 – I'm considering both recurrent and interlayer weight matrices), potentially making the comparison somewhat unfair. A more compelling demonstration would be if the deeper RNNs outperformed a standard RNN with the *same* number of parameters, or even better, the same number of units. For example, a two-layer DRNN compared to a standard RNN with an equivalent number of units, where the parameter difference doesn't unduly favor the DRNN. This would significantly strengthen the paper’s claims.

Additionally, instead of Figure 2, it would be more informative to see how various depths of DRNN perform on the benchmarks when trained from scratch, perhaps with wider layers in the deeper networks to compensate for differences in the number of parameters or units.

- Please define DRNN-AO and IO within the text, and not only in Figure 1. This paper investigates a hybrid of deep and recurrent neural networks called DRNNs, which are similar to deep networks but incorporate recurrent connections at each layer to process temporal data. The authors demonstrate that this architecture performs well on text prediction/compression tasks compared to existing methods. A substantial portion of the paper is dedicated to experiments aimed at demonstrating that higher layers process and represent more abstract and long-term structures in the data. These experiments are quite convincing, though I have a few reservations detailed below, and I would like to see a couple of additional experiments. I believe that exploring these kinds of deep temporal networks and gaining insights into their function after training is worthwhile. The paper is also well-written, intellectually honest, and thorough in acknowledging its own potential limitations – a quality I particularly appreciate.
