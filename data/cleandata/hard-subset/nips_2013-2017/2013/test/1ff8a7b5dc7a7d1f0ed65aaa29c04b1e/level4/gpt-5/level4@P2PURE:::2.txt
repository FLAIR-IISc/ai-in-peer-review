REPLY TO REBUTTAL:
- I largely agree with the one-task comment.
- It would still be worthwhile to try truncated backpropagation (e.g., 30 steps). While it is less likely to learn to balance parentheses (this should be verified), it may still achieve competitive entropies at a fraction of the cost.

This paper investigates the usefulness of deep recurrent neural networks. It reaches interesting conclusions: when the number of parameters is held constant, deeper recurrent networks outperform standard RNNs; an analysis suggests that deeper networks retain more long-term information; and plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, which was previously thought to be possible only with HF.

However, the paper claims to introduce the deep RNN architecture, which has been used before (e.g., Graves, ICASSP 2013: http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf), and likely earlier. The paper should therefore not claim to have introduced this architecture. It is perfectly acceptable not to propose new architectures and to focus on analysis.

The analysis, while meaningful, would be even more convincing if demonstrated on several problems—perhaps something in speech—otherwise the findings may be artifacts of the specific task.

Finally, the HF experiments were likely too expensive. Truncated backpropagation (introduced in the 1990s) was successfully used by Mikolov (see his PhD thesis) to train RNNs that are excellent language models. It is therefore likely that truncated BPTT would perform well here too, and it would be valuable to confirm this.

This work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs. The three main results are: 1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs when the number of parameters is fixed on character-level language modeling; 2) the deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure; and 3) truncated BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses. The results are interesting, but given that this is primarily an analysis, the paper would be much stronger if it included a similar analysis on a speech task (e.g., showing that deeper RNNs do better and that their deeper layers contain more long-range information).
