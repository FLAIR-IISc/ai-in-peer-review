- Authors' observation that older data may be more processed is valuable; using a deep RNN to help short-term tasks is reasonable.
- Suggest exploring additional nonlinearities between time steps as an alternative strategy.
- The role of gradient normalization and its connection to avoiding "bifurcations" is unclear.
- Cite ICML 2013 U. of Montreal work on gradient truncation/optimization in RNNs as relevant.
- Concern that normalizing gradients might interfere with SGD's averaging behavior.
- Recommend comparing to Mikolov et al.'s work on Wikipedia compression.
- Text generation experiment (Table 2) is the most convincing evidence, but surgical layer analyses are hard to interpret due to interdependencies.
- Key empirical question: do higher layers genuinely capture more abstract/long-term properties, or would relocating those units to the first layer produce similar results?
- Current comparisons may be unfair because the regular RNN has fewer parameters; need parameter- or unit-matched baselines.
- Propose showing DRNN performance across varying depths trained from scratch, with wider layers to equalize parameters/units, instead of Figure 2.
- Minor: define DRNN-AO and IO in the text, not only in Figure 1.
- Overall: paper is well-written, explores a promising hybrid deep-recurrent architecture effective for text prediction/compression, and is candid about limitations.
