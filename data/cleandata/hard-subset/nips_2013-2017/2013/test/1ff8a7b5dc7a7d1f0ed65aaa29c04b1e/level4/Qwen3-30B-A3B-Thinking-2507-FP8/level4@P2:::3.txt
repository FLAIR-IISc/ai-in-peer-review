

This paper introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), a novel architecture where each layer is a recurrent network that processes information from the hidden state of the previous layer, creating a hierarchical structure for temporal processing. The authors demonstrate that this architecture effectively captures multiple time scales present in time series data, which standard RNNs implicitly encode in their dynamics. The work builds on prior hierarchical RNN approaches [3,5,6,8] but offers a more systematic analysis of the temporal hierarchy and its relationship to the task, particularly character-level language modeling. The paper makes a compelling case that DRNNs naturally embed multiple time scales in their structure, allowing them to model long-term dependencies more effectively than standard RNNs.

The paper excels in quality, with thorough experimental validation across multiple metrics. The authors demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on character-level language modeling using simple stochastic gradient descent, matching results previously achieved only with more complex Hessian-free optimization. The experimental design is sound, with careful attention to hyperparameter selection and training protocols. The analysis of time scales through perturbation experiments (Figure 3) and text generation tests (Table 2) provides valuable insights into how different layers process information at varying time scales. The paper is technically rigorous and transparent about limitations, including the need for careful initialization and the potential parameter count disparities in comparisons.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the experiments. The authors effectively use figures to illustrate key concepts, though they should define acronyms like DRNN-AO and DRNN-1O earlier in the text rather than relying solely on Figure 1. The writing is accessible and maintains a professional tone throughout.

The originality of this work is moderate. While the DRNN architecture itself is novel, it builds directly on [8], which used stacked bi-directional LSTMs for similar purposes. The paper's main contribution lies in the systematic analysis of time scales across layers and demonstrating that this architecture achieves competitive results with simpler training methods. However, the paper could better distinguish itself from [8] by more explicitly addressing the differences in their architectural approach and the additional insights provided by the time-scale analysis.

The significance of this work is notable. The demonstration that hierarchical processing of time series through stacked RNNs can achieve state-of-the-art results with simple training methods has practical implications for the field. The insights into how different layers capture varying time scales provide valuable theoretical understanding of temporal processing in deep networks. This work contributes to the ongoing discussion about whether simpler optimization methods (like SGD) can achieve results comparable to more complex second-order methods, which has important implications for large-scale deep learning applications.

I have three main concerns that would strengthen the paper:

First, the comparison between DRNNs and standard RNNs is potentially unfair due to parameter count differences. The standard RNN has 2119 units (with 2119^2 parameters for recurrent weights), while the DRNN-1O has 727 units per layer across 5 layers (with approximately 5*727^2 parameters). A more convincing comparison would use architectures with comparable total parameters or comparable number of units, such as a 2-layer DRNN versus a standard RNN with the same number of units.

Second, the paper would benefit from additional experiments to address whether higher layers are truly benefiting from hierarchical processing. Specifically, it would be valuable to compare a DRNN with a standard RNN where the same units have been moved to different positions in the network, to determine whether the hierarchical arrangement itself provides an advantage beyond simply having more layers.

Third, the paper should more thoroughly address the recent work by Mikolov et al. on language modeling, which provides a relevant benchmark for character-level language modeling results. The authors should clarify how their results compare to this line of work.

The text generation experiments (Table 2) are particularly convincing, showing how the bottom layer is crucial for basic text statistics while the top layer captures longer-term patterns. However, the authors are appropriately cautious about interpreting the "brain surgery" experiments (removing layer outputs), acknowledging the complex interdependencies between layers.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to understanding temporal processing in deep networks. The work is technically sound, well-executed, and provides meaningful insights into how hierarchical RNNs process information at different time scales. With the suggested revisions to address parameter count comparisons and clarify the relationship to prior work, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
