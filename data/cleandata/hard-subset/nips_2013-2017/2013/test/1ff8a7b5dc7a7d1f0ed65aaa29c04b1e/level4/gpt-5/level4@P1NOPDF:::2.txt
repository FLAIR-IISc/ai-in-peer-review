REPLY TO REBUTTAL:
- I largely agree with the single-task concern.
- It would still be worthwhile to try truncated backpropagation through time (e.g., 30 steps). While it is less likely to learn to balance parentheses (worth verifying), it may achieve competitive entropies at a fraction of the computational cost.

This paper investigates the usefulness of deep recurrent neural networks (RNNs). It presents several interesting findings: (1) when the number of parameters is held constant, deeper RNNs outperform standard (single-layer) RNNs on character-level language modeling; (2) analyses suggest that deeper layers capture more long-term information; and (3) plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, a result previously thought to require HF optimization.

However, the paper claims to introduce the deep RNN architecture, which is not accurate. Deep/stacked RNNs have been used before (e.g., Graves, ICASSP 2013: http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf), and likely even earlier. The paper should avoid claiming architectural novelty. It is perfectly fine to focus on empirical and analytical contributions without introducing a new architecture.

The analysis would be more convincing if demonstrated on multiple tasks, not just character-level language modeling. Showing similar behavior on a speech task, for example, would help establish that the observed advantages of depth and the concentration of long-range information in higher layers are not artifacts of a single dataset or domain.

Finally, the HF experiments may be unnecessarily expensive. Truncated backpropagation through time (introduced in the 1990s) was successfully used by Mikolov (see his PhD thesis) to train RNNs that are excellent language models. It is therefore likely that truncated BPTT would also perform well here, at much lower cost. It would be valuable to include experiments confirming this, even if truncated BPTT does not learn to balance parentheses. As noted above, trying a truncation length around 30 steps could be a good starting point: it may not capture the longest dependencies required for parentheses balancing, but it could still yield competitive entropies on the language modeling task while greatly reducing training time.

Overall, this is an interesting and useful analysis of deep RNNs. The empirical result that deeper RNNs outperform shallower ones under a fixed parameter budget is compelling, and the layer-wise analysis of long-range information is insightful. The clarification that plain BPTT can learn parentheses balancing (contrary to prior assumptions favoring HF) is also noteworthy. The paper would be strengthened by (a) removing claims of architectural novelty, (b) extending the analysis to at least one additional task (ideally in speech), and (c) including a careful comparison to truncated BPTT to assess accuracy–cost trade-offs.
