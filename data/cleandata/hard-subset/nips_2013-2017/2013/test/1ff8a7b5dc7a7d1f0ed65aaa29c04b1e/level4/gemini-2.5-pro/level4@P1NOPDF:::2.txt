This paper presents an interesting analysis of deep Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). The main contributions are: 1) demonstrating that deep RNNs can outperform standard RNNs on character-level language modeling when the number of parameters is held constant; 2) showing through analysis that the deeper layers of these networks appear to capture more long-term information; and 3) finding that standard Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT) can successfully train an RNN to solve a parenthesis balancing task, which was previously thought to require more powerful optimizers like Hessian-Free (HF).

The conclusions are compelling. The finding that depth can be more beneficial than width for a fixed parameter budget is a valuable contribution. Likewise, the analysis showing that deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure provides useful insight into the function of these networks.

However, the paper has a few weaknesses that should be addressed.

First, the paper's claim to have introduced the deep RNN architecture is inaccurate. This architecture has been used before, for example, in Graves et al. (ICASSP 2013) and likely even earlier. The authors should revise this claim and instead frame their work as a focused analysis of this existing architecture. Performing a detailed analysis is a perfectly valid and useful contribution that does not require inventing a new architecture.

Second, the analysis, while meaningful, is based on a single task. This makes it difficult to ascertain whether the findings are a general property of deep RNNs or an artifact of the specific character-level language modeling problem. The paper would be significantly stronger if it included a similar analysis on a different problem domain, such as speech recognition, to show that the benefits of depth and the long-range information in deeper layers are more broadly applicable.

Finally, the reliance on the computationally expensive Hessian-Free (HF) optimizer is a limitation. It would be valuable to include experiments using truncated backpropagation through time (BPTT), a well-established and efficient method for training RNNs, as demonstrated in the work of Mikolov and others. It is likely that truncated BPTT would also perform well on the language modeling task, and confirming this would provide a crucial baseline, clarifying whether the observed performance gains are dependent on the use of a second-order optimization method.
