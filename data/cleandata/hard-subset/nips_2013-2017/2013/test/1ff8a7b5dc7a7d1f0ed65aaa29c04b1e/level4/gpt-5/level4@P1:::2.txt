REPLY TO REBUTTAL:
- I mostly agree with the single‐task concern: showing similar analyses beyond character-level language modeling (e.g., speech) would strengthen the claims and reduce the risk that the findings are dataset-specific.
- It would still be worth trying truncated backpropagation through time (e.g., 30 steps). It may be less likely to learn to balance parentheses (worth verifying), but it could achieve competitive entropy at a fraction of the computational cost.

Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), formed by stacking recurrent layers so that each layer receives the hidden state of the previous one. Two output variants are considered: output only from the top layer (DRNN-1O) and output from all layers (DRNN-AO). On Wikipedia character-level language modeling, DRNNs trained with SGD achieve state-of-the-art performance among recurrent models with similar parameter counts, and the paper provides analyses suggesting that deeper layers capture longer time scales. The authors also show that plain BPTT suffices to learn long-range dependencies such as matching parentheses, previously reported primarily with Hessian-free (HF) training.

While the paper presents the architecture clearly, stacked/“deep” RNNs are not new (e.g., Graves et al., ICASSP 2013; see also earlier hierarchical RNN work). The contribution here is not architectural novelty but an empirical and analytical study of stacked RNNs on a large-scale character-level task.

Strengths:
- Empirical result: Holding the number of parameters approximately constant, deeper RNNs outperform a single-layer RNN on character-level language modeling.
- Analysis: Multiple, well-thought-out probes (layer ablations, typo perturbations, context switch tests) indicate that deeper layers encode longer time scales and that a temporal hierarchy emerges through training.
- Practical insight: Plain BPTT with SGD trains models that capture long-range structures such as parentheses, narrowing the perceived gap with HF in this setting.
- Careful experimental setup: Comparable parameter budgets across models and transparent training details.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty: The paper should not claim to introduce the deep/stacked RNN architecture; it has been used before (e.g., stacked LSTMs for speech). Positioning the work as an analysis would better reflect the contribution.
- Scope: All analyses are on a single dataset/task. Demonstrating similar behavior on another domain (e.g., speech) would make the conclusions more robust.
- Baselines: The comparison to HF is interesting but likely overestimates HF cost; more importantly, truncated BPTT—effective in prior RNN language modeling work (e.g., Mikolov’s thesis)—is not evaluated. Verifying whether truncated BPTT can approach the reported entropies would clarify the training–cost trade-off.
- Limited architectural variants: Results focus on vanilla RNNs; comparisons with LSTMs/GRUs would contextualize the observed time-scale hierarchy.

Quality:
The experimental methodology is sound and clearly described. The analyses convincingly support the claim that deeper layers capture longer time scales and that DRNNs leverage longer contexts for prediction than a single-layer RNN. The parentheses experiments are informative, including both diagnostic probing and generation tests with clear success criteria. The main missing piece is a systematic evaluation of truncated BPTT baselines and an additional task to validate generality.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. However, novelty claims should be softened to acknowledge prior use of stacked RNNs. The training protocol (e.g., sequence length, BPTT truncation window, gradient normalization) is detailed and reproducible.

Originality:
Architecturally limited; stacked RNNs are prior art. The originality lies in the empirical analysis on a large real-world task and the finding that plain BPTT can learn long-range structures comparable to those previously attributed to HF.

Significance:
The results are practically significant. Many practitioners face a depth–capacity trade-off; showing that deeper RNNs improve performance at fixed parameter count, and that deeper layers capture longer time scales, is valuable. Demonstrating strong results with plain BPTT lowers the barrier to entry. The significance would increase with corroboration on a second domain and with truncated BPTT baselines.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Reframe claims to emphasize analysis rather than architectural novelty; cite stacked RNN/LSTM precedents explicitly.
- Add a truncated BPTT baseline (e.g., 30 steps) to quantify the performance–cost trade-off; verify its effect on learning parentheses.
- Include a second task (e.g., speech or another sequential domain) to test whether the layerwise time-scale hierarchy and performance benefits generalize.
- If possible, include comparisons with LSTM/GRU stacks under matched parameter budgets.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear empirical evidence that depth helps at fixed parameter count.
- Insightful and carefully executed analyses revealing learned time-scale hierarchies.
- Demonstrates that plain BPTT can capture long-range dependencies on large-scale text.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Architectural novelty is limited; claims should be tempered.
- Single-task evaluation; generality not demonstrated.
- Missing truncated BPTT baselines despite strong prior evidence of their effectiveness in language modeling.

Overall, this is a solid analysis paper with useful empirical findings. With a more accurate positioning of novelty and additional baselines/experiments, it would be compelling.
