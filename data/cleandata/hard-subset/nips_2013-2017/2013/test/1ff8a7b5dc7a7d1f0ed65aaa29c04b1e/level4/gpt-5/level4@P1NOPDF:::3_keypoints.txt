- Appreciates the idea that older data can undergo more processing layers and that deep RNNs are a reasonable way to improve short-term modeling and provide other benefits.
- Suggests adding extra nonlinear layers within the per-step transition (deeper per-step transition) and asks whether this was tried.
- Asks how important gradient normalization was in practice, why it “avoids the problem of bifurcations,” and whether it might interfere with SGD’s averaging; cites a relevant ICML 2013 paper on gradient truncation/optimization.
- Recommends comparing results on Wikipedia compression/prediction against prior work by Mikolov et al.
- Finds the text-generation analysis (Table 2) the most persuasive experimental evidence.
- Warns that “brain-surgery”-style layer interventions are hard to interpret because of complex interdependencies between layer outputs.
- Identifies the main missing experiment: whether higher layers truly capture more abstract, longer-term properties beyond what same units would capture in the first layer.
- Raises fairness concerns: the plain RNN appears to have substantially fewer parameters than the DRNN, which could bias comparisons.
- Recommends stronger comparisons: show deeper RNNs outperform single-layer RNNs matched for parameter count or number of units (e.g., compare 2-layer DRNN to single-layer RNN with same units).
- Suggests reporting performance across different DRNN depths trained from scratch, using wider shallow layers to equalize parameters/units, to isolate the effect of depth.
- Minor: define DRNN-AO and DRNN-IO in the text and not only in Figure 1.
- Overall assessment: the paper presents a valuable, well-written study showing strong performance of DRNNs on text prediction/compression and provides mostly convincing evidence that higher layers learn abstract long-term structure, but additional targeted experiments are needed.
