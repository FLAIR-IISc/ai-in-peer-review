Among the various tests designed to examine the role of each layer in terms of time-scale and possibly "abstractness," the one I find most convincing is the text generation (shown in Table 2). However, the authors themselves note in line 241 that explaining the impact of such "brain surgery" on RNNs due to complex interdependencies between the outputs of different layers can be problematic.

To me, the biggest missing piece in the empirical puzzle of this paper is the question of whether higher layers are better at processing more abstract and long-term features of the text than removing units in the first layer. That is, are they benefiting from processing additional layers that move them forward in a non-trivial way? After training, they take on this seemingly more abstract and long-term role, which is good, but there is not good evidence for this phenomenon.

I noticed that these tests have fewer parameters in RNNs than in the comparison, because 2767^2 5 > 2119^2 (I am counting both the recurrent and inter-layer weight matrices, so multiplying by 2), so the comparison may be a bit unfair. It would be more convincing if the deep RNN performed better than a standard RNN with the same number of parameters or even the same number of units, or even better, the same number of units. Suppose a 2-layer DRNN versus an RNN with the same number of units, where the number of parameters will not give the RNN such an advantage that the comparison would be unfair. In my opinion, this would greatly strengthen the paper's claim.

This paper discusses a hybrid of deep and recurrent neural networks called DRNNs, which are like deep networks but have recurrent connections at each layer that work over time. The authors show that such an architecture can work very well for text prediction/compression compared to existing methods. A large part of this paper is dedicated to continuous testing, which argues that higher layers are processing/presenting more abstract and long-term structures of the data. These tests are quite convincing, but I have a few objections, which are detailed below my entire review, and I would like to see a few more tests. I think it is worth looking at such deep temporal networks and getting an idea of how they work after training. The paper is also easy to read, and seems quite intellectually honest and thorough about its own potential problems and flaws, which I especially appreciate.
