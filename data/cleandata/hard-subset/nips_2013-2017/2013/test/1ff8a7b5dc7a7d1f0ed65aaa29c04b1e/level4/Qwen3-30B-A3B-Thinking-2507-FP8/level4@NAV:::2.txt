

I largely concur with the point regarding limited task scope. It would be valuable to test truncated backpropagation (e.g., 30 steps), as it may achieve competitive entropies at reduced computational cost despite being less likely to learn parentheses balancing. The paper presents an interesting analysis: 1) deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs with fixed parameter count in character-level language modeling; 2) deeper layers capture more long-term information; and 3) truncated BPTT trains RNNs to balance parentheses. However, the claim of introducing "deep RNNs" is inaccurate, as this architecture has been used previously (e.g., Graves et al., ICASSP 2013). The analysis would gain greater significance if extended to diverse tasks, such as speech recognition, to confirm findings are not artifact-specific. Additionally, the HF experiments appear unnecessarily costly; truncated BPTT (established since the 1990s and successfully applied by Mikolov for language modeling) likely suffices and merits validation.
