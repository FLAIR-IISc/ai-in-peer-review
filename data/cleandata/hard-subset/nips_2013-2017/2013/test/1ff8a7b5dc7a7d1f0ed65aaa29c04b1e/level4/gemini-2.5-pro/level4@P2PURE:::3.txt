This paper introduces a Deep Recurrent Neural Network (DRNN), which combines the hierarchical processing of deep networks with the temporal modeling of recurrent networks. The authors demonstrate that this architecture achieves strong results on text prediction and compression tasks. A significant portion of the paper is dedicated to a series of experiments arguing that higher layers in the network learn to represent more abstract and longer-term structures in the data. The work is valuable for exploring these deep temporal architectures and providing insight into their internal workings. The paper is well-written, clear, and intellectually honest about its own potential shortcomings. While the experiments are insightful, I have several reservations and suggestions for additional experiments that would strengthen the claims, as detailed below.

The paper makes a compelling point that in a deep recurrent architecture, older data points benefit from more layers of non-linear processing than more recent ones. This architecture seems like a promising approach for improving performance on short-term dependencies, among other potential benefits. A related architecture would be to add extra non-linear layers between timesteps within a single recurrent layer. Have the authors experimented with such a model?

Regarding methodology, could the authors elaborate on the importance of the gradient normalization technique? Specifically, how does it help to "avoid the problem of bifurcations"? A recent ICML 2013 paper from the University of Montreal on gradient clipping and RNN optimization might be relevant here. Furthermore, does this normalization scheme interfere with the implicit averaging behavior of Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)?

For the Wikipedia compression task, a comparison to previous results from Mikolov et al. would strengthen the paper's empirical evaluation.

The experiments designed to analyze the roles of different layers are insightful. The text generation results (Table 2) provide the most persuasive evidence for the hypothesis that deeper layers capture longer-term, more abstract structures. However, as the authors commendably note, interpreting these "lesioning" experiments is challenging due to the complex interdependencies that can develop between layers during training.

The main empirical question left unanswered is whether the hierarchical structure itself is crucial. Are the higher layers better at processing long-term, abstract properties specifically *because* they build upon the representations of lower layers? The current results demonstrate that deeper layers learn these roles, but this is incomplete evidence that depth is the causal factor. An essential control experiment would be to show that these units are less effective at modeling long-term properties if they were instead added to the first layer, thereby creating a wider but shallower network. Showing that depth provides a non-trivial benefit over simply adding more capacity to the first layer is critical to the paper's central claim.

Furthermore, the comparison between the DRNN and the standard RNN may not be entirely fair due to a potential mismatch in the number of parameters. To make a more convincing case for the benefits of depth, the authors should compare their DRNNs against a standard RNN with a comparable number of parameters, or even better, the same total number of hidden units. For example, comparing a 2-layer DRNN against a single-layer RNN with the same number of units would provide a more direct test of the architectural benefits. Such a comparison would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

Instead of the analysis in Figure 2, a more valuable experiment would be to present benchmark results for DRNNs of varying depths (e.g., 2, 3, 4 layers), each trained from scratch. To ensure a fair comparison, the layer widths for shallower models could be increased to control for the total number of parameters or units.
