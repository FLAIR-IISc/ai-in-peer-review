This paper investigates the effect of a hierarchy of recurrent neural networks (DRNNs) on processing time series data, specifically focusing on character-level language modeling. The authors propose an architecture where each layer is a recurrent network receiving the hidden state of the previous layer as input, allowing for hierarchical processing and the capture of temporal structure. They demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on a Wikipedia-based text corpus using simple stochastic gradient descent and provide an analysis of the emergent time scales within the network.

The core idea—questioning the necessity of indefinite memory depth in standard RNNs and proposing a hierarchical structure for temporal processing—is compelling. The authors correctly identify that common RNNs don’t explicitly accommodate temporal hierarchies present in many time series, and the DRNN architecture offers a natural way to address this. The observation that stacking RNNs could create different time scales at different levels is a key insight. Relating this work to the broader context of deep learning, the authors appropriately highlight the analogy to deep neural networks with hierarchical feature extraction. The paper builds on previous work in hierarchical RNNs [3, 5, 6] and stacked LSTMs [8], going further by providing a more in-depth analysis of the network architecture’s impact.

A key strength of this work is the empirical demonstration of state-of-the-art performance without relying on advanced training algorithms like Hessian-free optimization [19], achieving comparable results with simpler SGD. The detailed analysis of emergent time scales and the experiments on text generation, particularly Table 2, provide valuable insights into the network's behavior. The authors are refreshingly candid about the limitations of interpreting "brain surgery" experiments on RNNs due to complex interdependencies. Furthermore, the acknowledgement of potential issues and careful consideration of the experimental setup contribute to the paper’s credibility. The writing is generally clear and well-organized, though some sections could benefit from minor adjustments for conciseness.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the authors motivate the DRNN architecture by pointing out the arbitrary advantage of older data having more processing layers, the connection between this motivation and the architecture’s design is not fully explored. It would be beneficial to consider the inclusion of additional non-linearities between timesteps, and to discuss whether that was tested. The effect of gradient normalization and its relation to "bifurcations" needs further elaboration. The authors mention a relevant paper on gradient truncation from ICML 2013, and including a discussion of it would strengthen their analysis. Also, comparisons to related work by Mikolov et al. on Wikipedia compression would be useful.

A significant missing element is a more direct assessment of whether the higher layers genuinely process more abstract and long-term properties of the text than if the units were positioned in the first layer. Demonstrating that the hierarchical structure *causes* the abstraction, rather than merely observing it, would significantly bolster the claims. The comparison between the DRNNs and the standard RNN is slightly skewed due to the differing number of parameters. A fairer comparison would involve models with a similar number of parameters or units, perhaps a two-layer DRNN versus a standard RNN with a comparable unit count. Including a graph showing the performance of various depths of DRNNs trained from scratch, potentially with layer width adjustments to maintain consistent parameter counts, would be very informative.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   State-of-the-art performance on a challenging benchmark using a relatively simple training method.
*   Compelling motivation and a well-defined architecture for hierarchical temporal processing.
*   Detailed analysis of emergent time scales and network behavior.
*   Intellectual honesty and a thorough discussion of limitations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The link between the initial motivation and the architectural design could be strengthened.
*   The comparison to standard RNNs is not entirely fair due to parameter differences.
*   A more definitive demonstration of the higher layers’ abstraction capabilities is needed.
*   Missing comparisons to relevant work by Mikolov et al.




In summary, this is a valuable contribution to the field of recurrent neural networks. The DRNN architecture is a promising approach to capturing temporal hierarchies, and the experimental results provide strong evidence for its effectiveness. While some further investigations would strengthen the paper, the current work is of high quality and deserves consideration for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
