- Proposes Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs): hierarchical RNN layers that receive previous-layer hidden states to capture temporal hierarchies.
- Claims DRNNs naturally develop different time scales across layers and shows analysis supporting this.
- Demonstrates strong character-level language modeling results on Wikipedia using simple SGD, reporting state-of-the-art for recurrent networks.
- Builds on prior hierarchical RNN and stacked LSTM work but lacks full contextualization with more recent NIPS papers.
- Strength: Clear theoretical argument that hierarchical RNNs better capture temporal structures with layer-wise time-scale differentiation.
- Strength: Thorough experiments, using BPC and qualitative text generation to analyze layer contributions.
- Strength: Convincing temporal-dynamics analysis via typo-perturbation experiments showing emergent time-scale hierarchies.
- Strength: Well-written paper that transparently discusses limitations.
- Weakness: Comparison to standard RNNs may be unfair—DRNN has many more parameters than the baseline RNN.
- Weakness: "Brain surgery" layer-removal experiments are hard to interpret due to complex interdependencies between layers.
- Weakness: Paper insufficiently situates results within more recent text-prediction/NIPS literature (e.g., omissions of [18,21]).
- Weakness: Gradient normalization technique is asserted without theoretical or empirical justification and lacks connection to recent work on gradient truncation.
- Weakness: Claim of state-of-the-art for recurrent networks isn't convincingly compared against advanced training methods like Hessian-free optimization.
- Pro-acceptance: Theoretical contribution to understanding hierarchical temporal modeling is sound and novel.
- Pro-acceptance: Experimental analysis of emergent time scales is novel, thorough, and well-executed.
- Con-acceptance: Unfair parameter comparison, limited interpretability of layer analyses, and weak methodological justification undermine the paper’s claims.
- Recommendation: Paper is strong and valuable but needs fairer baseline comparisons, better justification of gradient methods, and stronger situating within recent literature before acceptance.
