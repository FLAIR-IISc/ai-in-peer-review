- Positive insight: older data may benefit from more processing layers; deep RNNs are reasonable for modeling shorter-term dependencies and other benefits.  
- Suggestion: investigate adding further nonlinearities between timesteps.  
- Methodological concern: gradient normalization and its mechanism for avoiding bifurcations need further explanation.  
- Reference suggestion: consider the ICML 2013 Montreal work on gradient truncation/optimization in RNNs as relevant.  
- Question: could gradient normalization disrupt the averaging effect of SGD?  
- Prior work: include a comparison to Mikolov et al. on Wikipedia compression.  
- Experimental strength: text generation results (Table 2) are the most compelling evidence.  
- Interpretation caveat: interpreting layer-manipulation effects is challenging due to complex interdependencies between layers.  
- Key empirical gap: need to show higher layers truly process abstract/long-term properties better than if those units were placed in the first layer.  
- Fairness concern: standard RNN has fewer parameters in experiments, introducing potential unfairness.  
- Suggested stronger evaluation: compare deeper RNNs to standard RNNs with comparable parameter counts or the same number of units (e.g., 2-layer DRNN vs equivalent-unit standard RNN).  
- Figure suggestion: provide a plot of DRNN performance vs depth trained from scratch, controlling for parameter/unit count (wider shallow models).  
- Minor request: define DRNN-AO and IO in the text (not just Figure 1).  
- Overall verdict: worthwhile, well-written, intellectually honest investigation with useful insights, though additional experiments and clarifications would strengthen the paper.
