The paper introduces a deep recurrent architecture that merges the hierarchical representation benefits of deep networks with the temporal modeling capabilities of HMMs/RNNs. The training procedure incrementally stacks layers, using supervised (pre)training on a next-character prediction objective. Empirically, the authors train very large models for roughly ten days and report improved performance on a Wikipedia dataset previously used in related work. They further provide an insightful examination of how the model solves the task: higher layers tend to encode longer-range dependencies, and opening/closing parentheses are handled via what the authors describe as a “pseudo-stable attractor-like state.”

Overall, the work is of solid quality. The writing is clear, the experimental protocol is sensible, and the figures effectively illustrate the claims. My principal reservation concerns the empirical evaluation: it relies on a single dataset that appears to have limited prior benchmarks, and the direct comparisons are confined to RNN-family baselines rather than including more classical hierarchical models (e.g., hierarchical HMMs) that can address similar problems. While Table 1 mentions other techniques, they are evaluated on different corpora, which weakens the comparability.

The paper’s main value lies in its analysis of how the proposed network operates (which is done convincingly) rather than in unambiguous gains over alternatives, yet the framing emphasizes the introduction of a new deep architecture combining hierarchical representation learning with temporal modeling. In sum, the manuscript is well written and the model behavior is thoughtfully examined, but the empirical conclusions are somewhat equivocal.
