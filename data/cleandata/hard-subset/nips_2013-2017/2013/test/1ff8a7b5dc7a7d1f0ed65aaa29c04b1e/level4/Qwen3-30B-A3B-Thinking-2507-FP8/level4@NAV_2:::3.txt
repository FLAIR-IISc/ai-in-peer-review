

I appreciate the paper's insight regarding the arbitrary assumption that older data benefits from more processing layers compared to newer data. The proposed deep RNN architecture appears reasonable for enhancing short-term processing capabilities alongside other potential advantages. An interesting extension would be to explore adding extra nonlinear layers between timesteps; have you considered this approach?

Regarding the gradient normalization technique, I'm curious about its significance in avoiding bifurcation issues and whether it might disrupt the averaging behavior of stochastic gradient descent. The recent ICML 2013 work by researchers from the University of Montreal on gradient truncation in RNNs could be relevant to this discussion.

For benchmark comparisons, it would be valuable to include results from Mikolov et al.'s work on similar Wikipedia compression tasks.

Among the experiments examining layer roles in time-scale processing, the text generation analysis (Table 2) is particularly convincing. However, as acknowledged on line 241, interpreting the effects of "brain surgery" experiments is challenging due to potential complex interdependencies between layer outputs.

The most significant gap in the empirical analysis concerns whether higher layers genuinely process more abstract, long-term text properties than if those units were placed in earlier layers. While it's encouraging that higher layers assume these roles after training, this observation alone doesn't conclusively demonstrate that they benefit from the specific hierarchical processing structure.

I note that the standard RNN in the experiments has fewer parameters (2119 units) compared to the DRNN-1O (727 units per layer across 5 layers), which may create an uneven comparison. A more compelling demonstration would compare DRNNs with standard RNNs having equivalent numbers of parameters or units (e.g., a 2-layer DRNN versus an RNN with the same total units), which would strengthen the paper's claims significantly.

Additionally, Figure 2 would be more informative if it showed how different DRNN depths perform on benchmarks when trained from scratch, potentially with wider layers to compensate for parameter differences.

Minor note: The paper should define DRNN-AO and DRNN-1O in the main text rather than relying solely on Figure 1.

Overall, this paper examines a promising hybrid of deep and recurrent networks called DRNNs, which incorporate temporal feedback at each layer. The authors demonstrate strong performance on text prediction tasks and provide compelling evidence of hierarchical time-scale processing. The paper is well-written, intellectually honest about limitations, and thoroughly addresses potential shortcomings. I believe this work is valuable for understanding how deep temporal networks function after training.
