This paper investigates the usefulness of deep recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and presents interesting conclusions: holding the number of parameters constant, deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs on a character-level language modeling task. Further interesting findings include an analysis demonstrating that deeper networks capture more “long-term information,” and the surprising result that plain Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT) can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses – a feat previously thought to require more sophisticated methods like Highway Networks (HF).

However, the paper’s claim of introducing the “deep RNN” is inaccurate. Deep RNNs, or stacked RNNs, have been used previously; for example, Graves *et al.* (ICASSP 2013, http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf) employed them, and likely earlier work exists as well. The authors should retract this claim and instead position the work as a valuable analysis of an existing architecture. Focusing on analysis is perfectly acceptable, and indeed strengthens the contribution if presented accurately.

The analysis presented *is* meaningful, but its scope limits the generalizability of the findings. Expanding this analysis to additional problems, such as a speech recognition or generation task, would significantly strengthen the paper. The current results might be an artifact of the specific character-level language modeling problem, and demonstrating similar benefits on a different task would lend more credence to the conclusions about the advantages of depth.

Regarding the experimental methodology, the full BPTT experiments, especially those involving Highway Networks, appear computationally expensive. Truncated Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT), introduced in the 1990s, has been successfully employed by Mikolov (as discussed in his PhD thesis) to train RNNs to achieve excellent language modeling performance. It is highly probable that truncated BPTT could yield competitive results in this work as well. Verifying this expectation would be a valuable addition, potentially at a fraction of the computational cost. I agree with the authors’ response to the previous review regarding the single-task focus, and suggest that exploring truncated BPTT with, for example, 30 steps would be a worthwhile investigation. While it might be less likely to successfully learn to balance parentheses, it could still achieve comparable entropies with significantly reduced computational demands.

In summary, this work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs. The key findings are: 1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs when the number of parameters is fixed for character-level language modeling; 2) deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure; and 3) plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses. While the results are interesting, the paper would be substantially improved by expanding the analysis to a different domain, such as speech, to demonstrate the robustness of the observed benefits of increased depth and long-range information capture. Additionally, clarifying the novelty (or lack thereof) of the architecture and exploring truncated BPTT would strengthen the experimental validation and efficiency of the work.
