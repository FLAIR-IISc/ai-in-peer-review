**Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates the properties and effectiveness of deep Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs). The authors present several interesting conclusions based on experiments with character-level language modeling:

1.  When the total number of parameters is held constant, deep RNNs outperform standard, shallower RNNs.
2.  An analysis of the hidden states suggests that deeper layers in the network learn representations that capture more long-range dependencies or "long-term information."
3.  The paper demonstrates that standard Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT) is sufficient to train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, a task previously thought to require more complex methods like Hessian-Free (HF) optimization.

**Strengths**

The paper provides a valuable and insightful analysis of deep RNNs. The central findings are significant: the demonstration that depth can be more beneficial than width for a fixed parameter budget is an important contribution. The analysis showing that deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure provides compelling evidence for a hierarchical processing of temporal information in these networks. Furthermore, the result that BPTT can solve the parenthesis balancing task is noteworthy, as it corrects a common misconception in the field.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the analysis is meaningful, the paper has three primary weaknesses that should be addressed to strengthen its impact.

1.  **Claim of Novelty:** A major concern is the paper's claim of introducing the deep RNN architecture. This architecture has been explored in prior work (e.g., Graves et al., ICASSP 2013) and potentially earlier. The authors should revise these claims to accurately position their contribution, which is the valuable analysis of these models, not the invention of the architecture itself. Focusing on the empirical investigation is a perfectly valid and strong contribution.

2.  **Limited Generalizability:** The empirical conclusions are drawn from a single task domain: character-level language modeling. While the results on this task are interesting, their generalizability is uncertain. The observed benefits of depth could be an artifact of this specific problem. The paper would be substantially stronger if the analysis were replicated on a different type of sequential data, such as a speech recognition task. This would help demonstrate that the advantages of deep RNNs and the hierarchical nature of their representations are more general phenomena.

3.  **Missing Baseline for Training:** The discussion of training methods focuses on full BPTT and Hessian-Free (HF) optimization. However, it overlooks a crucial and widely-used baseline: truncated Backpropagation Through Time (truncated BPTT). This method was successfully used by Mikolov and others to train state-of-the-art RNN language models and is known for being computationally efficient. Given that the HF experiments were likely very expensive, a comparison with truncated BPTT is essential for a practical and complete analysis. It is highly probable that truncated BPTT would perform very well on the language modeling task, and including this result would provide a more grounded perspective on the appropriate training methods for these models.
