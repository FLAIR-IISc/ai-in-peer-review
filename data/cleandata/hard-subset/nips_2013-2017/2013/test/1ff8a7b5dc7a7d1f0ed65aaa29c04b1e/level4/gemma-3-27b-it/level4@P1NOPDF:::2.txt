REPLY TO REBUTTAL: 

I largely agree with the author's comment regarding the single-task setting. It would still be worthwhile to explore truncated backpropagation with, for example, 30 steps. While it’s less likely to learn to balance parentheses, verifying this would be beneficial. Furthermore, it might still achieve competitive entropies at a significantly reduced computational cost.

This paper investigates the usefulness of deep recurrent neural networks and reaches several interesting conclusions. Specifically, the authors demonstrate that, when the number of parameters is held constant, deeper recurrent neural networks outperform standard RNNs. Other notable findings include an analysis showing that deeper networks capture more “long-term information,” and the surprising result that plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses – a feat previously believed to require hierarchical reinforcement learning (HF). 

However, the paper claims to *introduce* the deep RNN, despite the fact that this architecture has been used previously. For instance, it appears in Graves et al. (http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf), and likely in earlier work as well. The paper should therefore refrain from claiming novelty in the architecture itself, and instead focus on the value of its analysis.

The analysis is meaningful, but would be significantly strengthened by application to multiple problems, perhaps including a speech task. Otherwise, the findings risk being an artifact of the specific problem domain.

Finally, the HF experiments appear computationally expensive. The truncated backpropagation approach, introduced in the 1990s, was successfully employed by Mikolov (see his PhD thesis) to train RNNs into excellent language models. It is therefore likely that truncated BPTT would also perform well in this context, and it would be valuable to confirm this experimentally. 

In summary, this work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs. The two main results are: 1) deep RNNs outperform standard RNNs when the number of parameters is fixed in character-level language modeling; 2) the deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure; and 3) truncated BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses. The results are interesting, but given that this work primarily constitutes an analysis, the paper would be considerably stronger if it included a similar analysis on a speech task – for example, demonstrating that deeper RNNs perform better and that their deeper layers also capture more long-range information.
