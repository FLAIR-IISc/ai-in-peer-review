This paper introduces Deep Recurrent Neural Networks (DRNNs), a hybrid of deep and recurrent architectures, and demonstrates their effectiveness for text prediction and compression tasks. The core idea – that older data may benefit from more layers of processing than newer data – is compelling, and employing a deep RNN structure to capture short-term dependencies alongside potential broader benefits seems reasonable. I wonder if the authors have explored adding more nonlinearities between timesteps, as this could be a straightforward extension.

The gradient normalization technique is crucial to the approach, and the explanation of how it avoids bifurcations requires further clarification. The authors should elaborate on the mechanism at play. A related work by researchers at the University of Montreal (ICML 2013) investigates gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs, and may offer relevant insights. Additionally, it's worth considering whether this gradient normalization impacts the averaging behavior typically achieved with Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD).

When contextualizing the results, a comparison to Mikolov et al.’s work on similar Wikipedia compression tasks would be beneficial. 

Among the experiments designed to analyze the temporal and abstraction roles of different layers, the text generation results (Table 2) are the most convincing. However, I agree with the authors’ acknowledgement (line 241) that interpreting the effects of layer manipulation ("brain surgery") in RNNs is difficult due to complex interdependencies between layer outputs. 

A significant empirical question remains: do higher layers *actually* process more abstract, long-term properties than if their units were simply placed in the first layer? Observing such roles emerge after training provides supporting evidence, but doesn’t definitively prove that the increased depth is causally responsible. Demonstrating a non-trivial benefit from the preceding layers is critical.

The parameter count of the regular RNN in the experiments is lower (2767^2 * 2 > 2119^2 * 2) than that of the deeper RNNs, potentially creating an unfair comparison. A more persuasive evaluation would involve comparing DRNNs to a standard RNN with an equivalent number of parameters or units. For instance, a two-layer DRNN versus a standard RNN with the same total number of units, minimizing the advantage conferred by parameter count. This would significantly strengthen the paper’s conclusions.

Furthermore, rather than Figure 2, a more informative visualization would demonstrate the performance of DRNNs with varying depths, trained from scratch. Possibly using wider layers in deeper models to account for parameter/unit differences.

Minor comments:

*   The acronyms DRNN-AO and IO should be defined in the text, not just in Figure 1.



Overall, this paper presents a worthwhile exploration of deep temporal networks, offering valuable insight into their function after training. The writing is clear, and the authors demonstrate intellectual honesty and thoroughness in acknowledging potential limitations. I appreciate these qualities and believe this work has merit.
