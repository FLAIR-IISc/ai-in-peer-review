The authors propose a novel deep architecture that integrates the hierarchical structure of deep learning with time-series modeling, akin to Hidden Markov Models (HMMs) or recurrent neural networks. The proposed training algorithm constructs the network layer-by-layer, utilizing supervised pre-training with a next-letter prediction objective. The experiments demonstrate that, after training very large networks for approximately 10 days, the network performance on a Wikipedia dataset published by Hinton et al. surpasses that of previous work. The authors then delve into an analysis and discussion of the network's approach to its task, revealing insightful details. For instance, long-term dependencies are modeled in higher layers, while the correspondence between opening and closing parentheses is captured by a "pseudo-stable attractor-like state".

The paper is of good quality, with clear writing, well-designed experiments, and figures that effectively support the findings. However, my primary concern lies in the experimental setup. The comparison is limited to a single dataset, which appears to have received relatively little attention in the literature. Furthermore, the comparison is only made with other RNN-related models, omitting potentially more common hierarchical HMMs and other approaches that can tackle similar tasks. Although Table 1 lists other methods, they are evaluated on a different corpus, rendering them incomparable.

The paper's contribution lies more in its analysis of how the network operates, which is indeed analyzed quite thoroughly, rather than in its achievements. However, this is not the primary focus of the paper, which sets out to propose a new deep architecture that combines hierarchical features of deep learning with time-series modeling. In conclusion, the paper is well-written, the model is analyzed effectively, but the results are somewhat inconclusive.
