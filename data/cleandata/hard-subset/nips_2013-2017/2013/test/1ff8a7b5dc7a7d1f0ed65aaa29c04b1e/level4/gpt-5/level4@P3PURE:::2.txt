Reply to rebuttal:
- I mostly agree with the one-task comment.
- It would still be worth trying truncated backpropagation (e.g., 30 steps). While it is less likely to learn to balance parentheses (this should be verified), it may still achieve competitive character-level entropies at a fraction of the computational cost.

This paper investigates the usefulness of deep recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and reaches several interesting conclusions. When the number of parameters is held constant, deeper RNNs outperform standard (shallow) RNNs. An analysis suggests that deeper networks retain more long-term information. The paper also reports that plain backpropagation through time (BPTT) can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, whereas it was previously believed this was only feasible with Hessian-free (HF) optimization.

However, the paper claims to introduce deep RNNs, but such architectures have been used before—for example, in Graves (ICASSP 2013; http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf) and likely earlier. The paper should not claim to have introduced this architecture. It is perfectly acceptable not to propose a new architecture and instead focus on analysis.

The analysis, while meaningful, would be more compelling if demonstrated on multiple problems—for instance, a speech task. Otherwise, the findings may be artifacts of the particular dataset or domain.

Finally, the HF experiments were likely more expensive than necessary. Truncated BPTT, introduced in the 1990s and used successfully by Mikolov (see his PhD thesis) to train strong RNN language models, would likely perform well here too; it would be valuable to confirm this empirically.

In sum, this work presents an interesting analysis of deep RNNs. The three main results are: (1) deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs when the parameter budget is fixed on character-level language modeling; (2) deeper layers exhibit more long-range structure; and (3) plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses. The results are interesting, but because the paper is primarily analytical, it would be substantially stronger if a similar analysis were conducted on a speech task (e.g., showing that deeper RNNs perform better and that their deeper layers capture more long-range information).
