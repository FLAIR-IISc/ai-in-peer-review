Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes deep recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), i.e., stacked recurrent layers where each layer is a standard RNN receiving the hidden state of the layer below. The authors study two output schemes: DRNN-1O (only the top layer connects to the softmax) and DRNN-AO (all layers connect to the softmax). They train with SGD and gradient normalization, using incremental layer-wise training for DRNN-1O. On character-level language modeling for a large Wikipedia corpus, their DRNNs achieve bits-per-character (BPC) comparable to the best published recurrent models with similar parameter counts. They also analyze layer-wise time scales via perturbation experiments, ablations during generation, and specific long-range phenomena (e.g., closing parentheses).

The work connects to earlier hierarchical RNN ideas (e.g., El Hihi & Bengio; Fernández et al.) and stacked (bi-directional) LSTMs for speech (Graves et al.). Performance comparisons are made to multiplicative RNNs trained with Hessian-free methods (Sutskever et al.). It would also be useful to relate to Mikolov et al.’s character-level models and compression-oriented results on Wikipedia-sized text.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: conventional RNN unrolled depth does not necessarily induce hierarchical processing; stacked recurrence is a natural way to introduce temporal hierarchies.
- Simple, general architecture trained with plain SGD, yet reaching strong results with comparable parameter budgets.
- Thoughtful analyses of emergent time scales and layer roles: typo perturbations, context switch effects, ablations during generation, and a focused study on parentheses.
- The paper is well written and appropriately candid about limitations and interpretability challenges.

Main questions and weaknesses
- Gradient normalization: Please clarify how crucial it is and why it “avoids bifurcations.” What do you mean operationally by bifurcations in this context (e.g., sudden dynamical regime changes due to exploding gradients)? How does your normalization relate to gradient clipping (Pascanu et al., ICML 2013) and to truncated BPTT practices? Also, does per-update normalization interfere with the averaging properties of SGD (e.g., by removing magnitude information)? A brief ablation (with and without normalization, and possibly with simple clipping) would help.
- Alternative depth within a time step: In addition to stacking layers through time, did you consider adding extra nonlinear transformations per time step (deep transition RNNs), i.e., multiple nonlinearities between t−1 and t within a layer? This could isolate whether performance gains come from depth-in-time, depth-in-state, or both.
- Interpretation of ablations: The generation experiments in Table 2 are compelling, but as you note, interdependencies among layers complicate causal interpretation. Where possible, quantify ablation effects on validation/test BPC and report confidence intervals to complement qualitative samples.
- Are higher layers uniquely useful for long-range/abstract patterns? The most substantial missing piece is a controlled test of whether higher layers add capabilities beyond what the same number of units would provide if placed in a single layer. Two concrete suggestions:
  - Parameter- and unit-matched baselines: Compare DRNNs to a single-layer RNN with either (a) the same total parameter count or (b) the same number of hidden units as the entire stack combined. This would test whether stacking confers benefits beyond capacity alone.
  - Depth sweeps from scratch: Train models of varying depth (e.g., 1, 2, 3, 5 layers) from scratch, widening shallower models to match total parameters. Reporting test BPC across depths would be more informative than Figure 2 alone.
- Fairness of the current RNN baseline: Although you state all models have ~4.9M parameters, the single-layer RNN (2119 units) might still be under- or over-parameterized relative to the DRNNs depending on how inter-layer connections are counted. Please report the exact parameter counts (input, recurrent, inter-layer, and output) for each model, and if possible add a single-layer baseline explicitly matched on total parameters.
- Scope of baselines: Please include or discuss results from Mikolov et al. on similar Wikipedia-scale character language modeling/compression to better position your contributions.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Timely and relevant exploration of deep temporal architectures with solid empirical performance using simple training.
- Insightful analyses demonstrating learned temporal hierarchies and specific long-range behaviors.
- Clear presentation and thoughtful discussion of limitations.

Cons:
- Limited controlled comparisons to disentangle architectural depth from parameter count/width.
- Some training choices (gradient normalization, layer-wise training for DRNN-1O) lack ablations and theoretical grounding.
- Interpretability of layer-wise contributions is suggestive but not conclusive.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations on gradient normalization (vs. clipping; vs. none) and discuss its effect on stability and final BPC.
- Add depth-from-scratch experiments with parameter-matched widths; include a 2-layer DRNN vs. single-layer RNN with the same number of units.
- Consider a variant with deeper transitions within a time step to compare depth-in-time vs. depth-in-state.
- Expand related work to include Mikolov et al. and clarify comparisons.
- When analyzing layer roles, report quantitative deltas in BPC when removing each layer’s output contribution, with uncertainty estimates.
- Clarify how training data exposure and compute compare to prior work (HF on clusters), to contextualize efficiency claims.

Minor comments
- Define DRNN-1O and DRNN-AO in the main text when first introduced, not only in Figure 1.
- Tighten the explanation of “bifurcations” and the exact normalization rule (norm threshold, per-minibatch or per-parameter set).
- Verify that all datasets, character sets, and preprocessing choices are consistent across comparisons and explicitly stated.

Overall evaluation
This is a well-executed and clearly presented study of stacked recurrent architectures with both competitive results and useful analytical insights into emergent time scales. With stronger parameter- and unit-matched baselines, ablations on training heuristics, and a broader set of depth comparisons, the empirical case for the architectural benefits of DRNNs would be significantly strengthened. I find the paper promising and believe the additional experiments suggested above would make it a strong contribution.
