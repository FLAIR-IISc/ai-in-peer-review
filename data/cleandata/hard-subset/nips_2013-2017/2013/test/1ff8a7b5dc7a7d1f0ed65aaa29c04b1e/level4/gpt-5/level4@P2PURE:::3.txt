I appreciate the point about the arbitrariness whereby older data benefits from many more layers of processing than newer data. Building a deep RNN seems like a reasonable way to improve shorter-term modeling, with possible additional benefits. Another obvious idea would be to insert extra nonlinear layers between successive timesteps. Have you tried this?

How important was gradient normalization, and why does it “avoid the problem of bifurcations”? There is a recent ICML 2013 paper from the University of Montreal on gradient truncation and optimization in RNNs that may be relevant here. Also, could normalizing the gradient in this way interfere with the “averaging” behavior of SGD?

Regarding prior results on Wikipedia compression tasks, there is work by Mikolov et al. that would be useful to compare against.

Of the experiments probing the roles of different layers in terms of time scale and possible “abstraction,” the text generation results (Table 2) are the most persuasive. However, as the authors note themselves in the paragraph around line 241, interpreting the effects of this kind of “brain surgery” on RNNs is problematic because of complex interdependencies that can form among the outputs of different layers.

For me, the biggest missing piece is whether higher layers are actually better at processing more abstract, long-term properties of text than if those units were placed in the first layer—i.e., are they benefiting in a nontrivial way from the preceding levels of processing? The fact that higher layers appear to adopt more abstract and longer-term roles after training is suggestive but not conclusive evidence.

I also notice that the regular RNN appears to have fewer parameters in these experiments, since 2767^2 × 5 > 2119^2 (I’m counting both recurrent and inter-layer weight matrices, hence the multiplication by 2), so the comparison might be somewhat unfair. A more convincing result would show deeper RNNs outperforming a standard RNN with the same number of parameters—or, even better, the same number of units. For example, compare a 2-layer DRNN to a standard RNN with the same number of units, arranged so that the parameter-count difference does not favor the RNN so strongly that the comparison becomes unfair. This would substantially strengthen the paper’s claims.

Also, instead of Figure 2, it would be more informative to show how DRNNs of various depths perform on the benchmarks when trained from scratch, possibly making shallower models wider to compensate for differences in the numbers of parameters and/or units.

- This paper studies a hybrid of deep and recurrent neural networks (DRNNs), which resemble deep networks but include recurrent connections at each layer operating through time. The authors show that this architecture can perform very well on text prediction/compression compared to existing approaches. A large portion of the paper presents experiments arguing that higher-level layers represent more abstract, long-term structure. These experiments are fairly convincing, though I have a few reservations (detailed above) and would like to see a couple more experiments. It is worthwhile to investigate such deep temporal networks and to understand how they function after training. The paper is easy to read and is intellectually honest and thorough about its potential problems and shortcomings, which I especially appreciate.
