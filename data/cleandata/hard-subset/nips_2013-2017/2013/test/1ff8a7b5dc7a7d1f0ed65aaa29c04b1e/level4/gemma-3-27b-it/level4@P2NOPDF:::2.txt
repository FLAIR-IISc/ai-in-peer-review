This paper investigates the usefulness of deep recurrent neural networks, presenting interesting findings regarding their performance and internal representations. The core results demonstrate that, with a fixed number of parameters, deeper RNNs outperform standard RNNs on character-level language modeling. Further analysis reveals that deeper layers within these networks capture more long-range dependencies in the data, and importantly, the paper shows that plain Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT) can successfully train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses – a task previously thought to require more sophisticated methods like Highway Networks (HF).

However, the paper’s claim to *introduce* deep RNNs is inaccurate. Such architectures have been explored previously, for instance, in Graves et al. (2013) [http://www.cs.toronto.edu/~graves/icassp_2013.pdf] and likely earlier works. While novelty isn't essential, the paper should refocus its contribution as a detailed analysis of deep RNNs rather than their introduction.

The analysis itself is valuable, but its scope currently limits its generalizability. Extending this analysis to other tasks, such as speech recognition, would significantly strengthen the conclusions. The observed benefits of depth might be specific to character-level language modeling, and testing on a different domain would help determine if the findings are broadly applicable or an artifact of the chosen task.

Regarding the experiments utilizing Highway Networks, the computational cost seems potentially excessive. Truncated Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT), a technique established in the 1990s and successfully employed by Mikolov (as detailed in his PhD thesis) for training high-performing RNN language models, likely offers a more efficient approach. It would be beneficial to confirm whether truncated BPTT can achieve comparable performance in this context.

In summary, this work provides an insightful analysis of deep RNNs, highlighting their ability to outperform shallower counterparts and capture long-range dependencies. The main contributions are: 1) the demonstration that deep RNNs surpass standard RNNs in character-level language modeling when parameter counts are controlled; 2) evidence that deeper layers exhibit a greater capacity for modeling long-range structure; and 3) the surprising finding that BPTT alone can train RNNs to solve the parentheses balancing problem. However, the paper would be considerably stronger with an expanded analysis encompassing a different problem domain – for example, speech – to assess the generalizability of the observed advantages of depth and the behavior of long-range information capture in deeper layers.
