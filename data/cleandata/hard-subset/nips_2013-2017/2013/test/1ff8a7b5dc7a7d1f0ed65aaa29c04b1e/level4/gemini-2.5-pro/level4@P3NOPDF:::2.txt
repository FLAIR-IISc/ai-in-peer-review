This paper presents an analysis of deep Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and investigates their effectiveness on character-level language modeling. The work offers several interesting conclusions: 1) when holding the number of parameters constant, deep RNNs outperform their shallower counterparts; 2) an analysis of the network's hidden states suggests that deeper layers learn representations corresponding to longer-term dependencies; and 3) contrary to previous assumptions, standard Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT) is capable of training RNNs to solve the parenthesis balancing task, a feat previously thought to require more complex optimization methods like Hessian-Free (HF).

The analysis is meaningful and provides valuable insights into the behavior of deep recurrent architectures. The finding that deeper networks contain more "long-term information" is particularly noteworthy and contributes to a better understanding of how these models represent hierarchical temporal structure. Furthermore, the demonstration that BPTT can succeed on the parenthesis balancing benchmark is a surprising and useful result for the community.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed to strengthen its contribution.

First, the paper claims to introduce the deep RNN architecture. This claim is inaccurate, as deep RNNs have been explored in prior work (e.g., Graves et al., ICASSP 2013) and likely even earlier. The paper's contribution is not the introduction of a new architecture but rather a focused analysis of an existing one. This is a perfectly valid and valuable contribution, and the paper should be reframed to accurately reflect this. The focus should be on the novelty of the analysis, not the architecture itself.

Second, the empirical evaluation is limited to a single task (character-level language modeling). While the results on this task are interesting, they may be an artifact of this specific problem domain. The paper's conclusions regarding the benefits of depth would be far more compelling if they were shown to generalize to other types of sequential data. Adding a similar analysis on a second, distinct task, such as one from speech processing, would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about the general utility and properties of deep RNNs.

Finally, the experimental comparison of training methods is incomplete. The paper relies on the computationally expensive Hessian-Free (HF) optimizer, but it omits a crucial baseline: truncated BPTT. Truncated BPTT is a widely used and highly effective method for training RNNs, and it has been successfully used to train state-of-the-art language models at a fraction of the computational cost of HF (e.g., Mikolov's work). It is essential to include a comparison with this strong and efficient baseline. For instance, it would be highly informative to see the performance of a model trained with truncated BPTT using a moderate truncation length (e.g., 30 steps). While this approach might be less likely to solve the long-range parenthesis balancing problem, it is important to verify this and, more critically, to see if it can achieve competitive entropies on the language modeling task. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess the practical trade-offs of the proposed models.

In summary, this work presents an interesting and timely analysis of deep RNNs with several valuable findings. However, its impact is currently limited by an overstatement of novelty, a narrow empirical scope, and the absence of a key baseline training algorithm. The paper would be substantially improved by accurately positioning its contribution as an analysis, demonstrating the findings on an additional task, and providing a direct comparison with truncated BPTT.
