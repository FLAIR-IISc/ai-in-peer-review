- Paper is largely single-task; reviewer suggests testing truncated BPTT (e.g., 30 steps) as a cheaper alternative that may still achieve competitive entropies.  
- Testing truncated BPTT is important because it may not learn to balance parentheses but could reduce computational cost—this should be verified.  
- The paper investigates the utility of deep recurrent neural networks and presents several interesting findings.  
- With a fixed number of parameters, deeper recurrent networks outperform standard (shallower) RNNs on the task.  
- Deeper networks contain more "long-term information" in their representations.  
- Plain BPTT can train character-level RNNs to balance parentheses, contradicting beliefs that hierarchical feedback is required.  
- The paper incorrectly claims to introduce the "deep RNN" architecture; prior work (e.g., Graves 2013) has used similar architectures.  
- It is acceptable to focus on analysis rather than claiming a novel architecture.  
- The analysis would be much stronger if validated on multiple tasks (e.g., speech) to avoid task-specific artifacts.  
- HF experiments seem unnecessarily expensive; truncated BPTT (used since the 1990s and by Mikolov) likely performs well and should be tested.  
- Summary of main results: (1) deeper RNNs outperform with fixed parameters, (2) deeper layers show more long-range structure, (3) plain BPTT can learn to balance parentheses; extend analysis to other tasks to confirm generality.
